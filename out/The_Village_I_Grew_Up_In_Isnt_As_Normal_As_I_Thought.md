Hello! Last September I finally moved out of my tiny little village to go to University half the country away. It’s a 5 hour drive from home (which I know is very little to Americans), in the middle of a huge city, and could not be further from the kind of place I’m used to living in. Don’t get me wrong, I’m not a hobbit, I’ve left my village plenty of times, but the *lived* experience, the shape of daily life and goings on, has been a complete culture shock - positively, negatively, and everywhere in between.

This much, though, I expected! I knew it was gonna be a plunge - it’s kind of why I chose where I did. I was at least lucky enough to have housemates and coursemates alike that also grew up in little villages all over the country (and beyond) to share and confide in during the complete tonal shift of our lives. But I’m starting to realise aspects of my childhood and growing up where I did aren’t as universal as I previously thought. 

Weird things happen in villages. Things different to the weird things I’m sure also happen in cities. I always assumed the stuff that happened to and around me whilst I was growing up was the kind of weirdness that happened in every place that was an hours odd drive from a population centre above 1,000 - but apparently not. I’ve shared a couple of stories now with Uni friends that I thought were regular just to receive the most confused looks, laughs as if I’m joking, and genuine concern. One friend directed me here, said there’s plenty of people on this sub that would get some enjoyment out of some of the weird shit I remember, so that’s what I’m here to do. I’ve included the ones that I’ve told to people so far below, but even as I typed these out I started remembering some other stuff so you might hear from me again soon.

**I - The Tank**

The house I grew up in was on the edge of the village, specifically the edge that led out into farmland and fields for miles and miles. Our back garden was quite small, and there was very little to do in the village, so often my dad and I would go for walks out in the fields and just play. Sometimes we took a ball, sometimes we found sticks and played whatever imaginary game I fancied, sometimes we just explored a new path. Pretty regular father-son stuff I guess. 

We had done the latter today, after climbing a small fence and finding a path through some new field we hadn’t gone through before. I know “a new field” doesn’t sound exactly exciting, but there’s something about middle-of-nowhere English countryside I can’t really explain. When you put your back to the village, and go far away enough from any stray backroads, you suddenly become very aware you are somewhere very old. I even remember feeling it back then, as a kid, before I could really put my finger on it. You realise you’re looking at a land that hasn’t changed for a very very long time - longer than some of the countries you guys reading this have officially been around. There’s an energy to it just underneath the surface, and being amongst it makes you understand how people could be superstitious, thinking fae and other mythologies existed out beyond our lights and walls. It makes you understand how someone could think magic was real. 

We had walked for a while when we reached a nearly overgrown field, all the wildgrass flowing gold in the late sun. I’m guessing an out of season cattle pasture, with a clutch of huge bushes in the centre that looked like there was a hole leading into it. My dad pointed it out to me, lifted me on his shoulders so I could see it over the grass, before excitedly declaring we had to see inside. I remember I was interested, but also scared - the field straddled a slight hill, with the bush cove at the peak. The way the blue sky backdropped the crest of the hill made it look like the edge of the world.

We went anyway. It took some time wading through the grass almost taller than me, as the faded path veered off some other direction. The first thing I remember was the smell. It hit us half way through the grass, sort of eggy and cloying. My dad made a fart joke about it before telling me “it's just animal poop, there’s probably a few fox or rabbit holes in here” before he ducked inside the little cove entrance. I followed him and remember thinking the inside was as cool as I hoped it would be. A portion of it was a large mounding of nettles and such growing over it, but the rest was a flat shaded patch of grass that the bushes fully encapsulated, almost like an igloo of leaves. The gold of the day came through as a patchwork pattern that waltzed along the floor in a slow wind. I looked for animal burrows but couldn’t find any, couldn’t find much about the sulfury smell before I was distracted by my dad

“now what in the…”

He was crouched in front of the mound and I wandered over as my eyes continued to adjust to the shade. Peering out of the heaped earth before my dad was metal, rusted and gnarled but in a cohesive shape. I imitated his awe with something akin to a “wow” but honestly I had no clue what I was looking at. It was cylindrical, but mostly buried under the earth. All we could see was the rounded corner of one end and a sort of thick short pipe on the top that looked like an unearthed drain cover. The curved side of the cylinder was semi visible, the earth giving way in a diagonal strip between the face and the tube, but the rest remained buried.

“I think it’s an old tank” my dad said, pre-empting the question he knew I was going to ask. Now, I was young, and I had played a lot of video games by this point. I’m pretty sure my only run in with the word “tank” by then was *Halo CE*, so I remember assuming he meant *that* kind of tank - meaning childhood me was even more amazed. I immediately went to touch it, but my dad stopped me and pointed to the brown and orange layer of rust that clung to it like coral. 

“It's very old and very rusty. It could break if we touched it, and rusty metal makes for real bad cuts.” I remember nodding before asking 

“do you think it went underwater?” 

I really don’t know why I asked this, but I remember asking it vividly - I can only assume I was young enough to know the concept of a submarine but not enough to distinguish it from a tank, but I do have a very excited memory of picturing the field underwater at one point. My dad just gave the usual diplomatic 

“Maybe” response of a parent not knowing what the fuck their child is talking about. We left shortly after, talking about it all the way home.

Naturally, I bragged about it to my friends the next day - a real life tank, buried in a field that may or may not have once been under the ocean, that only I had seen. They didn’t believe me, even after swearing on my mother, so we decided to walk there after school, 5 of us. I almost forgot the way, and all the claims of “I bet you x y and z there’s nothing there” were coming out, but eventually we reached the field with the bush cove atop the hill. The edge of the world was somewhere else this time. Instead, a formless grey malaise of cloud hung over it all. No gold on that day. I remember them being as silent as I was when I first saw it, but I was just loudly gloating that time. 

The smell hit again, way worse this time, and I echoed my dad’s sentiment about animals at the kind of jokes they started making. The fingers pointed from one to another, jokingly accusing each other of farting. One of them went to say that my dad and I came here to go to the toilet as we ducked in one by one, but they trailed off as we all laid eyes on the tank.

“That’s not a tank” one said bitterly after a quiet moment of awe, but when I stubbornly asked what it was they just quietly shrugged. One of my friends went up to try and touch it. I spoke out like my dad, parroting his exact words this time, but he didn’t stop. He looked at me for a moment before crouching down right in front of it. I cried out for him to wait, tried to reach to grab his arm, but his small fist knocked on the metal three times before I could stop him. We all heard it echo dully inside, standing in silent anticipation as to what came next. Nothing at first. The kid who knocked spoke first, laughed whilst saying 

“Your dad’s a wuss, nothing's gonna-” before being cut off by three loud knocks returning from the inside of the tank. 

We all ran out yelling almost immediately, the two more scared boys running half way down the field. We sat and watched from the tall grass for a while, but nothing happened, even as we got closer again and crept back in through the cove entrance. After a while, the kid who knocked before became emboldened again and went to knock, the two scared ones yelling out for him to stop. The same three loud taps rang against it again, and we all held our breath for another silent moment. Sure enough, it sang back through loudly. Once again, we ran back out - not as far this time, just to the entrance in case anything happened, but nothing did. He went up a third time, and I remember he did a *dun-duduh-dun-dun…dun-dun* pattern as the two scared kids started to get increasingly panicked. The remaining kid other than me tentatively walked up to join him, asking for his turn next. I was stood between the two pairs, terrified but curious. The tune came back through perfectly, causing one of the scared ones to start crying. The other threatened to go home to tell his parents. The first to knock begged them not to, told them they’d leave in a second, but was cut off. 

The boy waiting for his turn had started to scream. He had pressed his hand flat against the curve of the buried metal cylinder, and from the way he began to lash against the mound he could not remove it. The sulfur smell doubled in intensity, and a horrible hissing sound joined it. The scared two were running in an instant, out and down through the grass as one of them cried out in terror tears. Me and my other friend ran to the one stuck to the tank, desperately grabbing at his arm and trying to pull it away. The boy was kicking and screaming, incoherently crying for help as smoke or steam or something else poured out from the edges of his hand. I remember looking down at it and stopping for a moment, realising the metal beneath his hand was beginning to glow hot from a horrible heat I swear I could feel from there. The smell was joined by the cooking of flesh.

I don’t know how long it took to remove his hand from the tank, probably only a few seconds, but I don’t remember much after looking down at his hand. I remember walking him through the field as he babbled and cried. I remember looking down at his hand and wanting to throw up. I remember not seeing the other two friends until the next day. I remember my house was closest to the field, so we took him there and my mother phoned his, after calling the ambulance. I remember we lied as well. I don’t really know why, I guess we just thought we did something wrong and tried to make it seem less so. We said it was a friction burn from a rope swing we went to sometimes, which must have worked to some degree because no one asked us many more questions. Maybe they knew we did something stupid and knew this would dissuade us from doing it again. They were only half wrong if that was the case - I had nightmares for a long time about going back to the tank and seeing the layers of his hand that stayed fused to the tank still being there, sizzling away. The friend who got burned came back into school a couple weeks later, his hand still wrapped up for a while though. We didn’t stay friends after primary school - he went to a private school I think, but for the rest of our time knowing each other none of us spoke about the tank again, although I’ve been in the field since on dog walks and such. The bush cove still sits there, the smell latched faintly to the wind sometimes, and I’m sure someone has or will wander in there again at some point. 

I remember I told my mother the truth about the event recently, many years after the fact. She seemed to vaguely remember, but when I finished the story she didn’t seem particularly surprised or shocked. Just said “Well, I’m sure that taught you to not go touching stuff you shouldn’t.” In all honesty, it didn’t.

  
**II - The Candle**

I think about a year after the tank incident, they started to build wind turbines in some of the further out fields - far away enough that I don’t remember walking out that far, but close enough that they crested the horizon and loomed upon the village. There was the outrage I’m sure every village went through when the wind farms started to go up across the country. People suddenly became very opinionated about things they don’t know a lot about. I remember thinking it was all silly even as a kid when a local, who as far as I remember had no credentials besides being old and angry, spoke at our school about how us kids should be against it. I just thought they looked cool, but I don’t recall really caring either way.

I do remember going home and seeing my great grandmother was visiting. I wanted to know if all old people just had a natural vendetta against windfarms, so I asked if she was angry about it too.

“I don’t care what they build. Doesn’t matter what I think if they’re always going to build it anyways. I just wish they would build somewhere else” she grumbled, I remember her jaw jittering with frustration as she did. I asked her if they were too close. 

“To us? Oh no, not too close, no. Just a silly place to put 'em.” When I asked her why, she leaned forward in her chair to get closer to my face and gave a creaking whisper. 

“That out there is fairy land. Mustn't go where they dance, now”. I was already a bit scared of my great grandma - I was kind of scared of all the very old people when I was really young, and this didn’t help. I remember running away and trying to forget the words, and the bits she muttered as I left. Something about circles. 

The wind farms started construction properly soon after, but bad news started coming from the site pretty quickly. The turbines had been talk of the village, including our school. It wasn’t just the weird assemblies with people talking for and against them; the huge lorries ferrying the colossal stems and blades passed through our tiny quiet village, right past the front of the school as we were all filtering in at the start of the day. One of the older boys claimed his dad worked at the site, and jumped on the buzz of excitement to garner some popularity. 

The first few days were just bragging about how cool it was, normal kid stuff, but soon it got weird.  The way he described it, or at least the way he described his father describing it, was that one of the turbines was haunted. Naturally we were all terrified and intrigued in equal measure. We would gather around every playtime to hear if his dad had told him any new stories from the site. All the others went up with no delay or setbacks, but one of them was the opposite. One day he said all the tools went missing, only to be found strung up in nearby trees. Another day he brought the story of all the portaloos locking their occupants in. Wires would be cut. Excavator fuel tanks found filled with leaves and chestnuts. Tidbits that felt straight from fairy tales that whipped through the playground like wildfire. I would go home and tell my parents about it, but they said the boy was just making things up. Dismaying, but I loved the idea of ghosts as a kid and was set on it being real no matter what, so every day I’d run over to hear the new crazy story from the wind turbine site. Ladders disappearing from underneath workers. Rings of mushrooms and flowers showing up across the site, bursting from the ground overnight. I remember one of the stories that gave me nightmares for a few days - a worker, last to leave the site at twilight looks up to the turbine, at this point a good 50 metres high, and sees the shape of people peering over the incomplete neck before quickly ducking away. The boy got told off a few times for trying to scare the lower classes, but he insisted he wasn’t lying. The gatherings became more covert, hiding from the teachers at breaktime and swearing secrecy. 

As usual, I went home and told my parents, half in awe and half in fear. They tried to calmly explain that, if it was true, it was probably just protestors trying to cause disruptions. My great grandma was staying with us again, as she did a lot towards the end, interjected from the other room. 

“Sounds like they broke a fairy circle to me.” My mother chided her, asked her not to try and scare me further, but I didn’t hear the rest after running away to play with toys and distract myself from her words. 

The older boy came with a new story a few days later, but I remember he seemed as scared as us that time. His dad had gone to leave for work to find his car turned upside down. It was just sat there, on its roof. No noise, no alarm. The village was way too safe for cameras or anything, so there was no evidence of the crime save the crime itself. I can confirm this story, if any others weren’t, was 100% truthful as I saw the picture in the monthly newsletter some time after. Either way, on the day, he was shaken, said he was worried the ghost had followed his dad home. 

He didn’t come to school for a little bit after that, but the next story we didn’t have to hear from him, as it reached the county news. Someone had fallen to their death from the top of the almost-complete turbine. He was performing some urgent work on the exterior, and his safety harness, plus both of his redundancy cables, spontaneously failed. No sign of breakage, and two other workers on standby at the top said he made no move to unclip himself. One moment he was there, the second he was falling away, the third he hit the ground below. Although it was almost complete, work was halted for a few days until a complete investigation and safety analysis could be complete. 

The investigation, as well as the construction of the turbine, never concluded. My dad woke me up in the middle of the night, ushering me to come and see. I didn’t understand at first, but I obliged as he gently tugged my arm. He guided me to the spare room at the back of the house, where during the day you could usually see the heads of the turbines just cresting the houses. At night, they stood unseen save for the heartbeat pulse of a red light upon the completed turbines. The blinds were open and my mother was sitting on the spare bed, hand over her mouth. 

The troubled, bladeless turbine was ablaze. There was a dim orange glow that clung to a street still wet from earlier rain, and I could see the faint silhouettes of people watching from the street cast in amber. The flickering light shaded the smoke pouring from it, framing the disembodied turbine head over a dirty orange smudge in an otherwise pitch black sky. I don’t remember how long we watched. It raged for most of the night, and we sat and watched quietly until whatever the flames fed on choked out and turned to embers, still visible like a cigarette cherry floating in the night sky. I kept watching after the ambient light of the fire died, after my parents went to bed, and I swore I could see things moving around the embered stem. I kept rubbing my eyes, thinking I was just tired, but I swear they were there. Tiny lights in the sky around where the turbine was, blinking in and out as they danced around in the distance. It wasn’t just the stars being dragged down to the horizon, nor the guiding lights of the turbine’s companions. They seemed to collide and merge and split and play. I was mesmerised, and I would go back every so often at night to see if I could see them there again but I never did. I told my parents, who I know didn’t believe me, even if they said how awesome it sounded. When my great grandma heard it though, she smiled and just said 

“I told you that was fairy land.”

The turbine is still up, motionless next to its finished kin. I’m guessing they tried to take it down or repair it but had more of the same elevating mischief occur. As far as I’m aware no solid cause for the fire, or the death, was found, but I can’t find any solid records beyond local news on either. But we still call it the candle, and I can still see it from the spare room whenever I visit home.
