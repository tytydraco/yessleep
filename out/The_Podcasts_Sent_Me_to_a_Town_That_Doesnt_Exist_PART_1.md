Common to most that frequent these forums, when I’m studying or doodling or lying on my bed staring into the void, I like having someone in my ear to make me feel less alone. Okay not like that, but you get it. True crime, fake crime, eldritch horrors, the works.

The aforementioned studying was for my computer science degree, which… was admittedly looking less and less enticing by the day. My concern, however, at that point at least, was paying for it. School wasn’t cheap and with all the classes I was taking it was hard to find a job that would let me work through the holes I had in my class schedule.

The last time I remember working on anything school related was November 18th, 2011. Now, you’ll probably notice… that was almost a decade and a half ago. I’m getting there, but it’s gonna take a minute. Anyways, back to November 18th. I put on a podcast as I worked on my final project for the semester. It was a true-crime story.

Maria Nunez, small town girl, randomly runs into a hotel magnate, Dorian Long’s son, Nathan, at the airport. They start dating and both of them reveal their artistic passions to each other. Maria writes a book and Nathan suddenly has a knack for painting. Nathan proposes and Maria finds out that PLOT TWIST he’s been stealing another artist's work. So, in an effort to break away, she sneaks back to her hometown… and then disappears. The name of the town? Lorem Falls. I’d never heard of that one before.

I kept listening. There were plenty of small towns in America I’d never heard of.

I kept typing as well, but when the next episode of another podcast auto played, my attention was redirected. The show, Opening the Void, had just released its 500th episode.

“Welcome back, everyone, to Opening the Void”.

The host, Mark Renfield, might’ve been the MOST loyal person to the show, right next to me. His entire crew had rotated out over the course of their five hundred episodes.

“We have returned once again to the great state of Wisconsin. This time we find ourselves as temporary residents of the Richardson Inn in Lorem Falls.”

Well, that’s interesting…

I stopped my writing and pulled up another tab to look for Lorem Falls. Search results were minimal. Most results were the exact reason I’d never heard of them. The town was annexed from another town. In the 40s. That’s definitely not normal. I tuned back into the podcast as I kept researching.

“David, thank you very much for your INCREDIBLE contribution to the show. Apparently, David’s father, Michael, was a resident of the hotel shortly after Mr. Richardson’s death… and after twenty-two years, he’s never left.”

The last bit of news on the town I could find was, ironically, their news station disappearing. The only other major thing I could find about the town was “Mega Mountain”, a 1980s themed amusement park… built in the 80s. Like the present tense of nostalgia…

As I continued listening to the podcast, some technical issues started to occur. People's voices would be layered over themselves… and then they’d just disappear. I was barely listening by the end. Apparently Mega Mountain had vanished in 2008. The attendees, staff, and rides all suddenly gone without a trace. I had to know more… my brain, the puzzle lover it is, was desperate for information about a town I’d never heard of before. I check for a Mega Mountain website. Broken link. Missing park, missing webpage… figures.

BUT, the park existed once… maybe the site did too. I plug the website into the internet archive and find an archived version of their website from 2008. Must’ve been from right before the park disappeared. I give it a look. Site looks pretty on par for late 2000s websites. Tabs on the top for park information, ticketing, lodging, photos, and park news… but then I checked the bottom.

Copyright 2008, Giza Computational Services. It’s like a never-ending chain of things I’ve never heard of. I look up Giza Computational Services. The only result is a webpage. They’re a tech company from the 90s. What they sell is anybody’s guess. The page is covered in stock images of tech and text suggesting they “look into the future” with their tech. No suggestion at all that they had anything to do with a theme park. I was about to leave the page when my mouse stuck and accidentally highlighted over some text. Text that I hadn’t seen before. It was hidden in the margins. White text on a white background.

It said “Apply”.

My curiosity was piqued.

Hindsight is 2020, blah blah blah. I missed 2020 so you’ll have to cut me some slack.

“Apply” was a hyperlink. I clicked it. The page refreshes and before it loads my webpage asks to use my microphone. I click deny because I have no idea why that would be necessary. The page finally loads and reveals a list of phrases, a microphone symbol, and at the top, a set of instructions.

“Record yourself saying the following phrases. Payment will be deposited to your account within three business days.”

What was this? Who would pay someone to say phrases? They weren’t even anything particularly interesting.

1. **A thick coat of black paint covered all.**
2. **The bombs left most of the town in ruins.**
3. **The case was puzzling to the old and wise.**
4. **The little tales they tell are false.**

Those were just the ones I remembered. There were a lot. Like, a few hundred. I spent the next hour and a half speaking to my computer, waiting to see what might happen when I reach the end.

4:15 AM. That’s what time it was when I finally finished speaking the sentences listed.

At the bottom of the page was a confirmation checkbox and a bunch of legal jargon I didn’t feel like reading. NOTE TO READERS: Read the legal jargon.

Suddenly, my browser flashed through a series of pop-ups that would be killed the second they opened. It was like watching my computer shuffle a digital deck of cards. I sat in silence for a moment… I didn’t know what that meant, but again, it was 4:15 AM, so I was too tired to focus further. I received payment of eight hundred dollars the following morning directly to my bank account( I didn't provide them my bank account, so I'm not exactly sure how they got it, BUT I was always told to not look a gift horse in the mouth), but it would be one week later that I'd learn exactly what I'd just done.
