That’s one of the things I have always been asked at the doctor’s office, ever since I was a little.  I answer a simple “yes” every time, thinking back to a vague yet dreaded memory from childhood, stuffed in between the more common anxieties that plagued my adolescence. The nurse moves on through her routine questions and I forget about it. I never had a reason to dwell on those bad memories. Not until this morning. 

It wasn’t really an allergy, not like you typically think of one; hives across the skin, throat slowly closing until you can barely breathe. It was a mental thing. I had just had surgery, tonsils taken out, and I was prescribed tropbulin to reduce the swelling.  

I was only ten when all of this happened, an only child, doted on by my single mother. About a year before the surgery, my mom and dad got a divorce. Mom never told me directly, but from muffled arguments and neighborhood gossip I was able to put together that Mom had seen Dad out with another woman, several times. I was shocked to hear this, as any child would be, but it was really, *truly,* shocking. It still surprised now as an adult. 

My father loved my mom, told her it all the time and showed her in every way that he could; buying her flowers nearly every week “just because”, slow dancing with her in the living room during rainy evenings, and even buying her a kitten despite him being allergic, promising that he would “just take a few Benadryls every day” and everything would be fine.  

Another thing that made it all strange was that no matter how many times he was questioned and accused, he never admitted guilt. Not only did he not feign it was some meetings with a co-worker or a woman who was only a friend, he denied it having happened at all. I remember hearing them yelling in the kitchen, their faint voices echoing under the crack in my bedroom door. 

“Whoever you saw, it wasn’t me,” Dad stated firmly. 

“You think I don’t know what you look like, David?” Mom started, “How you walk, what your voice sounds like? I was right there! I sat there for half an hour while you sipped red wine with her!” 

“It wasn’t me!” He replied. It sounded like a plea. 

“Oh, so who was it, David, your long-lost twin?”

The divorce was finalized soon after that. Mom got full custody of me and they sold the house. I got to see Dad every two weeks or so. He never mentioned the affair to me, but I could tell that he wanted to tell me, to promise me, that it wasn’t true. 

“I love you, never forget that,” he would always say. 

It seemed like when Mom and I were just getting settled into the new house, my throat started to hurt. Typical luck.

I took the first dose of tropbulin the day after my surgery, when I was still bedridden at home. My mother did everything she could to entertain me, bringing me ice cream and video games and puzzles. In the evening, she went on a short trip to the store to get groceries and some “get well soon” balloons she insisted I needed.  When she got back it was nearly nightfall. 

She gave me my pill and wished me goodnight as she left for her room. I swallowed it reluctantly, its bitter taste coating my tongue. The pain in my throat was present but dull and the low hum of the medicine filling my head soon lulled me to sleep.

I woke up suddenly. My heart was beating fast, but I wasn’t sure why. I turned to look at my alarm clock. 3:17. My mouth felt dry. I was about to get a drink of water, but before I could move, I saw something. A figure, standing flush with the wall opposite the bed. I froze. 

It was a man, tall and muscular, stood straight up, his face obscured by darkness. I examined the silhouette as much as I could, making sure I was seeing what I thought. It wasn’t my coatrack, and it wasn’t a jacket, it was most certainly a man. I couldn’t bring myself to move, though not that I really wanted to. I squeezed my eyes closed like a baby playing hide and seek, praying I could somehow disappear from his sight. With my eyes shut I reasoned that it had to be an intruder, and this scared me even more. I finally got up the nerve to open my eyes, half expecting the man to be right next to me. 

But he was gone. I must have imagined it, I thought, though it was hard to believe. I still couldn’t bring myself to move, so I closed my eyes and slumber soon took me again. 

The next morning, consciousness came back to me slowly, along with the dull ache of my throat. I got up and put on some clothes, almost forgetting about my spook in the night. Looking back, it felt hazy, and I knew it had to have been a dream. Just one of the realistic night terrors I had heard about on t.v. and from friends. But remembering the image of the dark figure still frightened me. I walked slowly downstairs to find Mom at the kitchen table eating cereal.

She shifted in her seat, “Hi, honey, you didn’t have to get up so early,” she stated softly, “Why don’t you go back to bed?” 

“No, I’m okay,” I explained. “I had a bad dream last night.” 

She tensed slightly. “Oh, sweetie, I’m sorry, are you alright?” she asked, “Remember, I won’t let anything hurt you, ever.” 

I shrugged. “Oh yeah, I’m fine, no big deal. I’ll probably just hang out in my room today, play some video games with my friends.” 

She told me that was fine. I was a little embarrassed that the dream had scared me so much, but I appreciated my mother’s comforting words. 

I spent rest of day playing video games, finishing off the evening by winning a game of Sorry against Mom.  For the second night I took my medication at her prompting and laid down. By this point I had completely forgotten about the previous night’s dream. It was not long after my head hit the soft, plaid pillow that I was asleep. 

4:21 am.  Once again, I didn’t know what woke me up. I looked towards the foot of my bed. There he was. The same man from the night before, but out of the shadows. He was once again facing me, mouth closed, staring at me. He had a nearly bald head and short brown beard and mustache. His eyes were light blue, and his hands were bawled into fists that sat by his side. 

It was my father. 

I froze again, unable to decide if this dream was scarier than last night’s. At least it was a familiar face and not a monster. I had heard night terrors seemed horrifyingly realistic when they were happening, but I was surprised at just *how real* it felt. I could sense the hairs on my arm prick up as I stared at my dad’s face. It was cold, unmoving, unblinking. I didn’t know what to do. My heart was pounding in my head. I don’t know why, but I decided to say something.

“D-ad,” I barely managed to croak out over my fear and swollen throat.

I blinked and he was gone. No noise, no trace of him. I was relieved, slightly, but still shaking. I couldn’t do it. I texted mom and told her I had had another nightmare. I was too young for a smartphone, but Mom had gotten me an old flip phone earlier that year so I could contact her in an emergency.  She was there within the minute. Tears were now welling up out of my eyes and down my face. 

“Oh honey, it’s okay. Mom’s right here,” she whispered.

“It was dad, he was just staring at me,” I cried.

“It’s okay, honey, none of it was real,” she promised.

She held me for a while, eventually walking me to her room. I slept with her for the rest of the night.

The next day went by in much the same way as the last. I played some games with friends and watched television with Mom as a cool rain washed the neighborhood. 

Dad came by for a little while, brought me some more ice cream and a new board game. My parents tried to be cordial with one another around me, but I could see the resentment in my mother’s eyes when she answered the door and the awkwardness in my father’s as he gave her a soft hello. It was a little scary to be met with his face again after my nightmare, but I reminded myself that it wasn’t real. He stayed for a little bit and left just before dark, walking back out into downpour without so much as a jacket.  

Mom asked me if I wanted to sleep with her that night. I told her no. I wanted to be brave, but sense of dread overtook me as she handed me the pill and left the room. Soft thunder crackled outside. I sat there in silence for a moment, the humming of the AC sounding like a swarm of bugs ready to attack. I swallowed the pill and laid down.  Unconsciousness soon overtook me. 

5:01 am, the blocky red letters of my alarm clock read like a warning.  It was still raining. I didn’t have to move to see him this time, he was closer than ever before, about three feet from the side of my bed. I looked up at him. He still had the face of my father, staring at me just like the previous night. I scanned my eyes back down to his torso. He had something clenched in his right fist. 

A large, open pocketknife. Red. My father had one just like it. 

Before I could move, he stepped closer, a slight smile appearing on his face. He moved just like Dad. He kneecaps were now touching my bedframe. I tried to squirm, but I couldn’t. I couldn’t move, couldn’t scream. 

The vision began to speak, slowly and softly. 

“I love you, never forget that” his voice was identical to my father’s.

He began to move his knife-wielding hand towards me, and somehow, I found my voice. I screamed a primal sound, like a gazelle in the teeth of a lion. 

The vision’s face changed into slight shock, and I watched as instead of disappearing, he ran, out of my room and down the stairs. His footsteps rang loudly from the hall. As the noise faded, my mother darted into the room, glancing slightly behind her before coming to my aid.

 I was able to move again, and I reached out to her, sobbing. I muttered spastically about how he had a knife and was about to kill me. She assured me again that it wasn’t real. There was a nervous air about her as she held me. I imagine she was shaken from hearing her son cry bloody murder in the middle of the night. 

Neither of us went back to sleep. She eventually brought me to her bed and we laid there until the sun finally peered above the horizon.

That morning, after scrolling on her computer for a while, Mom told me that she believed the tropbulin was what was causing the night terrors. She called the doctors and she told me that I wasn’t going to take it anymore. I went to sleep the next night in her bed.

The dreams stopped after that. No man in the corner, no father trying to murder me. I slept with Mom for about a week before returning to my own room. Every morning she would ask me: 

“Any nightmares?”

When I told her no, she would reply confidently, “It had to have been the tropbulin.”

 A couple weeks after the dreams stopped, my mother got a call while we were eating breakfast. I didn’t know until later, but it was the police. Dad hadn’t shown up to work in two days, so his coworker called for a welfare check. He had been found dead in his home. 

The coroner said it was a heart attack. A tragic but natural death with no foul play. Our whole family was shocked. He was in his 40’s and he had never had any heart issues. 

I was heartbroken, and I knew Mom was too, even though at times she seemed unaffected by it. She didn’t cry or show any emotion at the funeral, much to the delight of the neighborhood ladies as they had something to gossip about for the next week. I always just thought she was staying strong for me. 

We got on fine after that, just me and Mom. I grew up, went to college, and moved out of state. I’m 25 now, working for a marketing firm, living alone in a one-bedroom apartment. As I said before, I hadn’t thought about any of this since I was a kid. Not until last night. 

 I woke up with a start. I was falling off a cliff, classic bad dream. Before I could close my eyes to go back to sleep, I saw it. The man, standing by the wall opposite my bed. Still tall and muscular, still with the silhouette of my father. Memories from childhood flushed back, along with an unbearable feeling of dread.

“It’s just a dream,” I thought. “Just like all those years ago.”

As an adult it was still scary, but I made myself go back to sleep.

When I got up this morning I remembered the vision with a shudder, praying the dreams wouldn’t become a normal thing.  As I walked to the bathroom, I nearly stepped on a small metal object in the middle of the floor, kicking it into the wall with a soft thud. I walked up to it to see what it was. 

A red pocketknife. 

I don't have any pocketknives.

I stood there, confused, then horrified. 

I thought back to the figure, my dad’s affair and his everlasting denial of it, his ill-fated death, and my mother’s stoic air at the funeral.  

Her muddied words from all those years ago echoed in my mind. 

“So, who was it, David, your long-lost twin?”
