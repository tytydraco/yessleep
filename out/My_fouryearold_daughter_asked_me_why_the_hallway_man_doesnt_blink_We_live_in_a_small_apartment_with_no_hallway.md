My daughter is four years old. She’s at that age where her imagination is wild, so when she asked me a weird question three nights ago, I didn't think much of it.

We were sitting on the couch when she casually looked up and asked, "Daddy, why doesn't the hallway man blink?"

I chuckled, looking around. We live in a cramped, one-bedroom apartment. "Sweetie," I said, "we don't have a hallway. It’s just the living room and the kitchen."

She didn't laugh back. She just pointed her little finger toward the blank drywall right beside the kitchen counter. "The hallway behind that wall."

Kids say creepy things all the time, so I just ignored it and put on a cartoon to distract her. But the next night, she did it again. This time, I found her standing completely still in the dark, staring directly at that exact spot on the wall.

"Daddy," she whispered without looking at me. "He says you can't see it."

A cold spike of unease hit my chest. "Who says that, bug?"

"The hallway man."

I turned on every light in the place. I checked the entire apartment, banging on the drywall, looking for hidden panels, checking the landlord's old blueprints online—nothing. It was just a solid wall separating our kitchen from the neighbor's unit. No hidden doors. No extra space. Just a dead end.

I thought that would be the end of it, but yesterday morning completely broke me. I walked out of my bedroom and found my daughter sitting cross-legged on the kitchen tile, talking to the blank wall. She was answering questions. She would say something, pause for a few seconds like she was listening, and then answer again. It looked like a completely normal, fluid conversation.

When I rushed over and asked who she was talking to, she looked annoyed at me for interrupting.

"The hallway man," she huffed.

"What does he want, sweetie?"

She just shrugged her tiny shoulders. "He asks why you're still here."

Needless to say, I couldn't sleep last night. I lay awake tossing and turning until around 3:00 AM, when I heard it. Footsteps. Not heavy boots, but slow, bare feet dragging across the floorboards. They weren't coming from outside the front door—they were coming from inside the kitchen.

I grabbed my phone, turned on the flashlight, and crept out. The living room was dead silent and completely empty. But then I noticed my daughter's bedroom door was wide open.

She was standing right inside the doorway, wide awake, staring at that same wall.

And for the first time, I saw it too.

A thin, razor-sharp dark line was running from the floor all the way to the ceiling. It looked exactly like a doorway drawn into the drywall with a charcoal pencil. I don't even remember walking closer, I just remember the sheer, hypnotic dread pulling me toward it. I swear to God that line wasn't there during the day. I would have noticed it.

Before I could touch it, my daughter tugged hard on my sleeve. She looked genuinely terrified, her eyes welling up with tears. "Don't stand there, Daddy."

"Why? What's wrong?" my voice shook.

"Because he thinks you're the other one."

My blood turned to pure ice. "What other one?"

She looked at the wall, then up at me, and then back at the wall. She whispered, "The one that's trying to get back in."

Right then, the charcoal line began to open. Not swinging open like a regular door, but splitting apart like a wet crack in reality. Slowly. Silently. A thin strip of absolute darkness peeled open, and as my eyes adjusted to the shadows inside the crack... I saw my own apartment.

It was the exact same layout. The same furniture. The same framed photos on the shelf. Everything was identical, except someone was standing right in the middle of the room, staring back through the crack at me.

It looked exactly like me. Down to the shirt I was wearing.

And then, the version of me inside the wall smiled. A wide, unnatural, unblinking smile.

My daughter started screaming and crying. I grabbed her in a dead panic, threw her over my shoulder, and bolted into the living room, slamming my bedroom door shut behind us.

When morning came, I finally built up the courage to look. The wall was completely normal again. No line, no doorway, no crack. Just painted drywall. I tried to convince myself I had a severe night terror, that it was just a hallucination brought on by stress.

But tonight, about twenty minutes ago, I was putting my daughter to bed. She looked up at me from her pillows and asked a question that has me sitting in the dark, too terrified to move.

She asked: "Daddy... if you're the real one... why does the hallway man keep calling you by the other name?"
