I don't know how much longer this keyboard will last.

 

The keys no longer click.

They make a soft sound.

 

Like pressing wet meat.

It all started the way these things always do: with a post lost in a tech subreddit. I'm not going to link to it. It's already been deleted.

The user said their charger cable was "melting" without any heat. They uploaded photos. The black coating looked inflamed, like blistered skin. Someone commented that it looked like it was breathing.

We laughed.

Then more posts appeared.

A guy showed his deformed glasses on the table, the temples turned into a kind of gray sludge.

A woman uploaded a video where her toothbrush was dissolving into a dark paste while she was holding it.

 

The explanation was simple: manufacturing defect. A contaminated batch. Some chemical problem.

 

Until it stopped being just one batch.

 

The smell was next.

 

It wasn't sweet.

It was damp.

 

Like a sealed basement after years.

Like a forgotten rag in a sealed bag.

Like something that was once alive and shouldn't be anymore.

It didn't fill the room at once.

It seeped in.

And once you noticed it, you couldn't ignore it.

 

In my building, the first thing to fall was the hallway trash can. Hard plastic. It cracked silently. The next morning there was a gray mass on the floor, barely inflating and deflating, as if it were venting.

 

The building manager called maintenance.

Maintenance didn't come.

The news reported "isolated structural failures." Then hospitals started evacuating patients.

 

A cousin of mine works as a nurse. He sent me an audio message that I still have.

 

"The IV bags are losing volume. They're not bursting... they're thinning. Like something is scraping them from the inside." The IV lines became sticky to the touch. The gloves softened. The surgical masks sank into the staff's mouths as if they were being chewed.

In a hospital in my city, they had to move patients because the oxygen tanks started to collapse.

 

It wasn't an explosion.

It was a disintegration.

 

The term first appeared in closed forums: Gray Famine.

Then, someone leaked a PDF. Supposedly from a biotech company called POLYBIOGEN. I was never able to verify if it actually existed. The document talked about a modified bacterial strain designed to “optimize the biodegradation of persistent polymers in urban ecosystems.”

They had engineered something that ate plastic.

 

And it learned too fast.

Airports closed when the internal seals of the turbines began to disappear. They didn't explode. They simply stopped working.

Cars stalled on the highway because the insulation on electrical wires turned into a gray slime that oozed from under the hood.

PVC pipes began to seep.

The worst part wasn't seeing things fall.

It was hearing them.

It wasn't a crack. It was a murmur.

A low, continuous sound.

As if millions of tiny mouths were scraping at the same time.

The official statement came late.

The bacteria had mutated.

It no longer just degraded synthetic polymers.

It had learned to recognize similar structures. Long chains. Family links.

I thought it was paranoia until my neighbor, Marcos, started bleeding from the nose.

He had a heart stent.

The metal didn't dissolve.

The coating did.

They found it in the bathroom, clinging to the sink. The damp smell filled the apartment. It wasn't strong.

It was close.

As if it were seeping out of his own skin.

Scientists later spoke of microplastics in the blood. Decades of breathing, eating, drinking invisible particles.

They weren't lethal amounts.

 

But they were enough.

We all have traces.

We are all a mixture.

Three days ago I noticed my reusable water bottle was softer. I threw it away. Yesterday, the remote control casing sank under my thumb.

Today I woke up with a dry mouth.

Not sweet.

Metallic.

Like I'd been chewing on coins all night.

I don't have a prosthesis. I don't have implants.

 

But years ago I had knee surgery. Polymer-coated screws. "Biocompatible," the surgeon said.

 

This morning it hurt like something was sanding it from the inside.

Now it doesn't hurt anymore.

That's worse.

The skin over the scar looks… taut. Not red. Not swollen.

Thin.

Like something underneath is pushing, testing the limit.

There are areas of the city where the air seems heavier. The streets are covered in a gray film that spreads slowly, sticking to walls, posts, doors.

Modern buildings are the first to fall. Glass holds. Steel resists.

 

But everything that connects them becomes food.

 

The sound is constant now.

 

It doesn't come from outside.

It comes from everywhere.

 

I tried to go out a few hours ago.

 

The soles of my sneakers left residue on the floor. They weren't footprints. They were peeling layers.

 

I went back when I felt my knee vibrate.

Not move.

Vibrate.

 

Like a phone on silent.

I'm sitting writing this and every time I bend my leg I feel something slide around the bone.

 

It's not pain.

It's pressure.

The "A" key stopped working.

The "S" key sinks.

The space bar is sticky.

 

The smell no longer comes from the hallway.

It comes from me.

 

It's not strong.

It's internal.

I feel it when I breathe through my nose and the air passes at the back of my throat.

 

Like something is gently scraping from inside my chest.

 

I think it's no longer looking for plastic.

 

I think it's learned the difference between food and host.

 

And decided it doesn't matter.

 

The skin over my knee just opened.

 

It wasn't a wound.

 

It was a clean separation. Something gray is peeking through.

It's not moving fast.

It doesn't need to.

The keys are sinking beneath my fingers.

 

I don't know how much further—

The sound is clear now.

 

It's not a murmur.

 

It's chewing.

 

And it's not coming from the keyboard.

It's coming from my bones.
