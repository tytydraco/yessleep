([Part 1](https://www.reddit.com/r/nosleep/comments/1tq47ps/bellhaven_woods_if_you_remember_the_bellhaven/)) ([Part 2](https://www.reddit.com/r/nosleep/comments/1tr7c7p/bellhaven_woods_2_if_you_remember_the_bellhaven/)): By the start of October, the group was coming apart at the seams, and we were all pretending it wasn't.

Our pretending took different forms. Malcolm's form was to keep showing up: van in the driveway with the music on low. He texted everyone individually, often, checking in and asking specific questions so nobody could answer 'fine' and leave it at that. He drove Cecile home twice when she said she was okay to drive and wasn't. He sat with Iris on the nights she couldn't be alone. Summer's form was to work. She had the legal pad and a folder on her laptop to construct some form of a theory on the church. Cecile's form was to not be in the same room as Iris. When they were in the same place — twice, maybe three times in September — they were civil. Cecile would not look at Iris directly. Iris would not push her to. I, on my end, simply tried to keep living and doing what I could when I could.

Iris had good days and bad ones, and the gap between them was growing. On good days she was mostly herself — quieter than she'd been at the beginning of senior year, more careful about what she said and didn't say, but present, lucid, the person I recognized. On bad days she didn't show up places she'd said she'd be, or she showed up somewhere nobody had expected her, or she'd be in a conversation and I'd watch her leave it mid-sentence.

The blackouts had never fully stopped. They'd slowed in the summer, yes, enough that we'd let ourselves believe they were diminishing, and then in October they came back worse. She'd lose afternoons. She'd lose mornings. She called me once at four a.m. from a parking lot she didn't recognize and I stayed on the phone with her while she used her GPS to figure out where she was — a lot behind the strip mall on the eastern edge of town, three blocks from Saint Germaine's — and then I drove there and sat with her in her car until she was ready to drive home, and she cried in the passenger seat.

She said once, the second week of October: "I keep ending up in the same direction. Toward the facility." She looked at the dashboard. "I've been to the edge of the tree line three times. I remember getting there. I don't remember going back."

"Iris–"

"I'm not going in," she said. "I know that's what it wants. I'm not going in." She looked at me and she was very present, very Iris, and I believed her. "But I need you to know."

"We're not going to need to look," I said.

She looked at me. "You're stubborn," she said.

"Yeah."

"It's a problem," she said, and for a moment it was so close to the conversation we'd had in the fall, almost a year ago, that I felt the whole year telescoping, all of it contained in that one exchange.

"Stay on the phone with me when you feel it coming," I said. "Call me."

"Okay," she said.

She called me twice. Both times I talked to her for as long as I could hear her and both times she came back and both times she'd been on the eastern edge of town without knowing how she'd gotten there. The third time she felt it, she didn't call.

Malcolm called me on October 11th, a Sunday, late afternoon. He'd been quiet for a few days and he called rather than texted. I felt the same way I'd felt with Cecile — tense before I even picked up.

"Hey," I said.

"I went out to the fire roads last night," he said.

I waited.

"I know what you're going to say. I went alone, I know. I couldn't sleep and I needed to see the place again. See if the cars were still coming."

"Were they?"

"Not cars," he said. "People. On foot. I parked at the grain elevator and walked in from the north trailhead and there were people on the fire road going in. Four, maybe five. Single file."

"Any lights?"

"They didn't need them," he said, and paused. "Kolby, they knew the path." A pause. "I followed em for a while. I kept back in the trees. They went to the facility access road and then turned off before it. There's a path that branches south, I didn't know it was there, it's not on any map I've looked at. It goes down toward the eastern ridge."

"Malcolm, you shouldn't have followed them."

"I know," he said simply. "I didn't go far. I watched them go and then I went back to the van." He paused. "But I went back in the morning. Early, before anyone else would be out there. And on the south path, past where I'd stopped, I found something."

"What did you find?"

He paused for a long time.

"It's better if I show you. Can you come? Tomorrow after school. Come to the van and I'll take you out there."

"Is it bad?"

"I don't know what it is," he said. "I know it's not good. I think it's proof of something, but not sure of what."

"Okay," I said. "Tomorrow. Don't go back out there tonight."

"I won't. I'll be at the store until eleven. Boring Sunday."

"Okay."

"Kolby." He paused. "Do you ever feel like Bellhaven is waiting for something?"

The question caught me off guard. "...I'm not sure."

"Is she okay today? Iris."

"She was okay yesterday when I talked to her."

"Okay. Good." A pause. "Tomorrow."

"Tomorrow," I agreed.

That was the last conversation I had with Malcolm.

He didn't make it to school on Monday. I noticed at lunch, which was when I'd normally see him if we didn't have any of the same morning classes. I texted and got nothing. I texted again at the end of school and got nothing. I called on the drive home and it rang through to voicemail. I left a message that was deliberately light, because I was afraid to be anything else, and then I sat in my car in my driveway with the phone in my hand and tried to locate a reasonable explanation for why Malcolm wasn't answering: slept through his alarm, had car trouble, his phone died.

I called Summer eight minutes later.

She picked up on the second ring. "He's not answering me either," she said before I finished explaining.

"His mom?" I tried.

"I can't call his mom without it being a thing. Drive by his house. Just see if the van's there."

I drove by his house. The van was not there. His mother's car was in the driveway, the lights inside were on, and the house looked like a house where someone was home having a normal evening.

I called Summer from the street.

"Not there," I said.

She was quiet.

"He said he had work last night. He would have gotten home around eleven-thirty. He left for work from the house. His mom's car is there. So wherever the van is, he drove it somewhere after work."

"Or he went out to the fire roads," Summer said. "After he told you he wouldn't."

I didn't say anything.

"He found something," she said. "He found something and he wanted to show you and he couldn't wait."

"He would have called."

"Check your phone. Did he try to call you? Late."

I checked. Three missed calls from Malcolm between 11:47 p.m. and 12:23 a.m. I had been asleep. My phone had been on silent.

I had been asleep.

I called the non-emergency line and told them there was a vehicle I was concerned about and gave them the plate number and the description of the van and they told me they'd have a patrol check the area.

Then I called Summer back and told her about the missed calls and we stayed on the phone together in silence for a long time.

I won't describe what they found inside it. What I'll say is that the official account — violent, unexplained, a crime scene the sheriff's department spent eleven days trying to make sense of before they started looking for easier answers — doesn't fully capture what it was. What I know is that Malcolm had called me three times. What I know is that he'd found something on the south path near the eastern ridge and he couldn't wait and he went back in the dark, and whatever he found was significant enough to make him call, and whatever he encountered was significant enough that he didn't call again after 12:23 a.m. What I know is that I was asleep. I have lived with that for fifteen years and I expect to live with it for whatever years I have left.

By the following afternoon it was everywhere. Bellhaven had four thousand students in its school district and the news moved through them the way it moves through small communities, which is to say instantly and imprecisely. By noon on Tuesday people knew Malcolm Reyes was dead. By two o'clock people knew the van had been found on the fire road. By three, the name attached to it — the presumed suspect, they said — was Iris.

There were two witnesses. A couple who'd been driving back from the next town late Sunday night had seen a girl on the Route 9 shoulder walking toward the fire road turn-off. They described her hair, her jacket. The jacket was Iris's. There was also a gas station attendant who'd worked the Sunoco on Route 9 until midnight and thought he'd seen someone matching her description in the lot around eleven-fifteen, looking at the tree line.

The problem was that nobody could find Iris.

Her mother had last seen her Sunday evening. Her car was in the lot. Her phone went to voicemail and had gone to voicemail since Sunday night. Nothing in her room was taken.

The rest of our group were brought in separately for interrogations on Tuesday afternoon. Summer went first. Then Cecile. I waited in a plastic chair in the hallway for close to an hour. Pruett came to get me himself, which I hadn't expected. He shook my hand and said he appreciated me coming in before leading me back to the interrogation room: a table, two chairs, a window overlooking the parking lot. He had a folder open in front of him and a pen he didn't use.

He asked me to walk him through what I knew about Malcolm — his habits, his schedule, whether I knew of anyone who might want to hurt him, whether I'd noticed anything unusual in the weeks before. I answered everything directly. He wrote nothing down, which I noticed early and kept noticing. His follow-up questions seemed logical in sequence.

Then the questioning shifted to Iris.

"How close are you to Iris Mercer?"

"We're good friends."

"Have you noticed any changes in her behavior recently?"

I said I had, and described them in general terms: withdrawn, some absences, seemed stressed. He nodded slowly.

"Did she ever mention the woods near Route 9?"

"Not that I remember."

"Were you with Iris on the night of October eleventh?"

"No."

"When did you last see her?"

"A few days before."

"And Malcolm contacted you Sunday afternoon."

"Yes."

"What did you talk about?"

"School, mostly." I lied. "General stuff."

He nodded and asked a few more questions about my whereabouts Sunday night, whether I'd heard from Iris after Malcolm's death, whether she'd seemed frightened of anything recently. I answered everything. At some point I almost convinced myself I'd been wrong about the quality of his interest, that what I'd seen in that church had some reasonable explanation I hadn't landed on yet.

Until he mentioned Saint Germaine's.

"Have you ever attended services there?" he asked.

"Once. With some friends. Back in September."

He nodded slowly. "I thought I recognized you. I try to make the rounds at the congregations in town when I can. Community relations." He paused. "I noticed Iris was there with your group that Sunday."

"Yeah."

"Has she been attending regularly, to your knowledge?"

"I knew she'd been a few times."

"Did you ever speak to anyone there about her?"

The phrasing was wrong in a way that took me a second to locate. He hadn't asked whether anyone at the church knew Iris. He'd asked whether I had spoken to anyone there about her, which assumed the information flowing in that direction was the part worth accounting for.

"No," I said.

"Has Iris ever expressed any anger toward Malcolm? Any conflict between them?"

"No."

He looked at the folder and then back at me. "I have to ask you something directly. Standard question." A pause. "Do you have any reason to believe Iris Mercer was capable of harming someone?"

I thought about the footprints behind my house going into the tree line. I thought about the night at Cecile's house. I thought about Iris's blackouts and the other three deaths in Bellhaven. I thought about Malcolm in his van, alone on the fire roads.

"No," I answered.

"You're sure about that?"

"I'm sure, Sir."

He nodded. "She never mentioned anything that concerned you. Nothing that seemed, in hindsight, like a warning sign?"

"No."

Another pause. "One more thing. I want you to know we follow every avenue available to us," he said. "Sometimes cases close quickly because the evidence points clearly in one direction. That's not a failure of the process. That's the process working the way it's supposed to."

He said it with the same careful neutrality he'd maintained throughout, and somewhere in the middle of hearing it I understood that neutrality was the point, that it had always been the point, that the folder on the table was a prop and everything had been an exercise.

I don't fully remember standing up. The chair went back — I remember that clearly, the sound of it against the floor — and I remember Pruett's expression changing for the first time, something shifting behind his eyes. Then my hand connected with the side of his face and the room came apart into pieces: voices, hands on my arms, the wall very close, the floor moving under my feet.

They kept me in a holding cell through the rest of the afternoon and into the next morning. I sat on the bench for a while and then lay down and stared at the ceiling. At some point a deputy slid a paper under the door informing me I was being charged with assault of a law enforcement officer. Apparently they'd decided that holding me through a classmate's funeral while Iris was still missing was not a situation worth the attention it would draw.

My dad was there when they released me, standing in the hallway with his coat still on and his keys in his hand. He had the same expression he'd had in the parking lot at the last school, the one I'd spent a year hoping not to see again.

He didn't say anything on the drive home.

Iris was still missing the day of Malcolm's funeral.

It was held on Thursday — four days between the van being found and the burial, which is fast, but his mother had her reasons and nobody questioned them. The funeral home was on Ferris Street, a small building with folding chairs inside and a table with photographs near the door. Malcolm at twelve with a gap-toothed smile. Malcolm at fifteen with his arm around his cousin Alicia. Malcolm in the front seat of the van with the window down and the summer light on his face, which someone had printed out and set in the middle of the table, and I had to look away from it after a few seconds.

The casket was closed.

Malcolm had a gift for knowing a lot of people without appearing to work at it, and all of those people came. His coworkers from the grocery store stood near the back wall looking too young and slightly lost. Teachers. Neighbors. A group of guys from the JV basketball team he'd been close to freshman year. His mother in the front row holding herself very straight and Alicia beside her. Alicia had given me the map from the van the day before. I kept thinking about that — the map in my jacket pocket, the only thing of his I had.

Every thought led back to him.

I sat in the back with my dad and tried to listen to the minister. I looked at the closed casket and I thought about the van. The map above the wheel well and the Christmas lights and the way the engine turned over on the second try. I thought about our last conversation, the way he'd said tomorrow, the way I'd said tomorrow back. I thought about my phone on silent on my nightstand and three missed calls at 11:47 and 12:08 and 12:23 and nothing after that. I kept wondering what he had found. If he had been scared. I thought about how unfair it felt that somebody like Malcolm had ended up here. I thought about how many times I'd assumed there would be another chance to see him.

I wanted another chance to hear him say *tomorrow* and believe there would actually be one.

About forty minutes in, my phone buzzed in my pocket.

*Summer: Come to the back lot now. Bring Cecile.*

I looked up at the back of her head three rows ahead. She wasn't on her phone anymore. She was sitting exactly as she had been, waiting.

I leaned over to my dad and told him I needed some air. He looked at me for a moment and then nodded. I moved to the aisle and caught Cecile's eye on the way past.

The parking lot was cold, the sky white and low. Summer was by the back fence with a folded paper, which she spread on the hood of someone's car without asking.

The three of us had already talked about going to look for Iris, but between the interrogations and the funeral and the general velocity of those two days there hadn't been a window. The plan, loosely, was to wait until after the funeral when the department's attention would be elsewhere and Summer had more to go on.

"She was seen this morning," Summer said. "A woman on Millhaven Street was walking her dog along the eastern fence line of the Bellhaven State property at six a.m. She saw someone in the tree cover, moving south. She called the tip line." She smoothed the map. "The police are going to sweep from the north access. That gives us a window."

"How do you know about the tip?" Cecile said.

"Dina Krause still talks to me. She listens to the scanner."

Cecile looked at the map. Then at her dress shoes. Then at the tree line visible above the rooftops to the east.

"Okay," I said. "Let's go."

We went back in through the side entrance and I found my dad's eyes across the room and mouthed *I'll call you*. He nodded in the way that meant he didn't like it and was trusting me anyway.

Cecile had called me a few hours before we were brought in to the station. She'd heard from Summer about the witnesses, about Iris's name in the reports, and she stayed quiet for a long time after I picked up.

"Tell me what you think," she had said. "Actually."

"I think she had been on Route 9. I think the witnesses weren't wrong."

"And Malcolm?"

"I don't know what happened in that van."

Cecile had gone quiet.

"But you think she might have done it," she had said.

"I don't know what she's capable of anymore. I don't know what she's able to stop. If she hadn't been able to stop herself with you..."

"That was different," Cecile had said.

"Was it?"

There had been a long silence on the line. "I remember being stabbed," Cecile had said, "and I remember her face the second after." She had paused. "She was horrified. I think she left because whatever was happening to her slipped for a second.”

"I’ve know Iris for years," Cecile added eventually. “…She came to me crying one day in freshman year, and ended up talking about being abandoned by the saints and angels.” She paused again, before: “But Iris was an angel, so why couldn’t she be saved by them?”

The line went quiet again.

"We need to find her before the police do.” I had argued. “Because if they do—"

"We'll find her," Cecile had said. "But Kolby, I don't know what we're going to find. I'm not going in there with any assumptions. I think you shouldn't either."

That had been two days ago. And now here we were, the three of us in dress clothes cramped into Cecile's car. Summer had a printed map covered in handwritten notes.

By the time we reached the trailhead off Route 9 the last of the daylight had drained out of the sky. Two days earlier there'd been a hard frost and the ground still held onto it in places where sunlight hadn't reached. Leaves cracked softly under our shoes instead of rustling. Every so often one of us would catch a branch and the sound would seem absurdly loud against everything else. Summer walked ahead with the flashlight angled low toward the trail. Cecile stayed close behind her. I took the rear, looking back toward the road through the trees even after it had disappeared completely from view.

The south path was harder to find than Summer had made it sound. She'd come out once during the week to scout it and even with that, she overshot the turn the first time. The branch in the trail looked almost invisible until you were standing directly in front of it. At first glance it resembled a narrow animal path, but once the flashlight hit the ground you could see the dirt was packed too flat and evenly for that. People had been walking there repeatedly for a long time.

The path curved and descended and then leveled and then opened into a space that wasn't exactly a clearing but was less dense than the surrounding woods — older growth, the trees wider-spaced, the canopy high enough that we could see a strip of sky above us.

And in the middle of it, sitting against the base of a tree with her knees pulled up, her head back, and her eyes open, was Iris.

She looked terrible. She'd lost weight fast enough that it changed the shape of her face. Her once-tan skin was pale and bruised, and what seemed to be dried blood was splattered in areas. There were leaves tangled through her hair and caught in the hood of her jacket. Her jeans were wet from the knees down, stained with dirt. Even from several feet away I could see her trembling.

But her eyes were tracking.

When Summer's flashlight found her face, she lifted one arm sharply against the light with a sudden animal recoil and made a small choking sound before recognizing us.

"Oh," Iris whispered.

"Hey." My voice sounded strange in the clearing, smaller than I expected. I went to her and crouched down.

Iris stared at me for several seconds. Then her expression crumpled and she lunged forward hard enough that I almost lost balance, grabbing fistfuls of my suit jacket with both hands.

"Oh my God," she whispered. "Oh my God, don't let me fall asleep."

Her fingers were freezing.

Cecile sat down beside her almost immediately and put a hand against her shoulder.

"We're here," Cecile said softly. "You're okay."

At that, Iris turned and looked at Cecile, and for one terrible second I genuinely thought she might attack her. Something ugly and fearful crossed her features, and her breathing accelerated violently. Then Iris started crying. Deep, humiliating sobs that seemed to physically tear themselves out of her chest.

"I didn't mean to do it," she said. "I didn't mean to, I didn't mean to, I didn't mean to–"

"I know," Cecile said immediately, too quickly. Even while saying it, she kept glancing nervously at Iris's hands.

Iris's crying stopped abruptly. She looked down at her own hands resting in her lap.

"You think I did it," she whispered, slowly raising her head. "You do."

"No one said that," I replied immediately.

"But you think so." Her voice had flattened strangely, emotion draining out of it sentence by sentence. "You think I killed Malcolm."

Cecile inhaled sharply.

Summer still hadn't approached us fully. She stood several feet back near the edge of the clearing with the flashlight aimed outward toward the woods. The beam moved slowly between the trees in controlled sweeps.

"I can't tell what's real anymore," Iris continued. "How long have I been out here?"

"We don't know," Summer said. "Five days, maybe."

"Some of it I remember. Not in order. I was somewhere with people and I walked a long way. I know I ended up here and couldn't go further." She stopped. "I don't know what happened. I don't know if I—"

"We don't know either," Cecile said.

"It followed us," Iris said. "Followed me. Out of the facility. The night we went down there — whatever was in that chapel had been waiting and it attached and I don't–" She pressed her hands flat on the ground. "I don't know how much longer I can take it." She looked at the dark between the trees.

"Summer?" I said.

She didn't answer immediately. Finally, without looking away from the woods: "We need to leave."

Cecile looked up sharply. "What?"

"Now. We can talk about the rest of this when we're not out here."

Cecile and I got Iris to her feet between us. She could stand, barely, most of her weight sagging unevenly against our shoulders.

We had just started turning back toward the trail when the sound came from somewhere deeper south in the woods. A voice is the closest comparison I can make, though even that feels inaccurate. I remember thinking it sounded like several people trying to talk quietly at the same time from far away.

Before any of us could react, Iris started violently trying to pull away from us.

"No no no no no," she whispered rapidly. "It found us it found us it found us–"

Cecile grabbed her arm. "Iris–"

"Go," Summer interrupted. She was facing south, flashlight pointed at the tree line. "You need to go."

"We're not leaving you," I said.

"I need sixty seconds. Go."

I had Iris on my left and Cecile on my right. The three of us started back up the trail fast enough that branches whipped against us as we passed. Every few seconds Iris would jerk toward sounds none of us could hear, nearly dragging me off balance. Behind us, I could hear Summer moving through the woods.

Until I couldn't.

Cecile and Iris were still with me and Summer was not.

"Summer?" I yelled, turning around, flashlight raised.

The beam caught her maybe ten feet back on the trail. She was down on one knee with one hand braced against the dirt and the other gripping her leg.

"Keep going," she said. "Don't stop moving."

"Summer–"

"It's my leg. I can walk. Keep moving and I'll be right behind you."

"We're not–"

"Kolby." She looked up at me. "I need you to get Iris out of here. That is the only thing that matters right now. Get her out."

I looked at her. Then at Iris beside me, shaking so badly she could barely stay upright. Then at the trees beyond Summer, where the flashlight only reached a short distance before the dark swallowed it completely.

I made the wrong choice. I know it was wrong. I did it anyway.

"Stay with Summer," I told Cecile.

"Kolby, don't–"

I took Iris and I ran.

The north trail climbed uphill sharply and Iris could barely keep pace. Half the time we were stumbling more than running, the flashlight beam bouncing wildly across roots and rocks and tree trunks. The entire time Iris kept up a rapid whispering under her breath between gasping breaths. At one point I became convinced I could hear footsteps matching ours somewhere deeper in the trees to the right of us. I never turned the flashlight enough to confirm it because some part of me had already decided I did not want confirmation.

Then my feet went out from under me.

I don't know exactly how it happened: a root, a dip in the trail, Iris's shoulder suddenly against mine at the wrong angle. The edge of the trail had given way to a steep embankment hidden beneath leaves and undergrowth. We hit it sideways and went rolling down. My flashlight tore free from my hand and disappeared ahead of us, the beam spinning wildly between the trees before vanishing altogether. Something struck the side of my head hard enough that a burst of white exploded across my vision.

The world tilted and tilted and then stopped.

I lay on my back staring upward at a canopy I couldn't properly see, my vision swimming badly enough that the trees seemed to bend and shift whenever I tried to focus on them. When I finally rolled onto one side and pushed myself upright, blood and dirt spilled from my mouth together. Every movement sent another wave of dizziness through my stomach.

From somewhere ahead of me, I heard footsteps. The sound grew louder as a figure emerged from the darkness and stopped several feet away. I spotted my flashlight lying half-buried in leaves and crawled toward it.

The beam flickered once before coming back to life, and I raised it to see Iris standing in front of me.

Her eyes caught the light, reflected it back, stopped, then did it again, as if they were toggling on and off. Her cross necklace had come out from under her shirt. It hung against her jacket, catching the beam.

Just as I tried to push myself up, she began to speak. The first words sounded vaguely familiar without being understandable. I recognized enough of it to suspect Latin, though I knew almost none of it beyond what I'd briefly heard her speak before.

But the language changed.

She was speaking in full sentences in what seemed like a real language, but kept pausing. The words became harsher and less recognizable. Certain sounds stretched unnaturally. Others seemed to stop midway through. The longer she spoke, the less human it sounded. Listening to Iris then, I understood exactly what Cecile had meant months before. 

I spent several seconds trying to understand what I was hearing before realizing: she was speaking it backwards.

"Iris." I pushed myself higher despite the pain.

She continued speaking, staring directly at me, and without warning her hand shot upward to grab her necklace. She yanked downward with enough force to snap it immediately. I could see where the chain had bitten into her neck as it broke, a red line in the flashlight.

"Iris, I need you to–"

The sound that came out of her mouth wasn't something I'd ever heard another person make.

The pitch of her scream, if you could call it one, shifted unnaturally as it continued, rising and dropping in ways that made it impossible to place.

Iris’ scream finally broke apart as she staggered.

She stood there, her eyes looking at the necklace still clenched in her hand. When she spoke again, her voice was fragmented and barely audible.

"It's all I–" She stopped. "It's all I can feel inside of me."

Iris turned the necklace over. The broken end of the chain hung from her fingers. Then she pressed the point of the cross against her upper arm, just below the sleeve of her jacket. She gave a smile.

That's when she stabbed her arm.

The movement was abrupt and violent enough that I flinched. But before I could react further, she did it again.

Her motions weren't controlled. It looked less like somebody trying to hurt themselves and more like somebody frantically searching for something hidden underneath their own skin. The cross rose and fell repeatedly: one strike tore through the fabric of her shirt, another caught bare skin, another dragged downward and left a crooked red line behind.

"Iris, stop!" I shouted, lurching forward.

Her eyes found mine, and there was an unmistakable recognition in them. Her expression was her own — her face was her own — and it was horrified.

"It's not me." The words came out in a whisper as she frantically shook her head.

She turned and ran.

I shoved myself to my feet. The movement sent a wave of dizziness through my head strong enough that I nearly fell again, but I forced myself after her anyway.

"Iris!"

My flashlight beam swung wildly between the trees. I kept running, the woods stretching endlessly ahead of me. Every shadow became a possible figure. Every movement of leaves looked like somebody disappearing around another tree. I shouted her name until I could barely speak.

When I reached the place where she had disappeared, I found nothing. I had no idea where I even was.

Eventually, all I could think to do was find the gate, so I did.

It finally appeared ahead through the dark and relief hit me so hard it almost made me dizzy. The open space beyond the woods felt impossibly huge after the claustrophobia of the trail. As I stumbled into the fire road, I bent over with my hands against my knees, trying to catch my breath.

I waited at the gate for seven minutes before Cecile came through alone.

"Summer's behind me," she said. "She's slow but she's moving. She said go uphill and wait."

"What happened?" I pleaded.

"I'll tell you later."

"Cecile–"

Her expression changed abruptly as she froze: "Where is Iris?"

"I don't know." My voice cracked. I pointed uselessly toward the trees. "We fell down some embankment. I hit my head. She started saying things. She–"

The memory of it made my stomach turn.

"She's back in there," I muttered. "We have to go back in."

I went back toward the gate and Summer caught my arm. She had come through while I wasn't watching, moving quietly despite the limp, and she caught me before I got back into the trees.

"Don't," she said.

"She's–" I tried.

"What I saw in the trees. What was there." Summer paused. "It was not something that was going to let you follow her. If you’d gone after her, you wouldn’t have come back out. Either because of whatever’s inside her, or because of it.” She held my arm. "I need you to listen to me."

I listened. I did not go back through the gate.

Summer had a broken fibula. She'd caught her foot on a root in the dark and gone down, and what she'd seen in the trees while she was on the ground I have never been able to get the full account of. She's told me pieces over the years, different pieces at different times, and I've never pushed for the whole.

Cecile had separated from Summer during our exit. What happened in that time, she has not told me and I have not asked. She walked to the car when we got to the road, got in the back seat, sat there until Summer got in beside her, and then the three of us drove back to town in silence. We went to the hospital and said nothing to anyone in the waiting room about where we had been or what we had seen.

Iris was found at 7:41 the following morning, half a mile south of where we'd found her. The jacket she had been wearing in the clearing hung loose on her frame. The scrapes on her hands were deeper, packed with dirt. The leaves tangled in her hair had dried and become brittle overnight, and new bruises and open scratches marked her skin in places that hadn't been there before.

A short distance west of the site, search teams later recovered the remains of four or five additional people. Based on what remained of their clothing, they were believed to have been members of Saint Germaine's Church. None could be identified.

The note in the coroner's file about Iris's body having been moved was accurate — she had not died where she was found. Where she had died, and who had been with her if anyone, was not something the investigation pursued with much energy, because by the time she was found the case was already, as far as the sheriff's department was concerned, resolved. The church members and Malcolm's deaths were attached to her name, hers to the woods, and the whole thing was filed away.

The forest and fire road access was taped off for three weeks during the investigation. I never found out what Malcolm had found on the path.

Fifteen years is enough time for a town to start pretending something never happened.

Bellhaven still exists. People still live there and kids still go to Bellhaven High. If you drove through town today without knowing anything about what happened there, you would probably think it was just another place people pass through on their way somewhere else.

Saint Germaine's Church burned down in March of 2015. Officially the cause was an electrical fire that started in the basement sometime after midnight. Most of the structure had already collapsed inward by the time firefighters arrived. Nobody was injured because attendance had been dwindling for years and the building was usually empty overnight. There were rumors afterward that the bells inside the church had continued ringing after the fire started, and that a firefighter heard singing from somewhere inside while part of the roof was collapsing. None of that was ever substantiated officially, but it became attached to the larger mythology of Bellhaven almost immediately. What I can tell you is that Sheriff Dale Pruett was reported missing by his wife approximately four days after the fire. The case was investigated and then quietly closed. His disappearance was officially ruled unresolved and, as far as I know, remains so.

After the investigation ended, the surviving members of our group scattered. We stopped calling each other. Then months passed. Then years.

My assault charge took eight months to process, and by the time it resolved into a suspended sentence I was already halfway through packing up the house on Caldwell Street. My dad didn't fight me on leaving. I think he understood that staying wasn't something I was capable of anymore, and that the legal situation had made the decision for both of us in a way that let us avoid having to talk about the real reasons.

Cecile left Bellhaven shortly after me — sometime in late 2011, maybe early 2012. We spoke twice after the investigation ended. The second conversation lasted less than ten minutes. She told me she was changing her number and asked me not to contact her again unless it was an emergency.

Summer went in the opposite direction entirely. They spent years trying to uncover connections between Saint Germaine's and other organizations around the state — there actually were records connecting the church financially to rehabilitation groups and counseling programs across the county. There were periods where Summer disappeared completely for months and then resurfaced acting like no time had passed. The last message I received from them was in 2021: a photograph attached to an otherwise empty email. The image showed the woods behind Bellhaven Facility at night from inside a parked car. The headlights illuminated the trees enough to reveal somebody standing between them. The quality was terrible. It could have been anyone standing there.

I never answered the email.

As for myself, every October, without fail, I still receive messages. Most come from empty accounts — Bible verses, screenshots from archived paranormal forums, blurry photographs of the woods behind Route 9. Some are obviously sent by people who recognized my name from old records tied to the case.

A few were harder to explain. About six years ago I received a message from an account using Iris's full name. It contained one sentence: *you never told them about the footprints.*

I never mentioned that night publicly. I never included it in any statement. The only people who knew Iris appeared inside my house that evening were me and Iris herself. I spent almost a week afterward going back through old messages trying to convince myself I had described it somewhere years earlier and forgotten. I never found anything.

People have asked me over the years why I never came forward earlier if I knew the public version of events was incomplete. I used to tell myself it was because nobody would believe me. You tell somebody you think your dead friend may have been possessed by something living underneath an abandoned psychiatric facility and they stop listening to the rest of the sentence.

The bigger reason was the fear that talking about Bellhaven mattered somehow. I know how insane that sounds written out plainly like that. I've spent enough years in therapy hearing about trauma responses and survivor's guilt. I understand what a rational explanation for all of this looks like.

Sometimes I honestly believe what happened in Bellhaven was entirely human: a collapsing town that was home to an increasingly radical church, a group of emotionally unstable teenagers feeding fear into each other.

Then I remember waking up in the middle of the night and finding her standing in my hallway, looking toward my bedroom like somebody else was inside the house with us. I remember Malcolm calling me hours before he died and asking whether I had ever gotten the feeling Bellhaven was waiting for something.

Maybe there are explanations for all of this that I failed to see because I was seventeen years old and terrified. Or maybe something followed us out of Bellhaven Facility and is alive to this day.

I don't know which possibility I believe more.
