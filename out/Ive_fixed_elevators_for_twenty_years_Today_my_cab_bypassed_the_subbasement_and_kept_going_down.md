I work as an elevator mechanic, and for sure, I am not someone who believes in the supernatural. I am a rational person. But rationality cannot explain the blood on my hands, and it cannot explain what is waiting at the bottom of the city.

To make you understand, I have to take you back twenty years. I was fifteen years old. It was my first real job. I worked as a bellhop at a massive, historic hotel downtown. The building was old, constructed in the early twenties. It had brass fixtures, red velvet carpets, and a maze of back hallways that the guests never saw. My job was simple. I carried luggage, delivered room service, and ran errands for the front desk.

I hated the job. The uniform was incredibly itchy, the hours were long, and my manager was strict. I spent most of my shifts hiding in the sub-basement near the loading dock, trying to kill time.

That was where I met the dog.

It was a stray, a small terrier mix, mostly black with patches of matted brown fur. It was incredibly skinny. Its ribs showed through its coat, and its yellow eyes always looked terrified. It kept sneaking into the loading dock area, looking for scraps near the kitchen dumpsters.

My manager hated the dog. He constantly ordered me to chase it away. Every time I saw it, I would yell, wave a broom, and run it out into the alley. But the dog was stubborn. It always came back. After a few weeks, I stopped feeling bad for it. I just felt annoyed. The dog became a nuisance, another irritating part of a job I already despised.

One Friday evening, the weather was horrible. It was pouring rain, and the temperature had dropped near freezing. I was taking a break in the sub-basement when I saw the dog again. It had slipped through the propped-open dock doors to escape the rain. It was shivering, standing near the abandoned service elevator.

That service elevator was ancient. It was a massive steel box used for hauling furniture, but it had been out of commission for months. The cab was parked a few floors up, leaving the shaft doors in the basement closed but easily opened with a simple drop key.

I looked at the dog, then at the shaft doors. A cruel, stupid idea formed in my head. I decided I was going to teach the dog a lesson. I wanted to scare it so badly that it would never come back to the hotel again.

I walked over to the doors, used my drop key, turned the latch, and pulled the heavy metal doors apart. The shaft was pitch black. The concrete floor of the elevator pit was just a few feet down.

I grabbed a piece of stale bread from a discarded room service tray nearby, and tossed the bread into the dark shaft.

The dog was hungry. It did not hesitate. It walked forward, sniffing the ground, and hopped down into the shallow pit to get the bread.

As soon as it was inside, I pushed the metal doors shut. The latch clicked into place. The dog was trapped in the pitch-black shaft. I could hear it whining, scratching at the concrete walls. I laughed. I told myself I would leave it in there for an hour, just to scare it, and then I would let it out before my shift ended.

But the hotel got incredibly busy. A massive wedding party arrived, and I was assigned to run luggage to the top floors for the next four hours. By the time my shift ended, I was exhausted. I clocked out, changed my clothes, and walked out into the rain. I went home and went to sleep.

I completely forgot about the dog.

The next morning, I woke up with a severe fever. I had caught the flu. I called the hotel and told them I could not come in. I stayed in bed for six days. For that entire week, the dog never crossed my mind once. I was just focused on sleeping and getting better.

I returned to work the following Thursday. I put on my uniform and went down to the loading dock to clock in.

There was a horrible smell in the sub-basement.

I saw an older maintenance worker standing near the old service elevator. The shaft doors were open. He had a flashlight and a trash bag, and he looked sick.

"What happened?"

 I asked him.

He shook his head.

 "Some stray dog got trapped in the pit. Must have slipped in when the doors were open. It starved to death down there. The poor thing tore its paws up trying to dig through the concrete."

The realization bitted my mind painfully. The memory of the dog hopping into the dark, the sound of the doors clicking shut, the whining. I did that. I locked it in the dark. I left it there to starve. The guilt was suffocating. I went to the bathroom and threw up. I never told anyone what I did, and quit the job a week later.

I grew up, learned a trade, and  became an elevator mechanic. I spent the next two decades trying to be a good person, trying to forget the cruel thing I did when I was a teenager. I convinced myself it was a terrible mistake, a lapse in judgment. But the guilt never fully went away. It stayed quietly in the back of my mind.

That brings me to yesterday.

I work for a large commercial repair company. We handle maintenance for the major skyscrapers downtown. My dispatcher radioed me early in the morning. He told me to head to a massive, eighty-story commercial tower in the center of the city. The building had a dedicated freight elevator that serviced the lower basement levels, and it was completely entirely stalled.

I drove my work truck to the tower, signed in at the front security desk and took the service stairs down to the third parking level, where the local building engineer was waiting for me.

"Good morning," 

I said, setting my heavy tool bag on the concrete floor.

"Glad you are here,"

 the engineer replied. He pointed to a set of massive, scuffed steel doors.

 "It is the main freight unit. It is acting up again. This is the second time this month."

"What is the exact issue?"

 I asked.

"It stalls out," 

he explained.

 "Usually between the lobby and the lowest parking level. It just stops dead. The doors stay shut, and the motor just whines. We locked it out of service yesterday so nobody gets trapped inside."

"Alright,"

 I said, pulling my work gloves from my back pocket. 

"I will get the car moving, ride it down to the bottom of the hoistway, and check the door tracks. Might be debris caught in the sill, or a faulty relay in the control board."

"Take your time,"

 the engineer said, turning to walk back to his office. 

"We just need it running by the weekend."

I used my drop key to open the outer doors. The cab was sitting right at the floor level. I stepped inside. The interior was massive, designed to hold pallets of construction material. The walls were bare steel, scratched and dented. The fluorescent lights overhead buzzed with a low, annoying hum.

I pushed the doors shut, opened the service panel on the wall and flipped the switch to manual inspection mode. I pressed the button for the lowest sub-basement.

The elevator lurched, The cables groaned above me, and The car began its descent.

It moved slowly. I watched the floor indicators light up on the digital display. Lobby. Parking Level One. Parking Level Two.

Then, the lights cut out completely.

The cab went pitch black. The buzzing of the fluorescent tubes stopped instantly. The digital display went dark. Total silence filled the steel box. I heard the loud, mechanical clank of the brake solenoid tripping.

Then, the floor dropped out from under me.

The sensation was immediate and terrifying. The cab was in a total freefall.

Normally, an elevator dropping is mathematically and mechanically nearly impossible. There are multiple fail-safes designed to prevent exactly this. There are thick steel hoist ropes holding the car. If they snap, there is a mechanical governor. The governor detects over-speed. If the car moves too fast, the governor trips a set of steel jaws that clamp directly onto the guide rails. It stops the car immediately. Even if the governor fails, there are massive hydraulic oil buffers at the bottom of the pit designed to absorb a crash.

None of those systems engaged.

I was falling. The speed was incredible. The wind rushed past the outside of the metal cab, roaring through the shaft vents. I felt weightless. My stomach pushed up into my chest. My boots barely maintained contact with the floor.

I grabbed the metal handrail bolted to the wall, then held on with everything I had, and waited for the governor to catch. I waited for the sudden, violent jerk of the safety brakes. Nothing happened. The rushing wind grew louder.

I fell for what felt like minutes. A skyscraper has a long shaft, but I should have hit the buffer springs at the bottom within seconds. The duration of the fall made absolutely no sense. The shaft could not possibly be this deep.

I realized I was going to die. The speed was terminal. I curled my body, bent my knees, and covered my head. I closed my eyes tightly, and braced for the final, bone-crushing impact.

But the impact never came.

Instead of a violent crash, the downward movement simply stopped. There was no crunching metal. There was no shattering force. The cab just ceased moving, as smoothly as if it had landed on a thick cushion of air.

I stayed curled on the floor for a long time. My heart hammered against my ribs. My breathing was ragged and shallow. I was completely unhurt.

I opened my eyes. The cab was still completely dark, and The power was still out.

I reached into my pocket and pulled out my phone. I turned on the flashlight application. The bright beam cut through the darkness. The steel walls of the cab were perfectly intact. My tool bag rested on the floor exactly where I had placed it.

I stood up slowly. My legs were shaking, but I walked over to the doors. I gripped the inner edges of the metal panels and pulled them apart. They slid open with a metallic scrape.

I expected to see the concrete walls of the elevator pit. I expected to see the greasy buffer springs, the dirt, and the damp foundation of the skyscraper.

I did not see concrete.

I shined my flashlight out of the cab. The beam stretched forward into a massive, cavernous space. The ceiling was entirely lost in darkness. Giant, irregular stone pillars rose from the ground to support the unseen roof above. The floor was not paved. It was made of dry, packed dirt.

The temperature was freezing, and it smelled like ancient dust, dried earth.

I stepped out of the elevator cab. My boots crunched softly on the dirt floor. I moved the flashlight beam slowly from left to right.

The cavern was filled with skeletal remains.

They were piled against the stone pillars and scattered across the floor. I walked closer to the nearest pile. I saw human skulls. I saw ribcages, femurs, and shattered pelvis bones. They looked incredibly old, bleached white and covered in a thick layer of gray dust. Mixed among the human remains were animal bones. Huge, unidentifiable skeletons lay twisted in the dirt.

I realized I had fallen far past the lowest basement of the building, and maybe I had descended into an unmapped, subterranean level entirely separated from the city above.

The silence was absolute. It was the quietest place I had ever been.

Then, I heard a sound.

It was a low scrape. Like thick claws dragging across stone.

I froze, then slowly turned my flashlight toward the source of the noise.

The beam swept across the dirt floor. About thirty feet away, I saw a small object lying alone in the dust. I walked cautiously toward it, keeping my light focused on the area.

I stopped a few feet away, then aimed the light directly at the object.

It was a dog collar.

I knelt down slowly. I did not touch it, but I leaned in close. The collar was made of red nylon. The color was severely faded, turning brown from age and dust. The metal buckle was completely rusted and broken in half. But the metal D-ring, where a leash would normally attach, was still secured to the nylon. It was held in place by a piece of twisted copper wire.

I stopped breathing. My chest tightened until it hurt.

I knew this collar. I knew the specific way that wire was twisted. I had done it myself. Twenty years ago, when the stray dog first started showing up at the hotel, it had this broken collar. One afternoon, before I decided to hate the animal, I used a pair of pliers and a scrap of copper wire to reattach the D-ring.

It was the exact same collar.

A deep, vibrating growl echoed through the cavern.

I snapped my flashlight up.

A massive shadow detached itself from the darkness behind one of the stone pillars.

It was shaped like a hound, but it was the size of a commercial delivery van. Its body looked like solid, shifting shadows. The light from my phone seemed to sink right into its fur, illuminating nothing.

The thing possessed three distinct heads. Each head was enormous, perched on thick, muscular necks. The jaws were slightly open, revealing rows of impossibly long, sharp teeth. The eyes were completely black, like deep pits, but they caught the reflection of my light, glowing with an awful, intelligent hatred.

The beast stepped fully into the light. It stared directly at me. It knew who I was, and what I had done.

The creature lunged forward.

I dropped the phone. I spun around and sprinted back toward the elevator cab. Adrenaline flooded my system. The dirt floor slipped beneath my boots, but I kept my balance. I heard the massive claws tearing into the earth behind me. The beast was incredibly fast.

I reached the open doors of the cab and dove inside. I landed hard on my stomach, scrambling backward until my back hit the rear steel wall. I reached up and frantically pressed the door close button on the control panel.

The buttons were dead. The power was still out, and the doors remained wide open.

The beast reached the threshold. It was too massive to fit its entire body inside the cab. Instead, it shoved its three massive heads through the open doors. The metal doorframes groaned and bent inward under the sheer force of the creature pressing against them. The jaws snapped wildly, missing my legs by inches. The smell of sulfur and old blood filled the small space.

I realized I could not outrun it. I could not hide in the cab. It was going to rip the metal apart and drag me out.

I looked up. Every elevator cab has an emergency escape hatch built into the ceiling. It is designed for rescue operations.

I grabbed my tool bag from the floor. I stood up, ignoring the snapping jaws just feet away. I climbed onto the metal handrail mounted to the side wall. I reached up, pressed the release latches on the ceiling panel, and shoved the heavy metal hatch up and out of the way.

I pulled myself through the opening. I dragged my tool bag up behind me. I was now standing on the flat metal roof of the elevator cab, in the dark shaft. 

I looked down through the hatch. The beast was furiously tearing at the inside of the cab. Its massive claws ripped through the steel wall panels like paper. It was going to completely dismantle the cab. 

I opened my canvas tool bag, reached past the wrenches and screwdrivers, then  grabbed my portable acetylene torch.

I need to explain exactly how this specific type of traction elevator operates. The cab is suspended by thick steel hoist ropes. These ropes travel up the shaft, over a massive motorized pulley wheel at the very top of the building, and attach to a giant iron counterweight on the other side. The counterweight moves in the opposite direction of the cab. When the cab goes down, the counterweight goes up. The counterweight is designed to be much heavier than an empty cab.

I knew that in this older system, the hoist ropes connected directly to a tension bracket right next to the crosshead on the roof of the cab.

If I cut the main tension bracket, the hoist ropes would instantly detach from the cab. The massive iron counterweight, hanging high up in the shaft and no longer restricted by the weight of the cab, would plummet downward. Because of the way the secondary emergency lines were routed on this specific ancient model, the falling counterweight would violently yank the elevator cab straight up the shaft.

It was a dangerous, desperate plan. But it was my only option.

I turned the small gas valve on the side of the torch. I grabbed the metal striker and sparked the flint. A thin, incredibly hot blue flame hissed to life in the darkness.

I crawled to the edge of the cab roof. I located the thick steel tension bracket that anchored the main hoist ropes to the crosshead.

I heard a terrifying sound from directly beneath me. The beast had forced its way fully inside the cab. The entire elevator shook violently as it tore through the interior wall panels, realizing I was directly above it. I looked down through the open escape hatch and saw those three heads snapping in the dark.

I applied the blue flame directly to the steel tension bracket. The metal instantly began to heat up. The industrial grease coating the bracket caught fire, sending small orange flames flickering into the dark shaft. The torch hissed loudly. I watched the steel turn from gray to dull red, then to bright, glowing orange.

The beast lunged upward from inside the cab. It rammed its heads through the small escape hatch. The metal ceiling buckled around the opening as it tried to squeeze its immense bulk through the narrow gap. The black eyes locked onto me from the hole in the floor. It opened its jaws and let out a deafening roar, thrusting a huge paw up through the hatch, its claws digging into the roof right next to my boots.

I kept the flame steady on the bracket. The metal was melting, losing its integrity under the intense heat.

The beast surged upward again, trying to widen the hatch with its sheer size. It swiped blindly across the roof. One of its claws swept across the space and raked deeply across my right hand.

The pain was agonizing. The sharp claw sliced through my leather work glove, tearing deep into my skin and muscle. I screamed and dropped the acetylene torch. The torch clattered across the metal roof and fell through the open hatch, vanishing into the cab below.

The beast pushed harder, its jaws gnashing just inches from my face as the ceiling of the cab began to tear open.

Then, the glowing steel bracket finally failed.

With a loud, metallic crack, the tension snapped.

The main hoist ropes detached. High above in the shaft, the iron counterweight began to freefall.

The sudden, catastrophic loss of tension caused the secondary emergency cables to pull taut. The falling counterweight yanked the elevator cab violently upward.

The sudden acceleration was terrifying. The cab rocketed up the shaft with incredible speed. I was thrown backward, slamming hard against the metal crosshead. I grabbed a support beam with my uninjured hand and held on tight.

The beast was caught completely off guard. Half of its mass was shoved up through the jagged hole in the roof, while its lower body was still crammed inside the cab.

The cab sped upward, leaving the cavernous underground space, Shooting directly back into the narrow, concrete-lined section of the regular elevator shaft.

The moment we passed the boundary of that deep cavern, the beast stopped moving.

The creature just froze. Its three heads went entirely silent, the deafening roar cutting off in an instant.

Right before my eyes, the terrifying mass of the entity began to dissolve. The dark fur, the massive teeth, and the glowing eyes crumbled into a fine, gray ash. It turned completely to dust, blowing apart in the rushing wind of the ascending cab.

A cloud of dry dirt swept across the metal roof, coating my face and clothes. The creature's grip on the hatch dissolved entirely, and what remained of its shifting form simply vanished, scattering down into the empty cab as we shot upward.

I was alone on the roof.

But the cab was still rocketing upward. The counterweight was still falling. The speed was increasing. I looked up into the darkness. I knew the top of the shaft was approaching. We were going to slam directly into the concrete ceiling of the machine room at a terrifying velocity.

There was nothing left to do. I pulled my knees to my chest. I curled into a tight ball on the metal roof. I closed my eyes tightly. I braced my body, waiting for the final, devastating crash that would end my life.

I waited.

The rushing wind stopped.

There was no impact.

There was no sound of tearing metal. There was no pain.

Total silence returned.

I kept my eyes closed for a long time. I was terrified to open them. I slowly uncurled my body, reached out with my hand, and felt smooth, flat flooring beneath me.

I opened my eyes.

I was lying on the floor inside the cab.

The fluorescent lights overhead were humming normally. The digital floor indicator display above the door was glowing brightly. The steel walls around me were perfectly intact, completely devoid of the massive claw marks the beast had ripped into them. My canvas tool bag was sitting neatly in the corner, exactly where I had placed it when I first stepped in.

I sat up slowly. My mind raced. I was completely disoriented. I looked around the pristine cab. The doors were closed. There was no sign of a struggle. There was no dIRT on my clothes.

My two-way radio, clipped to my belt, suddenly crackled with static.

"Hey," 

the voice of the building engineer came through the speaker. 

"Are you down there? The power just flickered across the whole building. Is the freight car stuck?"

I stared at the radio. I could not speak.

My brain desperately tried to rationalize what had happened. I tried to convince myself that the power failure had caused a severe panic attack. I tried to believe that I had passed out on the floor of the cab and simply hallucinated the entire ordeal. The fall, the massive cavern, the bones, the three-headed hound, the escape on the roof. It had to be a dream. It had to be a manifestation of my buried guilt, brought to the surface by the darkness of the broken elevator.

I reached for my radio to answer the engineer.

I raised my right hand to press the transmit button.

Then stopped.

I stared at my right hand.

My thick leather work glove was torn open. Beneath the ruined leather, there were four deep, parallel claw marks cut directly into my skin. The flesh was sliced open, exposing the muscle beneath. Fresh blood was pouring from the wounds, dripping steadily onto the clean, scuffed floor of the elevator cab.

The pain was real. It was agonizing.

I am typing this post with my left hand after I treated my right one. The doctors thought I caught my hand in a piece of moving machinery, but I did not correct them. I cannot tell them the truth.

I am posting this because I need answers. I need to know if anyone else in my profession has ever experienced anything like this. Have you ever dropped below the lowest level? Have you ever seen the cavern?

But more than that, I am posting this to finally confess. I killed that dog twenty years ago. I thought my secret was safe in the dark. I thought the past was buried.

But down there, in the unmapped dark beneath the city, nothing is ever truly buried. Something found that collar. Something kept it. Something waited two decades for me to step into the wrong elevator.
