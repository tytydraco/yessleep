Ghost stories are not something I heard much growing up. Not because I was raised in a heavily religious family or anything. The only ghost story that I heard was around the campfire about the poor little girl that drowned in a lake and now haunts it when it storms. It wasn’t a very scary story, and I read far worse on the internet while using the school computers.

Growing up in the south was dull. The summers were miserable and the winters were always soggy. It only ever snowed around January and then it would melt and turn into black ice. Being a summer baby, I had many birthday parties at the lake or pool. It made sense, but I soon got tired of it around 12, I thought it was boring at that point. The one place we always went to for my birthday parties was the nearby state park, a man made lake with hiking trails, a small camp ground, a fishing area and too many snakes. It was quaint, I almost drowned there when I was like six, but it was one of my favorite childhood places. 

I recently decided to pick up hiking again. Life had become a drag and I needed to do something other than bed rot and play video games. The problem was that the only place even remotely close by was the state park, the other closest one being about an hour or so away. So I settled on the man made park, packed a bag with the essentials, and headed to South Bend State Park.

I don't usually like going places by myself. Social anxiety is a huge thing I'm still having to fight with every step of the way in any social interaction. But, none of my friends were off that day and my family were going to a softball tournament, so I had to go by myself. It was fine, I told myself, I needed to go places alone more to get out of my shell. 

I arrived late in the afternoon, as I didn’t want to be out during the hottest hours of the day. I parked in the main area, grabbed my bag, and headed up to the hiking trail map. I was going to pick an easy trail, something quick, but I wanted to push myself today. I needed the exercise anyway. I noticed a park ranger by the resource center who gave me a friendly smile and a wave. I shyly dipped my head and looked back at the map.

I chose the south loop, 5.7 miles of woods, birds, and chatty squirrels that definitely judged how much I huffed and puffed. Luckily, there was no one else on the trail to see me struggling along the two mile mark. I should have gone on the cabin trail. At least I was under the trees, the leaves glowing from the harsh sunlight shining through them. As I continued, it felt like something was watching me. I brushed it off that my imagination was running wild and playing tricks on me. I tried to just relax, walk, and enjoy the sounds of nature around me along with the sound of children that screeched from the swimming area across the lake. I could occasionally catch sight of the lake through the trees, the beaming sunlight bouncing off the calm grayish water and refracting right back into my eyes. I grimaced as I tugged my sunglasses down off my head and over my eyes.

I took a breather around the third mile, sweating like a sinner in church as I leaned against a tree and took a few gulps of nice cool water from my flask. It was very quiet around the surrounding woods, the birds and greedy squirrels seemingly not up for a conversation anymore. I had a bad feeling worm its way into my gut. Something was nearby that the animals didn't like. I assumed I was disturbing their peace and took a few more steps, went over the small hill and stopped.

A twisted, pale, gnarly looking tree stood just off to the left of the trail, right along the embankment that went down to the lake. This tree wasn't like the others. It stood out like a sore thumb. It wasn't an oak or cedar tree. It looked like a weeping willow tree, but wrong. So wrong.

It was shorter than the other trees, no leaves on its long finger looking branches, and the weirdly pale bark was spaced apart like scales on an armadillo. The top was broken and charred like it had been struck by lightning. Gross oily streaks running down its bumpy surface. It was like it was something pretending to be a tree and doing a horrible job at it. Just looking at it sent waves of 'hell no' down my spine. I stepped closer to it, even though the rational part of my brain said run. 

I ever so slowly touched the scale-like bark with my fingertips and immediately pulled my hand away. The bark was cold, ice cold. I knew that shouldn’t have been possible, the tree I was leaning on moments ago was warm and it was damn near ninety degrees. 

I attempted to get a closer look, the bark almost looked like it was moving. 

A sharp crack came from behind. 

I spun around, thinking a monster or crazy masked person was about to get me. 

A fat squirrel scampered away and hauled itself up a nearby tree with a chippy bark. I sighed and mentally scolded myself for being so jumpy. I really needed to stop watching so many horror movies.

When I turned to look at the pale tree again, it was gone. 

I blinked, confused, scared. I felt like I hallucinated the tree. Did I suffer from a heat stroke and make the tree up? I swore it was just right there in front of me. I felt the dirt under my shoe shift, so I looked down and stepped back with a choked noise.

There was a hole where the thing was, the dirt torn up like something wriggled free from the soil and skittered away. The woods went dead silent. Not a chirp, bark, or caw. Not even the cicadas felt up to singing their song. 

And after I looked away from the hole, my mind spun and sweat slipping down my neck, I heard a noise off to my right. At first, I swore it was a little girl crying, but when I listened closer, I realized what the noises were. 

Creaking. Snapping, like something big was moving around the woods and towards me. I didn't decide to stick around to find out what was making that noise. I booked it as fast as I could down the trail, breathing like a race horse when I nearly tripped over a root. I heard it chasing after me, the skittering noise of legs crushing brush made a shiver of terror race down my spine. I knew it was a bad idea to rest for a second, but my legs shook like jelly and I didn’t hear it anymore. 

When I paused to take deep, gasping breaths, I heard it again.

Snap. Crack. Creak. Creak creak. 

It was coming from behind, and it sounded like it was quickly getting closer. 

Creak, creak CREAK.

I quickly turned just in time to see its pale, tendril-like branches come to a halt around 6 feet away from me. My heart drummed so hard in my chest that I thought I was about to have a heart attack. 

The pale tree trunk was uprooted and stood on its roots like a spider, its bark shifted like it was itching to get me. And under that moving bark, I saw glistening eyes. Dozens of dark, angry eyes locked onto me. I nearly sobbed in terror seeing it behave like a weeping angel. I hate weeping angel creatures in video games, it always made me feel a primal sort of fear. Slowly being hunted by something that only moves when you're not looking at it terrified me. 

And now there was one right in front of me. I don't know how long I stood there, gasping for air and staring at the pale tree like it would go away if I mentally screamed at it to leave me alone. It didn't. It just kept watching me, waiting for me to look away so it could snatch me up. 

I eventually shifted back a step. My hands shook as I reached for my phone in my back pocket. I unlocked it, hesitated and then snapped a quick picture. I expected the thing to screech and come at me, but it stood where it stopped. 

I put my phone away and slipped the small pocket knife out of the side pocket of my bag. I brought it for self defense, and this felt like a damn good time to have it in my hand. I didn't want to turn my back on this thing. I didn’t know how quick it was, or what exactly it would do when it caught me, but I knew one thing for certain. I wasn't going to die out here. 

I kept taking small steps back, making sure I stayed on the trail as I made some distance between me and the pale tree spider thing. I only had two miles left. I just had to make it to the trail exit. I had the bright idea to take my phone back out, turn the camera on selfie mode, and hold it up enough to keep eyes on the monster through the phone. It didn't move, thank god. I don't know why keeping my gaze on this thing kept it from moving, but I didn't want to ask it, I just kept moving. The further I got from the monster, the more I swore I could hear crying.

When I went down the little hill, I lost sight of the thing. I waited for it to reappear on the phone screen, and when it didn't, I mentally screamed at myself. Why did I think it would function like a dumb monster in a game and keep coming straight at me? I immediately panicked and turned every which way to see if I could catch sight of the pale tree. 

Snap. Crack. Creak Creak Creak. 

There, from the left side. 

I screamed and turned just as twisted branches grazed my cheek. Tears slipped from my eyes while blood trickled from the small cut right below my eye, panicked gasps escaped my throat when I got a good look at the creature's trunk and its shifting bark. 

Its pupils were pinpointed and locked onto me, a low tap tap tap noise came from within its trunk like a million fingers tapping on a wood. I didn't see it before, but now that it was only an arms length away, I could see that it shook. Its bark shifted and clicked, its eyes twitched like it was pissed it didn't catch me. Its branches curled where my arm just was, creaking and tapping growing louder from inside its curled trunk along with the crying. 

"Fuck..." is all I could wheeze out as I stumbled back, vision momentarily blurred with tears. How come no one else had ended up on the trail? Why the hell haven't I seen anyone else in the last couple of hours? I was forced to focus back onto the gnarly tree looming over me when it wept louder. I used my pocket knife to poke at its bark, the clicking reached a crescendo, and swiftly pulled back. Fear kept me from plunging the knife between the bark, the blade probably wasn't long enough to kill it anyway.

The sun was starting to dip lower and lower. I didn't want to be out here when night fell. I couldn't see well in the dark and with this thing here - I just had to get the hell out of here. 

I kept facing the tree this time. I didn’t dare to take my eyes off it as I slowly, carefully walked backwards. My chest ached with each deep, shaking breath I took, the silence from the woods around me didn’t help at all. I could hear the lingering laughter from the lake below, the sun slowly being obscured by grey, muddy clouds. 

Right as I saw the sign stating I was at the end of the trail, I deflated like a balloon. The thing was still here, but at least now I was closer to other people. 

But there wasn't anyone else around me by the trail map. I risked a quick look around, panic clawed at my throat when I saw the parking lot was empty except for my sad looking car that was just as old as me. 

"No....what, no what the hell??"  I whispered to myself, my gaze snapped back to the weeping tree when it creaked closer. 

I took one last look at the tree thing and darted over to my car, my hands damp with sweat as I snatched my keys from my pocket and unlocked the doors. I hopped in, jammed the key into the ignition, and started the car as the weeping tree slammed into the side. I screamed and quickly turned to see its glittering eyes as dark as obsidian glaring back at me through the window. 

My whole body shook worse than the vehicle, hiccuping breaths racked my frame as I slammed my foot on the brake to shift it into drive. I peeled out of the parking lot way too fast, but my terror overwhelmed my rational thoughts. 

Sharp pops shocked me out of my fear driven panic, a jerk of the wheel and a horrible noise making me slam on the breaks. My car skidded to a halt and I saw what looked like huge thorns on the rocky gravel road in the rear view mirror. 

"Damnit-" I couldn't even yell out my frustrations until the tree was in my vision again. It patiently waited and watched. I swore I could make out a twisted smile on his trunk. Its branches shifted in the wind, like it gave me a teasing wave. 

I opened my phone, checked the service and nearly cried again when I saw I had no signal. Even if I could call someone, what the hell would I tell them? They wouldn't believe me, and I wouldn't blame them. 

I used my camera again to keep my eyes on it as I slipped out of my car, cringing when I spotted the flat, shredded tires. I didn't have time to think about how expensive it would be to get new tires. 

I turned to face the pale tree, my gaze swiftly looked over the state park from the hill I now stood on. From here, I could see the camp ground was empty, not another single soul in sight. 

“Oh thank god.” I whispered to myself when I caught a glimpse of a light in the resource center. 

I slowly made a wide circle around the monster, making my way down the gravel road to head back into the park. I could have gone towards the road to wave another car down, I should have, but I needed answers. 

Before I made it all the way to the resource center, I heard the door open and footsteps approach me from behind. I don't even remember collapsing against the poor park ranger, babbling with relief as I grabbed at his shoulders. 

"Thing- something chasing me. My tires-" 

The park ranger, probably around his mid forties and confused with my dramatics, tried to calm me down and put me at arms length. His dark shaggy hair fell past his shoulders, a bushy beard curling around his mouth as he frowned.

"Whoa whoa, easy now. What are you still doing here? The park was closed ‘round two hours ago due to the E. coli outbreak." The man said as he looked at me over, his deep green eyes narrowed with confusion. 

"I...I was on the south trail." I finally got out, voice cracked as I remembered the tree. I spun around, expecting it to be inches away from my face, but it was gone. I scanned the trees around us, mind spinning when I didn't catch a sign of it. I almost didn't notice the park ranger sigh and grab my shoulder to make me face him again. 

"Look, kid, you need to calm down and talk to me." He said as he gave me a look like I had a slice of cheese stapled to my forehead.

I took a second to breathe and dry my tears before I told him everything and showed him the picture I took. He didn't say anything for a few heart beats until he grabbed me by my upper arm and dragged me inside the resource center. 

"Hey- what are you doing? Let me go!" I snapped, my anxiousness flaring up as he simply ignored me and pulled me towards what I assumed was his office. 

'Steve Ridge, Head Park Ranger' is what the metal plaque on the door said as he swung open and shoved me inside. 

"Shut up and listen." He snapped back and slammed the door closed, the lock clicking into place loudly. I fell silent, too scared to object or say anything else. 

"That monster out there, it's not going to stop hunting you." He says as peeks out the window on the far side of the room. "I'm sorry you got wrapped up into this. She- That thing…." he trailed off like the truth hurt him.

I shuffled in place, hands fidgeting as I kept looking at the door. I wanted to ask him about the spike trap on the road, but seeing how he was upset, I kept my dumb questions to myself. Despite my stunned silence, he kept on. 

"It randomly strikes every summer. I never know when exactly it will strike, but it does, and then it disappears back into the lake with its victim." He says as he slips something from the sheath on his belt and hands me the long hunting knife. I held it with both hands like a child before I really took in what the man said.

“Wait, you know this happens every summer and you still let people into the park?” I asked with a baffled tone, but the more I really thought about it, the less pissed I became. What was he supposed to tell people? That a tree monster took people every couple of years with no evidence? Everyone would probably think he was crazy, until I remembered the picture I took of the weeping tree and immediately became upset again. “How come you haven't taken a picture of it or something?” 

Steve grunted and motioned me to get my phone out again, I did and he took it.

“I’ve tried.” he said as he clicked back on the photo of the tree to show me it.

“What?!” I yanked my phone back and swiped through my camera roll before going back to what would be the picture of the pale tree to see just a normal photo of the woods. No spidery limbs or glaring little eyes in sight. “It’s gone? But…how?”

“It’s not a cryptic or a monster.” He said as he grimly opened the tiny filing cabinet next to his desk to pull out a newspaper clipping that looked older than me.

‘Tragic lake incident, little girl drowns during sudden thunder storm.’ 

“It’s my daughter.” 

I couldn’t get my mouth to work with me, my gaze was caught by the shadow of branches in the window behind the older man.

“It’s in the window.” My voice trembled, lungs constricting like I was currently running a marathon. Its branches tapped against the window, my heart thumping along with the taps. 

Steve looked as well, his face drained of color as he stared at the creaking tree outside the window. The look on his face, the grief, the sorrow, I felt it just by the look in his eyes.

“Willow…” he whispered, his eyes glistened with tears as he stepped towards the window. “My baby, I’m so sorry.”

The weeping tree didn’t respond. 

The park ranger opened the window, the humid evening air rushed in like a swarm of wasps. I was frozen in place, scared to move, terrified to even blink when I saw the things eyes shine from the office lights. 

“I shouldn’t have let you swim that day.” 

The tree twitched, eyes blinking like it was thinking, the clicking noise from within its trunk slowly dying off. A soft, weeping noise started coming from the now shaking tree, its branches trembling as Steve sobbed.

*“Dad?"* a soft, echoey voice came from within the tree, a little girl’s voice. *“Why didn’t you save me?”*

“Willow, I…I couldn't. The storm was coming in and-” Steve was cut off by the branches lashing out around his waist and lifting him off the ground.

*“Why didn’t you help me?”* she continued as the branches shook and rattled. Steve tried again, only for her shrill voice to cut him off again.

*“Yet THEY were saved?!?”* the weeping tree screeched as it threw him across the room into the brick wall. I jolted, remembering that I was in fact, part of this. I stepped back as the tree turned to me, my heart sinking to my ass as it climbed through the window and after me, even though I was looking at it.

Oily looking water spilled from the top of the tree, the smell made my eyes water and made me gag. It was worse than the smell of road kill on the hottest day of the year. I didn’t take a second glance at the pale tree. I slammed into the door, forgetting it was locked before I smacked the switch and shoved it open. I could hear the skittering noise of the roots scraping against the polished tile floors, its wailing making my ears ring as I ran as fast as I could out of the resource center. I nearly made it to the parking lot, too scared and in a panic to think about where I was running to, before the roots slammed into my back. I face planted onto the asphalt, pain blazing from my definitely broken nose as blood spurted from my nostrils. Branches whipped around my limbs, yanking me up to the broken top of the trunk where the nasty water was bubbling out. I screamed, squirmed and fought with what energy I had left, but the angry spirit was stronger. 

*“You do not get to live, not after the water almost claimed you.”* the tree whispered as I saw the inside of the hollow trunk. 

Teeth, dozens, maybe hundreds of them that bent back like anaconda teeth spiraled down the inside of the monster. The water sloshed as the branches tried to force me inside, my feet firmly planted on the sides of the hollow trunk as I tried my best to keep it from pushing me in. I attempted to use the hunting knife to cut through the branches holding me, but the weeping tree made me drop it with a harsh yank. 

“Wait, please! Why me?? Why-” I cried as my left foot slipped and dipped into the water. It was so cold and slimy, almost like the underside of a slug. I gagged, sobbed and squirmed harder as my foot caught one of those wicked looking teeth. I felt my flesh tear and rip as the razor sharp tooth jabbed into my ankle and refused to let go. 

*“The water claimed me, it will claim you again.”* Willow calmly said as she tried to push me into the nightmare of sluggish water. I felt the fear of drowning wash over me, the choking panic overwhelmed my senses. I felt like I was little again, the memory of almost drowning came back up like a punch to the face. The swirling of bubbles escaping my mouth, the water rushing down my throat, my lungs giving up on me as I quickly ran out of air.

This was it. This is how I would die, is what I thought until I felt the tree go still. I snapped out of the terrifying memory. No more pushing or wailing. Silence. 

Steve was at the base of the tree, his arms wrapped around the trunk as he favored his right leg. His shoulders shook as he pressed his forehead against the scaly bark and squeezed his lost daughter. Tears slipped down his cheeks with a broken sob. 

 “I’m sorry, baby girl.” he murmured as he lifted his head, the pale tree’s dark eyes softened as the park ranger softly continued, “I should have been there for you. I should have saved you. It’s my fault and…I’m so so sorry, Willow.”

*“Dad…”* the girl’s voice croaked and wavered.

The weeping tree shook, the water below my foot rippled before it slowly started to drain. The branches around my legs and arms slowly loosened before I was lowered to the ground. I stumbled and fell on my ass, my ankle oozed blood as I attempted to sit up.

The tree’s bark splintered before it slowly withered away to show a little girl in a one piece bathing suit, swim shoes and branches tangled around her ankles. Her form flickered and wavered like it would disappear with a strong gust of wind. Steve fell to his knees, his hands went through his daughter's specter when he tried to hug her again. She said something to him, but she said it so softly that I didn’t hear. She looked at me, her eyes sad and her expression one of regret. She dipped her head, looked towards the lake, and then disappeared into a scatter of tiny, shiny lights that coasted up to the sky where the sunset had started to dip below the horizon.

We didn’t move for a while, not until the screech of an owl made me jump and groan in pain from the deep, jagged gash in my ankle. Steve got up and quickly helped me onto my good leg, my gaze still locked onto the stars. 

“I’m sorry.” I softly said as I looked back down at the ground.

“So am I.” Steve said as he led me back into the resource center towards the first aid station. 

After we took the time to bandage my ankle and clean the dried blood from my definitely broken nose, Steve drove us on a park four wheeler to my car. We didn’t talk much as he called a tow truck for me. We just silently sat on the ATV and watched the moon climb higher in the sky. When the tow truck arrived, he offered to come with me so he could pay for the new tires. I would have told him no, but I was short on cash and knew I couldn’t afford to buy four new tires. 

The tow truck driver gave me a concerned look, the bruised nose and bloody bandage around my ankle warranting the worry. The park ranger quickly lied and told the driver that a group of teens laid out road spikes that made me crash my car. I went along with it. The driver didn’t seem to believe us, but muttered something about “not getting paid enough for this” before he pulled off the gravel road onto the asphalt. The drive was silent besides the quiet country music playing on the radio. I wanted to say something, but I didn’t know what to say. 

Four new tires, a dent that I refused to let Steve pay for, and a panicked phone call from my mom, I drove Steve back to the park. I tried to offer a trip to the hospital, as he couldn’t be okay after being thrown into a wall, but he just shook his head and said he just needed to sleep it off. I hesitated to leave, unsure how I could after almost dying here a second time.

“Hey, kid, you can’t stay here all night burning gas.” he lightly joked, but I didn’t laugh.

“Thank you. I don’t know how to repay you for this.” I said hoarsely, my throat still ached.

He shook his head and waved a hand like he was shooing away a fly.

“Don’t mention it. Just promise me you’ll go get that ankle checked out.”

I just nodded my head, gave him a small wave goodbye, and backed up to leave. 

As I left South Bend Park behind, I took one last look in the rear view mirror to see the glimmering outline of a tree waving goodbye. I blinked back tears and looked towards the road as a withering, white twig fluttered beneath one of the windshield wipers.
