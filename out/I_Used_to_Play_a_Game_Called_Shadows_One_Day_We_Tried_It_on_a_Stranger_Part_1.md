When I was nine, we played a game called Shadows.

The rules were simple. You picked someone—always someone you knew, always someone safe, like a friend or a neighbor or a cousin visiting for the weekend—and you followed them, as close as you could get. The point was to stay in their blind spot, to match every step, and every turn, and every pause, until they felt it and spun around and caught you. If they caught you, you lost. If you made it to their house or wherever their destination was without being seen, you won.

We played it all summer in Joséke. Dillon and I, and a girl named Casey, whose last name I can *never* remember. The streets around our neighborhood were long and boring, and so Shadows was the kind of game that felt like it had real stakes even when it didn't.

I was good at it, too. I had quiet footsteps and a talent for reading the moment someone was about to turn, and I'd drift back just far enough, just in time. Dillon said I was cheating. I said I was just paying attention.

The summer I turned ten, Casey dared me to do it to a stranger.

I said no. She called me a coward, and at age 10, being called a coward is almost as bad as being called a pussy, and so I said fine.

The man was walking alone, heading toward Lilac Park. He was middle-aged, I think—though everyone over thirty looks the same age when you're ten. He had a dark jacket and an unhurried walk. He had his hands in his pockets, and he was looking at the ground in front of him.

I fell in behind him.

It was different with a stranger. With Dillon or Casey, there was an ease to it, a game-shape that both of you understood, even if only one of you knew you were playing. With this man, it felt like something else. Something wrong. I matched his pace exactly, left foot, right foot, a careful eight inches behind, and the back of my neck went hot and strange.

He walked for almost a full block before he slowed down.

Then he stopped.

I stopped behind him.

He stood there for a moment with his hands still in his pockets.

Then he turned around.

I don't remember his face clearly. I only remember that he was calm. That he looked at me for a long time without saying anything. That his expression was not angry or surprised or amused, but it was nothing I had a word for at ten, and I'm not sure I have a word for it now.

Then he said: "Good game."

I ran. Casey and Dillon were at the end of the block, and I ran past them without stopping, and I didn't look back, and that was the last time we played Shadows that summer.

Casey moved away that fall. Dillon and I drifted apart the usual way kids do. I grew up, left Joséke for college, came back in my mid-twenties when my mother got sick, stayed after she recovered because leaving again just felt like too much effort, and because Joséke has a way of keeping people.

I forgot about the man near the park almost entirely.

I'm thirty-one now. I work remotely, I live alone, I have a neighbor named Harold who waves at me every morning, and a cat named something I won't write here because I don't want this to touch her. She's innocent.

Eight months ago, my friend Renata sent me a video.

She'd been filming a reel for her business—she does alterations out of her house, and she was filming herself walking down the main strip downtown, something about the neighborhood aesthetic, I wasn't really paying attention to the reason. She sent it to me because I'm visible in the background for a few seconds, walking out of the coffee place, and she thought it was funny.

I watched it twice before I saw it.

I am walking out of the coffee place. I have a cup in my hand. I am looking at my phone. I am completely unaware of anything around me.

And there is a man directly behind me.

Not nearby or anywhere in the general vicinity.

Directly behind me.

He is perhaps an inch from my back. He is matching my stride exactly, left foot when I step left, right foot when I step right, the slight hitch I have in my right hip from an old soccer injury. His head is angled down and slightly forward, perfectly positioned in my blind spot, the exact angle where a person cannot see what is behind them without turning fully around.

I didn't feel anything, and I remember that moment too. I remember coming out of the coffee place, I remember checking my phone, and I remember the sun being bright, but I felt nothing behind me... nothing at all.

The video is four seconds long. He is there for all four of them.

I sent Renata a screenshot and asked if she knew who that was.

She said what man.

I drew a circle around him in the screenshot and sent it back.

She said she hadn't noticed him. She said it was probably just someone walking. She said I looked paranoid and asked if I was sleeping okay.

I wasn't sleeping okay. I watched the video forty, maybe fifty times. I screenshot every frame he appears in, and I lined them up, and I studied them, and I could not identify a single fucking feature of his face because his face was never visible, not once in the four seconds. He maintained his position with an accuracy that should *not* have been possible on a busy sidewalk, adjusting around the other pedestrians without ever appearing in my sightline.

Three days later, Renata called me about a client who had brought in a wedding dress that needed alterations in less than two weeks. I was half listening, but she was mid-sentence when she stopped and said, almost as an afterthought:

"Oh, random question. Who was that guy you were with outside the coffee place?"

I didn't say anything.

"In the video," she said. "I was watching it back to show my sister the storefront, and I noticed him. I think I've seen him with you before, actually. Is he a friend of yours or something?"

I asked her when.

"I don't know, just... around? I can't remember specifically. I just figured he was someone you knew." She laughed a little. "Why, is he not?"

I told her no. I told her I didn't know who he was.

She said, "Huh, that's weird," and moved back to the wedding dress.

I sat there after she hung up, and I thought about the word *around.*

I started checking. I went back through every video, every photo on my phone, every tagged post on social media, going back years. I watched myself at my mother's birthday party, at Renata's wedding, at the Fourth of July thing in the park three summers ago, my little sister's graduation, I scanned backgrounds, I zoomed in on crowd shots, I watched myself move through spaces I'd forgotten I'd even been in.

Nothing, weeks of nothing. Weeks of clean backgrounds and normal people, nobody matching my movements—nobody following me.

I started to think I'd been wrong about what I saw. I started to think the video was a coincidence, like a stranger walking in the same direction at the same pace, the camera angle making it look stranger than it was. Maybe Renata had just misremembered. I started sleeping again. I stopped checking.

Last Friday, my coworker from the old office in Tulsa tagged me in a photo from a work party, three years ago, maybe four. Big group shot in the break room, everyone holding plastic cups, the overhead fluorescents making everyone look slightly ill, and I'm in the center of the frame, laughing at something someone said, completely present in the moment.

I almost didn't see it.

In the back of the room, half-obscured by two other coworkers who are turned away from the camera, standing very still while everyone around him moves—

The photo was taken in Tulsa. I lived in Joséke. I had not been to that office in months before that party, and I drove up alone that day, and I remember the drive home, I remember stopping for gas outside of Claremore, I remember every part of that day as a safe and ordinary thing.

I don't know how long it's been happening.

I don't know how long he's been there.

I'm writing this in my living room. The curtains are closed. My cat is asleep on the couch next to me, and she's not looking at anything, not right now, so I think that means I'm fine. I've always heard animals can tell; I'm choosing to believe that right now.

I keep thinking about Lilac Park. I keep thinking about a man in a dark jacket who turned around and looked at me for a long time and said *good game* with a look I didn't have a word for.

I think I have a word for it now.

I think he was welcoming me.
