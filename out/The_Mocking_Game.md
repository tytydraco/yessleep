When you were a kid, did you ever do something stupid or kind of mean to try and look cool in front of people you wanted to impress? Something you probably really regretted later on?

I did. And now, I really, really wish I hadn't.

If I'd refused, then maybe...

I'm getting ahead of myself.

I grew up in a small town in the middle of the Midwest. It wasn't any place special, you've probably seen or driven through a couple hundred towns like it. It had a town hall, a church, a couple banks, a police station, a fire house, a library, a doctor's office, a few dozen restaurants and shops, a movie theater, and a school. 

The school was the oldest building in town, and probably one of the few things that could set my hometown apart from others. You see, there weren't separate buildings for an elementary school, middle, and high school: it was just one long building with different classrooms/areas meant for different students. Other than occasionally getting refitted for modern safety standards (like when they installed a new fire alarm system), no one saw a reason to change the building. 

This meant that the older and younger students tended to intermingle a lot. Most of the time, this wasn't a problem; a lot of kids (unless they saw a sibling or something) tended to stick to their age group.

But sometimes, kids tried to step out of their comfort zone and hang out with the upperclassmen. Most of the time, they got gently rejected... or laughed at. But, once in a blue moon, the upperclassmen would allow an underclassmen to join them. 

I was one of those lucky few... Though if I'd known then what I know now, I wouldn't have ever tried.

You see, I was homeschooled all through elementary school. My mother felt that public education wasn't good for a child's early development, and had my grandmother, a retired teacher herself, teach me. This went on until the summer I turned eleven, when I begged my parents to let me attend public school. My dad agreed with me; he didn't think it was fair that I was being denied a chance to make friends. Finally, Mom relented and had me enrolled in the local school.

Socializing had been hard at first, seeing as how I was the new kid. Some girls were nice, but most of them had been friends since kindergarten or earlier, and didn't trust an outsider. Thus, I had tried to reach out to the upperclassmen for friends. Unlike many other kids, I succeeded.

My "friends" were both two grades ahead of me. Let's call them Rachel and Johnny. At thirteen, Rachel was two years older than me. I remembered I'd thought she was practically an adult because she wore makeup and had a cell phone. Her dad was the head of the most successful bank in town, making her family the most well-off. She was a little vain, but was usually nice. Her cousin, Johnny, was fourteen, having been held back a year for reasons I still don't know. He wore a leather jacket to school every day, regardless of whether, wore sunglasses whenever he walked outside, and flirted with every girl in his grade or higher.

I don't actually know when or why they agreed to let me hang out with them; maybe they took pity on me. But looking back, I wonder if it was because they thought I was enough of a pushover for them to make me play the Mocking Game.

One of the benefits of going to such an old school is that there are numerous old spots and secrets that many of the newer teachers don't know about, places and secrets passed down to each generation of students. Most of them are things like where the best places are to make out without being interrupted, where in the school library you can hide if you want to cut class, et cetera. But there were some secrets remembered only by a few. One of them was the tree house.

Back in the 1950s, a group of students had built a massive tree house in the forest behind the school. It wasn't a simple one-roomed shed in the branches, but instead a massive, four-room structure with a waterproof roof, a permanent ladder, even a chimney. It had been a regular hangout for students back in the day, but only the upperclassmen. None of them were allowed to tell any underclassmen under threat of killing your social life. They were all seniors, so by the time the last of them graduated, knowledge of the tree house faded away. 

At least, until Rachel and Johnny found it... and came up with a new use for it.

"While a lot of people don't remember the tree house," Rachel explained to me as we walked to the structure, "it actually sits next to a well-used hiking trail in the park."

"Ghost Park?" I'd asked, trying to keep the fear out of my voice. Even I knew the rumors about the local park being haunted.

Both my older "friends" laughed.

"By the end of today," Johnny promised as we approached the tree house, "you'll know why it's called that."

After showing me the tree house (which had held up remarkably well for something built over fifty years ago), the two had lead me inside.

I was startled to find, of all things, a bird in a cage.

It wasn't a very well-kept bird; while there was a dish of water and pile of seeds on the bottom of its cage, neither looked like they'd been touched in a while. And it was missing quite a few feathers... many of them on the cage floor.

"My dad has an aviary of starlings in our backyard," Rachel commented as Johnny picked up the cage. "He doesn't actually count them; whenever one dies, he just goes to the pet store one town over and buys a new one. He never even notices when I take one."

I blinked, confused. Johnny, snickering, told the bird "Mocking Game." Then, leaning out the tree house window, he tucked the bird's cage onto two especially thick tree branches. 

The foliage was so thick that you couldn't see the cage unless you were looking right at it, and even then it was a challenge. The bird flitted in it's cage, agitated. Then it pulled one of its feathers out.

"What are we doing, exactly?" I finally asked.

"Having some fun," Johnny grinned.

It wasn't too long of a wait before someone walked up the trail. It was a woman dressed for jogging, a water bottle at her side. 

Rachel wiped off her lipstick, put her pinkies in her mouth, and gave a long, low whistle.

The woman heard her, and paused, looking confused.

"Wait for it," Johnny whispered.

Then, from seemingly no where came a high-pitched voice. It sounded human... mostly. But there was something off about it. It made a shiver slide up and down my spin.

*"So pretty. Pretty pretty."*

Startled, the woman took a step back, her head turning as she tried to see where the voice was coming from.

*"Pretty pretty come with me."*

The voice sounded a bit louder. Johnny and Rachel had their hands over their mouths, trying to stifle their giggles.

The woman lost her composure and took off, back the way she came. The jogging evidently paid off, because she was gone in seconds.

Rachel and Johnny burst out laughing while I sat there, stunned.

"What just happened?" I asked.

"Starlings are great at mimicking voices," Rachel chortled. "We used to just yell stuff into the woods in creepy voices. Then one day, I thought I'd try using one of my dad's birds. We taught it what to say, hid it in the tree, it works great!"

"The Ghost Park is called Ghost Park thanks to us!" Johnny guffawed.

For a moment, I wanted to leave. If my new friends' primary form of entertainment was scaring innocent people, then I doubted they were the kind of people I wanted as friends.

But... they were my only friends. I hadn't had any luck making friends with anyone else, and that age, feeling lonely sounded worse than feeling guilty. 

So I pulled up the corners of my mouth and faked a laugh.

To keep from getting caught, Rachel, Johnny, and I didn't play the Mocking Game all the time. After all, do it to often and you're bound to get caught. Typically we only went to the tree house every two weeks or so, and during the winter (unless it was unusually warm) we didn't go at all. I never stopped feeling bad about scaring innocent people, but I thought that as long as we weren't actually HURTING anyone (and didn't do it too often), then it wouldn't be too bad.

Over the next three years, I kept hoping that Johnny and Rachel would eventually lose interest in the Mocking Game. Not just due to guilt, but because of where we played it. While the tree house was meticulously built, something about it left me uneasy. As I spent more time there, I often wondered how it had stayed standing for so long, and in such good condition, with no upkeep? Other things concerned me, too: why were there never bugs or animals in the house, nor signs that they'd been there? How come rain never seemed to come into the windows, or pine needles for that matter? How could such a big tree house not be seen from a trail that wasn't more than twenty feet from it?

And then there were the starlings. The birds Johnny and Rachel used for the Mocking Game all dropped dead after only being used four or five times. Often, Rachel would leave the bird in the treehouse on her way to school so we could play the Mocking Game after. I even went with  her a couple times. The bird was always reasonably calm and happy when it was left there... but by the time we got back, it would be a ball of nerves with several feathers ripped out.

"Why do you call it the Mocking Game?" I whispered to Rachel and Johnny at lunch one day.

They paused, shared a look, then shrugged in sync.

"We were just hanging out in the tree house one day when we came up with the idea of scaring people," Johnny explained. "The name for it sort of just... came to us."

That topic, unfortunately, led to their discussing their weekend plans with me. That year, they were both graduating. Their respective parents both wanted them to go on summer college tours, which meant they'd be far too busy to do any of their usual summer antics.

So, that evening, they wanted to go play the Mocking Game one last time.

I was torn. On one hand, if it was the last night of the game, then I'd never have to scare people or go to the creepy tree house again. On the other hand, we'd never played it at night.

"But we don't have a bird," I whispered, hoping that I might talk them out of it.

Rachel smirked. "We didn't always use a bird, we can just make spooky noises. The park's reputation will do the rest. Come on, it'll be fun."

I sighed. I'd been a pushover throughout my childhood, and they knew by then that it didn't take much encouragement to get me in on their schemes.

After class that day, I stopped off at home long enough to stuff a flashlight, bottle of water, and snacks into my backpack. As I started to head out the door, I remembered something my dad, a forest ranger for the nearby national park, had said about bears being more active at night. Not wanting to risk facing a dangerous animal, I grabbed a can of my dad's bear spray and a box of matches from the garage. 

My parents weren't going to be in until much later that night. In the last few years, something weird had been happening in the nearby national park and at several neighboring farms. First, farmers had noticed chickens or ducks disappearing from their coops. Then turkeys. Then pigs. Then sheep. Then cows. Every couple weeks, it seemed, one or two animals would just vanish without a trace. When three weeks passed without any animals vanishing, the confused farmers hoped that was the end of it.

That is, until hikers started disappearing.

I made it to the meeting point on the school playground a little after seven; school had been closed for hours y then. Rachel and Johnny showed up a few minutes later, holding a bag of sandwiches.

"Here!" Rachel chirped as she handed one to me. "American cheese with mustard, your favorite!"

I hate American cheese, and mustard makes my lips burn. But she thought it was my favorite, so I thanked her and took a bite of the sandwich. 

As we walked towards the tree house, I spat out the bite I'd taken and tossed the sandwich into the trees.

Rachel and Johnny didn't notice as they inhaled their sandwiches.

By the time we got to the tree house, the sun was gone. A few stars could be seen through the tree branches, but other than that there was hardly any light.

"Do you think anyone's even going to come through here?" I asked as the three of us turned on our flashlights.

Johnny smirked. "I might have started a rumor that girls think the forest at night is romantic. We're bound to at least get a few kids from school."

Later, I would learn why he was wrong; several of the more popular kids at school were hosting a barbecue for the senior class, though all students were welcome to attend. 

Time ticked by as we waited for someone.

I think I'm the one who noticed something first.

You see, in nature, there's this phenomenon called "The Silence". Whenever a dangerous predator is in the area, every nearby animal will go completely quiet. No birds chirping, no squirrels chittering, nothing. Hunters, loggers, rangers, anyone who works outside can tell you that it's an eerie feeling. My dad says it's nature's way of telling you to watch your back.

That night, it was a warm spring, the kind that tells you summer is coming quickly. The nightbirds were starting to call, cicadas were going crazy, there was the scrabbling of small animals in the undergrowth...

Then, suddenly, it all stopped. No forewarning, no tapering off... It just cut off. Like turning off a radio.

Rachel and Johnny had been talking to each other and didn't realize what had happened. But they seemed to notice rather quickly that their voices suddenly seemed a lot louder than they'd been seconds before.

"What's going on?" Johnny whispered to me.

I peered out the tree house window, looking into the forest. "I don't really--"

I froze, my voice dying in my throat.

There was something standing in the treeline, staring right at us.

It looked like a stick-figure drawing of a man. Long, stick-like limbs with no visible hands or feet. An impossible thin torso than stretched up at least eight feet. And a round, lumpy head with two glowing red eyes.

Two eyes staring right at us.

Johnny and Rachel still hadn't seen it, but they noticed I was shaking.

"Hey, are you alright?" Rachel asked, her voice painfully loud in the silence.

The creature's head ticked to the side. 

It heard us.

"It's probably just drafty or something," Johnny flippantly responded. 

I wanted to scream at them to shut up, but my throat was closed. My eyes were still locked onto the creature.

*"Pretty."*

Johnny and Rachel froze at the sound of the voice. I did, too, but for a different reason.

I'd watched the creature's mouth open.

"Guys?" I whispered to Johnny and Rachel. "Did you ever actually see the starling say those things?"

Johnny's pupils shrank as Rachel's face paled.

"*Pretty pretty pretty. Pretty teach talk."*

As I watched, I noticed the creature was getting closer to the tree house.

"Turn off the flashlights!" Johnny hissed as he scrambled for his light. Rachel, tears and mascara running down her face, struggled but got her and my flashlight off.

*"Pretty teach talk. Pretty teach lure food. Pretty good teach."*

It's voice was growing closer. There was a creak on the boards of the ladder.

And then it was silent.

We waited. A minute passed. Two minutes. Five minutes.

Johnny, sounding like a scared little boy, was the first to speak.

"Maybe it's gone?"

Rachel turned on her flashlight.

And screamed bloody murder.

The creature was in the window, it's entire head the size of the frame. It was even more terrifying up close. The skin was a rotten orange-brown, it's mouth was filled with knife-like teeth. It's eyes were souless, pupil-less red orbs. A white rag of some sort was stuck in the side of its mouth.

*"Pretty good teach. Pretty now FOOD!"*

Screaming, Johnny shoved Rachel aside and ran for the other window. Rachel, crying and screaming, tried to run after him. I was still on the floor, frozen, and she tripped over me. As the creature started to reach inside the house, Rachel got up, shoved me towards the monster, and darted after Johnny.

I hadn't managed to regain my balance before something grabbed me around the middle, pinning my arms down. The monster's vine-like fingers dug into my skin. Trying not to cry or scream, I was forced to stare at the creature's face as it pulled me closer and closer to it's gaping mouth...

It sniffed.

*"Food giver. No eat food giver."*

It let me go immediately. I stood there, in shock... and noticed the rag sticking out of its mouth.

It wasn't a rag, it was a wrapper. The wrapper from... From the sandwich I'd thrown away.

*"Must find pretty. Pretty boy, pretty girl. Must have pretty."*

With that, the monster's face removed itself from the window.

I collapsed to the floor. I felt like laughing, crying, screaming, running away and throwing up all at once.

I debated leaving the tree house, creepy as it was, but decided against it. As terrified as I was, I barely knew how to get back to the school from here during the day, let alone in the dark.

With my dad's bear spray in my hands, I waited all night, barely daring to breathe.

I didn't move. Not even when I heard Rachel's agonizing screams. Or Johnny's, which seemed to go on much longer than Rachel's had.

Eventually, daylight started to creep through the woods, bringing warmth. That was a small comfort, though, compared to the sounds of morning birds calling. 

Birds wouldn't call if the creature was still there.

The knowledge that I was truly safe helped me finally relax. Exhaustion started to take over, but I still managed to climb out of the tree house. I was still wearing my backpack, but in my exhaustion I forgot the matches and bear spray.

It took a minute, but I made it back to the school. A police car was waiting there; my mother had noticed I wasn't home the night before and sounded the alarm. I was taken to the police station, where I reunited with my parents.

After giving us a few moments, a stern-looking deputy asked me to step aside and answer a few questions.

Johnny and Rachel hadn't returned home either, and everyone knew I was one of their only friends. I told the truth... The realistic truth, anyway. Johnny and Rachel had been planning a prank in the woods, and I'd been roped into it. Some animal had appeared and scared them off, leaving me alone in the tree house. When they decided I was telling the truth and did NOT know what had happened to Johnny and Rachel, I was free to leave with my parents.

I noticed, however, that the deputies had looked troubled at my mentioning the old tree house.

The next night, my dad was called in to help with a fire in the woods behind the school. 

I was never told the exact details, and the newspapers were asked, bribed, or threatened not to publish anything. But the rumor mill couldn't be stopped. According to the neighborhood gossips, someone had found the old tree house in the woods and either intentionally or accidentally set it ablaze. 

When they got it under control enough to go in, they found two sets of abandoned, bloodstained clothes. Johnny and Rachel's clothes, though the location of their bodies was still a mystery.

The chief fireman said that a can of bear spray had been used to start the blaze, likely something left behind by a hiker.

I don't know if that's truly what happened at the site of the fire. I may never know. What I do know, however, is that Johnny and Rachel's parents moved out of town that summer. Parents started insisting that their kids only go into the woods with an adult, and even some adults started avoiding the forest.

For a year or so, it was all anyone would talk about.

But as years passed, people forgot. The story of the two missing kids and the mysterious fire went from a rumor to an urban legend. Then a campfire story. There are few people my age in this town who've forgotten the whole thing.

But I didn't.

I can't.

I work in the next town, and have to drive by the forest every day to get to my office.

And this morning, as I drove near the forest behind the school, I heard a voice call out.

*"Pretty?"*
