I've never enjoyed hospitals, the combination of harsh white lights with the relentless sterile smell of bleach always in the air, who would enjoy hospitals? My wife had been overflowing with excitement ever since we found out she was pregnant but for me it took a while to get used to the idea of being a father. The thought of all the responsibility's stressed me out enough to give me a panic attack but when we did the ultrasound and I actually saw my baby, I wanted nothing more then to be a dad. Me and my wife used to fantasize what it would be like to finally become a family.

"Used to."

What was supposed to be the happiest day of our lives, in an instant, turned out to be the worst of mine and the last of hers. My wife died scared and screaming covered in her own blood. She was never even granted the chance to hold her own daughter.

"And neither did I. My daughter died in that hospital."

Every 24 hours that pass is one hour longer then my daughters entire lifetime. Just a mere 23 hours after my wife died my daughter followed suit. Not only was the love of my life taken from me that day but GOD felt he needed my beautiful new born child as well. It hurt me so deeply to know that my beautiful baby girl would have to meet her parents for the first time in heaven.

"No, I don't like hospitals."

Never before have I ever felt so broken, been so depressed. I was the soul survivor of my family's trip to the hospital.

The next year of my life felt as if I spent it looking up from the bottom of a pool, I was drowning. Every interaction I had was dulled by the overwhelming pressure of depression. After a year of suffering through the muffled condolences of blurry apologetic silhouettes I decided it was time for a change.

Even though I've spent almost all of my life in a city, my wife really had been the only reason I stayed. Crowded streets and cold weather no longer seemed manageable without her warmth next to me. I wanted to move somewhere quieter, warmer, Texas is what I landed on.

After all the money I spent on moving and hospital fees I was only able to afford a little apartment just outside of Dallas. The place was small and clean but most importantly there was no memories.

I found the thing most affected by this tragedy was my memories. Every happy memory I ever made with my wife now just made me sad and every bad memory made me angry at myself. Memories of my lost loved ones began to haunt me like ghosts.

In the face of every woman I only saw what I lost and in every baby I see what I'll never have. Falling asleep was becoming increasingly more difficult but on the rare occasion I was able to I was greeted by the recuring dream of my family's death. Some mornings when I wake up I cant remember if I dreamt of them but I could tell I had when I woke up crying.

Getting acclimated to a new environment is always challenging, the weather of course was a big change but I found getting used to southern hospitality the most challenging. Watching strangers greet one another like lifelong friends was such a foreign behavior to me. In the city when someone approached you with a smile it was to either sell you something or take something. My willingness to accept this new friendlier culture was immediately put to the test.

It was early Sunday morning and I was taking a break from unpacking to watch a little football (American) when I heard someone knock on my door.

Knock, Knock, Knock.

The 3 knocks identical both in sound and duration echoed through my semi furnished apartment. Begrudgingly I made my way to the door stepping over my carboard encased belongings and peered through my peep hole.

On the other side of my door stood a well groomed, sharply dressed man. The tall mans face was framed by slightly greasy long black hair accompanied by a large smile plastered across it. He looked like a nice enough guy but not seeing any package or the pizza I had ordered and not feeling particularly sociable I decided to just ignore him. I made it half way back to the couch before again I heard.

Knock, Knock, Knock.

I let out an audible sigh and decided to brace myself for the inevitable conversation, hopefully it would be a short one.

"Hidee ho neighbor!"

His introduction was overflowing with energy and his smile grew wider at the sight of me.

"Hi.... Can I help you?" I tried to hide the sound of my irritation.

"Matter of fact you can. I would love nothing more then if you'd be so kind as to invite me in for a nice neighborly chat. Now how good does that sound neighbor?"

Each word of the strangers request was spoken with such enthusiasm and kindness that I briefly considered granting him his request. But no matter how friendly the man seemed there was no way I was about to let some guy I just met into my home. I don't care how nice people are here, letting a stranger into your home wasn't normal, southern hospitality be damned.

Although the request was strange to me he had said it so casually that I assumed he was apart of some sort of welcome committee for the building. I had only been living there for a few days and I didn't want to be known as the rude new neighbor so I tried my best to respond in a way that wouldn't offend him.

"What? Listen, you seem nice and all but I'm actually not even done unpacking. So like the place is a huge mess and I just don't really think it's ready for company, you know?"

After I said this the man shut his eyes, clinched his fists and his smile began to quiver. Seeing that type of response to what I said made me suddenly very uncomfortable. It looked as if he was using all his energy to scour his brain for the perfect response. Just as I was about to close my door the mans eyes shot back open and his smile once again held firm.

"Oh that's no bother to me neighbor. Heck, I'm feeling so gosh darn generous that I'll even help you unpack. Now how gosh darn perfect does that sound neighbor?"

"Fuck." I thought to myself.

I could tell that this guy wasn't going to take no for an answer. You cant use excuses with people like this cause they'll always find a way to twist it to benefit them. I knew my earlier wish of not being known as the rude neighbor might not be coming true.

"Look man you can't come in. Now I got work to do and your keeping me from doin it so I'm gonna go."

I expected him to fully break down this time I was ready for a, "fuck you" or something along those lines but I was surprised when none of that came. It was obvious my response wasn't the one he'd been hoping for but even with his disappointment the man stayed positive and polite.

"Welp, alrighty then neighbor. Heck, If you don't want to have a nice friendly conversation between two neighbors that's your business. Plus there's always next time Robert. Maybe then you try being a little more sociable huh? You have a GREAT day now."

The man smiled, offered to shake hands (I rejected) walked down the hallway and turned out of sight. Happy the interaction had finally ended I shut the door and went back to unpacking my shattered past.

While unpacking some kitchen stuff I began to think back on just how strange the interaction I just had was. For some reason other then just his outlandish request I couldn't help but have a growing feeling of uneasiness about the conversation. Word by word I retraced everything the man said trying to find what was bothering me but for the life of me I wasn't able to figure it out, until. After replaying the conversation in my head more times then I'd like to admit I finally figured out what was making me so uneasy. Somehow, during the mans goodbye, he referred to me by my name.

"No, there's no way he could know that... right?" I couldn't answer my own question, somehow in a new apartment, in a new city, in a new state he knew me or at least my name.

Throughout the following week I began to notice that the usual relentless bombardment of depressing memories were becoming less frequent. Things that once made me cry merely by the sight of them now held little to effect on my emotionally state whatsoever. My change in emotion wasn't due to some result of sudden closure but instead I feared I was beginning to forget them. Somehow slowly forgetting my family wasn't the worst thing going on in my mind, that mantel goes to what was replacing them. I suddenly had all these new memories or  "False Memories" as I refer to them.

In place of each forgotten memory of my loved ones now sat one of that man. No matter how hard I tried I couldn't help myself from remembering times we'd seemingly spent together. Everyday I would wake to a new memory of us, whether it be one of us at a party or just drinking on my couch in this apartment.

"This apartment."

That was the tell. All the happy times we apparently had shared together almost had me fully convinced, if it weren't for that one detail. Somehow all the memories of this grand supposed friendship we shared, they all take place in this apartment. Years worth of false memories. Even though I was now able to discern which memories were fact and which fiction it was still ever prevalent that my mind was in fact betraying me.

In the decaying, rusted file cabinet that was my mind, now standing next to my wife and daughter was a tall, sharply dressed, smiling man.
