I didn’t notice it at first because I wasn’t looking for it. I only noticed because my neighbor noticed it first, and then she stopped talking to me entirely.

 

Her name was Diane. Unit 4B, directly across the hall from where I live (4A). We’d lived in the same building for three years — pleasantries by the mailboxes, complaints about the superintendent, the kind of neighborly rhythm you don’t think about until it stops. She was retired. Kept a cat. I can’t recall its name.

 

The first sign was a note slipped under my door in December. Handwritten, lined notebook paper, ink slightly smeared like someone has written it in a rush.

 

\*Please stop walking at night. \*

 

I don’t walk at night. I work from home for a data entry, logistics company and my routine hasn’t changed in two years. Up at seven. Coffee. Laptop. Bed by eleven. I don’t sleepwalk. At least, I never have.

 

I knocked on her door the next morning. She answered it but with the chain on, her face tight in a way I hadn’t seen it before. Not anger. Something else.

 

“Diane, I got your note. I think there’s been a misunderstanding.”

 

“I hear you every night,” she said. “Between three and four. Walking back and forth in the hallway. Just back and forth.”

 

“That’s not me.”

 

“It’s your door that opens.”

 

She shut the door in my face. Didn’t slam it. Just shut it, carefully, then I heard the deadbolt turn.

 

 

I set an alarm for 3:00 AM that night. Sat on my couch in the dark, watching the front door. Nothing. At 3:47 I went back to bed feeling stupid.

 

The next morning, another note.

 

\*You did it again. \*

 

I bought a security camera — cheap, the kind you mount with adhesive strips. It took two nights to arrive, two more to set up properly. By the time it was up, Diane had stopped answering her door. Her mail started piling up in the hallway box. Catalogs mostly. A couple of envelopes from what looked like a pension office.

 

For four nights the footage showed nothing. Empty hallway. My door, shut. The timestamp ticking forward in silence.

 

On the fifth night, at 3:14 AM, my door opened.

 

 

I watched it six times before I accepted what I was seeing. The deadbolt turned — smoothly, cleanly and the door swung inward. It stayed open for exactly forty-one seconds. Then it closed. The deadbolt turned back.

 

Nothing entered the hallway. Nothing left my apartment. The door opened and closed, from the inside, on its own.

 

I had a locksmith replace the entire mechanism that afternoon. New deadbolt, new knob, new strike plate. Two hundred dollars down the drain. He tested it twice before he left.

 

That night, at 3:14, the new lock opened.

 

 

I started reviewing the footage frame by frame, slowing it down. That’s when I noticed the vibration — the hallway light fixture, a cheap fluorescent panel, trembling at 3:13. One minute of fine, rapid shaking, like something heavy shifting its weight nearby. Stillness. Then the lock turned.

 

I checked all five nights. The vibration was there every time. Exactly one minute before.

 

I called the superintendent. He sent someone to look at the door frame, the hinges. The guy shrugged, said everything looked fine. I didn’t show him the footage. I don’t know why.

 

The forty-one seconds started to bother me. I timed it across eleven consecutive nights. Not a single variation. Whatever was happening, it was precise. It was doing exactly what it intended, for exactly as long as it needed to.

 

So, I started measuring. Forty-one seconds to walk to the end of the hallway and back? Twelve. To the stairwell? Eighteen. To Diane’s door and back? Six.

 

None of it added up.

 

Then I tried something else. I stood in my hallway and simply waited, counting in my head. Forty-one seconds of standing still, breathing slowly. It felt like the length of time it takes to settle back into sleep after being woken up gently. The length of time a body needs to go quiet again.

 

The door was not opening so that something could leave or enter.

 

 

I left notes for Diane after that. Slid them through the gap in her mail slot.

 

\*Did you hear it again last night? \*

 

\*I have it on camera. It’s not me. \*

 

Nothing back. Her mail kept piling up. On a Saturday I knocked hard, three times, and pressed my ear to the wood. I stood there for almost a minute. I almost called building management for a welfare check, but something held me back — not concern, exactly. Something closer to certainty. Like I already knew she was fine. Like I knew she was on the other side of that door, inches away, and had simply decided not to open it.

 

I left her alone.

 

 

On November 9^(th) I came home from the grocery store and found my apartment door unlocked. Not open. Unlocked. Middle of the afternoon. The deadbolt was in the open position, the knob turned slightly, like someone had just let go of it.

 

I stood in the hallway for a long time before I went in.

 

Nothing was out of place. The laptop on the desk was closed the way I’d left it. The glass in the sink was the one I’d used that morning. But there was a smell — faint, and wrong in a way that took me several minutes to place. I stood in the middle of my living room and breathed through my nose until I got it.

 

It was the smell of sleep. That close, warm, skin-and-breath smell of someone who has been lying down for hours in a still room. A body at rest.

 

My bed was made. Sheets pulled tight, the way I always leave them. But when I lifted the comforter, the mattress was warm. Not room temperature. Body warm. Like someone had been lying there for a long time and had gotten up only minutes before I walked in.

 

I hadn’t been sleeping alone.

 

I didn’t know for how long.

 

 

I moved out two days later. Didn’t break the lease properly — just packed what I could carry in two trips and left. Didn’t tell the superintendent. Didn’t tell anyone.

 

I want to be honest about what I believe, because I know how this sounds. I am not a superstitious person. I don’t believe in ghosts or hauntings or any of that. I think there is an explanation for everything that happened in that apartment. Something structural, something about that building, something I didn’t understand. Probably boring. Probably mechanical.

 

I set up the same camera in my new place before I finished unpacking. Same angle, pointed at the front door. I set an alarm for 3:00 AM every night. I sit in the dark and I watch the door and I wait.

 

For the first three nights, nothing happened. The door stayed shut. The lock stayed still. I started to feel normal again. Started to think I’d gotten away from it.

 

This morning, I woke up at 6:41, an hour something before my alarm. I laid there for a moment, and then I noticed it — the sheets on the other side of the bed.

 

They were pulled back. They were pulled back carefully, the way someone leaves them when they don’t want to wake up the person next to them.
