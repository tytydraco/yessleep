I shielded my face as I jogged down the courthouse steps, through the huddled masses of interested public and the vulturous press. Squeezing myself through the bodies, microphones, cameras and cheap suits, I found my assistant Julio’s hand. Thank fuck, I thought. I got into the passenger seat of my Towncar and there was an assuring thud as Julio closed the door and walked to the other side. My phone was dead. Julio threaded the car through the lingering news crews and turned onto the freeway. It wasn’t until then that it dawned on me. I had just won the unbeatable case.

I should back up a bit. Earlier this year, a woman named Vanessa S. was killed in the parking garage of her workplace. The body was found by a janitor and police arrived at a bloody scene. Seventeen stab wounds were found across her torso, any one of which would have been fatal, the county medical examiner later testified. Skull fractures to the back of the head and cheekbones. Both of her wrists snapped.

That’s when my firm received a call from Jonathan B.

Jonathan B. had been Vanessa’s supervisor at their office. Witness testimony from colleagues would later reveal that the two had had a heated argument on the day of the murder. Jonathan had retained my law firm a few months prior, when he allegedly smacked his golf caddy for giving him a six-iron instead of a seven. The two were rolling around in the greens when a group finally managed to separate them. I represented Jonathan then, reaching an out-of-court settlement. He had always been charming and personable – even in his county orange jumpsuit. I still remember when Abigail, my boss and managing partner, showed me the news on her laptop. Jonathan B. had been arrested in connection with a workplace homicide. We knew he would call. When he did, all he said was, “I want the blonde woman again.”

The evidence had been damning, to say the least. I was hanging my career on a thread, but I wasn’t saying no to Abigail, or the demands of one of our top clients.

Julio pulled into the driveway of my house and drummed his fingers on the steering wheel.

“Do you need anything else, Cynthia?” Julio asked. He kept his face stern, but I knew what he wanted. My husband was away on business again.

Julio left when we were finished – he never stayed after – and I finally had a chance to plug in my phone. There was a burst of notification bubbles and vibrations as the phone came alive. All congratulatory. There was a voicemail from Abigail.

“Girl, what?” her voice asked on the recording. “You are a fucking star, Cynthia! I’d say the partner conversation is well and truly underway. Call me when you get this. Thanks, bye.”

I jumped out of bed and immediately called Abigail. It was a huge moment. As I spoke on the phone, I watched the blood-orange sky transition to dusk through the window.

One thing every lawyer will tell you is to never ask your client if they did it. Every lawyer is lying. Over the course of my career, I’ve asked that question a handful of times. But only when I was sure the answer was no. I never asked Jonathan.

Toward the middle of the trial, the prosecutors threw us a plea deal.

“They’re offering twenty years with the possibility of parole,” I told him in the visiting cell, in a hushed whisper to make sure the guard by the door wouldn’t hear. It was a good deal considering the evidence. That was a day after the parking garage CCTV footage was released. Grainy as all hell, but sure to push the jury firmly against him.

“Not interested,” Jonathan said, a shackle and chain dangling off one hand.

“You should at least consider it. If this gets any worse, Jon, I don’t know if they’ll give us another–”

Jonathan raised his free hand slightly. “It’s your job to make sure it doesn’t get worse.”

“I have an obligation to tell you to consider this deal.”

“I need you to listen, Cynthia.” His face hardened. “I’m not going away for this.” Jonathan nodded toward the guard at the door, signalling the meeting was over.

It had been a different Jonathan from the first case. He was cold, demanding. A lot of clients usually wanted advice. Jonathan expected things, like he owned me, or the paralegals who came to collect statements, even the county jail itself. When he spoke he took the measure of you and held you in his gaze. Every time I walked down those dark cell corridors for our meetings, the sickening feeling of dread built with every step.

To establish a murder suspect, you have to put together means, motive, and opportunity. Jonathan was rich and physically stronger than the victim. That was means. They worked in the same office and he would have been aware of her routine and movements. That was opportunity. The prosecution made the case that Jonathan and the victim, Vanessa, had been on bad terms leading up to the murder. They argued that the murder was revenge over an earlier transgression. Let’s go back to the golf course fight. It had been a company event, and all the employees and favored clients had attended, mostly to lounge in the sun with their cocktails. One of the reasons we elected not to fight the assault charges in court was because a cellphone video of the incident had anonymously made its way online. Allegedly, Jonathan suspected Vanessa of releasing the video and that had been the source of their argument on the day of the murder. That was motive.

After checking my email and responding to a few more calls, I ordered pizza and ensconced myself on the couch for a night of Legally Blonde. The sun had already set and I could hear the soft staccato splatter of rain falling on the roof.

I had had one more meeting with Jonathan before closing arguments. The prosecution had promised another plea agreement. A guard escorted me to the visitation cell.

Jonathan was already seated, fists clenched on the table, wearing his orange jumpsuit. He had gotten worse with every day of trial. As I seated myself quietly, I knew we were going to lose.

“Hi, Jonathan.” I could barely mask my unease.

He didn’t answer, only watching me from across the bare metal table.

“The… the prosecution came back with another plea deal,” I said.

“I’m gonna stop you right there, Cynthia,” he answered in his usual flat, measured tone. The black bags beneath his eyes drooped low and his cheeks were hollow.

“Don’t you at least want to hear it? Jonathan, closing arguments are tomorrow, we can’t afford to–”

“Say it!” he shouted.

“What?” I waved off the guard as he came to check on us.

“Say it. Say you think I did it.”

My body felt tense. “What I think is not–”

“So you do think I did it?” I saw his knuckles go white.

“I didn’t say that.”

After closing arguments, it only took the jury two hours to deliberate. I didn’t even need to leave the courthouse. Not guilty. You see, in a last minute scramble, my team was able to obtain the source of the cellphone video from Jonathan’s previous case, proving that it couldn’t have been from Vanessa S. I argued that the prosecution had built their entire case on the false premise that Jonathan killed Vanessa in an act of revenge, when he had no reason to suspect her in the first place.

Jonathan had heard the verdict in his tailored, navy suit – emotionless even in victory. When I moved to embrace him, he stood stiff and whispered in my ear. All he said was, “I told you”.

I must have had a glass of wine too many because I passed out before the end of Red, White and Blonde, and woke up to a black screen on the TV. The rain had picked up now, a barrage striking the roof and windows of the house. And everything was dark. I stood up to go to bed, when I saw the figure in the window. A jolt of fear ripped through me. But just as quickly, the figure was gone. Even as every inch of my being willed otherwise, I walked over to peer through the window. I saw nothing but haze through streaks of rain. Then a knock came from the front door. I jumped again. I looked around the living room for my phone to see what time it was, but I must have left it in the bedroom. A knock came again.

Hesitantly, I walked toward it. There, I saw the figure again through the frosted glass of the front door. A man, it had to be, and large. I opened the door.

He wore all black, drenched in rain. And something else that caused me to freeze where I stood. Blood matted Jonathan’s chest and arms and parts of his jacket were torn.

Jonathan B. cackled, looking through me as I stood barefoot in my pajamas. “Cynthiaaa,” he said in a singsong voice. “I wanted to thank you, Cynthia.” I could see blood running down his chin and neck.

“Thank me? Are you alright, Jonathan?” My voice was shrill, and it took all I had not to scream.

“Yes!” He cackled again. “And yes. You found out it wasn’t Vanessa who recorded the video.”

“It… it got you off.”

“I called your firm, Cynthia. And they told me who it actually was.”

I regarded him again. The dripping blood. The ripped clothes. “What have you done?” I couldn’t breathe.

He seemed disoriented but his eyes found me again. They were a dark red.

“You never told me, Cynthia.” His voice turned calm again. “Do you think I did it?”
