   A pothole. Ugly. Round. Rugged. And miasmic. In the center of the narrow alley, I passed every day on my way back home from work. In the dark, populated by filth, dustbins, stray dogs, and moldering cow dungs from all around, the puddle almost dissembled itself into the flatness of the asphalt. If one were myopic, or visually challenged in any way, one'd scarcely evade snagging their foot under it and becoming its victim. I'd posit that nobody but me traversed through that accursed alleyway. I stumbled upon it every day since I first encountered it, which I estimate to be three weeks from now. When I first met it, it lay but scarcely more than four inches wide in diameter.

   One would be impelled to conjecture, not unjustly so, that I'm insane. It must be understood I am not. I am not weaving a romance or entertaining my fantasies, but merely confessing the truth, when I say that the puddle seemed to grow. It became wider and wider as each day passed. Its perimeter protracted further and further as each hour vanished into the repository of time. The stagnant, moldy water that swam under the puddle—the odious hotbed of pestilential cocoons—turned thicker and more fetid the more glances I oblate into it. It fascinated me greatly. I dreamt of it. Dreamt that celestial, not hellish, creatures—nymphs and fairies—rose from it to persuade me to plunge into it and join them in their abode where I could cloy myself with nectar and ambrosia.

   Each day as I passed through that alley, I stood at the edge of the puddle and gazed at the distorted visuals of my face reflected to me on the surface of the water before heading forward on my journey. For the first few days I could simply stride over it, then, as it spread its rim around, I was compelled to step around it, and when it eventually ventured to become so large as to encompass the entire alley into its wing, ready to swallow the sky, I was forced to creep along the walls that skirted the alleyway on either side. I was aware of another path that led to my house—a short cut, though I never took it because, you see, I was fascinated by the puddle.

   So, at the end of the second week, when I entered the alley to find it absolutely engulfed by the puddle, I distinguished a strange smell. A smell I couldn't fish out from the treasure trove of my memory. Yet, I'd endeavor to delineate the scent in words, however insufficient they may be: It contained a hint of boiled silkworms, carrying a salty tinge to it; however, the main component entailed the scent of a girl. I could imagine it. A beautiful, virgin girl, with a raven head and tawny skin, not more than seventeen years old. I went ten steps forward to stand at the craggy rim of the puddle and stare at the water under it. The water boiled and bubbled all over, like heat wriggled under it, producing columns of steam that settled across my face. I remained there, riveted to my spot, having my speech stolen by the circumstances presented to me.

   I do not know how many minutes, or hours, passed before I discerned a faint movement on the surface. A slight bulge upward. A tremulousness. Then I beheld a head, raven in color, gradually emerging from under it, with a tawny and smooth forehead. The head rose further and soon revealed to me the face uglier than a gargoyle. It stared at me with its ugly, fathomless, empty, owlish eyes. Empty. Literally empty. There lay no pupils, no iris, no retinas, just a dark, deep basin on either side of the face.

   My body trembled. My teeth chattered. Yet, I stood there. Watching. Just watching. The monster’s scalded arms shot out from under the water and seized my ankles. For a minute I did nothing and only stood there with my eyes squeezed shut. Then I pried open my eyes and started kicking my legs against the creature. I dashed against its face and arms, crushed them with my boots until chunks of flesh soiled the hem of my pants and the water turned red from brown. Then, I wrenched myself free, spun round, and sprinted back the same way I had come from.

   I ran and ran, dripping in sweat, huffing till my throat cramped, glancing at the spangled sky stretching above me, and reached home through the short cut I should have started taking long ago. I flung myself on the bed. Sleep stole over me almost immediately, and then, I conjecture, I fainted in my sleep, for the next day I awoke at twelve o'clock in the noon. I forgot to wash my face, or brush my teeth, just straightened my overcoat, and hurried out of my house to run towards the same alleyway and the same puddle that were the perpetrators of my delirium. Why I ran towards them? I cannot say, for my mind pulled my legs towards it of its own volition.

   I limped and skittered, hobbled and lurched, as the mild sunlight of late January poulticed my trembling veins, and finally arrived at the origin of my torment. The puddle stood barricaded from all around by a yellow tape, policemen strewn about it, with several locals murmuring and thronging about the whole alleyway. The puddle had now shrunken to its original size, and the water under it no longer bubbled or boiled over; and a girl, not more than seventeen years old, lay coiled around its edge, dressed in a tattered, pink silk frock, with scalded arms and a face crushed so brutally it was barely recognizable. But I recognized her. I did. It was my daughter. Missing for months. Now withered and shrunken in size. Lying dead. As a swarm of houseflies droned over her decaying wounds.
