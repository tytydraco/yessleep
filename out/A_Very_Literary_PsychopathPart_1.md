**I haven't been able to read Russian novels since that day.**

Hey everyone. It’s taken me many sleepless nights to finally be able to face what I went through, years later. Actually, the reason I’m writing what you’re about to read is to admit to myself that I’ve finally moved past it. Because nothing screams "I’m over it" quite like facing your fear head-on and saying, "I’m not going to torture myself because of you anymore." This isn’t just a story about a toxic and obsessive partner, folks. No, it’s a story about how blind a young man can be when he’s in love. It’s a story that shows how a single person is enough to turn your life into either a dream or a nightmare. If you’re ready, and more importantly, if I’m ready, let’s begin.

When this happened, I was a sophomore in the literature department. I hadn't exactly chosen the major with enthusiasm. I loved reading, but I wasn't keen on becoming a literary expert. I had no ambition to earn my living dealing with novels. You know those people who go to university just to delay adulthood and put off real life? Yeah, I was one of them.

It was the end of my sophomore year. I had to give a presentation on 19th-century Russian novelists as a final project. And guess what I did? Yes, you guessed right. I left one of the most daunting topics in literature until the very last day. I was the one responsible for getting myself into this mess, but at the same time, I was eating myself up with panic. I was both angry at myself for leaving the assignment until the last minute and trying to find legitimate excuses for why I’d been so lazy until then. Finally, after endless internal monologues, I realized I didn't have a single minute to lose, packed my bag, and headed to the library.

Since I wasn't the only person in the world with an assignment due, all the places for individual study where students could work in silence were taken. All that was left was the noisy "common study area." Cursing under my breath, I went down from the upper floors to the ground floor. I found a spot, threw my bag down to claim it, and went to the vending machine. My brain couldn't work without sugar, after all. I grabbed a pack of salted crackers and a can of cola. I went back to my desk, opened my laptop, and double-clicked on my PowerPoint presentation titled "19th-Century Russian Novelists." Guys, how can a person not do any work at all? I hadn't written a single line. In front of me, those massive, heavy novels were staring at me with a judgmental expression. Turgenev’s *Fathers and Sons*, Tolstoy’s *Anna Karenina* and *War and Peace*, and most importantly, Dostoevsky’s *Crime and Punishment*. Whenever I hear the name of that goddamn *Crime and Punishment* today, I’m filled with the deepest sense of disgust. I put my head in my hands and said, "Even the death of Ivan Ilyich won’t be as tragic as my own tomorrow."

Then I heard Her voice. A voice as smooth as silk, soft, making one's insides flutter... "I wouldn’t be so sure," she said.

I turned to my side, and in that instant, Eros’s arrow pierced my heart. A world-class beauty with chestnut hair, emerald green eyes, and pearly teeth. She had claimed my heart right then and there. People talk about "love at first sight," and believe me, folks, it’s real. I wouldn't have believed it if I hadn't experienced it myself. My face turned bright red, and I started stuttering. "I’m sorry," I said, "Did I say something inappropriate?"

She laughed; she laughed at me with those pearly teeth. "Oh, not at all! I just saw you looking so troubled and wanted to tease you, maybe make you smile a little. If I said something inappropriate, I’m the one who should apologize."

Could it ever be inappropriate? Could an angel like that ever say something wrong? "It doesn't matter at all," I said. "I just left my assignment to the last minute and I was panicking a bit."

She laughed again and replied with the kind of quick wit that I didn't yet realize would cause me so much trouble later.

"What is this assignment? Tell me, so we can 'panic' together."

"I have to prepare a presentation on 19th-century Russian novelists. These are the books I’m going to use. But I don't know anything yet."

Those emerald green eyes sparkled with excitement, and she said, "Ah, my favorite!"

I was surprised. If finding a beautiful and knowledgeable study partner was a lottery, I had hit the jackpot. “Are you serious?” I asked.

“Of course! I could read until morning and never get bored! I love each and every one of them like my own children.”

“Aren’t you a bit young, fair lady, to be adopting these old men who died centuries ago as your own children?”

The phrase “fair lady” had slipped out before I could stop it. *Damn it,* I thought to myself, *I’ve put my foot in it. She’s going to think I’m a creep.* She didn't. She blushed and started to grin.

“Oh, you really are a cheeky one, aren't you! I’m exaggerating, obviously, get it? I just mean I love them all, I adore reading them. Tell me, which one are you working on right now? Gogol? Tolstoy? Dostoevsky?”

“Actually, all of them.”

“All of them?”

“Yes, all of them. The assignment title is '19th-Century Russian Novelists.' So, all of them.”

She paused for a moment and scratched her chin.

“I think I can help with that. It’ll be a nice way for me to remember why I love all these authors so much, too.”

“Really?”

“Of course, I’d be happy to!”
