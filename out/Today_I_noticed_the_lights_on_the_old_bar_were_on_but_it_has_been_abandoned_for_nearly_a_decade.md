In my town, at the very end of a winding, foresty road, sat an old bar. 

It was a converted farmhouse, once painted a shiny white but the exterior has seen better days. Years ago, when the bar first opened, a young, wealthy couple was looking for a place to sleep. The bartender and owner took pity on the couple and allowed them to stay in one of the empty guest rooms for the night. The couple paid him a handsome amount, and as the bartender/owner pocketed the money, he amused the idea of a bar, with a conveniently located motel above it. Eventually, business boomed--people driving home long days of work, travelers needing a quick shuteye, and many, many sex workers--enjoyed the convenience of their head of a decent pillow to nurse a nasty headache from indecent liquor.

Sex workers ran rampant in my town. The schooling rates were far lower than they should have been, and desperate teens needed a way to afford living--whether it was to go to college and get the hell out, or continue to live in the same place their whole life. 

One day, a sex worker went missing. The news anchor discussed the story for a mere moment before moving to the next piece of news. The following week, another sex worker. A short discussion of the cases on the news. The week after, four bodies of sex workers were found. A brief segment on serial murderers and the likely target that was sex workers. At the end of the month, the toll was at 19 sex workers. Many of them were young, white, Catholic girls. The news talked about them for a long time.

The publicity killed business, and subsequently, forced the owner to cease all operations. He was in the converted farmhouse when he suffered a heart attach that put him to rest. 

Many others came along with different business ventures for the farmhouse. Some tried to recreate the bar/hotel combination, but failed miserably. Others tried to turn it into a home by throwing out some fresh flowers and adding lacey drapes--but the home was too cold and the air too grim.

Finally, the people with big dreams stopped coming. Lowly drug addicts and runaway pregnant teens began frequenting the location, where they smoked pipes and talked in hushed tones. Homeless men began making it their home base. They would have intercourse on the front lawn sometimes, and made neighbors angry. They petitioned the city, who then had the home condemned and banned all trespassing.

The doors were locked, windows covered, and the front lawn brimming with weeds and tall grass. The light has never been turned on. In years. Maybe decades.

But as I was driving home last night, there it was. The unmistakable warmth of amber illuminating drawn shades. It was in every window. The porch light was on. I could have sworn the old rocking chair on the front porch blew against the whisper of cold, autumn wind. I was in shock as I parked in front of the big white home. I needed to see what was going on.

An avant garde exposition? A candelight concert? A housewarming party?

I clicked the ignition off and scrambled to remove my seatbelt, eager to see what was going on behind those big, blue doors. As I stepped onto the front porch and raised my hand to the brass knocker, a voice screamed in my head.

"Turn and run. Now."

Ice filled my veins and turned my skin skin cold. Tears pricked the very edges of my eyes. I turned and sprang off the porch, waded in the tall grass, and bolted for my car. I turned the ignition on and drove. As fast as I could. Wherever I could.

The voice echoed in my head. It gave such a strong command, damning me to challenge its authority. But, I can't shake what I saw in the cracks of the lacey drapes.

In the home, a group of grotesque figures stood in front of each window. Their beautiful faces were marred by decaying flesh and jagged scars. Some of their bodies were disturbingly angled. They all wore the tattered remnants of their skimpy dresses and fishnet tights.

I saw the dead bodies of the strippers that disappeared decades ago. So, why were they still covered in flesh?
