I don't know how to start this. I've been staring at my screen for twenty minutes trying to figure out where exactly things went wrong. Maybe it started the night of the accident. Maybe it started before that. I honestly don't know anymore.

My name doesn't matter. What matters is that three months ago, I had a perfect life.

Sara and I had been married for six years. Six quiet, boring, beautiful years. We lived in a small house on Denton Street. She worked at a school, I worked from home doing accounting stuff. We had a cat named Biscuit. We watched movies on Friday nights. We argued about who left the kitchen light on.

Normal. Everything was so normal.

Then came October 14th.

I was driving home from my cousin's place around 11 PM. Rain was coming down heavy that night. I remember the wipers going like crazy and the road looking like a black mirror.

I don't remember the exact moment it happened.

One second I was driving. Next second — a flash of white light, a sound like the world cracking open, and then nothing.

I woke up in the hospital. Sara was sitting right beside me, holding my hand so tight her knuckles were white. She had clearly been crying for hours. Her mascara was all the way down her cheeks.

"You're okay," she kept saying. "You're okay. You're okay."

I had a cracked rib, a gash on my forehead, and a concussion. The doctor said I was lucky to be alive. A truck had drifted into my lane. The car was totaled.

I spent two days in the hospital. Sara stayed both nights, sleeping in that horrible chair beside my bed.

She was the most caring person I had ever known. And I loved her more in those two days than I ever had before.

Things felt normal when I got home.

For about a week.

The first thing I noticed was the smell.

Sara always smelled like this vanilla body lotion. She'd been using it since before we even got married. It was one of those small things I loved about her. Whenever she walked into a room, there was this soft, warm smell.

About a week after I came home from the hospital, she walked past me in the hallway and I smelled something else.

Something cold. Like wet earth. Like a basement that hadn't been opened in years.

I didn't say anything. I thought maybe it was something she'd picked up outside. Maybe from the garden. I let it go.

But it kept happening.

The second thing was the way she slept.

I've slept next to Sara for six years. I know how she sleeps. She always curls up on her left side, facing away from me, with one hand tucked under the pillow. Always. Without fail.

About ten days after the accident, I woke up at 2 AM for no reason.

Sara was lying flat on her back. Completely straight. Arms at her sides. Eyes closed. She wasn't snoring, she wasn't shifting, she wasn't doing anything.

She looked like she was laid out in a coffin.

I told myself people sleep in weird positions sometimes. I rolled over and closed my eyes.

But the next night, I woke up at 2 AM again.

Same position. Flat. Still. Arms down.

I put my hand near her mouth to feel if she was breathing. She was. But just barely. Tiny little shallow breaths.

I didn't sleep the rest of that night.

I want to say something here, because I know how this sounds.

I was not going crazy. I had no history of mental illness. Yes, I had a concussion, but the doctors had cleared me completely. I was not imagining things. I know what I saw.

The third thing was the photographs.

Sara loves photos. Our house is full of them. Framed pictures on every wall — our wedding, our holidays, random Tuesday afternoons she found beautiful enough to capture. She's always been like that.

One evening I was dusting the shelves in the living room and I stopped in front of our wedding photo.

It's a big one, right above the fireplace. The two of us, outside the church, me in my grey suit, her in her white dress, laughing at something one of the guests had said.

I looked at it for a long time.

Then I felt my stomach drop.

In the photo, my arm is around Sara's shoulder. Her hand is up near her face, laughing. Everything normal.

Except.

Sara's shadow on the ground behind her was pointing in the wrong direction.

My shadow stretched behind me, away from the sun. Normal.

Her shadow pointed toward the sun. Toward the light.

Shadows don't do that. Shadows can't do that.

I took the photo off the wall. I stared at it for a long time under the kitchen light. I told myself it was a camera trick, a lighting thing, something with the way the photo was printed.

I put it back on the wall and I never looked at it again directly. But sometimes, from across the room, I could feel it. Like it was wrong in a way I couldn't explain with words.

I started watching her.

Not in a crazy way. Just paying attention.

She never ate much. I noticed that. She used to love food — she could finish a whole pizza by herself on bad days. Now she moved food around her plate. She drank water but I never once saw her actually swallow a bite.

She stopped going to bed before me. She used to fall asleep on the couch by 10 PM. Now she sat in the chair in the corner of the bedroom, just waiting, until I got into bed. Then she would lie down.

Her phone. Sara used to be on her phone constantly. Texting her sister, scrolling through cooking videos. One day I picked up her phone while she was in the shower.

No messages. No call history. No app usage.

Like the phone had never been touched.

I called her sister, Meera, from my work phone.

"Hey," I said, trying to sound casual. "How are you? Have you talked to Sara lately?"

There was a pause on the other end.

"What do you mean?" Meera said slowly.

"I just mean recently. Like this week."

Another pause. Longer this time.

"Are you okay?" Meera asked. Her voice sounded strange. Tight.

"I'm fine. Why?"

"Sara hasn't — " She stopped. "Can you come over? Can you come here right now?"

I told Sara I was going to the pharmacy. She smiled at me from the couch. That warm smile I had fallen in love with.

"Don't be long," she said.

Meera opened the door before I even knocked. Her eyes were red. She looked like she hadn't slept.

She sat me down at her kitchen table and she put her hands flat on the table and she looked at me.

"Sara's been calling me," she said. "Every day. Twice a day sometimes."

I stared at her. "Okay—"

"She's been calling me from a number I don't recognize. Not her number." Meera's voice was barely above a whisper now. "She keeps saying the same thing every time."

I didn't want to ask. I asked anyway.

"What does she say?"

Meera's eyes filled with tears.

"She says he survived the accident but I didn't. Please tell him I love him. Please tell him to look at who came home."

I drove home in a state I cannot describe.

I walked through the front door.

The woman on the couch looked up and smiled at me.

"You're back," she said.

I looked at her face. The same face. The same eyes. The same smile I had kissed a thousand times.

"Who are you?" I whispered.

She tilted her head slightly. Like she was confused by the question.

Then she smiled again. Wider this time.

"I'm your wife," she said softly.

And somewhere in the house, I heard Biscuit, our cat, let out a long and terrible scream from under the bed.

He had been hiding there for three weeks.

He never hid from Sara.

Not once in six years.

I'm writing this from Meera's house. I haven't gone back.

The thing in my house still calls me. From Sara's number. Every night at 2 AM.

I never answer.

But last night it left a voicemail.

I played it this morning with shaking hands.

It was Sara's voice. My real Sara's voice. Crying.

She said only four words.

"Don't let it stay."

I don't know what came home with me from that accident.

But it is still in my house. Sleeping in my bed. Feeding my cat, who screams every time it comes near.

And I don't know how to make it leave.

If anyone has experienced anything like this, please. I need help.

I will try to update when I can. Please stay safe. Hug the people you love. And if something feels wrong — even something small, even something you can't explain — trust that feeling.

Trust it.
