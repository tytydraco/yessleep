It is disturbing how normal things felt—seconds before my husband and three-month-old daughter were taken.

I had just opened the nursery window, and sunlight spilled through the curtains. Dust hung in the air like strands of silk. Birds chirped in the distance. 

I stood in front of the dresser mirror, watching my baby girl wake up in my arms. She wiggled, then fluttered her eyelids and blinked awake. As her eyes focused up at me, she smiled.

I laughed. “Good morning, sleepy girl!”

She studied my lips, and her little eyebrows furrowed in concentration. Then her mouth twisted, and she let out a small coo. I kissed her forehead, and her smile brightened. Then I glanced up at the mirror. 

My reflection didn’t. 

Its eyes remained on my baby. 

My heart skipped a beat. I backed away. When I saw my reflection follow, I forced out a laugh. Sleep deprivation must have been hitting. Hard. 

I turned and lowered my baby into her bassinet. But when I turned back to the mirror… *my reflection had disappeared.* 

And to the right, the door rocked slightly on its hinges. I darted to the door. Locked it. *Had I gone crazy?* 

I stepped in front of the mirror. Everything else was there. Oak furniture. Pink walls. White curtains. 

Everything but… me.

I tapped a fingernail against the mirror. It felt solid. I waved my arms around, then stepped to the side to try a different angle. Nothing. I paced to the other side. But no matter where I stood, I was invisible. I glanced back at the door. Could my reflection be…

*No.* I refused to let my mind go there. Reflections don’t just crawl out and walk around the house. That’s insane. 

However—

If I was wrong, if something bad happened and I *could* have stopped it—but I didn’t? I would never forgive myself. 

I had to get my baby out of this house. 

I rushed over to the bassinet, lifted out my daughter, and held her to my chest. Then I unlocked the door and peeked out. 

The hall was empty. I stood still. Listened. Besides the tick of the downstairs living room clock, the house was quiet. 

I paced to the end of the hall and glanced down the steps. All clear. 

I descended. 

On the wall of the landing there was a mirror, and as we passed I gave it a glance. My baby appeared to float through thin air. My stomach twisted. It wasn’t only the nursery mirror that was malfunctioning. 

I stepped off the landing, peeled open the garage door, stepped out, and closed it behind me. 

Instead of loading her in her car seat, I would drive down the street first—then load her in properly. I opened the driver door and slid in. My baby stirred against my chest and let out a little scream. 

“I know, baby. *Shhh. Shhh.*” I started the engine. 

Then I reached up to the visor and hit the garage clicker. The garage door hummed open. I pressed my foot on the brake. Placed my hand on the gear shift—

And something cold and sharp slid across my throat. *A knife.*

Behind me, someone said, “Turn it off.” A woman’s voice. 

My daughter kicked and wailed. Slowly—carefully—I lifted my trembling hand off the gear shifter, brought it to the ignition, and turned the key. The engine quieted. 

“Please,” I said. “Don’t hurt my baby—”

“You need to listen to me,” she said. “Because I’m only going to say this once. If you don’t do every single thing I say, I’m going to cut your throat first—and then I’m going to cut hers. Is that understood?”  

“Yes.”

“Okay. Put the baby down. In the passenger seat.” 

Carefully, I laid her in the seat. She was crying so hard now her face had turned a dark red. *Please, God,* I prayed. *Don’t let this be the last time I see her.* 

“Now get out of the car,” she said. “I am right behind you.” I stepped out, and the knife left my throat for a brief second before sliding right back. Then she said, “Back inside.”

I paced to the door, opened it, and stepped in. My daughter’s sobs echoed inside the garage until the garage door was slammed shut. 

“Alright,” she said. “Upstairs.”

I paused. That must’ve been where she thought we kept the money. I cleared my throat. “We don’t keep our money up there. It’s down—”

“Stop. I know where the money is. I don’t want money. Now *go*.” I climbed the stairs, one step at a time, feeling a new sense of panic spread through my entire body. Her saying she didn’t want our money was deeply disturbing. If she didn’t want money, what else could she possibly want?

When we reached the top step, she said, “Nursery.” I trudged down the hall and entered. Then she shoved me in front of the dresser. And when she did, I peered into the mirror, but was only one person. 

Me. 

I held a knife to the throat of someone invisible.

“Okay,” this other version of me said. “Go on.”

“I… don’t understand.”

“Get in the mirror.”

“What?”

The other me narrowed her eyes, like she was studying me. Then her face seemed to soften. “It’s easier than you think. Just climb on the dresser—and crawl forward.”

“What’s ‘*easy*’?”

“You and I—we’re…” she trailed off, then said, “If you haven’t figured it out yet, there’s no sense in explaining it now. You should just get it over with. Come on.”

She pushed on my back and helped me onto the dresser. The surface was so small, my face practically touched the mirror. My breath even fogged the glass. And yet—the only visible reflection was the one behind me, holding the knife. “Okay,” she said. “Go on.” 

I pushed, headfirst, into the glass. But instead of hitting it, I sank through and tumbled off the other side. I braced for an impact—

But it never came. 

I blinked. 

I was standing in front of the mirror, staring at… myself. Then—*my reflection* raised its arm. 

I raised mine. 

My reflection waved. 

So did I.

Then it smiled. The muscles in my face twitched upward. I tried relaxing my face to fight against it, but shards of pain singed around my mouth. Then I tried moving my own arms and legs, but an overwhelming pain surged through those muscles which forced me to stop. Panic filled my chest. No matter how hard I tried, I couldn’t control my own body. 

Then my reflection opened her mouth, and my own jaw tore open and mouthed along as it said, “You had your turn. Now it’s mine.”

It headed for the door. My body copied it on my side of the mirror in perfect symmetry. The split second it left the room—all the tension in my body relaxed. I looked around the nursery, freely. It now seemed I could control my body, but still, something felt…wrong. I peeked down.

I saw nothing but the floor. 

My body was gone.

\*\*\*

Several months have passed. 

This morning, my reflection left her phone in front of a mirror. I tapped into it to document this hell I’m in.

All day, I float around in the world behind the mirror. Everything here is backwards. Twisted. Warped. I only exist when my reflection steps in front of a reflective surface, and it is only then that I catch glimpses of my real life being lived by this other me. 

And sadly, even though she’s terrible at being me, she has everyone fooled. Well—almost everyone. 

Whenever my husband gets home from work, he kisses her at the door like he always has. When our friends come over, they talk and joke and laugh with that *thing* like it’s the same old me.

But not my baby. 

Sometimes my reflection comes into the nursery with her. She cries constantly. She refuses to breastfeed from her. She doesn’t even like to be held by her.

Somehow…she knows. 

And when my reflection has had enough, and her patience has worn thin—she’ll pick up my baby and carry her up to the mirror, and only then does she stop crying. 

She looks up, her eyes wet with tears, and reaches her tiny hand up to touch the glass. And, every time, it shatters my heart. Because she wants to be with me, and I want nothing more than to be with her. 

But I can’t. I can’t speak to her. I can’t touch her. I can’t hold her.  

I can only stand behind the mirror.
