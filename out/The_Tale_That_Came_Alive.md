I’m Hanna, 35. Today scared the living shit out of me. I’m trying to write this clearly, but I’m scared. I don’t know how to describe this. It’s not fake—the children witnessed it and are now in for questioning by the police

.

For context, I work at (said school) and have since it opened. On this day, we had staff shortages, but it doesn’t really matter. We are a school in a rural area, so there aren’t many children who go to (said school).

The ones in my classroom were between five and ten years of age. We had exactly twenty-nine children. Sorry for stalling—let’s get to the point.

“Sit close, children... a little closer... now where were we?” said Mrs Reese.

“It’s reading time!” a girl, 7f, shouted

.

At the time, I assumed it was the old tale again, so I said, “It’s not the old tale again, is it?”

“The one about the teenage children,” somebody whispered from the back. I am unsure who did.

“The one about the girl and boy,” said Gabriel, 8m.

“Once upon a time—” Mrs Reese said before getting interrupted by Gabriel. He is smart but interrupts quite often.

“A very long time ago,” Gabriel, 8m, said.

I shushed him—then I heard it.

Thud. Thud. Thud.

I can only describe it as banging your feet on the ground or possibly marching. I knew for certain it was getting closer. It was very unusual, so I frowned.

Then a girl’s voice echoed: “Theodore, no—no, you can’t—you can’t fight against the scandies.”

“I must,” a boy said.

By this point, I was getting freaked out, and I could tell Mrs Reese was too.

Then Gabriel screamed.

“Shush, it’s just a story, Gabriel,” I said, trying to comfort him and ground myself as it was getting too real to be a story.

Then a girl corrected: it was a tale.

I looked around, trying to do a head count, and my eyes locked onto this girl who was very skinny. Her clothes were baggy and worn, and she had no shoes.

I looked at Mrs Reese, and she said, “Who are you? Put your shoes on.”

The mystery girl then said, “Shoes are gone. We couldn’t afford them…” Then she vanished within a blink.

I kept rubbing my eyes over and over. Everyone was in shock.

I managed to call, “Head count!”

Mrs Reese was gone.

Gabriel started coughing because it was dusty in there. I’m sure I muttered it out loud.

Then I noticed we weren’t in the classroom anymore.

We were in a church.

It was an old church for sure. It was lit by only candles. Then my stomach dropped..

“Hello again… I’m Mary,” said the mystery girl.

Now looking at her, she looked no more than 15f. She was holding a sword up to me and the children.

Gabriel, 8m, said, “This isn’t our classroom, is it?”

“It’s a church,” Mya, 10f, added.

“Originally Greek,” George, 9m, said, far too excited.

Then Mary snapped.

“Who are you?”

She rose her sword as the kids hid behind me.

“Mary,” I said carefully. Then I told her we weren’t going to harm anyone.

She then threatened to scream for the others. I don’t know what she meant by that.

I said we were part of the British.

“British? You must be fools. Who sent you? Was it the scandies? Or the Williams? Quintessentials?”

Then Mya, 10f, mentioned it’s too dark and asked to turn on a light—and you should have seen Mary’s face. It was pure confusion.

Soon we were in their native clothes. They were really baggy on us, and Mary called us giants.

“What year are you from?” asked Mary.

“Twenty-twenty-three,” I said.

Mary had gone quiet, which scared me. Then she went to talk, but a low hum was heard in the air.

A thud. Thud. Thud.

Like distant drums. My chest tightened.

Back at school, Mrs Reese’s face went pale.

“Only twenty-five…”

 she kept counting over and over.
