When I was younger, me and my father lived in the outskirts of a gloomy town in South-eastern India. Well, for the locals we seemed like aliens - Thanks to us being white. The thing is, we were one of the families that decided to stay in India post their independence. That being said, my father was a well known science teacher in the local convent school. My early life was quite a blur, except the part where I hung out with other native kids in my locality. It truly felt like a family, wherein their mothers fed me too, and I could visit any of their houses at anytime.

There, I had a friend named Thomas. Apparently I did. It seems that as a kid, I couldn't grasp the concept of a native child carrying an English name. But I grew fond of him quickly. We used to play when we were five or six years old, my father recollects. Throwing sand at each other, swimming in the temple pond nearby, walking through the paddy fields to play hide and seek with the kids who were more closer to the town than we were.  
  
But see, that's the problem. *I don't remember any Thomas at all.*

Having this talk with my father did not help. And my mother, she left us when I was a kid. My father almost never talks about her, but my grandfather told me that she went insane at some point. And I could never follow that thread, as life slowly hardened as I grew older. When I was 17, I had to move away to Delhi, far far away from my father and grandfather to make a living. My father was getting progressively sick, and my grandfather's farm was not yielding enough for the three of us. Well, now I am a shop owner. I'm 27, married and living happily. My dad, grandfather and wife are with me now, living in South Delhi.

On a random weekend, my grandfather bought him up. "Do you remember that *natkhat* back home?" He chuckled, putting his newspaper down. Natkhat means "Naughty" in Hindi. I assumed he meant one of those young teens who often did their shady business in the forest near his farm. After seeing the puzzled look on my face, he clarified. "Thomas? the electrician's son?" he sat up stiff, clearing his throat. His eyes searched mine, with hope and optimism; it was as if he was glad I couldn't remember him.

"What?" I exclaimed. The moment I heard that name, I felt something itch my brain. Not the feeling of similarity. Not how it feels when your brain taps into your memory to recollect a certain memory. If anything, it felt quite the opposite. The harder I tried to search, the deeper it felt. I didn't think much of it. Around a week later, I got to know that one of my childhood friends from my hometown was getting married. And upon talking to him, he recollected Thomas. "Him?" he squeaked. "I heard his parents died in some fire. Its been twenty years since then, and I don't seem to have much to work with after that." 

I set my phone down to process this information. Then I walked up to my grandfather and told him the news. He sat up vigorously, and shook his head. Then I saw it on his face. The similar feeling of somehow gripping a memory hard as it sinks into oblivion. "Thomas? the plumber's son?" he shook his head again. This didn't sit well with me as I scrunched my face in disbelief. "The electrician's son you mean?" I inquired. My grandfather bent his lips in a frown. "I can't seem to recollect." He shrugged. I took a deep sigh, as I heard my dad's voice boom from the other room. "Thomas must be that Indian child, born to Xavier, right?" he explained. I felt a creaking in the other room, as he stood up from his rocking chair and walked into our room.  
  
"Jonas' father, Xavier was an electrician. He worked in our house, and that's how me and mom introduced him to you." he stood near the door frame, smiling. I sighed, and it immediately clicked. Charles? Wasn't he Thomas? It was quite bizarre. It was as if someone, or something is consciously letting us discover his current existence. But if I had to figure this out, there was only one way. To go back to my hometown a few days before the marriage. That'll give me enough time to figure it out. But I didn't really discuss it with either of them. They'll ask me not to worry about him and tell me that this is unnecessary. But for me, my hometown holds a ton of cherished memories. It isn't that bad if I had to look into one to remind myself of my joyous time there.

"You will take care of the shop. And..." I smile as I caress Lydia's hair. "Make sure to take care of our baby. Take enough rest and eat well, okay?" I exclaimed. She gently nodded as she sat down on the chair, straining. "Our child is due for delivery anytime. Are you sure you want to go?" she smiled painfully. I sighed, giving her a slight smile. I patted her and stood up, swiftly walking away outside. I boarded a train, and found myself back to my hometown's local station. Upon getting down, I visited our old home, which was now let out to a nuclear family of four, with twin boys. They were kind enough to let me stay till the end of this trip in a room that faces directly to our farm. 

As soon as I reached there, I could sense it. Maybe I knew this Thomas, and maybe I could find him in the town, strolling around. He might look differently, but he will respond to his name being called. After a good three days of asking around, contacting the town's elderly and my grandfather's friends, I looped all the information to my family, discussing what's true and what isn't. It was puzzling how there were a lot of people who gave me quite the weird look, before telling me clues about him that were thoroughly inconsistent. One of them stopped me and asked me if I was Abigail's son; to which I nodded. She immediately closed her door on me. It was unsettling, but it made sense. My mom probably had her psychotic episodes, and that must've startled a few people in the town. I didn't make much of it. 

With all the details I gathered, I put together all the consistent information and derived his location to be a certain house near the paddy fields, behind the tree line. I convinced myself that IF Thomas were to be around, he must be hiding there, living off of nearby farms. Then I stood up, dedicated to meeting him. That night, I couldn't put myself to sleep. I racked my brain hard. Some blurry glimpses of my time with him slowly floated into consciousness. I remembered his curly hair, his toothy smile. I remember fighting with him about a toy when I was 9. But slowly and surely, I couldn't make sense of it all again. I checked my clock and it was 1 AM already, and I had to go sleep now in order to wake up soon. I figured it'd be safe if I visited him in the daylight.

The next day, I borrowed my tenant's bicycle to get to the house. They were too nice to let me use it, even though I clearly told them it was a decade since I touched one; that made them laugh. His house was somehow buried deep within pockets of trees, with paddy fields covering the area upto his home. I slowly started remembering the front lawn, where we played tag. Maybe I knew this kid. And then - I saw the house. It was quite large. Two stories with a small front lawn, with probably a dozen rooms. It was covered with vines and moss, and seemed abandoned. The wood on the build was still half burnt from the accident fifteen years ago. Maybe he burned to death along with them. Or maybe I could find any clue here if he hadn't. Anyways, I had to put an end to this. I parked my bicycle and walked towards the house, calling out for his name. His name. What was it again? Thomas. I tried to hold the word dearly to my heart, and kept yelling it so that I wouldn't forget it. Or him. Or even why I was here. I could slowly feel the adrenaline rushing through my body and the forest getting quiet.

As I got closer, there was a soothing pulse I felt. It was quiet, as if it weren't a sound I was hearing but a vibration I was feeling. I slowly pushed the front door, as it slowly and painfully creaked open. The house was dark. Not dark as in, the absence of light; It was as if the house was actively swallowing light entering it. I stepped in, yelling his name. And then I heard it. A slow painful groan from the basement. It felt like a blandly put out sound, like if the person was wounded but too wounded to scream. Monotone, and lifeless, yet full of suffering and longing. I remained alert as I slowly stepped into the basement. But as I got down the stairs, neither torch light nor my phone could light it up. The basement was pitch black, and I placed my hand on the left wall as I traced around the basement. A wet stench filled the air, as it got denser and colder. I couldn't hold my breath and comforted myself by confronting the nasty smell. Then I found what seemed like a lamp switch. I flicked it on, as someone groaned loudly in the room with me, sending chills up my spine. I could hear my heart pound in my ear as I held the table near me to balance myself. The dim light quickly illuminated the room, making me rub my eyes.

There he was. In rags, a young man with a long beard. He was skinny, almost too tall. His skin was pale and dusty, with dried up lips and barely functional eyes. At parts, his skin had open wounds, and I was at some instant, pretty sure I saw atleast one or two of his internal organs. It seemed terribly hurting. His hands and legs had overgrown nails, and as he stumbled on the hard wooden floor, reacting to the light hit his body his nails scrapped all over the wood; it made my teeth chatter as I yelled at him. "Thomas! I am here to help." That was when I saw him. It was the same Thomas, the dusky Indian with curly hair. Thomas Xavier. 

In almost what must've been two blinks of an eye, he stood up. Hunching over, he asked as his raspy voice echoed all around the room. "You remember, don't you Ellis?" he asked, almost painfully. It was as if every word he said costed him pain all over his body. His ragged breath confirmed it further. I wasn't even back to senses. I would've just stumbled backwards if I didn't hold the table. I could even hear my sweat tap on the wooden floor, snapping me back to what I was doing. I was confronting a mentally unstable man. "They forget, Ellis. They forget my name, my house. They act like... like I don't exist." The pale man sobbed, as his voice distorted. It was as if he was finding the right pitch to speak in. Like it has been a long time since he spoke. "I can save you, Thomas. Please... just listen to me carefully. O- Okay?" my voice stumbled, trying to console him. He seemed unfazed. Whatever I said was just for my own comfort, as his face turned more determine every second. He slowly stopped sobbing as he turned to me. His face slowly twisting into a grotesque smile. My heart rate shot up as I released the table from my grip, standing straight and erect.

"Do you remember Abigail?" He sniffled, slowly stepping forward. "Stay back, Thomas." I warned, as I slowly stumbled backward. I made sure to never let him out of my sight. "She was supposed to be the forgotten one. She was the rot. The infected. That witch..." his voice lashed out and echoed from the deep walls of the basement. It made me realize how spacious the basement actually was. However, I took a second to process what he said. "What?" I exclaimed. 

He slowly knelt, sobbing quietly. It almost felt human. Atleast, whatever that was left in him.  
"She always would play with me and you... and she took good care of me." His knee bones scratched the wood floor as he rested. His hands planted against his head, he continued. "One day... she disappeared. I missed her. More than my mother..." his voice grew louder. He was right. My mother slowly was locked away from me. I still remember nights where I tried to so hard to grip onto her memory but I couldn't. Just like, whatever his name was... Thomas. I comforted myself as he continued. My fingers slowly traced the contents of the table nearby as I gripped some tool. I couldn't however see what it was, as I cannot afford to take my eyes away from him. "My parents told me that she was a bad woman, and she was taken away..." He took a brief pause, with a deep breath. "Everyone started forgetting about her. But I? I remembered..." He moaned as I heard a snap. He was twisting his hands, as if preparing for something. "I slowly started forgetting, Ellis." He wept. "My school, my parents' names... everything except her. It was like she was calling me, Ellis. She contaminated me." He took a deep breath and went quiet. Almost eerily quiet. The room suddenly felt devoid of air.

"Stay down." I yelled authoritatively. "I found her." he exclaimed, his voice suddenly distorting to a higher pitch gradually as he said the sentence. It was almost child-like. "Don't you want to see her, Ellis? Don't you?" He slowly tried standing up as I screamed at him. Out of the sheer fear, I couldn't materialize words out of my mouth. I shrieked at him, ordering him to stay put. That was when he turned to his right and started laughing. It caught me so off-guard that I felt my heart jump to my throat. I staggered backwards as I landed my back on the farther wall, away from the stairs. If I had to make a run to the stairs, I had to run past him. And I realized what he was laughing at, as I heard a snap onto my left. 

I threw the light of my torchlight to his right, and to my shock, there she was. A skeleton, frail and tiny, chained to wall and bounded by shackles connecting her to the corners of the end of the room. Thomas' parents and mine had put her here, when they realized that she was contaminated. Perhaps when the folks around her started forgetting her. It was as if they wanted her to stay forgotten. And that is why they burnt the home, and fled. In hopes of somehow keeping this contamination in this basement... Far away from human contact. But how did he manage to get stuck here? He... He? Thomas? I turned my torchlight swiftly back around to see him not around. My heart pounded rapidly and heavily, making my limbs shoot up. I could feel the adrenaline travelling in me as I got aware of the situation I am in.

I paced back to the stairs. The darkness. The stench. The dense air. Everything tried pulling me back. Almost as if they wanted me to get sucked back. And that was when I heard bones snapping in the dark. I didn't turn, clenching the tool, which seemed to be the hammer. I landed my first step on the stair. A loud thud followed - what felt like something heavy, wet, slopping around, thudding against the wooden floor. Something that had impossible amount of legs galloping towards the stairs. As I paced up the stairs, a limb gripped me. I lost the tool in front of me. Somewhere. I couldn't see. I couldn't tell if it was a hand or a leg, thanks to an unrealistic number of fingers holding me down. I tried my best to fight it, as a voice, distorting up and down, booming from everywhere, asked me - "Do you remember? Our first fight, Ellis?" I screamed in agony. I didn't want to turn back. But I knew that whatever was behind me wasn't Thomas. It wasn't human. "Hold onto your memories tighter." it exclaimed, mimicking my father's voice, screaming extremely loud. If I had to escape, I had to turn and fight it back. I wanted to go back home. To Lydia. To our child. So I did.

I turned back. The room had engulfed the light remaining, but a sliver of light from upstairs illuminated a tiny amount of what it could in the room. A hand, made of withering flesh gripped me. It had all kinds of fingers all over the place, with extremely long nails, curled away. It made me want to puke up. I kicked the hand violently as it pulled me down the stairs. I immediately held the railing with one hand and the corner of the wall with another. I fought it relentlessly. Minutes, of what felt like hours. At some point, it let me go. I knew what to do. Picking myself up without a second to spare, I tumbled upstairs, looking out for a gas cylinder. I found one eventually, popped it open, and tumbled it down into the abyss. As the gas filled the underground, I heard something scream in agony. As if it knew what was about to happen. But somehow, it also sounded at peace. It sounded young, but old. So monotone, yet soulful. It knew that its time was up. I flicked my lighter up, and in that moment, the door of the basement moved to let light in. That was when I saw it. A long face, pale. Nothing in place of where its eyes should've been. Oddly, despite the screaming, it had an unnaturally wide, painful and vivid toothy grin. Like as if it had fulfilled its purpose. And that was when I knew it. I flung my lighter deep into the basement as I rushed out of the house, jumping out into the grassy lawn as the house burst in a loud explosion, almost deafening me. 

After what seemed like an hour or two, I picked myself up. Slowly but surely, I crawled and walked upto the bicycle. I didn't even turn back. I biked back to my house. On the way, I saw the sunflowers. I had passed by these on the way. But when I turned into the road going towards the town. I shook my head. I turned the other way and drove back to my home. The tenant greeted me, with confusion all over his face. "We thought the bike had been stolen..." he laughed, scratching his head. But I couldn't reply. I rushed back into the home, and sat down in my room and am writing this. I do not know what to do.

I've searched up every corner of the internet and am unable to find any entity related to the one I came across today. Please tell me if you know about this creature... every passing moment makes me forget about it more and more. I just want to make sure I'm completely safe, before I go back home to my family. My father and my grandfather; my wife, Linda.
