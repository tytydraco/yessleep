A few hours before the news became plastered across every TV screen in Missouri, I got the call. A man named Ajax rasped through my office phone. Said he was with the FBI. Claimed I’d be well compensated for my *expertise* at a developing crime scene out in the Ozarks. 

I cut him off there.

“I’m sorry *Ajax* from *the FBI,* let me see if I got this right: seven hundred an hour for my *“expertise”*? In anthropology? Seriously? Give me a fucking break.” I slammed the phone down. Figured they were just some bitter student channeling their pent-up frustration over a failing grade into a very strange, very bad prank. 

Then, the story broke. 

Every station in a hundred-mile radius was scrambling to report the grisly details. Articles flooded my feed. Kids whispered about it in the halls. There was a new serial killer to sate our morbid fixations, and by the looks of it, they’d been busy. 

It was the largest single-day discovery of unidentified remains in Missouri’s history. Not bodies, mind you - *remains*. 

No bones, no blood, no viscera. 

Just skin. 

I raced back to my office, praying I hadn’t squandered the opportunity. It wasn’t just about the money. My area of study was the history of the occult; if they were calling me, something truly bizarre had transpired out in the deep woods. Whatever it was, I wanted to see it. 

I wanted that more than anything.

Ajax answered on the first ring. They hadn’t found anyone else to fill my niche in the meantime. I asked him what I’d need to do, and he provided an address. 

I canceled my afternoon classes and got on the road. 

The drive was torture. Minutes ticked down on my GPS with a slowness that felt almost vindictive. The sun was low and gold when I finally pulled off the interstate. Smooth asphalt abruptly transitioned to craggy dirt. My sedan jostled in and out of potholes. I hoped my suspension would hold. If it broke, God only knows if I’d be able to get help out there - the only building I passed on the way to the crime scene was a small gas station with a single rusted pump. 

Parked police cruisers began dotting the roadside. The white oaks flanking the road grew more menacing with each passing mile, twisted and overgrown, gnarled branches reaching out like giant arthritic fingers about to pluck me from the car. Despite the doom crawling under my skin, though, I couldn’t seem to turn back. Curiosity really is a stubborn god.  

Then, my GPS chirped a single word: *Arrived*. 

The forest peeled away to reveal a sprawling hillside swarming with police. A single paved footpath connected the dirt road to the two-story farmhouse at the top of the slope. The grounds were tidy and well-kept. The place itself had a *Little House on the Prairie* sort of rustic charm about it. 

*This* was the epicenter of some sort of ritualistic carnage? 

I pulled to a checkpoint, rolled down my window, and explained to the officer that I was an outside consultant. 

Ajax wasn’t what I expected. 

He was a tall, middle-aged man with wiry arms and a chest that dipped in slightly at its center. His slacks were wrinkled, and his coffee-stained polo was this odd shade of blue-gray, a glacial color that seemed paradoxically bright *and* dull at the same time. He looked innocuous. Toothless, even. But as he led me up the footpath, my assessment slowly started to change. Maybe it was the fact that he never seemed to blink. Or maybe it was something about his body language - every movement was rigid and precise, almost robotic. I’m not sure what set off my intuition, but the message was loud and clear: his appearance was a trick. Camouflage. A facade designed to disarm. I suppose the Ajax and the farmhouse were similar in that way: savage things hidden neatly beneath harmless exteriors. 

He paused under the shade of the farmhouse. Looked up at it for a moment, then bowed his head. 

“You squemish, Rob?” 

“…Not particularly, no.” 

A smile coiled along his jaw. He lifted his head. 

“Alright. Fair warning, though: if you feel that opinion changing in the next few minutes, I’d recommend staying clear of the backyard. That’s where the…uh…mess was. We’ve cleaned most of it, but still.” 

Up close, the house’s age became more apparent. Mint green paint flaked from the rafters. The windows were small and opaque, caked with thick layers of dust. Splintered floorboards audibly shifted as we walked across the porch. A trio of pale-faced individuals with *FORENSICS* printed on their jackets burst from the front door. They paced past us silently. Ajax caught the lip of the door and gestured for me to step inside…

Some vestiges of the house it used to be were still apparent. Part of the floor was tile. Another part was made from carpet. Ten feet up, jagged shards of wood jutted from the walls; all at the same height. The remnants of a second floor, maybe. 

On one side, there was a giant metal box. The top of it nearly touched the ceiling. The outside was smooth and featureless, save for the side that faced the mural, which had a large window. 

Ajax slapped a hand on my shoulder. 

“So here’s what you need to know. The gardener found the remains. He’s been maintaining the grounds for at least a decade, never met his employer, never got a whiff of anything suspicious transpiring within these walls.” He wrapped his arm around me and began pulling me forward.

It depicted a group of people lying in a field. They were naked. Androgynous. Mannequin-like, but with just enough detail to look distinctly alive. Their legs were broken backwards. The limbs curled over their spine, ankles contorting so that their heels rested on their shoulders. No faces were drawn, but each had a physical coin suspended where a face *should* be, hung with twine from nails in the wall. Each coin was the same - silver with the profile of a man who appeared too young to have done anything important enough to warrant being immortalized on currency.

“We’re assuming the mangled ones are their victims, and *that* could be a surrogate for the killer themselves,” he said, pointing towards the rafters. I followed his finger. “But, hey, what do we know? That’s why you’re here. The mural, the imagery, the flaying - it all feels ritualistic, though this *particular* flavor isn’t something we’ve encountered before, so we’re at a loss for motive.” 

Sweat dripped down my fingers. The metallic aroma of oil-based paint lingered around the mural, but otherwise, the air didn’t smell like anything. Why did that bother me? 

“Got any questions?” he asked. 

“How many…?” I choked out, eyes darting between the broken, wheel-like bodies.

There wasn’t much to see - the inside was dark. I squinted. There was something wrong with his reflection. Was it blurry? I moved closer. No, his reflection wasn’t blurry; there were two *separate* reflections. The second was smaller and was displaced from the first, shifted a few degrees clockwise. 

The realization hit me like a sucker punch.

“Wait…is that wall a mirror?” 

“Not just *that* wall - every wall. And the floor. And the ceiling. Every surface inside this box is a mirror, and this window is one-way. There’s a door on the opposite side, too. A door with a lock.” He bent over and flipped a small switch below the mirror. Dim backlights clicked on inside the box. The doubling of Ajax’s reflection became clearer. His expression was effortlessly stoic, save his eyes - they were just a little too wide. 

The box scared him.

“I’ve been inside. It’s completely soundproofed, all you can see is a faint reflection of yourself in every direction, and the glass? It’s scratched to hell and back.”

I knew what he was about to say.

I grabbed a legal pad, started the coffee pot, and sat at my desk. Ajax’s parting instructions were simple: *“Mull over what you saw and keep your mouth shut.”* So, that’s what I did. I kept replaying it all in my head. The farmhouse. The mural. The box. The coins. The wheels. The mirrors. Hours passed. Dawn arrived in what felt like a heartbeat. I never wrote anything down. Never poured a cup of coffee, either. I was too lost in thought, desperate to uncover some semblance of meaning within the macabre design, and I felt close to a revelation; something important was on the tip of my tongue. 

What the hell did it all remind me of? 

I spent the rest of the day in my study, tearing through my collection of musty, dog-eared textbooks. *Drawing Down the Moon* by Adler. *The Black Arts: A Concise History of Witchcraft* by Cavendish. *Greek and Roman Necromancy* by Ogden. Page after page after page - alchemy, tarot, numerology, hoodoo, psychometry, ritual sacrifice, black masses, thaumaturgy - none of it was *fucking* right. 

I leaped from my chair, threw my arms down, and swept the books off the side of my desk. A shuffling clamor erupted around me. I glared at the resulting pile, breathing heavy, heat pulsing across my skin, silence slowly creeping back into my empty home.

For the first time in years, the memories felt alive, and with that came a familiar magnetism. Knowing wasn’t enough anymore; I wanted to feel the preternatural. The garage was impenetrably dark, but I could hear it perfectly. The noise was coming from somewhere in the blackness. Just a few feet ahead. As I throttled down the dirt road, exhaustion began to take its toll. I couldn’t focus. My mind wandered. I thought about the strange dream. Beyond the visceral terror of it all, something nagged at me. The tall figure was holding rope. Why were they holding rope? 

Recognition battered me like an avalanche. 

I remembered what ritual the farmhouse reminded me of. 

The Rope Maiden. 

It's a Japanese urban legend. A young girl would be selected to spend their entire life in a windowless room, deprived of all stimulation. A blinding mask would be nailed into her skull. Her caretakers would not touch her, nor would they speak to her, only giving her sips of water and a few grains white rice when absolutely necessary. Then, once she was old enough, she’d be led to a shrine and sacrificed. Five ropes, five knots - one for each limb and one for her neck - all pulled in different directions until the poor girl was torn apart. The deprivation and the sacrifice was said to harness spiritual purity. It’s not a perfect fit, but maybe the giant metal box served a similar purpose…
