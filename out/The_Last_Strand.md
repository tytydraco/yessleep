I came up here because of the stories.

Seven years ago, the construction crane over the old industrial canal was abandoned mid-build. The company went bust, the site went quiet, and people started saying the ones who fell from it didn’t fall right. They didn’t scream on the way down. They didn’t splash. They just… stopped being people. The bodies that washed up downstream looked wrong, too still, faces smoothed out like river stones. Cops called it suicides. Locals called it something else.

I’m not the type who believes every ghost story. But I’m the type who checks. Explorer habit, maybe. Or just needing to see for myself if the crane really does whisper when the wind moves through its lattice. Tonight, the wind is low, almost polite. I parked my truck two blocks away, cut the fence with bolt cutters, climbed the service ladder in the dark. Thirty-two meters up, barefoot on the narrow maintenance catwalk because shoes make too much noise on metal grating.

The city lights smear into long wet streaks below. The canal doesn’t reflect anymore; it just drinks the orange sodium glow and keeps it down there in the black.

I thought I was alone.

I was wrong.

There is something crouched on the counterweight arm, maybe fifteen meters behind me, where the crane’s spine thickens into concrete ballast. It doesn’t have a silhouette so much as it borrows one and wears it badly, like clothes cut for someone taller, thinner, hungrier. The shape is patient. It doesn’t move closer. It simply lets more of itself become visible each time I blink.

My eyes are burning from the cold and the height.

When I finally blink, it’s closer.

Not walking. Not crawling. It simply occupies the space I just finished not looking at.

The railing I’m holding has grown warm. My palms leave perfect handprints in the rust, handprints that weren’t there moments ago, still hot enough to steam in the night air.

I realize the invitation wasn’t a whisper in my head.

It was carved into the crane itself: faded warning stickers half-peeled, graffiti that reads like coordinates for people who shouldn’t come back. The thing on the counterweight doesn’t borrow just any shape. It wears the ones who came before. The ones who “fell right.”

It’s no longer wearing a stranger’s outline. It’s wearing hers.

The woman who climbed up here six months ago, chasing the same legend I am. The one whose photo still circulates on the explorer forums, smiling in the thumbnail, missing in every update after. Her hair moves even though there’s almost no wind. Her mouth opens and the rasp comes out, low, wet, familiar from the coroner’s audio leak someone posted once. The sound of lungs giving up after too long without air.

Distance means nothing to it once you’ve noticed. I don’t want to turn around.

I turn around.

She’s standing at the base of the crane now, four stories below, looking up with eyes that don’t catch the city lights. Just two soft black pools that swallow the orange the same way the canal does.

She raises one hand. Not waving, pointing. Straight up. Straight at the spot directly behind my left shoulder.

I feel it before I see it.

Breath on the back of my neck. Not warm. Not cold. Just present. Like damp velvet pressed to skin.

I understand now.

The crane never wanted anyone to jump.

It wanted someone to stay perfectly still while it finished climbing inside through the opening it made.

My mouth opens to shout.

Nothing comes out except that same wet rasp.

And then, slowly, deliberately, the railing beneath my palms begins to bend inward.

Not because I’m pulling.

Because something is pulling from the other side.

I don’t jump.

I don’t have to.

Hands, too many hands, close around my ankles, my wrists, my throat. They feel like rust and concrete and old grief. They lift me forward, almost gentle, like helping someone over a threshold.

The last strand isn’t mine letting go.

It’s the crane exhaling.

I fall forward into arms that have been waiting seven years, since the ones who fell here didn’t fall right, to catch what was already promised.

The water doesn’t make a sound when I hit it.

It just opens its mouth and swallows the scream I never got to finish.

Somewhere far above, on the catwalk, the shape straightens up. It brushes rust from its palms. It wears my face now, better than I ever did.

And it waits for the next one who comes to check the legend.
