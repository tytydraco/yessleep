At a young age, we’re often told that there are no such things as monsters. Sure, there are, but there are never ghouls, devils, or anything else that goes bump in the night. These reassurances from my parents helped me with my nightmares, as they did with others, but there was always a feeling of dread that would still linger in my room at night. I remember one night when I woke up from a dream, only to see a faceless man next to me. I closed my eyes for a bit and opened them, but he would still be there, staring at me. I would do this for a few minutes, and he would remain there, only to disappear. I never knew what it was, nor did I ever think of it again.

We have all heard stories about something, like a boogieman in the closet, or a monster under the bed, and we would all laugh now, but I remember one boy in second grade, Darren, who told us that a woman would tap on his bedroom door. He had brown hair, a buzzcut, blue eyes, and white (You could tell from the clothes he wore: a blank black t-shirt and cargo shorts).

“She’s my best friend," he would say, “She’s there for me.”

As you can imagine, we didn’t believe him, but it intrigued me. Obviously, it could’ve been his mother, but who would wake their kid up in the middle of the night, let alone on a school night? It just didn’t make sense. One day at lunch, I sat at the table with him and his other friends, diagonally across from him. I heard them talk about a show they liked. It took me a little while before I could get his attention. I was a bit nervous, mainly because I didn’t want to sound like a maniac, but I managed to get the words out.

I began, “Um... I heard about the tapping from one of my friends. What happens after the tapping?”

Darren hesitated at first, but then, softly, he told me, “She’ll come in and talk to me. Ask me about my ‘troubles.’ She’s nice. You’d like her if you met her.”

“How come she only shows up when everyone’s sleeping?”

“I don’t know.”

“Maybe she’s like a vampire.” One kid quipped.

They started laughing. I chuckled slightly, to not feel alienated. I was only trying to make sense of what I heard, even though it didn’t make sense at all.

During recess, I went over to my friend Kayla and told her what Darren told me. She was just as confused as I was at the time.

“Yeah, they laughed,” I told her.

“At least they’re not laughing about getting cooties from us,” she said.

“Yeah,” I laughed.

“We could invite him to my birthday.”

Every year, Kayla would throw a slumber party in the community house of our apartment lot, like a lock-in party. They called it the *clubhouse*. She’d invite all her friends, mostly the girls. Her dad would get permission from the owners to use the space, and we’d spend the night watching movies, playing silly games, but we’d end up always playing Duck, Duck, Goose, telling each other jokes, and, inevitably, we’d all fall asleep. I was usually the first one to wake up in the morning. While I waited for everyone else, I would stare out the window. Watching the cars drive off and onto the Sagamore Bridge while the sun came up felt euphoric. I always get up early to see the pretty scene in front of me. Luckily, the building I lived in stood on a hill, giving me the perfect view. The view wasn’t the best from the clubhouse, but that didn’t matter to me. I just wanted to see the cars – the lights – move on and off the bridge, as the colors of the sky changed from a dusky red to a bright blue.

As I’ve mentioned, she’d never invited a boy to her party, but we were dying of curiosity. We wanted to know who this mystery woman was. We were desperate.

“Would your parents allow that?” I asked.

“Yeah, they might.” She answered.

I had already wished her a happy birthday that morning. Normally, if the birthday didn’t line up with the weekend properly, her parents would schedule their plans for the Saturday after (if it was Friday, it would’ve been the next day).

We went inside for art after recess. Kayla finished making her clay pot early, so she spent her time making an invitation for Darren. It wasn’t as professional-looking as the ones her parents made to give to the class, but it was enough to convey the message. The real invitations had a silly character on them: A Dog with glasses, a button-up shirt, blue jeans, and a red tie. Its eyes were beady and black, with a round snout, topped with a small, rectangular nose. Her dad was a graphic designer, and he would take the extra time to add little things like this to make his work pop. Last year, the character he made for the invitation was a cartoon girl with big oval eyes, a pink skirt, and a blue shirt. She wore a pink cap and small high heels. I thought she was pretty when I first saw her. I ended up dressing like her that night for the sleepover, much to her parents' surprise. I still have the invitation taped to the side of my mirror. I named her Tina.

After art was social studies. We got the results of our U.S. States test back. I got a B, Kayla got an A-, and it seemed like everyone else passed the test, too. Before the lesson, our teacher let Kayla pass out her invitations to some of our classmates. We knew they weren’t expecting Darren to receive the amateurishly made invitation. She didn’t have the proper skills to recreate the dog, so she drew a smiley face. He felt honored either way. I bet a part of him knew we invited him for the tapping lady.

I had trouble sleeping that night. If what Darren said was true, how would this mystery woman enter the party? Through the window? The front entrance? The Basement? (That wasn’t very likely; mine and Kayla’s parents slept down there).

My bed felt cold. The window was open, but it was April. It can get chilly, but not as cold as it felt then. Come to think of it, it felt rather nice.

I stared up at the ceiling for what felt like hours. What was a white ceiling during the day became a dark, soothing blue at night. I looked at my alarm clock: 4:13 am. I went to bed at 7:30. Girls had to get their beauty sleep. I figured I’d have slept enough. I got out of bed and looked out the window, past the trees, and at the bridge. The lights on the bridge were on. The Arches of the bridge to the Cape faded into the fog, and the cars passed to and fro. White lights in. Red lights out. The trees became black from green, and the sky was a deep shade of purple. I could see the stars, faintly twinkling in the sky. I could hear the birds chirping, maybe a dog barking, and then... some geese. They swiftly flew over my apartment building, honking freely as they went.

I heard the door open behind me. I quickly turned around and saw my dad.

“Princess,” he said, in a hushed voice, “What are you doing up so early?”

I turned to look back at the bridge. He crouched down behind me and saw the cars on the bridge going in and out of the Cape.

“I know. It’s pretty,” He sighed. “Let’s get back to bed.”

I sat at the kitchen table, slurping down my bowl of Fruity Pebbles, excited for the party and whatever shenanigans we were gonna get ourselves into. I had the hiccups all morning, but they went away when Kayla came over. Her parents brought her over before each birthday event. Even if we knew I was being used as a distraction in a way, we didn’t care. We enjoyed each other’s company. We sat in the living room, watching whatever junk was on the TV that morning. My mom stood by to make sure we behaved, but I had a feeling she secretly liked the shows that came on.

As the characters danced on the screen, acting out the story of the week, Kayla said to me, “What if Darren is playing a prank on us?”

“Why would he do that?” I responded.

“I’m not sure. I mean, there’s no way this could be true.”

“You have already invited him. You can’t take back the invitation.”

“I guess so.” She replied.

We sat there, watching the characters on screen dance for an hour. We then went to the playground across the street for an hour. My mom drove us to an ice cream place near the beach, where we hung out for an hour. The sun wasn’t beating down on us, as it would before school let us free. I remember I had vanilla. Kayla had cookie dough. I had mint chocolate chip. We walked along the shore for a while. The waves splashed down beside us, and the seagulls squealed above us.

We got back to the apartment complex not long after and saw kids walk into the clubhouse. We didn’t see Darren; not yet anyway. We did see Sammy, our tomboy. She was really into basketball and played for the school team. She also had a level of sass to her that we wouldn’t dare replicate unless we wanted a smack upside the head from our mothers. Mom parked the car in our spot at home, and we walked to the clubhouse.

“Hey, Kayla,” We heard Sam’s snobby voice from across the room when we stepped inside.

“Hey, Sammy,” Kayla said back. I waved and smiled a little.

While the other kids from school were getting dropped off, our parents helped set up the snacks and other forms of entertainment. A piñata hung from the ceiling by a thin, white rope. Kayla and I watched the doors for Darren to show up. Just when we thought he would ditch us, we heard the door open, and the whole room looked towards him. He had his sleeping bag in hand and his bag with what I assumed to be his clothes for the next day. He walked over to the couch and sat down, his stuff beside him.

It all went as it does every year. The adults came out with an ice cream cake with her age for the candles. Sammy would be held back by the other girls to keep her from ruining it. We all took turns trying to hit the piñata, and just like last year, Sammy would knock it down. The night rolled around, and the adults went downstairs to let us sleep. They didn’t know it, but we weren’t gonna sleep, not yet.

We brought Darren to the center to explain to us the mystery woman he told us about. He ran through everything we knew.

“What’s her name?” I heard a voice from the circle say. It was Shelley, the class bookworm.

Darren paused for a moment. We were unsure if he ever got a name. He then opened his mouth.

“She goes by Ms. Penny.”

“What a stupid name,” said Sammy.

“It isn’t,” Darren shot back.

“Is she married to Mr. Dollar?” Asked Suzie, another classmate. There wasn’t much to say about her, but she was there.

Everyone started laughing quietly so as not to wake up the adults. Darren’s face turned red. Kayla and I were also giggling, until… *TAP. TAP. TAP*. Loud enough for it to stop us. It sounded like they were tapping with their fingernails. We heard it come from the other side of the room, but we didn’t know where. Was it the bathroom door? The front door? No, the front door was chained and locked so that no one could break in. The blinds were closed, and the windows were locked. We heard it again… *TAP. TAP. TAP*. It was the bathroom. We all looked at Darren. All seven of us watched as Darren walked over to the bathroom. He put his hand on the doorknob, but he didn’t know if he should open it or not. He glanced back at us, faced the door again, and opened it. Nothing. Pitch Black. We had a night light for those of us afraid of the dark, but it barely reached the other side of the hall. We watched the darkness, waiting for something. An evil old lady, maybe? Then, a red high heel. Then two red high heels, both connected to two fit and pale legs that went up into a red suit dress. She wore a blue button-up under the dress. It was faint, but we could see it sprout from her neck. She was slim, but she also had a prominent buttox. We looked up from her waist to her perky chest, then her neck, and we saw her face. Her face was smooth, almost like a doll's face. She wore red lipstick and black mascara to make her eyes pop. Not like they need to pop anymore than they did. Her eyes were almost round like a circle. Still oval-shaped, but not anything I’ve seen before. Her eyebrows were thin and brown. Her hair was also brown and in a bob-style cut. She looked like something out of a magazine or a poster in a disgruntled boy’s room.

She was beautiful.

“Hi, Darren,” She whispered, with a bright white smile. The room was so quiet that we could hear her. He waved back quietly. She glanced at the rest of us on the other side of the room.

“Who are your friends?” She asked.

“They wanted to see you,” Darren answered. “They didn’t believe me.”

She paused, looking at us, and her smile faded, but it came back. She then nodded.

“Aren’t you gonna introduce me to them?”

Darren grabbed her hand and brought her over to us. The room echoed with the clacking of her heels. We all introduced ourselves to her. She sat on the couch and listened, smiling at us. When we were done, she introduced herself.

“I go by Ms. Penny,” She said to us with a slight British accent. “I’m sure Darren told you a lot about me.”

We all nodded, almost in unison, like we were mind-controlled.

“Why do you come at night?” Sammy asked.

Ms. Penny’s brow furrowed. She squinted slightly, staring off into space. Her eyes widened again, not long after.

“I’m not sure if I could answer that, Sam.” She said, her eye twitched once before continuing. “I guess you could say I came from over the rainbow.”

Sam chuckled, but she wasn’t amused at all. For all she knew, a stranger crawled through the bathroom window to mess with us.

Ms. Penny stood up, “As much as I’d love to stay with you all, I believe it's time for me to leave.” She stared off into the ceiling, slightly above us. We looked up to where she was looking. The ceiling fan spun slowly. I walked up to her and tapped her shoulder. She snapped out of it and snapped her neck down to look at me.

“Can’t you stay for a few more minutes?” I asked. She knelt to my level, her hands on my shoulders.

“Sweetheart,” She said, softly smiling, “I won’t be gone long.” She glanced over at the rest of the group, “and besides, Darren has you gals to keep him company.” When she was talking, her breath zipped its way into my nose. She had pearly white teeth, but her breath smelled rotten. Not like a dog after getting its mouth on whatever it could, but almost like something died. A mouse could have crawled inside her mouth when she slept and couldn’t get out. “Why don’t we all just head to bed. I’ll tuck you in.”

We all went to our sleeping bags and zipped ourselves up. Ms. Penny came by to double-check that our zippers were fully closed and gave each of us a kiss on the cheek. I watched her walk to Darren and do the same for him, but she took longer than she did with us. Were they talking? If so, what about? I didn’t think much of it at the time. I closed my eyes, prepping myself to sleep. Ms. Penny started to hum a lullaby. I don’t remember which one it was, but it was soothing. I began to doze off. Her tune was almost hypnotizing. I started drowning out everything around me, and I slept peacefully, like a baby.

I awoke to screams from the clubhouse kitchen. I looked over and saw that our parents were trying to keep the kids from entering, but they couldn’t hold them back. The kids stopped questioning the adults as soon as they saw what happened. I walked over, rubbing my eyes, only to fully wake up as soon as I saw the scene in front of me.

Sam was on the kitchen floor, surrounded by a pool of her own blood. A chunk of her neck had been bitten off by what I thought to be some kind of animal. The blood around the wound had already dried up. I couldn’t move. I could only stare. Her eyes were blankly staring off into space. Her pupils were nothing more than tiny dots, as if someone took an ultra-fine point marker and tapped each iris once with it.

I couldn’t move. I could only stare. I said that some were screaming around me. A few others ran to the trash can and the bathroom to throw up. I wasn’t nauseous, but as my dad told me, I was pale—almost a ghostly white.

The police eventually arrived. The coroners and detectives scanned the scene up and down, in and out. They couldn’t find anything. I watched with tears in my eyes as they rolled the body bag out of the building. I looked at the other kids. My vision was blurred and abstracted by my tears to properly see anything, but I recalled the parents taking the other kids home as quickly as they could, and I couldn’t find Darren.

As you could have guessed, I was only seven. I am now twenty. My family moved away from that place not long after. We’ve been in a new apartment that wasn’t as well-maintained as the one before, but it’s the better of two evils as long as I don't see her again…

Then again…

I work the night shift at McDonald’s, and on occasion, from the register, I could see someone sitting in a booth by the window next to a promotional poster. She was well dressed, fairly pale, and just stared off into the night, occasionally looking around.

Then again…

On the bus ride home, early in the morning (around three or four, I wanna say), for a brief moment. Someone stood on the sidewalk as the bus passed by in bright colors. I swore they glanced over at me. How could they have seen me with the bus going by faster than they could process?

Then again…

Last night…

The reason why I’m writing this. The one thing out of all of these events that made me want, no, **need** to share this with you. Last night… Went through my nightly routine, shut off the lamp on my nightstand, and lay down in my bed.

Then…

Nothing. Just silence…

Then…

The occasional crow cawing…

Then…

*TAP. TAP. TAP…*

On my bedroom door…

*Please god… Don’t let it be her…*
