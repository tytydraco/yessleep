There’s a razor thin chance you’ve heard of the YouTube channel “Hobby Hawk”. If you have no idea what it is, don’t worry, few have, and it doesn’t matter now anyway. It has been scoured in it’s entirety.

If you have seen an episode, if it rings some vague, distant bell, then it’s likely Ella you remember. The pretty blonde with the gap tooth smile, with a presence so warm and angelic, the sun itself would hide behind clouds so as not to embarrass itself next to her. You won’t remember me. I was just holding the camera.

The channel’s purpose was simple: find hobbies that few people were talking about, document them and those that took part, and share it for perpetuity. It was Ella’s baby, her labour of love. The views and subscribers were meaningless to her. She saw her channel as a kind of ark, and she its Noah. 

“These hobbies, they’re part of these people’s lives.” She once told me, patches of red wine on her lips, “Their very souls, Pavi. Micro cultures that deserve to be saved, known about. We can’t just let them be forgotten. These videos could be their headstones.”

That was the night she came up with the channel. She’d get her ark, and we’d both get something to submit to our film making class. She was creative, but lacked technical ability. I had the technical ability, but lacked creativity. We worked well together.

I won’t deny the videos we made were special, and that lies almost entirely on El. She had a knack for bringing out people’s humanity. People bloomed in her presence. Our first video was on fly fishing, and if it was up to me, it would have probably been me reading out the Wikipedia page on it over some pretty b-roll. It was El’s idea to follow Bradley for a day. Bradley was 12 years old, and we had met him by complete coincidence on bus ride over to a nearby fishing spot where we hoping to run into some fishermen to interview. Bradley was sat there, alone with his fishing pole stuck out his backpack, and El decided to start talking to him. Bradley was more than happy to entertain the some older girls for the day, especially El.

I filmed him as he let his line whip over the water, watching the loops intersect the burning summer sun. He told us, in his broad rural accent, how his grandfather taught him to fish when he was younger, and how now he does it in his memory. He talked about it with a surprisingly resonant tone for a young boy, speaking of fly fishing as an almost spiritual practice, about how it tied him to the land and his ancestors. He spoke in mournful sorrow as his hand skimmed the algae choked waters, about the sickness and pollution killing the river. El was right; we had captured a part of his soul.

As the months went by, we expanded into more hobbies, captured more stories. Ham Radio was a decent hit with 106 views. I did some of my best work on Metal Detecting. More than anything though, I just liked being out there, with El, making something beautiful.

The final project we were working on was UFO Hunting.

It was not something we had in the pipeline already, but was simply dropped into our laps. El had caught wind of a convention for UFO hunters that weekend at a nearby hotel, and felt we needed to seize the opportunity. Now, in our channel’s history, we were no strangers to coming across odd people. It was almost an axiom, a necessity that if you’re documenting hobbies, you’re going to come across obsessive weirdos who are into those hobbies. The UFO convention had me concerned though. I imagined a grim hall, with a smattering of tin foil hat wearing, unwashed schizophrenics ranting about their experiences with the probing machines. El was chipper as ever though, and as it turned out, she was right to be. The convention was actually fairly crowded, with a diverse and for most part well groomed assortment of people meandering around the stalls. El picked out a skinny old guy with a wiry, grey beard to interview, and I’m ashamed to say that I was surprised by his eloquence.

“To me, it’s about hope.” He told us, “You look at the world, you look at people, and it’s easy to feel scared. Trapped. So you have two choices: you can either look down, ache in your neck, try to ignore it all, or…” he smiled and snapped a finger towards the ceiling, “You can look up. You can look up and realise that anything is possible.”

It was during our interview with him that we heard a commotion in the crowd behind us. Someone was shouting. We peered over and saw a man who looked like he hadn’t slept in days, the bags under his eyes olive against his pale skin.

“Fuck you!” He shouted, tears welling up in his eyes, “You don’t know. You’re all children, fucking children! You don’t know what these things are, what they do!”

We watched as hotel security dragged him through the door. I turned to El and saw a worrying look on her face. She was grinning.

“We need to talk to him.” She said.

We already had enough footage. Goddamn it, we already had enough…

“My name is Anthony Davidson, and three years ago, my little girl was taken.”

The face in the viewfinder was grave. It was not the typical subject for our videos. 

“We were hiking, on the moors.” Anthony continued, “It’s… it’s easy to get lost up there. It was almost Christmas, and the sun was setting fast. I thought we could make up time by going cross country, but… the mud, the bogs, it drags you, and Charlotte, she was only little.”

El sat below the camera, nodding, adopting the same sympathetic look as a therapist.

“It was dark soon. And so cold. I carried Charlotte for a while, holding a dim little torch between my teeth. I didn’t know where I was, where I was going. I just put one foot in front of the other. Charlotte, her… water got into her boots, she was freezing, the poor thing. Then, we saw the lights.”

Something froze inside Anthony. Something heavy.

“The lights, Anthony?” Prodded El.

“They were small at first. Like flies, but red, burning red, hovering above us. Then, there was this flash. Next thing I know, I can’t feel my legs. I can’t feel anything.”

The camera stayed focused on his face, but my eyes were caught on his foot, tapping rapidly against the floor.

“I woke up in this room, this blindingly white room. I felt like someone poured cement in my head. Charlotte wasn’t there. I could barely see. I couldn’t move. Then…”

The foot tapping increased. Anthony was going pale, sweat along his hairline.

“Then what?”

“I saw them. These… things.” Anthony spat. “Eyes black like sharks, as big as the palm of my hand. Their skin, it was… sickly grey, and sagging. They just… stared down at me. Maybe they did something to me, I… I woke up later, in hospital, hundreds of miles from home. I just wanted to know where Charlotte was.”

“Where was she, Anthony?”

Anthony’s screwed tight, red as burst ventricles. Hot tears seeped from his eyes before he could press his hands over them.

“She wasn’t there. She’s still with them. She wasn’t there…”.

The tram ride home was quiet. El sat next me, hunched down with her knees apart and chin resting in her hand, staring at the space between atoms. I sat up, stiff as an iron rod, trying and failing to come up with some way to break the silence.

“We need to investigate this.” El said sharply. I looked at her, perplexed.

“El, this isn’t for us. We make videos about old men who make model aircraft carriers, we don’t investigate child abductions.”

“Well, we can’t just let this slip away. I’m too curious now, anyway.” El smiled.

“El, that man just broke down on camera over losing his daughter, why the fuck are you smiling?”

“No… yeah, sorry.” El muttered, visibly ashamed, “I just… I don’t know. I want to help him. We spend all our time hearing people’s stories, but his is still going. I want to help him find closure.”

“Then recommend him a therapist, don’t make a documentary about it.”

After we went our separate ways, I wondered if what I said upset Ella, as she was uncharacteristically quiet the next two days. Absolute radio silence. That is, until I got a text late one evening - “come over, I think I have something”.

I didn’t hesitate to go to hers. Looking back on it, I find it funny how I was so quick to jump out of bed and walk in the pouring rain to her flat, no questions asked. When I finally got there, soaked to the bone, El feverishly opened the door and ushered me in.

“Oh fuck, sorry, I didn’t realise it was raining.” She apologised, ushering me over to the radiator, 

“Get undressed, I’ll get you some pyjamas to change into.”

I began to peel off my waterlogged clothes, throwing the heavy fabric over the radiator. As I stepped out of my jeans, I looked up and realised the coffee table in the living room, which was typically clear save a mug and maybe a couple books, was strewn with paper, El’s laptop shining an unnatural blue against the cosy yellow lamplight.

“Okay,” El said, handing me a pair of flannel pyjamas, “I’ve been doing some research.”

“Evidently.” I muttered, dressing myself.

“So,” El began, unfolding what appeared to be a huge map, “This is the area Anthony said he was walking through.”

“Oh, El, I thought we weren’t going to do this.”

“I know! It doesn’t have to be a video, I just couldn’t stop thinking about it, and one thing led to another and I need to show you this, please.”

I let out a capitulating sigh and motioned for her to continue as I sat down on the couch.

“Okay so, this is the area.” El continued, folding out the huge map and laying it out on the table, “What do you notice?”

I peered down at a huge green blob patterned with fingerprint like swirls.

“It’s fucking empty.” I said.

“Exactly. There’s nothing there. All the footpaths flow around  it, like water passing by a rock in a river.”

“Is that normal?”

“It’s not abnormal. But what isn’t normal is according to this map from twelve years ago, it looked like this.”

El held up her laptop and showed a near identical map to the one on the coffee table, only this one had two green lines going through the moor.

“Public footpaths.” I muttered.

“Yes. Now, a footpath or two going away isn’t too strange, and it makes sense considering that ten years ago, this suddenly became privately owned land.”

“Okay, so, the land was bought, people couldn’t walk through it anymore, mystery solved.”

“Okay, but why was it bought? You said yourself, there’s nothing there.”

“I don’t know. Maybe it’s for gamekeeping. Or an environmental conservation thing. Or someone was going to build something and the plan went under.”

“I did some digging…” El continued, thumbing through papers, “And found out the land was bought by Havisham, a non-profit environmental protection group.”

“There you go then, it’s a conservation thing.”

“Except Havisham doesn’t exist. Not outside this singular purchase of land. No website, no social media, nothing.”

“Maybe it was a start-up that flopped hard and fast.”

“Then why hasn’t the land been sold off, their assets liquidated? Why do they still own this giant patch of moorland?”

“I don’t know, El, I’m a film student, not a business-lawyer-person.”

“Okay, okay, just hear me out on this.” El sprang up from the couch and began anxiously pacing on the other side of the coffee table, “What if Havisham - hear me out - Havisham is a shell company owned by the government who bought the land because they know it’s an alien ufo whatever hotspot and they needed to stop people coming in.”

“You’re insane.” I told her, suddenly exhausted.

“No no, just-”

“If they were trying to keep people out,” I interrupted, “Then why not put up a fence or something? Call it an actual conservation area.”

“How do you put up a fence around an area that large?”

“I’d figure it out if it stopped people being abducted by aliens, El. Which they weren’t.”

Ella rolled her eyes and let out a frustrated grunt, before striding back to the sofa and wrapping her hands around mine.

“Do you…” She began, “Want to go there tomorrow?”

“El!”

“The weather is supposed to be nice. If nothing else, it can be a cute little day trip. We can bring a picnic.”

“Ella… I’m worried about you, with this. I’m not joking with you anymore, you’re scaring me a bit.”

“I know, I… I’m sorry. I just think if I can see it, then, I don’t know, maybe I’ll get over this… thing I’ve been obsessed over and we can move on.”

“El… no.”

“Pav… I… I’m going to end up going anyway. It’s okay, though. You don’t have to come with me.”

Her hands were still holding mine.

We set off early the next morning.

Ella’s car, a nineties hatchback with rusted wheel arches and leaves caught under the front window, struggled with the rolling moorlands. But El was right, the weather was nice, last night’s storm blowing out the clouds, leaving only icy blue skies. We scream-sang the pop songs coming from my phone speakers. It felt like things used to, before all this started a few days ago.

We parked at a beauty spot atop Calder Scar, a tall hill from which we could see the forbidden moorland just north of us. I made Ella promise we wouldn’t set foot on it.

“See anything, Mulder?” I asked.

“No…” El answered, peering through binoculars, “It’s completely indistinguishable from anything around it.”

“Funny that.”

“Pretty though.”

“It is. Think I’ll get some footage, while we’re here.”

I grabbed my camera from my bag and had hardly stood out from the car door before coming face to face with a ruddy faced old man dressed in a mouldering wax jacket.

“Can I help you?” He muttered gruffly. I didn’t respond, the tight sensation in my chest holding back any words from leaving my throat.

“Um, we’re fine, thanks.” I heard Ella say firmly, climbing out the car.

“Aye, naw trouble. You takin’ in’t views?”

“We’re film students.” I managed to choke out.

“We’re doing a project on natural beauty.”

“Aye, well, you’ve couldn’t have chosen a better place. God’s own country, this.”

“What can you tell us about it?” Ella asked.

“Ee, well, I’ve been here since I was a nipper. Don’t know much else. Nothing beats it. Seein’ sun shift along the hills an’ like.”

“Notice anything strange recently?” El asked. I could have killed her in that moment.

“Erh, naw, not much changes round ‘ere. Thank god. Tell you what though, there were summat, few year ago. My flock graze here, there’s a few sheep farms ‘round ‘ere. Little while ago, we had this spate of finding some of the sheep killed like. Now there’s always summat, you know, dogs or what have ye, but these ‘uns were strange. Their guts were gone. Everything. Now, if it were a dog or foxes, they’d have the organs, but they leave the intestines and that. These sheep, were like… scraped out, clean like.”

“That’s… strange.” I muttered.

“Aye. Few years ago, like I said. Anyways, I’ll leave you be.”

The old man raised his hand and departed down the hill. Ella was wide eyed.

“Pavi… cattle mutilations.”

“Ella, please…”

“It’s like, classic ufo phenomena.”

I wanted to argue, but the thought gave me a migraine. The cold sunlight and whipping wind seemed to blur everything. The world, became hazy and dreamlike.

“Pavi… I need to see it.” El said, speaking with a strange sense of grace, as though speaking an absolute truth. “At night.”

“Absolutely fucking not, El. C’mon, let’s go home.”

El and I got back in the car, and as I clicked in my seatbelt, I looked up to see tears in El’s eyes. I could tell from her face that they weren’t from the wind.

“Pavi, you know I love you, right?” She whispered. Her breath was shallow, her hands wrapped tight around the steering wheel. I could hear the plastic creak.

“Yeah, El.” I answered, “I love you too.”

“I’m sorry. About all this. I don’t know what’s wrong with me. I just… I can’t get rid of this feeling. Like there’s something there. Something real. And I can’t sleep until I know what it is.”

I didn’t respond. I knew where this was going.

“Pavi, please. Just one night. We’ll get a room somewhere. Please.”

I pressed my skull hard into the headrest. I wanted to scream. I wanted to go the fuck home.

“Fine.” I finally muttered. El coiled her arms around my neck like a snake. Her hair smelt like her bubblegum conditioner. She took my head in her hand and kissed me on the lips. I felt less in that moment than I imagined I would.

By 10:30, it was pitch black outside. The clouds had returned, and there were no stars in the sky. El and I were in a pub I don’t recall the name of. I had hardly drank anything, a hard kind of sickness stuck in my throat. I saw Anthony come in through the front door.

“What’s he doing here?” I asked.

“I called him.” Ella told me, “He agreed to show us where he went.”

At that point, I was too tired to resist. A grim detachment washed over me. Anthony sat down next to us. He was even paler than before, and seemed incapable of remaining still, constantly looking around him. We finished our drinks and drove out to the moorland. It was freezing out there, the howling wind whipping at my legs. By torchlight, we walked for what felt like hours over boggy moorland. Icy, muddy water seeped into my canvas shoes. I found I could not look at El without a burning rage electrifying my nerves. She brought me out here, on this death march, chasing ghosts and little green men. I had no torch, so I relied on my cameras infrared mode. Endlessly I stared at the viewfinder, nothing but my own numb feet pacing through this unnamed wasteland. So transfixed I was in my own Sisyphean hell that I completely missed when I separated from the other two.

They weren’t far off. A hundred feet or so. They hadn’t noticed either. I began to trudge back, following their lights. Until my foot became caught under a branch and I fell into the dark. I wiped the mud from the lens and sat up, ready to scream into the blackness. I looked back into the viewfinder.

It was not a branch.

I reached out to the shape in the dark. It was not a branch, I was sure. It was too smooth, too white. I picked it up, drew it from the sucking mud. In my hands, I felt the smooth ball-like curvature of one end and somehow, instinctually, undeniably, knew I was holding a human femur.

I dropped it. My breath escaped me. I heard my heartbeat pound in my ears. I tentatively reached down again. Wiping away the earth, I found more. Ribs. Vertebrae. A skull. Too small. Too small for an adult.

Suddenly, a blinding light appeared behind me, followed by screaming, shouting. I ran, trying not to slip, towards it.

There were figures there, dark against the light, forming a circle. In the centre, shrouded in a phosphorescent white light, was Ella, on the ground, hands on her head. Anthony stood above, someone in his grasp, a kitchen knife pressed a hard against their neck.

“Where is she?!” Screamed Anthony, his teeth gritted, spitting with every syllable, “Where is my daughter?!”

My eyes adjusted to the light, and I saw the person on the edge of Anthony’s knife. They were wearing a suit of thick, grey rubber, their face obscured by a gas mask. Those encircling them were dressed the exact same way, only aiming down rifle sights. Except one. One, a man, had taken off his mask and set down his rifle, approaching Anthony slowly, hands up. I couldn’t hear what he was saying over the wind. Above them, I saw the source of the light: two drones equipped with floodlights. A steady red light was on both.

I moved closer. El’s eyes, fearful under her arms, met mine. She looked she began to say something, when a blinding flash overtook me. My body went numb.

I woke up on the moors in the morning. I cannot say if it was the same place I fell. My clothes were dry.

Hobby Hawk has not uploaded any more videos.

Ella remains missing.
