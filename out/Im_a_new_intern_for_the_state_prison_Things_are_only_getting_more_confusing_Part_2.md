For any confusion about the timeline of my internship from [Part 1](https://www.reddit.com/r/nosleep/comments/1sko841/im_a_new_intern_for_the_state_prison_weird_things/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=web3x&utm_name=web3xcss&utm_term=1&utm_content=share_button), my first day aligned with last Friday, and I considered Day 2 and 3 the weekend. I realize now how unclear that was. However strange my internship schedule may be, I was glad I could take care of all introductions before the actual work began this week. In that regard, I have found out some more harrowing information after going to work the last two days. As I mentioned in the title, things have not gotten any better.

I could not sleep over the weekend. Every time I tried to get some rest, I saw the bloodied eyes of the inmate staring back at me. Being alone with my thoughts led to my imagination running rampant; before I knew it, I was terrified at the idea of stepping back in there on Monday as if nothing had happened. Despite all that, I showed up. My alarm did not have the opportunity to ring before I turned it off, already awake after yet another sleepless night. I got ready and drove all the way there with my heart racing. 

I entered the building using the brand-new ID card they gave me and went to Mr. Haile’s office. The metal door I stood in front of, which was painted a sickly tan color to match the cinderblock walls, and was marked with a nameplate that ‌read “M. HAILE.” Before I had the chance to knock, the door swung open. Haile greeted me, a smile on his face.

“Good morning. I was just about to meet you in the lobby. We’re going on a little field trip, so drop your stuff off and we’ll head right out,” he said.

I did as he instructed and met back up with him just outside the doorway. We walked to another room labeled “LAUNDRY,” which was one of the many rooms we passed on the walkthrough last week but did not enter. This time, he guided me inside.

Fifty or so inmates toiled away like worker bees, presumably so deep in focus that they didn’t even notice Haile and me entering. The large room was divided into two main sections, with washers in one part and dryers in the other. The linoleum beneath my feet was a sterile white, much like everything else in there. He had to yell over the sound of the machines in the room as we toured it.

“This is our laundry room. As with nearly every prison, we put our men to work in order to keep this place up and running. We assign every prisoner a job that we believe will suit them best in order to maximize performance,” he explained.

“How do you decide who goes where?” I yelled back.

“During their intake, Dr. Kendrick performs a psychological evaluation in which she asks all kinds of questions, and based on their answers, we sort them into the job they’d be best fit for. Besides the laundry, we have inmates work in the kitchen, library, and post office. Inmates can also perform janitorial work or other groundskeeping jobs. All of that is to say that we keep our men busy,” he concluded with a smile. 

As we geared up to leave, I felt a chill rack down my spine. It froze me mid-step and made goosebumps break out on my skin. At that moment, I understood how deer felt when some sixth sense told them a sight was pointed straight at their neck. I looked around in an attempt to find the source of my fear, only to see that every single prisoner had stopped in his tracks to look at me as I was leaving. They wore the same crazed, inhumanly wide-eyed expression as the tattooed inmate from a few days ago. My heart started pounding faster than it already had before, and I darted out of the room without sparing another glance behind me. 

Haile had already crossed the threshold of the door when I caught up with him, not too far behind but significantly more shaken than he was. Tears threatened to spill from my eyes, and my breath never quite made it to my lungs when they so desperately needed the oxygen. I needed to contain myself. I couldn’t blow this opportunity by showing my fear, weakness, or confusion. Not seeing the internal battle I was fighting, Haile moved to keep walking, but when I piped up with hesitation, he turned to face me.

“I don’t know if it’s just me, but I feel like the inmates here are acting a little strange,” I said meekly. This was an understatement, but I didn’t want to step on any toes when I just started working here. “Why is that?”

I remember in the research I conducted on the first day, and even when I was applying for the position, I had seen that it was a lower-security, more “humanitarian” prison. This was strange because it housed dangerous and mentally ill prisoners—“the lowest of the low,” some might say. People like that usually belonged in a maximum security penitentiary where their every move could be tracked, not in a place where they could wander and potentially cause trouble.

After a brief pause, he said, “Well, I’m not exactly sure what strange behavior you’re referring to, but I’m sure Dr. Kendrick can provide a better explanation of what we do here.”  
We made the trek back to her office in silence, and when we reached her door, he rapped his knuckle lightly against it. He opened it to Kendrick at her desk, her glasses perched at the end of her nose, heavily engrossed in whatever she was working on at her computer. At the sound of us opening the door, she looked up and said, “Ah, you two, come on in!”

Mr. Haile explained that he was just dropping me off once again while he went and did who-knows-what. I sat across from her and repeated what I asked Haile: why the hell were the prisoners acting so weird when they were supposed to be at peace here?

“As you might have read, we’re taking a more humanistic approach to corrections. Prisons in the United States have put too much emphasis on incapacitation rather than rehabilitation, which is proven to be the most effective way to correct behavior and reduce recidivism. We’re aiming to fix that by making a self-sufficient, commune-style correctional facility where people can really learn from their mistakes and reintegrate into society,” she said as if reading from a textbook. “Our prisoners are always like that. If they were behaving weirdly, I assure you our officers would have noticed.”

I couldn’t believe it. How could neither of them see how abnormal this is? Putting aside the fact that they were way too subdued, the look in their eyes was anything but normal. However, I had to accept her response as truth. We talked more about what knowledge and experience I have with corrections. I recounted the classes and volunteering I’ve done, and how this is my first time learning about a prison like this one. Haile came just as there was a lapse in conversation, and I was grateful I wouldn’t have to think about another topic to speak to her about. I was still incredibly suspicious of all the bullshit she spewed earlier. Mr. Haile explained that we were heading to check out the library next, since it was one of the nicer facilities in the prison, but he stopped mid-explanation with an exasperated look on his face.

“Shoot! I forgot I had a report to write and send to HQ. Why don’t you go and tour the library for yourself while I get a head start on this work? There shouldn’t be anyone else in there but a clerk at the checkout counter,” he said hurriedly before scurrying back to his office. I wondered what kind of pressing work waited for him. 

I meandered the rest of the way to the library, wasting time I would otherwise have spent doing the menial work Haile assigned to me. The library’s wooden double doors stood out from the concrete and metal the rest of the facility was constructed with. I opened them and gaped in awe at what I saw inside. What looked like hundreds of rows of books greeted me from their wooden shelves. It had that dusty old smell typical of libraries that didn’t see many visitors. I looked up to see a balcony lining the perimeter of the room with even more books on shelves. If it weren’t in a prison, I would’ve loved to sit here and do my homework.

In the middle of the room, my eyes found the prisoner manning the checkout counter, as Haile said someone would be. He had medium-length, disheveled hair that curled over his brow, and a full beard that covered the lower half of his face. Thin, wire-rimmed glasses covered the top half. His sleeves were rolled up to reveal black ink on his forearms. I was content to ignore him and browse the shelves in silence, but he apparently had a different idea.

“You must be the new intern. How’s the job treating you?” he asked me as I walked past where he was seated at the desk. His voice was deep but soft in a way I wouldn’t expect from a calloused criminal. I stopped in my tracks and faced the counter; it felt like there was an ocean of distance between us.

“It’s alright so far,” I replied, slightly nervous at how he had confronted me so boldly.

“What about the other inmates?” he pressed, looking at me expectantly. This was a weird line of questioning, but I was afraid that not responding would lead to a potentially violent confrontation.

“They’re… alright, too. A little strange, but…” I said, the rest of my sentence dying away on my lips. I was hesitant to reveal all that had happened with them in case he also didn’t believe me. “Why do you ask?”

“No particular reason.” I thought that was it, so I turned away, ready to go back to scanning the aisles of books.

“They look at you like that because they’re asking for help,” he said from behind me. I turned back slowly.

“What?” I asked, incredulous. How did he know that?

“Why don’t you meet me here again next time you work, and we’ll talk more? I’ve kept you here long enough,” he said as a gentle smile graced his face. I didn’t say anything as I turned to walk back to Haile’s office. I threw him one last glance over my shoulder before I left, and I saw he was looking at me until I closed the door.

I didn’t know what to do with what little information he gave me. What did he mean, ‘they’re asking for help’? It seemed like he was the only other normal person here, the only one who might be able to give me answers. I might just have to meet up with him again; satisfaction might just bring the cat back, after all.

I’m now up late once again thinking about the past few days at work and deliberating my options. Besides that prisoner, you are the only ones I can confide in. I would love to know your guys’ thoughts on what I should do next.
