This lamp must be haunted. It’s not the sort of lamp you’d expect to be haunted. Normally you’d imagine something from another century – made with beaded embroidery and constructed before electric wiring had been fully figured out.

No, this is a Modern&co lamp. And we bought it just today.

Even before purchasing it, I knew that something about it wasn’t quite right.

I can be quite particular when it comes to color. If a pot isn’t the correct shade of yellow, I will endure a few sleepless nights before it goes straight to the waste bin. Not because there was anything remotely wrong with it. A perfectly good yellow pot, that was not quite the right shade of yellow, into the waste bin.

I’ve thrown out entire dish sets for this reason. Curtains, clothing, everything I own has to be just right. 

I’m quite wasteful, but it’s not out of carelessness. I’m just sensitive. I can notice if something is out of place, even by a little. And when it is, I can’t stop thinking about it. To the point where I can’t fall asleep at night.

I don’t think it would be possible for me to live with roommates. None of their possessions would be safe.

And of course, as all such things go, I did not disclose any of this to my boyfriend before we moved in together. My paranoia about colors and framing and textures and contrast — none of this can be shared with someone who had for some reason decided that I was the right person for them. Luckily, he didn’t bring that much into the apartment. I wonder if he even noticed his kettle being replaced.

I’m really trying to hold myself back. Perhaps we could place it in the corner— the one you hardly see when you enter the room. And it could be fine. I could try and muster the will to ignore it every day, over and over. 

And the worst part about it is– I have no real excuse for hating this lamp. 

It is the exact hue of burgundy that I’ve been wanting. The sleek triangular cover is exactly what I have been praying for, months before Modern&co released it as a product. And it cost so much that even my boyfriend’s jaw dropped when he saw the number.

There is no excuse for that little nagging feeling in the corner of my mind.

That there is something terribly wrong about this lamp.

Something about its presence. 

And every part of me that normally protests when a color is wrong, when a reflection is not quite clear enough, when there is a slight scratch or when a piece is too dated— has been absolutely shrieking about this perfect lamp that didn’t have the slightest hint of a flaw in it.

A battle between me and my intuition.

My pride held firm for most of the day.

I did not breathe a single word of my irrationality to my boyfriend.

He could never know.

I thought I could get away with it. I thought I could smother the last bit of superstition still embedded in my mammalian brain. 

But at night as I tried to sleep, I'm not sure what came over me.

The lamp — I felt its presence nearby. Its triangular shadow looming, flickering from the light of the cars moving past the window.

It was there. It was thinking, planning, waiting to strike.

I had to watch it. So I put on my robe and came into my living room.

And now, I am still here. It is half past one in the morning and I am watching a lamp.

It stares back at me. A presence, an almost humanlike figure with an oversized, triangular head, and an unrealistically narrow body.

Why can’t I go to bed? Why can’t my mind shut down? There must be something wrong with me. I can’t be this freaked out by a lamp. I must be imagining all of this. My thoughts race, no longer coherent.

Why are lamps so frightening in the dark?

I can’t give in. That desire to take the lamp, and bring it outdoors. I could pretend that there was a break in. That some eccentric lamp enthusiast had come by and robbed the house without either of us noticing.

Yes, that could be it. The excuse. A silent burglar.

Obviously I'm not thinking straight anymore. I walk over to the lamp that has nothing wrong with it. An innocent lamp that never meant any harm. That would have spent its life calmly standing, patiently watching. I grab its metal frame, and then— there is a switch.

I understand it. I understand the lamp.

I am the lamp.

The lamp takes a good look at me, and I stare back; its brown eyes are embedded in a round human head, perched unnaturally on an oversized body that balances precariously on two legs, neither of which has a sufficient base. It lifts me up and carries me out of the apartment. I hit the stairwell a couple of times. My last cries for help. Someone opens their door to complain, but despite that, I am nonetheless swiftly taken outside and left on the curb. The lamp turns and looks at me, and smiles a cold, fluorescent smile, the kind I had never seen on a human face before. And then it turns and re-enters the building.
