It was a half-second at first.

That’s all it took.

I stream occasionally. Nothing big. A few hundred viewers on a good night. I keep a small monitor behind my camera so I can see the live feed.

One night I reached up to adjust my headset.

On the monitor, I watched myself reach up.

Before I did.

Not dramatically. Just slightly ahead.

I felt the movement in my shoulder a split second later.

Chat exploded.

“Why did you move before you moved?”  
“Lag?”  
“Rewind that.”

I laughed it off. Said it was a sync issue. Network delay.

We clipped it.

The replay looked normal.

Perfect timing.

But live viewers swore it happened.

So did I.



A week later, it happened again.

This time it wasn’t subtle.

I leaned forward toward the camera.

On the monitor, I was already leaning forward.

Not in motion.

Already there.

Waiting.

I froze.

The version of me on screen didn’t.

It blinked.

Slowly.

I hadn’t blinked yet.

Chat went quiet.

Someone typed:

“Why are you staring like that?”

I checked the replay again.

Normal.

No anomaly.

But I remember what I saw.

I remember being behind.



After that, I started noticing small things.

When I opened my phone’s front camera, my face was already centered. Perfect framing. Perfect angle.

Before I adjusted.

My doorbell camera sent me motion alerts seconds before I reached the porch.

When I checked the feed, it showed me approaching.

Before I remember leaving the couch.

I tested it.

I set up a camera facing my monitor.

Infinite recursion.

Layer inside layer inside layer.

At first it was just distortion.

At some depth — maybe twenty layers in — something stabilized.

In the smallest window, I was different.

Older. Still.

Not tired. Not distracted.

Composed.

It wasn’t copying me.

It was holding a pose.

Like it didn’t need to move anymore.

I waved my hand in front of the camera.

The deepest version didn’t wave.

It watched.



I shut the monitor off.

The screen went black.

The camera was still recording.

I reviewed the footage later.

Even with the monitor off, the deeper layer was still visible.

Not bright.

But there.

Standing in the dark recursion.

Centered.



The worst part happened three nights ago.

Video call with coworkers.

Eight people.

Normal meeting.

I glanced at my preview window.

The on-screen version of me leaned closer to the camera.

My real body stayed still.

I held my breath.

No one reacted.

The version on screen raised its hand.

Slowly.

Like it was about to speak.

I didn’t move.

My coworkers didn’t say anything.

They just kept talking.

Like nothing was wrong.

I slammed the laptop shut.

My heart was pounding so hard I thought I’d black out.

Ten minutes later, I got a message:

“Why did you stay on after everyone left?”

I told him my internet cut.

He said it didn’t.

He said I stayed in the meeting room alone for five minutes.

Looking straight at the camera.

Not blinking.



Since then, I’ve been careful.

I avoid mirrors.

I avoid front cameras.

I avoid opening multiple screens at once.

But it doesn’t matter.

Last night, I walked past my powered-off monitor.

It turned on.

Not fully.

Just enough to show a faint glow.

And in that glow, I saw myself.

Standing closer to the screen than I was in real life.

Close enough that my face filled the frame.

Watching me from inside the glass.

Waiting.

I didn’t move.

Neither did it.

But I felt something shift.

Like a weight transferring.

Like something deciding which version was more stable.

More aligned.

More correct.



If your screen ever moves before you do —

Don’t correct it.

Don’t test the delay.

Don’t try to “sync” yourself to it.

Because I’m starting to think it isn’t predicting you.

It’s replacing you.

And the more stable version is winning.



I haven’t turned my monitor on today.

But my webcam light flickered on by itself an hour ago.

And I swear I heard typing.
