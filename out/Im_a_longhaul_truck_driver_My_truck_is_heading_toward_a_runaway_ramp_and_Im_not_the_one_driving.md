I drive long-haul. I'm a woman, twenty-eight, and I've spent most of the last four years alone in a truck. I eat sunflower seeds and crack the shells with my front teeth, and my dentist told me numerous times that doing so will eventually ruin them. Still, I have to fit into the trucker stereotype somehow.

I know my truck the way you know your own hands. I know what my truck sounds like from outside and from inside. I know the difference between another rig idling two spots over and my own engine turning. I know how my tires sound on gravel. I know how the sleeper moves when I shift my weight.

I'd stopped for my ten-hour reset. There are federal rules about how long you can drive before the law makes you stop and rest, and the Electronic Logging Device is the thing that watches you to make sure you actually do it. It logs the drive time, location, engine status. You can't push through your hours.

I'd already driven most of the day. I pulled into a truck stop off the interstate in West Virginia a little before two in the morning, exhausted.

The trailer attached to my truck was sealed. The paperwork said medical equipment. The seal was intact when I picked it up in Pittsburgh, and you don't break a seal on freight like that. You check the number against the paperwork, you sign, you drive. I did that. So, I set the brakes, logged Sleeper Berth, and went inside.

The truck stop had the texture two AM always has in those places. Fluorescent light with no warmth. Coffee that had been sitting in the pot for hours. Food going slowly gray under heat lamps. There was a moth the size of my palm knocking itself against the window glass by the door, over and over, in the same spot. A handful of drivers moving around with shower bags, faces gone soft and droopy with similar exhaustion. A man near the window watching something on his phone with the sound on, no headphones.

I noticed the two of them by the drink coolers because they didn't fit the hour.

Everyone else looked worn down. They didn't. The woman had dark hair and a red jacket. The man beside her was thin, in a gray zip-up. They were standing very close together without touching, and they were both… too awake.

I got my shower code and went down the hall.

The showers at that chain are better than people expect. They have a locking door, real towels, and water hot enough to hurt a little, which is how I prefer it.

When I came out, the woman in the red jacket was standing at the sinks.

She stood at the sink with her hands resting on the edge of it, doing nothing at all, just facing her own reflection.

I said, "Oops, sorry." (Driving in a truck for most of the year makes an introvert even more socially anxious.)

She smiled at me through the glass without turning around.

I bought water, a protein bar, and a bag of extra salty sunflower seeds. The man was in the same aisle, in front of the beef jerky, holding one bag without looking at it. He was looking at the shelf behind it.

I walked past him. He didn't move. At the register I looked back once, and he was still holding the same bag, in the same place.

I went back to my truck.

The sleeper inside my truck is the one place that's entirely mine. Blackout curtain between the cab and the bunk, a second curtain around the bunk itself because I'm a super light sleeper, a weighted blanket, a satin pillow because of course I want my hair to look nice in the rare occasion I see anyone, a white noise machine the size of my fist, earplugs, a basket of wipes and lip balm and even more sunflower seeds (OK, before you say it, yes. I am basically a hamster now.), a warm little strip of light taped under the shelf. An iPad mounted by the bunk for my allotted hours of brain rot. My phone was charging on the dash, because that outlet works better.

The last thing I remember was watching on my iPad a woman pouring rice into a glass jar for organizational purposes, and I remember thinking, distantly, that she owned more kinds of flour than a person who baked would ever need.

Then I fell asleep.

The thing that woke me was the backup alarm.

Beep... Beep... Beep…

Truck stops are never quiet. There're reefer units, idling engines, air brakes letting go, somebody slamming a side box shut at four in the morning. So I assumed it belonged to someone else. I've slept through worse.

Then the sleeper moved underneath me.

I sat up too fast and hit my shoulder on the cabinet over the bunk.

The white noise machine kept going with its thin fake rain. I pulled one earplug out.

The backup alarm kept beeping. Tires turned over gravel. My gravel, under my truck.

We stopped. The air brakes sighed. Then we moved forward again.

For a few seconds my mind tried to make light of this. Maybe I was getting towed? Did I park in the wrong spot? Did another truck hit me? Was it another truck close enough to feel through the frame?

The curtain between the cab and my sleeper was shut. The dashboard light came around its edges, blue and green, shifting with the turns.

My phone was on the dash. The iPad was beside me.

I opened the curtain about two inches.

There were two people in the cab. The woman was in the driver's seat. The man was beside her. Both facing forward.

Her hands were on the wheel, evenly placed, the way a doll's hands are placed when someone else has set them there, fingers barely wrapped around the wheel. Her shoulders were square to the windshield. She didn't make any of the small, constant adjustments a body makes while actually driving a sixty-thousand-pound vehicle. She didn't glance at the mirrors, she didn't correct the wheel, no shift of weight through the turns threw her sideways. She was simply upright while my truck did the moving.

The man sat the same way. Forward. Still. Hands in his lap, palms up, fingers slightly curled.

I never saw either of them breathe. Neither of them spoke.

I let the curtain fall shut.

I don't know how long I stayed like that. But it was long enough for the gravel to give way to pavement. Long enough for the white noise machine to become the only thing I could hear. That darn machine made everything worse. Because now I couldn't hear the road, or the cab. Or them.

I wanted to turn it off. But I didn't want them to notice I was awake.

I checked the iPad. One bar. My phone was right there a mere three feet away plugged into the dash. Useless.

I opened the curtain again. Nothing had changed. Same posture. Same hands. Only the road outside was different. We were past the truck stop entrance now, angling toward the highway.

I know the route because I've done it dozens of times, the same way you know a hallway in the dark. There was a flat patch. A bend. Then the downward grade starts.

Other drivers don’t love this stretch. Nobody does.

I also knew what came after that.

There's a runaway truck ramp. It's a long bed of gravel and sand cut into the shoulder of a steep decline, built for trucks whose brakes have failed, so the incline and the loose surface can do the stopping that a guardrail can't. They’re meant to save lives.

**RUNAWAY TRUCK RAMP 2 MILES.**

**RUNAWAY VEHICLES ONLY.**

My truck isn't speeding. It isn't shaking or swerving. It's not doing anything remotely close to being out of control by any measure. The engine brake is working the way it should. The road is dry and clear. There's no reason for my truck to need that ramp.

I think that's where she's taking it anyway.

I opened the curtain again because I wanted my phone. Why in the hell do I leave it plugged into the dash??? I tried to think of ways to yank it without grabbing their attention. Everything within reach was too short, too soft, or too loud to move quietly.

When I looked up, their heads had started to turn.

Only their heads. Their shoulders stayed exactly where they'd been, square to the windshield. Her hands stayed on the wheel. His stayed in his lap. Just their heads, turning at exactly the same speed. For half a second, when the dashboard lights swept across them. Both sets of eyes caught the light wrong.

I stopped breathing.

Their heads stopped turning before either of them could have seen me. The angle was already wrong by then, somewhere between forward and back, past the point a neck can bend without dragging the shoulders along with it. Without pause, she adjusted the rear-view mirror.

I let the curtain fall and hoped it made no sound at all.

I'm typing this with the brightness down all the way, under the blanket, and I'm really hoping the small noise of my fingers tapping on the screen doesn't carry. I don't know if they can hear me breathing. I don't know why they stopped turning their heads.

We passed the next sign a few minutes ago. I saw the yellow of it through the gap in the curtain.

**RUNAWAY TRUCK RAMP 1 MILE.**

Again, I know this road. There's another sign at the half-mile mark, and then the ramp itself, coming up fast on the right, a hard climb through deep gravel at a bad angle.

The white noise machine is still running. I hate it now in retrospect. I can't separate the fake rain from the real road underneath anymore. Every change in the tires, I think we've hit the ramp. Every shift of the engine, I think we're turning. Every time the curtain moves with the air from the vents, I think it's a hand.

I looked again just now.

Phone, still on the dash. Her, still driving. Him, still beside her.

**RUNAWAY TRUCK RAMP 1/2 MILE.**

I'm going to try for my phone.

**Update:** We reached the ramp. My truck has come to a stop. They're just sitting still.
