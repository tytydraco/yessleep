I’m writing this in the hopes someone will understand why I did what I did. I’m not asking to be forgiven, but I couldn’t stand the idea of harsh speculation from those that never knew me. Don’t forgive me, but try to understand. 

I hate them. 

The ones that fly, the ones that buzz, the ones that clamber. I hate every single one. 

And worst of all they hate me too. Dark pupil-less eyes, hard bodies covered in barbed hairs. Odd-bending joints, terrifying mouths, and their mechanical movements. Every aspect of them disgusted me, and just proximity to a single insect used to send me into an anxious mania that would sometimes take hours to fully calm from. 

Severe ptsd, anxiety disorder, and selective muteness. As far as adoption was concerned I was a high risk case, and for most people, that was far beyond the scope of childcare they had hoped for. I was damaged goods, far worse than many of the other children in the care facility. 

Most friends I made got transferred out to foster care or group homes. After a while I stopped trying to make new ones, not that I'd ever been the first to approach before. 

Despite my loneliness, I enjoyed the lack of bugs inside the building. Everything was so sterile and quiet, and the workers were kinder than anything I’d known. I had access to cartoons and toys, and on Wednesdays we had chocolate pudding. 

So when I heard a foster family had applied to take me on, I was resistant to leaving the place behind. I had my own bed with actual blankets, I had coloring books and got to watch teenage mutant ninja turtles whenever I wanted. All of which I brought up to my case worker, only to be informed I would have all those and more at my new home. 

It took some convincing and a few breakdowns, but after meeting the foster parents my apprehension was replaced with an eager curiosity that delighted my therapist. They were willing to take me in, cracks and all, and for that I could never repay them. 

Living with them brought a new set of challenges, but they supported me the whole way. My therapist said this place was an opportunity for me to heal, but truthfully I felt more afraid of bugs than ever. The anxiety of a new home only worsened my fears, proving a difficult adjustment period. But they took utmost care to keep me away from any bugs, and for that I was grateful. 

————

It never occurred to me just how inexperienced I was with normal interactions. How strange was an adult I could talk to without immense dread, ones that actually listened to me. But I guess I’d never had a proper example of a parent.

My real mom was a lot different, she ignored me a lot and slept most of the time. Sometimes she would sleep entire days, only ever getting up when one of her boyfriends came around. 

That's what she called the men that came around, her boyfriends. Every couple days another would come by, sometimes a few at a time. Sometimes they were mean, but mostly they just ignored me. 

The apartment was always cluttered, trash and clothes strewn about the place like a tornado had come through. Random assortments of items my mom kept to “sell later” though she never did. The walls were a gallery of chipped paint and holes of various sizes. Small piles of drywall left white dust on the stained carpet, a physical reminder of the violence that created it.

And despite everything the worst part of it all was the bugs. The rotting carpet, the piles of trash, the spoiled food, a sanctuary for insects. Roaches prowled the house at all hours, flies and mosquitoes filled the air, and bedbugs spawned endlessly in meager stained mattresses and dank carpeting. 

The constant terror of their presence, the sounds of their buzzing, the feeling of them on my skin. At the time i was barely seven, and was only allowed out on rare occasions to fetch ice or use a vending machine down the hall. I was trapped utterly and completely, and held in place by my fear of upsetting my mother. 

————

Moving into my new home came with a new set of challenges. And for once I felt hyper aware of my behavior, and began to feel immense guilt. I knew I wasn’t rational, I knew normal people didn’t act like this. But everyone was so kind to me, and they were endlessly patient through it all. 

My fear of lice was so strong I had to keep my hair buzzed, though it made me feel ugly. But the feeling of my own hair brushing my neck or face was enough to send me into a frenzy of itching until my skin was raw. 

My eyes turned every ball of dust or speck of lint into an infestation. I would unmake and remake the bed until I was sure the mattress was absolutely spotless. I would scour the room top to bottom, sometimes spending entire hours cleaning. And instead of scolding me, my foster mom would bring me a bucket of cleaning supplies and even offer to help sometimes. 

She taught me how to clean properly, and what chemicals to use where, and what not to mix. And though I wasn’t able to go outside without a breakdown, I felt confident in her abilities to keep a clean home. 

I could wander the house without falling into a panic, and that was enough for then. I rarely spoke, but my foster mom did enough talking for the both of us. She said so long as I could nod, she would do the rest. 

I wanted to make her proud, I promised myself I would heal, if only for her sake. Someone believed in me, and I wanted to believe it too. 

—————

At my old house I slept on an uncovered mattress on the floor. The folds housed a seemingly endless army of bed bugs that left me covered in little itchy red bumps. And sometimes if I cried to my mom enough, she would even have one of her boyfriends spray it down with Raid. Which did little more than irritate my lungs, but it made me feel safer regardless. 

I used to keep my light on all night in an effort to keep the roaches away. But I found that left on long enough even the roaches could get used to its sickly yellow glow. And no amount of toxic spray could keep them away from the garbage and decay that littered the apartment. 

The smell clung to my skin and clothes, mildew, mold, and cigarette smoke. But I hardly noticed it anymore, having gotten used to all terrible scents in the apartment but one. Moms medicine, which smelled distinctly different from everything else. At least once a week, clouds of smoke would drift from my moms room that cut through the stench. A chemical burning smell that made me feel woozy, and usually marked the start of my moms sleeping cycle. 

She told me she was sick, and needed to take her medicine or else she would die. She would sell anything worth money, and to my protest I would always hear something along the lines of “You don’t want your mommy to die do you?”. I started deliberately damaging my toys in an effort to make them less desirable for sale, and made an extreme effort to hide the one dvd I possessed. 

Season one of the Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles was my prized possession. Ever since one of her boyfriends found it on sale at a blockbuster for me, I would play it on repeat on my tiny DVD player. How ironic it was that a bargain-bin dvd purchased as an afterthought to keep me distracted meant everything to me. 

After taking her medicine, she would spend a few days in bed. During this time she was impossible to communicate with, nodding off in the middle of conversations and speaking nonsense. And even though I had to fend for myself during this time, it was the lesser of her two evils. 

When she didn’t have her medicine for long enough she would become erratic. It felt like being in a cage with an agitated animal, she would jump between extreme emotions while ranting at me or anyone who would listen. Sometimes she became super-mom for a day, buying me toys, feeding me, bathing me, being kind. 

These days made me incredibly anxious, knowing that at any moment she would flip like a switch. Beneath her surface lurked a predator wearing the skin of kindness and motherhood. She would care for me with all the tension of a sharp object in a taught plastic bag, and I felt every razor thin moment. Sometimes I wished she would die, and the guilt of it consumed me. 

—————

In my new home, I had become increasingly agitated at my slow recovery. I felt guilty that despite all of my foster parent’s efforts, I had barely made any progress. My frustration grew so intensely that the next time I saw a bug I became enraged. And despite my near overwhelming fear, I charged at it and squashed it under my shoe. 

At first I felt intense nausea and anxiety so badly I fell to my knees. I began preparing for another episode, crying and shaking until I was a withered dehydrated husk. But instead, the growing feeling was suddenly swept away by intense satisfaction. A euphoric moment that made me feel not just happy, but powerful. 

My foster parents cheered at the news, reinforcing my own joy at having conquered my fear. It felt so good to make them proud, and to take control over something that tormented my life if even in a small way. I felt so good in fact, that I spent the whole next day scouring the house for another bug to squash. 

And when I did, I ground its meager frame into a smear under my shoe. And again I felt a euphoric release that sent tingles down my spine. Every subsequent bug I smashed brought me another step toward healing, I was able to walk around the house without fear. I became more talkative and even started playing with my foster siblings. 

Now I was eagerly searching for bugs to smoosh, searching in every dusty corner of the house. It became my new obsession, each insect destroyed brought me hours of relief. It’s like each time I did, my fear faded more. 

Only my anxiety never really went away, it simply changed. Suddenly if I went an entire day without killing a bug, I felt my panic return tenfold. An itch that if left ignored would grow into a panic attack more severe than before. And unable to reliably find bugs inside the house to satisfy my needs, I began exploring the garden outside. 

Again I was met with extraordinary praise at my steps outside, everyone viewed it as a huge leap forward. Of course I never told them why I was driven to go outside, as I felt ashamed. And further still I was beginning to feel less and less of a release with every bug extinguished. The time between the craving became shorter, and the payoff less satisfying each time and it was starting to scare me.

—————

Most meals at my old home consisted of chips that went on sale, and the occasional pizza or microwave slop. More often than not I would be going to sleep hungry, I was skinny even for a kid and sometimes the men that came around felt pity. Occasionally they’d give me a candy bar or a few bucks for the vending machine, but never anything more than a snack.

I learned to stop telling my mom I was hungry, as that seemed to agitate her most of all. I was an ungrateful nuisance as she often reminded me, and that she must be the worst mom in the world. God I wish just once I would have told her it was true, that she was indeed terrible. That I was a half starved, neglected little girl and she had failed me miserably. 

Instead I would comfort my own mother, a child grasping at sweetened lies to placate her outbursts. I would have to assure her she was a good mom, and everything was alright and that I loved her. As I knew all too well her wallowing could turn into utter vitriol at any moment. 

The lice and bed bugs became so bad, I began sleeping in our grime-stained bathtub. Stuffing blankets and pillows between me and the cold fiberglass tub, I would drift to sleep to the buzzing sound of the vent fan above. Its mechanical lull acting as a sound mask for the happenings of the apartment at night.

—————

My fears became realized as I smashed my next bug and felt hardly anything at all. Until all at once the relief faded entirely, and I became inconsolable for days. I hardly ate or left my room, it felt like I was backsliding on all of my progress and worst of all letting my foster family down. 

In my desperation I went scouring for another bug, hoping that even just for a moment I might feel relief again. After a frantic search I found a beetle on the back porch. And this time instead of stepping on it, I reached down and plucked it from the ground. 

Instantly I had shuddered involuntarily, my stomach lurching at the sensation of its chitinous body flailing against my hand. It squirmed desperately, attempting to pull itself free of my grasp. I struggled against my own body, seeing flashes in my vision. I remember seeing hundreds of flies buzzing around my head as I fled back to my room with the beetle. I clenched my eyes shut in fear of the swarm and felt my way back to my room. 

In the darkness of my eyelids I saw my mothers face, her expression mirroring the very last time I had seen her. An icy terror shot through my body, before my knees buckled and I fell to the ground. When I opened my eyes the flies where gone and I was in my room. And in my hand sat the beetle.

Without thinking I unfurled my hand, reached down, and pulled a leg from the beetles body. I watched it flail wildly on its back and felt a tinge of relief. So again I reached down and pulled another leg from its body. This time pulling a lump of soft flesh from its socket along with it, and I felt my own body relax in response. 

Slowly I pulled its limbs off one at a time, and this time felt more than relief. I felt a wave of satisfaction at it’s torturous death, as if I was transferring my own suffering into it’s tiny form. This is what I was looking for, relief and pleasure intertwined, my own medicine, just like mom. 

Soon I began experimenting with different methods, cutting pieces off them, burning them with matches, impaling them, I even drowned a few, though that method was never very precise. I would restrain them with scotch tape and poke them with needles, sometimes even heating it over a candle first. I was always looking for new ways to inflict suffering on them, and each one brought me more joy than the last. 

I would do this several times a day, and unlike just squashing them, the relief never started fading. But even as damaged as I was I knew it was wrong. Or at least I was afraid of letting my foster family down again. I was afraid of getting caught, afraid of being deprived of my outlet, and I was afraid I’d get in trouble. 

————

I spent a lot of time daydreaming at the old apartment, anything to help my mind escape from my real situation. I would pretend the ninja turtles showed up to take me on adventures and eat pizza and take me to a real school. 

Sometimes I’d get so lost in my pretend I’d go several days without so much as knocking on my moms bedroom door. Until one week the bugs had gotten worse than ever, clusters of flies in every room, roaches on the walls, nowhere was safe. 

Despite my fear of angering my mother, I reached a breaking point when I woke in the middle of the night to the feeling of a roach crawling across my arm. I sobbed outside her door, a howling cry hoping to wake her up without needing to enter. Usually this many days after taking her medicine she would come out needing more. 

But lost in my own world, I had failed to notice she hadn't so much as left her room in days. I knocked and called out her name for what must have been hours, too afraid to open it without permission and warrant her usual beating. I debated leaving the apartment for outside help, but felt sure that would earn me a beating as well.

—————

I tried to stop, really I did. But by then my attachment to torturing insects was inseparable. I couldn’t eat or sleep without it, if I refrained for even a day I would completely break down. But by then I was socializing and leaving the house, I was a whole new person. 

But to remain this way, I began spending my allowance at the local pet store. I’d buy containers full of feeder bugs, crickets, mealworms, roaches, just about anything I could get my hands on. I’d stash the live bugs in a shoebox where I kept my instruments of torture. I took great care to stash the box in different places so no one could find it, I wasn’t allowed to have matches and lighters after all. 

The pet store workers knew me by name now, I had to make up a fabricated story about having chickens I would hand feed. An uncomfortable lie that I had slowly begun to believe myself, I even debated asking my foster parents if I could get chickens. 

By now I was torturing bugs nearly every moment I had alone, no longer just to curb my anxiety, but for pleasure. It felt amazing, and no other description would do it justice. A few times I stayed up all night, capitalizing on the free time to indulge in my sadistic ecstasy. 

I was twelve years old, old enough to know better, old enough to understand what I was doing. But I was addicted, that was the truth of it, helplessly addicted and no one noticed until it was too late. 

—————

As I sat crying fruitlessly at my mothers door, I could make out a dull humming sound coming from within the room. It reminded me of the bathroom fan and the ice machine down the hall, its lull called to me, and despite my fear I finally tried opening the door. 

For a moment I struggled against a small pile of clothes and towels, the room was dim and smelt foul. So much worse than the rest of the house that I pinched my nose shut before pushing my way inside. 

Only then did I realize what the hum was, a cloud of flies filled the room, emanating a menacing buzz that drowned out all other sound. I ducked as my movement stirred up the swarm, hundreds of flies pushing out into the apartment as I stepped toward the bed. 

The room was illuminated by a single shaded lamp on the bedside, and in its yellow glow I could make out my mothers shape. I weakly pulled myself onto her bed and crawled towards her, calling out her name. As I neared her legs I felt a cold wetness soak into my stained pajamas. The smell of the liquid caused me to gag again, it felt slimy against my skin. 

To my horror I realized she was coated in insects of all kinds, flies, roaches, gnats, a disgusting swarm the size of which I’d never seen. Her face was covered in a writhing mask of dark bodied bugs, overlapping and scrambling about her skin. 

I tried shaking her awake, all the while calling out “mom! Mom, wake up! Mom please!” 

Her body felt cold under my hands, and unsure how else to help her I began batting the bugs away from her face. I sobbed as I felt them on my hands, I smacked and hit and batted at them. I could see more crawling from her nose and deflated sockets, clambering over each other to escape my attention. Her face seemed to writhe with them, her nostrils distending over their bodies as they freed themselves like living blackheads. 

Her mouth was agape, and from it poured insects and maggots and a foul smell that caused me to vomit over her cold chest. I cried and begged and shook her as violently as my thin frame would allow. Until I couldn't take the swarming insects anymore, and I fled the apartment. A half starved, filthy, nine year old girl, screaming for someone to stop the bugs. 

————

I thought I could hide the bug box forever, I even began planning a permanent spot outside the home. My little shoebox was nearly full of implements and containers of live insects, and I planned on expanding my operation. 

I got greedy, I started swiping scissors and kitchen knives. Things my foster family noticed missing, things I lied about without second thought. I even shifted blame to my younger foster brother, after all I was much too old to do something like that. 

But by then things had gotten out of hand, I craved the feeling at all hours. I would get physically sick if I tried stopping for even a single day. Some sessions went for hours upon hours and I would become agitated whenever I was interrupted. I hated the bugs so much, but I loved how they made me feel.

I watched them, bisection, segmentation, immolation.  
I hurt them, Inpaled, crippled, drowned, amputated.   
I destroyed them, crushing, stabbing, tearing.   
I hate them, I hate them, I hate them.

And everything would have been just fine, if my brother hadn’t found my bug box. On that day I had just got back home with a fresh container of crickets and caught him in my room. He was holding a lighter and kitchen knife, missing items I had blamed him for. 

“I’m telling mom, you’re in so much trouble” he chimed at me. 

I stepped toward him while he continued talking, but the only sound I could hear was the buzzing. The rumble of a thousand flies emanating from his mouth, I felt terror overtake me. I watched in horror as dozens of insects began clambering over the edges of his face toward his mouth and eyes. 

I screamed as they filled his orifices, as they wriggled beneath his flesh, yet his mouth continued moving but no words came out. He dropped the knife and lighter as I screamed, “No more bugs!”

Suddenly I was on top of him, pinning him in place, the knife tightly clutched in my hand. I continued to shriek.  
“Stop it! No more!”

I brought the knife down on the swarm, their carapaced bodies crunching under the blade.   
My brother wrenched violently underneath me, but I held him firmly in place, his frame much smaller than mine. I wouldn’t let the bugs kill him, I wouldn’t get in trouble, I wouldn’t let this happen. 

“No more bugs!” 

Again and again I brought the knife down on the bugs, killing more and more with every stab. But the swarm on his face was relentless and so densely packed, that I couldn’t make out his features below. 

“No more bugs!”

Frantically I continued to stab, each thrust sounded wetter and meatier than the last. I could feel crushed insectiod organs and juices coating the front of my body, the knife, my hands.

“No more bugs!” 

I continued until the handle was too slick to grip, and my arms were shaking with exhaustion. And as I looked down at him, the buzzing stopped, the fog lifted. And I realized what I had done. 

“No more bugs.”
