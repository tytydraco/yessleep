Today:   
Half past 10 tonight, I heard a knock on the front door, and when I opened it, no one was there, but resting on the welcome mat was a little mason jar with a filled up halfway with a bubbling substance. I reached down to pick it up, sealing the jar tight, and waited for the doughy mess to suffocate. I vowed to never mess with fermentation again after last years’ sourdough nightmare. 

—--------  
Let me explain. Last year, my grandmother disappeared without a trace, and I consequently inherited her sourdough starter after being sent to do some cleaning of her house. I know it doesn’t make any sense, but I think my grandma fed herself to the starter. 

Hear me out— we were super close, but about a month ago, she started acting kinda weird. She kept sneaking down to the basement with this massive cookbook. She was probably brewing something down there. 

On the day I went to her house, I worked my way up to her bedroom, looking for any last sign from her before packing up sweaters. In her drawer rested a single envelope with my name carefully printed on it. I tried to gently peel the flap open, and inside was a rusty key with a frayed piece of paper folded underneath it. I took the paper, and in the same pristine handwriting as the outside of the envelope, this piece simply said: *Feed Doughlores*. *Lock the door.* 

I didn’t realize she was also keeping a pet in the basement, but key in hand, I faced the basement door. No one ever went down there besides my grandma, and I didn’t know what kind of pet she had been  hiding down there. These last few weeks, she had been going in and out  all the time, sometimes multiple times in a few hours. 

I reached for the doorknob, and the key slid  in seamlessly. I twisted my hand clutching the key, and the door creaked open. It was a windowless basement, and as I took a step forward onto the first stair, fumbling for the light switch I was engulfed in the tangy smelling darkness. 

I took another step, and felt something squish beneath my shoe. I yelped, lost my footing and should have slid the rest of the way down the steps, but the further down I got, the more this gooey substance stuck to me. I fought my way out of it enough to stand up and reach around for the light switch. 

I flicked the switch up and suddenly the basement didn’t seem dark anymore. The walls weren’t the cinderblock I remembered, the steps and walls were coated in a  fleshy colored substance— not quite liquid, not quite solid, and it was bubbling. Angrily. The light reflected across the fleshy bubbles as if they were wet, making the room look too bright to be from one lightbulb

I turned to leave, hoping to wash off whatever this was, lock the door, and pretend I never found the key. But when I went to reach for the key, I realized it had long since fallen out of my hand and into the roiling  mess. 

I turned back towards the basement. It was almost as if…I second guessed myself in the moment, it was \*almost\* as if the bubbles were forming into letters. 

*“Hi darling”* it spelled out, undulating the words.  

“No.” I remember saying it aloud and made my way back to the top of the stairs. If I could get out, I could wait until someone else came home to help. But it followed, inching towards me with lumpy tendrils outstretched, like arms inviting me back. I tried to reason with myself. If I made it to the top of the stairs, I could get back to the main part of the house. But, I had no way of locking it in there, and if it could get up stairs, who’s to say it couldn’t open doors? 

“What are you?” I pleaded, begging for this to be some sort of hallucination. 

I could hear the bubbles popping and rearranging. It was not so different from that of cicadas in the summer— a low buzz that you can’t quite see. It looked like it was breathing, small sighs, pulsing the thing up and down.  “*Doughlores”* it spelled in that all-too-familiar pristine handwriting. 

Shit. *This* is what I had to take care of?  

If nothing else, I figured I could go upstairs and regroup. I nudged the spongy tendrils back with the toe of my shoe. Shoving the door closed behind me, and wedging a chair under the knob, I scanned the kitchen for anything to help. 

A massive cookbook was resting conveniently open to a page on sourdough starter maintenance. So *that’s* what it was. Dotted with what looked like to a dried version of the substance. Between sections covered with the stuff, I made out the idea that you need to provide your starter with consistent daily feedings of flour and water. 

I figured maybe Doughlores was just hangry, so I searched each cabinet for flour, disappointed when I couldn’t find any. As I closed the weighty cookbook, defeated, I sliced my pointer finger on the page. Blood instantly began pooling, and as I reached for a napkin to soak up the blood, I heard pounding on the basement door. 

*Thunk. Thunk. Thunkkkkk.* The door burst open, and Doughlores swept into into the kitchen. It paused for a second, as if considering what to do. 

*“Okay?”* It spelled, before springing forward onto me, latching onto my bleeding finger. I watched Doughlores’ oozing, stringy webs crawl up my palm. It was as if the glob wanted to comfort me, and I was reminded of countless scraped knees. I would come in sobbing and my grandma would clean it up, then embrace me in a warm hug until I stopped crying and she would say, “Okay?” and then I would respond with a meek “okay”. 

Surely this was some sort of coincidence. I was seeing things. I couldn’t bear the idea of this messy glob taking care of me the way my grandma would.

“I DON’T WANT A HUG DOUGHLORES!” I yelled, trying to scrape the thing off me. It was at this point I resented every single person who had ever brought one of these stupid beings into life. Call me crazy, but I don’t think food should need to be fed.   
  
It didn’t care. I watched as it inched up my fingers, until engulfing my whole hand. My fingers were stuck together, cemented, as Doughlores tried to ooze on.

“It’s just a bacteria. It’s not my grandma,” I whispered to myself. So then why was it wheezing like her? 

My hand twitched, and I felt Doughlores entering further into my bloodstream, taking over. If I ran it under water, I’d only be giving Doughlores more strength. So, I did what seemed most rational at the time. I picked up the giant cookbook with my unscathed hand, raising it up in the air, leaving my cemented sourdough hand on the counter, and then I dropped the cookbook. 

There was no blood and no pain, just a quick, clean snap. What was once my hand turned into a crumpled pile of dried flour and water, broken off cleanly as if I were a marble statue.  

“*Ow”* it spelled. 

I didn’t apologize and I certainly did not wait around to see what else it would say. I can honestly say I’ve never run so fast in my life and I have not returned since.

Today: 

  
My grandma’s body was never found, not that anyone was looking for it. I figured I should write everything down now in case anything happens. I’m going to go check on the jar. 

No. No. NO. Pressure built up inside of the jar and it exploded all over the kitchen. The freaky oozy fleshy substance is seeping over my kitchen.

*“Sorry”* it spelled. 

 It’s expanded into the hallway, and covered the door. There’s no way out. The front door is cemented shut. Those lumpy tendrils reach out to me, waiting to embrace me. 

I think I'm going to give it a hug.
