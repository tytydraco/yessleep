There was one last step left in the ritual. We were almost there.

I read off my phone.

“And after you blow out all the candles, all you have to do is wait. *Midnight* will hit, and soon after, *The Visitor* will arrive. An old God. A *failed* God, dressed in robes. Relegated now, but still holding vast power over our domain. Be careful before you let it in.”

I paused for dramatic effect, looking between my two friends and the stranger, cellphone glare playing the role of flashlight to illuminate my expression. 

“And no matter what, *do what it says.* Or else.”

I hammed up the last line like I was in theater class.

Trevor glanced down at his watch, then back up to me. “Alright, so do we…?”

“*Yes,*” I said. “Let’s.” 

I blew a dragon’s breath of air to extinguish the candles arranged in the shape of a sigil on the table we were seated around. Our already dark room turned *darker*—pitch black alleviated only by the light we’d left on in the hallway.

And then we waited. *Stillness.*

A cold tingle crept up my spine.

That was, until a voice I didn’t want to hear cut through the silence—

“And sorry, just checking, we’re *sure* the family who lives here won’t be coming back tonight?”

“Shhh,” I responded softly.

“Hey, Tay? *Manners,*” snapped Trevor. 

“I’m sorry but we’re six minutes from midnight. Can’t we just enjoy the ambience?” I asked.

“Absolutely, just, y’know, trying to be careful before the spooky happenings start,” she continued.

I exhaled. Not so subtly. Trevor was staring daggers. I fought hard to access the fake, masking kindness I hated to tap into on Friday nights.

“*Yes,* we’re sure.” I said. “The Trasks own like three other mansions. They’re literally never here. We’re fine.”

“It’s true,” Mikey started. “This is pretty much our go-to spot when we’re trying to do ghost stuff—”

“*No*—not *ghost stuff,* Mikey” I corrected. “Supernatural stuff. And I mean, even that’s a sort of loaded and vague word for what we’re trying to do. Point being—*we don’t have to dumb ourselves down, guys.* Even if we have a—” I caught myself just as Trevor’s cyclops eyes beamed through me, “*very lovely guest.*” 

Truthfully, my beef wasn’t purely with *her.* It was with the situation. You see, when you’re part of the cool kids table as myself and my two compatriots were, for all the *pros* that came with being at the top of the highschool social hierarchy—and certainly, there were many—the trade-off was that anything remotely out of vogue or mainstream, anything unusable for vapid social media posting, and anything unaligned with perpetual inaction showing even a fraction of earnest interest, was frowned upon. So much so that our foray into ‘supernatural stuff’ had to be done in secret. Quietly. With no fanfare.

And now, I was sitting across from someone, someone doing her best to climb the same social ladder I was sick of. *Dating Trevor* to escape irrelevancy, forcing herself to be somewhere she didn’t even want to be.

I looked at the time. Three minutes to midnight, now.

“And so, if I might ask, have any of you seen something ‘out of the ordinary’ before?” she asked again.

“Yes.” Me.

“Nah.” Trevor.

“*Kind of?*” Mikey.

I took in the surprising divergence. “What? *Guys?*”

Trevor started, “I mean, if you’re talking about the night with the ouija board—”

“Yes, *obviously* I’m talking about that night—”

“Well, uh, I mean those… boards, they kind of like—work subconsciously, right? Like, if you really want something to happen—”

I stared at him, annoyed he picked this moment to put a megaphone to his skepticism.

“Then it’ll happen,” he finished.

And I realized it really, truly was crazy how much people could change their viewpoints depending on who was around them. 

I got up. “I’m gonna go grab another drink from downstairs.”

“*Wait, but,* shouldn’t we all stay together just in case—”

I interrupted her. “Let’s not kid ourselves.”

I left the room, hearing on my way out the muted whispers and restrained gesticulations that usually arose whenever something awkward happened.

*People really don’t realize how obvious they are.* Doubly so in the case of friends who forget you know *exactly* how they operate. They’ll talk and worry about you, thinking they’re being subtle about it.

I trudged down the illuminated hallway. It was funny—like Mikey had said, we’d been at this giant manor with enviable square footage many, many times now. So much so that what initially elicited jealousy and awe now just felt like any other place.

I heard the echo of soft, jogging footsteps come through behind me. An awkward run.

“*Tay,* if you could just hold for a second,” she said. I stopped. I suppressed emotion.

I turned. Faced Liz. 

“Actually I forgot to mention,” I said. “I prefer to go by Taylor.”

She froze, having closed the distance. “Right. *Sorry?* I kept hearing them call you Tay, so I must’ve mistakenly thought—”

“What can I do for you?” I asked.

“*Right.* I just, uhm—I don’t know if we’ve maybe gotten off on the wrong foot or something, but I, you know, wanted to…”

I waited for her to drop the word salad and get to the meat of whatever she was trying to say.  
“Just wanted to thank you, really, for all of your patience with my questions.”

“Don’t mention it.”

“Just trying to, y’know, be as *informed* a member of this crew as I can.”

“You say *crew* as if it’s permanent.”

She did a quick double take. “Well right, Trevor said you guys get together every other Friday. That it’s been a thing for years now?”

“He said that?”

“Yeah… yeah he did.”

“That’s so interesting. It sounds like he *really* wants you to be involved.”

“Yeah,” she laughed, “I don’t know, I mean, maybe?”

I smiled. “I love that.”

She smiled back—a more sincere one. Seemingly, anyways.

“I’m gonna go steal that drink,” I said. “Let me know if you want anything.”

“I’m alright, thanks—” and then she paused deliberately as if to show attention to detail, “*Taylor.*” 

She hit me with the ol’ finger gun as she said it. 

I turned back towards the staircase, curl of my lips dissolving to frown the second she couldn’t see my face. I granted myself permission for a thorough eye roll, then descended down the grand steps to the main lobby.

Not for a bevvie, actually, but for a quick, meaningful respite. Alone time. An energy reset.

*KNOCK. KNOCK. KNOCK.* 

I froze at the bottom of the steps.

Time slowed for just a moment, as I thought—

And conjured up the near-preposterous amount of rituals our group had done over the last few years, all of them leading to *nothing of substance happening.*

After all, *nothing ever happened, really.* Even when I tried to convince myself otherwise.  
All of this was just play. Desperate, miserable attempts to broach the ether. 

*KNOCK. KNOCK. KNOCK.* 

I glanced back to the top of the staircase. Trevor and Mikey had joined Liz—all three standing motionless, huddled together in the hallway.

“*Is that the Trasks?*” Trevor whispered loudly from up top. 

“Why would they be knocking on their own door?” I whispered back.

It took me a moment to internalize the words I’d instinctively blurted.

I edged forward methodically, my heartbeat loud in my ribcage, wondering what this strange new feeling I was feeling was as I crept to the front door.

My careful steps were drowned out by Mikey and Trevor—with stranger in tow—rushing down the stairs in a too-fast tiptoe.

My eyeball met peephole.

By the time I saw the towering, robed figure through the tiny glass circle, I was flanked by my crew. In a way, I didn’t have to take in the staggering sight alone.

An old God. A relegated God. Just outside on the doorstep.

*A visitor.*

And two simultaneous feelings filled me—first, a whirlpool of *awe* at the revelation that after years of throwing aimlessly at the dartboard, something supernatural had stuck.

And second, in tandem, an oppressive wave of fear washing over me. Fear that maybe any bravery that came from throwing said darts at said dartboard was emboldened by the subconscious belief that nothing would actually ever hit paydirt. 

*KNOCK. KNOCK. KNOCK.* 

And this time, I saw the motion bring about the sound. 

“*It’s real,*” I said, detaching from my narrow view of what waited outside. 

Trevor looked at me, astonished, clearly in denial. “*How are you making that sound happen?”*

“We need to let it in,” I said, ignoring him. 

“*Don’t* let it in,” said Liz. My eyes went to her. *We* were our trio, and *she* was meters away, standing at the bottom of the stairs grabbing her arm. Nervous. Stressed.

I stifled a bitter laugh. “I knew you didn’t care about any of this stuff.”

With that, I reached for the doorknob. Trevor was pressed against where I just was, face wearing a level of concern I’d never quite seen on him.

“Tay, wait,” he started, “maybe we should—”

I unlocked, pulled it open, forcing Trev to step back. And just like that, there it was—

*The visitor*. Looking completely out of this world. Midnight curling behind it like a backdrop.

And from where I stood—inside a mansion that wasn’t even mine, in the remote suburbs that suddenly felt a bit too remote—it hit me.

And my breath caught.

In fact, I felt the quickening breath of everyone around me.

And I’m not sure why, but the dumbest word came to my mind.

“Yes?” I asked, as if we hadn’t invited it.

“*Will you let me enter?*” it responded, in what sounded like a sea of whispers, overlapping yet clashing like a choir falling out of sync. 

I couldn’t move. It felt like I’d forgotten how to exhale. I was struggling to conjure up new words. It was like—for a moment—the English language had all but left my mind.

But there was now a void where an answer should’ve come, and no one else from our group was providing it. Hence, I spoke again, the repetition of the word coming *not* from a sense of inquisitiveness, but rather dread that a response unaligned would bring about an even more horrible recourse than yielding.

“Yes.” 

I backed away. Mikey and Trevor both split to separate sides to make way for the red carpet.

And with the sea now parted, the visitor advanced with deliberate, drawn out steps. I grappled with the unreality of the presence—a layering of darkened robes obscuring the being in such a way that I couldn’t make sense of what was beneath. A clattering sound with each movement, and an energy emanating that felt like nothing I’d ever experienced before.  
It looked around, presumably taking in the sight of the place, before landing its gaze on Liz. 

And as that gaze lingered, I heard it—a series of soft, mechanical clicks. I turned to see Mikey, having stealthily taken out his phone, the flash of his camera cutting right through the dark.

Then I heard a *snap,* and an electronic *crackle,* and the distinct sound of glass fracturing. The phone’s light blinked out, and just like that, the expensive device looked fried—jammed, shattered, instantly useless.

“Wait, what the fu—”

“*Shh*.” I cut off Mikey’s pointless frustration at his own stupidity in the face of supernature. My eyes then flitted to Liz, *still horrified,* Trevor rushing to grab her hand and comfort her.

Despite it all, I was still annoyed she was here.

The visitor shifted away from its singular focus—*seemingly*, it was a bit hard to tell with its face obscured beneath a hooded robe—and addressed the group.

“*Who are you?*” the collection of whispers asked.

We all just stared. Stupid as it was, whatever had caught my throat had clearly caught everyone else’s as well. Inaction felt safe, and the sandgrains of *wonder* and *awe and amazement* were trickling through to the other side of the hourglass marked by fear. Fear of the unknown. Fear of fucking up. I didn’t have the luxury of saying “yes” again. And I was afraid of saying something new.

“The guide you read said do what it asks, *no matter what,*” said Liz, hushed but forceful. “I have to assume that means *answering* it too.”

I snapped to. “I’m—I’m Taylor. Taylor Farris.”

“Liz Vale.” Then, she corrected herself. “Elizabeth Vale.” 

“Mikey—sorry, *Michael Bridgeford,*” he said, with alien kindness.”Sir, or… *Ma’am?*” he tacked on. 

“Trevor Rolfe.”

We let our self-declarations stand in the air.

No inhalations, movements or expressions from the entity to suggest contemplation or receipt. 

It just lingered, as we shuffled eyes at each other, and I hoped, with a somewhat growing frustration, that someone—like our other guest for the evening—wouldn’t jump the gun with a stupid question.

Finally—after seconds that felt like separate lifetimes—the entity shifted forward.

It extended a covered arm, revealing a long, spindled hand with too many joints. The kind of hand that might’ve passed for human if you weren’t looking closely. 

Unfortunately we were all looking closely.

Just as I wondered if it was looking for a handshake next—

It spoke again. 

“*Give me something of utmost importance to you.”*

The words reverberated in my ear as if played on loop.

Immediately, I pulled out my wallet. Something of *financial weight? Sentimental weight?* Bank cards and identification were all I could find. *My hands—*rings on them but none of them particularly costly or important—

My eyes flicked to Mikey—not one for self-preservation it seemed—comically doing what seemed like a pitch.

“I have this coin,” he said, searching. “A lucky coin.” He dug it out of his wallet and then held it in front of him—

And suddenly, I felt a pinch on my heart. A painful pinch. 

*And static*. A loud static ringing in my ears. Unbearable.

Desperately wondering if the bullet train of affliction was hitting just me, my gaze darted to the others to catch them reeling as well.

“What’s… what’s happening to me—” Trevor.

“...Fuck…” Liz. 

Mikey, hand still outstretched, seemed to be in the worst hell of all.

“*Don’t try trickery*,” the visitor said, a swarm of angry voices, “*it won’t end well*.”

I struggled to keep myself together, and looked and thought—desperately.

*Something important.*

*There was only one thing I had that was really—important.*

My locket.

*Fuck.*

I tore it from my neck, hoping to kill the eardrum-rupturing noise and the pangs at my heart that were bordering on *attack* at this point—nevermind the sinking sensation overtaking me—

“This!” I shouted, dropping it into the aberration’s hand. “*This* is the most important thing I have.”

And no sooner than I dropped it did the static cease, the squeeze on my heart stop, and numerous other ailments in misery, generalized pain and lost oxygen that I hadn’t even clocked vanish.

I took breath back from the world—then, selfishness subsiding—brought my focus to the others—

Liz rushing over, in pain—

“Photo of my old friends… from before I had to move here… take it everywhere I go—”

And she placed it, and near-immediately I saw relief hit her too.

“Guitar pick,” said Trevor, struggling. “Dad got it from a Joe Satriani show.” He forked it over.

Meanwhile, Mikey was collapsed against the ground at this point, struggling, straining as if his   
soul and body both were folding in on themselves.

“*Mikey,*” said Trevor, post-torture, “*for fuck’s sake you have to give it something right now!*”

“I… don’t have… anything on me… that’s important…” he said, looking as if he were about to throw up his own skull.

I rushed over, dug them out of his pockets—his wallet and busted phone.

I threw both onto the visitor’s hand, then tagged, for justification—“all of his cards, IDs, his phone sim, it’s all here. The value of these—in terms of time it takes to replace them—is *unimaginable.*” I stressed it. “He takes *forever* to replace things.” Sensing no movement. “Laziest guy I’ve ever met in my life.”  

The entity turned its full attention to Mikey.

“It’s…” he choked out, “*true.*”

I looked at him, then back at the visitor—nothing at all to decode or make sense of the madness, until—

“*Very well.*”

Then I heard the dramatic release of Mikey’s exhale. Coughing. Breathing. 

I resettled back into my body.

“*Jesus fucking Christ*,” said Mikey, gathering his bearings.

“*That’s… insane*,” said Trevor, under his breath, half-fixed on the visitor, half-side-eyeing the front door as if contemplating bolting for it. 

I felt Liz come up beside me. 

“Are you happy now?” she whispered pointedly. “Is *this* what you were so keen on?” Her first show of any backbone to me. 

“You didn’t have to be here tonight—” 

“That photo meant a lot to me—” 

“That *locket* meant a lot to me, and *again, no one asked you to be here*—” 

“I told her to come,” Trevor butted in, “now, respectfully guys, calm it the *fuck* down, we are staring death *right in the face.*”

I shifted my gaze to him. “You asked her to come?”

He kept his eyes locked ahead, as I realized my anger was misfiring, messily deflecting from the real fear clawing at me. At the thing still standing just feet away. I re-centered myself.

The entity swept its hooded, faceless gaze over all of us. And then, it said its next command.

“*Bleed.*”

And this time, I didn’t want to wait. Didn’t want to risk an escalating punishment for taking too long to fulfill the task.

I clocked Liz rushing to the kitchen. The rest of us made our way over as well.

Inside, she was already desperately flinging open cupboards and cabinets, looking for a vehicle to unlock life force. She finally found the one she was looking for. The one that housed—

*Kitchen knives.*

She secured hers, left the drawer open so we could grab our own, and addressed us as we did.

“*Okay*,” she started, “presuming the task is literal and presuming it’s not looking for an *ungodly* amount of blood, I think the best bet would be for us to cut ourselves open in front of him—*it?*” She did a mock demonstration in front of us. “A quick flick across your fingertip. It’ll hurt, but it’s the safest spot.” Her eyes flashed to Mikey. “So no slashing across your hand because you saw it in an occult flick. Good way to kill yourself.”

“Remind me to cheat off you in Biology,” I said, as we headed back together in a group—

“Brave of you to assume we’re going to survive this,” she muttered under her breath, just loud enough for me to barely hear, and before I could parse how I felt about her reflecting back the same energy I’d been giving her all night—

We were already back, in front of the visitor, standing in a line, equipped with our instruments. 

I felt the throb in my chest and static shriek in my skull come rushing back in tandem.

“Carefully,” said Liz, guiding, “but intentionally.” She held out her index finger, then sliced across the tip with the blade, letting out the slightest squeal of pain as she did. 

I pricked next—*ouch.* Held my finger out, as the red droplet fell from appendage to ground.

It was funny, in a way. We’d all subconsciously picked and aligned our way of presenting our offering to the visitor. A sort of theatrical display. 

And with the crescendoing pain and mechanical whirring in my head immediately subsiding, it seemed like we’d picked correctly.

I watched Trevor take his cut next, and then shifted to Mikey, who seemed to have really nicked himself. “*Gah, fuck!*” he yelped, holding his bleeding finger with his hand.

“She said *carefully,*” I said.

And now the floor of the main lobby of the third or fourth mansion of the Trask estate had been successfully marked with everyone’s crimson—right in front of the imposing presence that I—and, I assumed, the others—were hoping would be gone.

And I was being forced to reconcile with some of the bad math I’d been doing in my head over the last few years.

We scanned one another—just to confirm no one was still climbing whatever invisible pain-rung the visitor was capable of pulling us up. 

Meanwhile, with whatever degree the word ‘carefully’ applied to this towering supernatural being, it shifted away and started ascending the expansive staircase up to the second level.

We used the reprieve we’d been granted.

“Did the doc say anything about making the visitor disappear?” Liz. 

“I… I can’t remember.” Me.

“Or like, maybe ways to *kill it*?” Liz.

“No, it… no it definitely didn’t have that, but more importantly you *shouldn’t assume it can’t hear us—*we don’t know what the fuck we’re dealing with right now!” Me. 

“Okay, so what, we’re just stuck *forever?*”

“It was called ‘*the visitor’* in the guide—I would assume *visitor* means temporary—”

“I don’t know if we can trust the linguistic continuity of a document that helped us summon a freaking demon—”

“If it was *right* about the ritual, then it’s probably *right* with the rest of its word choices—”

“Guys…” Trevor and Mikey, together now.

I looked up, my annoying sidebar disrupted by the scene of the entity standing at the top of the stairs, looking down upon us again.

“[Follow the leader](https://www.reddit.com/user/WorldAwayTweedy/)*,*” it said.
