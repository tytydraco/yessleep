I've been told before that I'm a bit of a snoop, but I'd argue that a healthy degree of curiosity is only natural when it comes to one's neighborhood. When I was a kid, while other children fantasized about having superpowers like flight or super-speed, I longed for the ability to freeze time so I could walk into peoples' houses at whim and take a look around. As I've aged, I've become all-too familiar with the isolating nature of suburbia, and my curiosity has only grown with time. Sometimes, I'm grateful for the anonymity, and other times, I am horrified by the notion that my neighbors could be dead and rotting inside their homes, and I'd be none the wiser until the authorities showed up to whisk their corpses away. 



In my spare time, I take to the internet to satisfy my curiosities. I hop from Google Maps to Zillow to public records, digging up whatever I can find about the histories of the homes in my area. It's an odd hobby, I know, but it brings me a strange kind of serenity. I have said less than 100 words to my neighbors across the street, but I know everything about their property, from the age of the house, to the number of times it's changed ownership, to the protected status of the oak trees dotting its perimeter. This knowledge soothes me despite its uselessness. It grounds me in this sprawling city, in this neighborhood with all its tall fences and security cameras. It makes me feel like I've earned a place here. 



A few days ago, I arrived home late in the evening, as usual. Exhausted and sore from my long commute, I flopped onto the couch and opened my laptop to engage in my strange pastime. Lately, I've been particularly taken with \_\_\_\_\_ Lane, which is the longest continuous residential street in my county. I was watching a YouTube video on the history of the street when a recommendation in the sidebar caught my eye: a four minute long video entitled "come\_to\_my\_house.mov." It had been posted fifteen years ago and had amassed only one-hundred and eighty-three views. I clicked on the thumbnail, which featured a wide shot of a single story house. 



The video's description read:



*9323* \_\_\_\_\_ *Lane. You're invited to my house. Everyone's invited to my house.*



Ah, I thought, so that's why the video was recommended to me: it's a house tour of a residence on the street I was researching. I clicked play, expecting the jerky, handheld enthusiasm of a realtor, but what I encountered was something far stranger. The footage was grainy and stuttery, likely captured on one of those early digital cameras from the mid-2000s. The colors were washed out, giving the mid-afternoon a sickly, jaundiced hue. It was hardly a house tour—the cameraman never even went inside the building. Instead, he walked around the house's perimeter with a measured gait, panning the camera across the beige siding. It was a standard, ranch-style home, but there was one detail that left me somewhat unsettled.



As the cameraman circled the structure, he would occasionally lift the camera and press the lens flush against the glass, attempting to give his audience a view into the various rooms. However, there was no interior to see, because the inner-side of the glass windows had been covered in what seemed to be dark gray paint. There were no reflections of furniture, no silhouettes of houseplants. There was only an opaque barrier suggesting a total, suffocating rejection of the world outside. All the while, the audio was dominated by the narrator's voice. He sounded like an old man, his accent thick and unmistakably local. He never wavered in his delivery as he recited a variation of the video's description over and over again. 



"Here she is, folks. 9323 \_\_\_\_\_ Lane. You oughta come to my house. You're invited. 9323 \_\_\_\_\_ Lane. Everyone's invited. Everyone's coming to my house."



The second the video ended, I was on Google Maps, punching in the address to the house. Lo and behold, not only was it the same street in my neighborhood, but the 9323 address is on my side of town, barely a one mile walk from my place. Oddly, though, despite the fact that the house on Google Maps matched what was in the video, I couldn't find information about the property on any of my usual websites. There was a chance it had been torn down and the Google Maps street view just hadn't been updated, but even if that was the case, it was unusual for me not to be able to find any information about the lot.



Now, I'm no social butterfly (if you couldn't already tell), but as I laid in bed that night, I thought about paying 9323 a visit. I had a feeling that the man who recorded the video was not the current resident of the house (if the house still existed). Perhaps he was a dementia-addled previous tenant, now safe and sound in a retirement home, or perhaps he was an angry neighbor taking vengeance on the residents of 9323 by plastering their address on the internet. Sure, the video was old and it hadn't exactly gone viral, but it could still be dangerous, especially if the current homeowners didn't know about it. 



The following day, before I could change my mind, I took the short walk over to \_\_\_\_\_ Lane. I came prepared with the video loaded up on my phone and a peace lily in my hands (it seemed rude to show up on a stranger's doorstep empty handed). 



When I finally reached the property, I did a double-take. The house in the video had been unremarkable: a tidy, if slightly uninteresting, stereotype of suburban life. The house that stood before me, however, was a sagging monument to neglect. The video's green lawn was now mostly dirt with a few patches of yellow, matted weeds. The exterior siding was peeling away in jagged strips, and the concrete walkway leading to the porch was riddled with cracks and holes.



I paused at the sidewalk and looked down at the curb, double-checking the faded numbers on the sidewalk gutter to ensure I hadn't wandered onto the wrong lot. It was indeed 9323. I moved forward with caution, eventually setting the peace lily on the bottom-most stair of the porch before stepping up to one of the front-facing windows. When I looked inside, I saw that same, impenetrable gray I'd seen in "come\_to\_my\_house.mov". Seemed like the paint was the one thing that had survived the fifteen years. 



All of a sudden, I heard a woman's voice. 



"Hey!" it said. "Y'all get away from there right now!" 



I quickly spun around to look at the house next door. There was an older woman standing on her porch, staring daggers into me as she waved me away with her hands. She looked like she was shooing a raccoon away from her garbage can. Embarrassed, I jogged back to the sidewalk. 



"I was just trying to talk to the owner," I said, aware of how strange I must have looked while peeking through some random person's windows. "Do you know if—"



"I don't know nothing and I don't take kindly to busybodies. Why don't y'all go on home and leave them alone." 



I tried a final time to explain why I was there, but after being swiftly reprimanded once again, I walked home with my tail between my legs. So much for getting to meet some of my neighbors. It seemed pretty obvious that no one lived at the house on \_\_\_\_\_ Lane. From the state of it, no one had likely lived there since "come\_to\_my\_house.mov" was posted to YouTube. Oh well. Case closed. Or, perhaps more accurately, case gone cold. 



That evening, the familiar quiet of my home felt less like the sanctuary it usually did. I went through my Saturday night routine—making tea, throwing on some jazz, and curling up with a book on urban planning—but a persistent sensation kept crawling up the back of my neck. It's a difficult feeling to describe; it was *not* that distinct feeling of being watched, but I still found myself rising from the couch to peek around my little house anyway. I walked through the still, quiet rooms, flicking on the light switches one by one. There was no one else in my house, but I knew something was wrong all the same. 



The bedroom in particular gave me pause. Somehow, my bed felt too large, and the room itself also seemed unnaturally cavernous. I crossed the wooden floor and ran my fingers along the cool bottom sheets, my mouth twisting at their color. After visiting 9323, gray was starting to leave a bad taste in my mouth. As I exited the bedroom, I resolved to purchase a new set of sheets, ideally one with brighter colors. 



The moment I turned on the light in the kitchen, my gaze fell upon my snake plant on the windowsill, and I immediately realized that I'd left my peace lily at the house on \_\_\_\_\_ Lane. No wonder I felt like I was forgetting something! I'd abandoned that poor plant in its quaint little pot on such a dreadful property! Despite the creepiness of the house, I knew I had to return for the plant in the morning. Call me weird for caring so much about a plant if you want, but I had worked hard for that lily, and it for me, and I didn't want to abandon it. The strange, almost lonely sensation that had befallen my home was made slightly less stifling by the realization, but I still had a difficult time falling asleep that night in my bed which felt so strangely large and cold.  



I left for my rescue mission early in the morning. The dark sky threatened rain, and I didn't want the lily to be drowned before I could retrieve it. 



As I walked, I kept finding myself looking up into a gloomy, gray ceiling. The rainclouds, so deep and dark they seemed ready to unleash a torrent at any moment, lacked the usual texture of a storm. There were no roiling edges or shifting shades; instead, the sky a flat, unyielding wash of slate.



My mind, maybe in an attempt to keep me entertained on my walk, constructed a vivid image of a girl standing on the other side of that gray expanse. She was a reflection of me, her face pressed against the exterior of the atmosphere as if it were a window. I imagined her leaning in, her fingers tracing the smooth surface of the sky. I could almost feel her frustration—that maddening sensation of knowing there was a life, a warmth, a *home* just a fraction of an inch away, yet knowing she would never be invited inside.



Before I fully registered that my walk was over, I was standing at the base of the steps, looking down at what I could only assume was some kind of convoluted practical joke. The vase was where I had left it, or at least, a vase which *looked* like my own had been left in its place. Inside was a peace lily, but the plant was larger than the one I had forgotten. The stems were far longer, and all of them were angled away from the street, the white flowers straining toward the door as though grasping for its weathered handle. It didn't make any sense. This was very obviously not the same plant I had left behind, but the idea that someone had repotted a different lily overnight seemed almost as absurd as the idea of the plant doubling in height in a matter of hours.



I looked up from the plant and caught a flash of movement in the window of the house next door. It was the same woman from yesterday, her silhouette framed by the glass, watching me with a look of loathing. My embarrassment from the previous day flared, but it was quickly smothered by a desperate need for an explanation.



As I stepped off the porch to confront her, the girl in the heavens began to cry. A few fat raindrops splattered against the cracked sidewalk as I crossed the dying lawn. I wouldn't be shooed away this time. I pounded on the old woman's door until she finally cracked it open.



"I told you two to leave, like I tell everyone who's ever come poking around that damn house," she hissed. "I don't know why I even bother anymore. Well, you got what you wanted now, both of you. He's a part of the whole now, and you don't even know what you've lost."



The door slammed shut with a force that rattled its hinges. I stood there in the intensifying downpour, a building sense of panic making my heart race. I reached out, my fist hammering against the wood. "Hey! What are you talking about? What is the whole? Who is 'he'?" I yelled, but the house remained silent.



Frantic, I reached for the doorknob with my left hand, intending to force my way in, to demand she make sense of her words. But as my fingers curled around the cold brass, I stilled. There, sitting firmly at the base of my ring finger, was a band of gold: a simple, worn wedding ring. 



The strange sensation that had haunted my home the night before finally clarified. It wasn't the fear that someone who didn't belong was in the house with me, but the feeling that the house was missing something that should have been there. The rooms felt too large and the bed felt too cold because I was living in the wake of a vacancy I couldn't name. As I stared down at my hand, I knew I had lost something I could never get back, and I realized with horror that I couldn't even remember his face.
