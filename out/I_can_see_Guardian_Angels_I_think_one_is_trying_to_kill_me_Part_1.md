***Matthew 18:10***

***“Their angels do always behold the face of my Father…”***

I have always been able to see them.

For as long as I can remember, I’ve been able to see what people call “guardian angels.” No one else ever could. I learned that early.

When I was a child, I asked my mother why the woman at church had something standing behind her. I told her it looked wrong.

My mother squeezed my hand and said it was unbecoming for a young lady to say such things.

So I stopped asking.

I alone saw them everywhere after that. Hovering just over someone’s shoulder. Gliding behind them in grocery store aisles. Standing silently at the foot of hospital beds. They leaned in when someone whispered secrets into a phone. They watched over exams in classrooms. They bowed their heads during prayer.

No one ever looked where I was looking.

The angels, however, sometimes looked back.

Not with curiosity. Not with concern.

Just awareness.

They never spoke to me. Never followed me. Never lingered.

They simply existed to remain with the person they were bound to.

And I existed separately.

That is, until recently.

***Psalm 91:11***

***“For he will command his angels concerning you to guard you in all your ways.***

I never noticed one for myself.

I would see them beside other people; attached to someone else, resting a hand on a shoulder while they cried, or leaning close to a newborn in its stroller. They were present for everything. Joy. Grief. Boredom. They moved through a person’s life without interruption, existing quietly alongside it.

Some of them looked at me in passing. Not with interest. Not with recognition. Just acknowledgment; the way you might glance at a stranger in a waiting room.

They always returned their attention to the one they belonged to.

I never thought much of that. Some people had them. Some didn’t. I assumed I was simply one of the latter.

Once, on my walk home from work, I thought I saw one standing alone in an alley I passed every day.

It wasn’t hovering. It wasn’t turned toward anyone.

It was just… there.

I didn’t stop to look.

I told myself it belonged to someone I couldn’t see yet, someone just inside a building or just out of view.

By the time I reached the end of the block, I’d already dismissed it.

Out of sight, out of mind.

\~

The next time I noticed it, I was getting ready for work.

I don’t know how much time had passed since the first glimpse; days, maybe longer. Time has a way of smoothing over things you don’t understand, especially when they don’t insist on being remembered.

I had just stepped out of the shower and was crossing my apartment, still wrapped in a towel, when I passed the window. My unit sat high enough that I rarely thought about being seen from below.

That morning, I looked down.

In the field behind my building, just on the edge of the treeline, something stood motionless\*\*.\*\*

This time, I didn’t catch it by accident. I stopped and looked.

The dawn hadn’t fully broken, and the light flattened everything into silhouettes. I could only see its shape;  tall, upright, its edges blurring where shadow met mist. It resembled the other guardian angels I’d seen, at least in outline.

But it wasn’t positioned beside anyone.

It wasn’t angled toward a door or a window or a passing figure.

It simply stood there.

Watching nothing.

Or waiting.

I studied it the way I always studied angels; carefully, methodically. Letting my eyes trace its outline, searching for some invisible tether that might explain it.

There was none.

I glance down to my notepad to document this encounter, as I look back up, it’s gone.

No sound. No movement. No retreat toward a person.

Just absence.

For the first time in my life, I considered something I had never allowed myself to before.

Could one belong to me?

The thought felt almost embarrassing.

I had gone my entire life without one. I had made peace with that.

Angels followed people. They stayed near them. They were constant.

This had been distant.

Detached.

And why now?

Still, the question lingered longer than it should have.

I finished getting dressed. I left for work. The day passed the way days always did.

But even then, I couldn’t stop thinking about how far away it had been.

***Hebrews 13:2***

***“Be not forgetful to entertain strangers: for thereby some have entertained angels unawares.”***

The first time I asked my mother, I was very young.

“Who is the man standing behind you?”

She turned, instinctively, as if she expected someone to be there. When she saw nothing, she looked back at me and smiled. Not indulgent, not dismissive. Just gentle.

“Behind me?”

I nodded.

She studied my face for a moment, like she was deciding whether I was joking.

“Well,” she said carefully, “maybe that’s my guardian angel.”

I remember that pause. The way she chose the answer instead of blurting it out.

“Everyone has one,” she added, tapping my nose lightly. “You do too.”

She told me what she’d been taught growing up; that when someone you loved passed away, they were in heaven. That God sometimes sent angels to watch over us. Protect us. Guide us.

“My guardian angel could be your Uncle Kenny,” she said. “I like to think he keeps an eye on me.”

Uncle Kenny had died before I was born. I only knew him from photographs.

“That’s not him,” I told her.

She laughed softly. “Sweetheart, angels don’t look the way we do. Not anymore. The Bible says they have wings. They’re beautiful. Radiant.”

She pulled out one of her children’s Bible storybooks, the kind with glossy pages and golden halos. Angels with soft faces and sweeping white wings. Gentle hands. Kind eyes.

While she talked about heaven and love and protection, I kept looking over her shoulder.

At the thing standing there.

I had only ever seen my uncle in still photographs. But I knew, even as a child, that this was not him.

My uncle had been short, shorter than my mother. The figure behind her stretched nearly to the ceiling.

It didn’t shift when she shifted. It didn’t breathe.

It simply remained.

And most unsettling of all—

It had no eyes.

***Psalm 34:18***

***“The Lord is close to the brokenhearted and saves those who are crushed in spirit.”***

As I grew older, I learned to ask people carefully about their guardian angels. Gently. Casually. Enough to understand why one followed them.

Jenny Hartwell was known around school as the emo girl. She kept to herself. But one day, as she moved through the hallway, I noticed a guardian angel trailing behind her.

They were never identical.

Some were taller. Some slight. Some broad, some thin. Their forms varied the way people’s did. Their surfaces varied too, if you could call it a surface.

They didn’t have skin. Not exactly.

They were opaque. Somehow see through, but never empty. I could see through them, but not clearly. Everything behind them warped and blurred, milky and wavering. Some shimmered like opal. Others rippled like light on water.

No two were the same.

Much like people.

Jenny Hartwell’s angel was no taller than the rest of us twelve-year-olds.

It stood unnervingly close behind her, its form a muted, velvety red. Not bright, not violent. Just deep. Saturated. Heavy.

Unlike my mother’s, this one had eyes.

Pearl-white and sunken, they caught the low fluorescent light of the hallway and held it.

When I approached Jenny for the first time, her angel turned to look at me before she did.

It had no mouth. No brows. No eyelids. Nothing that should have conveyed emotion. And yet, as it stared into me, I felt something unmistakable settle in my chest.

Sorrow.

Jenny turned a moment later. “What do you want?”

“I, uh… wanted to know if you’d like to sit with me at lunch?”

It seemed like a better icebreaker than *‘Hey, has anyone close to you died recently?’*

She blinked, clearly not expecting that. Then she looked me over once; quick, practiced.

“Why?” she asked. “People don’t usually sit with me for fun.”

A pause.

“What’s the catch?” she asked. “Is this a joke or something?”

“No,” I said quickly. “I just wanted to get to know you. I think we’d get along.”

She narrowed her eyes, suspicion settling back into place. Her guardian angel leaned forward with her, closing the space between us until its pearl-white eyes hovered just centimeters from mine. If it could breathe, I think I would’ve felt it. Still, I didn’t step back.

“Just one lunch,” I said. “If we don’t get along, I’ll leave you alone. I promise.”

For a long moment, the angel remained there, staring through me. Then, just as suddenly, it withdrew, slipping back into its place behind her.

Jenny exhaled softly.

“Fine,” she said. “One lunch.”

She reflected for a moment.

“Don’t make it weird.”

As she turned to leave, her angel moved with her in perfect synchronicity. Together, they disappeared into the current of the hallway crowd.

Of course, my interest had been entirely selfish. I wanted to learn more. About the angels, about why hers looked the way it did. But the lunch went well. And then another after that.

She was right about one thing. Neither of us fit cleanly into the hallway hierarchy. Just for different reasons. I didn’t care. Jenny Hartwell turned out to be the best friend I could have asked for.

***Ecclesiastes 3:11***

***“He has made everything beautiful in its time. Also, he has put eternity into man’s heart, yet so that he cannot find out what God has done from the beginning to the end.”***

Yesterday, I was making breakfast.

I don’t eat as often as I should, but when I do, I enjoy making my own meals; something about the routine grounds me. When I finally sat down to eat, I found myself glancing out the window again, the same one overlooking the back lot of my complex.

It was a warm Sunday morning, and people were taking advantage of it. At least two dozen of my neighbors were scattered across the grass, enjoying the first real hints of spring. The majority had guardian angels.

One hovered close to a mother, which seemed common enough. Another followed two small children, twins, who appeared to share a single, two‑headed angel. I’d seen that variation before, though rarely. The behavior was different, too. The two‑headed angel trailed behind the children carefully, as if trying not to disturb their chaos. Or perhaps trying to contain it. I could only guess.

I often found myself wondering what their intentions really were. Sometimes I gave them small personalities they likely didn’t have, just to make them easier to understand; something my mind could hold onto in place of the unknowable.

After my mother first told me they were angels and read to me from the Bible, I became deeply interested in the text. As I grew up, I read it cover to cover more times than I could count. I wouldn’t call myself traditionally religious; when you spend your life seeing things that contradict the Word of the Lord, belief becomes complicated. Still, I went to church every Sunday, listened to sermons, and sat through Sunday school, hoping I’d find answers there.

Could these lessons explain what they were? Why they were here? Could I learn how to understand them;communicate with them, even?

In short, no.

But my mother was proud of my enthusiasm, so I kept going until the day she passed. In my mid‑teens, I branched out, studying other religions. Shintoism held my attention for a while, with its belief in spirits inhabiting the mortal world. For a time, I thought I was close to something. But none of their entities or deities quite matched what I saw every day.

Still, I kept it in the back of my mind. Wondering if perhaps this was something humanity had never fully understood. Or worse, something we’d stopped worshipping altogether.

As I was lost in thought, poking at the remnants of my breakfast, I glanced back down to the families in the backlot. Just off to the edge of everyone, something caught my eye.

The same angel I had seen before.

It stood at the far end of the grass, near the tree-line. Separated from the clusters of neighbors by a wide strip of open space.

I leaned closer to the glass.

In the full wash of late morning sunlight, its silhouette sharpened.

It was tall. Taller than the others nearby. That much I could tell immediately.

And darker.

Not the soft shimmer of opal or the wavering translucence I was used to.

This was dense.

The light struck it and didn’t pass through cleanly. It clung.

Even from this distance, I could see that its surface wasn’t uniform. The black wasn’t flat, it gleamed in places, caught with slick browns, streaked faintly with something sickly yellow.

I couldn’t make out fine details of its face, but there was structure there. Hollows where light should have reflected. A suggestion of sockets.

And then I noticed its outline.

Arms.

Not implied. Not briefly formed and withdrawn.

Defined.

Long enough that even from five floors up, their proportions looked wrong.

They hung at its sides, too long. Too thin.

Ending in elongated shapes that suggested hands, too defined to fade back into its mass.

Most of the time, I never noticed arms on them. It was as if their bodies were draped in fabric, never showing legs or feet. They simply glided behind their hosts with a lithe, effortless motion. On the rare occasions I’d see one reach out, perhaps to caress a child’s face, it would extend a hand briefly, then retract it again, as though it was never there.

Another anomaly: the hands never emerged from a fixed place. They appeared wherever necessary, often pushing out from the center of their mass, as if something inside was reaching outward before being yanked back in.

That had terrified me at first, in a way I never fully shook. I’d wondered if things could be trapped inside them. As far as I could tell, though, this was simply how their anatomy worked.

That being said, for this one to have arms— arms that were always there, was both fascinating and deeply unsettling.

As I took in all its features, I noticed something else. Something wrong.

Often, these angels watch the world around their host. Sometimes it’s protective. Sometimes it’s curious. They observe what their person observes.

My mother’s angel, “Uncle Kenny,” would often watch me as I moved around the house, even when my mother was distracted and unaware of my presence. It felt less like surveillance and more like acknowledgement. As if it simply knew I was there. Other times, it would lean far over the table to watch me while I did homework or studied religion. These moments were never long. It would always return to my mother’s side once it seemed to get whatever it wanted from me.

I wasn’t the only one it did this to. When my mother had guests over, it behaved the same way. Leaning in, watching them closely, as if measuring their intentions.

None of this was new.

So when I noticed the black-tar angel staring in my direction, and only my direction, I understood something immediately.

It wasn’t watching the people around it. It wasn’t acknowledging the children playing nearby, or the adults passing too close. A teenage boy ran straight through its form without hesitation while chasing a baseball, and still its attention never wavered.

It was looking at me.

Me, standing in my apartment. Behind glass. On the fifth floor.

Once I realized this, I stood up so suddenly that my chair toppled over behind me. My breath caught painfully in my throat.

This was new.

They always fixate on something. On their host, or on whatever comes too close to them. They don’t care about anything that exists outside that narrow focus. I don’t know who this thing’s host is. I don’t even know if it has one.

So why was it looking at me?

I couldn’t help but to hold its gaze.

My breathing was unsteady, a dull ache blooming in my lip as I chewed at it in anxious thought.

I closed the blinds.

7:45 A.M.

The next train was going to arrive any minute, and Jenny was late again.

It’s funny, so much of my life is spent watching angels, cataloging their behaviors, wondering why me, of all people, and yet I somehow manage to stay punctual. Jenny never does.

At least she’s consistent. I don’t think I’ve ever seen her early to anything. If I’m running behind, I know I’ll still get there before she does.

*‘The 8:00 A.M. Liberty Station Train to Pineview Station now boarding.’*

I moved toward the center of the car, our usual spot, and leaned slightly to my left, letting my bag spill into the empty seat beside me.

The train is an interesting place to observe the angels. Harsh fluorescent lights. Crowded bodies. Limited space. They react to it much like their hosts do, compressing inward, phasing closer until they hover just above their person’s head;like a bobber floating above a fishing line.

Even here, they perform their small rituals: leaning down to inspect someone standing too close, slowly rotating their heads in full circles, fixating on flickering bulbs or reflected movement in the windows. It makes the already packed car feel even tighter, bodies layered with other bodies, space filled twice over. I imagine it would be overwhelming if I weren’t used to it by now.

‘*The 8:00 A.M. Liberty Station Train to Pineview Station now departing.’*

Just as the doors begin to slide shut, Jenny slipped inside at the last possible second.

She was out of breath, hair shoved into a crooked bun, pajama pants peeking out beneath her button-up. I pulled my bag back into my lap as she collapsed into the seat beside me, immediately launching into an explanation. Something about crosswalks clogged with pedestrians, cyclists training for a marathon, and being chased by chickens.

I don’t think any of our neighbors own chickens.

“You know,” I said, once she paused long enough to breathe, “you could take a later class than me. I’m pretty sure your mom would survive you switching your nine A.M. to an eleven. Or noon.”

Jenny’s angel, Velvet, as I’d started calling it, settled into place above her, glancing at me before turning its attention to the passing city outside the window.

“That’s ridiculous,” Jenny huffed, finally catching her breath. “How would I bother you if we didn’t have any classes together? Besides, after this semester we’ll have to start taking major-specific courses, and then I’ll never see you. Ever. How do you even major in religion, anyway? What do you do;study the Dead Sea Scrolls? Starve yourself like Gandhi?”

Something like that.

As the semesters slipped by, Jenny turned out to be right. We saw each other less and less once our classes no longer lined up. We still made time;coffee dates, study sessions (me helping her with homework, more like), long drives to the coast during summer breaks; but even that never felt like enough. Our time together was thinning, stretching itself too far.

Still, we always had the train.

After graduation, she started her dream career in biology as a laboratory technician. I went into Human Resources. Even then, we still took the train everywhere. Just like it always was, Jenny;brilliant, driven, endlessly capable;was late.

‘*The 7:30 P.M. Liberty Station train to Queens Landing now boarding.’*

I made my way to the center of the train and took our usual seat, leaning left so my bag could spill into the space beside me. The train always felt crowded, mostly because every passenger was doubled for me. Faces and angels pressed together in layers, overlapping until it became difficult to focus on any one of them for long.

At the last possible moment, Jenny hurried onto the train and dropped into the seat beside me. She launched immediately into a story about work. Something involving contaminated petri dishes, pockets full of loose change, and one particularly cranky researcher who apparently believed she was personally responsible for the state of the lab.

“So, how’s Human Resources?” she asked. “Anyone quiz you today on the logistical differences between Jesus’s crucifixion and Joseph Smith finding the gold plates?”

“I don’t really see the connection—“

“They never do,” she said. “They just don’t get religion like you do.”

She never missed an opportunity to question my bachelor’s degree. Still, it got me a job in HR, so I couldn’t complain.

While Jenny talked, I noticed Velvet staring at me longer than usual. What was typically a brief, almost courteous glance lingered instead, unbroken.

I tried not to react. I’d learned early on not to acknowledge the angels too openly, I didn’t want anyone to think I was crazy.

So when Velvet slowly retracted back into Jenny’s body, only to push out again from her chest, staring intently into the crowded train car, I couldn’t help myself. I stared.

Thankfully, Jenny didn’t seem to notice where my attention had drifted. I knew she’d never let me live that one down.

“Jenny,” I interrupted, as she continued recounting her day. “Are you alright?”

“What? Yeah— yeah, I’m fine.” She looked at me, confused. “Why?”

“It’s nothing,” I said too quickly. “I just thought I noticed something with how you were talking.”

A bad lie.

“Nope. Peachy-keen,” she said with a grin. Hard to believe this was the same moody girl I’d met years ago. She’d grown into someone softer, brighter. Someone I never would’ve expected.

‘*Now stopping at Queens Landing.’*

“That’s you,” she said, nodding toward the doors. Velvet still hovered strangely, half-emerged from her chest, its attention fixed somewhere beyond us.

I stood, slinging my bag over my should\*\*er. “\*\*Be careful getting home, okay? I love you.”

“Don’t worry,” she laughed. “I’m tough.”

The doors slid open.

“See you soon,” she added. “Love you too, cranky.”

I stepped onto the platform.

The doors closed behind me.

***Proverbs 15:3:***

***“The eyes of the Lord are everywhere, keeping watch on the wicked and the good.”***

Work became one of my favorite places to be.

Surprisingly, HR afforded me a lot of time to myself when I wasn’t dealing with employee disputes or disgruntled clients. It gave me space to think. To revisit the things I’d seen that day, or thumb through my notes and research one more time, hoping something new might finally click.

I also enjoyed seeing people’s angels on the job with them. The office brought out peculiar variations. Some grew narrow and rigid, their forms drawn tight as if pressed between invisible panes of glass. Others seemed to thin at the edges, their outlines sharpened into something almost angular.

I never knew whether the building shaped them, or if they arrived that way.

Besides that, my favorite pastime at work was watching the angels interact with the office itself. There was something deeply satisfying about seeing one stare intently at a coffee machine while its host waited for a document to print, or hover mere centimeters from the pot as it finished filling. That one, especially, I could relate to.

Most days ended the same: paperwork, minor disputes, the occasional termination to preserve ‘the bottom line.’

That too had its own strange fascination. When their hosts were sad or disappointed, something changed.

Not in expression, they don’t have expressions.

But in presence.

They dimmed.

As if someone had turned the lights down. They were still there, but harder to see.

The office didn’t change.

A ticking clock. Filing forms. Approving PTO. Mediating minor drama.

‘Yes, James is allowed to use the copy machine every morning at 9:00 a.m. He got there first.

No, you can’t have me write him up for that.

…

Yes. I will still write that down.’

I don’t particularly mind. This place feels like my kind of safe space. I find peace in repetition.

Click. Tick. Print. Shuffle. Assign. Ring. Knock. Repeat.

Knock. Knock. Knock.

The cycle continues.

“Yes, come in,” I said, without looking up from my computer.

“Uhm;hi. Yes. I wanted to talk to you about a potential leave of absence. You see, I have family in—“

I glanced up mid-sentence and catch myself staring a little too long. Luckily, I think he assumes I’m sizing him up, trying to intimidate him. Not studying the features of his angel.

One eye is a rarity among angels. Something I only see once in a blue moon.

While his host nervously stutters through the details of his request, a pencil-thin angel hovers just over his shoulder. Papery white skin drapes loosely around its frame, as if it doesn’t quite fit its body. The same skin hangs from its head, obscuring any clear suggestion of a face.

The only distinct feature is a single, enormous eye in the center of its head. Perfectly round. Pale yellow, flecked with orange. No pupil.

“Fascinating,” I murmured before I can stop myself.

“S-sorry?” he asked, pulling me back into the room.

“Your leave of absence is approved,” I say, still not breaking eye contact with the angel. “Enjoy your trip.”

He stands quickly, thanking me far too many times, and hurries out of my office. Even as he rushes out and his angel follows, it maintains its stare;its unblinking eye fixed on me until the last possible moment.

As my day is winding down and coming to an end, I thumb through the stacks of paperwork that have made their way to my desk.

Dispute, dispute, leave of absence, workplace safety seminar, complaint, dispute, PTO request—

I pause.

I picked up the complaint, a carefully clipped together packet with no supervisor notes, no responses attached.

The complaint shouldn’t have been on my desk.

Intake reports like this are meant to pass through department heads first. They’re handled, documented, and resolved before HR ever sees them.

This one skipped all of that.

I opened it.

The issue itself was trivial. According to the report, reception had refused to let him into the building restroom without a visitor pass. He claimed he’d explained himself, that he’d been polite, that he’d only needed a moment. The receptionist had insisted on procedure. Security had been called. He’d been asked to leave.

On paper, it was nothing. Exactly the kind of thing that normally died at the front desk.

What stood out was how he wrote about it.

There were paragraphs and paragraphs. Explanations instead of complaints. He spent more time describing his intentions than the incident itself. How he hadn’t meant to make anyone uncomfortable, how he understood rules existed for a reason, how easily people could misinterpret someone who was just trying to handle a basic human need.

He emphasized, more than once, that he only needed the restroom.

That he only needed our restroom.

That it would have been easier if someone had just let him in.

I closed the folder halfway and picked up the phone.

The intake extension rang twice before someone answered. I gave my name, then said, “Hey;quick question. I’ve got a complaint here that should’ve gone through a department head first. Do you know why it came straight to HR?”

There was a pause. Papers shifting. A breath.

“He insisted,” the woman said. “Wouldn’t leave until we told him someone from HR would look at it.”

“Did he say why?”

“No. Just that it was important. That procedure was being misused.”

Another pause.

“And… you’re the least swamped today,” she added, apologetic.

“Right,” I said.

I hung up and looked back at the file.

“What a weird way to end the day,” I sighed, and began processing the complaint.

‘*The 6:00 P.M. Oceanic Station Train to* Queens Landing arriving soon.’

pinholes;

Their angels hovered above them, leaning toward flickering fluorescent lights, phasing through the walls, spinning slowly to watch the rush of bodies.

Dense, rolling fog swallowed the path as I took slow, deliberate steps forward.

Angels pushed up from the earth to watch me pass.

I glanced at each of them as they appeared to me, then back down at my feet.

I wasn’t here for them.

I stopped in front of the last headstone in the row.

“Hi, Jenny.” I whispered.

Velvet slowly lifted from the soil. We held eye contact. Then it rose fully, curling around the headstone, cradling it.

Her mom called me frantically, begging me to go check on her since I lived closer.

I was too late.

She was already gone.

Velvet lay with her on the ground, holding her closely. An arm protruding from the center of its mass to stroke her hair.

The note was short.

“I’m sorry. He ruined me.”

That night, when Velvet was acting off on the train, someone followed her off at her stop.

I don’t know all the details, just that he hurt her.

I stayed with her for days afterwards, until she insisted she was okay again. She spoke softly, avoided my eyes, and moved like the world had grown heavier overnight. Even then, I kept checking in, visiting her work and apartment. She promised me she was okay, that she just needed time.
