Part one.

When the house next door sold, everyone was relieved. I was the last house at the end of the cul de sac and the house was to my right.

For years, it had been owned by an elderly couple who rarely left. After they passed away, the place sat empty—until one morning, a moving truck appeared without warning.

By sunset, the truck was gone.

And the new owner was already home.

He stood on his front porch, hands folded neatly in front of him, watching the neighborhood taking in the sight of his new home.

When he saw me looking, he smiled.

I smiled back and waved. He waved and started walked over.

“I just wanted to say hello, and introduce myself he said. The name is Daniel …… Daniel Crawford. Nice to meet you my name is Nick I replied.

He looked around my yard slowly, like he was memorizing it.

“You live alone?” he asked casually.

“Yeah recently divorced so it’s just me” I said.

He nodded, as if he had already known the answer. Me too bachelor life for us I guess!

“Well,” i said with a polite smile, “if you ever need anything, I’m right next door”

Actually Nick do you happen to have a shovel I could borrow. I’m going to try to get this yard in order and get some of these overgrown bushes out of here. Sorry it’s such an eyesore right now. Sure thing I grabbed mine from the garage and brought it over to him. Thanks neighbor you’re a lifesaver he said with a smile.

As he walked back to his house He stopped and looked back “oh Nick I’m a bit of a night owl and I’m planning on doing a bunch of renovations to the house but I’ll try to keep the noise down.

No problem man, give me a shout if you need any help with anything. I replied I might just do that he said with that same smile.

Over the next few weeks, Daniel became the perfect neighbor.

He waved at everyone.  He helped Mrs. Langley carry groceries.  He pet neighbor dogs out on walks.  People liked him.  But I noticed patterns.

Every morning at exactly 6:32 a.m., he left his house.  Every night at exactly 11:47 p.m., he returned.  Never late.  Never early.  And sometimes, late at night, I’d see him standing in his front yard.  Not walking.  Not checking his phone.  Just standing there.  Listening and looking up at the sky.

The first disappearance happened in June Mrs. Langley’s dog went missing.  She searched for days, putting up flyers, knocking on doors.  Daniel volunteered to help.  Three days later, he showed up at her house with a bouquet of flowers.  “I’m so sorry,” he told her softly. “I hope you find him.”  She cried.

Daniel stood there silently, his expression calm.

A week later, Mrs. Langley herself disappeared.  Her house was suddenly empty.  Her car gone.  Her mail stopped arriving.  People said she moved to be closer to family.

I started watching him more closely.  Not obsessively.  Not at first.  But enough to notice details.  Lights were on in his house usually all night. You could hear the faint sound of his saw going at night, hear hammering occasionally.   He never had visitors.  No friends.  No family.  No deliveries.

Slowly over the next couple of months I noticed there had been more and more missing cat and dog posters going up around the neighborhood. Something I typically wouldn’t pay attention to but it seemed like there was a new poster every week for another animal.

I couldn’t sleep which wasn’t uncommon for me I had never been a heavy sleeper and a bit of an insomniac

Around 1:20 a.m., I looked out my bedroom window.  Daniel was in his backyard.  Digging.  The yard lights illuminated the grass with soft yellow light.  He wasn’t frantic.  He wasn’t rushed.  He dug slowly, carefully, like he was following instructions only he could see.

I watched for 5 minutes or so.  What the hell was he doing digging at 1:30 in the morning.  Then he stopped. He stood still.

And slowly turned his head.  He looked straight at my window.  I dropped to the floor instantly.

My heart felt like it was trying to escape my chest. There was no way he could have seen me it was pitch black in my room. When I looked again, he was gone.

The next day, I walked past his house pretending to go for a run. Up close, everything looked normal.

The grass was flawless. The fence gleamed.

The windows reflected the sky like mirrors.

But as I passed the side yard, I heard something faint.

A thump.

Then another.

Not loud enough to be obvious. But not random either.

Rhythmic.

Like someone knocking from far away.

I slowed down.

The front door opened behind me.

“You exercise every morning,” Daniel said.

I turned around.

He was standing there, smiling politely.

“Trying to,” I said.

He nodded approvingly.

“Routine is important,” he said. “People who break their routines tend to disappear.”

He laughed softly.

I didn’t.  Why would he say that it didn’t even make sense.

That Saturday morning I was doing some yard work and I noticed two police cars coming down the street and stopping at the Johnson’s house.  I grabbed a beer from the garage fridge and sat in a chair  in the shade wiping the sweat from my face. They rang the bell and went inside. I finished my beer and went inside to grab a shower. After getting out of the shower I heard knocking on my door. I went to the door in my towel and the two police officers were standing there. They asked to come in and I said sure what’s this about. One of the officers replied well your neighbors have filed a missing person report for their 15 year old daughter. I said oh ummmm her names Ashley right? Right replied the officer. She never made it home from school yesterday. We are just asking everyone in the neighborhood if they happened to see her or have seen anything suspicious lately.

I thought for a moment and said no not that I can…. I paused and remembered the other night. I ….. uh don’t want to sound crazy but I saw my neighbor a couple of nights ago digging in his backyard in the middle of the night. He’s always up late and I can hear hammering and sawing all hours of the night. Which neighbor they asked I pointed next door.

Then they went to Daniel’s house.He invited them inside like they were guests.I watched from my porch.

They stayed for nearly forty minutes.  When they left, one officer walked over to me.

“Everything checks out,” he said. “His house is immaculate. No signs of anything suspicious.” He showed us all the renovations he’s done. What about the hole he was digging?  Yeah he walked us back there he planted a new maple tree back there. It’s odd to do in the middle of the night but unless you see anything else….. he trailed off. What about the Ashley.  He looked at me, sighed and said she’s a 15 year old girl we get calls like this all the time. They run away for a few days, go stay with a friend and then show back up. If you see anything else give us a call.

As they left, I watched Daniel from across the street.

He was standing in his doorway, hands folded neatly, smiling at me.  He raised his hand slowly.  Not a wave.

More like a reminder. He knew what I had done.

The next morning I was taking my trash out and as I went to throw the bag in I noticed something sitting in the trash can when I picked it up, my hands started shaking.  It was Ashley’s school ID.  A faint smear of dirt across her picture.

I ran inside and called the police immediately.  When they arrived, they didn’t look surprised.  “Where exactly did you find this?” one asked.  “In my trash! I didn’t put that there!” They exchanged that look again. The one that says we’ve heard this before.

“Sir, we’re going to need to take a look around.” I said of course go ahead. They moved past me and into the house putting on rubber gloves. As I looked up I saw Daniel in his yard smiling he waved and mouthed the words “got you”.

They searched my house. My garage, my shed, and that’s when they found it.

A pink backpack.

Hidden behind old paint cans in my garage.  Ashley’s name stitched on the inside flap.  I felt the blood drain from my face.  “Son of a bitch,” I whispered.

But it was in my garage and it was real.  I was immediately placed in handcuffs and taken to the police station.

I sat in holding for hours. Finally a detective came in to question me. I’m detective Matthews and you’ve got some explaining to do. I said “you’ve got to believe me I’ve been set up it’s my neighbor he did it.”The detective looked annoyed and said “Well Mr Crawford let’s run down a bit of this evidence and see if you can explain some of this”. He sat down with a stack of pictures. The first one…..a picture of a shovel. “this shovel was in the back of your Tahoe any reason you would have a shovel back there, he asked. Look detective I let my neighbor borrow that shovel a while ago he never gave it back.  He….. he must have put it there, I pleaded to no avail. Detective Matthew’s sighed ok….. well what about these trash bags that were in the suv?  I don’t know those aren’t mine I tried to explain. And of course here’s a picture of her backpack and student id, but you knew about those didn’t you?  He said throwing down more pictures. Do you like keeping souvenirs from your victims Mr Crawford?

We also found some blonde hair and what looks like blood in the back of your Tahoe…. It’s been sent off to the lab and I’m willing to bet my pension on it that it’s a dna match to that missing Johnson girl.

I started to hyperventilate you’ve got to believe me sir I would never…. He cut me off I just want to know if the girl is alive and where she’s at.  I don’t know  I yelled.

Another detective came into the room and told detective Matthews he needed to see him.  He walked out and closed the door. I sat in silence pondering how I let myself get into this situation and what I could possibly do to get out of it. There was nothing they had me dead to rights. There is no way in hell a jury would ever believe me with all this evidence and god only knows what else he’s planted in my house.  So now I sit in this integration room waiting to hear my fate.
