*After that weird identity incident…*  
  
I woke up this morning with the smell of bleach on my hands and burns on my palms. According to my work portal, I was just being "productive". I tried to count the seconds to prove I was still here.   
  
I got to ten, blinked, and it was twenty minutes later.   
  
They call it Idle Capacity Recovery. I call it being a prisoner in my own skin.  
  
I started noticing it on Wednesday. Time at home didn’t feel like time anymore. I would sit down to eat dinner and when I looked at the clock, ten minutes had passed in what felt like three.   
  
My stomach churned from confusion. I blamed stress, maybe daylight savings, something ordinary. It didn’t feel like a real problem at first, but a small, nagging panic had begun to grow.   
  
By Friday, my nights were stretching in ways I couldn’t control. I used to go to bed around 1am, then 2am. Now I found myself scrolling or reading until 3am, wishing I could just stop, wishing I could pause but the hours slipped away like water through my fingers.   
  
I tried to track them; phone usage, steps, TV runtime but nothing made sense. The logs were precise, neat, orderly. Yet my memory felt hollow. Half of my life had already happened and I wasn’t in it.   
  
I checked my work portal, hoping routine or schedules would explain it. The dashboard greeted me with a section I had never seen before: Temporal Allocation. Under my name, it listed Idle Capacity Recovery. I froze. My chest tightened, my breath caught.   
  
Someone or something had measured me.  
  
Not my work.  
  
Me.   
  
And they had taken pieces of my evenings, quietly, with no warning, no explanation.

At first, it was just minutes. Fifteen here, twenty there. But by Sunday, I noticed evidence of activity I couldn’t remember: an email sent, tasks completed, notifications marked "done".   
  
I hadn’t typed those words. I hadn’t moved those files. I had only been present in body, while my mind had drifted elsewhere.   
  
Even my partner started noticing. "You were up late again last night", said casually one morning. "You were…so productive. Didn’t say a word for hours".   
  
I laughed it off.   
  
But their voice carried an edge I couldn’t ignore, a hint that what I had assumed was mine was never fully under my control.

By Monday, the pattern became impossible to ignore. I would sit down with a book, intending to read for half an hour and the clock would jump twenty minutes forward. I tried to force myself to slow down, counting seconds in my head, pacing the rooms, anything to reclaim some measure of control.   
  
It didn’t work. Time moved without me.  
  
The house betrayed me further. Tasks I didn’t recall doing were completed. Dishes washed, surfaces scrubbed, laundry folded. My body had moved but my consciousness hadn’t.   
  
Then I noticed the physical proof. My hands, faintly burned from scrubbing, the lingering smell of bleach stubborn under my fingernails and callouses that hadn’t been there yesterday.   
  
I sank into my chair, hands trembling. The portal blinked again.   
  
A small notification in the corner I had stopped expecting:   
  
\>>>  
\[SYSTEM NOTIFICATION: TEMPORAL ALLOCATION\]

Timestamp: 12:15  
Status: Idle Capacity Fully Recovered  
Thank you for your contribution  
\>>>  
  
Clean. Impersonal. Indifferent.   
  
My partner’s voice broke the silence again: "You were so productive last night…didn’t say a word for four hours".   
  
I looked at my hands again. The proof was undeniable.   
  
The system hadn’t taken hours, it had taken me.   
  
My body existed, moved, completed tasks but my mind had been absent.   
  
I was a guest in my own life.   
  
The room felt smaller, the walls pressing closer as if the minutes themselves had shrunk. The clock ticked on, indifferent. I realized then that it wasn’t lost time it had never been mine. It had never been. And yet, the system had left me enough to keep existing, to keep pretending, while quietly reclaiming what I thought I controlled.  
  
I sat back in my chair, exhausted in a way I had never known. My phone still unlocked with my face, the calendar still displayed appointments but the minutes had already left me behind.   
  
I did not resist. I could not.   
  
All that remained was the faint flicker of the portal’s interface. I watched it, wondering how much had been taken, how much I had left without knowing.   
  
Even if I closed it now, even if I turned away, I could feel it ticking, quietly, inexorably, reshaping my days.   
  
I exhaled slowly. Tomorrow would begin the same way and I would move through it, as I always had, pretending.   
  
Pretending that the hours I touched, the nights I spent, the life I felt I was living...was still mine.
