I want to start with something so you understand why this scared me the way it did. I've changed a tire in a ditch in January. I've pushed a car out of a snowbank by myself at 2 AM. I've been broken down on the highway in a sleet storm waiting three hours for a tow truck. None of that scared me. I know how to handle being stuck in bad weather.

What I am not equipped for, what nobody is equipped for, is finding out, while you're stuck and alone in the dark and the temperature is dropping, that you are not the only one out there.

This happened on the night of December 14th, 2025, on a rural state highway in northern Wisconsin, about fifty miles from the nearest city. I was driving back from a hunting trip alone, a four-hour drive, and a storm that was supposed to hold off until midnight moved in around nine.

I'd driven in worse. That was my mistake.

My truck went into the ditch at 9:47 PM. I know because I looked at my phone the second it happened. I wasn't speeding, I was doing about 35 miles per hour, but there was a patch of black ice under a thin layer of fresh snow and the back end came around before I could do anything about it. The truck slid off the shoulder and nosed into the ditch at about a forty-five degree angle.

I spent ten minutes trying to get out. Wheels spinning, digging deeper. Not going anywhere. I checked my phone. Zero bars. The temperature outside was several degrees below zero. My truck's fuel tank was a little under a quarter. I could run the heat for a while, but not all night.

I sat there and thought through my options. Standard procedure is to stay with the vehicle, they tell you that for a reason. But this road had been empty since I'd turned onto it an hour before. Nobody was coming. I had a flashlight, heavy boots, and layers. I knew there was a small town somewhere behind me, I'd driven through it maybe twenty minutes back. That was doable on foot if I had to. Before I made that call, I looked up the road.

About a quarter mile ahead, barely visible through the snow, there was a vehicle on the shoulder. Dark. No lights. Just a shape. Could be another stranded driver. Could be an empty truck somebody had left there. Either way, it was closer than the town.

I grabbed my flashlight, pulled my balaclava over my face, and started walking toward it.

It was a cargo van. Older, dark blue or black, hard to tell under the snow. The engine was cold, completely dead. Both rear doors were hanging open, swinging slightly in the wind, which is what had caught my attention from down the road.

I called out. No answer. I pointed my flashlight into the back of the van.

I'm going to tell you exactly what I saw because I think the details matter and I've been careful to remember them.

The van was not empty. The back of it was set up, and I mean deliberately set up, with a sleeping bag, a plastic bin with a latch, and a metal bar bolted along the interior wall with zip ties hanging from it. The kind of zip ties you'd use to bind someone's wrists. A row of them, maybe eight. Most were cut.

On the floor there were clothes. Not folded. Scattered. Different sizes. A woman's shoe. A man's jacket. A child's winter hat.

I stood in the cold holding my flashlight on that van for a long time.

That’s when I reached in and grabbed the one thing I actually needed, a wool blanket folded on top of the sleeping bag. I told myself I'd explain it to whoever owned it later. Then I took a few steps back and looked at the van and the road around it.

The driver's door was closed. The driver's side window had been fogged from the inside and then frozen over. Which meant the van had been occupied recently. Someone had been breathing in that van and then left it, or been taken from it, in the last few hours.

There were footprints leading away from the driver's side. Into the dark, off the shoulder, heading into the flat open field to the east. One set of footprints. Heading out. None coming back.

I pulled out my phone again. Still no signal. I was standing in the dark, with a van full of things that told me something horrible had either happened here or was about to, and whoever was responsible was somewhere out in that field.

I started walking back toward my truck fast. And about halfway there I stopped.

There was a light in the field. A flashlight beam. Moving. Coming roughly in my direction, still far enough away that I couldn't make out anything behind it. But moving toward the road.

Toward where my truck was sitting nose-first in the ditch with my hunting gear visible through the windows. I started running.

I got back to my truck and locked the doors and killed my interior light so the cab went dark. I sat low in the seat and watched through the windshield.

The light in the field kept moving. Unhurried. Like whoever was carrying it wasn't looking for anything specific, or like they already knew what they were coming back to.

I tried my phone again. Still nothing. I had a CB radio in the truck, old one, came with it when I bought it, and I grabbed it and started running through channels. Static on most. On channel 19, a trucker's voice faint and cutting in and out. I held down the transmit button and gave my location as best I could and said I needed help. He either heard me or he didn't.

Then the light reached the road.

I could see the shape behind it now. A man. Heavy coat. Moving along the shoulder in the direction of my truck. He stopped about thirty yards back and held his light up, sweeping it across my truck, across the ditch, across the road.

Then he started walking toward me again.

I had a rifle in the back seat. Unloaded, case locked, because that's how you legally transport it. My ammunition was in the toolbox in the truck bed. So that was essentially useless.

What I had immediately available was my truck. Four thousand pounds of it.

When he was about twenty feet out I turned the key and hit the headlights. Full brights, straight at him, then he stopped. Threw his arm up to block the light. I could see his face for a second, bearded, older, squinting into the beams, and then I dropped the truck into four-wheel drive, gunned the engine, and went straight up the slope of the ditch. The wheels caught. The truck lurched and bottomed out and then came up onto the shoulder. I fishtailed right and straightened out and drove.

I didn't look in the mirror. Not once. I got signal eight miles up the road and called 911. I gave them the location of the van. I gave them the description of the man. I stayed on the phone until two county sheriff units confirmed they'd found the van.

A detective called me the following afternoon. He confirmed the van had been on their radar in connection with a missing persons case, he didn't specify which one, and I didn't push. He told me I'd made the right call getting out of there. He asked me if the man I saw had come any closer than twenty feet. I told him no. He said: "Good." That one word has been sitting in the back of my head [ever since](https://www.youtube.com/@MrDisturbingNightmares).
