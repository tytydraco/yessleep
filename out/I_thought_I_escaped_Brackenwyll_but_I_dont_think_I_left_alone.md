Part 2:  [Something in this town makes people act like nothing is wrong. But I know they can see the ravens.](https://www.reddit.com/r/nosleep/comments/1s5jms8/something_in_this_town_makes_people_act_like/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=web3x&utm_name=web3xcss&utm_term=1&utm_content=share_button)

I’ve been driving for a while now.

I don’t know how long exactly. The road doesn’t really change, and neither does anything around it. The trees look the same. The fog sits at the same height. Even the curves feel familiar, like I’ve taken them too many times already.

I passed the town sign a long time ago.

At least I think I did.

“**Welcome to Brackenwyll**”

*“Hope you like fishing!”*

I didn’t slow down when I saw it. I didn’t even look at it properly. I just kept going, hands locked on the wheel, trying not to think about anything behind me.

For a moment, it felt like it worked.

The road stretched forward, empty. No movement in the trees. No shadows where they shouldn’t be. Just silence, broken only by the sound of the engine and the tires against the road.

Then the feathers came back.

They didn’t fall.

They were just there.

One second I could see the road, the next the windshield was covered. Thick, black feathers pressing against the glass, sliding over each other like they were alive. The wipers dragged across them without moving anything.

I pressed the accelerator instead of the brake.

I didn’t want to stop.

The car shook as more of them hit, not from above, but from everywhere at once. The sides, the hood, the roof. It felt like something was closing in on me, like the car was being swallowed whole.

Then everything stopped.

No sound. No movement.

The feathers dropped.

I was parked in front of the house.

Same place. Same angle.

The engine was still running.

I sat there for a while, staring at the road ahead of me, trying to understand how I got back. I didn’t remember turning around. I didn’t remember slowing down.

I tried again.

And again.

Every time it ended the same way.

The only thing that changed was how long it took for the feathers to come.

Sometimes it was right after the sign.

Sometimes it took longer.

But it always happened.

After a while, I stopped checking the mirrors.

There was never anything there.

The last time I tried, something was different.

There was someone standing in the road.

I noticed him before I reached the sign. Just a shape at first, barely visible through the fog. Standing perfectly still, right in the middle of the road.

I slowed down without meaning to.

Something about the way he stood there felt familiar.

Not just familiar. Known.

I stopped a few meters in front of him.

He didn’t move. Didn’t react. Just stood there, looking at me.

Then he stepped closer.

And I felt it immediately.

That drop in my stomach, like missing a step in the dark.

It was my dad.

Same face. Same clothes. Same posture.

Exactly the same as when I last saw him in the kitchen.

For a second, I almost opened the door.

Almost said something.

But something didn’t sit right.

His eyes didn’t focus the way they should have. They didn’t move with the rest of his face. They just… stayed there. Fixed on me, like they didn’t need to adjust.

And when he got closer, I noticed his skin.

It wasn’t still.

There was something under it. Not moving fast, not violently. Just… shifting. Like something pressed against it from the inside and didn’t quite fit.

I locked the doors.

I’m sure I did.

He walked around the car anyway, reached for the handle, and opened it without resistance.

He sat down beside me like it was the most natural thing in the world.

Like he had always been sitting there.

I couldn’t look at him properly.

“Dad?” I said.

My voice didn’t sound right.

He turned his head slowly and smiled.

It was perfect.

Too perfect.

“You’ve been asking the wrong question.”

I tightened my grip on the wheel. “What are you?”

He tilted his head slightly.

“You should be asking when.”

I frowned. “When what?”

“When you think you arrived here.”

I tried to answer, but the thought slipped away before I could form it.

I tried again.

Driving.

The sign.

The house.

But it didn’t feel solid. It felt like something I had repeated too many times, not something I actually remembered.

“You don’t remember choosing to come here,” he said.

It wasn’t a question.

“That’s because you didn’t.”

My chest tightened. “That’s not true. I drove here. I remember it.”

“You remember what you were given.”

His voice didn’t change.

“You killed one of my eyes.”

The words landed slowly.

Then all at once.

The raven.

“You interfered,” he continued. “You changed something small.”

I shook my head. “You don’t get to control everything. People don’t work like that.”

“You do.”

He leaned back slightly, looking past me, through the windshield.

“You always did.”

Something in my head felt wrong.

Like something had shifted out of place.

Like I was missing something important, but couldn’t tell what.

He leaned closer.

“***Wake up***.”

The world collapsed.

—

I was on the ground.

Cold dirt under my hands.

The clearing.

The same one from before.

The house stood in front of me, silent, empty.

For a moment, nothing moved.

Then I heard it.

Wings.

Not one or two.

Hundreds.

I looked up.

They were everywhere.

In the trees. Above them. Filling the space between branches like they had always been there.

Watching.

One of them moved forward.

Larger than the others.

Still.

Its eyes caught the light.

Blue.

“You’re persistent.”

The voice wasn’t sound. It pressed directly into my head.

I pushed myself up, my legs unsteady, my hands shaking.

“You weren’t supposed to change anything.”

The trees shifted slightly, bending as if making room for it.

“You broke something small.”

I grabbed the first thing I could reach.

A stone.

My fingers tightened around it.

“But you didn’t stop.”

The bird tilted its head.

“You’re fun.”

I didn’t think.

I threw the stone.

It hit.

I don’t know if it mattered.

I didn’t wait to see.

I ran.

Branches tore at my arms, my clothes. Something brushed against my back, my neck, my legs. I didn’t look.

I just ran for the house.

I slammed the door behind me and pressed myself against it, trying to catch my breath.

“It can’t be that easy,” I said, more to myself than anything else.

The air felt wrong.

Heavy.

Then everything shifted.

—

I’m back in the car.

The engine is running.

The road stretches out ahead of me.

The town sign is behind me.

I remember passing it.

I remember not stopping.

There are no feathers.

No ravens.

No house.

Just the road.

I don’t know how I got here, but I don’t think it matters anymore.

I’m driving.

And this time, nothing is stopping me.

I just needed to write this down before I forget.

Before it all starts to feel normal again.

So if something happens, there’s proof that I was here.

That this place exists.

That something is wrong.

I keep checking the mirror without thinking about it.

There’s nothing there.

Just the road behind me.

Just darkness.

It’s quiet.

Too quiet.

I think...

I think I can hear something.

Faint.

Behind me.

At first I thought it was the engine.

But it’s not.

It’s slower.

More precise.

Like something tapping from the inside.

No.

Not tapping.

Pecking.

It’s coming from the trunk.
