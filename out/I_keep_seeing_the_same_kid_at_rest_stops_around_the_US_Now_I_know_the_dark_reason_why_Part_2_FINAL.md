Living in The Waiting wasn’t so bad, at least at first. Mikey and I stole as much junk food and as many sodas as our little bodies could handle. We’d just punch in the number, and the machines spat out anything we wanted, free of charge: Rold Gold pretzels, Sprite, M&M’s, Dr. Pepper, Snickers, Doritos. At the bigger truck stops, we could steal real food. Cooks would place whole meals on diner counters, and we’d snatch them right in front of the wait staff. They never noticed. Mikey was right. No matter how much we ate or drank, we never changed. We gained no pounds. We never grew taller. We never aged, period. 

Aside from eating, we stole lots of cars and motorcycles. Land Rovers. Ford F-150s. Harley-Davidsons. A Rolls-Royce or two. Even semi-trucks. At fifteen, I already had my learner’s permit and had driven my father’s Subaru a bunch of times, so I was somewhat well-versed in handling vehicles. But my stint in The Waiting made me an expert. Having all the time in the world and so many cars available for the taking allowed me to learn all the ins and outs of anything with wheels and a motor. The first car I stole in The Waiting was a Corvette Z06. The driver had stepped out to throw up in the parking lot. Must’ve been carsick or maybe drunk driving. Either way, he left the driver’s side door wide open. I jumped in and pulled out of the lot, tires screeching. “See you in Hell, suckas!” I screamed as I drove away from the rest stop. The driver didn’t notice. Drivers never noticed as we got behind the wheel. No cops ever stopped us either. It was exhilarating. For a few minutes at least. We couldn’t drive more than a couple of hundred meters before that same bright flash brought us to another rest stop. And another rest stop. And another. And another. The constant shift became disorienting, then nauseating. That’s why Mikey often walked from one location to the next if we were traveling. Unless we needed to escape the Gumbys, of course. 

We usually slept in tents and sleeping bags, which we carried in heavy rucksacks, like we were backpacking across the country. We stole the gear from a camper van at one of the rest stops. Sometimes we’d sleep on benches or even on the ground if it was a particularly warm night, staring up at the stars. They seemed to shine much brighter in The Waiting. 

We mostly wore the same clothes, but there was always a bathroom available to wash up, and some rest stops even had showers. If a thunderstorm or blizzard broke out, we’d just head for a different rest area that had bright, sunny skies. We were the masters of our secret domain, answering to no one but ourselves… and our unseen enemy. 

Now and then, I’d ask Mikey about the Red Gumbys, but he rarely elaborated on the subject other than to say, RUN. 

“I won’t let them catch us again,” Mikey said one day. 

“Is that what happened to the other kids in The Waiting?” I asked. “The ones who are gone now? The Red Gumbys got them?”  

Mikey nodded. Solemn. 

“What do they want?” I asked. “Why take us?” 

“Let’s get some sleep, Dash,” Mikey said, crawling into his sleeping bag. “We can discuss this another time.” He said that a lot whenever I started digging into the Gumby subject. Any time I brought them up, it triggered a great deal of anxiety in Mikey. I could only imagine how traumatic it had been to see dozens of other kids fall victim to these supernatural intruders over the years.   
   
— 

Our freedom was fun, but it grew old. And time flew by. A month had already passed in the real world, but it felt like only a couple of days in The Waiting. Everywhere we went, everything was rushed: The people, the cars, the weather. In and out and back on the road again. It was like our perception was tied to the liminal spaces we now inhabited.   

“We call it Life at High Speed,” Mikey said. “Everyone who becomes trapped here feels the same way. I’m surprised it’s been almost two decades for me,” he said. 

I saw signs that my parents were searching for me, but I never saw them or anyone else I knew. There were posters at the rest stop where I disappeared with a $50,000 reward for anyone who found me. I wondered if I could collect that money if I somehow made it out of The Waiting. I really missed home, and kept asking Mikey about ways to escape…

“What if we dug underground?” 

“You’ll just end up underground at another rest stop.” 

“How about driving really fast? Pedal to the floor.” 

“Tried that. Did nothing.”

“Hiding in the back of someone’s car while they drive off?”

“Tried that, too.”

It seemed every idea I came up with, no matter how outlandish, Mikey or one of his old companions had already tried it. And they got nowhere new. 

But I finally stumbled upon something Mikey hadn’t tried before. As we were eating eggs and bacon from a greasy spoon diner at a truck stop, a random thought popped into my head. “How do you know if you’ve been to every rest stop in the country?” I asked. 

Mikey paused a moment, sipping his coffee. “I mean… after all these years…”

“Yeah, but have you or any of the others ever created a map or list of all the places?” I asked.  

Mikey scrunched up his face, thinking. Then, shook his head. 

“We should make a map of all rest stops in The Waiting,” I offered. “And check off each one we visit.”  

“Why do that?” 

“'Cause maybe one of these places has a way out,” I said. “Or something else that could lead to a way out.” 

“What makes you think that there’d be anything like that?” Mikey asked. 

“I dunno,” I said, pushing my scrambled eggs around the plate. “I have no idea what we might find. But it’ll give us a good starting point. My dad used to say, ‘You never really know a place until you’ve mapped it out, whether it's uncharted territory or your own hometown.’ I think there’s still more that you don’t know about The Waiting, even after all these years, because you’ve never seen it all laid out before.”

Mikey finished his cup of coffee. A wide smile creased his face. 

—

From then on, Mikey and I had a mission. I tore down a Missing Persons poster with my face on it and used the back as a blank sheet to start our map, and we both stole some pens and pencils from a tourist info office in Colorado. I drew the map (I had a pretty decent hand from years of making my own little comics as a kid) while Mikey named each rest stop. The Great Waiting Map (as we called it) was a massive undertaking. We picked a single direction and kept going straight, stopping only to sleep and eat. There were thousands of rest stops to catalog, some of which Mikey had never seen before. Or at least he couldn’t ever remember visiting them. 

At each location, I’d draw a unique or notable feature (a Paul Bunyan statue at the entrance, a kiosk on coyotes, a busted vending machine) and show how it connected to nearby rest stops. Finding unique features was hard at most locations. We had to pick things like the number of parking spots or a funny-looking cactus near the facilities. I also learned a lot about people on this mission. We saw so many at the places we visited, from all walks of life. I realized that most of us have some level of anxiety, running behind on a trip, leaving something important back at home, work issues, relationship issues, existential crises. Mostly, I saw how lonely people were, whether traveling alone or in a large group on a tourist bus. Everyone was glued to their iPhones or tablets, lost in their own little world. Just waiting to get to their next destination. 

“Man. I really miss my folks,” I told Mikey one day after we stopped for a snack break at a rest stop in Kansas. I hated to think of them. I knew my Mom would be an absolute wreck. She grew paranoid if I texted her back a half hour late. And my Dad was probably drinking himself into an early grave, blaming himself for letting me wander on my own at that big truck stop. They both probably feared the worst. 

“You know, I can’t even remember my parents,” Mikey said. “Like, I know who they are, but I can’t picture them anymore. When I try to picture their faces, it’s just… Blank.” He looked at me, teary-eyed again. “You think they still miss me?”

“Of course they do,” I said. “They’ll never forget you. They probably think about you every single day.”

“Really? After all these years?

“Absolutely.” I excused myself to go to the restroom and burst into a sob that lasted close to an hour. 

–

The Map Catalog was a slow, cumbersome process, but it felt good. It felt like we were getting somewhere, even though we didn’t know the destination. 

“This is pointless,” Mikey said one day, looking worn down. “The damn map will never end.” 

“There are only so many rest stops,” I told him. “And besides, each new one is another step closer to finding a way out.”

“You still believe that?” Mikey asked. 

“There’s no proof that we’re stuck here,” I said. “For all we know, the next rest stop will show us a way out.” 

Mikey just laughed. 

The next rest stop would be his last. 

It was a bright, sunny day. Mikey and I had just arrived at a way station in the middle of the New Mexico desert, not far from Roswell. There were no cars in the lot. No people lounging around. Nothing for miles in any direction. It was unusually cold for the location and time of day. The last four rest stops we’d cataloged were in the desert, and each one was hotter than the last. Now, I was practically shivering. 

“Why’s it so cold?” I asked soon after we arrived. We stopped behind a tiny adobe building to get out of the wind. It was the only building on the lot. 

Mikey was shivering even more than I. He looked around, wide-eyed. “This is… Shit. This isn’t right,” he said in a low voice, almost like a whisper. 

“Why? What is it?” I asked.

Mikey’s face grew ashen. “It’s them.” 

The cobalt blue sky suddenly darkened, as if a thin haze had settled over the atmosphere. In seconds, the midday sunlight turned into twilight darkness. I spun around. A vast, inky cloud filled the air. It rose from a jet-black semi-truck barreling down the highway. The truck appeared small at first, just a black blob on the horizon. But that blob grew bigger by the second. It was driving over a hundred miles per hour. 

“Run!” Mikey shouted. 

We bolted for the opposite edge of the rest stop. By now, I could hear the animalistic growl of the truck’s massive engine. We were almost at the property’s edge. Ten steps away… Then the bright flash—

Arriving at another similarly desolate rest stop a dozen miles away. The sun was still shining—no inky menace in the bright blue skies. I welcomed the oppressive desert heat, feeling elated. But that elation disappeared the moment I turned around. 

Mikey was gone. I was alone at the new rest stop. 

I should’ve kept running. It was what both of us had decided to do if the other were taken. “Don’t stop. Don’t even look back,” Mikey had said. “Just get to the next rest stop, fast as you can.” 

But I couldn’t keep going. Mikey had become my only friend left in the world. My lifeline to sanity. Perhaps I could still drag him with me into safety. I went back— Flash—

It was pitch dark at the previous rest stop. “Mikey?” I called out. I could barely see the adobe structure amid all the thick black smoke from the tanker truck. But this didn’t feel like regular smoke; the kind that scratches your throat and makes you cough. This smoke felt like cold mist, and it made me calm, in a strange, sleepy way. 

“Mikey?” I said again, lower.  

The tanker truck’s rumbling engine was so loud it shook the ground. I was close to the vehicle. Very close. Too close. 

“Mike, are you there?” I whispered. 

That’s when I first saw them, flashes in the darkness. They intermittently appeared as smoke billowed around the oily trunk. I stopped dead in my tracks, hiding behind a nearby trash can, peering at the strange visitors. There were three of them. Mikey was right. They looked a lot like Gumby, only much bigger and blood-red. Their rubbery humanoid shapes were all wobbly and misshapen. *Were they wearing suits or was that their skin?* I wondered. It was hard to tell from the brief glimpses I caught as the smoke moved in and out of the way. 

The Gumbys had their backs turned when I saw them. They crowded around Mikey, who lay face down on the asphalt beside the tanker truck, his eyes half-lidded. He didn’t seem so frightened. More like, confused, or drugged. The Red Gumbys grabbed a series of oily black tubes from the tanker truck’s side. Each tube had an accordion shape, like you’d see on an old vacuum. The strange beings placed the sucker ends of these accordion tubes on Mikey’s body: his legs, his back, his head. Each tube connected with a loud and sudden noise: THWAP-THWAP-THWAP! They attached a half-dozen of them to the helpless boy. Then, one of the Red Gumbys pulled a big, greasy lever beneath the truck’s tank. The tubes started wriggling like giant black worms. Mikey’s body contorted as the tubes sucked something out of him. *Blood? Guts?* I couldn’t see what it was. Soon, his body began to shrink, deflating like a balloon with tiny pinprick holes in it. Mikey groaned. His insides were melting away, disappearing into that big blank tank atop the truck, becoming some sort of obscene “fuel.”

“STOP!” I shouted. I said it without even thinking of the consequences—a reflex. A stupid decision, but I couldn’t bear to watch my only friend left in the world die before me. 

All three Gumbys turned my way, and I finally got a good look at their faces. They were NOT wearing suits. Their heads were just big red rubber squares with no mouth or nose. Only eyes. Eyes that contained multitudes. Writhing. Infinite. Eyes like miniature black holes. I could hardly look at them. 

Mikey and I shared a glance in that moment. The boy was barely conscious now, his body flattening against the pavement. ‘R-r-r-r-r-un,’ he whimpered. Seconds later, he was nothing more than a pile of skin and clothes lying on the ground. 

I bolted in the opposite direction, headed for the edge of the rest stop. But before I made it to the property line, a pair of strong, rubbery hands grabbed my shoulders. One of the Red Gumbys had snatched me. The creature’s oily stench made me dizzy. Then, the creature spoke inside my head; its voice like a humming radiator. 

**‘Do you want to go home?’** it asked.

I could only nod in agreement. Tears clouded my vision. My whole body was shaking. 

**‘Then bring us another, like you. And we’ll grant you your freedom.’** 

The creature pushed me forward. There was a bright flash and— I fell on some warm desert sand and passed out… for a long… long… time.  

*—*

I woke sometime later (*Days? Months? Years?*). I was at the same rest stop. Only now it was a typical day again. Bright sunshine above. No black smoke. No tanker truck. The desert heat had returned. So too had all the motorists, zipping along the highway, always in a hurry, oblivious to any cosmic horrors lurking on the horizon. 

I slowly got to my feet, dusting myself off. I searched the area for any lingering sign of Mikey, the Black Tanker Truck, or the Red Gumbys. But there was none. It was as if the whole experience had been some horrible nightmare. 

***Bring us another, like you…*** *Were they telling the truth? Would such an act really free me from this prison?* I shuddered to think what those rubbery bastards would do to the new kid if I even found one. I stayed at that rest stop for days afterwards, not eating, barely drinking, mostly crying. *Would I simply die if I stopped doing anything,* I wondered? *Would my body turn into a dried-up husk? Would it finally free me from this Hell?* But another, stronger thought rose out of my depression: *You have a Map to finish, Dash! The Great Map of The Waiting!* Mikey and I had already cataloged over 300 rest stops, and there were many, many more to go. One of them might still be a way out. Instead of wallowing in despair, I plunged my remaining energy into continuing the Map. I owed it to Mikey. If nothing else, I would leave behind a record of our time in this strange place for the next unfortunate soul to find themself stuck here. Maybe it would help. 

I visited so many new places, I had to grab a stack of posters to continue building out the map, taping them together along the edges. It grew into this massive tapestry of drawings with so many crisscrossing lines that it looked like a madman’s murder board. Even with my busy schedule, the loneliness never left. In fact, it only grew worse. There was no one to share in any of my latest discoveries, from a rest stop with the world’s largest potato statue to one shaped like a giant alligator. I started talking to myself, pretending that Mikey was still there with me: 

* “This would be a good place to visit in the Winter, right, Mikey?” 
* “Wow. I’ve never seen a vending machine that only sold Fanta.” 
* “We can see the Grand Canyon from here. Isn’t that incredible?” 

But no matter the view, it was never as exciting as seeing it with someone else.

—

I was at a rest stop in upstate New York when it happened. Beautiful Fall weather. An apple orchard grew nearby, not at the rest stop, but right next to it. One of the tree’s branches hung over the property line, and a big, red, juicy apple dangled from its tip. It’d been ages since I’d last tasted fresh fruit. Everything had been processed junk food from a vending machine or a heavy meal from a greasy diner. I was on my tippy toes, trying to snatch the fruit when I heard her soft voice. 

“Hey. Are you Dashiell?” 

I fell over in shock, crushing my folded-up map. When I looked up, I saw the prettiest girl I had ever seen. She was about my age, with long auburn hair and big brown eyes. It took me a moment to realize she was actually looking at me and not through me like all the others. Then, I became very self-conscious, smoothing my hair with my hands. “W-w-what?” I stammered. 

“Are you Dashiell Bowman?” The girl asked. She stayed put, as if she didn't want to scare me away. 

“Y-yes,” I said. “Y-y-you can see me? Hear me?”

The girl nodded. “I’m Tricia,” she said. “I’ve seen you at a few rest stops before. And I’ve seen your poster.” 

“Call the police. Tell them…” but I stopped myself. *Tell the cops what? That I’m stuck in another dimension where tall rubber monsters suck the life out of kids, and only this girl can see me?* No one would believe that story. Even I had trouble believing it. 

“It’s ok,” Tricia said. “I’ve seen others like you.” 

“Others like me?” I asked. 

“Ghosts,” Tricia said. 

“But I’m not a ghost,” I said. I could still eat. I could still drink, and piss, and shit. But that wouldn’t make any sense to her. This girl was looking at me the same way I looked at Mikey at the fateful truck stop in Arizona—a lost soul. 

“You said you’ve seen others like me?” I asked. 

“Yes.” 

“Like, at rest stops?” I hadn’t even noticed the chill in the air yet.

“No,” Tricia said. “But sometimes, when I’m at certain places, they come to me. Seeking help.” 

“What happened to these other ghosts?” I asked. It had grown darker. *Was the sun setting already?* 

“I help them remember who they were and where they came from,” Tricia explained. “So they can find peace.” 

It was much colder now. Black smoke curled into the sky. The Tanker Truck was on its way, barreling towards the rest stop. 

***‘Bring us another, like you…’***

Tricia was still watching me, waiting for me to respond. I didn’t have to do anything. My job was already complete. Somehow, I’d lured in another victim. All I had to do was wait for them to arrive and then… And then… 

***'And we’ll grant you your freedom.’*** The Red Gumbys had said. *But how could anyone trust them?* A terrifying thought occurred to me then. Those creatures had almost certainly made the same promise to Mikey before my arrival. And they had probably promised the kid before him, and the kid before, and before, and so on. All those lonely children, desperate for a way home. There’s no way those monsters would honor such a promise, even if it were possible. They were too greedy. They only wanted more fuel, no matter who it was. And if there was ever someone who didn’t deserve to be fuel, it was a kind soul like her. 

“Listen, Tricia. I’m not like the others,” I said, backing away from her. “I don’t need your help. Go back to your family. Get out of here. Forget you ever saw me.” 

“What?” Tricia walked towards me. “You just asked me to call the police moments ago.” 

“You don’t understand. I’m not like the others,” I said. There was a loud hiss—air brakes. The Tanker Truck had just pulled into the rest stop. Cold mist-like smoke filled the area. 

But Tricia noticed none of this. It was still a bright autumn day to her. “I want to help you,” she said. 

I could see the blood-red crown of one of the Gumbys’ rubbery heads bobbing behind the rest stop building. Headed our way. 

“You can’t help,” I said. “Get away from me.” 

But Tricia kept moving closer, worried. “What’s wrong?”

“Trish? Trish!” A man’s voice called out. “Are you still in the bathroom? We need to go,  
honey.”

“Is that your dad?” I asked. “Go to him. Get out of here. NOW!”

“Oh, he’s always fussing over nothing,” Tricia said. “My parents hover over me too much.” 

I groaned. This was getting nowhere. I needed to do something else if I were to save this girl from my fate. Something drastic. Just as those long rubbery legs appeared from behind the rest stop, a new idea shot into my head— a batshit crazy one. I handed my folded-up map to Trisha, “Here. Take this. Tell my parents I love them.” My heart leapt when I realized Tricia could hold the Map. Perhaps I could get a message out to the world after all. But there was no time now. All three Gumbys had rounded the corner of the rest stop building. 

I ran headlong TOWARDS them. “This way!” I shouted.

“Where are you going?” Tricia asked. 

I ducked beneath one of the creature’s long legs as I ran around the side of the truck stop.  All three Gumbys turned in my direction, away from Trisha, who was probably super confused by now. 

I kept looking over my shoulder at the tall rubbery monsters, making sure they were following me. They didn’t seem angry or scared. More curious than anything else. I ran beneath a kiosk, squeezed between some parked cars, then across the parking lot, and beelined for one vehicle…    

The Black Tanker Truck. 

By now, the Red Gumbys were running after me. I had their full attention. But it was too late to abandon the mission. I had to finish it, whether it ended in failure or success. I could see the creatures gaining on me, reflected in the truck’s side window—no time to hesitate. No second-guessing. I pulled the driver’s side door handle. Click. It was unlocked! 

“Yes!” I had a feeling it would be. *Why would such monsters ever bother to lock their vehicle when they knew the terror they struck in their victims? No one would be stupid enough to try—no one, except for me.* The heavy metal door swung wide as I climbed into the truck. “Please be here. Please be here.” I searched the cabin. It appeared normal. There was the speedometer, a CB radio, and an ignition switch. But it was empty. 

*Shit!*

I slammed the door shut and locked it moments before the Gumbys arrived. Then, I continued searching for the key. There had to be a spare somewhere. I checked behind the visor, in the glove compartment, the center console–

BANG-BANG-BANG! One of the Gumbys slammed a fat, rubbery hand on the side window. I could tell it was angry, its black-hole eyes sore and bleeding. I heard screams issuing from inside the monster’s head. Human screams. The sounds were awful, but I couldn’t let them distract me. I reached down under the driver’s side mat. 

*“Help me,”* a familiar voice pleaded. It was Mikey’s voice, coming from within the Red Gumby’s head. *“You’ll destroy me if you leave. You’ll destroy us all.”* 

There was another chorus of ear-splitting screams. More banging on the windows. All three Gumbys surrounded the truck cabin, trying to get in. They were desperate now. It had to be in here, somewhere… I felt beneath the driver’s seat. My fingers grasped something small, cold, and metal. A key. *Success!* I jammed it in the ignition, turning it over. The engine rumbled to life, vibrating my seat. “Ah ha!” I looked at the Gumbys, still trying to claw their way inside. “So long, fuckers,” I screamed. Then, I stretched my leg to reach the brake and pressed hard on it as I shifted the gear into drive. My childhood dream of being a truck driver had come to life. I pushed down on the accelerator, and the truck jolted forward, leaving the Red Gumbys behind. Leaving the whole rest stop behind.

I strained to pull the massive steering wheel, bringing the tanker truck onto the highway. It was a straight road, with dense forest on either side. Barely any traffic, but that didn’t matter. The truck just barreled straight through all other cars on the road like a ghost. I kept my foot down on the accelerator. I was already a mile from the truck stop, something that would’ve been impossible on foot. *I’ve done it,* I thought. *I’m finally leaving The Waiting.* But the highway soon transformed into a white asphalt road, running through a black, frozen desert. There were no other cars on this strange path. No rest stops. No exits. No gas stations or fast food restaurants. No signs of civilization, period. 

—

I’m still on that highway now, searching for an exit. Any exit. A way out. A way home. It’s nighttime here, but there’s no moon, and there are no stars— just inky blackness overhead, with the occasional wisp of icy clouds. The Tanker Truck’s yellow headlights provide meagre illumination, revealing an endless landscape of desolation. A cold wind whips across black dunes on either side of the white road, whipping up tufts of sand like wraiths. I don’t know if I’ve been driving for forty minutes or forty years. This road is endless. At times, I pull over and shut the engine off. I wonder if I should’ve just carried on with their game, let the Red Gumbys have their new victim. Let them drain me of everything. All the memories of my parents, my family, my friends, school, road trips, Mikey, even my memories of The Waiting. All turned into fuel. Perhaps they would have drained all the pain, the anxiety, the depression. The loneliness. But those thoughts only last for a moment. I shed a few tears, and then banish them. And I’m back on the road again, driving towards a destination unknown.
