I could outrun every one of my brothers, feet barely touching the grass before the wind itself seemed to chase me. McArthur used to say I was born to carry the king’s messages swift as plague, silent as debt coming due. At ten I already trailed the royal horses by a few strides, lungs burning, tasting iron and dust, while the hooves thundered like judgment just ahead of me.

Some days I slipped over the jagged mountains to raid old man Haggard’s citrus groves. The fruit hung low and heavy, skin splitting under my fingers, juice stinging the cuts on my palms. Then came the hounds, black shapes boiling out of the brush, jaws wet, eyes like dying coals. I always beat them home, but not without their teeth grazing my heels, leaving thin red lines that burned for days.

My brothers got away with things I never had. I learned early to keep my head down. Father was a hard man who spoke little and struck often. His corrections came in the flat crack of an open hand or the duller thud of a fist, delivered without anger, as though pain were simply the language he knew best. Mother lay wasting in the back room, breath shallow and rattling, so Father carried burdens no man should bear alone, and passed them on to us in bruises and silence.

We were three brothers, and what little we owned came down in a chain of decay: threadbare rags passed from eldest to youngest until they reached me, the runt, where the holes gaped widest, like mouths that had already swallowed most of the cloth. Mother had been bedbound for years, her body a fading outline beneath the blankets, too weak to mend so much as a seam. We wore our tatters as best we could, the wind fingering through the rents in winter, the sun baking the filth into our skin in summer.

Our days followed a brittle routine. Mornings meant hunched over cracked slates, scratching letters and sums under Father's eye. The rest of the hours were ours, provided we kept clear of the kitchen and his workroom. He was a master farrier, horseshoes hammered so true they seemed to fit the hoof before it was shod, but iron and sweat bought little bread for three growing boys with hollow stomachs. The clink of his hammer rang through the clay walls of our keep from dusk to dawn.

One morning Sedrick, second-eldest, announced he was going into the dark woods Father had forbidden us since we could walk. “Just to see,” he said, as though curiosity were a harmless thing. Rules existed to be tested, and I, eager to prove I wasn't the useless youngest, tagged along, though Sedrick only tolerated me because I could shinny up trees faster than any of them if we spotted fruit or honeycomb worth the risk.

That was the last morning I saw my brother whole.

We moved cautiously at first, stepping over roots that looked too much like knuckles, the undergrowth pressing close and whispering against our legs. The light dimmed quickly, as though the canopy above drank it.

“Sedrick.”

“Shhhh.” His hiss cut sharper than a blade.

“Sedrick.” Softer this time, barely breath.

“What.”

“Can I carry your bow?”

“Keep quiet, you simpleton. We don’t know what’s out here.”

We pressed deeper, Sedrick leading as though some unseen map had already been burned into his mind, a trail no one in our village had ever truly dared follow. Branches clawed at our sleeves like fingers testing cloth for weakness. The path, if it could be called that, narrowed until the trees leaned in, their trunks dark and ridged like old scars.

I kept glancing at the bow slung loose across his back, the string taut, the wood oiled and warm from his body. An idea sparked, childish and reckless. I could outrun him. I always could. In one fluid leap I launched forward, wind rushing past my ears, snatched the bow from his shoulder with a sharp tug of leather, and bolted.

“You can’t catch me!” I shouted over my shoulder, voice cracking with laughter that already felt too thin in the heavy air.

“I’m going to cut off your hands when I get you, you little runt!” Sedrick roared, boots pounding close behind, close enough I could hear the huff of his breath, the snap of twigs under his weight.

Adrenaline flooded me, sharp and bright. I vaulted fallen logs black with rot, ducked under low boughs that whipped my face, weaving through undergrowth that parted only to close again behind me, like doors sealing shut. The forest seemed to rearrange itself around my flight: roots rising just where my foot would land, vines snagging at ankles as though trying to slow me for sport. I ran the way I always had, faster than hounds, faster than horses, faster than fear itself, until my lungs burned and my vision tunneled to the next patch of ground.

Minutes stretched as the pounding footsteps never quite faded, yet never drew nearer. Then, silence. Not the sudden stop of pursuit ending, but an absence so complete it pressed against my ears.

I slowed, chest heaving, bow clutched like a trophy I no longer wanted. I glanced back.

Sedrick was gone.

No flash of Sedrick’s shirt through the green gloom. No angry silhouette crashing after me. No sound at all except my own ragged breathing and the distant, indifferent drip of water somewhere deep. The space where he should have been, right on my heels, as he always was, was empty. Not empty like he’d fallen behind. Empty like the forest had simply opened its mouth and swallowed him whole.

I stood there, bowstring cutting into my palm, waiting for the yell, the curse, the inevitable tackle that would end in bruises and grudging laughter. Nothing came.

“Sedrick!”

My voice cracked against the trees, swallowed instantly by the damp green hush.

“Sedrick, you can come out now!”

Nothing answered. No rustle, no mocking laugh, no snap of branch under boot. The silence pressed in like wet cloth over my mouth.

“Sedrick, I’m sorry, I’ll give back your bow. Please.”

The words hung, pathetic and small. The bow felt heavier now, an accusation slung across my back.

I spun toward where I thought we’d entered, back the way the path had been, but the trees had shifted, or my memory had. Every trunk looked the same: black, ridged, watchful. The canopy fused with the bruised clouds above, blotting the last gray light. Dusk crept forward on soft paws, turning the undergrowth into a maze of shadow and thorn.

I bolted anyway, calling his name until my throat rasped raw. No reciprocation came, only echoes that mocked me, bouncing off unseen walls of bark. My heart hammered so hard it felt like it might crack ribs. Had I lost him, or had he lost me? Or had something else claimed the space between us?

I picked a direction at random and ran. Thorns tore at my already ragged sleeves, roots snagged my ankles like hands from below. The forest fought me harder than on the way in, as though it resented my escape while cradling whatever it had taken. Legs burned, lungs screamed, but I ran.

Clear sky broke above me, a cold sliver of moon rising like a blade. The familiar crooked fence of our farm appeared through the gloom, the low house squatting dark and unchanged. Relief flooded me, thin and false. I sprinted the last stretch like a hare from a storm, slammed through the door so hard the hinges groaned.

Father stood at the hearth, broad back to me, hammer still in one callused fist. My other brothers froze mid-motion, eyes wide, mouths open. No Sedrick. The absence punched harder than any blow.

Father turned slowly. His face was stone, but his eyes burned with something colder than anger. Before I could speak, his hand rose, thick, scarred, inevitable. The slap connected with an open crack that rang through the room like a hammer on anvil. My head snapped sideways as the world tilted white-hot. I tasted blood and floorboards as I dropped. Tears came unbidden, spilling over cheeks already stinging, but they weren’t for the pain of the strike.

They were for Sedrick. For the truth I now had to carry alone in this house of iron and silence. For the certainty that if Father learned what waited in the dark woods, or what had waited there for his second son, the consequences would be far worse than any hand or fist. He would not merely correct. He would break.

The room stayed quiet except for the low crackle of the fire and my own choked breaths. No one moved to help me up. In that moment, I understood: the forest hadn’t just taken Sedrick. It had left something behind in me, a seed of dread that would grow in the dark corners of our home long after the bruises faded.

I wiped the hot stripes across my cheek with the back of my hand, the skin already swelling tight. Consciousness flickered back in painful pulses. I swallowed against the blood and broken tooth taste in my mouth.

“Father.”

I kept my eyes on his boots, scuffed, iron-stained, and the fist still clenched at his side, knuckles white as bone.

“Spit it out, runt.” The words came through gritted teeth.

“Father, it’s…it’s Sedrick. We went…”

“You went where.”

He cut me off before the lie could form. His voice was unforgiving, same as his hand.

“We went to the forest.” The sob broke free despite my clenched jaw.

He seized my face as hard, callused fingers clamped my jaw like a vice forged in his own smithy. The pressure ground teeth against bone. “Had your mother not taught the bastard how to speak? Should I move your jaw for you?”

“Sedrick is gone,” I forced out, the words mangled, fighting past the vice. Tears spilled freely now as I couldn’t hold them, couldn’t hold anything. He squeezed tighter, as though pain might wring the truth faster.

Then he released me with a shove that sent me sprawling again. My skull rang against the floorboards.

“What do you mean, bastard?” His voice dropped colder. “He is right there.”

He pointed.

I followed the line of his thick finger across the dim room. There, standing a few metres away among my other brothers, who watched with wide, silent eyes, was Sedrick.

Whole. Unmarked. Bow still slung across his back as though nothing had happened. His face was calm, almost serene, the same half-smirk he wore when he’d won at wrestling or stolen the last crust. He met my gaze without blinking. No anger. No relief. Just quiet, patient regard.

The room tilted. My pulse thundered in my ears, drowning the fire’s crackle. I stared at the boy who had vanished into the green dark, who I had left behind in the silence that swallowed footsteps, and who now stood here, breathing the same stale air as the rest of us.

He looked exactly like Sedrick.

But something in the way he stood, too still, shoulders squared at an angle that felt rehearsed, made the hairs on my neck rise. The forest had opened its mouth and taken him. And now it had spat something back.

Father’s shadow loomed over me again. “You see him, don’t you? Standing there like always.” A pause, heavy with warning. “So what game are you playing, boy-bastard?”

I couldn’t answer. My mouth worked soundlessly. Across the room, Sedrick, or whatever wore his face, tilted his head slightly, as though listening to a distant sound only he could hear.

The fire popped. No one else spoke.

In that moment, I understood the true horror wasn’t that Sedrick was gone.

It was that he had come back.

“Retrieve him from my sight,” Father snarled, “and don’t ever defy me again, or I’ll have you fed to the wolves.”

He spat a thick rope of mucus onto the scarred wooden boards at my feet. It glistened in the firelight like something alive.

“McArthur,” he continued, voice dropping to the low hammer-clang he used for final orders, “tie the bastard to the stable post. Make sure he cannot trot away like the wild dog he is. No food. No water. Three days.”

McArthur said nothing. He knew better. Any protest, any hesitation, and the same iron fist, or worse, would find him next. He stepped forward, eyes locked on mine. Not anger there. Disappointment, deep, quiet, the kind that cuts sharper than rage. Disappointment that I had been so reckless, that I had dragged the family into whatever abyss now yawned beneath us.

He hauled me up by the arm, grip firm but not cruel. As he dragged me toward the door, he paused, glancing back at the figure standing among the other brothers.

“Why didn’t you bring him with you?” McArthur asked Sedrick, voice low, almost pleading. “You always watched out for the runt. Why leave him behind?”

Sedrick did not answer.

He simply stared, unblinking, unreadable. The half-smirk was still there, frozen in place like a mask that had slipped slightly askew. No retort. No boast. No flash of the old fire that had always sparked between them, the endless competition that masked how fiercely they both guarded their youngest brother. The silence stretched, thick and wrong. McArthur’s shoulders stiffened. I felt the tremor pass through his hand where it held my arm.

Sedrick tilted his head, just a fraction, as though listening to something far off, something the rest of us couldn’t hear. Then he looked away, gaze sliding past McArthur like water over stone.

McArthur exhaled sharply through his nose.

“Mac… I’m Sorry, I didn’t.”

“What were you thinking following Sedrick. You could have got lost in those woods, or worse.”

I didn’t say anything, I couldn’t find the words or comprehension to explain that for some reason Sedrick might still be out there, and that whoever was standing there was not our brother.

He pulled me outside into the cold night air, the door thudding shut behind us like a coffin lid.

The stable smelled of damp hay and old manure. McArthur bound my wrists to the post with rough rope. The looseness of the rope was his one last mercy. He didn’t meet my eyes as he worked.

“Three days,” he muttered, more to himself than me. “Maybe if you’re good, he will let you off earlier this time.”

He turned to leave, then stopped in the doorway. Moonlight slanted across his face, carving deep shadows.

“If that isn’t Sedrick in there,” he said quietly, “then what is it?”

He didn’t wait for an answer. The door closed. The bolt slid home.

I was alone with the horses shifting uneasily in their stalls, the wind moaning through cracks in the boards, and the knowledge that something wearing my brother’s face now slept under the same roof as the rest of my family.

Three days without food or water would weaken me. But the real hunger, the one that gnawed deepest, was the certainty that when I returned to the house, whatever waited inside might no longer be content to stand silent and watch.

The third night the stable became a listening post for slaughter.

Father’s voice tore through the dark, not the familiar hammer-clang of reprimand, but a raw, animal bellow I had never heard before. It came from the back of the house, from Mother’s room. Words drifted out in shards, carried on the cold wind that slipped through the stable slats:

“…the bastard… brought this… disloyalty… ruin…”

My name wasn’t spoken, but it hung in every syllable. I was the root, the stain, the proof of whatever betrayal Father had nursed in silence for years.

Then McArthur’s cry, sharp and desperate. “Father, no!”

A crash of wood overturning. Furniture splintering. The heavy thud of a body striking floorboards, followed by a choked gasp that could only be my brother’s. The house itself seemed to shudder, as though the violence inside was shaking its bones loose.

I yanked at the rope until skin tore and blood slicked my wrists. The knots held. My legs buckled under me. Three days of no food, no water had turned muscle to water. All I could do was listen as the sounds grew wetter, more intimate.

A scream, Mother’s, high and final, cut short by a gurgling choke. The noise of liquid spilling, thick and rhythmic, like water poured from a cracked jug onto bare boards. Then silence. Not peaceful silence. The silence that follows when breath stops being drawn.

A sob ripped out of me, involuntary, animal. I wrenched harder. Something in the rope gave, a frayed strand, a moment of mercy, and my wrists slipped free. I stumbled out of the stable on legs that barely obeyed, bare feet slapping cold earth, lungs burning with every ragged inhale.

The back door stood ajar. Lamplight spilled out in a bloody rectangle. I pushed inside.

The scene waited.

Mother lay across the narrow bed, sheets and nightgown soaked black-red. A wide gash yawned across her throat, edges ragged where the blade had hesitated, then committed. Blood still pulsed weakly from the wound in slow, dying spurts, pooling beneath her head and dripping to the floor in soft taps. Her eyes were open, staring past me at the ceiling, as though searching for something that had already left.

Father stood over her, right hand locked around the handle of his farrier’s knife, the same blade he used to trim hooves, now glistening crimson to the wrist. His chest heaved. His face was a mask of rage and something emptier, something hollowed out. The knife trembled, not from guilt, but from unsatisfied hunger, as though one throat had not been enough to slake whatever fury had been fed to him.

A sound escaped me, half sob, half whimper.

Father turned.

“You…” The word was a growl, scraped from deep in his throat.

Behind him, standing just inside the doorway to the hall, the thing that wore Sedrick’s face watched.

It had not moved to intervene. It had not spoken. It simply observed, posture relaxed, hands loose at its sides, the half-smirk still fixed in place like a carved expression on a wooden saint. When our eyes met, the smirk deepened, just a fraction, just enough to show satisfaction. Not triumph. Not glee. Quiet, patient contentment, the look of a craftsman regarding work well done.

McArthur staggered up from the floor behind Father, blood trickling from a split lip, eyes wild.

“Alex, run!”

The word broke the spell. My legs remembered how to move. I spun and bolted back through the doorway, bare feet slapping wet boards, then cold dirt. McArthur’s footsteps pounded after me. Father’s roar followed, wordless, bestial, but we were already running, two shadows fleeing toward the only place darker than the house we left behind. We didn’t have time to even consider taking the horses.

The woods opened their arms to receive us once more.
