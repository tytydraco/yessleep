The thing outside my motel room is breathing through the keyhole.

Not sniffing. Breathing.

Slow. Wet. Patient.

I’m sitting on the carpet with my back against the bed, staring at the thin yellow line under the door while the cheap air conditioner rattles hard enough to shake the wall. My phone says 3:14 AM, but the clock has been frozen there for almost an hour now. Or maybe longer. Time feels wrong after three days without real sleep.

The breathing stops.

I stop breathing too.

Then my mother’s voice slides through the door.

“Ethan? Honey, open up.”

Every muscle in my body locks.

It knows her perfectly now.

Same soft voice. Same tiny crack at the end of sentences. Same gentle knock she used when I was sick as a kid.

Three little taps.

I squeeze my eyes shut and press my forehead against my knees.

“You’re not my mother.”

Silence.

Then the voice changes instantly.

Now it’s my brother.

“Dude, come on. Please let me in.”

The thing never transitions naturally. That’s how I know. One second it’s my mother, the next it’s my brother, like someone switching radio stations.

I don’t answer.

The hallway carpet creaks.

Not footsteps.

More like weight dragging itself forward.

A smell crawls under the door a second later—rotting meat soaked in rainwater.

My stomach twists.

It’s getting impatient.

The first time I see it is three nights ago behind a gas station outside of Leeds. I’m driving home after midnight when I notice a woman standing near the diesel pumps in a yellow dress. She’s facing away from me, completely still while rain pours down hard enough to blur the windows.

Something feels wrong immediately.

Not fear exactly.

Recognition.

Like my body knows something my brain doesn’t.

I’m pumping gas when she asks, “Can you help me?”

Her voice sounds normal, but her body doesn’t move when she speaks.

No breathing. No shifting weight. Nothing.

I look closer.

Her feet are backward.

I get back in the car without answering.

When I pull away, she’s suddenly facing me.

I never see her turn around.

She smiles too wide.

And then she runs.

Jesus Christ.

I can still hear the sound her feet make against the pavement—like bare hands slapping wet concrete.

She chases my car for six miles.

Six.

Miles.

At first I think I’m hallucinating because nothing human moves like that. She keeps pace beside me at sixty miles an hour, yellow dress fluttering violently, head twitching at impossible angles while she grins through the passenger window.

Then her face starts changing.

Not morphing smoothly.

Breaking.

Like different faces are forcing themselves through wet clay.

Old woman.

Little boy.

Man with no eyes.

My own face.

I nearly crash when I see myself running beside the car.

Same haircut. Same terrified expression.

Except the copy is smiling.

I abandon the car at dawn after it wraps itself around a telephone pole. I don’t remember crashing. One second I’m driving, the next I’m upside down tasting blood and gasoline while something scratches on the passenger door. My door won’t open and my vision is getting blurry. 

I don’t think I’m going to survive this..
