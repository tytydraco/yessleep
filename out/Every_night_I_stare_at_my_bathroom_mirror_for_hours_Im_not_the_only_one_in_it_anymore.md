My wife and I started working for the mayor’s family no more than two years ago.

His house was big, bigger than anyone could ever need. It sat just outside of town, alone on top of a hill, and from his bedroom he could see everything.

My wife was great with his kids, and they loved her. She took them to and from school every weekday, cleaned their rooms, and helped them with homework.

Me, I was supposed to be some kind of “private security handyman.” That’s what he called it, anyway. In reality, I just fixed things, cleaned up, and made sure nothing went wrong.

We came every morning and left every night, until two weeks ago, when he told us he and his wife were leaving town and needed someone to stay overnight with the kids.

He was very specific about one thing. He wanted me awake most of the night. At the time, it didn’t seem strange. God, I wish I’d questioned it.

During the day, everything felt normal. Neither my wife nor I ever saw or heard anything unusual.

But at night, the house felt different.

Alive.

My wife and I stayed on the bottom floor in one of the guest rooms, as decided by his wife. The family’s rooms were all on the second floor. I barely ever used that bed. I wasn’t there to sleep, and I went back home every morning.

My wife, on the other hand, stayed with the kids all the time. She slept and ate at the house, only coming home after taking them to school, then leaving again when she had to pick them up.

For the first three nights, nothing strange happened. I heard some howling coming from the woods out back, and the wind would sometimes push doors open and shut around the house, even when I tried to keep them in place.

On the fourth night, I started noticing something else moving around the house with me.

At first, I thought it was the kids trying to prank me. I followed the sounds, thinking I’d catch them and send them back to bed without waking my wife. The sounds led me to the second floor. As soon as I reached the top of the stairs, the bathroom door moved on its own and the light flicked on.

I went in to check, but at first I couldn’t see anything, until a shadow moved in the mirror. It wasn’t mine, and it didn’t belong to anything in the room. I could only see it through the mirror.

I tried to clean it, thinking it was some kind of stain. But how can a stain move on its own? When I touched the surface of the mirror with a wet sponge, I heard the switch, and the light went out. The only light left came from under the door.

I turned the light back on.

The shadow was gone.

I told my wife when she got home, but she laughed, thinking I was just trying to scare her with a ghost story.

The fifth night, as soon as the kids and my wife fell asleep, I went back to that bathroom and turned on the light, hoping I would see the shadow again.

Nothing.

I stood there for an hour, staring into my own eyes in the reflection, trying to catch something moving just at the edge of my vision. That’s when I heard something break on the first floor.

Armed with my mop, I went down, ready to scare away some unruly teenager. A vase seemingly fell on its own. Then another. And another. All falling in the same direction.

The door to the room where my wife was sleeping opened. I slowly walked in, thinking I’d just tell her I knocked something over. But she was asleep.

The bed was reflected in the window across the room. And in that reflection... something was already standing in front of her.

It didn’t look like a person hidden in the dark. It had no hair, no clothes. It looked human, but not enough.

I hit the light switch and shouted her name.

The moment the light came on, the figure disappeared.

She jumped out of bed and stared at me, furious. “What the hell are you doing?! You almost gave me a heart attack!”

I didn’t answer. I was too shaken.

The silence broke when the kids screamed from upstairs. We both ran to them. When we entered their room, a window had shattered, glass covering the floor. The kids were shaking on the bed.

“We saw it! We saw it!”

They kept crying. My wife stayed with them, trying to calm them down, while I cleaned up the glass. The rest of the night passed as if nothing had happened.

I called the mayor the next morning and told him everything. He reassured me, said sometimes things like that happen. Then he asked me if I had seen anything.

I told him no. I didn’t want to sound like a lunatic talking about some shadow in the mirror.

That night, when I got back to the house, something felt off. Not in a way I could explain. Everything looked the same. The lights were on, the kids were watching TV, my wife was in the kitchen. Normal. But I couldn’t shake the feeling that I had missed something, like the house had been busy while I was gone.

I didn’t say anything. I just started turning on lights. Every hallway, every room, every corner I could reach.

My wife noticed almost immediately and asked me what I was doing. I told her I just wanted to see better. She didn’t argue, but I could tell she thought I was overreacting.

Then I started checking reflections.

Windows, picture frames, the oven door, anything that could show me what was behind me.

At first, everything matched.

Then something small didn’t.

A chair in the reflection looked slightly turned.

I stopped and looked at it directly. It was in place.

Back in the window, it was still off.

I moved it myself. Waited. Watched.

It didn’t change right away.

Then, slowly, the reflection corrected itself.

My chest tightened.

I raised my hand.

In the reflection, it was already halfway up.

I hadn’t moved yet.

When I lifted it for real, the reflection followed.

Too slow.

Too late.

That’s when I understood something was wrong in a way I couldn’t ignore anymore.

It wasn’t just appearing.

It needed time.

The longer I looked away, the more things didn’t match when I looked back.

After that, I didn’t let anything stay out of sight for long.

My wife stopped asking questions. She just followed me, watching what I was doing.

I showed her the chair.

For a moment, nothing happened.

Then she grabbed my arm.

“Don’t move,” she said.

I froze.

“Look at the window,” she whispered.

We were both standing still.

But in the reflection, she was already turning.

Slowly.

Toward something behind us.

My chest tightened. I didn’t turn. “What do you see?” I asked.

Her grip tightened. “There’s something there.”

“Don’t look at it.”

“I’m not,” she whispered. “I’m looking at the reflection.”

That’s when I saw it too. Something behind us, not clear, not fully there, but shaped enough to know it didn’t belong. And it wasn’t still.

It shifted.

Not like a person moving, but like something adjusting.

I forced myself not to turn around. If I turned, I had a feeling it wouldn’t be there. But in the reflection, it was getting closer, not walking, just… appearing closer every time we blinked.

“Why isn’t it coming all the way?” my wife asked.

I swallowed. “Because it can’t.”

“How do you know?”

I didn’t answer. But it felt like I knew. It only moved when it wasn’t being watched properly, not directly, not fully. The reflection wasn’t enough to stop it, just enough to slow it down.

“We can’t let it out of sight,” I said.

“For how long?” she asked.

I didn’t have an answer.

We didn’t sleep that night. We kept moving, together this time, making sure every room stayed lit, every corner checked, every reflection watched. The kids must have been exhausted, because they slept through it.

By the time morning came, it was gone again, or at least, we couldn’t see it anymore.

I went home like always, but I didn’t sleep. I kept thinking about how it moved, not toward us, but toward being… something.

The seventh night started before I was ready for it. The moment I walked into the house, I knew something had changed.

The lights were still on. All of them. But the house felt darker, not because of shadows, but because something was already there. Waiting.

My wife was standing in the hallway when I came in. She didn’t say hello. She didn’t ask how I was. She just looked at me and said, “It’s not waiting anymore.”

My stomach dropped. “What do you mean?”

“I saw it,” she said. “Not in a mirror.”

That was enough. “Where are the kids?”

“In their room. I didn’t let them leave.”

I nodded. “Good.”

I didn’t need to go looking for it.

We both stood still in the hallway, looking at the end of it, just against the wall, we could see it.

Not clearly. Not completely. Just enough.

A shape that didn’t belong to anything, standing where nothing could have cast it.

My wife stepped forward.

“No,” I said immediately, grabbing her arm.

“It’s different,” she said.

“It’s not trying to get closer anymore,” she said quietly.

She was right. It wasn’t moving. It seemed like it was stabilizing.

“It’s running out of places to go,” she said.

My grip tightened. “Then we keep watching it.”

“And then what?”

I didn’t answer, because we both knew. If it finished whatever it was trying to become, it wouldn’t need reflections anymore.

She looked at it, then at me. “It doesn’t want this.”

“What are you talking about?” I asked.

“It stops every time we really see it. Not just look. See it.”

The shape flickered. For a moment, it almost looked human, then it slipped again, like it couldn’t hold it.

“It’s going to keep trying,” she said. “And every time we look away, it gets closer to staying like that.”

I shook my head. “Then we don’t look away.”

Her expression changed, not scared, resolved.

“That’s not enough,” she said.

I felt it before she moved. “No,” I said again, but weaker this time.

She stepped forward.

The shape reacted instantly, not moving toward her, but collapsing inward, like it had nowhere else to exist.

“Stop!” I shouted.

She didn’t.

“I’m not letting it keep doing this,” she said. Then, quieter, “It doesn’t want to become one thing.”

My chest tightened.

“And I don’t want it to have a choice anymore.”

She ran and stepped into it.

For a second, everything went completely still, like the house itself was holding its breath.

Then the shape snapped into place.

And disappeared.

She was gone.

Just like that.

No sound. No light. Nothing.

I didn’t move.

I kept staring at the spot where she had been, waiting for something to happen.

Nothing.

The house is quiet now, too quiet. No movement. No reflections lagging behind. No shapes where they shouldn’t be.

I checked that the kids were fine and called the mayor. I told him everything this time. He didn’t interrupt me, didn’t question anything. When I finished, there was a long silence.

“You should have called me... I asked you if you've seen anything...” he said.

“I... You're... right.” I whispered.

Another pause.

Then he hung up.

I’m home now. I haven’t really slept yet.

For the last week, I’ve been standing in the bathroom, staring at the mirror for hours.

Waiting.

Hoping.

I don’t know what she did. I don’t know if it’s gone, or if it’s just… somewhere else.

But sometimes, when I stare long enough, I think I see something behind me, not moving, not shifting, just there.

And once…

just once…

I thought...

if I didn’t look away...

if I kept watching...

then maybe my reflection would become ours,

my love.
