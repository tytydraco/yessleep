I always used to assume kid's shows were a form of harmless background noise. Cheap costumes, bright colors, fake smiles, the kind of background white noise that you don't really know exists until you're in a store and an old theme song plays on a television near the toy aisle and you suddenly remember being five years old.

I never thought I would forget The Fun Hour with Al Gator.

It was 1998, and I was eleven years old, sick at home with what felt like a fever that hollowed out my bones. My mom left for work just after 6 AM, having already set up the blankets on the couch for me. She gave me some cough medicine, a plastic bucket in case I threw up, and then turned on the TV before walking out the door.

The channel had already started showing kids' programs.

I vaguely remember falling in and out of sleep while cartoons flickered across the screen. But around 8 or 9 that morning, the programming changed.

A muddy brown title card flashed across the screen.

“The Fun Hour with Al Gator”

A corny whistle played along to a banjo as a cartoon alligator waddled onto a vibrantly colored set that was decorated like a swamp.

Al Gator himself looked wrong.

Not in a scary way at first, but just…off.

He was dark green, shapeless, and bulky. His eyes, bright yellow, never blinked at the same time. His teeth, much more realistic than the rest of him, were razor sharp and wet-looking as he grinned. He wore suspenders, a floppy straw hat, and looked like some weird country mascot.

I remember him turning his head to stare directly into the camera.

"Good mornin, little critters," he croaked in a thick, southern drawl. "You're lookin' sick today."

I tried to change the channel, but I was too sick to even sit up properly for more than a few minutes at a time. So I laid there and let the show continue, slipping in and out of consciousness.

And then I fell asleep.

And had an awful dream.

In the dream, I was lying on the couch still, only it was dark in my living room and completely silent. No cars driving by outside, no humming appliances, no noises at all.

Al Gator was standing alone on the TV screen on his swamp set.

He slowly turned his head and looked over at me on the couch.

Then he started climbing out of the television.

It wasn't anything like jumping through a hole or flying out. He just sort of stepped over the edge like it was a door.

I remember every tiny detail. The loud thud of his feet hitting my floor. The overwhelming smell of stagnant water and mold that filled the room.

He crouched beside me.

"You shouldn't watch things you don't understand." he whispered.

Then he reached out and grabbed the sides of my head.

I felt the claws sink into my scalp.

And he pulled.

The pain was excruciating. It felt like a wet tearing, a pulling, a pressure, and my spine felt like it was stretching infinitely up toward the ceiling as my body was held to the couch.

Then Al Gator laughed.

It wasn't a cartoonish laugh, but more realistic, almost like a human.

It was deep and wheezing, and it sounded like he was barely able to breathe.

I woke up screaming, and as a result, I threw up on the carpet.

A different show was playing on the TV now.

That should have been the end of it, a nasty nightmare that was amplified by the fever.

But things began to happen afterward.

Sometimes, I would hear a faint banjo theme in the background at night, even when nothing was on.

Other times, I would see flashes of green in reflective surfaces.

But the most unsettling was the voice.

"Hey there, little critter..."

It would be whispered from the dark corners of my room.

A few years later, I asked my friends if they remembered a cartoon called “The Fun Hour with Al Gator”. Not one of them knew what I was talking about. It became even more bizarre when my mom swore up and down that the channel I mentioned didn't show kids programming in the morning, at least not after 1996. She thought I was just delirious from being sick.

I spent all night one time sitting online trying to see if I could find any trace of it. I found one single thread in a very old forum. There were only three posts:

"Did anyone else have nightmares after watching Al Gator?"

"Don't look him up at night."

And one that said, "He comes out of reflective surfaces."

I never forgot that sentence.

That's when I had started to notice glimpses of him in mirrors, a flash of a snouted reflection in the bathroom mirror, an eye watching me from the darkness reflected in my bedroom window, and the hint of a snout over my shoulder that would be gone when I turned around.

I stopped being able to sleep. Every time I closed my eyes, I dreamed he was peeling the top off my head like it was a jar lid.

Then came the hallway incident.

I was nineteen by then and had almost convinced myself I was clinically insane, I got up for water just after two in the morning. The apartment was pitch black except for the glowing blue numbers on the microwave in the kitchen. I took one step into the hallway and heard the familiar banjo theme music coming from behind me. I stopped and slowly turned.

There stood Al Gator at the end of the hallway, but he wasn't just the normal sized cartoon version anymore. He was realistic and enormous. His head scraped the ceiling, water dripped from his scales and stained the carpet beneath him, and his suspenders hung loose from his rotting, real-looking scales. His grin stretched impossibly wide.

"You're bigger now." he rasped at me.

I ran.

I slammed the door to my bathroom and locked it from the inside, hyperventilating so hard that I nearly blacked out. Then came the three distinct, slow knocks on the door.

"Little critter..."

Another knock.

"I wanna get into your head again."

Then, with a horrible, creaking sound, the doorknob began to turn. I backed up against the bathtub, and the lights flickered out.

I don't really remember what happened after that, only fragmented images. The door swinging open. His shadowy form in the doorway. The sickening smell of swamp water. His claws around my skull.

When I finally woke up, I was in the hospital. According to my neighbors, I had passed out in the bathroom of my apartment and caused a water damage situation. The police officers asked me how I'd torn down the bathroom door from the hinges, and they seemed confused by my lack of response, which they readily shared.

But right before they let me go, the doctor asked about the five, deeply grooved marks that covered the sides of my scalp, marks from Al Gator's claws in my nightmare, but looking to him like they'd been made by a human thumb and forefinger, as if five deep bruises could really only be caused by someone's hand.

I moved after that.

But the experience didn't stop there.

Last night, I woke up to wet footprints next to my bed. And this morning, as I was shaving, I noticed him in the mirror behind me. A bright yellow, unblinking eye staring out of the darkness.

At this point, I don't think he's just a hallucination anymore.

I think he's been trying to drag me somewhere for years, ever since that first dream, and lately, I've been waking up with mud under my fingernails. I'm afraid to go to sleep because all I do is dream about his fake swamp, the sound of cheering kids, and Al Gator standing next to a television, waiting for me to climb through.
