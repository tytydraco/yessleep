
Don’t speak back. Learn from my mistake.  

It was the summer of my seventh birthday. I had never seen stars before, not the real stars. All we had in the suburbs were the tiny, weak points of light that peeked through the encroaching glow of the nearby city.  Their light so measly they didn’t garner a second glance.  

So when my best friend invited me to camp, I just thought it would be a fun weekend in the woods. And it started that way. 

Mike was a Baptist. A southern Baptist, to be precise. The kind that eschews communion wine for grape juice, because alcohol is a sin (my protestations that one of Jesus’ first miracles was turning water into wine went overlooked). Even though we were both still a few years away from partaking in that communion wine, we’d have these discussions. 

We did normal youth camp activities- swimming. Volleyball. Games and snacks. It all seemed so normal until the evening session. 

I was fully unprepared for the revival. Set beneath an oversized gazebo, above the gentle buzzing of the cicadas,  the portly youth pastor strode front and center, speaking ten miles a minute, exhorting us to  open our heart and accept Jesus within. It the next Great Awakening, and he fancied himself a modern Jonathan Edwards, lighting a fire that would sweep across the land, saving souls and driving out demons.  

This was a far cry from my dour Lutheranism, where we would quietly and meekly pray to God with little expectation he would notice, much less speak back. Suffer in silence, and give thanks. 

This frenzied group of madmen (and women), taken entirely by the Holy Spirit, sang and danced loudly, stumbling to the front and proclaiming to the crowd and the entire heavenly host that they accept Jesus as their Lord and Savior. Meanwhile I shamefully slouched down into my plastic chair, uncertain. Was it rude, I asked myself, not to come up, to show exuberance? Or was it better to remain truthful. Let your yes be yes, and your no be no. 

He looked at me, the man who was not quite a pastor, sadly, and made one last plea for anyone still untouched to cry out to God, and ask him to open their ears so his voice can be heard. This, I could do. I pled with all my might that God speak to me— for I truly wanted to hear the voice of God and have the Holy Spirit take me over. To speak in tongues and handle snakes (one of these which was happening at this youth conference). 

So when we first heard the voice, I assumed my prayers had been answered. That the voice I heard was God. It wasn’t until much later we realized how wrong that it was. 

Once the sun was fully set, the sky lit up. To me, in these pre internet days, it was like the Fourth of July. Mike and I discussed his experience, what it was like to speak in tongues, as we walked along the path. Then the pine trees opened up to a large meadow, and we fell silent. 

Below this magnificent canopy, I felt, for the first time, what can only be described as an almost Biblical awe. The Milky Way, faintly visible, laid a canvas upon which was painted endless points of light. 

Telescopes were set up, so we could peer into the depths of space. Another youth leader, this one younger and sprightly, had us pair up beside a telescope. He spoke, in a slightly nasally voice, of the wonder of God’s creation as he pointed out various constellations. 

My wonder grew, but Mike eventually started to get bored. He pointed at a dark patch in the sky. ‘What’s there?’ And turned the telescope. I immediately felt like I was back in my seat, doing something wrong. Especially here, where surely God was watching. 

Mike sighed, disappointed. ‘Nothing’.

‘That’s because you looked at an empty spot’!

He adjusted the telescope, and looked at another empty spot. ‘I’m going to discover a new galaxy.’

Just then, the youth pastor started talking about the constellation Sagittarius. 

I pushed him out of the way. After that revival, I just wanted to stay in line. Avoid attention.  I pointed the telescope towards the spout of Sagittarius’ teapot. As I was carefully calibrating, I saw what I first thought was a smudge on the lens. 

When the viewfinder  focused there, I head a deep voice say “en ho logos” vibrating through my entire body. My teeth chattered as my body shook. It said it again, in a voice ancient and gravely. “En ho logos” I whispered with it. 

‘What?” Mike said. 

With great effort, I looked at him. “What do you mean, what?”

“I didn’t say anything.”

“Yes, you said something about a ho.”

“ I was looking for Sagittarius” I explained. 

He said they had moved on from Sagittarius, and I was just standing there staring dumbly at the telescope. He asked me what I saw, and when I replied nothing, he pushed me aside to look. 

When his eye hit the viewfinder, he stiffened. It looked like he stopped breathing. 

‘Mike’ I said. No response. 

‘Mike’ I said louder. Then touched his arm. Everything went dark. 

My head swam, and I heard voices as if from far away. Telling me to breathe deep. I did, and things began to get clearer. I realized my eyes were open, my vision watery. Strange people looking down at me, framed by the night sky. Suddenly everything came into focus, and I sat up. 

“Mike” I exclaimed, looking around. 

He still lay there, catatonic. 

***

It took him much longer to come around. On the walk back, he was silent. Mike was never silent. 

Lying in our bunks that night, I couldn’t sleep. I was sure that I had heard God’s voice speaking to me. What else could it be? My prayers had been answered. I was euphoric. 

Mike’s voice startled me. “En ho logos.” 

“You heard it to!” I exclaimed, excited to share this experience with my best friend. 

‘Yes’ he said. “We have to go back.” 

“We can, tomorrow” I replied. 

“No, tonight.” 

I started to argue, and then realized he was right. I knew it, to the core of my being. The voice- what I thought was God- had more things to tell us.

Things it wanted us to do.
