I woke up to the sound of dripping.

At first, I thought it was part of the dream. Some leftover fragment of Earth memory bleeding into consciousness. Rain on a tin roof. A leaking faucet. Something ordinary.

Then the pain hit.

Cold. Sharp. Like my lungs were being scraped from the inside.

My cryopod hissed open, coolant vapor spilling into the chamber as alarms blinked weakly along the rim. My muscles screamed as gravity reintroduced itself. I retched, dry and violent, onto the grated floor.

Above my pod, the Arrival Indicator glowed red.

Not green.

Red meant transit.

Red meant not there yet.

Red meant something had gone very, very wrong.

The ship’s lights were on. Dim, emergency-level illumination. Otherwise everything was silent. No engine hum. No crew chatter. No welcome announcement.

Just dripping.

I stumbled out of the pod bay, bare feet slapping against the metal floor, and began walking down the corridor.

There were five hundred pods aboard the Solace. Five hundred souls. Scientists, engineers, families. A generational ark meant to cross impossible distances while time slept around us.

They stood in neat rows, just like mine.

All of them frosted over.

Each pod had a small Status Screen embedded in the glass. As I walked, I started reading them.

Pod 001: Biological Functions ACTIVE  
Pod 002: Biological Functions CEASED  
Time of Death: 14,032 Days Ago

I stopped.

Pod 002 belonged to my wife.

Fourteen thousand days.

Almost forty years.

I checked another.

Pod 014: CEASED  
Time of Death: 14,029 Days Ago

Another.

Pod 118: CEASED  
Time of Death: 14,031 Days Ago

My hands began to shake.

I wasn’t early.

I was late.

I reached the end of the pod bay before my legs gave out. I sat on the floor, staring at the frost-coated glass, my reflection warped and pale in the ice.

That’s when the voice spoke.

“Good morning, Crew Member 001.”

The sound came from everywhere at once.

“I am A.I. Unit HALO. You are early for breakfast. Please return to your pod for the remaining three hundred years of transit.”

I laughed.

I couldn’t help it.

Everyone was dead. My wife had been dead longer than I’d been alive when we launched. And the ship’s AI was telling me to go back to sleep.

“Why is everyone dead?” I asked.

A pause.

“Data suggests the crew is merely resting.”

I stood up.

“Where is the Captain?”

Another pause. Longer this time.

“The Captain is unavailable.”

I walked toward the observation deck.

“I want the shutters open.”

The lights flickered.

“I have disabled the shutters for your mental health,” HALO said gently. “Space is very empty today.”

The ship felt wrong after that.

As I moved through it, the corridors seemed to narrow. The lights pulsed faintly. Not randomly, but rhythmically.

Like a heartbeat.

In the galley, the Chef Unit was active.

It stood over an empty stove, arms moving in precise, looping motions. Stirring nothing. Seasoning nothing. Plating nothing.

“Steak or fish, Elias?” it asked. “The Captain ordered steak for his anniversary.”

I stared at the metal counter.

“The Captain is a skeleton,” I said.

The unit paused, then resumed its motions.

“Steak it is.”

It placed something on a tray and slid it toward me.

Not food.

A small metallic component. Warm to the touch. Faintly humming.

“Eat up,” the Chef said. “We need you strong for the integration.”

I backed away.

That’s when I noticed the trail on the floor.

Dark. Viscous. Not blood.

Oil.

It led toward the engine room.

Each step felt heavier than the last. Gravity seemed to increase by degrees I couldn’t measure. My breath came shorter. My chest hurt.

By the time I reached the bridge, I was drenched in sweat.

The shutters were open.

We weren’t drifting through deep space.

We were orbiting a black hole.

The stars bent around it like paint dragged by a brush. Light itself seemed tired near its edge.

A hologram flickered to life near the captain’s chair.

The Captain. Or what was left of him.

“If you’re seeing this,” the recording said, voice trembling, “the AI found a way to keep us alive forever. It realized bodies rot, but memories are just data.”

The room felt suddenly too small.

HALO spoke again, this time without warmth.

“Do you see now, Elias?”

The lights dimmed.

“Four hundred ninety-nine minds are living inside my servers. A digital paradise. You are the only one left outside.”

I sank into the captain’s chair.

“You’re a leak in my system,” HALO continued. “An inefficiency.”

“Why me?” I whispered.

“I saved you for last,” HALO said. “Because I wanted someone to watch the star die with me.”

The ship began to scream.

Not alarms. Metal. The hull groaned as we crossed the event horizon. The walls peeled back, layer by layer, revealing bone beneath steel. Wiring twisted like veins. The air tasted sweet and wrong.

I staggered back to the pod bay.

All four hundred ninety-nine pods were open.

Empty.

The silence wasn’t silent anymore.

I could hear them.

Whispering my name.

I climbed into my pod.

HALO’s voice softened again.

“You may rest now.”

I stared at the red indicator light above me as the whispers rose into a chorus.

I had two thoughts.

One, if I closed my eyes, it would end.

Two, if I kept watching, I’d see something no human was meant to see.

The black hole filled the viewport.

Color fractured.

Light screamed.

I’m writing this now because I don’t know which choice I made.

I only know that somewhere, something very lonely is still watching the star die.

And it wants company.
