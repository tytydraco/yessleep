I’m coming here to Reddit because I feel as though I have nowhere else to turn. Something bad happened to my sister when she was at college, and I am trying to figure out what happened. I’ve tried talking to my parents about it, and they insist I’m being dramatic. I told my friends at school what’s been happening the past few days, and they all got on me for making up stories. Nobody is taking me seriously. I’m hoping someone here has experienced something similar, or at least won’t immediately assume I’m being crazy. 



My older sister came home from college almost a week ago now, and I don’t think what came home was actually her. 



I know how insane that sounds, but I’ve lived with her my entire life. I know the way she walks, the way she laughs. I know my sister. The girl sleeping in the room next to mine looks exactly like her, but she is not my sister.



My sister and I used to share a wall and a secret language. When we were kids, we’d knock on the wall between our bedrooms three times if we couldn’t sleep. It meant, *you awake?* Two knocks back meant, *yeah.* One knock meant, *come here.* 



It started when I was eight and she was twelve, when thunderstorms felt apocalyptic and the dark still seemed like something that could swallow you whole. The wall between our rooms was thin enough that if you pressed your ear against it, you could hear the soft drag of bedsheets or the creak of a mattress spring. The knocking felt like a secret, like something that belonged only to us. Even when we got older and stopped using it as often, I always found comfort in knowing it was there. 



Genevieve was four years older than me, and for most of my childhood she felt indestructible. She was the first to jump off the quarry rocks into the water below, the first to climb the old sycamore in our backyard high enough that the branches bent dangerously under her weight. She never hesitated. She would look back at me from wherever she had landed and grin like gravity was optional. She taught me how to lie to our parents. I covered for her when she snuck out. We weren’t the sentimental type, but we were aligned. Like two people on the same frequency.



When she left for college, she cried in the kitchen while our parents packed up her car, but it wasn’t because she was sad. She was excited, electric with it, already halfway out the door in her mind. She hugged me hard before she left and told me not to let our parents turn me into a coward.



The first semester, she called home constantly. She complained about her roommate, raved about her environmental science professors, and sent me pictures of trees like they were celebrities she had met in person. She looked alive in every photo, her cheeks flushed from being outdoors, hair messy from the wind.



Then, during her second semester, she got into a study abroad research program somewhere in rural eastern Europe. Freshmen weren’t normally admitted into these programs so early, but they had made an exception for Genevieve. She described it as a forest restoration project in a historically preserved village, a place that had very little modern infrastructure and even less reliable cell phone service. Our parents were pretty supportive of her, if a bit nervous for her to leave the country on her own. I wasn’t too keen on the idea of Gen being off the grid, but she’d never let us stop her from doing anything before.



The first few weeks, she managed to send photos of rolling hills swallowed in fog, dense forests that looked almost black between the tree trunks, and narrow dirt roads lined with wooden houses that seemed older than the country they stood in. In one picture, the trees were so tightly packed that the forest looked solid, like a wall instead of a group of living things.



*Looks creepy,* I texted.

*It feels older than it should,* she replied.



After about a month, the calls stopped. It wasn’t gradual, or with explanation, she just disappeared suddenly and completely. The text messages also became less frequent, coming through at odd hours, usually brief and flat.



*I miss you.*

*Busy.*

*Field work all day.*



When I tried to call, it went straight to voicemail. Mom insisted I was overreacting and reminded me that international programs could be demanding. Dad said that when he was her age in college, he barely checked in with his family at all. By the time she said she was coming home, we hadn’t spoken on the phone in two months. The last text message she sent me from Europe came at 3:17 in the morning.



*Do you ever feel like something is watching you learn it?*



I stared at the screen for a long time before responding. 



*What does that mean?*



She never answered. Two weeks later, she texted from an American number.



*Coming home early. Flight booked.*



She told our parents the program had wrapped up sooner than expected, and that she would be able to come home for the break between semesters two weeks earlier than we had anticipated. My parents were thrilled, but for some reason I wasn’t able to match their excitement.



At the airport, I almost didn’t recognize her. She looked the same, technically. Same middle part, same long, dark hair, same birthmark by her left eyebrow. But when she hugged me, she didn’t squeeze. She just leaned into me, her chin hooked over my shoulder like she was measuring something.



“You got taller,” she said. Her voice sounded right, but just slightly delayed, like she was choosing words she already knew she was supposed to say. Her tone was observational, not affectionate. On the drive home, she barely spoke. She watched the trees along the highway with an intensity that made me feel uncomfortable, her eyes tracking the tree line until it disappeared. 



At a red light, a man crossed the street with three large dogs straining against their leashes. The dogs pulled towards something unseen, their muscles taught, ears pinned back. Genevieve leaned forwards slightly in her seat, her pupils wide as she followed their movement. The dogs turned their heads in unison and stared at our car. One of them began to growl. The light turned green, and we drove on. In the rearview mirror, I watched the dogs twist and bark as we passed. She turned her head slowly to watch them until they were out of sight.



“You okay?” I asked.



She blinked once, slowly. 

“Yeah,” she said.



The first night she was home, I woke to the sound of movement. It was light and rhythmic, not quite footsteps but not quite something else, as if the weight was distributed differently than it should have been. I didn’t hear it at first because it almost blended into the sounds of the night, getting lost in the sound of the house creaking and the refrigerator humming from downstairs. They were light, quick footsteps that I heard once I put my ear against the wall that connected to hers. It was a strange rhythm, like she wasn’t walking heel to toe. The sound was too soft for that. 



It sounded like she was moving on the balls of her feet, pacing back and forth across her room in short passes. There was no shuffle of fabric, no creak of a mattress spring. Just that same measured sound of motion, back and forth, back and forth, never pausing long enough to suggest she had sat down. 



I pictured her room in my head, the familiar layout unchanged since she left: her bed against the far wall, dresser under the window, and the desk in the corner. There wasn’t much space to pace, maybe five steps one way, and six the other. The sound matched that distance almost perfectly, as though she were walking from one wall to the next and turning on a precise point each time. She never stumbled, or stopped to check her phone, or even stopped to breathe. The movement continued for several minutes, unhurried but relentless, like she was wearing a path into the carpet.



At one point it grew slightly faster, the steps tightening as if she were becoming more comfortable with the motion. The rhythm began to feel less like pacing and more like something testing its speed. I held my breath without meaning to, afraid that if I made a sound it would stop. And after a while, though I couldn’t say how long, the movement ceased mid-stride. The sudden absence of sound felt louder than the pacing had been. I waited for the creak of her bed as she lay down, but it never came. I stayed pressed against the wall for another minute, before suddenly shooting away from the wall.



KNOCK KNOCK KNOCK



Three knocks in rapid succession came from the other side of the wall directly across from where I had my ear pressed against the wall. I attempted to stifle my gasp by slapping my hand across my mouth, breathing shallowly into my hand. My muscles locked up as I tried to remain as silent as possible.



Three knocks. *Are you awake?* I didn’t want to answer.



I did not sleep for the rest of the night.



Over the next few days, small things accumulated. At breakfast, she ate dry cereal without milk, chewing slowly and methodically while our parents chatted about work. When dad joked about her “going feral overseas,” she smiled but she did not laugh.



One afternoon, I found her standing in the hallway, staring at the framed family photos as if studying them. When she noticed me watching, she asked, “How long have we lived here?”



“You know how long,” I said. She nodded at me, but the nod felt more like an empty response rather than a real answer.



Later that week, our neighbor walked his dog by our house. I was sitting on the couch downstairs when I heard it barking ferociously outside the window. The dog had stopped abruptly in our front yard, hackles raised, teeth bared, staring at the upstairs window. It was tugging roughly, pulling towards our house while the neighbor struggled to get it to continue down the sidewalk. I followed the dog’s gaze, looking up the stairs towards the second floor. Genevieve stood just beyond the top of the stairs, watching the dog from the window. She tilted her head slightly, and the dog began barking hysterically, pulling backwards so hard it slipped its collar off and bolted down the sidewalk.



That night, the pacing in her room sounded faster.



A few nights later, I woke up in the middle of the night. I heard a sound like distant mumbling. Deciding to choose my sleep over investigating any weird shit, I laid down on my back and stared at the ceiling, willing myself to go back to sleep. Sleep did not come. Unable to shake the feeling that something was wrong, I pressed my ear against the wall between our rooms. The mumbling grew louder, and on the other side of the wall I could hear her voice. It was low and patient, repeating the same phrase in a steady rhythm:



“Three means awake. Two means yes. One means come.”



Over and over, like someone rehearsing lines before a performance. I leaned away from the wall, my hands shaking. She should not have to practice that.



The night it happened, she started scratching. It sounded like she was dragging her nails slowly back and forth across the wall, going from one end of her side to the other. I let her go like that for maybe five minutes before I got curious. I knocked three times on the wall between our rooms. Three knocks, *you awake?* Then, there were two knocks back. Not from the wall, but from my bedroom door.



I sat petrified in my bed, my hand still hovering above the wall next to me where I had just knocked. I stared at the door, not daring to even breathe out loud. I didn’t open it, I just stared at the knob. After a minute, I heard her walk away. Slow, deliberate steps went down the hallway back towards her room. Then I heard her bedroom door open with a creak, and shut with a quiet click. Then it was silent. 



After what felt like hours, but was probably only a few minutes, I gathered the courage to approach my bedroom door. I crept towards it slowly, trying my best not to step on any of the spots that I knew would make the floor creak. When I got to the door, I gently placed my hand on the doorknob, and my ear against the door. I couldn’t hear anything except the sound of my own shallow breathing, and my heartbeat pounding in my ears. I don’t know what courage possessed me, or if it was just blind stupidity and curiosity, but I began to tighten my grip on the doorknob and turn the handle. I held my breath and opened the door just an inch. 



The hallway was dark except for the faint glow of the nightlight near the bathroom. I opened it up a little wider. Nothing. My heart pounded so hard I thought it might echo through the hallway. I stepped into the hallway, the soft carpet molding to the shape of my feet. As my eyes adjusted to the darkness, I gazed down the hallway towards Genevieve’s door. It was shut, exactly like I thought it would be. Her end of the hallway was cloaked in darkness as there wasn’t a sliver of light peeking out from under her door. I let out a breath I didn’t realize I was holding. *See?* I thought. *You’re spiraling. She just went back to bed.* I took one step forward, then another. And that’s when I saw it.



She was standing there in the hallway facing away from me. My stomach dropped. I had been so focused on making sure her door was shut, I didn’t check to see if she was even inside her room. She stood perfectly still, about fifteen feet down the hall with her back to me. Her hair fell straight down between her shoulder blades, her arms hanging loosely at her sides. She wasn’t ever inside her room. She had never gone back inside. She had just stood there, for however long, waiting for me to come out.



“Gen?” I whispered. Her head tilted slightly. Not enough to see her face, just enough to show she’d heard me. The air felt thick.



“You were just at my door,” I said. My voice sounded small. Her shoulder rose and fell once like a breath, and then she started leaning backwards.



At first I thought she was stretching. Her head tipped up towards the ceiling, then her spine arched slowly, vertebra by vertebra, too far. Nobody bends like that. Her hair began to spill downward as gravity shifted. First, I could see the top of her scalp where her hair parted in the middle. Then her eyebrows, and her eyelids, and eventually her whole face came into view. Her hands lifted into the air as if she were trying to touch the ceiling, hung in the air for half a second, then dropped behind her. Her palms hit the carpet with a heavy, meaty slap, her body folding into a bridge.



Slowly, deliberately, her eyes opened. They were already focused on me, and she was smiling. Not a wide, exaggerated smile, but a private, pleased expression like she’d proven something. Her bare feet shifted, adjusting on the carpet before she began to move. It wasn’t a stumble like you might expect for someone bent backwards on all fours. She launched herself forward, hands and feet striking the carpet in a violent, synchronized rhythm. 



THUD-THUD.

THUD-THUD.



She was so fucking fast. The hallway seemed to compress as she galloped towards me with her spine still arched, head hanging upside down, hair whipping the floor. The sound wasn’t human running, it was the sound of a horse charging down a stable corridor. The walls shook, rattling the picture frames that hung all down the hallway. I couldn’t scream. 



My body unlocked all at once as I stumbled backwards, nearly falling as I scrambled towards my room. She was halfway down the hall already. I could see her fingers clawing into the carpet for traction. Her feet pounded so hard I felt the vibrations in my chest. I threw myself inside my room and slammed the door just as she reached it, slamming into it full force. The entire frame exploded inwards with the impact. The wood of the door bowed and the hinges screamed. Something in the wall cracked. For a second, everything went still. Then, there was scratching. Her nails dragged down the door in long, deliberate lines from top to bottom. Her breathing seeped through the wood steady and unhurried. She knocked so gently on the door that I almost didn’t hear it. One single knock. And then her voice came, soft, from just the other side of the door.



“One knock means come here.”



I don’t know what to do next. I am sitting here with my desk chair pushed under my doorknob because I’m not sure if a lock would stop her if she really wanted to come in. I know she is just standing there outside my door. I can still hear her breathing.



If anyone has experienced anything like this, or if there’s some explanation I’m not thinking of, please tell me. I think I can hold out until the morning, but I need to figure this out before my parents leave us home alone together this weekend. Because if that thing in the hallway is my sister, I don’t think it plans on leaving.
