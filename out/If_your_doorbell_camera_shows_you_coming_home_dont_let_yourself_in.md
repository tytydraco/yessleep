I used to think doorbell cameras were for paranoid people.

That sounds stupid now. Before all this happened, I was the person who laughed at those neighbourhood app posts where someone shared a blurry clip of a man walking past their bins at 2 a.m. and wrote DOES ANYONE KNOW THIS PERSON???? as if the poor bloke wasn't just taking a shortcut home from the pub.

My sister bought me the camera when I moved into the house.

"It'll make me feel better," she said.

"It'll make you feel better?"

"You live alone now."

That was how people said it after the breakup. Not, *you're single now*, or *you've got your own place*. Always *you live alone now*, like it was a diagnosis.

The house was nothing special. Two-bed terrace at the end of a row, ten minutes from town if you walked fast, twenty if you didn't. Red brick, narrow hallway, tiny garden, old carpets that smelled faintly of someone else's plug-in air freshener. Every noise had an explanation until you were lying awake at 3 a.m. hearing all of them at once.

Pipes ticking. Fridge humming. Floorboards settling. A neighbour coughing through the wall.

Normal things.

I'd been there six weeks when the first notification came.

Thursday night. Still at work because my manager had decided half an hour before closing that the stockroom needed a proper sort out, which meant I got home later than usual and hated everyone. I was in the staff room with a vending machine coffee, waiting for my sister to text back about Saturday, when my phone buzzed.

*Front Door: Person Detected.*

The preview was black apart from the yellow glow of my porch light. I tapped it.

The live feed loaded slowly, the little circle spinning.

Then I saw myself.

Standing outside my front door.

Same black work trousers. Same green coat. Same hair tied up badly because I'd forgotten a bobble and used an elastic band from the office drawer. Same tired face.

Looking directly into the camera.

My brain did a strange little stumble, like missing a step in the dark. I looked around the staff room as if someone might be playing a joke. Nobody there. Just the vending machine, the abandoned mugs by the sink, rain tapping against the high window.

On my phone, the other me lifted her hand and knocked.

Three times.

I heard it through the app.

Knock. Knock. Knock.

Then she leaned closer to the camera and smiled. Not a horror film grin. A small, tired, familiar smile.

My smile.

"I'm home," she said.

I dropped the phone.

It hit the table hard enough to make the coffee jump. I sat there staring at it, face down on the plastic table, while my heart thudded so hard it hurt.

Then I picked it up.

The feed had disconnected. I opened it.

Empty porch. No one there.

I watched for nearly a full minute. Rain moved through the porch light. My front door sat there, closed and ordinary.

I checked the saved clip.

There wasn't one.

That bothered me more than anything at first. The app saved everything. Cats, delivery drivers, my neighbour's teenage son smoking by the wall when he thought nobody was watching. But the clip of me standing at my own front door while I was sitting three miles away in my work uniform did not exist.

I called my sister.

She answered with, "What's happened?"

"I think someone was at my door."

"Are you home?"

"No. Still at work."

"Then don't go home yet."

"I saw myself."

Silence.

"What do you mean?"

"The camera went off and I opened it and I was there. Outside my house."

"That's not funny."

"I know it's not funny."

"Could it be someone who looks like you?"

I wanted to say yes. Someone with the same coat, same hair, same build. A weird coincidence in bad porch lighting.

But she had my face. And my voice.

"I don't know," I said.

My sister came to pick me up. We went to the house together. The street looked normal. Wet cars. Curtains closed. Bins along the kerb. My porch light still buzzing softly. No one waiting outside. No figure in the alley. No version of me standing under the yellow glow.

She made me stay in the car while she walked up.

"Don't," I said.

"I'm just checking the lock."

She tried the handle. Locked. Looked through the letterbox. Nothing. Then crouched and touched the doormat.

"What?" I called.

She didn't answer.

When she came back, her face had changed.

"There's a footprint."

"So?"

"Barefoot."

I laughed because I didn't know what else to do. "What?"

"Wet. On your mat. Like someone stood there without shoes."

I got out despite her telling me not to.

She was right. A single wet footprint on the brown coir mat. Toes, heel, the curved arch of a foot. Small. About my size.

I was wearing boots.

My sister wanted me to stay at hers. I almost did. But standing outside my own house, looking at that locked door, something stubborn kicked in. I was tired, cold, I had work in the morning. I hated the thought of being frightened out of my own home by a glitch or a prank.

So we went in together.

Every light went on. Hallway. Living room. Kitchen. Stairs. Landing. Both bedrooms. Bathroom.

Nothing.

No one under the beds. No back door forced. No broken windows. No muddy footprints inside. My sister checked the loft hatch with a broom handle and nearly brained herself when it shifted half an inch.

"Empty," I said.

She looked at me. "You saw yourself at the door."

"I saw something on a tiny screen in bad light."

"With your voice."

"Maybe the app played an old clip."

"An old clip of you knocking on your own door barefoot?"

I didn't have an answer for that.

She stayed until nearly midnight. We drank tea neither of us wanted and watched some cooking programme with the volume too loud. Before she left she made me promise to call if anything else happened.

After she left I locked the door, put the chain on, checked the back twice, and took a knife from the kitchen drawer.

I felt stupid carrying it upstairs. I felt stupider putting it on the bedside table.

I didn't sleep much.

At 3:22 a.m., my phone buzzed.

*Front Door: Person Detected.*

The phone lay face up beside the knife.

*Front Door: Person Detected.*

*Front Door: Person Detected.*

I picked it up.

The live feed showed my porch. Rain. Door. Mat. Nothing else.

Then the handle moved.

Not much. A twitch downward.

I stopped breathing.

Down. Up. Down. Up.

Someone was trying the door from outside. Slowly. Carefully.

I slid out of bed and crept to the top of the stairs. From there I could see the front door at the bottom of the hall. A dark rectangle with the chain glinting faintly across it.

The handle moved.

This time I saw it with my own eyes.

I called 999.

The operator asked questions. I whispered answers. Address. Name. Was I alone. Was the person still there. Could I get to a room with a lock.

The handle stopped while I was talking.

Then my phone buzzed.

Not the doorbell app.

A text. From my own number.

*Open the door. I forgot my keys.*

I nearly dropped the phone down the stairs.

The operator asked what had happened.

"I got a text."

"From who?"

"Me. From my number."

The letterbox lifted.

A strip of black appeared near the floor. Then fingers slid through. Four fingers, wet and pale, barely moving. Feeling around slowly. Reaching for the chain.

I ran.

Locked myself in the bathroom. Sat in the bath with the knife in my hand and the phone pressed to my ear. The operator told me police were on their way.

From downstairs came the sound of the chain moving.

A soft metallic scrape. Then another.

I had put the chain on properly. I know I had. I remember sliding it across, tugging the door after.

But I heard it fall.

The front door opened. Not a crash, not a kick. Just the small ordinary sound of a door opened by someone who belonged there.

A pause.

Then footsteps in the hallway. Bare feet on floorboards. Slow. Wet.

One step. Then another.

They stopped at the bottom of the stairs.

Then my voice called up from the hall.

"I'm home."

I don't remember much after that.

The police found me in the bath with the knife still in my hand. The front door was closed and locked. The chain was on. No signs of forced entry. No wet footprints. No fingerprints on the letterbox except mine and my sister's from earlier. No messages from my own number.

The 999 call was real. The operator had heard me crying. Had heard me say, *that's my voice.*

But there was no one there.

One officer was kind. The other looked bored. The kind one asked if I had been under a lot of stress.

That was when I knew they were done.

My sister took me back to hers. I didn't argue.

For two days, nothing happened.

I slept on her sofa, badly, but I slept. She worked from the dining table and kept glancing over at me. Her husband tried to be normal. He asked if I wanted toast. He said, gently, that the mind could do strange things under stress.

I almost believed him.

Then, on Saturday morning, while my sister was in the shower and her husband had gone to the shops, my phone buzzed.

*Front Door: Person Detected.*

I opened the app.

My front door. Daylight. Grey morning. Rainwater dripping from the gutter.

A woman stood on the porch.

Me.

Barefoot again. But this time wearing my pyjamas. The blue ones with the bleach mark on the sleeve. The ones I was wearing at that exact moment on my sister's sofa.

She looked into the camera and raised one finger to her lips.

Then she turned and walked away. Not down the path, not out to the street. She walked through the closed front door.

I screamed for my sister.

She came running in wrapped in a towel, hair full of shampoo, and I shoved the phone at her.

By then the porch was empty.

But this time, the clip had saved.

We watched it together. Once. Twice. Five times.

My sister sat down slowly.

"That's you," she said.

"I know."

"She went through the door."

"I know."

She called the police again. Different officers this time. They watched the clip and asked if anyone else had access to the account. No. Could the footage be edited. No. Was I sure I hadn't gone back to the house that morning.

My sister answered that one.

"She's been on my sofa since Thursday night."

They went to check my house. Twenty minutes later one of them called.

"Are you sure you haven't been back there?"

My sister grabbed my wrist.

"No," I said. "Why?"

"Your bed's been slept in."

I closed my eyes.

"And there's food in the kitchen bin. Toast. Coffee in the mug by the sink. The shower floor is wet."

"That wasn't me."

When he spoke again his voice was careful. "We're going to arrange for someone to take a proper look around."

They didn't find anyone.

They found signs someone had been living there, no sign of how they'd got in. Locks intact. Windows sound. The back door was bolted from the inside. The loft was empty.

But my clothes had been moved.

Not stolen. Moved.

My green coat was hanging on the back of the kitchen chair. My boots placed neatly by the front door. My toothbrush was wet. The towel from my post-shower routine was damp, folded over the radiator exactly as I always folded it.

In my bedroom, the framed photo of me and my sister had been turned face down.

That upset her more than anything.

I didn't go back inside. I stood on the pavement while the officers came and went, and neighbours pretended not to watch from behind curtains.

A woman from three doors down came over. Mrs Harlow. I'd spoken to her twice before, once about bins and once about a parcel.

"Are you all right, love?"

I said yes because that is what people say.

She glanced at my house, then at the police car.

"Is it happening again?"

My sister heard that.

"What do you mean, again?"

Mrs Harlow's face tightened. "Nothing. I shouldn't have said anything."

"What happened before?" I asked.

She looked suddenly older.

"The girl before you. She was only there a few months."

"What girl?"

"Tenant. Before it was sold. Quiet thing. Worked nights, I think."

"What happened to her?"

Mrs Harlow looked at my front door.

"She said someone was coming in."

My sister stepped closer. "Who?"

"Herself."

The pavement seemed to tilt.

"She said she kept seeing herself in the windows. On that little camera by the door. I thought she was having a hard time. You never know with people."

"What happened to her?"

"She left."

"When?"

Mrs Harlow swallowed.

"That's the thing. I didn't see her go. One day she was there, next day the curtains were open and the house was empty. Landlord said she'd done a runner. Owed rent."

"What was her name?"

"I can't remember. Emily, maybe. Or Ellie."

Then she touched my arm.

"I'm sure it's nothing to do with you."

She didn't look sure.

That night I stayed at my sister's again. We turned my phone off. Completely off. Not silent. Off.

At 2:41 a.m., my sister's doorbell rang.

The sound went through the whole house.

Her husband said, "What the hell?"

Nobody moved.

It rang again.

My sister's phone buzzed on the bedside table.

She picked it up.

I knew before she turned the screen toward me.

Her front door camera showed me standing outside.

Barefoot. In the rain. Wearing the blue pyjamas. My hair wet and stuck to my face. My hands by my sides.

My sister whispered, "You're here."

On the screen, the other me lifted her head. She looked into the camera. Then past it. Toward the upstairs window.

Toward us.

Her husband got out of bed.

"Don't," I said.

He went to the window and pulled the curtain aside with two fingers.

Empty street. No one at the door. No one on the path.

But on the screen, I was still there. Waiting.

Then the other me smiled.

The phone buzzed.

A message appeared under the feed.

*Front Door: Familiar Face Detected.*

My sister made a small choking sound.

Because where the app usually said *Person Detected* it had written my name. Not my account name. Not my email.

My full name.

Then the speaker crackled.

"She doesn't live there."

No one spoke.

The other me leaned closer to the camera.

"She lives here."

The feed cut out.

All three of us stayed in that bedroom until morning. We didn't sleep. We didn't talk much. At sunrise, her husband went downstairs with a cricket bat from the loft.

The front step was dry. No footprints. No rainwater.

But the clip had saved.

The police watched it. Took statements. One asked if I had any enemies. Another asked if I had ever sleepwalked.

My sister snapped at him.

"She was in the room with me."

He apologised but I could see what he thought. What all of them thought. A woman living alone gets frightened by a glitch. Her sister feeds into it. They lose sleep. The mind fills in blanks. There are always sensible explanations when you aren't the one hearing your own voice at the front door.

By Monday, my sister was exhausted. Her husband was scared but trying not to be. I could feel myself becoming a problem in their house. Not because they said it. They didn't. They were too kind. That made it worse.

I booked a hotel.

My sister hated the idea. "Stay here."

"It came here."

"So you're going somewhere alone?"

"I can't keep doing this to you."

The hotel was a business one near the ring road. Clean, plain, bright lobby, bored receptionist. Third floor room with a heavy door, a chain, and a keycard lock. No letterbox. No back door. No garden. No way to stand outside and knock without being seen by half the building.

I felt safer there.

For about six hours.

At 1:13 a.m., someone knocked at the room door.

Three soft knocks.

I froze.

The TV was on low. Rain blurred the window. The air conditioning hummed.

Three more knocks.

I picked up the hotel phone and called reception.

"There's someone outside room 312."

"One moment." Typing. "Sorry, there shouldn't be anyone up there. Do you want me to send someone?"

"Yes."

"Is the person still there?"

I looked at the door.

A shadow beneath it. Two bare feet. Toes touching the carpet outside.

"Yes," I whispered.

"Lock the door, please."

"It is locked."

"And the chain."

"I have it."

"Security is coming."

The feet shifted.

Then my phone buzzed.

I had turned it back on earlier because I needed to call the police, my sister, the hotel. I thought it didn't matter anymore. I thought whatever this was had moved past the app.

The notification was not from my doorbell camera.

It was from the hotel app.

I had not downloaded the hotel app.

*Room 312: Keycard Used.*

The little green light on the door flashed.

The handle dipped.

The chain caught.

The door opened two inches and stopped.

Through the gap, I saw one eye.

My eye.

Looking in.

The mouth below it smiled.

"You're in my room," she whispered.

I screamed.

The door slammed shut. Not from her side. From mine. As if someone inside the room had shoved it closed.

The bathroom light flickered on.

I turned slowly.

The bathroom door was open. It had been closed before. I always close bathroom doors in hotel rooms because they make the room feel less empty.

I couldn't move.

Security found me standing in the middle of the room, not screaming anymore, just staring at the bathroom doorway.

There was no one in there.

They gave me another room. Then, after I refused to stay in it, coffee in the lobby while I waited for my sister.

That was yesterday.

I'm writing this from her kitchen table now.

My sister is asleep on the sofa because she won't leave me alone. Her husband is upstairs. The police have my phone. They have the camera clips, the hotel lock records, the 999 recording. They have everything except an explanation.

I borrowed my sister's laptop to write this down.

About twenty minutes ago it pinged.

An email notification. From me. Sent from my own address.

Subject: I'm sorry.

No message. Just an attachment. A video file.

I should not have opened it.

The video was from my front door camera. Tonight. My house, my porch light on, my front door opening.

I stepped out.

Not the other one. Me. I could tell by then. I don't know how to explain it except that I had watched her long enough to see the difference. She stands too still. She smiles before she needs to. Her eyes copy her face half a second late.

This was me. I came out barefoot, wearing my green coat over my pyjamas. I was crying.

Behind me, in the hallway, another figure stood in the dark.

Also me.

She raised one hand.

I walked down the path and out of frame. The door closed.

The clip ended.

I don't remember going there. I don't remember leaving my sister's house. My feet are clean. My coat is upstairs in her spare room.

But the video is real.

I can hear my sister breathing on the sofa. I can hear the fridge. Cars going past on the wet road.

And underneath all of that, something else.

A key turning in the front door.

Slowly. Carefully.

My sister's front door doesn't open with a key from the outside when the deadbolt is on.

But I can hear it turning.

The laptop just pinged again.

Not an email.

A notification from my doorbell app. The app that is on my phone. The phone the police have.

*Front Door: Person Detected.*

I clicked it.

It's showing my sister's front door. Not mine. Hers.

The camera angle is wrong. Too low. Like the feed is coming from something crouched on the path.

There is a woman standing outside.

Barefoot. Wearing my green coat. Holding my sister's spare key. Looking into the camera.

She is not smiling.

She looks tired. Wet. Frightened.

She looks exactly like me.

And she is whispering the same thing over and over.

*Don't let her in.*

There is a knock at the door now.

From inside the house.

Three soft knocks.

Coming from the cupboard under the stairs.

My sister just woke up.

She asked me what that sound was.

I don't know what to tell her.

I don't know which one of us is outside.

I don't know which one of us came back.

I don't know which one of us is writing this.

But if your doorbell camera ever shows you coming home when you know you are somewhere else, don't go back. Don't call out. Don't open the app again.

And if you hear your own voice on the other side of the door, begging to be let in, make it prove it's you.

Because I think I failed that part.

I think I let the wrong one in.

And now I can hear my sister unlocking the cupboard.
