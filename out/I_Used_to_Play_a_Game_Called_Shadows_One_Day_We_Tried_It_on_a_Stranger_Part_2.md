I found something after the Tulsa work photo.

I want it to be clear that I didn’t spiral, I didn’t pace, I didn’t do any of the things you’d expect. I just sat with my laptop open, the photo on the screen, and my cat on the couch next to me, and I looked at it for a long time. The man was half-obscured by my coworkers, standing still while everyone around him moved. Three years ago, in a city I don’t live in, on a day I drove alone and drove home alone, and thought about for approximately zero minutes after it happened because it was just a work party and nothing fucking significant occurs at work parties.

I thought about what it could mean that he was there.

I thought about what else he might have been there for.

Then I closed the laptop and went to bed and stared at the ceiling until my alarm went off, and that was that.

Here is what I told myself in the days after:

There is a reasonable explanation. There are people who look similar, especially with how camera angles distort. Memory is not reliable, and I am not reliable, and Renata saying *around* meant nothing; she is not observant, she once drove past her own street three times in a row because she was talking, and I have watched her do this personally.

I held onto that for a while.

Then I found the one from St. Vincent’s.

I need to explain something about my mother before I explain this.

Three years ago, she was diagnosed with a mass in her left lung. The word they used in the initial consultation was *aggressive*. The word they used at the follow-up was *uncertain*. For about six weeks, from the first scan to the biopsy results, we lived in a space that had no floor. You know the feeling, I'm sure most people know the feeling at some point. You walk around doing the things you do: you go to work, you eat, you answer texts, and underneath all of it is just nothing, just open air, and you are constantly aware that you are one phone call away from the ground disappearing completely.

She’s fine now. I want to say that because otherwise this becomes a different story entirely. The biopsy came back, the mass was reclassified, there was a treatment plan, and she is fine. She gardens. She called me last week to tell me about a bird that keeps coming to her feeder, and I listened to her describe this bird for over eleven minutes, and I was grateful for every single one of them.

But there was a night in the middle of those six weeks where I didn’t know that yet.

There was a night where she had a complication—it was nothing related to the mass, some unrelated infection, but at the time we didn’t know it was unrelated—and she was admitted to St. Vincent’s at eleven pm on a Wednesday night, and I drove forty minutes in the dark, and I sat in the waiting room until two in the morning and then I went outside because I couldn’t sit in there anymore.

I sat on the curb in the parking lot of St. Vincent’s Medical Center at two in the morning on a Wednesday in October, and I cried. The broken kind, the kind that happens when your body decides it’s done holding anything in and just does what it needs to do. I sat there for maybe twenty minutes. A nurse walked past, and a car left the lot. Nobody paid any attention to me because people cry in hospital parking lots all the time; that’s just what happens there.

I thought it was one of the most private moments of my life.

I thought it *belonged to me*.

My cousin Deja posted a photo from my mother’s birthday two years ago. Not the birthday at St. Vincent’s, but the birthday the following year, after everything had resolved—the celebration. Big family gathering in the backyard, an afternoon that feels important *while* it’s happening. I’d seen the photo before. I’d been tagged in it, and I’d liked it at the time and moved on.

I looked at it differently now.

He wasn’t in it.

I almost felt relief. I almost let myself breathe.

Then I looked at the shadow.

In the lower left corner of the photo, partially cut off by the frame, there is a shadow on the grass; it's long and belongs to someone standing just outside the shot. The angle of the afternoon light puts whoever casts it directly behind me.

It’s a shadow. It could be anyone. It could be an uncle I don’t remember standing there, it could be someone who wandered into frame and out again. I know that. I do.

I also know the length of it, the proportions, the asymmetry in the shoulder line that I have looked at enough times now that I know it the way I know my own handwriting.

I sat with that for hours.

Then I went looking for St. Vincent’s.

I didn’t expect to find anything.

That’s the truth. I went looking because I needed to eliminate it, because my brain had started going there on its own, and I needed to close the door on *it* specifically. Nobody photographs hospital parking lots at two in the morning; that's just weird. Nobody in their right mind photographs someone in that state, that private, that *without-an-audience* state. I was going to look, find nothing, and that absence of evidence was going to give me something to hold onto.

I searched through everything. My phone, my cloud backup, tagged photos from anyone who might have been at the hospital that night—my aunt, my cousin Marcus, my sister, anyone. I searched for two hours and found nothing, and I was starting to feel something loosen up in my chest.

Then I found my sister’s Instagram story archive.

She posts everything; she always has. She’d been at St. Vincent’s that night too, sitting in the waiting room while I was outside, and she’d posted one of those nobody-actually-watches-these waiting room stories. Fluorescent light with the vending machine in the background. Her coffee cup in hand with a caption that said: "Please pray." Fourteen views, because it was two in the morning and nobody was awake.

I was view fifteen.

The story was nine seconds long. The last three or so seconds, she panned slightly, just holding the phone up out of habit, and the frame moved past the waiting room window.

Outside the window: the parking lot.

Outside the window: the curb where I was sitting.

Outside the window: me, with my head down, in the October dark.

And directly behind me—

I’ve been trying to figure out what I feel about it, and I don’t have a clean answer.

The fear is there, I mean, obviously, the fear is there. But underneath the fear is something... dreadful?

He was there that night.

He was standing behind me while I sat on that curb at two in the morning, thinking I was going to lose my mother. He was an inch from my back while I cried in a parking lot in October and thought that nobody could see me.

He saw me.

He has always seen me.

I keep thinking about the game. I keep running back to what it means to be good at Shadows—the attention of it, the way you have to learn how someone moves before you can follow them without being caught. I was good at it because I paid attention. I was good at it because I cared about reading the person in front of me, the way their shoulders moved, the shift of weight before a turn; it's all learned behavior from watching someone.

I wonder how long he’s been paying attention to me.

I wonder what he knows about the way I move through the world.

I wonder if he was there before the coffee shop, before Tulsa, or even before St. Vincent’s. I wonder how far back it goes, and I have not let myself go looking again because I am not sure I want to know the answer, and I am not sure I don’t.

My cat has started sitting differently.

I don’t know how else to say it. She has always been a particular cat; she has preferred spots, specific windows, and a rotation I could predict. In the last two weeks, she has abandoned the rotation. She sits facing the front door now. Not really alertly or anything, not the way cats sit when they hear something. Just facing it, sitting still.

She did it again this morning.

I watched her for a while, and she didn’t move.

I told myself she’s fine. I told myself cats do inexplicable things, and this is one of them, and it means nothing.

I closed the curtains.

I checked the locks.

I am writing this from the same living room I was sitting in when I found the Tulsa photo and the St. Vincent’s story and the shadow on the grass at my mother’s birthday party, and I am aware that I am running out of things to check, and I am aware that not finding something has stopped feeling like relief.

I went back to the St. Vincent’s story four times today.

In the last frame, before my sister’s phone swings back toward the vending machine—

I am on the curb with my head down.

He is directly behind me.

And his head is up, looking right at the camera.
