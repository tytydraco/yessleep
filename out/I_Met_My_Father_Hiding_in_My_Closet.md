As a child I was scared of the dark—now I’m terrified of it.

There was always something ominous about how the fading sun dragged the light through the windows. Shadows morphing together, so you could no longer make out who or what cast them.

In my room, the small plastic star emitted a soothing light. I’d be safe, as long as the nightlight kept glowing.

This is the story of how I met my father—hiding in my closet.



During my childhood, my mom and I never had a place we could call home.

*“Kyle, we need to go,”* she said. *“Pack your things.”*

I did, as always—clothes, games, everything. I mostly kept my frustration to myself, but when I didn’t, she would start giving strange excuses to why we had to move so urgently.

*“The neighbors are conspiring,”* or *“Your father found a new job.”*

I didn’t have a father.

This time, I remember clearly, shortly after my tenth birthday—we moved again, from a big city apartment to a rural village house.

In her mind, there was no time to waste. Like buildings mark fire exits—mom always knew how we could leave, keeping an eye on available rentals.

We left within days, lived in a motel for about two weeks, and moved into our next abode without ever having a walkthrough. It was decrepit, awfully so. It looked like a one-star accommodation—the kind that could ruin an entire trip.

Knowing we wouldn’t stay long gave me the courage to step inside. Furniture sat on a rustic oak floor—pieces that might’ve once been expensive, if not for the chips and cracks. A wooden cross mounted on the wall, something reminding me of wasted Sundays. Beside it hung a painting of a meadow, flakes of paint beginning to peel.

My mom walked right past me and started fumbling around with all the unfamiliar keys, trying to lock the cellar door. I didn’t pay it any further attention. She had her priorities and I had mine.

So, I set out to the first floor to look for a room to claim. Calling the place a ruin would be an exaggeration, but back then it certainly felt that way.

The room I chose came with a view onto the street. An old, unsightly closet already sat on the blue carpet, nothing that stickers and posters couldn’t fix. 

It took me a while to clean things up and carry in my luggage. I set up my air mattress, console and nightlight, while my mom was away getting us something to eat. And just like that, the day had almost passed.

Moving places was intimidating, no matter how often I had gone through the process. The first few nights in an unfamiliar house or apartment were the worst. So, my mom gave me the nightlight to ease those fears.

It has always been just the two of us. As for my father—he was absent from the day I made my first memory. It wasn’t a topic my mom liked me to bring up and I didn’t really have a reason to.

She never told me what kind of man he was, but she did mention him once as she put a padlock onto the cellar door. She said to me, *“Don’t ever go down there. Your father’s work is not meant for you to see.”*

Whenever we moved, securing the cellar shut was her first priority. It was irritating, but I knew from experience; asking about it would only ruin my mom’s entire day. Instead of wracking my brain about it, I focused on the digital world on my TV screen and the easy glow of the nightlight.

As the sun left for tomorrow, the electronic luminescence kept the dark away.

I laid on my mattress, illuminated by the starry light, marking my new home with the first night of sleep.

*“Kyle.”*  Faint, breathless. *“Wake up, Kyle.”*

My eyes slowly adjusted to the pitch-black swallowing my room. I tried to keep still, but every breath caused the air mattress to creak and rustle.

A hand twitched like static noise to nudge the closet door open. Bottomless eyes peeked from between my shirts. Someone lurked in there and he knew my name.

*“There was a blackout,”* he continued.

*“Who are you?”* I asked him.

*“I am the Shepherd. You are my little plastic ground sheep,”* he spoke softly, before retreating deep into the closet. *“Follow me.”*

I acted faster than my brain could muster, jumping from my bed and sprinting to the light switch. I pressed it in desperation—to no avail. He mentioned a blackout, of course it wouldn’t work.

I made sure to keep my distance from the closet while I left my room. The hallways that felt alien during the day became malignant in darkness. Letting the weathered tapestries guide my hand, I reached my mom’s bedroom.

My fingers pressed down on the cold door handle. I pushed the door open, carefully, so as not to alert anything to my presence. My mom wasn’t in her bed. Reluctantly, I went downstairs to the ground floor, hoping to find her somewhere around.

There it was—the cellar door, now wide open, with the faint flicker of candles inviting me down. I summoned my courage, grabbed the handle, and pulled it shut.

I continued forward, cautious not to trip over the faulty kitchen tiles and stuffed moving boxes. A narrow line of light squeezed in through the gap beneath the storage door. I got closer and it opened for me.

Filled with a sense of inevitability, I took a careful first step with my trembling foot. Flickering candlelight and molten wax running down the sides drew the path for me.

*“Why do we bury those we love, pull them further from heaven and raise a wall of dirt in between? Do we condemn them to the other place?”*

I reached the bottom, a cave-like room with old beams keeping the dirt from pouring in. Candles, arranged in a line, led me to a hole in the ground with a coarse wooden cross beyond it.

I inched closer, enough to peer inside, where finally, I found my mom.

*“Mom?”* I called out. *“Mom!”*

*“Wherever she is, there is either fire or the sky. There is light regardless. Don’t be scared, my child, my plastic ground sheep, for I’ll guide you to a meadow with infinite grass to feed on.”*

Forgive me, Mom, but I ran back upstairs, out the front door, exiting into the moonless night that would forever haunt my life with its deep, devouring darkness.

He let me leave, with the silent promise he’d return. The news never reported a pitch-black night, or houses with ritual graves in their cellar, making my story sound even less believable. As such, my mom’s disappearance and death was ruled unsolved.

I’m terrified of the dark—because of what comes after.
