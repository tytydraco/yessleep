It’s been a long, tough winter here in New York.

So when the weather finally broke, I didn’t waste any time. I got out to do some shed hunting and see if I could spot early signs of turkeys.

A state park borders my property where hunting is legal with a permit. I’ve had a valid permit for years. I wasn’t trespassing. I wasn’t pushing boundaries. I wasn’t doing anything out of the ordinary.

I know this place like the back of my hand.

It’s a maze of deep ravines and steep ridges, blanketed in old-growth forest. The land was never commercially logged—not because anyone wanted to preserve it, but because it wasn’t worth the risk. The terrain is too uneven, too rugged, too dangerous to haul timber through.

Trees grow however they please out there—twisted, leaning, tangled together as if they’re holding each other up.

Sound behaves strangely in those ravines. It doesn’t carry the way you expect. Sometimes it seems to fade almost immediately, like it gets swallowed by the ground.

I started my walk well before sunrise. A full moon and cloudless ski illuminate my walk

everything felt flat and unfinished. Frost clung to the shaded ground, and my breath fogged in front of me with every step.

I followed an old game trail down into one of the deeper cuts in the terrain, planning to loop back toward a familiar roosting area. I’d walked that route dozens of times over the years.

That’s why I noticed almost immediately when something felt… different.

Not wrong.

Not threatening.

Just unfamiliar.

The trail was still there, but it looked disturbed in subtle ways. A few branches had been snapped clean instead of left to rot. Several stones had shifted into a loose line along the slope, as if something large had passed through and nudged them aside.

Nothing that clearly pointed to human activity.

Nothing that fully made sense, either.

I told myself it was probably runoff from melting snow. Maybe another hunter had passed through in the last season.

Still I slowed down.

The deeper I went the heavier the woods seemed to feel.

The usual background noise the rustle of squirrels, the chatter of birds was gone.

Not faded.

Not distant.

Gone.

Even my own footsteps sounded wrong, muffled by the ravine walls.

Just my breathing.

At the bottom of the ravine I came across a small clearing.

Maybe fifteen feet across.

No fire pit.

No trash.

No clear footprints.

Just bare earth.

Not the kind that happens naturally. The leaves had been pushed back to expose dark soil and the surrounding rocks had been shifted into a rough, uneven oval.

It didn’t look random.

It looked repeated.

Like something heavy had rested there more than once.

I stood at the edge longer than I meant to listening, though I couldn’t have said what I expected to hear.

I couldn’t explain why I didn’t step any closer.

There was nothing visibly dangerous about it.

Still, something in me resisted the idea of entering that space.

So I didn’t.

I gave it a wide berth and continued on.

Eventually I reached a large oak overlooking a natural funnel between two ridges and decided to set up there. If birds were moving, they’d pass through sooner or later.

I settled in.

Let the woods calm down around me.

The wind thinned out. Even the leaves seemed to stop rubbing against each other.

Then I made my first soft tree yelp.

It carried farther than it should have.

Nothing answered.

I waited a full minute.

Then I gave a few sharp clucks.

Nothing.

No shock gobble.

No distant response.

Not even a curious crow call.

The woods didn’t just go quiet.

They felt emptied.

Like something had pressed a hand over the forests mouth.

After about twenty minutes, I stood up slowly and began easing along the ridge, scanning for scratchings, droppings, dust bowls anything.

That’s when I found the  feathers.

A pile of them.

Not neatly plucked like a bobcat’s work.

Not scattered like a coyote kill.

Shredded.

Violently.

Stems snapped in half Quills crushed flat. Some bent backward like they’d been chewed and spit out.

Like whatever had did this got spoked and ran off.

The ground around it wasn’t right either.

No clean tracks.

Just churned soil.

As if something had paced in tight circles.

I crouched to look closer.

That’s when I noticed the smell.

Not rot.

Not blood.

Wet earth… and something sour underneath it.

Like breath.

Close.

Too close.

Behind me, somewhere deeper in the trees, something gave a single soft cluck.

Not a turkey.

Too slow.

Too deliberate.

Something pretending to be a turkey.

And it came from right where I had been sitting.

I ran.

I crashed through brush, over fallen limbs, across a field I don’t remember entering. My lungs burned. My heartbeat drowned out everything else.

I never saw anything.

Not a shape.

Not a movement.

Not even a shadow that didn’t belong.

That might be the worst part.

Because I knew I was being watched.

The sun was climbing over the ridge, throwing long, warped shadows out of the gorge. Everything looked stretched. Bent.

Wrong.

Something whispered behind me.

Not words.

Not wind.

Sounded like someone taking their last breath.

I looked back.

Nothing.

But the air felt heavy.

Close.

Smelling faintly of iron and something worse.

Something that didn’t belong to any animal I know.

I ran faster.

And I realized, with a sinking certainty, that whatever followed me didn’t need to be seen to know exactly where I was.

When I finally reached my house, I slammed the door and locked it.

I don’t know what to do.

I can’t call the police.

I don’t even know what I’d say.

It made a noise.

It felt wrong.

I know I have to do something?

I’ve been through that park hundreds of times. Nothing in there has ever scared me like this.

I usually stick to the east side.

It’s quieter over here. And I don’t have to drive anywhere. I just walk out my front door, cross the back edge of my property, and I’m in it.

One narrow main road.

A few rental cabins scattered through the trees.

Some hiking trails with dirt parking lots that never seem to fill up.

It doesn’t get much tourist traffic.

Locals don’t really talk about this side of the park.

Not in any official way.

They just… don’t go there.

Most people head west, toward the visitor center.

There’s a big old lodge over there, the kind they put on postcards.

I’d love to tell you what it looks like inside, but I’ve never bothered to go.

Something about that side of the park feels… staged.

Like it’s meant to distract you

The real beauty is on the east side.

It’s protected, in a way, by the deep gorge that cuts through the forest.

The waterfalls are massive. The tallest one drops a hundred and seven feet straight down into black water. The roar never really stops you hear it long before you ever see it.

That same gorge keeps boaters and swimmers out.

It also makes the bodies hard to find.

Not every year. But in the ten years I’ve been hunting here, five people have died.

Slips.

Falls.

Accidents.

That’s what they say, anyway.

Most folks blame bad footing. Slick rocks. Poor visibility.

Maybe they’re right.

But a couple of the old timers mentioned something once after a few beers.

They said most of the bad ones happen right around the same time of day.

When the sun slips behind the edge of the gorge.

When the light drops fast.

When the shadows climb out of places they shouldn’t.

I always thought those story where bullshit but after this morning I might be a believer

My coworker that grew up here and introduced me to this place was already old when I met him—more than twelve years ago now.

His family had lived on this land long before it became a park. Long before the word “conservation” meant anything good to people. His great-grandfather was born on a stretch of land that was once a reservation, now absorbed into the park boundaries.

Then the state took it.

They called it preservation. Protection. A gift to the public.

He called it theft.

He didn’t have much family left. His spouse had passed years earlier. I only met his children once, at his funeral. From the way he spoke, I got the sense he hadn’t been a very attentive parent. Work always came first. Silence always came second.

So instead, he talked to me.

He shared the stories that had been passed down through generations. One of those stories is half-buried on a state historical sign, bolted near an enormous old tree that sits in a large field, just across the street from my house.

The tree is impossible to miss.

Its branches don’t spread outward like they should.

They curve upward.

Twisting. Reaching.

Like fingers.

Massive fingers, thick as telephone poles, covered in knots and dark scars that never seem to heal.

Locals call it the Torture Tree.

And when you stand beneath it, staring up at those twisted limbs… you can almost feel the weight of every story it has witnessed

The plaque says, in careful, polite language, that during the frontier period the tree was used as a gathering point.

That’s it.

That’s all the state is willing to say.

My coworker told me the real story.

Back when the land was still contested, when settlers and soldiers pushed deeper into territory that wasn’t theirs, people were sometimes brought to that tree alive.

Not for punishment.

Not for ransom.

They were tied there.

Left standing.

Not allowed to sit.

Not allowed to lie down.

Sometimes for days.

Sometimes longer.

The purpose wasn’t information.

It was offering.

He said some believed suffering carried weight. That pain could be shaped. That if enough of it was given in one place, something beneath the ground could be kept quiet.

I asked him what that something was.

He shook his head.

“Doesn’t matter,” he said. “Names make you think you understand. You don’t.”

Then he told me something I still can’t explain.

He said the tree didn’t grow that way on its own.

It started bending upward after the first offering.

And it’s still bending.

Slow.

Steady.

Like it’s listening.

Waiting.

I laughed when he told me.

He didn’t laugh back.

He then began to tell me another story about the tree.

During the colonial period, a small group of scouts was sent into the area on orders connected to George Washington. Their job was to locate nearby villages so the main force could destroy them.

They never made it back.

According to the version passed down through my coworker’s family, the scouts were captured before they ever located a village.

At first, the men believed they might be traded.

That hope faded when they were marched to the tree.

Not all at once.

One by one.

He wouldn’t go into detail.

He said the point was never to kill quickly.

The point was to take things from a person.

Little by little.

To reduce them.

To leave them aware.

Sometimes pieces of clothing were removed.

Sometimes pieces of the person.

Always while they were still alive.

“Bound to the tree in a way that made escape impossible. Forced to move until they couldn’t anymore.”

Forced to move.

Slowly.

Around and around the trunk.

Until they couldn’t anymore.

He told me the ground around the Torture Tree was once so dark it never fully froze in winter like the heat from the victims kept the ground alive.

I asked him how many scouts died that way.

He shrugged.

“Doesn’t matter,” he said. “They weren’t the last.”

I asked who ordered it. Who decided it should be done.

He shook his head again.

“No one decided,” he said. “They just remembered what worked.”

I didn’t ask what he meant by worked.

Some answers feel heavier than ignorance.

I wish I had asked him more questions.

He taught me a lot about the outdoors. Not just how to move through it, or how to survive in it, but how to respect it. He used to say the woods weren’t kind. They weren’t cruel either. They were something older than both.

He treated me like a grandson.

He’d tell me to listen before I ever spoke. To look before I ever stepped. To understand that once you’re deep enough out there, no one is coming to save you.

“The wild doesn’t care if you’re good,” he’d say.

“It only cares if you’re prepared.”

He talked about the outdoors the way some people talk about a living thing. Not loving. Not hating. Just existing. He said that if you wanted to belong out there, you had to learn its rules. And sometimes, you had to become a little like it.

I didn’t understand what he meant back then.

Now, I think I do.

when I’m alone out there, I feel the weight of it all—the rules, the stories, the silence. And I remember his warning: the wild doesn’t care.

It only cares if you’re prepared.

My father didn’t teach me much—he expected me to already know. He isn’t a bad man.

He was hard. He ran his own company, and in his mind, I was always an employee first and a son second. If there was work to be done, I was expected to already be doing it. If we went hunting in the morning, my job was to have his clothes laid out. Boots lined up. Jacket ready. Rifle case by the door.

If something wasn’t perfect, he’d raise his voice.

Not screaming. Not raging.

Just… disappointed.

It used to make me angry. It used to make me feel small.

But now, I think I understand.

He was preparing me.

He never explained what for.

He wanted me to know something before he ever taught me. To struggle with it first. To feel how heavy ignorance could be.

We didn’t talk much, my father and I.

But sometimes, I’d catch him watching me when he thought I wasn’t looking. His jaw tight. His eyes tired. Like he was measuring something he didn’t like the answer to.

Lately, I’ve started noticing something else in those memories.

Regret.

Not the kind that begs for forgiveness.

The kind that comes from knowing you’ve followed a path too far to turn back…

and realizing your son is walking right behind you.

Things have been better between us lately.

We went through a rough patch after I told my father I’d be inheriting my coworker’s farm—the one that borders the state park. I don’t think he fully understood it. A man who wasn’t my blood, handing me a piece of his life. It seemed to bother him in a way he never quite put into words.

My coworker’s kids weren’t upset. Quiet, mostly. I always had the sense that their memories of the place were too painful to revisit. The land held stories they weren’t ready to face stories older than any of us, woven into the fields and the woods, lingering in corners of the house that refused to let go. They were relieved to let it go to someone who might give it a chance to heal.

It is a well-kept farmhouse. Not fancy, but cared for. You could feel that about a place sometimes the way the floors don’t creak in complaint, the way doors close softly instead of slamming. The first time I stepped inside, I knew someone had loved it.

And yet, standing in the doorway, I couldn’t help but sense that the land itself remembered more than anyone living, that its stories were still waiting for someone willing or unlucky enough to listen.

It’s mid-afternoon now, and I know I don’t have much longer before it gets dark in the gorge.

My coworker’s  passed before I could see him one last time. I can’t shake the feeling that he was going to tell me something—something important—before he died. I never got the chance.

I’m still in shock that he left me the house and a few acres—no neighbors for miles, just trees and cornfields… and that Torture Tree.

Inside the house, I found a a lot of drawing he painted on the side usually early fronter style painting but I found this unfinished, one and I think… maybe he was trying to tell me what to do. How to heal the land. Or at least, how to try.

The unfinished drawing lay on his desk. It was the Torture Tree, its twisted branches clawing toward the sky through licking flames. The tree seemed alive, writhing in pain—or perhaps relief—finally letting go of its agonizing memories.

I was on my porch just before posting this, watching the tree line thinking about the drawing. The sun had passed noon, and I knew the shadows were already creeping into the bottom of the gorge. That’s when I heard it—

A sound these woods shouldn’t make anymore.

I swear it was an elk bugle.

Long. Hollow. Echoing.

Not close—but not far enough.

Elk haven’t lived here in nearly two hundred years.

If something was kept quiet here once, it isn’t anymore.”
