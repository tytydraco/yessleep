I used to believe that there was a menacing figure that lurked outside my window as a kid. It would only be there in the summers, when I was staying in the family’s duct taped together trailer, deep in the woods of Wisconsin. I never feared its presence in the heart of Chicago, when the winters were harsh and grueling, but there in the woods, when the summers were hot and sticky, I’d watch the windows. When a storm would come and the patter of the rain would boom against the roof threatening to come down on me, I’d see a figure in the distance. Just past the campground’s swaying wire fence separating the deep woods from us, it’d stand there. Cloaked in leaves and mud, dirt running down its face, it’d stare back. 

At twenty-one years of age, I’d decide to move into that little trailer to spend my summer away from college. During the days I’d work at the campground, fixing up the rundown areas torn up by age, before coming back to the trailer and doing the same thing, except always with a beer in hand. When I’d stand on the roof of the trailer as it creaked beneath me, sweeping the branches and leaves that have fallen on it from previous storms, I’d look out into the woods. There in the far distance, it would linger. Frowning as I brought the beer to my lips. As the long hot days of summer went on, it never left. Just watching. I’d toast it with whatever drink I had that day and it’d raise a twisted arm back to me. As if telling me to enjoy myself. 

	

On the days where I came home after a long night turned to morning, it would stand at the window for me. Crying while I screamed about the way my life was turning out. I’d slam the blinds shut and it would move to the next window. Then the next, until all I could hear was its whimpers outside my thin trailer walls. 

	

Towards the end of the summer, as my drinking got worse, it grew closer. Never leaving the window, as though it were lying in wait, until I stumbled out of my car one night. Shutting the lights off and killing the ignition. I tripped walking in the door slamming my head onto the dirtied floor and instead of entering fully, I just laid there. Half in- half out. 

	

“It’s time. You know that.”

	

The voice came from behind me but I couldn’t bring myself to turn, “No-” 

	

“How miserable must you get?” It asked, voice coming in short raspy breaths like a smoker at the end of his rope. 

	

“I don’t want to stop, but I also don’t want to live like this,” I told it, attempting to suck my legs in from the outside. 

	

“The time is here. You’re miserable enough.” 

	

Softly with a whimper, as my knees smacked into the frame, “What if I’m not though? What if I just get worse?” 

	

“You have nothing left to lose by trying,” It snapped out harshly. 

	

	

I didn’t respond, as I managed to get my legs in, sprawling my battered body on the floor beneath me. I didn’t close the door. 

	

When I wake the next morning, sun beaming onto my bruised legs, clutching my head away from the light, I’d wonder why I never closed the door.
