My neighbour Marcus has been leaving food on his porch every night for three weeks.

Not scraps or a bowl of kibble for a stray, but actual meals in Tupperware containers. Sometimes he left a plate covered in foil, and once an entire pot of what smelled like beef stew. Every morning the containers are empty and stacked neatly by the door. Every evening he puts out more.

I asked him about it in October, across the fence while we were both doing yard work. He is the kind of man who takes his time answering. A retired schoolteacher in his late sixties, deliberate with words. I guess people get that way when they have spent decades being misunderstood by teenagers.

“Feeding something,” he said.

“A dog?” I asked.

“No.”

That was his answer.

I waited, but that was all he offered. He went back to raking and so did I. And that was the end of it.

My street is quiet. It is in a small town called Maple Hollow, tucked into the northern part of New Hampshire, about twenty minutes from the nearest main road. Twelve houses. One way in, one way out. The road ends at a conservation area. Forty acres of protected wetland and second-growth forest the city has not touched since the eighties.

At night you can hear things moving in there if the wind is right. Small animals mostly. Sometimes deer. Once, a few years ago, a black bear wandered out and sat in the Petersons’ driveway for two hours until animal control arrived.

We are used to the woods. They are background noise.

Sometime in October, the background noise changed.

It is hard to explain because it was not really a sound. It was more like realizing something was missing. You would be sitting in the living room and suddenly notice you could not hear the usual night noises anymore. No frogs. No wind moving through the trees. Even the distant sound of cars from the main road was gone.

Just a heavy quiet sitting outside the house.

Around the same time my dog stopped wanting to go into the yard after dark. She is a seven-year-old Lab who once tried to make friends with the bear in the Petersons’ driveway, so this was not normal for her. She would walk up to the back door, look out, then turn around and look at me like she wanted to apologize for something she could not put into words.

I mentioned it to my wife. She said maybe the dog was just getting older.

A few days later I brought it up with Marcus while we were both outside leaning on the fence.

He listened, then nodded slowly.

“When did you notice it?” he asked.

“A couple of weeks ago,” I said.

He thought about that.  
“That makes sense,” he said. “They have been coming closer. Getting used to the area.”

“Coming closer?” I said. “What is?”

He did not answer right away. He just looked at me like he was deciding how far to go.

“You would probably call them animals,” he said finally. “That is the simplest way to put it.”

I know how this sounds, so I will just say what I did.

I put up a trail camera. One of those motion-activated ones people use for deer. I fixed it to the oak tree at the back of my yard, pointed toward the fence where my property meets the conservation land.

The first night did not show much. A raccoon passed through around two in the morning. Wind moving the tall grass along the edge of the trees. At one point something small and quick crossed the frame near the fence, moving toward Marcus’s house. It paused for a few seconds, just long enough for me to notice it was facing the camera, then it was gone.

I assumed it was another animal and did not think much of it.

The next night, just after four, something came into view near the fence.

The infrared washed out the details, but the shape was clear enough. It stayed low to the ground, about the size of a big dog maybe. The way it moved did not look right though. Too smooth. The limbs bent in a way that didn’t match how animals usually move.

It stopped at the fence and turned toward the camera.

There was no face. No eyes or ears. Just a smooth front where a face should have been, and a small opening pale in the infrared light that might have been its mouth.

It stayed there completely still for several seconds. Then it backed away and disappeared into the trees.

I showed the footage to Marcus the next day on my phone.

He watched it without saying anything, leaning close with his glasses on. When it ended he watched it again. Then he handed the phone back to me.

“That is a young one,” he said.

“A young… what?” I asked.

He thought about it for a moment.

“I do not really have a name for them,” he said.

I pressed him a bit more this time and he finally explained. He spoke slowly, choosing his words the way he always does.

He said he first noticed them about a month ago. Movement along the tree line. The same kind of quiet I had been describing, only he picked up on it faster. He grew up near woods in rural Quebec and said you learn early what it sounds like when a forest goes still around something big.

But these were not predators. Or at least that was not the right way to think about them.

They had not threatened him. They had not come closer. Mostly they watched.

Curious was the word he kept coming back to.

Leaving food started as a test. Not to tame anything. He was clear about that. More like an introduction. A way of saying he knew they were there and was not reacting out of fear.

The food was always taken. And the containers always came back.

“Came back?” I said.

“Stacked by the door,” he said. “Every time.”

“They understand the containers,” I said.

He nodded.  
“They understand a great deal,” he said quietly. “That is what would worry most people.”

I did not sleep much after that conversation.

Not because I was scared exactly. It was more the feeling of trying to fit something into a mental category and realizing there was not one that worked.

I kept thinking about the footage. About the way it had stood there still, like it was weighing something.

That night before bed I left some food out on the back step. Just a bowl of leftover pasta from dinner. I did not plan it. I did not think it through. It felt more like responding than deciding.

The next morning I checked the trail camera.

Between three and four in the morning four of them had come up to the fence line. Different sizes. They did not bunch together. They just stood there loosely, all facing the camera.

One of them, the largest, was slightly ahead of the others.

At 3:52 it stepped over the fence into my yard.

It moved toward the oak tree and stopped beneath it. Then it lifted one of its limbs. The reach was long enough to touch the camera housing.

It did not damage the camera.

It adjusted the angle.

When I watched the new frame I understood what it wanted me to see.

My back door.

The steps where my dog would not go at night.

The porch light I had started leaving on.

And the bowl I had set out with the pasta.

It was empty. Stacked neatly on the step.

I went over to Marcus’s place that afternoon and told him what I had seen. He listened without interrupting. When I finished he stayed quiet for a long moment.

“You have been leaving food,” he said finally.

“Just once,” I said. “I did not really think about it.”

He nodded and looked toward the tree line.

“So what happens now?” I asked.

He took his time answering.

“When I started this,” he said, “I thought I was being careful. Doing things in a controlled way. On my terms.”

He paused.

“Lately I have been thinking the careful part was on their side. And that they have been setting the pace the whole time.”

“That does not really help,” I said.

He looked at me with that familiar patient expression.

“You left food,” he said. “You got a response. You had a camera adjusted so you would see something they wanted you to notice.”

He turned back toward the trees.

“I think you are past the point of opting out.”

That night there were seven of them standing along the fence.

I watched them through the trail camera feed on my phone while I stood at the kitchen window. None of them moved. They just waited.

My dog stood next to me.

She was not looking at the screen.

She was staring at the back door, her tail moving slowly and carefully the way it does when she is trying to figure something out.

I have been leaving food out every night since then.

By morning the containers are always back on the step. Clean and stacked carefully.

Sometimes there is something left behind.

Once it was a smooth stone I did not recognize. Another time a small bundle of dried plant matter tied together.

Marcus came over for coffee a week later.

“They are not animals,” he said plainly. “And they are not from here.”

“They have been watching,” he said. “Watching how we respond. What we accept. What we choose to give.”

“That is why the food,” I said.

He nodded.

“It was a test.”

Before he left he stopped in the doorway.

“When something intelligent makes first contact,” he said, “it does not start by knocking. It starts by learning how much space it is allowed to take.”

Last night I woke up at three in the morning for no clear reason.

The house was quiet. My wife asleep beside me. The dog at the foot of the bed, awake, watching the window.

I checked the trail camera.

Nothing.

No movement in the yard. No shapes along the fence. No outline at the tree line.

Just an empty frame.

I put the phone down and lay there.

That is when I noticed the silence again.

Not outside.

Inside the house.

In the room with us.

It seems to me that whatever these beings are, they do not quite know what to do with sound yet. So they pull it away instead, leaving a heavy silence behind.

My dog turned her head toward the corner near the wardrobe and began to move her tail slowly.

I did not need to look.

The silence told me they were there.

I am writing this instead of turning my head.

I think that choice matters.

I believe they are waiting to see what I do next.

I think it is going to be fine.
