I hadn't wanted to go hunting. Not because I'm some city boy who can't handle nature, even though that seemed to be what my future father-in-law thought, but because this whole weekend felt like a test I'd already failed before we even arrived.

"Welcome hoooome," Vanessa said, her voice bright as we pulled up in our rental car. Her parents stood on the porch, him tall and broad-shouldered next to his petite wife who looked like she'd blow away in a strong wind.

I shook his hand - Dale - and felt my knuckles grind together under his grip. His eyes were blue ice chips that seemed to bore right through me.

The cabin was rustic, no word more apt than that. Wooden walls rough-hewn from the surrounding forest, floors creaking with every step like they were warning me away. The air smelled of earthy tones, as if we weren’t even indoors.

The weekend was a series of subtle jabs. Dale correcting every made-up hunting story I had, Vanessa's mother smiling apologetically when he'd go too far. Dinner conversations about the land and the animals like they were sacred texts only true believers could understand.

Saturday morning came early with the sound of the screen door swinging open. Dale was already on his porch drinking coffee from a mug that said "If you can read this, I'm not hunting." He nodded at me when I emerged, handed me a cup without asking if I wanted one.

"Let's go get us some breakfast," he grunted.

I followed him into the woods, rifle heavy in my hands. The snow crunched underfoot like broken glass. We walked for quite awhile before we saw anything living, just tracks and droppings that Dale pointed out with reverence.

"The doe and fawn are just over that rise," he said calmly.

I looked through the scope and saw them, two smaller shapes huddled together under an evergreen tree, their breath clouding in the cold air. The doe lay protectively between us and her child.

My finger tightened on the trigger. This was my chance to prove myself, to show Dale I could handle this life he seemed so determined to keep me out of.

But something stopped me, the way they huddled together, the fear in their eyes as if they knew what we were capable of. The doe watched me steadily through the scope like she understood exactly why I was there and what it would mean for them.

I lowered my rifle slowly, feeling Dale's gaze on me. He shook his head disapprovingly when I did, just barely, but enough for me to know that I had failed.

We walked back to the cabin without speaking much after that—the unspoken understanding hanging between us like mist in the morning air.

That night at dinner, Vanessa told her parents about how we'd almost hit a deer on our way up. Dale grunted and smiled at what he probably attributed to my incompetence.

"These city boys don't know what they're doing out here," he muttered around a mouthful of venison stew - game from last season's hunt.

I bristled at the comment but kept my mouth shut, focusing instead on cutting into my meat. Vanessa shot me an apologetic look across the table before changing the subject to her work.

The conversation drifted through topics I barely paid attention to: their neighbor's new tractor, crop yields this year compared to last. Dale drank steadily from a bottle of whiskey he kept close at hand while his wife cleared plates and refilled glasses without complaint.

When we finally excused ourselves for bed, Vanessa pulled me aside in the hallway outside my room. It was an old-fashioned thing with heavy curtains drawn across the window and an ancient quilt spread over the mattress that sagged under my weight when I sat down on it later after brushing my teeth in the small bathroom sink.

"You okay?" she asked softly as we stood there alone together finally, away from prying eyes and ears.

"I'm fine," I lied through clenched teeth. "Just tired."

She kissed me gently on the cheek before disappearing into her room down the hall, the one where she'd been sleeping since childhood while her father taught her how to track game through snow-covered fields and shoot straight at moving targets.

I lay awake for hours listening to owls hooting outside my window, wondering what kind of man could raise a daughter like Vanessa. She was so warm and caring despite being raised in this frozen hellhole where every conversation felt like an interrogation session about whether I was worthy enough to marry into their family tree that stretched back generations into Wisconsin's red dirt soil.

The drive home the next morning started off quiet enough. The snow had fallen overnight, blanketing everything with fresh powder that sparkled under gray skies overhead as we pulled out onto the winding road leading away from Dale and his domain.

But of course, tension began to build between us, this time not just because of my failed attempt at bonding over hunting.

This was something else entirely, the kind that builds slowly like a pressure cooker about to explode until there's nowhere left for steam to escape except through angry words thrown back and forth.

"You could've at least tried," she said finally, breaking the silence with an accusation that hung heavy in the air between us like smoke from a firecracker set off too close to your face.

"Tried what?" I snapped back before realizing how defensive it sounded coming out loud. "To be something I'm not? To suddenly become some country boy who loves killing animals for sport?"

She sighed heavily and looked away through the passenger window at bare trees whipping past outside; just branches reaching up toward heaven like anemic fingers clawing at clouds overhead.

"I didn't ask you to change," she said softly after a moment. "I just thought maybe if we spent some time together out here, you'd understand why this place means so much to me."

"Why does it matter?" I growled under my breath. Snow began pelting the windshield harder now, like nature itself was taking sides in our argument.

"Because it's part of who I am," she said simply. "And if we're going to build a life together someday, shouldn't that be important enough for you to try and appreciate what makes me… me?"

I gripped the steering wheel tighter as frustration boiled inside me, at Dale and his condescending attitude, at this godforsaken wasteland pretending to be paradise on earth where people actually wanted to live like pioneers instead of normal human beings with modern conveniences.

But mostly I resented how easily she could make me feel guilty for not loving something that made no sense whatsoever. It was like trying to explain why you hate Brussels sprouts when everyone else thinks they're delicious and you just need "to give them a chance."

I opened my mouth to argue back but never got the chance. The world outside suddenly exploded into chaos as a massive buck leapt directly onto our hood with an ear-splitting crash that sent us skidding wildly across icy road.

The impact threw me forward against the steering wheel hard enough to knock breath from my lungs. Vanessa screamed beside me and I barely managed to wrench control back as evergreen branches began to thwap against the roof.

I slammed on the brakes as snow sprayed up around us in blinding clouds that obscured everything beyond the nose of our car where the buck lay sprawled across the hood - a lifeless heap of fur and antlers splayed out like a decoration someone had placed there for effect rather than reality.

"Oh God," Vanessa breathed beside me, hands covering her mouth as she stared at the carcass with wide eyes. "Is he...did we..."

I killed him. That's what she meant but I couldn't say it aloud.

"No," I managed hoarsely after a moment, though part of me wondered if maybe this was exactly what Dale had been waiting for all weekend. Proof that city boys didn't belong here among real men who knew how to handle nature when it came knocking at your doorstep.

We sat there stunned for a few long minutes, staring at the dead animal across our hood while flakes continued falling around us like confetti from a twisted universe celebrating car crashes involving wildlife instead of weddings or birthdays.

"I'll take care of it," I finally said as calmly as I could before climbing out into cold air that bit my skin immediately, reminding me how easily this place could strip away layers until nothing remained but bone and sinew exposed to elements.

After clearing the hood and sloughing the creature unceremoniously into the ditch, we sat there for a moment, breathing heavily in the darkness broken only by the amber glow of our dome light. Then I shifted into reverse and slowly backed out onto the road proper.

That's when we saw them; deer carcasses littered the entire length of highway ahead, some whole, others torn apart with entrails spilling out onto the frozen ground in a sickening holiday display. Their jaws gaping wide open like they'd died mid-bleat. Vanessa made a sound that might have been nausea or prayer.

"What is this place?" I whispered hoarsely. A stench of decay began to fill the car despite all windows being closed tight against the cold outside air. "Keep going," Vanessa pleaded from beside me, voice trembling with fear I'd never heard before in someone usually so composed. "Please just drive away from here."

I put the transmission in neutral and let gravity slowly roll us forward until we came to rest right among them, close enough to reach out and touch their stiffened fur if I wanted. The engine idled quietly like it knew better than to make noise here where silence reigned supreme except for an occasional gust of wind whistling through bare branches overhead.

"Don't," she warned again, but this time there was resignation in her tone rather than panic, like maybe deep down part of her already suspected what I might find when I got out to investigate further.

The snow crunched under my boots as I stepped onto the shoulder and approached cautiously. The first one lay on its side with legs splayed at awkward angles, throat slashed open wide enough for me to see bone beneath shredded flesh. Its eyes stared blankly back at nothing in particular, but something about their expression made me shiver despite being fully clothed against the biting cold.

I walked slowly along the row of corpses, each one more grotesque than the last. The scene looked staged almost… deliberate somehow.

Then I saw what I didn’t know I was looking for: a single set of human footprints leading away from the carnage, disappearing into the trees on the opposite side of the road where no tracks came out again.

"Oh God," I breathed, turning back to stare at her through the windshield with dawning horror in my eyes. Realization hit home hard enough to make me stagger slightly on unsteady legs. All those lectures about being a real man, hunting your own meat… that was his test and I'd failed spectacularly instead of finishing what we started yesterday morning.

I couldn't even look at her anymore without feeling sick to my stomach knowing this display was here just because some backwoods redneck wanted an excuse to scare me away from his daughter. The rage boiled up inside so fast it nearly blinded me. How dare he? How fucking *dare* he?

I went back to the car and closed the door without emotion. "There's gotta be another way around," Vanessa said quietly after we'd sat there long enough for our breath to fog up both windows. "You can just... go off-road, right?"

I wasn't sure about that, but the GPS on her phone showed a back route, something called County Road 23, that would loop us back to civilization without having to move those corpses ourselves.

The detour was worse than I expected. The road narrowed until it felt like we were driving through someone's private property, and the snow here had drifted deep enough that my tires spun twice before revving again on packed-down ice beneath.

"Maybe we should just turn around," Vanessa said, but her voice lacked conviction. We both knew what that meant; another hour driving back to find a different route, another hour away from home on these empty roads with a dead deer graveyard behind us.

Eventually, a warm glow cut through the darkness ahead like a beacon promising safety and warmth and civilization.

A diner. A small place called "FOOD" according to its neon sign flickering weakly against the snowfall.

Vanessa leaned forward in her seat, squinting at the building as we pulled closer. The parking lot was mostly empty except for two other vehicles: a beat-up pickup truck and what looked like an older sedan covered in frost that hadn't been cleared off since this morning's thaw.

"Does it look... clean?" she asked hesitantly as I killed our engine outside one of the few open spots near the entrance. "Like, do you think they wash their dishes?"

I shrugged because honestly, at this point I didn't care if they served roadkill on toast. I was hungry enough that even mystery meat sounded appetizing after the weekend we had endured. As long as it wasn’t venison.

We sat there for a moment longer than necessary while Vanessa checked reviews online (limited results from what looked like mostly years-old posts) before finally stepping out into the cold night air together. Both of us were desperate for food but also the relief of being somewhere other than alone on those empty roads with dead things behind us.

The bell above the door chimed as we entered, a cheerful sound that felt almost mocking given how everything outside seemed designed specifically not to cheer anyone up. The walls were covered in old photos of Wisconsin landscapes, farm fields mostly, and there was a jukebox playing something country near one corner booth where an older man sat hunched over coffee.

He looked up when we came in, not just glanced our way but actually turned his whole body toward us with deliberate slowness that was frankly a little bit strange.

I sat across from Vanessa in the worn vinyl booth, my stomach growling despite the uncertain state of this place. The diner's incandescent lights flickered overhead, casting everything in a sleepy yellow glow that made even Vanessa look tired.

"Well," she said with forced cheerfulness, "that other guy hasn’t dropped dead from food poisoning." She tried to smile at her own joke, but I could see the doubt creeping into her eyes as she scanned the menu. Grease stains marked the table between us, smelling like old coffee and regret.

"We're hungry," I reminded myself more than her. "And it's not like we have a glut of places to choose from."

Vanessa nodded slowly, running a finger along one of those greasy spots before pulling out my menu for me; old habits from when she'd had to help me read everything because the words would blur together.

"Yeah, but I'm hoping they at least wash their hands," she muttered under her breath as we waited for someone to take our order. The waitstaff was nowhere in sight. There was only the old man in the far booth, hunched over his coffee like he hadn't moved in years.

He watched us with eyes that seemed too sharp for such a withered face; a crow observing two mice who'd wandered into its territory.

"Don't look at him," Vanessa whispered urgently when she caught me staring back. "We don't know what kind of person—"

But it was too late; he raised his cup in greeting and smiled with teeth that looked like they hadn't seen a dentist since Nixon's first term.

I raised my hand in a half-hearted wave back, trying to look casual about it. "He's just being friendly," I said, though even as the words left my mouth, they didn't sound convincing.

Vanessa leaned closer across the table, her voice dropping to barely above a whisper. "Friendly people don't stare at you like that for three minutes straight without blinking." She gestured subtly toward him with her menu. "And look, he's not even eating or drinking anymore; he's just... watching us."

It was true. The old man had set down his cup and now sat perfectly still, those dark eyes fixed on our booth with an unabated intensity.

"Maybe we should ask for different seats?" Vanessa suggested, already starting to slide out from her side of the booth. "There's one open near—"

The waitress finally appeared then, a middle-aged woman exuding an acute cigarette smell and an apron that looked like it had surrendered long ago against whatever was going on in the kitchen.

"You two gonna order or what?" she barked, not unkindly but certainly without patience for indecision. Behind her, I could see the old man's expression shift into something almost predatory as he watched our exchange with passive interest.

Vanessa slid back into place quickly and grabbed both menus now, probably so we'd make a decision fast enough to satisfy this woman who clearly had better things to do than wait on us.

"Two coffees," Vanessa said firmly, "and two of your specials."

The waitress grunted acknowledgment before turning away. As she walked back toward what I assumed was the kitchen area behind her, passing right by that old man's booth, I saw him reach out and touch her arm as if asking a question.

She shook her head sharply at whatever he said, then gestured dismissively in our direction without looking back at us before disappearing through a swinging door.

The old man resumed his staring immediately after that exchange, if anything even more focused than before, and I got the feeling that unknown people didn’t usually frequent this place.

We finished our meal in near silence, both of us too distracted by the presence across from us to really engage with each other. The food wasn't terrible, just... unremarkable, and at least it filled the hole that I had come here to fill.

Vanessa kept stealing glances over her shoulder as she ate, and I caught myself doing the same thing more than once. Each time we looked back, he was still there in his booth, that hunched posture unchanged except for his eyes which seemed to track our every chew and whisper with unnerving precision even when we weren't looking directly at him.

"Okay," Vanessa said finally as she set down her fork seconds after finishing her meal. "I'm done pretending I can ignore this."

She pulled out her wallet and tossed some bills on the table, enough to cover both our meals plus a generous tip, and started gathering up her things with quick, efficient movements.

"We're leaving," she said quietly but firmly as if reminding herself (or maybe me) that we had agency here. "We just go before he can do... whatever it is he's going to do."

I nodded in agreement even though part of me wanted to stay longer, maybe order dessert or coffee because there was still a night of driving ahead, but Vanessa was already sliding out from the booth.

As we stood up, I caught one more look at that old man. He raised his cup again, that same slow gesture, and smiled with those yellowed teeth before taking a long sip of what had to be cold coffee by now.

The bell above the door chimed as we left into air that felt noticeably fresher than inside. We didn't speak until we were both settled in our car, doors locked and engine running. Only then did Vanessa let out a shaky breath she'd been holding since we stood up to leave. 

We finally left the diner, our stomachs full but our nerves still frayed. He didn't follow, he just sat there in that booth like some kind of guardian or gatekeeper... waiting for whoever came next through his territory.

"Okay," she said again, this time softer but still determined not to look back at that diner now fading into darkness behind us as I pulled away from the curb. 

"That was weird," I agreed, but chuckled a bit at how on-edge she was. The parking lot snow crunched under our tires as we pulled out onto the dark highway.

"God, can you believe how that creep was watching us?" Vanessa muttered, pulling her coat tighter around herself in the passenger seat. "Did you see how he stared?"

I glanced at her face illuminated by the dashboard lights - she looked exhausted despite having just eaten. The whole weekend had been a disaster from start to finish. 

"Yeah, I noticed." I kept my eyes on the road ahead. "Let's just get home and put all of this behind us."

County Road 23 stretched endlessly before us, a grey ribbon cutting through white fields under a moonless sky. We were about halfway back to the city now, maybe another two hours if the roads stayed clear.

Vanessa shifted in her seat, trying to find a comfortable position despite being tired from sitting so long at dinner. "My dad was awful yesterday," she said quietly after several minutes of silence. "I know he's protective and all, but... I'm twenty-six years old."

"I get it." And I did, sort of. My own father had never been particularly interested in my relationships either way. But Vanessa and I both deserved better than the cold shoulder her dad had given me.

Ahead, something moved across the road. I slowed down instinctively as a figure stumbled into our headlights, a person caught out on this freezing night.

Vanessa sat up straighter beside me. "What is that?" As we got closer, the headlights revealed a familiar sight that made my stomach drop. It wasn’t a person, it was another barricade; dead deer carcasses spread across both lanes of the highway like a makeshift biological fence.

"Oh no," Vanessa breathed as she saw it too. "Not again."

I stopped the car about twenty feet away and got out to inspect the situation, crunching through fresh snow in my boots. The temperature had dropped even further. I could see my breath forming clouds in front of me now.

"This is... this can't be happening," Vanessa said when I returned to the car, her voice rising with frustration. "We’re on a completely different road now! How did we end up back here?"

I pulled out my phone and opened Google Maps, studying our route carefully by the glow of the screen in the darkness.

"You're right, this is weird." My finger traced along County Road 23 on the map. "According to this, we’re on the correct road, which means this is a completely different set of bodies."

Vanessa leaned over to look at my phone, her brow furrowed. "So what... did someone move those other deer? Set this whole thing up again?"

"I don't know." A bad feeling settled in the pit of my stomach as I realized we might not have a choice about turning around. "But if this is intentional... someone wanted to make sure we didn't leave."

Vanessa's hand found mine, squeezing it tightly. "What do you think we should do?"

I looked at her face; tired but determined despite everything that had gone wrong today. Dale hadn’t done this. Sure, he hated me, but he wanted Vanessa to be happy, and this was messing with her just as much. He wouldn’t do this to his little girl.

"We go back to the diner," I said firmly. "Find out what's going on."

We pulled back into the diner's parking lot, our headlights sweeping across the building before I killed the engine. The place looked just as it had an hour ago, warm lights glowing in the windows against the dark night.

Vanessa was already unbuckling her seatbelt before I could fully stop. "Come on," she said urgently. "Let's get to the bottom of this."

We hurried inside, shaking snow off our coats and stamping our boots to clear them of packed ice. The waitress who'd served us earlier looked up from behind the counter with a tired stoniness. "Back so soon?" she asked, though her tone was completely devoid of caring.

Vanessa stepped forward immediately. "We need help. There's a barricade of dead deer on County Road 23 about ten miles west of here. We went around another one over an hour ago and now we can't get home either way." The waitress's expression didn't change much, which I found oddly unsettling given how concerned Vanessa sounded.

"Oh," the woman said casually as if discussing something mundane like the weather. "Yeah, that’s happened before. The highway department will clear them up in the morning."

Vanessa's hand tightened on my arm. "But it happened on the other road too! Is that really normal!?"

The waitress shrugged. "Maybe the visibility is bad and there’s been more accidents? I don’t really know..."

I glanced around the diner, noting that no other tables were occupied this late at night, when my eyes landed on the booth in the corner. And there he was, the strange old man from earlier, still sitting alone with a coffee and pie. He hadn't moved an inch since we'd left. 

Vanessa noticed him too and her grip on my arm tightened even more. "That's the man from before," she whispered urgently. "He must know something about this!"

I looked between her determined face and the old man's impassive expression in that glossy red booth, then back at Vanessa again. "How could he though?" I asked quietly but firmly. "Look at him. He hasn't moved since we left an hour ago. How would he have time to kill a bunch of deer down the road?"

Vanessa bit her lip, clearly frustrated by my logic even as she seemed to acknowledge its truth in the way her shoulders sagged slightly.

"I don't know," she admitted after a moment of tense silence between us. "But I \*know\* something's wrong here."

Vanessa's frustration finally boiled over. She marched directly toward the old man's booth, her boots clicking sharply against the diner floor with each determined step.

I followed behind more cautiously, my arms instinctively raising in the ‘I’m sorry’ expression.

"Excuse me," Vanessa said curtly when she reached his table, standing rigid and tense. "We need to talk to you about what's happening on the road outside."

The old man didn't look up from the piece of pie he was slowly working through with a fork. He took another deliberate bite before responding.

"Mmm," he said around his mouthful of food, chewing thoughtfully as if considering her words. "And why would I know anything about that?"

Vanessa's voice rose slightly in exasperation. "Because you were watching us earlier! And now there are more dead deer blocking the road home!"

He finally looked up at her then, those dark eyes cold and calculating as they studied her face.

"Dead \*deer\*," he repeated slowly, emphasizing each word with an almost mocking tone. He set down his fork carefully on his plate before continuing in that same measured way: "How interesting that you seem so upset about dead deer when I've seen your kind hunt them for sport or run over them without a second thought while driving."

Vanessa's face flushed red, whether from anger or embarrassment, I couldn't tell. "That's different," she protested. "We don't \*deliberately\* block roads with body parts!"

The old man leaned back in his booth seat, crossing his arms across his chest as he regarded us both with an expression that might have been amusement if not for how unsettling it was on his weathered face.

"Is it?" His voice dropped lower now so only we could hear him over the ambient noise of the diner. "You come here accusing me of something without any proof… because I looked at you during dinner and happened to be sitting in this booth when you returned."

Vanessa opened her mouth to respond but he cut her off with a raised hand.

"And as for those deer on County Road 23..." He paused deliberately, letting the words hang in the air between us. "Maybe they're not dead at all. Maybe someone just wants an excuse to keep people like \*you\* from passing through this area."

The old man's words felt heavy and oppressive. Vanessa seemed at a loss for how to respond, her mouth opening and closing wordlessly as she tried to process what he'd just said.

I felt my own confusion rising with growing unease. "What do you mean ‘people like \*us\*'?" I asked, stepping forward slightly in front of Vanessa protectively even though part of me knew how ridiculous that probably looked: a middle-aged man confronting an old stranger in a diner.

The corner of the old man's mouth twitched upward in what might have been a smile. "Exactly what I said." His tone was more conversational now, almost casual as if discussing something mundane rather than whatever bizarre situation we'd found ourselves in.

Vanessa finally seemed to find her voice again, though it came out shakier than usual: "But why would anyone want to do that? We're just trying to get home!"

The old man's dark eyes didn’t leave Vanessa's face as he spoke next. "Are you now?" He tilted his head slightly, studying her like a scientist might examine an interesting specimen under glass. "Or are \*you\* one of those people who pretends not to see the blood on your hands?"

"I don't have any—" Vanessa started indignantly before stopping herself mid-sentence as realization seemed to dawn across her face. The hunting trips. The deer stands in autumn growing up. The time we hit a buck with the car just tonight and I dumped it into the ditch...

All those moments where death was just a minor annoyance came flooding back, each one now seeming darker somehow under this old man's unblinking scrutiny.

Outside, something began to change without either of us really noticing at first. The gentle snowfall that had been drifting down when we'd arrived started falling heavier... faster... until it became almost a blizzard raging against the diner windows.

I moved closer to Vanessa as the wind howled louder outside, and then I saw something through one of those frosted panes that made no sense.

Deer. Dozens upon dozens of them, running in terrified herds across what should have been empty fields behind the diner... but they weren't just running. They were being torn apart by arrows and bullets, their bodies twisted and mangled as shrill bleating filled the air outside and cut right through those thick windows into our little bubble of warmth inside...

Vanessa gasped beside me and I looked away from the window just long enough to see that her face had gone pale with shock; she must have been seeing it too.

"Look at them," he commanded, gesturing toward the window where those tormented deer continued their endless loop of suffering outside. Some were torn apart by unseen hunters while others simply ran themselves into exhaustion against invisible barriers surrounding our vehicle. "Do you even *see* what they are?"

Vanessa's voice came out small and trembling: "They're... they're just animals..."

"Just *animals*," the old man repeated slowly, each syllable dripping with something that might have been disappointment or disgust, perhaps both at once. 

"Do you know how many times I've watched *you* hunt?" His voice had dropped lower still until it was barely above a whisper but somehow carried through every corner of this suddenly too-small diner. "How many has the woman brought down with her bow over the years? How many bucks have been left bleeding on that highway or hanging from a rack?"

I felt Vanessa stiffen beside me at those words. I knew she was thinking about her childhood, her father, how she had so desperately wanted him to approve of us. None of that seemed important now.

"At least when they’re killed efficiently, they don't suffer like this," I protested weakly even as part of me recognized how hollow that argument sounded given what we were witnessing outside.

The old man's expression didn't change, but something shifted in his gaze making it feel heavier somehow. "Is *that* supposed to make it better?" His tone carried genuine curiosity rather than accusation.

"You think that because *you* don't enjoy watching them die means it doesn't matter how many times they've screamed themselves hoarse trying not to?"

Vanessa's hand found mine and squeezed hard enough that her nails dug into my palm. "We didn't know," she whispered desperately as if trying to convince not just this strange figure before us, but also herself.

"Didn't *know*," he echoed back softly now standing directly between us and any possible escape route, though part of me wondered if there was even anywhere left for us to go... "Is that really the best defense you can offer? Does that excuse the cruelty?"

The old man's dark eyes shifted from Vanessa to me now, the direct focus of his attention felt like static electricity before a lightning strike.

"You both think you're better than those people who hunt for sport or run over critters without stopping," he continued speaking directly at us, never raising his voice above that soft conversational tone.

I walked to the door quickly, dragging Vanessa behind me, and reached for the door handle. I pulled hard, but nothing happened. The door didn't budge an inch. My breath caught in my throat as I pressed both palms against it.

"There's no door," the old man observed quietly behind us, not quite mocking but certainly noting our futile efforts with an almost clinical detachment. "There never was one here for you to open."

"I... I couldn't pull the trigger," I finally managed to get out, my voice cracking slightly as emotion threatened to overwhelm rational thought. "When I went hunting with your dad, there was this doe and she looked right at me before..." 

He turned to face me directly: "You. Your compassion is genuine at least. You feel bad about what happens out there in those woods." A slight nod followed this observation before he continued speaking again, but this time addressing Vanessa. "But \*you\*..." His gaze locked onto hers with an intensity that seemed to bore right through flesh and bone straight into whatever lay beneath. "You're just pretending, aren't you? Saying what you think I want to hear because of fear."

Vanessa's mouth opened, then closed again as protest died before it could even form sound in her throat. "I... no! That's not..." She tried desperately, but her voice cracked under the pressure. 

The druid stepped closer now, placing one gnarled hand on her shoulder. “You're just scared I'll punish you somehow if you don't say what seems expected." His tone wasn't angry. It carried no malice whatsoever, instead sounding almost sad.

"I'm sorry," she whispered finally. "You're right - I am scared... but also..." Her voice cracked again, unable to continue the sentence.

"Compassion isn't the same as action though," the man said gently, interrupting before Vanessa could finish whatever thought process occupied her mind. Perhaps he could see what she was thinking in real time.

"I'm going to let the man go," the druid said quietly. His dark eyes shifted back toward Vanessa, still sitting frozen in the booth. "But you… you haven't learned anything yet, have you? Still stuck pretending about the divine importance of your life.”

He reached out towards her again, extending his palm upward, as if in invitation. "Wouldn't you say that's worth addressing?" His tone wasn't cruel, though it carried no warmth whatsoever. Vanessa made no move to react, and his gnarled fingers closed around her wrist with surprising gentleness. Her eyes widened. I could see them even through my own tears and the blur of everything else happening too fast.

"Wait!" The word tore from my throat before I could stop it. "Please don't hurt her!" But he wasn't hurting her, not physically anyway. His touch seemed almost tender as his other hand moved in a strange gesture, fingers curling inward then spreading outward again like grasping at something invisible hanging between them.

"Please," I whispered again, though I knew I held no power here. The change started at Vanessa's fingertips. Her delicate pink nails that had been manicured just last week began darkening and lengthening simultaneously. Her jeans dissolved away thread by thread without leaving any physical evidence behind except for exposed skin underneath turning grey-white like winter bark on dead trees.

Vanessa's face - my Vanessa, with those bright eyes that had laughed at my jokes over coffee - was twisting into something else too. Her features were becoming more cervine while still retaining traces of the woman I loved beneath them; a muzzle forming where her nose used to be, ears pushing through hair turning from brown to russet, fur spreading across her scalp.

The worst part wasn't watching her change though, it was feeling that transformation happening inside myself as well, even if my body remained unchanged physically. My heart ached with each passing second seeing someone I loved so deeply being pulled away piece by piece until only this creature would remain where Vanessa had been moments before...

Her fingers merged together into what could clearly be identified as hooves. "Vanessa?" I breathed out her name like a prayer hoping against hope that maybe if I just said it, reality would shift back to how things should be.

But there was no answer except more gasping breaths coming faster now as the change accelerated rapidly throughout her entire body. Her shoulders changed next; bone structure rearranging itself into a configuration better suited for quadrupedal movement instead of walking upright.

I watched helplessly as it continued spreading across her neck, where collarbone disappeared beneath fur growing thicker, covering her entire body except for small patches of skin still visible around eyes staring directly at me with recognition slowly fading away.

Vanessa's blouse had completely disintegrated at this point, leaving nothing but grey-white fur covering her entire torso. Her smaller head sat atop a longer neck, attached to shoulders now positioned higher up and further back, allowing better balance when standing upright on four legs.

Vanessa's legs were the last part to change. They seemed almost eager, stretching out longer before contracting back down again several times until reaching the proper length. Her knees bent wrong, backwards at first before snapping forward into proper position once tendons and ligaments finished rewiring themselves.

Vanessa was now fully transformed. Where my girlfriend had been moments ago, there was instead a doe. 

I could see her chest rising and falling rapidly beneath the fur covering her torso. Her eyes met mine, containing a mix of emotions too complex to untangle; they contained acute awareness, but all sense of familiarity had disappeared.

I reached out, wanting to touch her and reassure her somehow, but as my fingers caressed her scalp, I knew she was gone.

The rest of the drive passed in silence broken only by occasional sounds from outside: tires crunching snow and wind whistling through bare branches overhead. When I finally pulled into the driveway, the late afternoon sun slanted across my yard, casting long shadows. Everything looked different somehow.

I climbed out stiffly onto my concrete walkway leading up porch steps where my neighbor stood watching with a curious expression on his face. "Everything okay?" he called out as I reached the top step.

I nodded numbly at his question - what was I supposed to say? That I didn’t know what was real anymore? No thanks, I'll pass on that one tonight if it's all the same with you sir. "Just tired," I said. He nodded understandingly.

I sat on my futon and stared blankly ahead, my mind sluggishly plodding along to make sense of everything that happened. It felt like a lifetime ago, and was already fading under the weight of exhaustion slowly overtaking my senses until nothing remained but darkness waiting patiently for me to surrender control.

I walked past the kitchen alcove on my way to bed. Outside, dawn bled across the horizon, staining it with bruises of pink and purple that mirrored the ache blooming behind my eyes. I reached for my phone, its case cool against my palm, but before I could dial her number, a soft snuffling sound from beneath the table made me freeze. My breath hitched in my throat as two [amber eyes](https://youtu.be/wMdIEhnOCks) blinked up at me from between spindly legs and a familiar patch of russet fur brushed against my leg.
