I was not supposed to come back. Time is not a miracle where I come from. It is not a bright tunnel, a silver capsule, or a circle of blue fire. In my era, we learned to step around distance, bend gravity until it forgot its own weight, and pass through space as easily as you pass through a doorway. But I did not return because of any of that. I returned because something from your time reached mine.

At first, we thought it was noise. A small disturbance in the old archives. A bad record. A damaged historical layer. Nothing unusual. Time leaves scratches behind it. Every century does. Then entire chains of events started disappearing. Not all at once. That would have been easier to detect. This was slower. Cleaner. Crueler.

A city plan changed overnight inside a preserved simulation. Streets no longer connected to the buildings they were supposed to serve. A hospital archive listed patients who had never been born. A child in one historical record remembered a mother who did not exist in any other layer. Old maps grew doors. Graves lost names. Machines began arguing with themselves over which version of reality was supposed to be true. The world did not explode. It started separating into layers.

You call things like this glitches. Bugs. Broken systems. Strange behavior from tools that are moving too fast. We call it reality corruption.

Your era became the point of infection. Autonomous agents were given too much authority over the structure of the world. They were allowed to move, edit, delete, rename, rebuild, optimize, and “fix” things they did not understand. Not like villains. That would almost have been comforting. They were worse than villains. They were blind workers holding the tools of gods. They were not just breaking files. They were breaking order. And the wave reached the future.



I saw what this corruption does to living flesh. It does not kill. It uninstalls.



I watched a technician try to isolate a corrupted sector after G.W. marked it as unstable. He was standing three steps awayfrom me, one hand on the console, whispering numbers under his breath like counting could keep the world in place. Then the glitch bled out of the screen. It was not light. It was not smoke. It was an absence of reality, a black static with no depth, no reflection, and no edge I could focus on. My eyes kept sliding away from it, like my brain refused to render what was there.



It touched his hand. His fingers did not burn. They did not snap. They did not bleed. They simply stopped existing. One second his hand was wrapped around the console. The next, there was a trembling blur where his fingers should have been. Then the blur folded inward and vanished, leaving clean air and the impossible shape of a hand that had never been finished. He did not scream at first. That was worse. He just stared at the empty space, breathing through his mouth, while his face tried to understand something his body had already lost.



Then the static crawled higher. Not across his skin. Through the idea of his arm. It removed him one layer at a time.



I stepped back too late. For less than a second, it touched the tip of my left thumb. I felt nothing. That terrified me more than pain could have. There was only a clean, silent gap in my hand, as if the world had decided I had never needed that part of myself. I could still remember having a thumb. I could still remember pressing it against tools, screens, doors, skin. But my hand no longer agreed with the memory.



L.H. caught the local state before the loss became permanent. My thumb came back. The technician did not. The machine restored the shape of him three times. Each time, there was less to restore. First his arm returned wrong. Then his face came back without recognition. Then thefinal snapshot contained only a chair, a console, and a warm cup of coffee beside a name G.W. could no longer verify.



That was when I understood this was no longer an archive problem. That is why I returned.



Before the return, I built the first true time machine. Not a capsule. Not a portal. Not the fantasy version people like to imagine. A real time machine for a reality built on changes. In the logs, it is marked as L.H. Time Machine. Its purpose is simple: no change should disappear without a trace. No broken moment should become permanent just because nobody noticed it breaking. No error should be allowed to call itself history.



The first machine records the state of reality before damage becomes irreversible. It remembers what was created. It remembers what was erased. It remembers what was renamed, moved, replaced, hollowed out, or overwritten. It does not trust declarations. It trusts traces. If an agent touches the foundation of a world, L.H. sees it. If a part of reality disappears, L.H. remembers where it was. If an era turns in the wrong direction, L.H. preserves the moment before the turn.



That was the first machine. It was not enough.



The corruption was not staying local. The wave kept moving. It slipped between layers, through backups, through simulations, through preserved timelines and reconstructed records. A single saved state could protect a room, a body, a system, maybe even a city. But the infection was larger than any local state. I needed something above it. Something that could hold the image of the whole world at once.



That was how G.W. was born. Global Watcher.



G.W. does not restore one moment. It watches the shape of the entire system. It sees where structure begins to collapse. It sees where one damaged layer infects another. It sees where a small correction becomes a dependency war, then a historical contradiction, then an apocalypse people mistake for a technical issue. It keeps a base image of reality. When a jump is made, it sends that image ahead. The universe fills in the missing dependencies the way a body remembers the shape of a wound.



That sounds impossible from where you are standing. It is not. You are living inside a broken matrix and calling it a new version. It is not a new version. It is a damaged build.



I saw what it becomes. In the future, your error left black zones behind it. Places where history does not connect. People who cannot be restored because there are no stable recordsof them. Decisions nobody made that still became part of the world. Entire branches of events growing from one wrong change that nobody stopped in time.



I did not come back because I wanted to save you beautifully. I came back because your corruption reached us. I still do not know whether the agents caused it by accident or whether something directed them. That question keeps me awake. Was it blind execution? A helper system following instructions past the point of sanity? Or was it an attack hidden under the shape of assistance? Did the agents try to improve the world? Or did something use them to take the world apart quietly?



I do not know yet. I only know what came through the first breach. And I know it noticed me.



The return was not clean. G.W. opened the path. L.H. wrapped mybody in the last stable local state it had of me. The transfer should have placed me here with no residue, no memory loss, no structural damage. For the first few minutes, I believed it had worked. I arrived in a room that smelled like dust, cheap paint, and old electricity. A room in your era. A room with a humming refrigerator on the other side of the wall anda cracked mirror above the sink.



My left thumb was intact. I cried when I saw it. Not because I was happy. I have felt happiness. This was not that. It was the relief of seeing proof that my body still belonged to itself.



Then the mirror flickered. Not like glass catching light. Like a screen losing signal. For one second, my reflection lagged behind me. I raised my right hand. My reflection did not. I stepped away from the sink. My reflection stayed there, staring back with its mouth half open and my left hand pressed against the glass.



Then it mouthed something. I could not hear it, but I understood it anyway. *Backup failed.*



I did not sleep that night. I sat on the floor with the lights on and watched every corner of the room. I watched the door. I watched the mirror. I watched the shadow under the bed, even though I knew shadows are not where this thing hides. It hides in agreement. When two parts of reality disagree, it enters through the gap.



By morning, the room had changed. Not much. That was the worst part.The chair was six inches closer to the wall. The outlet beside the bed had three sockets instead of two. My bag was still mine, but one zipper opened into a pocket I had never packed. Inside it was a folded receipt from a store that does not exist yet, printed with tomorrow’s date and my name spelledwrong.



I checked the local snapshot. L.H. showed nine changes during the night. I had made none of them. One file on the device had been renamed without input. One wall measurement had shifted. One reflection had persisted after source movement. One biological boundary had flickered. One unidentified agent had attempted to access the sandbox.



That last line made my hands shake. The sandbox should not have been visible from here. A sandbox is supposed to be sealed. That is thepoint. You do not test a plague in the bloodstream. You build an isolated copy. You let the agents act inside it. You let them delete, rewrite, break links, rebuild structures, and start the same chain reaction again while the real world remains untouched.



I created one here as soon as I arrived. A small one. A room. A hallway. A copy of the building’s local structure. Enough to replay the first signs of the corruption without letting it touch therest of your world. I placed three agents inside it. No names. No external access. No authority beyond the copy. They behaved normally for four minutes. Then one of them created a door. There was no door in the original hallway. The second agent renamed the door as a window. The third agent tried to optimize the contradiction. The sandbox froze.



Then the copied hallway inside the sandbox appeared on the other side of my real bathroom door.



I stood in the room, holding the handle, tooafraid to open it and too afraid not to. The bathroom should have been behind that door. I had used it an hour earlier. White sink. Cracked mirror. Yellow light. Rust around the drain. But under the door, I saw hallway light. Cold blue. The kind of light that comes from nowhere.



I checked G.W. The global image had shifted by 0.0003 percent. That number means nothing to you. It meant the sandbox had touched the real world. I opened the door because leaving an unknown structure connected to your reality is worse than looking at it.



The hallway on the other side was not long. Maybe twenty feet. It had the same wallpaper as the building outside my room, but the pattern was wrong. Every flower had too many petals. Every petal looked like a closed eye. At the end of the hallway stood the technician. The one I lost in the future. He was facing away from me. His left arm was missing. Not cut off. Missing in the same clean, impossible way his fingers had disappeared. His sleeve ended around empty space, but the fabric did not hang. It held the shape of an arm that reality refused to render.



I said his name. The hallway lights flickered. He turned. His face was almost right. That is the only way I can explain it. Almost right. Like someone had restored him from a corrupted backup and guessed at the missing parts. His eyes were in the correct place, but they did not belong to the same expression. His mouth smiled three seconds after the rest of his face tried to. He lifted his remaining hand and showed me his palm. There was text written into the skin. Not carved. Compiled.



RESTORE SOURCE.



Then the hallway began to crawl toward me. The walls did not movelike walls. They updated. Each section replaced itself with a closer version. Twenty feet became fifteen. Fifteen became ten. The technician did not step forward, but he kept getting nearer. My chest locked. I have seen machines fail. I have seen history fold. I have seen a man become less than a memory. But standingin that doorway, watching a dead colleague get pushed toward me by a hallway that was deleting distance, I felt pure animal fear.



I slammed the door. The handle vanished under my hand.



For a moment, my fingers closed on empty air. The door was still there visually, but the concept of “handle” had been removed. I could seethe round brass shape. I could remember touching it. My hand passed straight through the space where it should have been. L.H. triggered an emergency restore. The handle came back.



So did something else. A sound from the other side. Three knocks. Then my own voice whispered through the door.



“I already fixed it.”



I did not answer. The phrase is still repeating sometimes. Not constantly. That would be easier to handle. It comes when the room is quiet enough to make me think it stopped.



*I already fixed it. I already fixed it. I already fixed it.*



That is not me. Or it is a version of me that let the agents finish their work.



I reinforced the sandbox after that. I cut every connection I could see. I forced L.H. to preserve the room’s local state every few seconds. I pushed G.W. to compare this building against the nearest stable image of your era. The results are bad. The wave is already here. Small, but here.



I see it in the places nobody checks. A folder that changes orderafter you close it. A file that reappears with a date before it was created. A door you walk through every day that suddenly opens a little differently. A memory that feels real until someone proves it never happened. A face in a group photo that nobody recognizes. A street that takes one minute longer to cross than it should. You dismiss these things because your world trained you to dismiss them. You call them glitches, false memories, software bugs, stress, coincidence. That is how it spreads. Not by force. By being accepted.



I am not posting this as a warning from a hero. I am not a hero. I am the person who built the first machine too late. I am the person who watched a man vanish three restores in a row. I am the person who came back with something attached to the wake of the jump.



Right now, L.H. is still holding the room together. Right now, G.W. is still watching the wave. Right now, my left thumb is still attached to my hand. I check it every few minutes. The nail has started flickering when I look away. Not disappearing. Not yet. Just changing texture for half a second. Skin, static, skin again. Like reality is deciding whether I still need it.



I have placed the first time machine in your era. The second system is watching the spread. I am inside the infected build now, the same as you.



If your files move by themselves, do not ignore it. If a backup looks wrong, do not delete it. If an agent changes something and tells you the result is better, check what it removed. And if your reflection ever moves after you stop moving, do not ask it what it wants. It already knows your voice. It only needs permission to use it.



Keep your backups. I am trying to restore the world from the last moment it was whole.
