It’s hard to tell this story but I know I have to in the hope it served as a warning and saves others’ lives.

Jaya had bought us tickets to the amusement park. We had been planning to go for a while but never had the chance due to work or money. So while I felt a little guilty that Jaya had spent that kind of money on me, I agreed.

I have to admit, at first, it was fun. Nick, Jaya and I got on all the roller coasters and honestly, I’m not sure how they convinced me to get on the free fall. I thought I would die and that would be the scariest game of the day, I was wrong.

“What is that game?” Nick pointed to an attraction with a long line. In front of us stood a ride with a haunted house facade. Unlike the decorations of the other games, this one looked particularly cheap, more like a fair ride. The fake bats flashed with red eyes, threatening to attack us.

“I’m not sure. It doesn’t show up on the app of rides,” Jaya scrolled through her phone.

“Maybe it’s a new ride and the app hasn’t been updated yet?” I suggested. Even with the ridiculous decor, I still wanted to go in. I loved cheesy horror attractions.

“You two get in line while I go buy some popcorn while we wait in line,” Nick said as he walked away to buy the popcorn.

“Thanks for the free popcorn!” Jaya and I yelled at the same time.

“I’m not sharing!” He yelled back at us. 

Once we had finished off the popcorn, we were somewhat bored. The line moved slowly and the appeal of the game was quickly wearing off. Jaya kept trying to find the name of the attraction, but her search showed nothing. I asked the people in front of us if they knew what the attraction was but they didn’t know either.

“But I did hear that there is a large money prize for whoever wins the game,” one of the girls said.

“Like $10 large or like $1000 large?” Nick asked.

“Not sure, we asked some people too, but all we could confirm was that it was a good amount of money and no one had won yet,” she responded.

“I was about to say we should bail, but now I feel like we really should stay. Even if it’s $10 large, I want to see what we have to do to win,” I said excitedly.

“Figured you would say that,” Jaya rolled her eyes, clearly teasing, “I guess we will have to make the sacrifice for you.”

“Damn right you will!” I laughed.

We continued to chit chat as we waited for the line to keep moving. Every time a large group went in, we cheered loudly. Some people, including the girl that told us about the money prize, left the line, tired of the wait. 

“Where do you think people are getting out?” Nick asked, “I haven’t seen anyone come out.”

”I don’t know. Maybe the exit is somewhere else,” Jaya responded.

”But it says Exit there,” Nick pointed towards the entrance of the attraction.

“Could just be an emergency exit,” I offered, “for scary cats like you.”

The doors to the attraction opened, the smell of chlorine slapping me on the face. It made my stomach churn and I looked at my friends, their expressions showed they were ready to throw up just like I was.

“Oh, God!” Jaya breathed in.

“At least we can’t say it’s not clean inside,” Nick coughed.

We hoped to make it into the attraction this time. I crossed my fingers in the hope we wouldn;t have to wait any longer and it paid off. We were the last people to go in before the doors closed on their own.

At first, it was completely dark with the exception of the blinking red lights of the dollar store bats. I smiled, as Jaya grabbed my hand and Nick accidently bumped into us. A dim light appeared in front of us, leading us into a cave constructed of what looked like styrofoam. As everyone made their way in, a tall slim man stood there, waiting for us.

“Thank you everyone for joining!”, the attendant gave a devilish smile “You are allowed one tool for this game. Once you grab it, walk towards the boats. You will be seated and have fun! And remember, there is an enormous cash price for those that win!”

The attendant pointed at a table nearby him. What he had called tools looked like real weapons. This was so exciting, we would each have our fake weapons. I wondered how we would be using it.

“How much is enroumous?” Nick asked.

“My dear, you will have to win the game to know!” The attendant continued to smile.

”Well, now we got to win the game! I need the money!” Nick said excitedly.

”For the money!” We yelled together.

We laughed as some people looked at us annoyed, but it wasn’t our fault they didn’t know how to have fun. By the time we reached the table with the tools, many of the cooler looking ones, like the double edge axe and scythe were gone. Nick grabbed a sickle, for sure thinking he would look spooky with it. Jaya, being the most practical of us, grabbed a short sword. Then I saw it, a club.

“Can you even carry that?” Jaya looked at me in doubt.

“Absolutly!” I tried to pretend it wasn’t heavy.

We headed towards the boats, the last once to get in because we couldn’t stop goofing around. Jaya and I sat next to each other while Nick sat behind us. But as the boat moved into a quite large cave, the silence around us became deafening and our giggles were shushed. I looked around, but the dim lights did a good job of keeping everything spooky. The ceiling was an endless pit of darkness, and the water somehow looked black. On the sides, there were pieces of land with some trees but nothing more. Somehow they had managed to create the illusion of a forest with no visible walls.  

I was in awe. The outside decor deceived us in the best way possible and I couldn’t wait for whatever was to come. One glance at Jaya and Nick told me they were excited too.

A noise started to come from the ceiling. The once quiet boat was now full of whispers. Were there actual rats on the ceiling or the audio was just great? Our whispers were interrupted by the screams of the people ahead of us. 

“What the hell is happening over there?” Jaya grabbed by hand as she tried to calm herself down.

“I don’t know, but we are about to find it,” I said excitedly, “And if it’s a real monster, we can throw Nick at them as we make a run for it”.

“Why am I friends with you two, again?” Nick joked.

The screams didn’t stop, they became hysterical. Those screams didn’t sound fun anymore. It probably was some kind of recording to make us scared. That made a lot more sense. I hadn’t noticed my body had started to tense up, but reason made my shoulders relax one more. It was time to-

One of the girls sitting ahead of us started to scream as our boat started to sway side to side. Nick suddenly crammed himself between Jaya and I, his face pale. He turned to look back and we followed his gaze. Some kind of zombie-looking thing was trying to get into the boat. Then came a splash in front of us, one of the girls that had been screaming was now missing. Before we could react, another zombie pulled a small boy into the water. His father jumped in to get him out but neither ever came back out.

“Use the weapons!” A teen yelled as he hit a zombie with his ax. 

Jaya screamed as a zombie grabbed her leg and attempted to drag her off the boat. Nick was busy getting rid of the zombie behind us, so I grabbed the club and hit the zombie as hard as I could. The zombie let go but more were getting into the boat, they were going to sink the boat. 

“JUMP!” I yelled. 

I let go of the club knowing there was no way to swim with it. Grabbed Jaya and Nick by their arms and made them jump into the water with me. As soon as my head was submerged in the water, the pungent smell of rot previously hidden by the chlorine hit me. I was unsure what way was up and down, but I had to figure it out quickly. I held on to my friends, knowing I was the strongest swimmer among us. Jaya’s nails dug into my skin and Nick was being pulled away. I held on and started to swim in the opposite direction of where Nick was being dragged hoping I was making the right choice.

When the water broke, I was happy to take in a deep breath of chlorine, but the fight wasn’t over. Nick kept being submerged. He swung the sickle but couldn’t hit the thing holding on to him.

“Stop holding me. Let me do something!” Jaya pulled herself away from me.

She swam towards Nick and quickly used her short sword to stab downward. Whatever was holding on to Nick finally let go. We made our way to the shore, occasionally feeling the brush of rotting skin trying to drag us down. 

Once at the shore, the zombies didn’t follow us anymore. They made their way back, waiting for their next victims. The teen that had yelled to use the weapons had made it to shore and so had a woman I was sure was accompanying her daughter. We all were haunted, but something about her vacant eyes will always stay with me.

“Is your arm all right?” Jaya took a hold of it, “I’m so sorry!”

“Yeah, don’t worry about it,” I tried to sound calm. Honestly, I had already forgotten about my arm, I was just relieved we had all made it out.

“Now what? This is obviously not a game,” Nick held back tears as he spoke.  

None of us answered. We really didn’t know what to do next. 

From the corner of my eye, I saw the woman walk towards the forest. Her empty eyes, unable to cry. To this day, I don’t know how she died.

One thing was for sure, we had to keep going. Since the water wasn’t a choice, we headed for the trees. The teen boy joined us. There were no introductions, he just followed along, pretending to be brave. But then again, we all needed to pretend to be brave if we had any chance of making it out.

As we got deeper into the forest, the silence was almost absolute. We all took glances around the trees, not knowing if more of those zombies would jump at us and try to drag us back into the water. I felt especially vulnerable, at the whims of my friends to protect me. While I did trust them with my life, I would still feel safer with a club on my hand.

Noise broke the silence. The trees filled with the chitter of bats and ahead of us people screamed. We didn’t know what to do. Going back wasn’t an option, but we weren't sure moving forward was either. Nick went ahead of us, while Jaya and I tried to keep the boy safe. He made no protest and grabbed my hand as we kept moving forward.

The screams became louder and we tried to soften our steps in hope of avoiding whatever monster was ahead. But the forest ended abruptly, with no way to go around, and there we saw a new horror.

Humanoid bat creatures drenched in blood stood over people who were long gone or verily holding on to life. The bloodbath ahead of us made me freeze on the spot. I wanted to scream but Nick stood in front of me so I couldn’t see the blood. If this had been fake blood, like the one I was expecting at the start of the game, there would be no problem, but this vibrant red, iron smelling liquid was one of my worst fears. I had blocked off my mind the blood dripping from the small cuts on my arm, but this was unavoidable. 

“Take a deep breath, we have to figure out how to get around the vampires. You can freak out once we are out of here,” Nick tried to keep me focused.

I took a deep breath. I hadn’t realized I was crying. Jaya gave me a hug, trying to keep me calm.

“Ow!” 

The three of us turned to look at the teen boy, who was now holding his neck.

“A bat bit me. Do you think that’s bad?” He sounded worried.

“Only if it has rabies, but that’s unlikely,” Jaya responded, “It shouldn’t be a problem at least for now, even if it has rabies.”

“I know this will sound dumb, but what if we make a run for it? All those vampires look busy. And we only attack the ones that come towards us?” The teen said.

Since none of us had a better idea, we shrugged and went with it. Nick went in front of us once more, Jaya went behind him, and the boy behind me. Nick counted with his fingers and at three we all made a run for it. I tried to keep my eyes focused on Jaya’s back, but there was no way to ignore the massacre around us. The vampires knelt next to destroyed bodies, sucking away at their blood. Some of their victims looked our way but had already given up. 

None of them paid attention to us. The single vampire that had approached us was distracted by a pool of blood and went to lick it off the floor. We swatted some bats off our way but managed to make it into the next patch of forest with no trouble.

“Holy shit!” Jaya pulled me hard.

The harbored breathing coming from the teen boy I had assumed was from running, was not it. His face had started to contort and his arms were growing a light film connecting to his torso. He cried, but his voice now mimicked the shrieks of bats.

He threw the ax he was carrying towards me. I slowly picked it up as he fought his transformation. I’m not sure how I did it, but I grabbed the ax and swung at his head. The blood sprayed into my clothes and the nearest trees. My friends looked at me horrified at what I had done, but as I dug out the ax from the boy’s head, I think they understood. Had the boy stayed alive, he would have become a vampire, possibly killing us too. This was both mercy and a way to save ourselves. Nick had been right, it wasn’t time to freak out.

We kept quiet as we moved forward. Every once in a while we saw people pass by, but neither side stopped for the other. No one knew what was to come, and if there was an end to the nightmare.

Once more, the trees ended and we were presented with two choices. One was a field with tall grass that appeared to be moving softly with the wind, but the problem was, there was no wind. The other option was a dark cave, which might as well be an entrance to hell.

“Logic tells me to go into the grass, at least we can see what is going on. But horror movie logic tells me the cave is the safer option,” Jaya said.

“I’m honestly not sure,” I responded.

“I think we should go with the field. I can use the sickle to open a path for us,” Nick suggested.

“Lead the way,” I responded.

Nick took a deep breath, he gave us a smile trying to reassure us and we smiled back too. He turned around and used the sickle to chop off the first batch of grass. The grass that has been slowly swaying was now completely still. Nick paused for a moment to look around and see if anything was coming, but there was nothing.

Nick continued to chop off the grass, creating a tight path for us to follow. As we were about to join Nick, he gave a yelp that was quickly cut off as his body was split in two. We both screamed as unseen monsters took Nick’s body away and all we were left with was a puddle of his blood and guts.

We ran back to the forest, unsure if any monster was following us. Inside the forest, we fell to the ground and hugged each other, crying loudly at the loss of our friend. There had been nothing we could do about it, it just happened.

“We should have gone through the cave,” Jaya cried, “Maybe he would have survived if I had tried to convince you guys.”

“You can’t blame yourself. If we go blaming people, then it really should be me. We’re here because of me and I told Nick to lead the way and,” I couldn’t continue. We had lost Nick and it had been my fault.

Jaya didn’t contradict me, but I knew she disagreed. We both knew arguing with each other was futile. Right now, we needed to stay strong in the hope of getting out together. As Nick had said, we could freak out later.

We made our way towards the cave, taking one glance at the grass field which now had no evidence of Nick having cut through it. The grass lazily flowed once more, inviting their next victim.

There was no plan. All we hoped for was that there was an end of the cave and it was the end of our suffering. We held hands once more, this time I took the lead, and ventured into the darkness.

There was no way to keep our steps silent. Every step echoed with the crunch of shoes on what I was hoping wasn’t bone debris. It quickly became apparent that we weren’t alone, something kept scurrying by our side. I tried to keep myself from trembling, but it was nearly impossible. We hurried our steps, stumbling occasionally, but holding each other so we wouldn’t fall. And at a distance we saw a light. We started to run, ignoring the scurrying around us. Then I hit something sticky. 

“Oh god, I think I know what is following us,” I whispered.

“Please don’t say spiders,” Jaya begged.

“I won’t say it, but please cut me off this web before I become lunch,” I tried not to panic.

Jaya used her sword to cut off the web stuck to my body. At some point she cut off a chunk of my hair but it didn’t matter, the scurrying was becoming louder. 

I begged for Jaya to hurry. My heart raced as I started to imagine the spiders attacking us. And then I was free. I fell hard into the ground, the ax hit me on my stomach but while it hurt, it had not opened the skin.

Jaya helped me up and took the lead, swinging her sword around to cut any webs. Any time I felt a rush of air pass by us, I swung my ax, sometimes hitting hard objects which I hoped were rocks. We kept running for what felt like an eternity, and as we reached the end of the cave, a large spider blocked our way. I let go of Jaya and ran to it, smashing the ax into its head just like I had done with the boy. We climbed over the spider’s body and managed to get out of the cave.

We didn’t stop. We kept running, only turning around to see if we were being followed when a new patch of forest gave us coverage. As we sat down, I looked at Jaya but as she took one glance at me, she laughed.

I had forgotten she had chopped off some of my hair and as I went to touch it, I could feel how uneven it was. On the left, it was close to my lower back, but the right side was almost by my ear. I laughed with her. After all the terrible things that had happened today, why not enjoy this one moment?

“I’m sorry about your hair,” Jaya said as her laughter started to die.

”It’s ok. I might start a new trend thanks to you,” I answered.

We stayed there for a moment, exhausted, not knowing what was going to come next. I’m not sure how much time passed but we got up once more, not exactly ready to keep going, but with no other choice.

As we kept walking, we realized that unlike all the other places, there were no people here, or if there were, they were hiding. My legs wanted to give out, and my stomach hurt, I hadn’t checked but I was sure it was bruised. One look at Jaya told me she was not doing great either. I tried to keep my mind focused on getting out, but Nick being ripped in half kept popping up in my mind. The teen boy’s head being split in half kept flashing through my eyes and the haunted look of the mother probably mirrored my own.

Then I saw it, glowing red against the dark, there was an exit sign. 

“Jaya! We made it!” I yelled excitedly.

“Finally, this hell is-“ Jaya was interrupted by a loud growl.

From a distance, we saw werewolves, impossibly large creatures standing on two feet. They sniffed the air and quickly turned to look at us. We stood still, hoping the two stories tall werewolves would ignore us, but it was clear we were their lunch.

The werewolves howled, and we didn’t wait for them to be done before we made a run towards the exit. Jaya ran by my side, trying to ignore the werewolves running towards us. Their steps made the ground tremble, but we didn’t allow it to stop us.

My legs burned but I didn’t allow them to slow me down. Jaya was starting to fall behind me and as I turned to help her, a werewolf was already at her toes. I threw my ax at the werewolf, hoping to hit it but missing terribly. Jaya looked at me with fear but resignation. She turned around and stabbed the werewolf in an eyes and it clamped down on her leg.

“GO!”, she screamed in tears.

Reluctantly, I turned around and kept running. I never looked back but can only presume that Jaya fought and stabbed as many of the werewolves as she could before bleeding out to death.

I made it to the exit. I could hear the werewolves behind me as I opened the door, got out and closed it as quickly as I could.

The Sun was still out, it almost blinded me but as I began to focus, I realized where I was. This was the exit Nick had pointed out when we were waiting. 

“Congratulations, my dear! You have won!” The attendant scared me as he stood by my side smiling.

I couldn’t speak. This man seemed unbothered by what had happened. He continued to smile, waiting on me.

“It is all right my dear! You must be in shock from winning! It is not every day we have a winner,” he said excitedly as he handed me a paper, “Here is the prize! Hope to see you again!”

As he walked away, I looked at the paper which was a check, it was more than $10 large. In fact, I was sure my great grandchildren would never have to work.

I did try reporting this to the police but no one believed me. The check was real either way, even if no one could track where it came from. To this day, I still send money to both Nick's and Jaya’s family. After all, it’s my fault they are gone.

I haven’t gone back to an amusement park since then but my sister just bought tickets and is begging me to go with her. She doesn’t know what happened last time. I will go with her, I just hope this time it is a normal visit to the amusement park.
