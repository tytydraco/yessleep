His real name was Eli Voss, but nobody used it.

Ghostboy since third grade. One of those names that attaches itself because it's accurate enough that nobody questions it. He was thin and pale and moved through hallways like he was trying not to displace any air. Dark circles that looked permanent. Same three hoodies in rotation. Headphones around his neck even when nothing was playing, and a small digital recorder the kind with a gray windscreen, the kind journalists use in movies  that he carried everywhere. Sometimes he'd hold it up in the middle of a crowded hallway and just record. The noise of lockers. People. Nothing in particular.

We were twelve. Everything that didn't look like us was strange.

I want to be honest about what I was back then, because the rest of this doesn't make sense without it. I wasn't cruel the way some kids are cruel. I didn't go looking for it. But I laughed when other people laughed, stayed quiet when quiet was easier, and told myself that not starting something was the same as not being part of it. I knew better. I just didn't want to pay the price of acting on it.

The one who started it was Connor Reese.

Connor had been held back a year, which at that age translates directly into power. Bigger than everyone, and he'd long since figured out what that was worth. His best friend Derek Park's entire personality was laughing at whatever Connor did. Together they'd spent middle school finding whoever had the least protection and testing how far they could go.

Eli had no protection. No group, no older sibling, no social currency. He ate lunch alone. He existed in the school the way a stone exists in a field  just there, indifferent to whether anyone noticed.

The first time was at lunch. Connor sat across from Eli without being invited, the way people sit when they've already decided they own the space.

"What's that thing?" Nodding at the recorder.

Eli looked up. He had this quality of not looking scared when he probably should have. He just looked at Connor the way you look at something you've already made a decision about.

"A recorder."

"What do you record?"

"Sound."

Connor picked it up without asking. Turned it over. Put it back down.

"Sounds like something a freak would do," he said, and left.

But I watched Eli's face after they were gone. He looked down at the recorder, then up at the cafeteria all of us, the whole room  with an expression I couldn't read then but understand now.

He was filing it away. He was paying attention.



After that it was open season.

Connor knocked the headphones off Eli's neck in the hall. Sat behind him in class and flicked his ear until Eli shifted, at which point Connor went perfectly still and Eli was the one who looked like the problem. He'd take the recorder and pass it down a row of hands while Eli sat and watched it travel, not reaching for it, not saying anything.

I was there for most of it. Close enough that my silence meant something. I knew that. I told myself it didn't.

The day in music class was the worst of that first year.

The teacher had stepped out. Connor stood at the front of the room like he was substituting.

"I heard you make music. Let's hear it."

"No."

One word. Flat and quiet.

Connor took the recorder. This time Eli reached for it and Connor just held it higher, already pressing play.

What came out of the tiny speaker stopped the room.

It was layered and slow rain against glass, traffic from somewhere far away, something low and humming underneath that made the room feel bigger than it was. Voices folded in, pitched and softened until they weren't voices anymore, just texture. It sounded like the inside of somewhere warm on a cold night. It sounded like something real.

Four, maybe five seconds of silence.

Then Connor laughed, and that was all it took.

"What is this baby garbage?" He held the recorder up like evidence. "Who listens to this?" He collected the laughter like it was his, and then he dropped the recorder onto the tile floor  not threw it, just opened his hand and let it fall  and stepped on it once.

Something cracked inside.

Eli picked it up without speaking. Pressed play. The audio came out muddy and distorted. He listened to that for a moment, then turned it off and put it in his bag.

I laughed. I want to make sure I said that. I laughed with the room, and I watched Eli hear me do it, and I looked away.



I started sitting near him in the library about two weeks later. Not next to him. A few seats down. I told myself I was just studying.

He had a laptop running audio software  waveforms and timelines, colored blocks stacked on top of each other. He'd work for a long time barely moving, then lean back and close his eyes and listen through his earbuds and something would move across his face that wasn't there the rest of the time.

One afternoon I sat close enough to hear what was leaking from the earbuds and it stopped me completely. Something new, bigger than the recorder piece, and it had this quality of being exactly as long as it needed to be. Not a second more or less.

He noticed me listening and pulled one earbud out.

"It's really good," I said.

He looked at me a moment, deciding something.

"Thanks," he said, and put it back in.

That was it. But I kept coming back, and after a while he started to expect me, and then one day he just handed me an earbud without me asking and I sat there listening to something he was still building, and that was how it actually started.



His name was Eli. He'd been making field recordings since he was eight, when his dad handed him a recorder the week before he left and said he thought Eli would like it. He started because he liked the idea of capturing something exactly as it was. A photograph can be staged, he told me. A painting is always the painter. But a recording is just  what was there. The truth of a place at a specific moment.

He'd been layering recordings into music for three years by the time I met him properly.

I asked him once if it bothered him that nobody heard it.

He thought about it. "Someone will," he said.



Eighth grade was worse.

Connor found out we'd been spending time together. He cornered me by the lockers.

"Heard you're hanging around with Ghostboy."

"We have the same study period."

He looked at me for a second. "Don't," he said, and walked away.

I didn't stop. But I started being careful  sitting with Eli when Connor wasn't around, drifting when I thought someone was watching. I told myself I had reasons. I did. They just weren't good enough.

The day with the laptop was in March. A Friday.

Eli had finished something new. The biggest piece he'd made. He played it for me in the library and I sat there with the earbud in and felt something I still have a hard time describing like standing at the edge of something without knowing what was below. Loss and the anticipation of loss at the same time.

Connor came in while we were sitting there.

He came straight to our table. Looked at the waveforms on the screen.

"This the music?" He raised his voice, playing to the room. Derek appeared from behind the shelves like he'd been waiting.

"Leave it," I said.

Connor grabbed the corner of the laptop and slid it toward himself. Eli put both hands on it. For a second I thought it would be enough. That Connor would read something in Eli's face and back down.

Derek grabbed Eli from behind and pinned his arms.

Connor walked to the window.

We were on the second floor.

"Put it back," I said. My voice came out quiet, which I hated.

Connor held it out over the ledge with one hand, looking at Eli.

"Say something," he said.

Eli looked at him. That still, weathered look, like he was watching something move toward him that he'd known was coming for a long time.

Connor let go.

We heard it hit the courtyard below.

Derek released Eli and they left. Connor glanced briefly at what he'd done on his way out the door and I watched something cross his face not guilt exactly, more like mild surprise at himself  and then it was gone.

Eli stood at the window for a long moment.

"Four years of recordings were on there," he said.

"I know."

He picked up his bag and left. I stood at the open window with the cold coming in and felt the specific shame of having been there the whole time.



I went to the school counselor Monday. Names, dates, exactly what happened.

Connor got five days at home and an apology letter so clearly written by an adult it didn't even pretend to be from him.

When he came back he was smarter. He'd found Eli's music account  the one he posted to under a different name. He and Derek spent a week in the comments. By the end, the account was deleted. Three years of work, gone twice.

I found Eli in the library the day after. He didn't have his headphones on. He was sitting with a notebook open in front of him, not writing.

"I'm sorry," I said. "I should have said that a long time ago."

He didn't answer right away.

"Why did you start sitting near me?" he asked.

I could have lied. "Because I felt bad."

"For me."

"Yes."

"Is that still why?"

"No," I said. "It's not."

He looked at me for a moment. Then he nodded.

"I'm starting the recordings over," he said. "Some of them I think will actually be better."



The thing in April happened on a Tuesday.

Connor had downloaded some of Eli's tracks before the account went down. He edited them  put them under a video, something ugly and deliberate  and posted it. By Tuesday afternoon half the school had seen it.

I found Eli on the path behind the baseball fields. He had his headphones on, moving fast. When I called his name he slowed but didn't fully stop, so I walked beside him.

"I'm going to the principal tomorrow," I said. "I'm going to push until they actually do something."

He shook his head.

"I'm tired of it taking up space in my head," he said. He paused. "I'm not saying that to be resigned. I just" He stopped.

I didn't know what to say.

The light was going gold through the trees and somewhere close a bird was making a sound I'd never heard before  layered, recursive, almost rhythmic  and I watched Eli's head tilt slightly toward it. Even now. After all of it.

We heard Connor's voice before we saw him.

He came around the bend with Derek half a step behind. He saw us and stopped. Then he grinned.

"Perfect," he said.

I felt Eli go still next to me. Not tense. Still. Like something settling.

"You see the video?" Connor said, looking directly at Eli. "I thought the music was a nice touch. Really elevated it."

"Walk away," I said.

Connor didn't look at me. He reached out and grabbed Eli's headphones off his neck.

"You want these back?"

He dangled them. Eli looked at them and then at Connor's face and said nothing.

Then the sound started.

Low. Not from anywhere specific  from everywhere. From the air, from the ground. A frequency you felt before you heard it. It rose slowly, and I recognized it: the cathedral piece, the big layered field recording, rain and voices and distance, and underneath it that bird  the one from just seconds ago  folded in, like it had already been captured and waiting.

Connor went still.

The headphone cord moved.

There was no wind.

It moved slowly, once around Connor's wrist, the way water moves around something in its path.

He made a sound I'd never heard from him. Short and confused, then frightened. He tried to drop the headphones and they didn't fall. He grabbed at the cord with his other hand and it tightened.

Derek was already gone. I hadn't seen him leave.

The sound grew. I had the sudden and total certainty that everything Eli had ever recorded was in it  every moment and every place and every hour he'd spent paying attention to a world that had not paid attention back. Every sound that had ever been taken from him.

"Stop," Eli said.

Quietly. One word.

The sound dropped. The cord fell slack. Connor stumbled back, staring at his wrist where a red mark sat like a bracelet, and then he looked at Eli.

I had watched Connor Reese for years. I had seen him cruel and confident and contemptuous. I had never seen what was in his face in that moment.

He turned and walked away fast without saying anything.

Eli crouched down and picked up his headphones. Turned them over, checked them carefully, found them okay, and hung them back around his neck.

I stood there.

"Did you do that?" I asked.

He was quiet for a long moment.

"I honestly don't know," he said. "I don't fully understand it." He paused. "Not yet."

We walked in silence. The bird was still going somewhere behind us  the same recursive sound, looping back on itself and Eli had his recorder out.

I noticed he didn't point it at the bird.

He pointed it at me.



That was eleven years ago.

Eli's music is on every platform now. He has an audience  the devoted, quiet kind. I put his records on sometimes when I'm driving at night and feel that same thing I felt the first time I heard it through one earbud in the library. The edge of something. The peace of not needing to know what's below.

He lives eight minutes from me. We have dinner most Fridays. He still carries a recorder  newer, smaller, better. He'll hold it up mid-conversation when something catches his ear and he doesn't apologize for it anymore.

I've stopped noticing that he does it.

Except sometimes I'll catch him pointing it at me when he thinks I'm not watching, and I'll think about that path, and that bird, and the way the cord moved with no wind, and the way he said *not yet* 

And I'll wonder what exactly he's been collecting.
