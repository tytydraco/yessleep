Mud is colder than you expect.

It isn’t just wet. It’s alive with cold. It clings to my back and shoulders in a way that makes the skin there feel like it isn’t mine anymore, like I could peel away from myself and leave an outline behind. Rain hits my face in hard, clean drops that taste like metal when they slide into the corners of my mouth. Every breath drags smoke into my lungs. Old smoke, not fresh flame, the lingering kind that lives in fabric and hair and the back of your throat.

Above me, oak branches knot together like ribs.

The canopy is so thick that only a small cutout of sky shows through, a jagged patch the color of bruised water. When lightning flickers somewhere far away, the leaves flash pale and then go black again. The tree groans with the wind. Not creaks. Groans. Like it’s under pressure.

I try to move and pain flares through my ribs. It’s sharp enough to make my vision tunnel. My right forearm is a band of torn skin and dried blood. The wound stings in the rain and aches underneath, deep. My shoulder feels wrong, like the joint isn’t seated the way it should be.

I’ve never been so tired in my life. Not the kind of tired a nap fixes. The kind that makes you consider letting your eyes close and never opening them again.

Somewhere in the woods, something cracks a branch. A slow, deliberate snap. Then silence.

I focus on my breathing because it’s the only thing that feels like mine. In and out. The smoke and rain make it taste like pennies. I’m lying on my back under a massive oak at the outskirts of a town I don’t know how to name in my mind anymore, because my mind keeps trying to keep parts of it separate. Before and after, normal and not normal, married and hunted.

If you’re reading this in bed and someone you love is asleep beside you, take a second and listen.

Not for traffic. Not for the refrigerator humming downstairs.

Listen for the breathing next to you.

Slow. Steady.

Because the absence of that sound is what first taught me something was wrong.

I began waking at 3:17 a.m. about three years into my marriage. Not once. Not occasionally. Every night, like an alarm clock wired into my bones.

The sensation was always the same. A clean jolt, sharp and precise, like someone snapped their fingers an inch from my ear. My eyes would open before I had time to decide whether I wanted them open. My mouth would taste metallic, like I’d been chewing on a penny in my sleep.

I’d stare at the red digits on the clock. 3:17. And the other half of the bed would be empty. Not rumpled. Not freshly vacated. Cold.

Then, right when my brain tried to find a normal explanation, I’d hear it: A careful click. The back door latch. Bare feet across the deck boards.

It sounds like nothing when you’re reading it. It sounds like insomnia. A midnight habit. A person stepping outside for air.

But there’s something about a careful sound at 3:17 a.m. that makes your skin tighten. You can hear intention in it. You can hear someone trying not to wake you.

I’d get up and walk into the kitchen. The tiles were always cold enough to sting. The darkness there always felt thicker than it had any right to be. When I pressed my forehead to the sink window, the glass felt like ice.

Outside, our backyard was a rectangle of moonlit grass fading into the treeline. Beyond that, the woods began without warning—dense, unmaintained, swallowing sound and light. Even in summer they smelled damp: wet bark, mushrooms, loam, and something faintly sour beneath it all, like earth that had been turned too many times.

And there she’d be.

Ellen.

Barefoot. Thin nightshirt. Standing at the edge of the woods like a fencepost. Still. Not shivering. Not moving.

Just staring into the trees like she was waiting for something to speak first.

The first time I whispered her name, she didn’t turn. She just lifted one hand slightly, palm out. Not yet.

When she finally came inside, her skin felt cold when she brushed past me. Not “cool from night air.” Cold like stone. She smelled like wet leaves and something faint and chemical underneath, like lightning on the edge of a storm.

The next morning I asked her, trying to sound casual.

“What are you doing out there every night?”

She didn’t look embarrassed. She smiled the way someone smiles when you ask if they locked the car.

“I keep watch,” she said.

“For what?”

She set her mug down with a porcelain click that sounded too loud.

“For what’s owed.”

Then she kissed my cheek. Her lips were cold enough that I felt it in my teeth.

I laughed. I made a joke. I told myself she meant adulthood or debt or some poetic nonsense. You tell yourself what you need to.

I met Ellen in 2011.

Coffee shop downtown. Exposed brick. Warm bulbs. The air smelled like espresso and scorched milk and cinnamon. I was in line debating whether to buy a muffin I didn’t need when she stepped in behind me.

I caught her face in the reflection of the pastry case glass.

She smiled like we already knew each other.

Two days later, we reached for the same carton of eggs under fluorescent grocery lights. Her fingers brushed mine. They were cool. She laughed and apologized like it was the cutest thing in the world.

A week after that, we crossed at the same intersection. Then again at a bookstore. Then at a traffic light, her window already rolled down before I even looked over.

“You again,” she said.

Her head tilted slightly, the way someone tilts their head when listening for a distant sound.

I laughed. “Are you following me?”

She held my gaze a second longer than normal. Not flirty. Measured.

“Maybe we’re meant to keep finding each other,” she said.

It felt like fate…Now it feels like placement.

We married in 2012 under string lights between oak trees. Phones up recording vows. Family laughing. A day you’re supposed to keep safe in your memory.

When I said “I do,” she squeezed my hand so hard my knuckles ached. She leaned close.

“You said yes,” she whispered into my ear.

Her breath was faintly sweet with something metallic underneath, like old pennies in a warm pocket. I thought it was nerves. I didn’t understand what kind of yes she meant.

After the wedding, Ellen said she wanted to move somewhere quieter. Somewhere with “roots.” A better place to raise kids.

She meant Forster, Pennsylvania. Her hometown.

I assumed moving to her hometown meant family. Warm relatives. Childhood friends. A built-in support system. But none of her loving relatives still lived there. Her parents were gone. An aunt she “didn’t talk to.” No cousins dropping by. No dinners. No photos on the fridge from a big close clan. 

Just…the town. And the woods.

When I asked why Forster if no family was there, Ellen smiled softly.

“It’s where I’m from,” she said. “It matters.”

At the time it sounded sentimental…Now it sounds like a requirement.

Forster treated her like someone returning to a job, not a home. People didn’t greet her like “welcome back.” They greeted her like “you’re on time.” 

I didn’t understand that until the BBQ.

The BBQ was late summer. The air smelled like charcoal and hot dogs and sugary lemonade. Kids screamed through sprinklers, their laughter sharp and bright against the drone of cicadas. Lawn chairs sank into grass. Someone had a speaker playing old rock too softly, like background noise in a waiting room.

We arrived and Ellen was immediately surrounded. Not hugged. Not “oh my god, it’s been years!” Surrounded like a crowd surrounds someone about to speak.

“How’s the home treating you?”

“Feels good to be back, doesn’t it?”

“You’ve been keeping watch?”

That last one made my neck prickle. The man who asked it smiled like he’d said something harmless. His head tilted slightly, waiting.

Ellen laughed softly.

“Of course,” she said. “It’s all in order.”

The man’s smile widened. A woman nearby lifted her cup and murmured, almost like a toast: “To order.”

A few voices repeated it under their breath.

“To order.”

No cheers. No clinking cups. It wasn’t celebration. It was acknowledgement.

I drifted toward the grill to distract myself. That’s when I noticed something that made my stomach dip. Every person holding tongs flipped meat at the exact same moment. Not one person. A line of hands moving in unison. Burgers turning. Hot dogs rolling. The charcoal flared, and for half a second the flames rose too cleanly and too evenly, and I thought I saw straight lines inside them. Intersections, angles. Like something drawing itself in heat.

Then the flare died. Normal.

Later, as the sun dipped, every adult turned toward the woods at the same time. Not dramatically. Not obviously.

Like they all heard a sound on the same frequency. Heads tilted. Smiles soft and distant. The air pressure shifted in my ears like descending in an elevator. Ellen slid her cold hand into mine.

“Stop worrying,” she whispered. “You’re safe here.”

Safe. It didn’t sound like reassurance. It sounded like ownership.

That night at 3:17, she stood at the treeline again. And deep in the woods behind her, I swear I saw a tall, thin shape between trunks. Too still. Too patient. Then gone.

Our sons were born in that house at the edge of those woods. Thomas in 2015. Daniel in 2017.

Quiet babies. Not peaceful. Quiet.

They stared at the same corner of their rooms. Sometimes they smiled at it. Sometimes they looked like they were listening.

One morning over cereal, Thomas tapped his spoon against the bowl. Clink. Clink. Clink.

“Mama says you’re temporary,” he said.

“What does that mean?” I asked, forcing my voice light.

He shrugged.

“It means you help.”

Daniel later pressed his hand to his chest and said, “It’s pulling.”

Ellen always had a reason.

“They’re imaginative.”

“You’re stressed.”

“You need more sleep.”

Maybe she was right. Except the world outside started to feel…slanted. And then there were the moments where I was the only one who noticed.

At a diner near our house, I watched a man pour gasoline over himself and light it while chanting in a language I didn’t recognize. I smelled it. I felt heat on my face. I heard his screaming except it didn’t sound like pain. It sounded like joy.

I jumped up, knocking my drink over.

The waitress stared at me.

“What?”

The space was empty. No smoke. No smell. No body.

Ellen squeezed my arm.

“You need sleep,” she said.

Across the diner, a man stared at me with his head tilted slightly. Then he looked back down at his phone. Normal.

At church, I heard humming before the organ began. Low, unified, vibrating in my teeth. When I looked around, everyone stood perfectly still. Then the organ played and the humming stopped like it had never happened.

At the grocery store, a woman whispered under her breath in that same thick rhythmic language, faster and louder until I stepped back. Then she blinked and smiled politely.

“Sorry,” she said. “Long day.”

No one else noticed.

Ellen always had an explanation.

“You’re anxious.”

“Stop looking for patterns.”

But I wasn’t looking for patterns.

I was trying not to drown in them. Then one night, I did the thing you’re not supposed to do. I followed her into the woods. 

My phone lost signal halfway through the yard. The forest swallowed sound. The air tasted damp and metallic. The clearing I found wasn’t on any map. The ground was blackened.

In the center stood a structure of bone and antler bound with braided strands that looked disturbingly like hair. 

Around it knelt townspeople. Faces from the BBQ. From the diner. From church. Their mouths open. Chanting. Slow. Building.

Ellen stepped forward.

Her spine arched backward with a wet crack. Her jaw split downward. Something black and viscous slid from her mouth and pooled at her feet.

When she looked at me, she didn’t look surprised.

“You weren’t meant to see yet,” she said.

Yet.

Two days later, I packed the car before dawn.

I told the boys we were going on a trip.

I didn’t wait for Ellen to come back from “keeping watch.”

I just drove and at 3:17, the dashboard clock flickered once before correcting itself, and neither of my sons blinked.
