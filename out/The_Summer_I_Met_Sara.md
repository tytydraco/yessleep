The summer before I turned fifteen, I met Sara. She was the most beautiful girl I’d ever seen, with long blond hair the color of sunshine, eyes like a springtime sky, and lips like rosebuds, soft and pink. I didn’t know it at the time, being in the closet, but I had a crush on her.

She was everything I wasn’t. Where I was chubby cheeks and stubby legs, she was lithe, graceful, elegant. Sara moved through the room like she was blessing it with her presence, like we were all lucky to look at her. We’d met at the pool locker room, me trying desperately to pull the Walmart-brand tankini my mother made me wear down over my tummy. Sara was wearing a bikini, and though we were both technically teenagers, it was obvious she was fast on her way to being a woman, while I was still stumbling through baby steps.

Sara looked up from where she was applying lipgloss in the mirror, and smiled when she saw me trying to wrestle my unruly curls into a ponytail. She offered me a wide-toothed comb, “Here,” she said, her voice soft and warm. There was innocence in it, and sweetness, but also depth, like dark chocolate filled with caramel. I took the comb, and smiled at her.

After that day, Sara and I were inseparable. We went everywhere together that summer, riding our bikes down to the boardwalk, swimming together at midnight, playing carnival games and stealing beer from my parents. I’d never had a friend before, not like Sara. Oh, I wasn’t bullied, nothing so cliché. People liked me well enough, and nobody ever said anything untoward, but I was a lonely kid.

I still am, sometimes, if I’m being honest.

But Sara changed that.

She dragged me out of my shell, pulling me into her life. She had this way about her, a kind of gravity. Like she was the sun, and I was lucky to be in her orbit. Sara could’ve made friends with anyone, like Becky G, the head cheerleader, or Danielle R, our school’s homecoming queen two years in a row. I felt chosen, silly as it seems looking back on it now.

There were…strange things about Sara I can see now, with the gift of hindsight. I don’t think I ever once saw her eat. We’d grab lunch at the food court in the mall, and she’d order and pay just like me, but my mind seems to draw a blank on if I ever saw her actually bring a bite of food to her mouth.

Sometimes, when she thought nobody was looking, she went completely still. Not just relaxed- statuesque. No fidgeting, no blinking, I don’t even think she’d breathe. Then, she’d catch me staring and flinch at me, shouting “Boo!”, and we’d laugh it off like she was just trying to scare me. Sometimes, I’d catch her looking at me with this rueful expression, like she felt guilty for something she hadn’t done yet.

I ignored these strange quirks, because I had finally found my first real friend, and everyone was a little weird. Maybe she just had a small appetite, and maybe she really was just trying to scare me. And if she looked at me, well…I wasn’t going to complain.

I introduced her to my parents a few weeks after we met at the pool, having begged them for a sleepover for weeks. They were hesitant. People had been going missing around town, too many stories of people not coming home. But I’d worn them down.

Sara looked uncomfortable standing in the doorway of our small home. My dog, an old Chihuahua named Duke, didn’t like her. The moment she stepped inside, he stood between us, barking his little head off with so much force he was practically jumping. He even snapped at her at one point, growling low in his throat.

“I’m sorry about him,” I said after I put Duke in the backyard, “it’s strange, he normally loves everyone he’s ever met,”

“He must not like my perfume,” Sara smiled, laughing awkwardly,

I nodded, leading her down the hallway to my bedroom, “Come on, why don’t we hang in my room until my parents get home? My dad is bringing home pizza, and my mom got a horror movie from Redbox! Rated-R,” I said, pushing open the door. I was a little embarrassed by the state of my room. It wasn’t messy, but there were still stuffed animals on the shelf, and we hadn’t redecorated it since I was ten. I was starting to regret that.

Sara sat on my bed, and jolted to her feet as my parents voices filled the room. They weren’t actually home- she’d sat on my old Build-a-Bear. “What’s this?” She asked, her voice soft and non-judgmental

I picked up the stuffed animal, readjusting his clothes. “This is Mr.Bear-Bear, creative, I know. I got sick when I was little, and I ended up spending weeks in the children’s ward. My parents couldn’t always afford the time off to be with me in the hospital. So they made me Mr.Bear-Bear, so that they were always with me, even when I was alone.”

Sara’s face softened, and she nodded, clearing her throat, “That’s so sweet, my parents…never did stuff like that for me,” her voice was thick, heavy. “Do you have any siblings?” She asked suddenly, an almost desperate tinge to her voice.

I decided to ignore it, nodding as I put the bear down. “My older brother, Mike. He’s in Afghanistan right now, but he’ll be home from deployment in a few weeks. I hope,”

Sara nodded. She looked like she was about to say something more, but the front door opened and cut her off.

“Julie! We’re home!” My mom called, and I smiled at Sara, grabbing her hands and pulling her to her feet.

“Come on,” I said, squeezing her hand reassuringly, “Coming!” I called to my mom.

Sara looked at my parents, smiling at them a little shyly.

“Hi, Mister and Mrs.Delgado,” she said, waving, “it’s so wonderful to meet you,”

My mother smiled, her face warm and bright, “You must be Sara, Julie’s friend. She’s told us all about you. Please, call me Maria,” my mom said, pulling Sara into a warm hug. My mom had always been a hugger, big on physical affection.

My father instead shook her hand, his large calloused palm engulfing her delicate fingers, “Just call me Gabe,” he smiled.

Pizza and soda and snacks were laid out on the coffee table, a veritable feast for any teen. Then, my mother handed me the disc, Paranormal Activity. I had been begging her for months to let me watch it.

“I don’t get how you like that scary stuff, it gives me nightmares,” she shuddered

I laughed as I put the movie into the dvd player in the living room. “That’s the point, Mama, it’s meant to be scary,” I teased her. All the while, Sara said little. As the movie started, the sun setting behind the curtains, I settled in next to her on the couch.

We shared a blanket, and I could feel the warmth of her seeping into my side. It was nice, undeniably so. As a jumpscare broke the quiet with a loud bang, I yelped, grabbing onto Sara’s arm, burying my face in her shoulder. When I had the courage to look up, I realized she was blushing. I was too.

“Sorry,” I whispered

But Sara only smiled, looking away. “It’s fine. I don’t mind,” she said softly.

As the movie ended, we switched the TV to late-night reruns of Ghost Hunters, my favorite show. It was more for background noise than anything, we were more focused on ourselves. We were giddy, giggling, talking about everything and nothing at all. I was painting Sara’s nails.

“So, you asked me about my siblings, but what about you?” I asked her, looking up. Sara’s eyebrows furrowed for a moment before she nodded, “Two, an older brother and sister. James and Amy. We don’t get along,” she said,

I nodded sympathetically, “Well, that sucks,”

Sara hummed noncommittally, “What about you and your brother?”

I smiled a bit, “Mike? He’s great. He brings me stuff from wherever he gets deployed. And I send him care boxes when I can. I hope you get to meet him soon,” I said, blowing softly on her nails to help the polish dry

Sara and I talked for a while longer about things I don’t remember anymore. Clothes, shoes, celebrity gossip that felt bigger than it really was. I only remember what happened next.

“Have…have you ever kissed anyone before?” Sara asked softly, swallowing and blushing down to her chest.

I looked up into her eyes, shocked, “I- I um, I mean- like, not really? It’s never…come up…” I stuttered, rubbing my neck. I’d had thoughts about it, seen the movies, the tv shows, all the things I was supposed to want. Every time I imagined the future, there was some faceless man filling the blank as I played the role of a Wife, Mother, etc. I didn’t know why at the time, but it always felt wrong.

“Do you…want to?” Sara looked me in the eyes, and I saw a reflection of my own yearning staring back at me. My breath caught in my chest.

“You mean…us?” I said quietly. I knew about gay people, I’d seen Ellen on tv and the parades that they threw in bigger cities, but all of it seemed so far away, and Sara was right here. She was beautiful, and soft, and sweet, and her lips looked so nice…

I didn’t realize I was leaning in until my lips touched hers. She tasted like lipgloss, and sugar, and something sharp I couldn’t quite name. She let out a sigh, deep and soft, as she pulled me closer. I lost myself in that first kiss, in her. I clung to her, liquid and warm and pliable under her hands.

I forgot everything, everything but this, the reality of her lips on mine, her hands on my body, my hands on hers. Everything else was just confetti-

Until the tip of one of her teeth split my lip. It wasn’t on purpose, but the pain broke the spell we’d woven around ourselves. I jolted back, my hand flying to my lips, and Sara gasped, covering her mouth.

“I’m- oh my god- I’m so sorry-“

I winced, looking down at the blood on my fingertips. It wasn’t a lot, but enough. “It’s fine, Sara, I’m okay,”

The blood had already stopped, and I wiped my mouth off with a slight hiss. I could still taste her lipgloss, now tinged with the metallic tang of my own blood.

After we had calmed down, we laid side by side on the floor in front of the TV. I interlaced my fingers with hers, and so many words sat unsaid between us. I thought we’d have forever to say them.

“Goodnight,” I whispered softly,

Sara squeezed my hand, “Goodnight…” she replied, her voice sweet and aching.

She was gone before I woke up, a note on the coffee table.

‘My parents picked me up early, I’ll see you soon- S’

Next to the note, she’d drawn a little heart. My mind was still reeling when my parents came out of their room and I hid the note in my lap.

“Everything okay? Where’d your friend go?” My mom asked, wrapping her bath robe tighter around herself,

“Her parents picked her up early,”

My dad nodded, checking his phone, “It is Sunday. Maybe they’re the religious type,” he shrugged.

As my father started in on making breakfast, my mother helped me clean up the living room. “Sweetheart…is everything okay at home for your friend? She seemed…distant,”

I nodded, tossing empty soda bottles in the bag, “She doesn’t get along with her parents,” I said, shrugging, “she doesn’t like to talk about it much,”

My mom clicked her tongue, “Poor thing. Well, tell her she’s always welcome here…” she paused as she got a good look at my lip

“What on earth-“

I cleared my throat “I tripped…going to the bathroom…?”

My mother gave me a look that told me she didn’t quite believe me, but let it rest.

The next day, I met Sara at the mall, locking my bike to the rack and smiling at her. She smiled back, and we walked inside. I can’t remember how the topic came up, but somehow we decided to do a Photo Booth together.

In the first picture, we’re making peace signs. In the next, duck lips.

The last one sticks out to me, even now. Sara kissed my cheek, holding me close. I could still see the shock on my face in the picture, the blush. How sweet I’d been, shy and young and innocent.

We both took a strip, smiling at the pictures. Sara had this look on her face, not quite regret, but not quite joy either. Something in between.

“My family is throwing a bonfire next week. My…my parents asked me to invite you. They really want to meet you,” she said, looking at me. I nodded, excited.

“I’m sure my parents won’t mind,” I said, taking her hand.

They, unfortunately, did mind. “A bonfire, in the woods, at night? Julie, we haven’t even met Sara’s parents! Besides, haven’t you been paying attention to the news?”

I looked up, confused, “What do you mean?”

My father rubbed his face. He looked tired, and irritated, “People have been going missing, Julia. Of all ages. Have you been so stuck in the clouds you haven’t noticed?”

The comment stung, but only because it was true. “I just- I thought- Sara wouldn’t let anything happen to me!” I said, crossing my arms defensively.

“Sara is a fifteen year old girl. She can’t protect you. You’re not going, Julia, end of story.” My father rarely put his foot down, but he wasn’t folding on this. And if this were anything else, I might’ve listened to him. I would’ve been a good girl, done what he said.

But it was Sara.

And Sara was…everything.

I still remember that night. My heart was beating out of my chest as I opened my window and popped the screen out, slowly lowering myself down to the ground. I had propped pillows in my bed, unconvincing at best. It was the tail end of summer, and the August heat was sweltering as I rode my bike to the campground Sara had told me to meet her at, smiling when I saw her waving by the sign. She took my hand when I got off my bike, squeezing it reassuringly.

She was quiet as she led me into the woods, but I chalked it up to nerves. I was babbling mindlessly about pretty much nothing, looking around. The sun had set, and I could see the first stars starting to shine as I smelled smoke.

About twenty yards away, I could see it. A large fire, and a group of people. All of them had the same blond hair as Sara, the same blue eyes, the same perfect skin. It was…unsettling.

I could feel the hair on the back of my neck sticking up, but I ignored it. I trusted Sara. She would never hurt me. I would have kept walking, if she didn’t stop.

Sara stood, her breathing heavy. I could see panic on her face, written in every muscle. “Sara?” I asked, squeezing her hand, “Sara, what’s wrong-“

Sara turned to me suddenly, and she looked different. Her eyes were wide and wild, reflecting light in a way that wasn’t quite human, “I’m sorry,” she said suddenly, her voice thick and rough, “I’m so sorry,”

“What are you talking about, Sara are you trying to scare me-“ I laughed, clinging desperately to the idea that this was all a prank, all pretend.

The desperation in her eyes told me it wasn’t.

“Julie, you-“ she swallowed, panting heavily, “You need to run, now! Go!”

Suddenly, a woman’s voice split the air, high and silver and singsong, “Sara! Did you bring your guest?” She called, and there was malice in it. Malice, and hunger.

I could hear more voices. Laughter, surrounding us. Sara’s head whipped around, her long blond hair swinging, “Julia, run! Now! Don’t look back, whatever you do!” She pushed me, hard. “Go. Go!”

Something animal in me gripped my spine, something older than the human mind. It was prey instinct, the fear of a rabbit before a wolf. I knew, in that moment, what I was. And what Sara was.

I stumbled, one step, two, before I turned and ran.

I could hear something behind me. The organic squelch of flesh and cracking of bone.

I looked back.

Sara wasn’t Sara anymore. She wasn’t human anymore.

Her limbs had elongated, stretching beyond the limits of what I thought was possible, like something within her had yanked them out of their sockets. Her hands dangled past her knees, clawed hands clutching and gripping at the air. And that face- that beautiful face I had come to love- wasn’t hers anymore. Her rose petal lips split at the edges, stretched, opened to reveal a mouth of daggers. Rows and rows of them, too many, sunk into pink gums. Her eyes had sunken into her skull to reveal deep black pits. Her skin was stretched taut, as though any movement might burst it. The only thing that was still her was that long blond hair, flowing down her back in the same waterfall of gold I had come to know.

I heard a cry of rage, and more of the creatures poured out of the clearing. They moved wrong- all impossible angles and elongated limbs, their beautiful masks discarded like trash on the side of the road.

And Sara, my Sara, the thing she had become, turned and raised her arms wide, shielding me.

“RUN!” She roared. It was guttural, thick and loud and wet. But it was her voice- her voice, from the mouth of a monster.

I ran, even as I heard the crash of bodies colliding, the tearing of flesh from bone, cries of pain and fury. Sara was holding the line, for me. She was protecting me.

But one got past.

Branches whipped my face, tearing at my clothes as I sprinted headfirst through the woods. Roots grabbed my ankles, and I had to take care not to trip. I didn’t know where I was going, only that if I stopped running, I’d die.

I could hear the creature behind me, laughing and panting, slathering over itself. “Come on little girl!” He cackled, his claws swiping at my back. The thin fabric of my hoodie caught and tore. I sobbed, crashing through the forest gracelessly.

I could see light ahead, a road. I sprinted headlong into the dark, gasping for air. I ran directly into the road, not caring if there were cars coming.

I heard the squeal of tires breaking, saw headlights cutting through the darkness, and I froze. There was a moment where I thought the truck might hit me, and that seemed a preferable fate to whatever the creature would do. Then, it swerved and struck the monster behind me instead.

He went flying into the dark, and the drivers side door opened to reveal my older brother, Mike.

“Julie?” He gasped, and I sobbed, running into his arms.

He wasn’t listening to my frantic explanation and simply shoved me in the truck as a wet growl filled the air behind us.

He reached into the bed of the truck, pulling out the shotgun he kept there. As the creature leapt, Mike didn’t hesitate. A crack split the night, and buckshot scattered the inside of the creature’s chest. It fell on the road, a wet, guttural cry filling the air.

This cry wasn’t rage or hunger. It was fear. Simple, animal fear.

Mike didn’t stop, instead he reloaded and stepped forward. The creature shuffled back on broken limbs, and it almost sounded like it was pleading. I closed my eyes and turned away as the second crack sounded.

He got in the truck, keeping the gun in his lap.

Even from here, I could see his hands shaking. “Julie, what the fuck was that?” He asked, glancing over at me as we sped through the night.

I tried to respond, but everything felt heavy. My back was wet, and warm. Mike looked over at me, turning the light on, and his eyes widened. There was so much blood everywhere. When the creature had torn my hoodie, he’d gotten skin too.

I later woke up in the hospital, needing a course of antibiotics and sutures after everything. What had happened that night trickled down to me, cleaned up and sanitized.

The Greenwood Massacre. Some wild animal had attacked a family, and a young girl had gone missing. The news and town gossips were abuzz with it for weeks, until they found some other tragedy to glut themselves on. The Greenwood Massacre became nothing but a cold case for YouTube mukbangers to ruminate on for years to come.

Mike, I later learned, had been intending to surprise us with his early return home, and it was sheer luck that he happened to be driving by when I ran into the road.

Sara was still missing, but was presumed dead. My parents comforted me, and scolded me in turn for sneaking out. Mike told the police we had been attacked by some kind of animal, a bobcat or something. But when he took them to the spot the next day, all that remained was a splotch of dark blood.

I didn’t tell anyone that I had seen Sara again. It was a few weeks later, and I was staring out my window, Mr.Bear-Bear in my arms. I’d been quiet and withdrawn since Sara’s disappearance, and everyone was giving me space. My fifteenth birthday was a few days away. I was thinking about that when I saw her.

Stepping out of the woods behind my house, illuminated by moonlight. She was human again, or at least looked like one. Her eyes reflected light all wrong, and there was dried blood and gore smeared across her mouth and chest. Her hair was still beautiful, somehow clean and untouched despite the dirt everywhere else. I pressed a hand to the windowpane, my eyes wide. But Sara didn’t come closer, she didn’t step into the light. Instead, she nodded at me once, this look like love in her eyes, and walked backwards into the dark.

It’s been fifteen years since then. I’m not fourteen anymore. I came out, went to college, I have a beautiful girlfriend I love more than anything and a good job I enjoy. I moved away from Greenwood a long time ago. But, sometimes, late at night, I think of her still. Sara, and the summer we shared. Sometimes, I swear I see her, a flash of blond hair or those springtime-sky eyes. Sara, eternally fourteen, still as beautiful as the day I met her.
