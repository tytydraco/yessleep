I’ve always been quiet.

Not shy. Not awkward. Just quiet.

I never understood why people need so much noise—music in their ears, television in the background, constant chatter filling every open space.

Silence has never bothered me.

Most people can’t stand it.

I think that says more about them than it does about me.

I’m saying this because I haven’t left my house in three months.

I locked the door the first night just to see how long it would take.

No one tried to open it.

The first month went fine.

Sun on my face. A cup of coffee. My own work, uninterrupted.

Life felt clean.

People called once a week at first. Then less. Then not at all.

It didn’t bother me.

Then one morning I looked in the mirror and my nose wasn’t there.

Not gone.

I could feel it when I touched my face.

I assumed it was the lighting. The sun can flatten things.

I turned my head.

Still nothing.

No matter how close

or how far

from the glass I stood,

it didn’t return.

I could still breathe. I could feel the bridge beneath my fingers.

I decided I could live with it.

A few days later, my mouth was missing too.

I pressed my fingers where my lips should have been. I felt them there.

In the mirror, there was only skin.

I tried to speak.

The sound felt distant.

This too, I could make do with.

There was no one I needed to speak to.

I decided to test something.

I opened my phone and held it up to my face.

Face ID failed.

“No face detected.”

It didn’t stop with my face.

After some time, my hands began to thin in the mirror.

Not vanish.

Just lighten.

As if the glass were losing interest in them.

When I lifted one, the reflection followed.

But the edges were soft.

Uncertain.

I learned to work around it.

That changed when I tried to get water.

I reached for the glass with my left hand.

My fingers closed around it.

It slipped through them and struck the counter.

I tried again.

In the mirror, my hand looked thinner than the glass itself.

When I touched it, the glass did not move.

It simply did not respond.

It occurred to me that I should step outside.

Just to be certain.

I walked to the front door.

The house was quiet.

I wrapped my hand around the knob.

It turned easily.

The door did not move.

I stood there for a while.

Eventually, I let go.

After some time, people began to come by.

At first I thought I was imagining it.

Voices in the hallway.

The door opening without resistance.

Footsteps that did not hesitate.

Friends.

Family.

They moved through the rooms carefully.

As though something fragile had been left.

My mother picked up the sweater from the couch.

She held it to her face.

I stepped closer.

I reached for her shoulder.

My hand passed through the fabric and into the air beyond it.

She paused.

Then she rubbed her arms.

“There’s a draft,” she said.

She walked to the kitchen and checked the windows.

They were closed.

I remained where I was.

The light did not bend around me.

She folded the sweater.

She placed it in a box by the door.

I tried to tell them I was still here.

The words did not carry.

They didn’t disturb anything.

Neither did I.

Typing this is becoming inconsistent.

Some keys do not register.

Sometimes entire words fail to appear.

If this posts, then something of me still does.

The house is quiet again.

It seems content.
