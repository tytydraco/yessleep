

I work as an RA over the summer in a small college town, the reason is not super relevant but suffice to say I can’t go back home anymore—though that’s also partially because “home” for me is an eight and a half hour journey back down to the other side of the state. There’s an old joke that our college is somewhere between bumfuck and nowhere. That is to say if you drove an hour out you’d touch the outskirts of the nearest city but if you walk a little ways outside of main street you’d encounter nothing but farmland. Trust me, me and a few friends back in freshman year walked as far as we could north to see what was out there. There was nothing but corn and the scariest overwhelming sense of dread, but that’s a story for another day. It’s because I had been raised in a completely different environment that so much about small town life has made me marvel at what this summer has taught me so far. The main thing being that this place is actually pretty walkable in comparison to where I’m from. 



I’m no stranger to walking everywhere, despite being 20 I still don’t have my license, however most of the time I get by back home bugging my friends for rides in exchange for gas money or stuff I would pilfer from my job at the convenience store. Unfortunately, here I don’t have the luxury of bugging my friends like it’s a typical semester. Though to be honest it’s never been much of an issue, I like walking around here. It’s a five minute brisk walk from the dormitory I manage to the main street which has a bunch of shops and food places that reminds me so much of home I have to stop myself from thinking if I turn around I’ll see the beach.  It’s real homey in the way only a college town is, the locals know me and I know them. It’s comforting and all that jazz that people like to put on slogan signs that welcome you to town. 



Yet, the biggest downside to staying in a college town over the summer is that a college town has no need to keep its shops open past 4pm on a weekday in the tail end of June. In fact even most fast food places that are in the area close at 10pm at the latest— which means when I work late night I either have to walk 3 miles up the hill or bother my roommate to drive me to the all night gas station. This summer though I couldn’t mooch a ride up the hill at 3am like I so desperately wanted. Instead I would have to walk if I wanted sustenance.



 I knew the rules, both self imposed and the ones told to me by my coordinator: don’t leave campus when you’re on call, and if you’re walking anywhere at night bring a buddy or pepper spray. You’d think that because I’m a woman in her early twenties I'd take this sage advice to heart. Unfortunately I’m the type of person to not think it was a problem until it immediately became one. I reasoned in my munchy addled brain that it was a light enough night, that the stars were out and the moon was bright enough that I didn’t really need anything other than stubborn determination to be safe. Plus, I further thought as I slunk out of the dorm, I dressed in baggy enough clothing that from a passing glance someone would think I was just a random guy. My mistake unfortunately.



The walk up the hill wasn’t bad. The roads were empty, as per normal and the last of the streetlights that lay far and few in between buzzed ever so softly behind me. I silently sent a thanks to whatever god had let the humidity finally break after a week of miserable heat. I was beginning to enjoy myself more as the first hill evened itself out and my headphones sputtered the opening notes to All I have to do is Dream, a song I loved which made me shudder for a second as I took in my surroundings in the now fully darkening road. 



The only thing that really bothered me about my walk was the graveyard. I mean truly it shouldn’t bother me as much as it did, every town has one of those graveyards. The old decrepit kind that people say is a historic hallmark but in reality is just creepy and decaying. But what was once a semi-decent looking area during the day now looked vaguely threatening after midnight. It sat right between campus and the gas station so I’d passed it dozens of times before both night and day.



That night felt different. Though I mostly  blamed it on Lucy from the Main Street cafe for my hackles rising as I speedily made my way past it. She had mentioned the other day while we were chatting over my morning death by espresso that the graveyard had once been owned by a man who held a larger estate up the hill. She had wiggled her fingers and put on a stupid croaking voice saying the estate couldn’t be sold because of the rumors that went around after the owner had up and disappeared. I shook my head as I continued to make my way 



You know that feeling when someone is watching you? I’m not talking about the paranoid kind though. I’m more so talking about the kind that hits you so hard that you start to turn around before you even realize you've made the decision. That’s why I stopped suddenly. I gave a quick look behind me, then swept around both sides of the street. Nothing. Just the dark trees swaying in the wind and the empty solemn yard of graves. I just muttered to myself “Cool. Love that.” and quickly switched to a better song.  I still hold the belief even after all this that if the song you’re listening to isn’t right, ghosts and ghouls can’t come to kill you in the night. I let Hatsune Miku take the wheel and then walked faster.



The all-night gas station sat at the bottom of the hill tucked across from an intersection of a local burger and beer joint and every so slightly down the road that led to a Denny’s turned semi-classy buffet. I liked the gas station, it was like some lonely beacon for bad decisions and way too many energy drinks. It wasn’t some typical 7-11 that you’d get in my neck of the woods, though I appreciated those too. It was big enough to sell anything from condoms to assorted dairy products.



Inside, the fluorescent lights were painfully bright to my unadjusted eyes. I took a moment to just breathe and peruse the aisles despite knowing exactly what I was gunning for. I grabbed a can of monster and a family-sized bag of chips because living on your own in adulthood meant that nobody could stop you from making the worst possible nutritional choices at three in the morning. Scary Carrie, the nickname given to my second favorite cashier, gave me an eyebrow raise, “No ice-cream? I’m shocked.”  I just shrugged and scrunched my nose, “Jeremiah isn’t here to make it.”



Jeremiah was a friend of mine who worked overnights occasionally who typically was left in charge of the ice cream counter. I liked bothering him often enough that Carrie assumed I only ever came for him instead of my daily raspberry swirl habit. She nodded and rang me out, pointedly giving me the workers discount that Jeremiah occasionally would sneak me. I smiled at her and she winked. I felt weirdly honored, there was a reason her nickname was Scary Carrie and it wasn’t because she was known for giving out discounts. I was delighted at the prospect that just maybe I had wormed my way into the little world that was the gas station crew. I decided not to push any further as I gave the older women a little two finger salute and pushed my way out to the benches to enjoy my purchase.



 I stepped back outside, I noticed someone sitting on the bench across from the entrance. A guy who had to be my age, maybe a little older. He wasn’t one of the regulars that I would see. He didn’t look like one of my residents, nor any of the townsfolk around my age that I would wander into on my daily runs, and he certainly wasn’t one of the graying or balding truckers I’d politely nod along to as they called out to me.



He looked like he’d stepped out of an old photograph and for a minute I was definitely staring. I fiddled around with my pockets for a second to search for my headphones, side-eying the man carefully. He was wearing what looked to be old definitely on their last leg jeans and a beaten corduroy jacket. Though what made me want to gape was the shagged haircut that belonged in 1974. He was cute, unnecessarily so in my opinion. 	Now, any reasonable woman would have immediately gone the other direction. I am not a reasonable woman, especially when it’s 3am and I have been starved for any cute guy my age for over a month. To be fair as well, I’m a hopeless romantic with, as my friends say, the survival instinct of a small rodent.  So instead of leaving, I sat down on the other end of the bench and cracked open my monster with what I hoped was ease and confidence.



“Nice jacket,” I said, gesturing towards him. He blinked at me and for a moment as embarrassment filled me I wondered if I should just play it off and book it back to the dorm.



“Thank you?”  His voice sounded oddly careful, like he wasn't used to being complimented. I smiled and silently rewarded myself for once not making my nightly trip in pajamas.



“Is it vintage?” I questioned, hoping his stilted answer didn’t mean a stiff conversation. I remembered that something about him seemed familiar. However in a small school that was common, maybe he did go to the college and he was just a townie. I tried to exude a mysterious vibe, thinking that just maybe a summer romance could bloom and this was a story I could shove in Jeremiah’s face.

The guy's eyes drifted toward my neck and I felt a cold pit in my stomach. My luck if the cute guy ended up being a creep. 



“I like your medal.”



I looked down and absently toyed with my necklace. My St. Christopher medal. The chain glinted under the flickering gas station lights.  
	



“Oh! Yeah, he’s the patron saint of travelers. He belonged to my Grandma.” I waved a hand. I wasn’t religious, and neither was my grandmother by the time she gave it to me, but it was my one real connection back home.   




The guy smiled at me “My mother made me wear one when I left home.”



“Oh well…” I didn’t know how to respond. “I’m not really all that religious,” 



He smiled faintly. “Neither was I.”



For a moment neither of us spoke, I felt some form of kinship with this guy. Though to be frank I’m more empathetic then I should be towards random people and things— hell I still pick up the worms on the sidewalk when it’s hot because I’m scared they’re in pain. I nodded awkwardly. The parking lot was completely empty. No cars, no passing traffic. It was all eerily quiet and it made me think of the graveyard. The only noise that I could make out as I strained my ears was the distant chirping of crickets. I took a breath and decided that no cute guy was worth getting shanked and that I’d most likely prefer not getting a netflix documentary made about me. There were already two made about this town. I didn’t need to be lucky number three. 



As I rose from the bench and chugged the last third of my drink  his expression changed. What worried me though was that he was staring past me, toward the road. The color drained from his face. 



“You should go,” he said.



“What?” I was two seconds away from shuffling back into the gas station and begging Carrie to shelter me or drive me back to the dorm. In fact I might’ve been ready to say fuck it and call my Co-RA Finn, whom I hated more than anything, to drive over and pick me up.



The man’s voice wavered as he blinked. “You should go. Right now.”



I panicked hard enough that I laughed, trying to ease the tension. The Gas station was a brisk ten feet away and somehow I was glued to where I stood.



“That's not ominous at all.”



He stood so suddenly the bench rattled. His eyes never left whatever was behind me.



“Go.”



The word came out as a whisper. I turned, fearing that I had actually made the worst mistake ever and this guy was actually whacked out on LSD or whatever drug was prone to giving vivid hallucinations. At first I didn't see anything, I heard my pulse in my ears and wondered if maybe this was actually just my own crazy dream. I’d had vivid enough dreams before. My brain took a second to catch up to my eyes before I noticed the shadow.



 stood in the middle of the road. At first I thought it was a trick of the light. The gas station sign flickered enough in the way that stretched everything into strange shapes. My brain helpfully supplied a dozen rational explanations before settling on the truth of the matter, the worst possible explanation: Someone was indeed standing there. Someone who was tall, way too tall. A totally not human tall. A tall in the way a scarecrow is tall. Long limbs. Long coat. A silhouette that seemed to bend at the wrong places. I squinted, if I had been less disturbed I might’ve cursed out the fact I forgot my glasses. 



The thing didn't move. I just watched. It looked vaguely man-like so I hoped that maybe just maybe it was that. Maybe just a fucked up man with some weird condition. Maybe that’s what spooked the man on the bench. I couldn’t help the horrible pressure that settled in my chest. Every instinct I possessed screamed at me to leave, to get to safety. To sprint and hide. I turned back to the guy.



“What is that?”



But the bench was empty. Gone. No footsteps, not a peep, the guy was just gone. I looked back towards the shadow to see it stiffly take a step forward, then more confidently it took another. The lights of the gas station flickered and despite the fact that I could’ve most likely made it back into there I turned in the complete opposite direction, away from looking at that thing. I wish I could say I acted with dignity, that I combined all my knowledge and love of horror media and made a sound decision. However, fear is unfortunately irrational.



I made a whimper noise akin to a small animal and booked it as quickly as I could. Though I was running I could hear it, the tread of boots, slow and steady. It should have been impossible to hear but I could. This thing wasn’t running but walking. That’s what scared me the most. When I had run just enough past the hill that I thought I was in the clear I had one thought: I was going to get ganked. 



It’s funny now looking back on it later but somehow in the moment when you think you’re going to die you don’t linger on the word ganked being funny. I could feel my adrenaline skyrocketing as I clutched the strap of my bag tighter, I wasn’t a good runner by any means and I had slowed down a lot as the hill took its toll. I wondered if my life was worth a family sized bag of chips and a monster. 



I couldn’t hear the boots anymore. I should’ve felt more at ease but the paranoia got worse as I began my trek past the graveyard. I started praying, I don’t know why but it felt right. It wasn’t the prayers I would say at the roadkill I passed or the shriveled worms, it was a deeper sermon. One of the few I learnt from my aunt when I was a kid. 



The temperature dropped so fast my breath fogged and I felt something grab my wrist with a cold death grip. I shrieked loudly this time before I was quickly shushed. 



“he’ll hear you!”



I stared in a dumbfounded awe as the guy from before stood in front of me, wild eyed and deathly pale. He looked like he was bleeding but from where I couldn’t tell. I racked my brain for the subpar medical knowledge I had before it dawned on me to be scared. The man shook his head at me and started leading me to the graveyard. I was too stunned to fight. I just followed. Every horror movie I'd ever watched flashed through my head. Unfortunately none of them had prepared me for being led through a graveyard while too scared to speak. I risked a glance over my shoulder again, the shadow was back and walking towards us. Just walking, like he was on a leisure stroll around town. I decided whoever this guy leading me was he was cursed and it was all my fault for thinking I could have one meet-cute in this god forsaken town.



The thing I’ve picked apart most is that when you’re being chased you’d assume that there's usually effort involved. Running, yelling, that sort of thing. A sense that if you're faster you might actually get away. However the shadow that followed us didn't seem concerned with either of us getting away as we hurried through the graveyard. It was my first time being so far into this place but in the glances I stole backwards the shadow walked with the confidence of someone following a route they'd taken hundreds of times before.The road stretched behind it in darkness. Every few seconds the moon would peak through and illuminate it just enough for me to make out details. A long coat and broad shoulders, as the minutes wore on the shape wasn't distorted anymore. That somehow frightened me more— now it looked human. Not quite but almost. The guy beside me grabbed my sleeve as I slowed a bit. 



"Don't stop."



The figure was still a few yards away, yet I found myself staring. It was the same way you stare at a car accident, or God forbid an open grave. Something in me really wanted to look. I tore my eyes away quickly and stared at the guy as we started to match pace.



"What is that?" I asked again.  
This time he answered. "Thomas."



The name hit me like the cold water from the dorm shower. I remembered Lucy’s story of the graveyard and the estate for the first time. I really looked at the man leading me. I saw things I had overlooked in my brief assessment. An awful realization began assembling itself piece by piece.



 My stomach dropped. "No."



He didn't answer. The silence was enough to spur me to continue. We kept moving while the footsteps continued behind us. They were still slow and measured, just as loud as before when I was running. Boots against gravel made my skin prickle.



Never faster. Never slower. I don't know how long we walked into the graveyard before I noticed something strange. We were moving uphill, towards an overgrown section that led far past any graves. I made the mistake of looking back again. I could make out a figure still standing lonely at the cemetery gate.   
Watching us. The distance between us had somehow increased but the footsteps continued directly behind me. I nearly tripped.



"Do you hear that?"



The guy nodded. His face had become pale enough that I could see the veins beneath his skin. "He does that."



The way he said it made my chest tighten. I had the realization that this wasn't the first time, that this wasn't even unusual for him.  




We reached what looked to be a dilapidated churchyard entrance that I had definitely only seen on maps. An old iron gate sat half-open, rusted nearly through. The little stone church behind it looked like it had been abandoned longer than I'd been alive, maybe even before my mother was. I remembered when I had been in my weird freshman research phase hearing that services hadn't been held there since the eighties. The windows were all boarded up and the bell tower leaned slightly to one side. The moment we crossed the gates the footsteps stopped. Complete and utter silence, even the crickets vanished. I took a deep breath and looked around. The night suddenly felt empty and the wrongness of everything that had been pulling on me since I walked passed the graveyard earlier washed over me.   




The guy finally released my sleeve. For several seconds neither of us spoke, I fiddled with my necklace absentmindedly once more.  
 



Quietly I heard the man’s voice, "He won't come onto church grounds."  




"Why?" I spoke softly, feeling the icy fear creep back into my veins.  




His eyes drifted toward the cemetery. "I don't know."



That answer scared me more than if he'd said demons or curses or holy ground. It meant he had most certainly tested the theory. With that the full realization settled over me slowly. I felt weirdly calm as if the immense fear and adrenaline spike had finally left me hollowed out in the absence. I took in more of the man in close proximity. There was dirt beneath his fingernails, a faint dark stain beneath the collar of his corduroy jacket. The wound I'd thought I'd imagined wasn’t a bleeding gash like I thought but a rope burn. As he stood in full light from the moon shifting his jacket I saw what it had covered in full. A dark purple band circled his throat. I took a step backward and took a deep breath. His eyes met mine. There was no anger there, no malice, just a deep exhaustion. I felt a pang of sympathy for someone who had long been dead  




"What happened to you?" For a moment I thought he wasn't going to answer. Then he sat down heavily on the church steps.  




"He picked me up outside town." The words came quietly, hushed like a prayer, fitting considering where we were. "I was hitchhiking." The shadow of the bell tower stretched across the grass.  




"I was trying to get home. He said he'd give me a ride." The guy stared toward the road, toward the cemetery's edge, toward the dark shape standing motionless beside the gate. "He seemed nice."  




I didn't say anything, I sat next to him.   




"He talked about church." His voice cracked. "We talked about good people and bad people." The shadow by the gate didn't move. "I got scared."  




There was a long pause, bile rose in the back of my throat as I remembered one of the stories Lucy had told me. How a string of hitchhikers were killed back in the early 70s, how there were four disappearances but only three bodies were found.  




"I tried to leave." The guy looked down at his dirt covered hands then at me. "He didn't like that."  




I felt sick. The silence afterward seemed endless. The shadow near the gate, Thomas remained perfectly still. Just watching. I could vaguely make out his face as the moonlight illuminated his face for a fraction of a second.  




I wish it hadn't, because he looked normal. Not monstrous or inhuman like the shadow following us had before. He looked like just an ordinary man. The kind you might pass at the grocery store or at the gas station with his old pickup. The kind nobody would ever suspect of being less than courteous. That was somehow the worst realization I'd had all night.  




The guy beside me followed my gaze. "He wasn't crazy."  




I frowned. "What?"  




"Everyone says he went crazy." The hitchhiker shook his head. "No." His voice became very quiet. "He knew exactly what he was doing."  




For the first time all night, the figure by the gate moved. Not toward us, but not away. He simply tilted its head, like the man had heard us. Almost like that thing was listening. The guy beside me immediately fell silent. For the rest of the night neither of us spoke another word. We sat on those church steps while the darkness slowly thinned. While Thomas stood at the gate watching and waiting. Patient as the graveyard stones. The strangest part wasn't that he stayed there until sunrise. The strangest part was what happened when dawn finally came. As the first sunlight touched the cemetery, Thomas turned away. He didn’t fade out like in some old movie, didn’t vanish like the hitchhiker had before, he just… turned. As if there were somewhere else he needed to be. As if his night was over.  
I



 watched as he walked deeper into the graveyard, not near the church gates but somewhere deeper past rows of leaning headstones toward the oldest section near the hill. The hitchhiker watched him go. For the first time all night, his expression softened into something more at ease. He seemed relieved for a moment. Then I blinked. And the space beside me was empty. I was alone on the church steps. The morning birds had started singing, making noise again that sounded clear through the breaking daylight. All of a sudden I heard more noise as cars moved along the road, early morning commuters through the side streets.  




The world looked normal again.  




Almost.  




When I finally stood to leave, something caught my eye. I wasn’t brave enough to go the path that the shadow that had once been Thomas went but I was too curious not to look as I shuffled past. Right near the edge of the path sat a weathered gravestone. It was half sunken into the earth, covered in a thick layer of moss. The inscription was barely legible but I could still read the date, carved in thick and bold. 1974, beneath it a name. It was the same one Scary Carrie quietly repeated to me this afternoon when I asked if she'd ever heard of a man named Thomas Warden. A name that had even Lucy go pale hearing when I pestered her for more information. It’s a name that appears exactly three times in the next town's local newspaper archives before abruptly disappearing. A name that belonged to a boy who never came home. The hitchhiker. The one I'd spent the night sitting beside in quiet terror. The one whose mother made him wear a St. Christopher medal when he left. The one who had been buried in the cemetery all along.  




The next time I pass the graveyard– even if it’s during the day, I’ll tell myself not to look too deep. I’ll repeat to myself that I might not like what I find even in the daytime. Even so when I walked to the gas station earlier, past a route way longer but that skips anywhere near the graveyard. I swear I could see someone in the reflection of my sunglasses. A young man in a corduroy jacket, not looking at me but instead watching the road. Maybe he was looking for other travelers like him, not to harm like Thomas had, but to warn them. Just like he had tried to warn me.
