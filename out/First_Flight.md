I hadn’t been back to Marrowfield in twenty-three years. Not since the afternoon my mother dragged me down the front steps with anger in her eyes and my father revved the engine like he was afraid the house might try to keep us. I told the realtor I was only here for the weekend. Sign papers. Empty what was left. Put the place on the market and be done with it.

The town looked smaller than I remembered. Streets narrower. Trees taller. The houses seemed to sag under the weight of years that hadn’t touched mine. Ours still stood at the end of the cul-de-sac, pale blue paint faded into a tired gray. The porch light was still crooked, and the attic window still faced directly toward the Shirley residence next door, so close you could lean out and almost touch their roof.

Almost.

I was wrestling with the rusted mailbox when I heard a voice.

“Daniel? Is that you?”, I turned.

Mrs. Shirley stood on her front lawn, exactly where I remembered her tending roses all those summers ago. Her hair had thinned and turned completely white, but her posture was unchanged, straight and composed, with her hands folded gently in front of her.

“Mrs. Shirley,” I said, and the name felt strange in my mouth after so long.

She smiled as though no time had passed and said, 

“You’ve grown.”

“I suppose I had to.”

She laughed softly at that. 

“You must come in for tea. It’s been so long.”

I hesitated. The house behind her looked untouched by time. Same yellow curtains. Same dark shingles. Same attic window staring at ours.

“I’d like that,” I said.

The inside smelled the same. Lavender. Old wood. Something faintly metallic underneath. The furniture hadn’t moved. The wallpaper still held its faded floral pattern. The shelves looked exactly as they had when I was eight years old, decorated with Mr. Shirley’s handicrafts. Tiny ships in glass bottles. Wooden bi-planes suspended from fishing wire. Model trains. Hand-carved animals.

My chest tightened unexpectedly.

“He never stopped making them,” Mrs. Shirley said from behind me, setting down a tray with delicate porcelain cups. “Kept his hands busy.”

I nodded, my eyes drifting toward a familiar shape on the far shelf. A small wooden plane, red cloth wings stretched tight over a delicate frame. A tiny brass propeller at the nose.

I stepped closer. It couldn’t be.

“Do you remember,” she asked, stirring her tea, “the last day you were here?”

“I vaguely remember,” I said. “We were leaving. I came to say goodbye.”

“Yes.” Her spoon clinked softly against porcelain. “Harold was very fond of you.”

Harold. I had not thought about Mr. Shirley’s first name in decades.

“He gave you something,” she continued.

The memory came back in fragments at first. The warmth of the afternoon sun. Cicadas buzzing. Cardboard boxes stacked in the hallway of our house. Mr. Shirley kneeling in his workshop, smiling as he handed me the plane.

“You take her,” he had said. “She flies better than she looks.”

I remember gripping it carefully, promising I would keep it safe. I remember wanting to see it fly before we left.

Mrs. Shirley’s voice pulled me deeper. “You were always so curious.”

I stared at the plane on the shelf.

The same red cloth wings. The same scratch near the propeller.

I had not noticed my hands shaking until my teacup rattled in its saucer.

“I flew it,” I said quietly.

“Yes,” she replied.

The memory came all at once.

I had run outside, my heart pounding with excitement. My parents were finishing the last of the packing. The car was already loaded. Then I threw the plane into the air. For a moment, it soared perfectly. The propeller spun with a soft whir as it caught the breeze.

I remembered laughing and watching it climb into the sky, gliding against the evening orange until the wind left its wings and it struck something with a piercing crack.

The flower pot.

It had been sitting in the Shirley attic window, made of heavy clay and filled with dark soil and dying geraniums. The plane struck it sideways, and one of the left wings dug itself into the brown soil.

I remember freezing with panic.

I did not want to admit I had broken something. I did not want to ruin the goodbye. So I ran upstairs to our attic instead, thinking I could reach across and grab the plane from the ledge between the houses.

The windows were so close. I pushed mine open and leaned out, and there it was, caught in the soil of the flower pot.

I stretched my arm as far as I could in an attempt to free it, but then I heard my mother’s voice behind me.

“Daniel! We’re leaving! Now!”

Her footsteps started climbing.

My heart pounded in my ears as my fingers struggled to reach the plane.

I lunged.

My fingers closed around it. To steady myself, I pressed my other hand against the flower pot perched precariously on the inner edge of the window frame.

I felt it shift, the sudden weightlessness, the scrape of clay sliding against wood.

Then came a thunderous crash. A sickening, hollow sound echoed up through the open windows.

I yanked myself back inside, unclutching the plane as I saw the pot fall past the attic window.

My mother caught me halfway down the stairs.

“What were you doing?” she demanded.

“Nothing,” I said, catching my breath.

We left minutes later, and as the car pulled away, I looked back at the Shirley residence growing smaller in the distance, fading behind the horizon.

The present returned slowly.

Mrs. Shirley was watching me over the rim of her teacup.

“You asked where Harold is,” she said gently.

I could barely breathe.

She set the cup down.

“He died that afternoon.”

The words landed softly. Almost kindly.

“There was a terrible accident,” she continued. “A flower pot fell from the attic window. It struck him at the bottom of the stairs.”

My ears rang.

“He had been calling for me,” she said. “I was in the kitchen. By the time I reached him…”

She let the sentence hang unfinished.

The house felt colder now.

“I always wondered,” she went on, her voice steady, “how that pot moved. It had been sitting securely for years.”

My mouth opened, but nothing came out.

She stood slowly. On the shelf behind her, the wooden plane seemed to stare at me.

“I found this later,” she said, picking it up carefully. “On the attic floor. It must have been blown in.”

She stepped closer and placed it in my trembling hands.

The wood felt familiar and intact, almost as if it had been untouched by time.

“I kept it,” she said. “As a reminder.”

“Of him?” I managed.

She smiled thinly.

“Of that day.”

A floorboard creaked somewhere deeper in the house. Not the gentle settling of old wood, but a distinct step.

At the base of the staircase behind her, the shadows seemed darker than they should have been.

“You know,” she added softly, “he lingered there for quite some time.”

My pulse thudded painfully in my throat.

“What do you mean?”

She tilted her head.

“At the bottom of the stairs.”

Another creak.

Slow.

Measured.

Something shifted in the darkness.

A shape, tall and narrow, just beyond the banister. Not fully visible, but present.

Mrs. Shirley’s eyes never left mine.

“He was always so proud of his planes,” she said. “He hated when they were damaged.”

The air felt thick and hard to move through.

I glanced toward the front door.

It was closed, though I had not heard it shut.

The shape at the staircase seemed closer now. One step lower, the wood groaning under some unseen weight.

Mrs. Shirley’s voice dropped almost to a whisper.

“He’s been waiting to see you again, Daniel.”

Another step.

I clutched the plane in my hands and the propeller began to spin slowly.

Though there was no wind.

No open window.

Just the quiet ticking of it turning.

And the sound of someone climbing down the stairs.
