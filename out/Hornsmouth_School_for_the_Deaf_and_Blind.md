When my mother began teaching at Hornsmouth School for the Deaf and Blind, I was eleven years old. I am seventy-eight now, and I have learned that memory rearranges things over time. But that first summer of 1959 has never rearranged itself. It sits where it always has, sharp and intact.

People speak of the deep Southern woods as if they are empty. They are not empty. They are simply not ours.

It was early spring when my mother told me we were moving to Alabama. We were from Chicago; I had never known anything but pavement and noise. My father had left us two years prior, and the city was growing harder on her. Teaching paid little and cost much. When she told me she had been offered a position at a residential school in northern Alabama, she spoke as if it were rescue.

She was a Special Needs teacher. Patient. Meticulous. She had learned Braille and American Sign Language long before it was required of her. She left me with my grandmother for several weeks that February to interview. When she returned, she said we would live on campus. It would be close to full time, day and night. I would attend school there as well, though I had no disability.

It did not trouble me much. She had already taught me Braille and enough sign language to hold a conversation. Some of my closest friends in Chicago had been her students. I was sad to leave them, but children are adaptable. Or so I believed.

When we arrived, I was startled by the size of the place. It was less a school than a small town folded into the woods. Teacher cottages lined gravel lanes. Modest homes housed students whose families lived too far away. In the center stood large brick academic buildings arranged around a square. Everything was encircled by trees just beginning to green.

It was beautiful. That was the first mistake.

I met the Beverlys my second day there.

Beverly C. was blind. Beverly H. was deaf. They were the same age, the same height, and nearly identical. Pale hair pulled tight back from narrow faces. They dressed in plain, old-fashioned dresses that made them look misplaced in time. BC was the mouth. BH was the eyes. They moved together with such fluid coordination that it was difficult to tell where one ended and the other began.

They could communicate by tracing letters into each other’s palms. I would learn that eventually. What unsettled me was how rarely they seemed to need to.

I was alone in the small field behind our cottage when a voice called from the tree line.

“Hey you.”

I turned but saw no one.

“We know you aren’t like us. We don’t want you here.”

They were perched on a thick limb about ten feet up, just inside the woods. I do not know how long they had been watching me. BC’s head tilted slightly upward, sightless eyes unfocused. BH stared directly at me.

“My mother is teaching here,” I said. “I can read Braille.”

“Because you chose to,” BC replied evenly. “Not because you had to.”

It was not shouted. It was not angry. It was simply stated.

“My name is Beverly,” BC said, touching the arm of the girl beside her. “And she is Beverly.”

“…I’m Caroline.”

“She can’t hear you, stupid.” BC said. “She is deaf.”

BH smiled faintly as if she had heard.

There was a soft creak above them.

BC’s expression changed first. Her hands searched blindly for BH’s sleeve.

“Wait,” she said quietly.

The branch split with a sharp crack. Both girls fell hard to the ground.

I remember running forward. I remember shouting for help. I remember adults rushing from cottages.

I also remember the looks.

The Beverlys told them I had been standing beneath the branch. That I had frightened them. That I had done something.

Children’s testimony carries weight in places built for their protection.

By the end of that week, I had a name.

Creepy Caroline.

I learned quickly that reputations in closed communities calcify. That name followed me into classrooms and down corridors. I told myself it would fade.

It did not.

The first time I saw what lived in those woods was four nights later.

I could not sleep. The campus at night felt different than during the day. The air grew dense. Sound carried strangely. My mother slept heavily after long days of meetings and orientation. I told myself I was simply exploring. I was eleven and convinced that curiosity was harmless.

I stepped past the last row of cottages and into the trees.

That is where I saw the boy.

He stood facing a large oak, about a hundred yards ahead, his back to me. He appeared no different than the other students. Thin frame, dark trousers, light shirt. He did not move.

“Hello?” I called softly.

No response.

I remembered where I was. School for the Deaf and Blind. He likely could not hear me.

I walked closer, careful not to startle him. The woods were quiet except for the low hum of insects. As I neared, I heard something else.

Murmuring.

Low, rhythmic, almost conversational. But not English. The syllables were wrong. Sounds slid into one another without structure. It was as if someone were imitating speech without knowing how language fit together.

I stopped.

Perhaps he was deaf and unaware of how he sounded. That explanation settled uneasily in my mind.

I shifted my weight and stepped on a twig. It snapped cleanly.

The murmuring ceased.

The boy’s head turned.

Too far.

Not quickly. Not sharply. Slowly. Deliberately. As if rotating on a hinge that allowed more motion than bone should permit.

He did not turn his body.

Only his head.

I stepped behind a tree without thinking.

From the edge of the trunk, I saw his profile.

The outline was wrong. The forehead sloped oddly. The mouth extended forward slightly. In the dim light, I saw texture where there should have been skin. It was hair. Coarse. Dark. The eye that rolled into view reflected no light at all.

It continued turning, searching.

I pressed my hand over my mouth to stop my breathing. My heart pounded so loudly I was certain it would give me away.

After several long seconds, the head rotated back to its original position. The murmuring resumed. The sounds were softer now, almost thoughtful.

I did not wait to see more.

I moved backward at first, careful, then turned and ran. I did not stop until the cottages were visible again and the lights from the administrative building cut through the trees.

I told no one.

It was hard to sleep that night, but I had to. There were only two weeks left of classes, but my mother insisted I go. Just to be introduced and make friends before Summer started. I told myself it was a trick of the light. It was just a deaf boy playing in the woods. That’s why he was murmuring. But how did he hear the twig crack?

The next morning, the boy was seated three rows ahead of me in class.

When the teacher did roll call, they would walk around the room calling out names, but tapping on the desks of students who were deaf. The teacher tapped his desk as he walked by, so I now knew he WAS deaf. 

But when the teacher called my name, he turned his head directly toward me.

And smiled.
