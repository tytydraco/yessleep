The previous post is still up. That’s a comfort amongst this. I’m still fighting. Where did I leave off?   
   
Right. Jerry’s talk. That night after his comment about squatters was horrible. I could tell that something wanted me to believe it. It wanted to distract me. Needless to say, I wasn’t blessed with much sleep. An hour or two, maximum. I was subjected to my mind and whatever told it something was in my house. Every natural creak or sound from outside granted me a chill that ran from my shoulders down to the middle of my back as the warm sensation of unease coated my chest like paste. I hid in my blankets like a child, had them pulled up over my nose so I could still see.   
   
The blue darkness of a moonlit night only made it worse. If I could see nothing, I would have been better off. If I could feel nothing, I would be better off. If I was dead, maybe I’d be better off. The fear eats at you and makes you think things like this. It lasted until a little past 5. I was hot and dehydrated from staying under the covers and refusing to get up and change the thermostat. My body didn’t sweat much, it just felt clammy, somewhat damp, and weak when I woke up.  
   
My first thought wasn’t comforting.  
   
*“Something’s different.”*

I didn’t know what it was. But something was off again, like when this all started a mere 4 days ago. Today was Friday.  
   
I peeked around corners, opened doors quieter than usual. I didn’t know why. I felt like I needed to, as if my safety depended on it. While brushing my teeth, I did it cautiously, like the toothbrush would break if I was too careless.   
   
I called in sick. I had that project, but for some reason it wasn’t mentioned when I called. Maybe it wasn’t as urgent as I thought it had been. Some minutes later one of my buddies I hadn’t seen in ages texted me asking if I wanted to hit the bar that night, it being Friday and all. I don’t remember the last time I’d seen him and happily agreed. It would be good to catch up and down a few drinks. Maybe I could forget about this nonsense for a little bit.  
   
To try and get my mind off everything *now,* however, I decided to spend this free day relaxing, but definitely out of the house as I began getting that feeling again, the one that couldn’t stand being anywhere near my home. The faintness and dread threatened me, so I quickly made my way downstairs and drank a glass of water from my replaced cups. Remembering how they changed for no reason made my skin crawl, but it wasn’t like I could do anything about it.   
   
I remembered the allergies, though this time I didn’t blame them for anything. I wanted them to be out of the question as I went about my day. If nothing happened, good. If something still happened where I felt off or something, then I knew it wasn’t the pollen. The first thing I decided to do was buy some allergy meds to conduct this experiment, but, when I opened the cupboard, there were already some there. I didn’t, and don’t, remember where I bought them from.  
   
Along with another glass of water, I downed two pills and left the house. Once outside, I got in my car and immediately plugged my phone in to play some music. It helps me calm down. As I drove away, I suddenly had the idea that maybe, if someone *was* in my house and I didn’t know it, I could catch them if I parked a way up the road and walked back. At first I denied the idea, but something told me it was a good one and I should try. Better safe than sorry, right?  
   
I drove around the neighborhood, then parked. I got out, locked the car, and started my walk. I didn’t seem to have a choice. I also recalled forgetting something in my room. I didn’t know what it was, but I knew I’d forgotten it, so this trip really had two purposes.  
   
From my understanding, if you’re living in someone’s home, you would try and grab what you could before sneaking back into the attic as quickly as possible. This meant you would have to check the windows first, which, if someone was in my house, they definitely would have done already. My logic also reasoned that a squatter would have to be extra cautious of the front door, so, in my re-entry, I would sneak through the *back.*  
This I did, quietly placing each step in front of the other with a gentle heel-to-toe stride. Once I was at the back door I put my ear against it, listening. I only heard white noise and the oceanic sound of blood rushing through my body. Even still, I quietly retrieved my keys from my pocket and inserted the correct one into the lock. I turned it, ever so carefully, until the mechanism gave way with a satisfying dull click and unlocked the door and I was able to get inside.   
   
My back door is close to the kitchen, so if anyone was in it, they would have definitely heard the door and panicked. This didn’t happen. Instead, I saw that everything in my kitchen, to my knowledge, was as I’d left it. I checked the few rooms there were on the main floor and went upstairs. I talked loudly, calling out ridiculous phrases to abate my fear that someone was hiding around each corner.  
   
“Hey! I know you’re here!” I yelled. “Just come out, I won’t hurt you! I just want you out of my house!”  
   
There was nothing in the bathroom or the spare bedroom on the second floor. I entered my room and found nothing. I looked around, then jumped out of my skin as I heard my phone ringing. I turned and saw it on the bed. I sighed with relief and went to pick it up.   
   
“Hey man, you still good for tonight?” said a familiar voice when I accepted the call.  
   
“Riley?” I asked.  
   
“Yeah dude.”  
   
“It’s been a while, how – how are you doing?”  
   
“Good, man. Just wanted to catch up and see how *you’re* doing. One of my buddies is going to Grinder’s tonight and asked me to tag along. That used to be the spot.”  
   
I hadn’t heard that name in a long time.  
   
“That sounds great. Thanks for the invite. I’ll be there,” I answered, still catching my breath.  
   
“Sick. See you there.”  
   
He hung up and I pulled the phone away from my ear. I slipped it in my pocket and decided there was no one home. I also felt a swirl in my chest and didn’t want to feel it, so I left in a hurry. It was like the house wanted to tell me I’d forgotten my phone and called me back, only to shoo me out once I’d found it.  
   
Of course, I didn’t forget it. I brought it with me when I got in my car and left it there when I came back. But this never crossed my mind until I got back in the driver’s seat and remembered how I’d been listening to music on the short drive.  
   
I audibly gasped and dropped the phone then picked it back up and surveyed every detail of it. It was the same, but... new. Unlike the glasses, the same. But like the glasses, replaced.   
   
Something was learning. And it was learning quickly.  
   
The dread built and only receded as the distance between me and my house grew greater.   
   
I stayed out the rest of the day, thinking. I’ll spare you the panicked ramblings my head became captive to. They were mostly the same thing round and round and round.  
   
I drove until I reached a coffee place I hadn’t tried before by the name of Weave. I wanted something I didn’t know. Something that, if it changed, I wouldn’t notice. I needed a break. Why not a coffee break as a precursor to the beautiful alcohol I’d partake in later? I couldn’t wait. It’s selfish, but I was more excited for the drinks at this point than catching up with a good friend.  
   
Inside the shop, I ordered a chai latte. The lady at front taking my order was extremely attentive to me, but I wasn’t in any mood for starting anything.   
   
“Can I get a name for the order?” She asked, pushing her shoulder-length dark hair behind her ear as she retrieved a permanent marker and took the cap off.  
   
“Daniel... Dan,” I stuttered.  
   
She smiled as she wrote my name. “Been a while?” she asked.  
   
“Huh?”   
   
“Since you’ve been in here. Been a while.”  
   
I gave her a puzzled look. “I don’t think I’ve been in here before. Isn’t it sort of new?”  
   
“Sure,” she laughed softly. She put the cup down and placed both hands on the counter, ever so slightly leaning forward. “Thanks, Dan. We’ll have that out soon.”  
   
I thanked her, paid, then sat down at a table.   
   
“Odd,” I muttered.  
   
Instinctively I pulled out my phone, but not to scroll on it or kill time. I was trying to find imperfections. Unfortunately, I had a case, and I hadn’t dropped my phone before buying it, so it was hard to find anything. While there was some dust, that was expected. Again, the only thing I could notice that felt different was just the newness of it. I can’t explain it any other way. It was just new. Worn, but new, like it had been restored then sprinkled with dust to simulate ages of use.  
   
“Dan?” called a gentle voice.  
   
I looked up and vaguely saw the head of the woman who served me through bottles of syrups lined up by the espresso machine on the desk. I turned and saw someone else at the register, taking an order.  
   
I sighed and walked over. I smiled politely, took my cup, and said thank you. She merely nodded once with a look on her face like she wanted me. It was the eyes. Considering my luck with women, this was the most forward anyone had ever been, and last week I would have been into it. But my head was messed up, and I didn’t want to think about anything else, least of all a relationship or whatever she wanted with me. My sole focus was to figure out just what in God’s green earth was happening.  
   
I didn’t stay there and drove around aimlessly.   
   
At one point I stopped by a park to take a walk, but the silence was too loud. I went into a library, but the book names were making me think, and I didn’t want to think, so I went and saw a movie. It was some stupid comedy I would have scoffed at any other day. But I tried to laugh at it and feel happy. In the afternoon I ate lunch at a Mexican place and ordered a margarita. Drank it down. Felt a little better.  
   
And at 8 o’ clock, after trying to sleep in my car in some empty parking lot I found in a complex of buildings, I drove to Grinder’s.  
   
Riley was there, along with another guy named Chris, and I felt a little better as we caught up. He’d been working a construction job for the past few months and hated it, but the pay was good. I updated him on my job, overexaggerating the bad parts of it and underselling the good parts. I told him about the project I had on my plate and how it was mostly on me to get done.   
   
“Hate when that happens,” he said, taking a swig from his second Dragon’s Milk. Despite his rough exterior, the guy had taste. “Feels like the moment you do a good job they get all on ya. They want ’cha to take more responsibility.”  
   
“Exactly,” I said, sipping a whiskey. I know mentioned looking forward to the alcohol, but more than panicked and afraid, I felt depressed. I’d drink what I enjoyed and then drink another. I’d take a shot if someone asked me to, but I wasn’t in the mood for anything. I was starting to grow numb. I didn’t care anymore.  
   
Chris was shooting his shot with some woman who looked like bad news on our left, and after watching his half-drunken efforts for a few minutes, Riley turned to me, emptied the bottle and asked, “You seeing anyone, Dan?”  
   
I shook my head. “Nah. Not right now.”

“It’s been a year, man, you’re still not over her?” Riley laughed.  
   
“Who?” I asked.  
   
“You don’t remember?”  
   
I shook my head again, taking another sip of my drink. “Can’t recall having a girlfriend.”  
   
At this he laughed again, louder. “Man, you’re telling me – I guess. She was a bit of a handful, anyway. Beautiful as hell, though. I don’t blame ya for not remembering. Don’t remember half of the women I’ve had.”  
   
I frowned. He, at least, what I remember Riley to be like, wasn’t this flippant about relationships. Guess the stereotypes about construction guys was mostly true. Maybe it was just the beers. There was a reason he never drank harder liquor.   
   
But what surprised me the most was the fact that I’d forget about having a girlfriend. I wouldn’t put it past me, all that’s been going on, but it feels too integral. Why would I forget my first girlfriend, as, in my mind, this would’ve been the first?  
   
“How long ago did I know her?” I asked.  
   
“Like I said, ‘bout a year.”  
   
“Who was she?”  
   
“Hell...” Riley cursed and thought for a moment. “She uh, she had brown eyes, long hair. It was almost black, I think.”  
   
He squinted his eyes and looked up. “Quiet. Didn’t say too much unless she was talking to you.”  
   
He looked over at me. “I think that’s what made you two so good together. You made her light up.”   
   
“Why’d we break up, then?” I was dying for details.  
   
“Don’t recall that part as much,” Riley said, playing with the empty beer bottle in front of him. “Just remember you telling me you’d split up.”  
   
There was a silence. I finished the last of my whiskey. Ordered another.  
   
“She worked at a coffee shop,” Riley said suddenly.  
   
“What?”  
   
“Yeah, a little one. Not too far from here. Has a funny name. Reminds me of baskets, ha!”

*“No...”* I thought. *“There’s no way, is there... of course there is... but I’d never been there! That’s why that girl was acting so weird. Did we break up on good terms? What happened?”*

“Did I meet her there?” I asked.  
   
“You sure you don’t remember her at all?” Riley exclaimed. “You hit your head, Dan?”  
   
I shrugged. “I dunno. I just... I don’t remember her... guess she wasn’t good when we got down.”  
   
It was a crude joke; one I wished I didn’t make. It was out of character for me. I just wanted to say something to make him think I wasn’t crazy, and I wanted to be the one to ask all the questions.  
   
He laughed aloud, regardless. I tried to as well but just felt sick. This was all worse than I thought.   
   
   
The rest of the night was as you’d expect. We drank some more. Chris convinced us both to take a few shots. We laughed. Reminisced about college days, which, thankfully, I seemed to have remembered correctly. Someone started singing and we, along with others who had their inhibitions dampened, sang along. It got late, we said our goodbyes, but before Riley could leave, I asked a final question.  
   
“Hey!”  
   
Riley stood by the driver’s side of his car. The door was open and he was fiddling with his keys. He looked up.  
   
“What was her name?” I called.  
   
He smiled and cursed. Thought for a moment.  
   
“You get your head checked, you hear me, Dan?” He yelled.  
   
I nodded playfully, laughing along with him, having disregarded any of my situation being medically associated.   
   
“Can’t believe you don’t remember your own girlfriend’s name,” he continued. “Do I gotta do everything?”  
   
I raised both my hands in an exaggerated shrug. “I guess so!”  
   
He bent over in laughter. “Willow, Dan,” he said finally. “Her name was Willow. How do you forget a name like that?”

I spent the weekend at home, but not entirely inside. There was no way I could do that. Every few hours the nausea and dizziness took over, like a hangover that refused to leave. I wasn’t hungover on Saturday. The day started out fine. And then the now-familiar dread hit. This time, though, it was so overwhelming to a point where I sprinted downstairs and ran out the door. It was random, too. I tried sitting in different rooms, but that didn’t matter. Eventually I felt that thing pushing me to leave.

I know I said the main symptoms were nausea, dizziness, and dread, which is enough to make me try and find comfort, but there was something else as well. It was *wrong* to be inside when that feeling hit. Like I didn’t belong. Like it wasn’t my house. Like *I* was the intruder, not the entity I was convinced lay in wait around each corner.

Yes, I was convinced. There was no mold and allergies weren’t a concern. Whatever was happening to me, I couldn’t take it anymore. So after a few rounds of running for the door, vision blurry, gag reflex pulsing at the back of my throat, waiting, then going back inside only for the cycle to repeat, I stayed put where I knew I would feel okay. Outside.   
   
At times I ran inside for a glass of water or some food, hoping I could move fast enough to get it outside without the feeling. I only succeeded in this once. Every other time I barely made it, gagging at the sink or tripping at the threshold of my front or back door while clutching a glass of water that wasn’t mine in one hand and a collection of random snacks in the other while my vision blurred and my head swirled like I was knocked off my feet by a wave of neurotic dread and cataclysmic ponderings on life and identity itself.  
   
It was pathetic. What would people think of me if they saw me in this state? Was I the one that was crazy?   
   
No.  
   
I was certain at this point. This wasn’t me. I hadn’t done anything different since Monday, which was only 5 days ago. It felt much longer. But questioning anything only seemed to make it worse.  
   
Of course, I had work this coming Monday, and I wasn’t above sleeping outside, especially now. But I thought to myself once it started getting dark, *“What’s the harm in trying one more time?”*  
\* \*  
I was sitting in the backyard, staring at where the grass started and the concrete ended. An empty glass of water sat beside me, crumpled wrappers stuffed in my pocket as a sickening sweetness stuck to my stomach; the lack of real food gnawing at my core and begging for something substantial. I didn’t want to go inside. But I wanted to sleep. I had work in the morning. My phone was upstairs. I couldn’t call in sick again, not with that project looming over my head, as unimportant as it seemed now. I couldn’t lose this job, it paid too well.   
   
The sunset was gone by now, a blue darkness coating the sky. It was beautiful, looking back. But at that time, I despised it. I was angry that the day decided to end, that it grew dark and signaled a time of rest when I couldn’t. I needed relief.  
   
I could drive to a friend’s house, but who knows? Maybe they wouldn’t recognize me. Maybe they’d turn out to be someone I wronged but don’t remember how. I wondered if something at work had changed. What if something changed and I don’t remember how? Like it was wiped from my memory the same way my apparent girlfriend had been.   
   
I’d been trying to remember all day.   
   
The first thing that disturbed me was, besides the loss of memory, the fact that I was dead set on the fact that Willow was my deceased cat, which, for whatever reason, had no evidence of ever existing in my house. Yet I remembered her.   
   
Riley said it’d been a year. The timeline was too specific to be a coincidence. And why on earth would I name a cat after a girlfriend? I wouldn’t. That’s just it. I wouldn’t do that.  
   
I swear, I had a cat. She was one of my best friends. I have no memory of a human named Willow, much less a woman I was romantically involved with.  
   
No, I remember Willow the cat. She would... she was a small tortoiseshell with black and brown fur. She liked to meow a lot. Always sat very quiet when it was time to feed her, waiting patiently, like all her vocal tendencies were only meant to ask for food.   
   
But I can’t be sure of that, can I?  
   
I wanted to remember something real.  
   
This uncertainty, this questioning, this unease of not knowing what was real anymore, it wore me down to a point where the mixture of that and the creeping darkness turned me dramatic. I hated living. I hated the thought of having to run and question and second-guess everything I thought I knew. As the warm night enveloped me, I went back inside.  
   
I made it upstairs and lay in bed. Now that I thought about it, the feeling never came at night. Maybe it was sleeping. Regardless, I took the pocketknife from my drawer in case it came back and held it close to me. It was a drastic step, I know, but I was exhausted and my judgement became clouded. I didn’t want to die, not really. I just wanted to feel normal again. But there was something comforting about clutching the knife. Maybe it was the illusion of choice. If I really wanted to end it all, I could, even though I’m sure I wouldn’t. Whatever it was, the cold steel warmed against my chest until morning.   
   
Monday arrived and the night went smoothly. I went to work like usual. The feeling hadn’t shown up, not even a little. Maybe it’d left the house for a while. But as I drove to work, something snapped. Right outside the door I felt a wave of nausea and dizziness wash over me.  
   
“No!” I panicked, hyperventilating and trying to shove it away somehow. Tears formed in my eyes as the one place I’d felt normal in throughout this whole time, this building, turned hostile and foreign. It pushed me back a few steps. I pushed forward, then gagged. I moved back, and a tangible feeling in my chest pressed me further, almost to the sidewalk.   
   
As I bent over and felt my knees give way, it all vanished, like it’d moved straight through my body. I’d never felt it do that before. Up till now it was just strange sensations of being moved or needing to move, but never like a force had injected itself into my core, moving through every fiber of flesh before leaving me and rushing away. That weighty dread was too real, and, not knowing what else to do, I rushed inside and walked to the elevator, rudely ignoring reception and wiping tears from my eyes like a lost kid at an amusement park.  
   
Once the doors closed in front of me I crumpled pathetically and allowed myself a brisk 15 second sob. It’s a good thing no one needed the elevator on my way up. 

Despite the feeling moving in then out and away from me, I didn’t feel better. It must have left something in me, like a disease of small dread. A little twinge. Smoke from a match that never dissipated. A spot of black paint on a colorful painting of spring.   
   
I walked to my desk in a daze, but not before visiting the bathroom to ensure I looked okay. I had to start on that project today. That was when I noticed how I’d forgotten my daily ritual, if you will. Usually, I go through a few steps to begin the workday, but today I couldn’t remember what they were. My office looked strange. The window was wrong. I didn’t know what it was, but the window was wrong. The keyboard was wrong. Too new, like the phone, but with a faux type of wear on it.  
   
The feeling had been in here. Changing things.

The task on my screen was wrong, so I didn’t do it. The project, though. I remembered that. But it had disappeared from my list, both sticky notes and digital notes. The task was gone. I searched everywhere. But I couldn’t find it.   
   
“Of course,” I whispered to myself. “Why the hell not?”  
   
“Hey, Dan,” said someone. I didn’t recognize him, but he seemed to know me.  
   
“Yes?” I asked.   
   
The man just stood there, and I blinked. He blinked as well. After a moment he said, “Are you done with it yet?”  
   
“Done with what?”  
   
“Come on, man, the Steeple invoice. I’ve been waiting on it for two weeks and you said you’d have it by last Friday.”

I had no idea what he was talking about.  
   
“W-what?”  
   
“I need it by end of day,” he said, irritated, and sighed before leaving.   
   
I felt hot and like I might start sweating. I put my head down and resisted another panic that came with tears. Not at work. I tried breathing exercises and instinctively rubbed the back of my neck and then my chest, trying to push the fear away. Got up and started walking to the kitchen for some water, then sat back at my desk and got to work on that invoice I’d apparently forgotten about.   
   
The other tasks, thankfully, went smoothly, and no one made a single mention about the Kellis account. Maybe someone else was handling the beginnings of it, and I wasn’t about to complain about that. I did wonder what happened to it, though, and watched with curiosity as people began filing out of the office, packing up their things, stretching and saying “good evening” to their desk neighbors. It was early and a Monday at that. I knew the project needed some extra hours from the team, so let alone leaving the office early.  
   
“Lighter day?” I asked a passerby as she walked past my office.   
   
“First in a few weeks,” she responded, pulling the keys from her bag and clutching them in her fist. “Have a good evening, Dan!”  
   
I didn’t recognize her.  
   
I went to find Jerry and felt relieved that he was in the same spot, zipping a laptop carrier closed and sliding it into his backpack.   
   
“What’s up, man,” he said, turning around as he heard me approaching. He chuckled to himself. “Find any squatters?”  
   
I mentally sighed with relief. He remembered the conversation.  
   
“Have you heard anything about Kellis today?” I asked, cutting straight to the point.  
   
“What?”  
   
“Kellis. The Kellis account. That big project I was supposed to be handling. Any update on that?”  
   
“Are they new?” he said after thinking for a moment.  
   
“Yeah.”

“I guess I haven’t heard of them yet, but...” He slung his backpack over one shoulder and motioned his hand to one side dismissively. “That’s Monday’s problem.”  
   
“You haven’t heard of them?” I asked.  
   
He shook his head.  
   
“We just talked about them the other day.”  
   
Jerry shrugged. “I dunno.”  
   
“Come on, man, stop...” I clenched my fist and opened it again, flexing my hand at my side. I looked down. Cursed harshly.  
   
“You okay?”  
   
I nodded.

Slowly, I turned to go. My eyes felt hot as I packed my things and made for the elevator, feeling utterly alone, crazy. I didn’t like dealing with it anymore. I didn’t like growing used to it. I wanted it over. I wanted it done, and as the car door unlocked, I decided that once I got home, if nothing changed or got worse, I would stop this. I really did think about it. Of course, I didn’t really want to die, but I was hopeless now, and I felt like nothing could change that unless everything went back to the way it was. 

Like Scrooge on Christmas Day, I would be thankful. I’d give anything for the stress of everything I used to stress about. Seemed like nothing now. So small and easy to dismiss. Back then I’d complain about them, waiting for the day to be over so I could sigh with annoyance and talk badly about the day.   
   
I used to hate thinking about how alone I’d feel sometimes, my family being a state away. But I realized now that I had friends and if I really wanted to visit my family the drive wasn’t so bad.

What I’d give to feel normal.  
   
I pulled my car into the wrong street and knew I’d find my house in the middle of the court. It was there and I wasn’t surprised to see it. I drove into the driveway and saw Willow prancing around the corner of the house. I parked and went inside. She followed then ran upstairs. I was intrigued, not because I felt nervous, but because I knew I owned a cat. She was at the top of the stairs like she always did and used her little head to push the door open. I smiled and then entered my room, immediately falling forward as the strongest episode of the feeling yet overtook me. I gagged as my head spun, landed on my hands and knees, and waited. I knew it had to give. Nothing asked me to leave. Nothing. The feeling absorbed me, stayed for a moment, asking nothing more of me than to be still as the nausea faded, and then left.  
   
I coughed. Coughed twice, then stood up. Willow ran into the closet. I looked around and felt slightly out of place. Every object, everything I owned, it all looked... the same. But it wasn’t. They must have been replaced. Everything I owned had been replaced with an exact copy. They came new, but with all the flaws intact. Why had the objects stayed replicated but other things in my life had changed altogether?

I couldn’t make sense of it. Maybe the feeling liked what I had.  
   
But in an instant, something like clarity or memory, I can’t discern which, flooded my mind. I rushed over to my computer and quickly turned it on, repeating the same thing over and over so I wouldn’t forget. The feeling could make me forget, but I tried everything I could to keep it at bay.  
   
I went to my first post, reading the entire thing top to bottom.   
   
I wish I’d added more detail, like how I bought allergy medication during that first day since I know I did that. How else would I have it in my cupboard? How else would I have taken it the next day with that glass?  
   
I don’t recall going to the store. But I bought them all the same. Somehow.   
   
Why was I afraid of the glasses? A quick search just now shows the glasses I’d always had, same name, same everything as they’ve always been. I checked downstairs just to be sure, and I didn’t feel afraid of them. They’re just my normal glasses. I noted in my post how I remembered owning glasses that were “smaller” and “more squat.”   
   
That can’t be right. I must have just forgotten. Maybe I’d been panicking that entire day for nothing. Checking my phone also confirmed that I did indeed have photos of Willow. Silly ones, ones of her being cute when she’d fallen asleep in funny positions. The same as any cat owner would have. 

I really was off my rocker this week. Why would I delete photos of my supposed “late cat?”  
   
I really was crazy. The more I think about it, maybe I’m getting better.

In an instant everything went black and I was immediately where the other me stood, looking at where my previous self would have been a millisecond ago. But all *that* nonsense was over.
