My Roommate is a Huntsman: Part 2

[Part 1](https://www.reddit.com/r/nosleep/comments/1rz6n1y/my_roommate_is_a_huntsman/)

To recap; I got a new roommate. It’s name is Mary and it is a huntsman spider that somehow knows how to clean and make a grilled cheese sandwich. As far as how it learned to talk and do all the other things that a spider shouldn’t know how to do, I guess it learned from watching tv. If I had to be honest though, that doesn’t completely make sense to me. I have since given up on trying to understand the whole situation. When I came home that Monday and saw that it wasn’t dead after the maintenance crew sprayed my apartment, I had to accept the fact that this was going to be my life for the foreseeable future. As long as it didn’t disrupt my personal life and kept its end of the bargain that we agreed on, there was going to be no problem. Or so I thought.  

Mary did not sleep in my room. I wouldn’t have allowed it if it insisted. Instead it slept in a crawl space that was accessible in the laundry room. That was fine with me. I half expected that it would want to sleep in the same bed as me. Boy, I was glad that was not the case at all. Mary kept to itself most times and just did the chores around the apartment. I would come back from work and it would already be making me food and a drink would be ready for me on the counter. Sometimes, it was juice and on Fridays it would be a chilled beer. I didn’t think about where it was getting all these items, I just went along with it. I realized after a time that I made less trips to the market, food was always readily available at home. The beer on Fridays, admittedly, was a nice touch. At the end of the night I would turn on the tv and Mary would hang in front of the screen and just watch whatever was on. I would just sit on the couch and study the creature.

 If I was going to live with a huntsman spider I needed to do some research. After a bit of online searching, this is what I found. Huntsman spiders are native to Australia. Not sure how this spider found its way to LA but this was already an unusual situation so I didn’t question it. Huntsman spiders are not dangerous to humans. Although they are venomous, their bites do not kill and at most will leave a bump. Looking at the size of Mary’s fangs I did not feel comfortable wondering if it would leave a bump. Their diet consists of bugs and some small lizards. Its favorite food seems to be cockroaches. I suppose having a huntsman spider living in a roach infested apartment building is a good thing.  Here's another fun fact; the average length of a huntsman spider is around twelve inches. 

Curiously,  I got up, grabbed a measuring tape, and approached Mary. 

“Excuse me, do you mind if I measure your length?” I asked wearily. 

“*I don’t mind at all. What would you like me to do?”*

“Just stay there in front of the tv. I will be quick.” 

Mary stayed suspended in front of the television as I extended the tape rule vertically across its body. I estimated around fifteen inches when I first saw Mary in the kitchen, which is already bigger than the average huntsman spider, but not by much. I looked at the measurement and was shocked at the results. Mary was around twenty inches. I closed the tape measure and returned to my seat on the couch. 

“That's all I needed, Mary, thank you.” 

“*My pleasure.”*  It responded sweetly. 

I looked at the google search on my phone and was surprised that my fun fact was wrong. The average size of a huntsman spider is not twelve inches, that was the largest length in recorded history. Mary is bigger than the biggest huntsman spider. 

I continued my research. I was looking for the most important piece of information, even more important than if it was dangerous to humans. What was the life span of  a huntsman spider? I found my answer; it's between one to two years. Now this begged the question; if Mary is bigger than the biggest huntsman spider known to man, will it live longer than the average huntsman? Is Mary even a huntsman spider to begin with? 

That last thought was the most frightening of all. I closed out of the google app and drifted to my room, beer in hand. 

“*Are you going to sleep?”* Mary asked as it turned towards me. 

I looked back at it and a shiver ran up my spine. 

“Yes, I feel a little tired. Thank you for the beer, Mary.” I tilted the bottle towards it.

“*No problem.”* 

“Also, where did you get this? I haven't bought beer in weeks.” 

Mary rubbed her feelers together and said; “*Mr. O’Neal, room 203, He has a lot and so I took one*.”

“Is that what you do with the ingredients for the grilled cheese as well? The chives?” I asked in surprise. I mean, I should have known that it took these items from someone if not from me. 

“*Yes. Is that bad?”* it asked, now no longer rubbing its feelers. 

I thought about it. This last week had been very easy on my wallet and, in all honesty, Mr. O’Neal could go without a few beers. If it wasn’t hurting anybody, I didn’t see why I shouldn’t allow it. As long as Mary didn’t hurt anyone for these items, I supposed it was okay. 

“No, not at all.” I responded and tried to crack a smile. “Just don’t get caught and I’m sure it's fine.” 

“*Understood. Goodnight.”* It went back to the TV and continued to rub its feelers together. I think it was satisfied with my answer. 

It went like this for weeks and, for a time, it was like having an eight legged maid. I didn’t bother trying to clean up after myself anymore. I left my clothes on the floor all the time, I hadn't dusted the shelves in ages, and I rarely picked up my plate after eating a grilled cheese. Mary took care of all of it without any questions. It really did work hard. 

The life span thing really did make me think. It is clearly not a normal spider. If it is expected to be twice as big in size it may live twice as long, maybe even three times as long. I may not be able to get rid of it for another six years. 

Bringing friends over was out of the question. They already thought that I was weird for mentioning the fact that there is a talking huntsman spider in my apartment. I explained everything to them and all they did was look at me as if I said a long-winded, over-drawn joke. After some time, they became distant and hardly talked to me. I’m sure they didn’t want to talk to a crazy guy. Except, I'm not crazy. Mary is real and it really does make a damn good grilled cheese. I hate to harp on that fact, but I’m sure you would also be very surprised (delighted even) with a well made sandwich from an overgrown spider. You would be lying to yourself if you said otherwise. 

Even though the number of friends was waning by the day, I was getting used to having Mary in the apartment. However, that was short lived.

In time, it wasn’t just the bugs that began to disappear. 

In the lobby of the apartment there is a community cork board where tenants are allowed to post things like events or promote their small business. It was free for anyone to use as long as they didn’t post anything obscene. There was a man in the apartments named Larry who would try to post things about recent alien sightings or paranormal witch hunts in his home-made newsletter. Those were usually taken down  as quickly as he put them up. Every once in a while I would get mail from Larry informing me that I have inalienable rights from our founding fathers and that there is a movement forming to take down the tyrannical management of the apartments for impeding on his first amendment right to free speech. I usually just throw them away. Rarely do you see missing pet posters on the cork board. Pets rarely go outside unless it's Miss Susie walking her multi prize winner terrier. What prizes may those be, I have no idea. She just likes to spout on about how it's a pure breed and how its diet is finely crafted for a sleek coat and healthy teeth. That prized terrier is now on a missing pet poster on the community cork board, along with several other posters displaying missing pets. 

“Someone broke into my apartment and stole Peanut!” Miss Susie yelled at Barb, who was barely keeping her eyes open. 

“I will assure you, Susie, we will do our best to get down to the bottom of it. Did you notice anything else? Was the lock broken or the window smashed?” 

Susie breathed heavily, tears fell freshly down her round face.

“I LOCKED MY DOOR!” she yelled with the intensity of an opera singer. “No windows were smashed! Everything seemed to be in order until I saw that Peanut was not in her crate! Whoever the dog thief is, they broke in without breaking a lock or smashing a window.” 

A heavy sob escaped Miss Susie. I felt genuinely bad for her. I couldn’t imagine Mary being the culprit. All she ate was bugs. Then again, the possibility is there. I would have to ask about the terrier. 

Miss Susie looked over at me in the lobby. I was in the middle of throwing away this week's issue of Larry’s newsletter.  

“Jules, Did you see anything? You live down the hallway from me. Please, tell me you caught a glimpse of what happened?” Miss Susie begged for answers that I couldn’t give. This was awkward. 

“I’m sorry I don’t understand what's happening.” I responded regretfully. 

“I was just out at the super market to grab some groceries just now. I ran out of chives again and I needed to restock on some cheese.”  She steadied her breathing to tell the rest of the tale. “When I came back, the apartment was clean and my sweet Peanut was out of his crate, nowhere to be seen.” She sniffed and began to sob again. “Someone stole my sweet puppy from right under our nose and no one has seen a damn thing!” 

Miss Susie swung around to Barb and pointed a finger in her direction. 

“IF I DON’T GET MY PEANUT BACK I WILL HOLD YOU ACCOUNTABLE, BARB!”  

“I didn’t steal your fuckin dog, Susie.” Barb clapped back while also handing Susie a tissue. “Besides, my little kitty wouldn’t like a stinky dog. Like I said before, sweety, I didn’t see anyone go through that looked suspicious. Just Larry. But, that crabby bastard wouldn’t steal a dog. Best we can do is call the police and maybe they could see about dusting for prints or something.” 

“When I find out who took my Peanut, I will sue them for all their worth! I will break their legs and feed their entrails to sharks! I’ll-” 

That's around the time I hurried up to my room. 

Mary was in the middle of humming a tune while dusting some shelves when I came through my front door. Three flights of stairs caught up to me as I gasped for air. After I was able to breathe again, I approached Mary. It paused its dusting and humming to turn to me. 

“*Welcome home.”* 

“Did you see a dog at all today?” I asked. My voice was hoarse from still being slightly out of breath.

*“Yes.”* 

I paused. It answered without hesitation. 

“Did you… do anything to it?”

“*I took it*.”

“Where is it, Mary?” 

“*The crawl space.”* 

Oh God. 

“Well, can you release it? The neighbor is trying to sue the person who took her dog.” 

Mary placed the duster on the shelf and looked at me. It hesitated for a second. 

“Mary? Do you understand me?” 

“*Are you sure you want me to do that?”* 

“Yes, please. I need to return Peanut.” 

With another pause, Mary reluctantly made its way to the crawlspace in the laundry room. I followed it and watched as it entered a big hole in the ceiling. Sounds of rummaging and constant movement could be heard from the hole. I started to worry a bit. 

“I greatly appreciate this, Mary. The last thing I need is for the neighbor to suspect something.” 

More shuffling and then a dragging sound approached the hole. I stood back to give Mary some space. Then…

PLAP! 

A mass of flesh fell through the hole. White, filthy fur was present and two beady eyes stared back at me with looks of anguish and fear. Peanut’s tongue hung from the side of its open mouth as if it screamed one last howl before it died. Its body was all but bone and skin. Mary suspended down on a web and looked at me. 

“*Peanut*?” 

“I’m afraid so…” I replied. “What the hell did you do to it?” 

“*I ran out of cockroaches. I was feeling a bit peckish. But now you can return Peanut and your neighbor won't sue us.”*  

I crouched down to get a better look and there was no doubt in my mind; I cannot return Peanut in this condition. 

“If I brought Peanut back to Susie like this… she would kill me.” 

“*That doesn’t sound like a good neighbor.”* 

I looked up at Mary and for the first time its spidery body didn’t unsettle me. 

“Neither does killing a neighbor dog just because you got the munchies! That's not very nice of you, Mary. What if I killed your dog!?” 

“*I don’t have a dog.”* it replied. Its voice seemed worried, even frightened. 

“We cannot keep this here. We have to get rid of it.” 

“*I can put it back with the others. And then it will be gone for good.”* 

That last comment set fear back into me. Now I looked at Mary as if it was a killing machine and not a maid that cooks me grilled cheese. 

“What do you mean by others?” 

Now it was rubbing its feelers together cautiously. It was like I caught a child sneaking cookies in the middle of the night. Except it wasn't a child. It was a twenty inch, overgrown spider whose nature got the better of it. I watched as it thought of its next words carefully. 

“*I haven’t seen a cockroach in a while. I did a good job keeping them away. The next building over doesn’t even have cockroaches. Not even flies swarm the trash cans here.”* 

I just sat there and listened to Mary speak.

“*All I could find were birds, fish, lizards, and dogs. I didn’t want to take them but… I don’t know. I saw them and couldn’t help but want to try to… It didn’t even taste good.”* 

“Mary…” I began cautiously. “Have you been eating people’s pets?” 

“.*.yes*”

“You ate dogs, birds, and cats?” 

It shrieked and recoiled back. 

“*No no never cats. They frighten me*!” 

I stood up and slowly walked across to the other side of the room. Now I had to figure out how to feed a creature that hasn’t stopped growing. It dawned on me that I may have to feed it for years. Six years or more by my calculations. For some reason I always thought that it wouldn’t try to eat other animals and stick to just eating bugs. I was clearly wrong. How am I going to fix this?  

At least Barb’s cat will be safe. 

“*Are you mad at me?”* 

Not only is it a murderous killing machine, it's also insecure. 

I chose the only action I could take at the time. 

“No, I’m not mad at you, Mary. I just need to figure something out.” I plotted my next move. I may have a plan to fix all of this, but I would have to make one thing clear to Mary before I do. 

“I will head out for a bit. What I need from you, Mary, is to stop killing people’s pets and wait for me to return. Got that?” 

 It continued to rub its feelers together. In a little pitchy voice it made its response; “*I understand.”* 

I got my keys and made my way to the front door. 

“Mary, make sure to… you know…:” I made a gesture towards the mangled Peanut on the floor. 

*“Okay…”* 

It grabbed Peanut and crawled back into the crawlspace without another word. 

LA is big, that much is obvious. In this vast city there were many types of pet stores. I made my way to one of the exotic pet stores I looked up on my phone. The answer to my problems came in a package full of deceased mice that one would feed to boa constrictors. Hopefully, these would be enough to feed the beast. Maybe this will stop the killing long enough to keep the neighbors off my tracks. Not that they were suspecting me. Yet. 

When I came back, I looked around the apartment for Mary. It was nowhere to be seen. It probably never left the crawl space. I walked over to the laundry room and cleared my throat. 

“Mary? You there?” 

A moment passed and then  hairy, dead tree legs crawled out from the crawlspace. Mary did not fully leave the dark cave. 

“*Are you going to evict me?”* 

I shook my head. 

“No, I am not evicting you. I promise. Look, what you did was… horrific. But that, I think, was just a mistake on your part. You were hungry and had to eat and all of that is completely understandable.” 

Mary looked at me, feelers touching nervously. 

“I just wish you had come to me about the food situation instead of- you know.”

*“I didn’t want to be a bother.”* It responded in her tiny, pitchy voice. 

“I understand that. But, we are roommates, Mary. When you have a roommate you have to communicate. If you needed food I could just go out to get some for you.” 

Mary inched closer out of the crawlspace now more erect and alert than before. 

“*You would do that for me?”* 

“Uh huh.” I raised the bag of dead mice. 

“*What is that?”* Mary said with wonderment in its eyes. 

“I’ll show you in the kitchen.” 

I walked to the microwave and eight legged tapping followed behind. I opened the bag and placed a couple of frozen mice on a plate. Mary jumped onto the counter and watched me curiously. The sudden jump startled me. Mary’s speed was dizzying at times. It rubbed its feelers in anticipation as I closed the microwave and punched the time in. The mice thawed and steamed over. The room gained a pungent aroma that I could not quite describe. I guess it just smelled like cooked mice. I grabbed the plate and placed it in front of Mary. 

“There. Try that out and tell me what you think.” 

Mary took no time at all to dig in. It wasn’t until then that I realized that I have never seen a spider feast. Fangs uncurled from under its face and jabbed into the mice like knives. Savage instinct took over as Mary began to ravage the dead bodies. Legs twitched and its entire body engaged in the process of devouring the food. Sucking noises could be heard from Mary as it took delight in its feast. Sweat broke out on my forehead and the back of my neck. I collapsed in the nearby chair and watched in horror as Mary ate. In the back of my mind, I thought about being on the other end of those fangs. I thought about Peanut. This was how it devoured Peanut. 

Mary finished the mice and left two deflated bodies on the plate. 

“*Oh my! That was delightful.”* Mary turned its body towards me and stared me down. I sat up and held in a scream. 

“*That really hit the spot. Thank you so much for buying me dinner.”* 

I swallowed saliva and tried my best to not show absolute fear. 

“Y-Your welcome, Mary.”  I licked my lips. “It's kinda like a hot pocket, huh?” 

“*I am not sure what that is but I suppose it is.”* It produced a noise that sounded like a chuckle. 

My night was done for sure. All I wanted to do was go to my room and close the door. I didn’t want to see Mary for the rest of the night. Watching it eat took it out of me. 

“I think I will go to my room now.” I stood up and made my way to my room. I made sure to  deposit the other frozen mice into the freezer. 

“Mary, if you need anything else from me just let me know. Okay?” 

“*I will. I promise to communicate.”* 

As I made my way across the room, I stopped in my tracks and looked back at Mary. It was still looking in my direction. 

“You wouldn’t eat a human, would you?” 

It tilted its head, confused. *“I don’t think so.”* 

That answer did not make me feel better. I gave a nod and then continued to my room. 

The following days continued as normal. Mary now had a supply of microwave mice that it could eat to supplement its diet if I did not find any bugs. Now, every time I walk across the lobby of my apartment building, I purposely ignore the community cork board. Seeing all those missing pets that met a gruesome fate by Mary’s ravenous appetite only brought me guilt. My roommate was at fault for all these missing pets, and I was not quick enough to prevent it. 

Though I fixed that issue, a new one arose. 

After some time, I measured Mary again to find that it had grown to around 35 inches. Its body filled up a noticeable amount of space in the apartment. I knew that it was going to get bigger, that was expected. What was not expected was its new found obsession; me. 

Every day, since I gave Mary the mice, I noticed its gaze. It’s always looking at me. In my peripheral vision, I would see it turn towards me and just stare. When I looked in its direction, Mary quickly went back to its task as if nothing had happened. I would avert my eyes away and then it went right back to looking at me. Mary never said anything. Just silently stared. It cooked the grilled cheese as normal, but would add little details to the plate like a ketchup spear in the shape of a smile or a heart. I thought it unusual, but did not think it was anything to be concerned about.   

One night, I woke up to something on my bed laying on top of me. It felt like a weight was pressed against my body, and for a second, I couldn’t move. I stirred awake and in that moment the weight was removed and a sound of skittering legs rushed out of the room. I looked up and saw that my door was swung open. I thought that I dreamt the whole thing, but I know now that I did not. Mary was on my bed. Mary was on top of me.
