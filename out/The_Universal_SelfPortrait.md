**Part One** 

I was living the NYC dream in a 300 square-foot room above a struggling dim sum restaurant. I worked 39 hours a week at Potterhouse Grill and when I wasn’t doing that, I posted videos of my DoorDash deliveries to my 834 TikTok followers. 

That was before the break up. 

I hadn’t left my room in weeks. 

What was I doing?

Nothing.      

When Stepney invited me to the gala, she wouldn’t take no for an answer.

The “Universal Self-Portrait” exhibit was supposed to be transcendent, but the promise of free champagne and bite-sized miracles sold me.

I came to NYC to wait tables, write a novel, and someday earn a city-view penthouse that wasn’t just a wet dream.

So I agreed to go. 

I needed to leave my month-old depression behind, along with the fermenting piss bottles crowding my floor and the delivery trash I gave up pretending I’d take out.

The true pain of that break up is hard to remember now—that misery was a mere inconvenience compared to what *The Portrait* gave me. 

**Part Two**

I pulled on my oversized peacoat and shoved my curls under the lip of a knitted beanie. Snowflakes caught on wild strands of my hair as I shuffled into the subway. 

I swung into a seat on the train and slumped. 

Everyone around me had their own lives, passions, expectations.

Maybe that’s what the “Universal Self-Portrait” is trying to prove—that beneath it all, we’re all cut from the same cloth.

But it couldn’t be truly universal. No image could represent everyone—there’s always a standard. 

I exited the train and Stepney rushed at me on the platform. Her boyfriend Tom waved, holding Stepney’s Louis Vuitton bag so she could wrap me up in a death hug. 

I don’t know how to explain it, except that both Stepney and Tom look like they are from Pennsylvania. Strong Protestant traits ruled their genomes. 

“Alex, I’ve missed you!” Her voice was artificially high, but her joy was real.

She’s always been a show boat but well-intentioned. First person to present her pottery to our class and last to take direction.  

“Hey man, missed seeing you at Trivia the last few weeks. We haven’t won since you left.” Tom clapped me on the shoulder.     

“We got third two weeks ago. But we were just lucky the topic was Failed Social Gatherings. ‘Cause you remember…”

I smiled. “When you waited eight hours in the sun at Tanacon?” I stepped back and took in Stepney’s pastel scarf and bumblebee brooch. 

She always walked the line between fashion and feral. 

“I also went to Fyre Fest.” Stepney looped her arms with mine and pulled in Tom. She skipped forward and pulled us towards the exit. 

“That was a disaster. I couldn’t even charter a boat to come get you.” Tom laughed as we were pulled along. 

“I hope that’s no indication on how this night is going to go.” I clenched my jaw. 

  

Each skip brought me closer to the exhibit and the sudden urge to head home washed over me. 

”Relax Alex. We are just going to see art in upper Manhattan.” Tom chuckled, but it sounded like someone else trying to imitate Tom’s laugh. Yet I let that lull me and I was swept away towards *The Portrait*.  

**Part Three**

Stepney led us to a tower and flashed a blank white piece of stationery at the doorman. He ushered us into the elevator, turned a key, pressed “PH,” and vanished behind the steel doors.

“You told me we were going to an art gallery,” I side-eyed her.

“We are. It’s my boss’s private event. He told us to invite at least two people.” Stepney dug through her bag and applied a fresh red lip. 

I thought she invited me because of the breakup. But they hadn’t said a word about it—not even a sad glance. We had been on countless double dates. They liked my ex—sometimes more than me. 

“I hope Renee isn’t here, she’s been such a cunt at work.” Stepney snapped closed her compact.    

Tom had clawed his way up from the south side of Brooklyn, using the ashes of his competition as chalk for his climb. He was fiercely loyal, a mild douchebag, and a loud drunk.  

Stepney always found something to complain about and Tom backed her up no matter what. I used to think it was cute and ridiculous, now—I just want them to shut up and let me breathe. 

My lungs strained to fuel my rushing heart. The walls of the elevator felt like they were shrinking. 

“Whoa, Alex—are you okay?” Stepney asked as I pulled in a massive breath. There was genuine concern in her blue, bambi eyes.  

My nerves settled and the weight left my chest. “I’m okay. The speed of the elevator got to me.” 

Just as I said it, there was a click and the doors opened. 

The air hit me first—so thick with fragrance it felt fluid. Floral notes and rich wood laced every inhale while the tube lighting above washed everything in a sterile, surgical white.

I followed Stepney out and the hall expanded impossibly high around us with narrow floor-to-ceiling windows appearing at our periphery. The Hudson River looked like a line of crayon and the buildings like playthings. 

The whole room swayed slightly from the gusts outside. 

“This is sick.” Tom marched ahead and I followed. 

The walls closed in but the hall seemed to go on infinitely. 

Inlets along the walls displayed African masks and wooden weapons. 

Ramps and stairs broke up displays of Polynesian pottery and shell jewelry.  

“It feels like we are walking straight but we are actually walking in circles. There is a forced perspective happening with the rooms and elevation.” Stepney pointed at slits in the walls. 

After a couple minutes she had stopped looking at the art altogether and strutted forward on a mission to get to the main event.  

I liked the displays and such but a lot of the artifacts felt like history. 

A water basin with the last wild herds of bison etched onto American Chestnut—Masks used to please the gods and ferry the dead—Stones carved from ravished lands like the last drops of blood from a gutted deer.



They felt like items not intended to be owned by one man on top of the world.

Lost in thought I bumped into Tom not realizing that we now stood at the end of a line of well-dressed people. 

“Are you excited?” Stepney beamed beside me. 

“Are we there? What is it exactly?” I asked “Is it a painting that looks like everyone?”   

“That’s exactly it! It’s a Universal Self-Portrait!” Stepney shook my shoulders. 

I looked at Tom and he shrugged. 

The line started slow and the whole room was packed.

They all surrounded a white canvas square in the massive gallery. It was ten-foot by ten-foot and housed The Universal Self-Portrait. 

A line formed at one end and Stepney pulled me into the crowd. Like a lamb I followed. 

**Part Four**

The man in front of us introduced himself by chugging a flute of champagne and crushing our hands in a vice-clamp handshake. Tom immediately went to networking, and I went to eavesdropping. 

Clarence ran the northern boroughs. Tom worked finance downtown. But the way they talked about the city—it was like a mutual ex they both still secretly loved.

They laughed, Stepney rocked on her heels, smiling and nodding along, and I studied the faces of the people leaving the exhibit. 

We were closer and I got the gist. Each person approaches the curtain, goes in alone, and leaves after about a minute. Some last ten seconds and come out smiling. Others take longer and look shaken but seem convinced. 

Most people wore variations of the same outfit. Then I noticed—Clarence was the only Black man in the room. A few Asian people wandered around but the vast majority of the crowd were tall, blonde, white people. 

“And what about you?” Clarence leaned around Tom and I could smell cigar smoke on his breath. 

“Huh? What? I’m sorry…” I asked. 

“What do you do for work?” Clarence removed his bifocals and polished the lenses with a cloth. 

“Oh I work… worked at Potterhouse. I’m in between things right now.” 

“The one on 33rd or 82nd.” 

“33rd.” It felt humiliating to admit—the place that called a week into my no-show streak and fired me over voicemail.

“Tom here says you're a damn good bartender. Take this and go to Brick and Stone Bar. If you can make a Manhattan good enough for Joey, you can serve mine on Fridays.” Clarence nodded and handed me a white business card with his name in black looping letters. 

On the back were the words “Chief of Patrol”.

“Thank you S-Sir.” The card could’ve been made of solid gold with the way my arms shook, but I needed to learn how to make a Manhattan. I’d never tended a bar in my life. But front-of-house at a place like Brick and Stone? I’d be lucky to ever find anything as good. 

Clarence walked into the exhibit 

Tom stood up front, then Stepney, then me. 

I didn’t mind going last. Actually I preferred it, after all I did not believe that I would see my own face in the portrait and didn’t want to ruin it for Stepney. 

In about thirty seconds Clarence walked out with a thumbs up and an exaggerated aww face.  

“Did you see yourself!” Stepney leaned towards him. 

“I did! Took a second but it was definitely me!” The policeman smiled and walked off. 

“This is so cool!” Stepney squeezed Tom’s hand and he disappeared behind the curtain. 

Twenty seconds later Tom stepped out—eyes wide. “No shit. It’s real.”

Stepney squealed and rushed in. I saw nothing during the brief moment the curtain flew open. But it just couldn’t be real. 

“We will wait for you right here. Then we can check out the drinks or whatever.” Tom spoke but it was clear his mind was elsewhere. 

“Was it really a picture of you or is it a mind trick? Maybe an optical illusion?” My question snapped Tom out of his trance. 

“No, it was most certainly me in that painting. It took a second but it was real. So real. You’ll see. Stepney’s gotta be done by now. It was beautiful dude. Just—beautiful.” 

I looked at Tom intensely. When was the last time I’d heard him call anything, pretty, gorgeous or—beautiful? Never. Not even Step. 

Just as he said it the curtains opened and Stepney flew out.

“So worth it! Go Alex! It’s cool!” Stepney hung off of Tom. 

Something didn’t feel right. I reached through the curtain and slipped inside.

The air didn’t smell like flowers anymore.

Immediately, I knew—I hadn’t trespassed on it.

It had trespassed on me.

**Part Five** 

First came the chill. A golden light hovered in the center of the room. It promised warmth—but I understood it was just a veneer. 

I wanted to turn around—plaster a fake smile—pretend I saw myself and move on but a glimmer caught my eye. The golden orb was surrounded by a golden frame. 

I tried peering around the golden light but it drew in my eye and the brightness obscured the painting behind it. I stepped forward. 

My gut twisted, begging me to turn around. 

But there was something behind the light and I needed to see it. I crossed the room and the gold light shrunk but remained fixed in between me and my view of the painting. 

There was no smell in the room, or noise, just cold light. I reached out to grasp the shrinking golden bulb in my hand. My fingers closed in around the light. 

Don’t do it—

The thought raced through every fiber of my being. This was an offering but I didn’t know the price. 

The light radiated no warmth—no promise—only hunger. 

I snatched my hand away. The golden light burst and a yellow coin hit the marble floor. 

I looked up and saw the portrait unobscured. 

There was a massive pure white canvas surrounded by a golden frame with the word "Liberty” stamped on a bronze plate. 

My eyes dried out as I stared at it, wisps outlined an amorphous upper body where only white blankness had been moments before. 

I leaned closer. It wanted me closer. 

I knew I should’ve run—should've pretended I never saw it. But just as my suspicions of her cheating compelled me to unlock her phone at midnight, the secrets of this room planted my feet and quelled my worries. 

Two eyes split open a pure white face. 

The spell broke. I threw myself backwards and landed hard on my ass. The area above the painting’s white chin broke into a grin—a perfect set of human teeth materializing around the pair of plaster white lips.  

It had to be an LED screen or something. My heart hammered against my ribs like a man buried alive.  

White needle-thin fingers appeared in frame. It waved from the painting. 

I steadied myself. It was just a projector—an art installation. I was overreacting. 

Lifting my chin, I watched as the creature pointed toward me. An oblong nose shifted onto its face. It lowered its hand. 

I expected the claw to disappear where the frame began. 

The creature reached out and white knuckles curled around the golden frame. It leaned forward and its other arm swung out of the portrait. 

It splattered onto the marble like a newborn foal. Its arms were too long for its stunted legs and malformed hips. Yet it raised its head and launched at me. 

Adrenaline detonated in my veins. I shot backward, threw myself upward and darted out of the room. I slammed into a tall woman waiting her turn and we both careened across the waiting area. 

It took me a moment to regain my bearings. The vision of the creature bored into my mind. It had a formless yet jagged structure—as if all of its bones were broken.

“We need to get out of here.” I got to my feet and shouted to the room. Tom and Stepney were at my side. 

“Alex. What is going on? What happened?” Stepney whispered. 

“There!” I pointed to the white creature stumbling out of the exhibit. Tom and Stepney followed my finger and smiled. Even the people near the creature acted as if it were a golden doodle. 

My heart raced, head pounded, yet there was no reaction amongst anyone else when the monster trembled and coughed. It was less than ten feet away from me, growling and jerking. 

Head low, it had slunk closer, hacking like the harbinger of some biblical plague.  

I clawed at the floor and shot to my feet but Stepney and Tom held me still. 

“What are you doing? Can’t you see it!” I rammed Tom. I didn’t want to hurt them but I couldn’t let them just hold me down. 

I tried to wiggle free but Tom had my arm twisted behind me. 

The portrait stopped at my feet. It bowed its head and released an agonized screech. With its cry, gold coins showered the floor. 

It hacked again and pottery shards poured out. Again, jade jewelry appeared. Stepney and Tom released me to collect the treasures at my feet. Others gathered around the creature, admiring it as it vomited wealth onto the floor.

The Portrait raised its chin and spit up chains of silver and diamonds. Clarence reached directly into the breast’s mouth and pulled out a heavy diamond encrusted cross pendant and chain. 

The moment he touched it, his hand turned plaster white. As he slipped it on, the melanin in his skin rushed from his extremities.  

Everyone around me was turned the same white color as the painting. Tom laid half dissolved into Stepney. Where they met back to back became a white mass of undulating liquidity. 

“Pretty. Pretty.” Stepney pawed at various gemstones, not quite able to reach them on account of Tom reaching for the coins—each stamped with the single word Liberty. 

“Beautiful”, Tom tugged against Stepney.     

I stared because I couldn’t help it. As my friends touched other individuals they clung to them like globs of glue. Their struggle incorporated everyone into a single mass that swelled and constricted.  

Them stuck to one another—that stuck in my mind. 

The heat from the mass between me and the beast broke my concentration and I ran. 

A foot from the exit I froze. 

An emotion I can’t express washed over me like a cold sweet. I turned on a dime and faced the creature. It compelled me from the marrow in my bones. 

I was hoping someone would be running for their life alongside me at the very least but all I saw were the last of their white limbs writhing like rats in a glue trap. 

The thrashing ceased all at once, the limbs collapsed and a single perfect circle pooled at the feet of the monster. 

The disk of pure white liquid was beautiful. 

It was a solid halo, softer than moonlight, smooth as milk. 

A proboscis unfurled from the monster’s maw and dipped into the pool of white. Surges of white liquid pumped through the creature as gulp after gulp extended the creature’s stomach, and elongated its rear legs and spine. 

Regardless of the horrific transformation I witnessed I remained transfixed upon the beauty I saw. 

Patches of flesh pried up and peeled from the creature’s abdomen and rear as it neared the size of a minivan. It was a massive fleshy pinecone, with each uncurling bit of flesh revealing a pulsing, blood-red sphincter. 

The dozens of swelling orifices triggered my trypophobia and my stomach emptied in one smooth throw. As I gained my bearings I witnessed the first sets of plaster white hands emerge from the pulsing rosebuds peppering the pinecone.        

The spell broke and the creature stopped feeding to stare at me. Its front end held itself above the shimmering pool and I could swear—the face it was wearing—was mine. 

**Part Six** 

Reddit, I haven’t been able to sleep.

I see the creature everywhere—

not just in my dreams.

But I’m trying.

It’s been a week since the gallery and today is the day I get my life back. 

The portrait was over. 

No way I’ll be allowed back in that gallery after running through an emergency exit and pulling the alarm. It was time to learn how to make a Manhattan. 

I took the train to Potterhouse Grill.

“Alex, I’ve been worried.” Najja unlocked the door and pulled me in. A few people were camping at tables but I walked directly to the bar. 

“I know this is crazy and against company policy, but could you teach me how to make a Manhattan, and maybe how to work and clean the bar?” I rocked on my heels. Clarence's card burned a hole in my pocket. “It’s really important.” 

Najja furled her brow. “Yeah, I can’t do that, but I’m free this weekend if you want me to teach you at my home bar.” 

“You have your own?” I thought Najja just joked around—turned out she took her shit seriously.

“Bartending is hard. It’s a craft and sport. The weight of the bottle, the showmanship of the pours…” 

“I don’t need all that. Just one drink—done right.” I glanced at the ingredients, but Najja had already cleaned everything up. 

“This weekend. I’ll give you a crash course.” Najja softened her features and led me to the doors. 

I wanted to learn then so I could go to Brick and Stone the next day for Thursday lunch, but maybe Najja was right. I needed to learn the basics. 

Over the next couple of days I watched YouTube video after YouTube video. I read Reddit posts and pulled on all my experiences as a server. 

When it was time to visit Najja, I didn’t realize how much I’d struggle just to look normal juggling so many measuring tools and open bottles. After hours, I hit the flow state and I created my very first perfect Manhattan. 

I left Najja’s apartment with one piece of the puzzle complete. Now all there was left to do was to go to Brick and Stone and pray Clarence’s disappearance doesn’t make things awkward when I hand them his card.

I like working with Najja. I didn’t see the portrait once. Maybe I was just imagining the white fingers when I saw them outside my dreams. 

Maybe I’d be okay. 

**Part Seven** 

The exterior of Brick and Stone was exactly that—a brick and stone facade encapsulating rocks that dated back to the construction of New Amsterdam. The same stones that adorned the walls were under the toes of fur traders and natives in the cobble streets of the 1600s. 

The lights were low yet seats were sparse. 

Even for a Monday lunch, the place was popping.    

A host delivered me to the bar and I handed Clarence’s card to an Italian man with the girthiest neck I’ve ever seen. 

He grunted. 

Nodded. 

I followed him into the restaurant. 

I wanted to speak, but something about him told me this wasn’t a man for small talk. So in silence I walked until I ended upstairs where the private rooms were, and where another bar stood. 

The man I assumed was Joey nodded at the bar. He took a seat and waved me on. 

This was it. 

I was dressed in my best suit, I had memorized the recipe, practiced my motions, and prepared for success. 

I needed this job more than I could express. Rent was due, the COVID protections were gone, and my landlord had stopped pretending to be polite.

I scooped the ice—poured the whiskey. 

It was a simple drink but my flair had to be on point. 

Yet, Joey stared at me unimpressed. He hadn’t asked anything of me—just watched as I stirred the drink and poured it for him. 

I stabbed a small black cherry and rested it above the liquid. Hopefully Joey hadn’t noticed my trembling hands, or the need on my face.  

My drink was beautiful. The amber-gold Manhattan shimmered and the Luxardo cherry hung over the mixed drink like a dark moon. 

I pressed it towards Joey. 

He remained unchanged. 

Silence laid bare between us. 

After four minutes he nodded and waved. 

My heart jumped to my throat. It seemed like he was accepting me but with the little reaction I’ve seen from him, I didn’t know if the nod was a ‘follow me to the exit and never come back, dipshit’ kind of nod, or a ‘you did great, here’s a million bucks’ kinda nod. 

I followed him downstairs but instead of taking me back out to the main dining room we took a left and he pointed me at an office. 

This was it. Everything I had worked for. 

“Onboarding is already pulled up. Fill it out and come find me.” Joey nodded and disappeared. 

I walked into the cramped room. A Dell desktop sat beside countless papers. I shook the mouse and a generic form appeared on the screen. 

I sank into the chair. My nerves finally relaxed. 

I did it. 

I put in the effort. 

I got the job! 

I…

I typed my first name into the first box and when I clicked over to fill in my last name, the screen went blank. 

I couldn’t see any of the paperwork. The only thing that I saw was a glowing orb of golden light. 

The chair crashed behind me. Papers flew off the desk. 

As I stumbled backwards the golden light grew. Behind it, the rectangular frame of the screen shimmered. 

Fingers clutched the desk as the creature’s head squeezed out of the monitor. 

Fuck.

Not again.

Please—just leave me alone.

I felt tears coming. I wanted this so bad, I needed it. But the creature was beyond negotiation. 

I rocketed out of the office and into the main room. At the bar I saw Joey carrying what I knew was my Manhattan.

I watched as he carried it to the far end of the bar where three people sat, one black man in a suit and a couple making out.   

I don’t know how, but I watched Clarence lift my Manhattan to his nose and take a long, intimate sniff. He noticed me and we locked eyes. 

He nodded, lifted my drink to the sky, and downed Manhattan in one gulp. As he lowered the glass, I saw straight into his mouth. Everything was pure, plaster white.

He was real. He survived. Whatever happened in the penthouse, it wasn’t death.   

The couple beside Clarence stared at me. Tom and Stepney looked like dolls. Those features that rooted them to Pennsylvania were erased. 

I watched my old friends shake their heads in disapproval. Several bees crawled over Stepney’s cheeks.    

My heart twisted and burned. They cost me that job, my sanity, my trust. 

Reaching for the nearest drink I attempted to snatch a wine glass from a patron and chuck it as Clarence and the couple. As I grabbed it, a white hand unfurled from the drink menu beside it and caught my wrist. 

I screamed as if I’d been shot. 

The cold heat of the creature broke off as I darted for the exit. 

Fuck them. 

I hated how that restaurant felt.  

Dark and cold.  

I sprinted block after block, but the creature followed me. I saw golden orbs in every glass pane in the skyscrapers I passed. Orbs dotted the square faces of the Cartier watches.  

I rushed past and orbs covered LED billboards and taxi advertisements. 

As I ran through the city my whole view was of golden orbs but no matter how fast I ran, I heard the clinking of coins behind me.

The creature could’ve appeared from any of the million windows or tv screens. I felt its energy coiled in the very square concrete slabs I flew over. 

I was surrounded but my adrenaline kept me going. I got to my building, saw an orb at the elevator, so I launched up the steps. 

Back to my room, I collapsed on the bed, and sobbed into the pillow.

**Epilogue** 

Saying I’m struggling doesn’t scratch the surface. Four weeks in bed after the breakup felt endless. But the past four months? Hell.

My debt is high and this life is unsustainable, but I can’t go into the city. The orbs are still everywhere. 

The day it all started, I collapsed on my bed and cried. I dragged myself to the bathroom and looked in the mirror. 

I expected to see an orb, but I didn’t. The only thing staring back at me was myself. 

I traced my brows in the mirror—they are my father’s and they lift in the same way when I laugh. 

My knuckles are from farmers and hunters—from survivors and fighters. 

I smiled and it was my grandmother’s. My hair is ours too.

My features were solid. Distinct. Mine.

I knew who I was. 

A week of denial, turned to a week of grief, then a week of anger—but never acceptance. 

A month in, I started writing again. The white notebook pages farmed from the falling of countless forests only had the faintest golden orb which disappeared when I wrote my first phrase…“I was living the NYC dream”.  

I still see the monster and orbs.  

But I can also create, disrupt, expand. As a novelist, it’s important that I’m transparent, so when light strikes me, it reenters the world fractured into its many colors.  

This is my light. A rainbow of mistakes, dreams, and perspectives.  

Reddit, humor me. 

Share some of your light.

Let me see something other than gold. 

Because at the end of the day—the only thing universal about our self-portraits—is that we paint them ourselves.
