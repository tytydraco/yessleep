I read r/nosleep and r/shortscarystories to fall asleep.

I know that sounds backwards.

Most people put on rain sounds or a podcast. I scroll horror threads until my eyes go heavy. There’s something soothing about it - the structure, the rhythm, the steady escalation toward resolution.

Even the long series are comforting, especially the long series. Watching Part 1 turn into Part 6. Refreshing before bed to see if the OP updated. Reading the comments where everyone stays in character and asks if they’re safe. It feels communal. Contained. And when I put my phone down, the story stays inside the screen.

That’s what I believed.

\*\*\*

There was a series about someone who found their missing sister’s old computer. They uncovered a blog post from the night before she disappeared - a run-down theatre, a projector turning on by itself, private moments recorded and played back to a room full of friends.

The final screen read:

YOU’RE WELCOME.

The narrator promised to investigate the people who had threatened them in the comments. There was never an update. The post remains, but the account does not.

Another was written like a clinical report - structured in phases, detached, observational. It read like documentation of something being tested. It ended mid-line: “Test subject expired. No further testing required.” There was no continuation. The formatting lingers; the profile does not.

A third ended with, “I’ll update soon.” It didn’t. That account has no trace beyond the thread itself - no comments elsewhere, no cross-posts, no history. Just the story, fixed in place and unattached to anything beyond it.

\*\*\*

Unfinished stories are normal in those subreddits. People abandon throwaways. Ambiguity is part of the format. What unsettled me wasn’t that the series stopped, but that the accounts had no other life attached to them. There were no stray comments, no unrelated activity, no evidence they had ever interacted outside that single narrative. One footprint, and then absence.

One night - too late - I started checking older favourites: the sister and the theatre, the clinical experiment, the rushed final entry. All are still readable. All are missing the person who wrote them.

They aren’t marked deleted. There’s no “\[deleted\]” placeholder. The profile simply fails to resolve, as if the username was never assigned to begin with.

The internet doesn’t erase cleanly. Even deleted accounts leave residue - cached pages, mentions, screenshots, cross-links. When someone has existed online for any length of time, there is bleed-through.

With these accounts, there isn’t. It’s as if they existed only long enough to tell the story and were then removed at the root.

I told myself I was pattern-seeking because it was 1:30am and I had been reading horror for hours. But I kept checking. I started keeping a record - usernames, dates of final posts, the time between the last update and when the profile stopped resolving.

Most vanish within a week. Some within days. The older ones lingered longer; the newer ones disappear faster. That might be coincidence. Platforms change. Policies shift. Still, the interval is shortening.

I realised how many series don’t end with a climax. They end with silence - not just silence from the account, but silence around the person. No one claims them. No one resurfaces. No one ever says, “That was mine.”

Just a voice.

Then static.

\*\*\*

There’s comfort in believing it’s fiction. That’s the rule. Everything is treated as true, but we understand it’s performance.

Except sometimes it doesn’t read like performance. It reads like documentation. Like someone typing quickly before something changes.

If they weren’t crafting horror but recording it, then maybe the disappearing account isn’t the cause. Maybe it’s the timestamp - the last visible confirmation that they were still here.

\*\*\*

I still read before bed. I still follow new series when they start, but now I notice when the tone shifts - when a post feels less structured, less performative. More immediate.

When that happens, I check the username and note the date of the final post. I give it time. Occasionally there’s an update. Often there isn’t.

If the account stops resolving, I add it to the list. The list is getting longer, and the interval between final post and disappearance is getting shorter.

It may be coincidence, or it may be that the act of noticing alters the outcome.

Anomalies tend to self-correct, after all.
