I've been trying to write this for three weeks.

Every time I get close to the part that matters I stop. Close the document. Make coffee. Stand at my window and watch the street for a while. Then I come back and start again from the beginning because starting from the beginning means I don't have to get to the part that matters.

But it's November now. The light has changed. The smell is gone from the air. And I think if I don't write it down while I can still feel the shape of it I'm going to lose something important. Some understanding I almost touched.

So.

---

The apartment was on Keswick Street, upper floor of a two story brick building that sat at the end of a block of similar buildings, all of them built sometime in the thirties, all of them wearing their age the way old things do… not badly, just honestly. The brick had gone the color of dried leaves. The wood trim was dark with decades of weather and repainting. The building had aluminum numbers above the door, 114, and a row of mailboxes in the foyer that nobody had put names to in years, just unit numbers.

I sublet it for October from a guy named Paul who I knew loosely through a friend of a friend. Paul was doing a month in Portugal, some remote work situation, and wanted someone reliable in the space while he was gone. I was between leases. The timing was clean.

We met once to hand off the keys. Paul was easy enough, mid thirties, the kind of person who describes himself as low maintenance and means it. He walked me through the basics quickly, like a man with a flight to catch, which he was.

"Heat's finicky," he said. "The left burner on the stove runs hot. Super is in 1B if anything goes wrong, his name is Walt, he's old but he handles things."

I said that was all fine.

He paused at the door the way people pause when they've almost said something and decided not to.

I noticed it. I didn't say anything about it.

"October's a good month up here," he said finally, which wasn't what he'd been about to say. I was certain of it then. I'm more certain of it now.

He shook my hand and left.

---

The apartment itself was comfortable in a sparse, bachelor kind of way. Clean, not decorated. Functional furniture. Bookshelves with the books of a person who reads without being precious about it. A kitchen table with two chairs, one of which was clearly never used.

The living room had two windows facing the street and one on the side wall facing the narrow gap between 114 and the building next door. It was this side window I noticed first, before I noticed much else.

It faced a brick wall maybe eight feet away. The other building's wall. No windows on that face, just brick, and between the two buildings a channel of shadow even in full daylight. In the evening, when the sun dropped behind the buildings to the west, that channel went dark early and completely in a way that the street never quite did.

The window had no blind. Just glass.

I made a note to get something to cover it and then didn't, the way you don't do small things when you're settling somewhere new. There's always a reason to do it tomorrow.

---

The first week was fine. Better than fine.

October on Keswick Street was exactly what you'd want it to be. The trees that lined the block were full into their color, amber and rust and a deep red that looked almost brown in the evening light. People had put pumpkins on their stoops. Someone down the block had done their hedges up with orange lights that came on at dusk.

I worked from the kitchen table during the day. In the evenings I'd open one of the street-facing windows and sit close to it with a book or my laptop and let the air come in. That October air. If you know it you know it. Something in it that isn't quite any one thing… woodsmoke, cold, something dying slowly and sweetly in the way that autumn things do. It made the apartment feel inhabited in a way that was pleasant rather than lonely.

I slept well. I cooked simple things. I called my sister on the weekend. A good week.

The second week was when I started noticing.

---

Nothing dramatic. That's important to understand. Nothing that would make a story if you said it out loud to someone.

Just small things.

The way the side window caught my attention at odd moments. Not because of any sound or movement but just… a pull. A gravitational thing. I'd be washing dishes and my eyes would slide to it without my deciding to look. I'd be reading and realize I'd read the same paragraph four times because some part of my attention was not in the room with me but rather directed toward that window and the dark channel of space beyond it.

I bought a tension rod and a curtain panel from the drugstore on the corner. Hung it. Left it closed.

I felt better immediately, which bothered me more than the looking had.

---

The dreams started in the second week.

Not nightmares. That's the thing. They weren't frightening in any way I could articulate when I woke from them. Just a persistent, textured unease that I carried into the morning like something caught in the fabric of waking up.

In the dreams I was always in the apartment. Always evening light, that low October gold that comes in flat and warm through the west windows and turns everything amber. Always a sense of the apartment being very quiet in a way that felt less like absence of sound and more like the presence of listening.

And always, in every dream, the curtain over the side window was open.

I never opened it in the dreams. It was simply open. And I would stand in the living room and look at it and know with complete dream certainty that I should not cross the room and look out. And I never did. I would stand there in the amber light with that knowledge and then I would wake up.

Every morning for eleven days.

---

Third week of October.

The smell in the apartment changed.

This is the part that's hardest to describe without sounding like I'm reaching for effect. I'm not. It was simply true.

The apartment developed a smell. Faint enough that I second guessed it constantly. Something underneath the normal smells of a lived-in space, underneath coffee and cooking and the outdoor air. Something older. Not rot exactly. Not damp. Something that made me think of the back of closets in old houses. Of things kept in the dark for a long time not because they were hidden but because they preferred it.

I checked under the sink. Checked the bathroom. Checked the small coat closet by the front door.

Nothing. Clean. Dry.

The smell was worst near the side window.

---

I want to be careful here about how I tell this next part.

Because what I'm going to describe is something that I cannot fully defend. I have no photograph, no recording, no witness. I have only my own perception and the animal certainty that what I perceived was real.

It was a Wednesday. Third week. Maybe the 18th, the 19th. Evening. I'd made dinner and eaten it at the kitchen table and was sitting with the last of a glass of wine just letting the day settle. The street windows were showing that last good light, the sun almost gone, everything on the street below lit in that sideways amber that makes ordinary things look briefly significant.

I was not looking at the side window. The curtain was closed.

But I became aware of it the way you become aware of a sound you can't quite hear. A frequency just outside the range of conscious perception. I sat with my wine glass and I did not look at the curtain and I was aware of it with every part of me that wasn't currently pretending to be calm.

Something was on the other side of it.

I don't mean on the fire escape, though there was a fire escape. I don't mean in the gap between buildings. I mean on the other side of the glass, close to it. Close enough that if the curtain were open and I were standing at the window we would have been very near to each other.

I don't know how I knew. I knew the way you know when a room that was empty is suddenly not, before you've seen or heard anything to confirm it. The quality of the air changes. Some ancient part of the brain that predates language registers the presence of another thing.

I sat very still.

The curtain didn't move.

The smell was very strong.

After a while, I don't know how long - time had become unreliable. I felt it go. Not saw, not heard. Felt. The way a pressure releases. The apartment breathed out. The smell thinned.

I sat there for a long time after.

Then I got up and poured the rest of my wine down the sink and went to bed with every light in the apartment on.

---

I called Paul the next day.

He answered on the third ring, background noise of somewhere sunny and populated.

I told him the apartment was great, no complaints, just a quick question. I asked him if he'd ever had any trouble with the fire escape on the side of the building. Kids maybe, or animals, anything like that.

A pause.

Not a long pause. But a pause.

"Not really," he said. "Why?"

I said no reason, thought I'd heard something, probably nothing.

Another pause.

"The side window," he said. Not a question exactly.

I said yeah, the side window.

He was quiet for a moment. I could hear somewhere sunny behind him. People, music, something that was not Keswick Street in October.

"Just keep the curtain closed," he said. "That's what I do."

I asked him how long he'd been doing that.

He didn't answer for long enough that I thought the call had dropped.

"Since the first of October," he said. "It's only October."

He said he had to go. He said he'd see me at the end of the month.

He did not say what he meant by “only October”.

He did not explain what the first October had shown him.

I didn't ask.

---

I kept the curtain closed for the rest of the month. I did not stand near the side window. I did not let myself look at it the way it wanted to be looked at.

The dreams continued but I stopped being afraid of them. They were just the apartment processing something I couldn't directly perceive. A pressure gauge.

The smell came and went. Worse on cold nights. Gone entirely on the two days it rained.

I saw nothing. Heard nothing. Nothing touched me, nothing threatened me, nothing happened that I could point to and call an event.

And yet.

And yet every evening when that low October light came through the west windows and turned the apartment amber and gold and the air outside smelled of woodsmoke and the slow sweet dying of the year, I would feel it there. Patient. On the other side of that curtain. In that dark channel between the buildings where the shadow fell too early and stayed too long.

Waiting in the way that something waits when it has all the time it needs.

---

I moved out on November 1st.

The light had changed by then. The trees on Keswick Street had gone from amber to bare overnight the way they do in that last turning. The air was just cold now, just winter coming, nothing in it that spoke of anything older than weather.

Paul came back tanned and easy and helped me load the last of my things.

At the door I almost asked him.

I almost said: “What happened the first of October? What did you see? How long has it been there and what does it want and does it do anything other than wait because I need to know if Keswick Street is a place I can walk past in October or if I need to take a different route for the rest of my life.”

I didn't ask.

He shook my hand. I went down the stairs. I walked to my car in the November light which was clean and cold and ordinary.

I have not been back to Keswick Street.

I think about October the way I think about certain dreams. Something that felt completely real and completely unreachable at the same time. A logic that only holds in the dark.

Whatever is in that gap between the buildings. I don't think it follows. I don't think it leaves. I think it's specific to that place, that channel of shadow, that window.

I think it's been there a long time. Long before Paul. Long before the building.

I think October is just when the distance between it and everything else gets thin enough.

Paul re-listed the sublet last week.

October 2025. One month. Keswick Street. Upper floor.

Good light. Quiet block.

The listing says the apartment faces west.

It doesn't mention the side window.
