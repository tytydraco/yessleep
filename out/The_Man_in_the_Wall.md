When I was a kid, my family would take regular trips to my grandparents’ house. They lived in a cabin made with their own hands and it was the perfect place for a child to spend their curious years. My parents were untrusting of the school system and the government in general, just like my grandparents, and had decided to homeschool me. But both of them worked, which left neither any time to school me at all. It was a bad mix but times were hard. Morals and responsibilities clash, sometimes you can’t have both. When we moved into my grandparents house I was not only isolated, but also immensely bored.

The farm wasn’t operational anymore, not with my grandparent’s age. Sometimes they would keep cows when their friends went traveling. They’d let them graze on the wild hills of their property or sometimes they would rent out the land to make ends meet. I found myself wandering through those fields, leaping at anything that sounded like a rattle before I learned that the wilder portions of my papa’s land weren’t to be feared. I used the indoor swimming pool a lot, sprinted up and down the hill out of sheer boredom, and experimented with the electric fence in ways that consistently ended the same; me crying, and sitting inside while I recovered from the surprise of a strong shock. 

It might surprise you to hear that I am not an only child. I have two brothers and a sister. Being the second youngest should have meant that I was in school, seeing as my younger brother was when I was home. While it all makes sense now, it confused me as a child. Why they went to school, why they made friends and were normal, while I was homeschooled. I only got a brief taste of their life– not even a year– and I longed for it. I ran down the hill again and again just to pass the time so I could see my siblings again. The oldest, my sister, came around a lot less. My older brother was going out and doing things with friends. Things with dirtbikes and guns. Things that I wasn’t allowed to do. Even if I was, who would I do it with? Robert was my youngest brother and the only one I did anything with. He had trouble making friends at school and told me he felt different. Different like I was and I had trouble balancing guilt and hope. Hope that he was like me and that I wouldn’t be so alone. Guilt that I would wish that on anyone.

Though the entire idea of playing outside was to burn off my energy, I found my second wind when Robert came home. We were always fighting and I am pretty sure he hated me most days. He was my only friend, though, and I wonder if he realized that. Younger than me, but I needed him more than he could ever need me. He’d come down the gravel road from the bus and we’d whirl up chaos around the house. Sometimes we were fighting, other times we were playing rough. It became a moment that my grandparents dreaded, when Robert and I were around without our parents to keep us in line.

In and out the front door. They hated that, especially since we didn’t seem to understand the concept of slamming doors. Inside the house, where metal doorknobs could damage drywall. Outside, where our throwing the door turned the home into a drum. My grandfather would tell me time and time again to be gentle with it, to go slowly, but there was just so much to do while Robert was around. While I had him and sunlight. Before the next day came and he was gone again.

I remember his bus coming home early. It stood out as a memory because that *never* happened. I leapt off my bed, abandoning the notebook I was writing in, and threw the bedroom door open. 

Whether it had received too much abuse or something else had inspired its falling, I do not know, but the rubber knob that was meant to stop the door handle from impacting the wall had fallen off. The distinct sound of metal impacting the hollow surface of drywall echoed through the room. I froze in place, fearing the loud voice of my grandfather from upstairs. He was outside, I slept in a room in the basement, and he hadn’t heard me. 

I winced when I pulled the door. Distinct memories of white powder stuck into my mind years later. The dusty, damp scent of paper in my nose. The lock on the door handle had dug a hole into the wall. A nine year old’s worst fear had been realized– my grandfather would be angry, and my father would be furious beyond reason. I looked down and saw the rubber stopper sitting on the ground, the adhesive on the back clinging to bits of paint it had stolen when it fell. I knelt down, picked it up, and did what any sane child would have done.

I covered the hole with the stopper.

I will never know which it was that started everything. Whether it was the hole I had made, or if it was because I hesitated in the doorway. One more step and I would have forgotten it all. One more step and I’d be sprinting up the stairs to play with my little brother. It could all have gone differently.

But I went to the rubber stopper and gave it a good tug. I reasoned that it might fall off again if someone opened the door. That someone would see the hole in the wall and there would be hell to pay. It needed to stick strong. It couldn’t fall off again until I was an adult and living far, far away. I was initially surprised to find that it held quite well. For some reason I felt inspired to give it one last pull. Pure curiosity, wondering just how strong it was.

It popped free and the panic set in again. What if it wouldn’t stick again? Why had I done that? I knelt down to peek through the hole, an unreasonable fear convincing me that the doorknob had somehow gone *through* the gap in the wall and busted the neighboring storage room. 

My little heart stopped when I saw light on the other end. It seemed so distant and I knew immediately that there was no way that there was that much space between the rooms. Then again, I wasn’t allowed inside the storage room. I pressed my eye close. The hole on the other end was so small that only a tiny bit of light came through. It looked like sunlight.

“Sam!” That call is vivid whenever I think about it. It pulled me away from the wall, brought me back to my own mind, and I covered up the hole as quickly as I could. I only had to hope that the hole in the storage room was faint enough that no one would notice.

I had all but forgotten the hole by the time the day was up. The issue was light on my mind because my older sister was sleeping over at a friend’s house. She had a knack for sniffing out trouble like that but. It was only once we all got to bed– Robert and I in our own room, Luke in the one across– that the weight of the issue came back. In the dark, where it was both quiet and not quiet enough, my thoughts were too loud. I could only think of how mad they would be that I ruined the wall. That plagued me the entire night so badly that I didn’t sleep a wink.

I have tricks now, to fall asleep. Tricks learned thanks to that night. 

It started as a little scratch. Being out on a farm, so far away from everything, rats were not uncommon. When I heard nails on the inside of the wall, I thought little of it. Then it came again. It was so gentle that it caught my attention through all the panicked thoughts. It brought me out of my fear. Was that a mouse? A rat? Maybe even a roach. Something alive was definitely creeping around in the wall.

“Hey.”

The whisper sent chills through my little body. People talk about their first time having a crush, their first time feeling true loss; that was my first time being afraid. Not just being scared, but feeling actual *fear*. I thought I knew what it was before but the dread that I felt then was paralyzing. All the nightmares I had when I was a kid, all the shadows I jumped at, they all became so small because I knew this one was real.  
*Tap tap tap.*

“Hello?” A muffled voice asked again, one that was *right* on the other side of the wall. Something shifted right next to my pillow, right where my eyes faced the wall. Inches away, it came again.  
*Tap tap tap.*

“Having trouble sleeping?”

I kept my voice so quiet, so low, hoping that whatever it was wouldn’t actually hear me. “What are you?” I whispered so gently that I realized I hadn’t actually said any words at all. It had just been air.

But whatever was behind the wall heard me. “A friend.” The silence hung for a while. “I know a lot of other friends too. I think they would like you.”

His voice was so quiet that I had to scoot forward. “How are you in there?” It was the only question that came to my kid mind.

“Tunnels. Little tunnels everywhere, like caves. Do you want to see?”

I had gone to see a cave before. It was a national park a few years ago, one of my earliest memories. I often dug holes looking for treasure underneath the cabin. If there were caves underneath the house, they would have gold. They would have gems! Tantalizing for a young boy’s mind. But he was a stranger, and he was an adult. 

Those two facts clashed in my mind. “I can’t go. I’ve got to go sleep and… and you aren’t supposed to be here.”

“That is just fine. I know a place called Sleep. You don’t have to be alone there ever again. So many kids come to play. So many that you won’t ever want for anything again.”

“There’s other kids there?” I thought he meant the kids that the others went to school with. “Like, the older ones?”

I didn’t hear his reply the first time. It sounded like he was getting further away, so I pressed my ear against the wall. There was nothing there, no sound at all, not even breathing.

“All sorts.”

I flinched from the wall. Some animal instinct came back to me, one that told me what was obvious; that none of it was right. People didn’t creep around inside walls. No one dug tunnels through the ground in the middle of the night. 

“I can’t go. I won’t go.” I was concrete in my decision.   
He wouldn’t convince me otherwise and I think he knew that. The thing in the wall didn’t fight me. “If you change your mind, don’t forget. Sleep waits for you.”

Whatever bonds had kept me awake were gone. Despite the fear, my eyelids grew heavy, and I couldn’t fight them off. The voice was gone, as were the scratches, and I was out.

I still have nightmares about that morning. It should have been my parents coming to wake me up, or something else. Anything other than me opening my eyes to the ceiling as if nothing was different. I got out of bed, completely forgetting my conversation with the man in the wall, and slipped from the top of the bunk bed. 

My eyes were immediately drawn to the rubber stopper on the ground. It had fallen, but… but the door wasn’t open. On the rare occasions that Robert got up first, he *never* closed the door after himself. I turned around to see his bed empty and unmade. I felt suddenly light, my heart pounding as I knew something was wrong even though I didn’t know what it was yet.

Robert wasn’t upstairs. It was a Saturday morning, they meant to let the kids sleep in, but the mood changed when I told them I couldn’t find Robert.

The search went on for days. Within an hour they became certain he wasn’t in the house. My mom cried every single day, my dad was gone all night searching the hill and the forest. Eventually they started searching the river’s edge and, when everything else failed, the locals started searching the river itself. Enough time went by to assume the worst.

I had tried to tell them what had happened. I told them that there was a voice in the wall, that there was a strange man behind the drywall. I showed them the hole that I had made, drenched in tears, and I told them the truth. I told them exactly what I believed. That Robert went where I refused, that Robert went to Sleep.



That was all years ago. I grew up and started my own life. I moved away from my family. Ever since that day, I couldn’t look at them the same. I don’t think they saw me the same either. When Robert disappeared, I wouldn’t stop telling them what I knew was the truth. That my little brother had been convinced by the man in the wall to go to Sleep. At first they entertained my story as the product of my frightened mind. They were patient but didn’t take what I had to say seriously. When I started talking about tunnels in the walls, of caves that the man used to crawl around behind the wallpaper, they chided me. Eventually my mom broke and screamed at me that Robert was gone and that my stories would not bring him back. It wasn’t a story to me, not when I was that young. When they were angry with me, I realized that they blamed me. I should have been watching him, I should have noticed when he snuck out the door and got outside. 

That was the story. He’d snuck out the front door when everyone was asleep. The river currents were strong. When he drowned, his body was pulled where no one would ever find him. Down in the dark, where it was cold. I couldn’t believe that because, even though the man in the wall scared me, he said that I wouldn’t be alone. He said that there were other children that would want to be my friend. Wherever Robert was, there was a chance that he wasn’t alone. The bottom of a deep river was worse. 

Sometimes I entertained those thoughts as an adult. My early twenties were hard and I saw little success. Working small jobs that only paid enough for me to split rent with two others meant that I couldn’t go anywhere. I couldn’t grow and every paycheck only saw ends *barely* meet. 

When covid came around, I got sick. Very sick. It was back during the beginning of it, where there were still many people that questioned its validity. In my area there were a lot of people that didn’t believe it was as bad as it was. So, when I was sick for weeks and my lungs struggled, I didn’t get the help that was needed. Recovery was rough and I lost my job. The market for work was tight and I didn’t have any friends. Whatever was wrong with me when I was a kid hadn’t left. What kept me from going to normal school also kept me from making friends, both as a child and an adult. Motivation became very rare, money vanished, and my roommates had to find someone else to fill the void my unemployment left. I don’t blame them, I would have done the same thing. 

Being homeless drove me to the edge but I know I didn’t hit rock bottom. My grandparents saved me. The outdoor seating at a cafe allowed me to charge my phone and I was surprised to see a text that wasn’t from a collection agency. I hadn’t heard from family in a while, let alone my grandfather, but his contact showed in my notifications. 

“Hey buddy. I asked your dad…”

I was afraid to open it. I was scared to disappoint him but that fear was swept aside. He had already spoken to my dad. He would have known that I was a failure. Sure, he might not know how bad it had gotten, but my father would have told him. I already guessed the rest of the text. He would tell me I need to get off my butt and work. That my parents were worried about me, or that he was worried too. That a man had to work and all that mental stuff that was holding me down was pretend. That I was just lazy and needed to wake up.

I cried about things my grandfather never said before I finally opened it. 

“Hey son. I asked your dad where you were living. We wanted to see everybody on our next trip. Sounds like things aren’t going well for you. Your grandmother and I were very poor before your dad was born. We don’t want that for you. We will fly you up to Washington and you can live with us while you get yourself on your feet. You can help me get the place cleaned up. We love you very much.”

The truth made me cry more than what I had feared. It was the only kindness I had received since becoming an adult. I had been on my own for so long that I forgot what it was like to have help. So foreign was the sensation that I had almost turned him down. 

Then my heart sank. The text was from a month ago. It had been weeks since I charged my phone and I feared that the offer was expired. Desperate, I texted him back that I was sorry. That I didn’t have a way to charge my phone until then and that I wanted to go live with him but that I didn’t want to bother them. That I was sorry for even asking. After I sent it, I realized just how disgusting the text looked. No punctuation, too many apologies, and such a raw desperation that I knew would make him see sense just like the rest of my family.

I stared at my phone for an hour before the message showed as read. Though one part of me was certain that he wouldn’t reply right away, that he would have to find a way to let me down without coming across as a bad person, but I still waited eagerly for the reply. I needed to know, one way or another. And, if his answer was no, then I would find rock bottom and stay there. 

“Can I call you?”

That text changed my life. They sent me money after I convinced them that I hadn’t touched drugs or alcohol. I don’t know how I had gotten that lucky, but the pull towards those vices never caught me. It was all around me– the worst of addiction, out on the streets. Maybe it was a blessing that I didn’t have a dime to spend. So, when I got the money, I did exactly what I was told. I cleaned up, thrifted some cheap clothes, and got myself to the airport. My ID was still good and before long I was on a flight back home. I feel like my life fell apart at nine years old, sleeping in the basement of my grandparent’s home. It was ironic at the time that it would start back up again in the same spot. Despite all the bad memories, I was excited. I was hopeful. It felt like a real chance to be happy, even if the life they offered was small.

My grandmother’s embrace was the first time I felt love in years. I teared up there, not quite as much as she did, and my grandfather’s hug nearly crushed me. He was a tall farmer and somehow hadn’t lost his bulk over the years. 

A grin split his face. “Hey son!” He rubbed the top of my head, like I was a kid again, and I felt like one too. I felt like I had before it all happened and my smile was real. “You look great!”

I didn’t, but I appreciated the compliment.

Seeing the cabin again gave me mixed feelings. It was the place I lost my little brother, my only friend, and the place that changed me. It was so much smaller than I remembered. The emotional weight that the building held over me lessened when I stepped out of the car. Was this the place I remembered? It had been nothing less than a castle before. As an adult, it seemed half as grand as it had. But the walls were made from the same logs. The willow tree out front hadn’t changed. The carpet inside felt the same on my toes as it had so long ago.

And, when I breathed in, I traveled back through time. The scent of the wood and the pellet-fueled furnace healed wounds I didn’t know I had. The only thing my grandfather really cooked was breakfast. Breakfast for lunch, for dinner, and first thing in the morning. Eggs, sausage, pancakes, bacon, and venison– I don’t know if he was aware there was other food out there. The only seasoning he was familiar with was butter. 

I always thought that was funny, but it all made sense when the labor started. We had to get the land fixed up to sell and that meant putting up new fences. The old ones were too rotten to repair. I didn’t know what I was doing, but I had two arms and a stomach full of pure protein. I thickened out quicker than I would have thought. Just a few weeks saw me beat the soreness and make progress that my old grandfather couldn’t match at his age. I earned my keep and took nothing that they gave me for granted. I owed everything to them and so, every single morning, I was out there doing something. Some days the labor was lighter than others. Sometimes my grandfather and I went hunting instead. 

It was routine and I needed that. I *thrive* with a good routine.

I slept in my older sister’s room, opposite my old one and closer to the stairs. I hadn’t stepped foot in my childhood bedroom. My grandfather asked me if I wanted it and I turned him down without hesitation. The dark shadow of my childhood memories loomed over the frame. A dark energy threatened my heightened spirit whenever I looked at that door. My grandfather told me that it was used for storage anyway, and that getting all the old boxes out would take forever. To my comfort, he locked it, and I think my grandmother put some boxes to barricade it too. I didn’t mention that part, neither did they. They knew that opening it had the chance to break me down again. Maybe my grandmother thought I feared it was haunted. That, like when I was a kid, I thought a man would come crawling out of the wall to snatch me. To take me through the little hole to his land of Sleep. Part of me wanted that to happen, back when I was young. Maybe I’d get to see Robert again. Maybe I could get him back.

The weeks I spent at the farm changed me for the better. Months went by and I felt like a different person. Not restored, but completely different. I felt real.

Nothing good lasts forever. I guess the people that normally rented my grandfather’s land either didn’t need it anymore, or fell out of the business entirely. Money was drying up quickly and the only option loomed over the cabin. They would have to sell. He built it with his own two hands; my grandfather, grandmother, and their children. The labor I did for them suddenly felt wrong. I was fixing it up for someone else, making it pretty so that it would be more likely to sell. Restoring it so that it could go to someone else. 

We were in the storage room– the one adjacent to my old room. It had been converted to a non-perishable cabinet. With all that is happening in the world, my grandfather became something of a prepper. Food guaranteed to last longer than we’d ever need lined the shelves. 

I slammed a glass jar against more glass and the impact had me certain something would shatter. It was fine, though, and pickled juices didn’t flood the floor. I glanced at my grandfather, laughed, and told him that we needed to install a fresh bulb to brighten it up.

“That bulb’s been burning longer than you’ve been alive.” He claimed.

“Well it used to be a lot brighter.”

I had him scratching his chin. “Well I’m sure it's lost some luster over the years, but I reckon it looks the same. We had this room locked up tight, though, having my guns and all. How’d you get a look at it?”

I had to sit there for a second before it came to me. The only time I saw the inside of the storage room was back when I put a hole in the wall, a hole that somehow punched through into the room I was standing in. A hole which poured in bright light, like an afternoon sun.

“When I busted the wall… I could see in here.” I managed despite the worser feelings the memory brought. “I guess the handle went through.” 

Again, he shook his head. “No. No, son. If you’d managed that, you would have taken the door off the wall.” 

But I *did* see into the storage room. “Maybe there was a hole already. It would have been very small.” I dared to look over at the wall, right where I knew it should have been. “You probably patched it already.”

“That’s the thing, son. I… never patched up the hole. Not one in here, nor the one you made back when…” The expression on his face echoed my own heart and I knew it would be better for us if we didn’t continue. 

I shrugged. “Who knows.” But it was weighing on me. I know what I saw, I knew it better than what I claimed to have heard that night. The hole in the wall and the light on the other end were so vivid in my mind that, when I tried to sleep that night, I couldn’t get any rest. I tossed and turned but everything about what happened the morning before my little brother disappeared came back to haunt me. 

And when I heard scratching on my wall, my heart stopped. I could only breathe again when I realized it was the same sound that I had heard countless nights before. Rats had become a big problem around the house. For most people that would be a terrible, looming infestation. Sure, we were fighting them, but the squeak after the scratch helped me relax. No voices from the other side of the wall. Nothing to confirm my worst fears as true; that the man behind the wall was real and that he had taken my little brother with him.

But I still couldn’t sleep. Staring at the ceiling, I heard Robert’s voice. It was an echo of a memory but one I hadn’t drawn up in a long, long time. I think I had forgotten what he sounded like, maybe even what he looked like too, but it came back to me. Suddenly I remembered a time when he had stopped out in the yard when we were playing. His foot had landed in an ant hill and I remember panicking when he cried. In my kid brain, I thought there was a chance he could die. He was fine, of course, but for some reason that was the memory that came to me when I looked back on my childhood.

That was what came up when I heard his voice in my head. I sat up. Not because I was startled– I didn’t actually *hear* him– but because I really couldn’t sleep. My peace was wrecked not by the sorrow of that night, but because my pride was hurt. I was ridiculed for my story when I was a kid. I understand why; there was no way it was real. While the hole in the wall and the pinhole into the storage room might seem small, it was an important detail to that day and the only thing I knew was concrete. An anchor when everything else was off. I found myself going to the door. I opened it and looked to my left, where that piece of wood separated me from all of it. That, and the turn of metal piece. A single door handle locked from the outside. People only put locks on the outside if they meant to keep something in.

As quietly as I could, I moved the small stacks of boxes that my grandmother made. The barrier to my trauma came away easier than was comfortable and, with them gone, there was no way to truly put it back. 

The turning of the lock seemed so loud in the dead of night. Why hadn’t anyone heard Robert leaving? Not a single sound, though they claimed he had gotten out the *front* door. I turned the handle and the click was obnoxious. My heart pounded in my head and my vision turned fuzzy.

Those hinges squeaked. They would have heard the hinges squeak! 

My throat went dry as the door slowly opened, swinging inward on its own weight with the slightest push. The room was dark but not empty. My grandmother had lied to me about using it for storage. Or maybe she never meant that it kept boxes. The bunk bed on the other end was haunting. When Robert disappeared, I slept in my older brother’s room. They were worried I would get lost looking for him during the night. 

How many times had my parents come in there, looking for memories of their lost son because they couldn’t find him? How often did my grandparents cry on the floor before I came to stay with them? The blankets were still there, a lot of toys I had completely forgotten about too. 

Despite my body wanting to refuse, I stepped inside. The carpet was the same as the hallway but it felt colder. An absence of life hung in the air in the form of thick dust, disturbed by my arrival, causing me to cough as quietly as I could. I hadn’t come into my old room to reminisce. It had to be in and out before memories had a chance to drag me back into the pit I had climbed out of.

True as my grandfather promised, he had not fixed the hole in the wall. Looking at the doorhandle, I realized he was right. There was no way it planted itself deep enough in the wall to puncture another room. I knelt down to the hole, almost big enough to fit my hand inside, and looked.

A pinprick of light caught my eye. I knew it! What it proved, I didn’t know, but it meant something to me. My grandfather wouldn’t care, neither would anyone else, but I did. The longer I stared, the more I realized that it was not a hole in the wall of the other room. As impossible as it seemed, the light was far too bright and far, *far* too distant. I wondered if some sort of reflection was making an illusion– a nail in the wall, a shard of metal as a byproduct of construction? But it didn’t behave as a reflection when I moved my eye back and forth. It held its position, like a flashlight was shining from the other end. As if a window were letting in the sun.

Or if a distant door was open, waiting for me to walk through it.

I hadn’t noticed it when I was a kid but the light was rectangular. The angles were too perfect. The man in the wall talked about tunnels and caves, and I couldn’t help but feel I was looking down one. I left the room and went to the storage room. There was no hole on the other side, which I already knew, and I was tempted to carve one right there. 

I closed the door, locked it, and went back to my room. Somehow I knew it wasn’t shut, not like it was before, and I felt like my mind couldn’t leave where it had gone. Looking into that hole drove me into an obsession. Though I did research on the history of the land, even the folklore of the state and its cryptids, I found nothing. Nothing about a man in the wall. The rabbit hole earned me nothing but a night of lost sleep. When morning came, I was nearly useless. With my thoughts wandering and my body exhausted from staying up all night, little got done. I found myself back downstairs in the basement, where the door of my old bedroom waited for me. I opened it and went back to the hole. Though nothing else was different– not even the lighting– I found that the hole was completely normal. With the light on I could even see insolation and a stud right next to the hole.

My grandmother was concerned about my lack of energy. “I noticed you moved the boxes.” 

I explained that I wanted to see the room again. I needed to see it for closure.

“I couldn’t change anything. It just felt… wrong. I don’t know what to do if we leave.” They’d put it all in storage. That was the only option, and that didn’t bother me as much as it seemed to bother her. “You shouldn’t go in there. I don’t want you getting hurt.” I thought she meant it would bring back bad memories, but she came closer and confided in me something that sent chills up my spine. 

Her voice stayed low so her husband wouldn’t hear. “That room is a bad place now, darling. Something is wrong there. Something dark.”

That claim hung over me and I couldn’t eat dinner. When night came, she checked on me. She put the boxes back to block the door and once again told me to please stay out of the room. It wouldn’t do me any good opening old wounds. I was doing very well with them, she said, and it would break her heart to see me hurt.

I heard her, agreed to her requests– which were never demands– and waited for a late hour when I knew she would be asleep. I made sure to bring my phone this time, set on taking pictures of the hole so I would have proof. I was even more quiet about moving the boxes this time, quiet about turning the lock. When I pushed the door in I was slow, very slow. I managed without making a single sound. This time, though, I closed the door behind me. For some reason I scanned the room, expecting to see someone or something. I half expected my grandmother to be hiding inside, waiting for me to disobey her, but she felt the same thing I did. A heavy presence. Even a grandmother’s concern wouldn’t drive her to do something that crazy, not in a place like that. 

I shined my phone’s flashlight through the hole and found nothing but insolation. The drywall sank into it, forever marking the dent where I had smashed the door. When I turned the light out alongside my flashlight, there was no pinprick. Nothing. 

I leaned back, disappointed. Whatever was open was closed. The entire thing reeked of desperation to me and I began to wonder what exactly it was I was looking for. Robert was gone. Even if he hadn’t drowned in the river, there was no way he was alive. What would answers serve me other than to tell me what I could have done? I am an adult now and there is no man in the wall. That I was certain of, I *had* to be certain of it. But I wasn’t, not really. I never was. I had convinced myself that it couldn’t have happened but sitting in the room, in the dark, alone… I don’t know. It has a way of bringing the scared kid in me back out. 

I *had* to know. So I grabbed my blanket and pillow from my room, went back into the locked bedroom, and closed the door. With my phone close by for recording just in case, I tucked myself against the wall and shut my eyes.

Unlike the last two nights, the door to sleep remained wide open. No tossing and turning. Mere seconds and I was out.

I woke up to the rats again. That is what I thought, that is what I immediately told myself. With my heart pounding in my ears, I waited to hear the squeak that never came. My pillow was so close to the wall that I could hear him breathing, that man in the wall, and it sounded like he was *right* there. Right by my ear, knowing exactly where I was sleeping.

My throat was so dry but I couldn’t swallow. He’d hear that and know that I was there. But he already knew.

Knew that I was in the room.

Knew that I had woken.

He didn’t say anything, though. Instead I heard footsteps echo away, as if there was a hall of stone just on the other side of the wall. I scrambled out of my covers and crawled hands and knees over to the hole in the wall. There, in the distance, was the light. It was bright as ever and I was certain it was a doorway. I strained my eyes to see anything else. The footsteps were faint, sent from the end of a long tunnel.

A silhouette broke the square light. Not completely, just enough to make out a shape. Not some crooked, tall creature sent to steal my soul. No demon ready to drag me to hell. Instead I saw the outline of a child, assuming the doorway was of normal size. He held the edge of the frame, staring towards the hole in the wall. Staring at me and waiting.

*So many kids come to play. So many that you won’t ever want for anything again.*

At the end of the tunnel that couldn’t exist, I was staring at a kid. Some delusional part of my heart told me it was Robert. Why can’t it be? Tunnels in walls, distant doorways, voices behind the drywall? That *could* be Robert! I went for my phone but it wasn’t in my pocket. I hurried, desperate to use its zoom to be certain. To get a picture to know I wasn’t crazy!

But it was gone. I threw my blankets apart, destroyed my pillow, and turned my pockets upside down. It might have been back in my other room– as impossible as that would be– but I was afraid that I would lose what I had found. I went back to the hole to find it like it was before. It was open, just as the man promised me, but I couldn’t go. I couldn’t fit through the tiny hole. No one could!

I needed my damn phone. I took the risk and stepped outside the room. It wasn’t anywhere! My hand brushed my pocket and I found resistance, then weight. My phone had been in there the entire time. That couldn’t be, though, because I had checked there.

When I hurried back to the hole, I found the light was gone. I dug my fingers inside, shined my flashlight in, but there was nothing. I cried on the floor, not caring who would hear, and called Robert's name into the hole. 

I… know I am not okay. I know that something is happening to my brain because of the trauma. That is what reason tells me. For years I had convinced myself that none of it was real, that there was no place called Sleep and no man in the walls of my old bedroom. None of that reason did me any good. There is only one way to know for *certain* that my little brother was stolen from me. That he didn’t sneak out, that he didn’t drown in the river. Robert wouldn’t have done that.

Tomorrow night, I am going to sleep in my old bedroom again. I am going to curl up against the hole in the wall and, once I hear his breathing and scratching, I am going to tear that wall apart. I am going to go with him and take Robert back home. I’ll come back here and update this post, or something. I will come back with proof, with pictures, with videos, and with answers. 

But if I go to Sleep and don’t come back, then that means I am right. If your child talks about voices in the walls and a place called Sleep, listen. There might very well be a hole in their wall and a door on the other end and, if you aren’t like me, you might not be allowed to follow.
