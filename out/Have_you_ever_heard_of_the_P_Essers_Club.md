It was the first day of summer vacation 1997 when my mom brought me a letter.  I never got mail in those days, I was 11 years old after all and I'd just finished 5th grade.

My mom knocked on my bedroom door, I cracked it just an inch or two.

“This came in the mail for you, Brian”

Through the crack of the door, her slender hand presented me with a small red envelope with green writing.

“Huh? What is it, mom?”

“How the hell should I know what it is?  Probably …idk…one of your little friends wanting to be penpals or maybe a little girl who likes you.”

I snatched the letter and shut the door, making sure to lock it again.

I laid down on my bare mattress and held the envelope to the bright sunlight streaming in from the window behind my headboard.  The front of the envelope read my first and last name, spelled incorrectly. Prian Seyer was written in a childlike scrawl in green marker.  No address. No return address.

My first thought was it must be one of my friends from travel hockey, Joey. The kid was tough as nails on ice but a little light between the ears.  He was in remedial language arts and math so it sorta tracked in my mind.

I tore the little red flap away roughly, splitting the envelope in two.  Inside was a white birthday card, only it wasn't for me and it definitely wasn't my birthday.  The card was dingy like someone had dug it out of the trash.  There was a little cartoon bear on the front but all of the printed writing on the inside had been scratched out.  In its place someone had written in inky green chicken scratch.

It read, “pe satch pou slay plmost svery pay sach pecess sou prow sig pnd saller pell se preat sriends phe sreatest pne say put sont pell sour pum sor pour sather.”

I tossed the card down.  The card was signed, “Pours, Simmy Pue.”  On the back of it I  saw a little stamp that read, “P. Esser Club.”

“MOM?!” I called out.  I was young but not naive enough to think this wasn’t strange as hell. What can I say?  I trusted my mom more than anyone and thought she should get her eyes on it.

“It's something really weird.  It’s written in Chinese or Russian or something.  I don't know who it's from and there's no return address.”

Mom hustled up to my room where I stood now with the door wide open, red envelope in my hand.  She took it from me and I watched as her eyes scanned what was written within it.

“What the fuck is…” she cut herself off.

“Mom?”

She looked with great intent at the words written on the birthday card.

“It's nothing…chain mail,” Mom said.

She disappeared from my doorway.  I thought nothing of it and went back to bed rotting and playing Simpsons Haunted Mansion on my Gameboy.  It wasn't until the doorbell rang that night that I knew something was up.

“Mom, Dad, is someone here??” I shouted downstairs.

“Put some clothes on, Brian, we have company. Some of your friends are coming and we need you to keep them in your bedroom!”

I was shocked at the idea of it.  Suddenly I had friends coming over?  I threw some shorts and a t-shirt on.  I was hearing adult voices from downstairs.  In hushed tones they were saying something…something p esser.

At the very same moment, I heard footsteps bounding up the stairs.  Clyde and Calijah Carmichael, twins from school, basically busted my door down trying to get in.

“Brian?”

“Brian, are you in here?”. The twins knocked incessantly on my bedroom door.

“Your mom said…”

“Mrs. Meyer said we could play with you in your room”

“Ok guys, just a few ground r...”

The twins bowled me over and started playing with my plastic dinosaurs.  I took a second to look downstairs to see what was going on.  More headlights were coming up my driveway.  There even looked to be a squad car there.

Fifteen minutes later, my room was filled with kids from my class.  There was Clyde and Calijah, Sarah Miller and her baby sister Cheyenne.  Tony Scarbona, Lindy Sloan, whose dad was a cop, and Justin Ramirez was there wearing his Black Power Rangers mask and jammies.  I watched in shock and awe as they destroyed my room by pulling out every toy I owned.  The noise was deafening for an only-child like me.

“Guys!  Guys???  What's going on?  Why are you guys here?,” I yelled.

The room went silent.  Baby Cheyenne spoke up first.

“Cuz a da wed wetters,” Cheyenne said.

“Shut up, Cheyenne!!” Sarah held her hands over her sister's mouth.

“Stop, Sarah! Let her go!  What do you mean, Cheyenne? The red letter like the one I got today?” I asked.

Lindy Sloan spoke up.  “We all got ‘em.  That's what daddy said. From the P Essers club.”

“What's a P…”

“What's a P Esser Club?,” the Carmichael twins asked in unison.

“Idk but my daddy thinks they're some kinda creeps trying to get kids.  Guess they try to kidnap ‘em or something…kill ‘em probably.”

Baby Cheyenne and Justin started crying.  We all tried to comfort them.  

“It's okay. Our mommies and daddies are here to protect us,” I offered.  

Just as the words left my mouth the lights in my home went dark.  Suddenly, there were a lot of sounds.  Parents were downstairs shouting something unintelligible and we all heard the sound of broken glass.  Then we heard a beating of footsteps up the stairs.

Baby Cheyenne cried out, “Mooooommyyy,” and rushed to the door.  On instinct I held her back.  The door knob rattled.  More footsteps were heard coming up the stairs.  Then suddenly there was light and quiet.

I went to open my bedroom door to shout down to my mom if everything was okay.  But when I did, I found it.  Another red letter lay at the precipice of my bedroom door.  I grabbed it, closed the door, and locked it.

“Brian?  This is Officer Sloan, Lindy’s daddy.  Son, can you give us that letter?  Please?”. Officer Sloan tried to open the door.  I felt like I was in a trance.  The other kids watched me as I tore open the letter.  My body froze as I read the words written on some poor kid’s funeral program.

“Pou sittle piars sitches pabies se pold sou pot so pell sour pom snd pour sld pan. sow pne sf pou sas po say phe srice pith sheir pife.”

“Brian, we need to read it, we don't understand without seeing it, honey,” my mother begged.

I opened the door and handed over the letter.  Parents streamed in, comforting their children, making sure they were still there to hug.  Pretty quickly afterwards, everybody was spread to the wind.  Kids and parents gone back to their own homes.  

Mom and Dad made me sleep in their room that night.  I wasn't mad about it.  I didn't really know what happened but I knew I felt safer between the two of them that night.  After my parents heard about the disappearance of little Cheyenne Miller, snatched out of her bedroom with her sister in the bed next to her, they kept me in their bed til I was almost 16.

I'd forgotten about this whole thing for 20 or so years.  That was until I went to get the mail this afternoon.  There was a red envelope addressed in a childlike green scrawling of marker.  The letter was for my son.  It was signed Pheyenne Siller and stamped The P Essers Club.
