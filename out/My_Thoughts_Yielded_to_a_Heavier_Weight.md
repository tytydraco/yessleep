Rain trickled down the back of my neck—a freezing needle working its way beneath the stiff collar of my issue jacket. I didn't move. Moving meant the wet nylon would rustle against my harness, and out here in the damp brush, even the slightest friction sounded like a tear in the silence.

Before they threw me into this wet clay, my life was small. I spent years in a grey industrial town, working a dead-end logistics job at a shipping terminal, watching trains haul freight I’d never see arrive. I enlisted because I wanted a way out, a clear structure, and an identity with weight. For months, the training depot had been my entire world. I endured the endless screaming of the instructors, the bone-deep exhaustion of the rucksack marches, and the grueling hours learning to disappear into the terrain. This field problem was supposed to be our final capstone evaluation—the last hurdle before graduation and our official release into an active-duty assignment.

The cadre called this a routine surveillance test, but they kept us completely in the dark, offering barely any information before dropping us at the tree line. They told us the man in the lens was a simulated "bilateral candidate" for the sake of the grading rubric, an actor playing a political defector to test our observation skills. But through the glass, it didn’t feel like a game. The concrete compound looked too real, the security guards moved with a genuine, tense alertness, and the heavy atmosphere didn't match the standard training ground theatrics. I was beginning to realize this wasn't a graduation exercise at all; it was a live operation disguised as a test to keep an unwitting trainee from asking questions.

I pulled my face away from the optic, the sudden rush of night air making my eye water. My jaw ached from hours of clenching it against the shivering chill. Reaching down purely by feel, I unclipped the waterproof logbook from my chest rig. My fingers were stiff, white at the tips, and clumsy as I gripped the graphite pencil.

Visual contact confirmed. Subject active.  
I didn't know the true scope of what I was looking at, only that the lead weight of the assignment felt far too heavy for a schoolhouse drill. I leaned back into the optic, the cold rubber sealing against my skin once more, watching the candidate light a cigarette. The tiny orange spark flared in the distance—a fragile point of heat in a cold world, completely unaware of the watch being kept from the trees.

——

I awoke with a grainy headache.

I stared forward at my hand upon a metal table. 

I could move my muscles but to what purpose I could not fathom.

The air inside the secure briefing room didn’t warm; it thickened.

I kept my eyes fixed firmly on the floor near Nero’s boots, deliberately avoiding the gaze of the man sitting across the digital map display, a name that I do not remember knowing.

First came the vibration in my jaw—a deep, low hum that vibrated my teeth, like standing next to a high-voltage generator idling under a heavy load. Then, the silence. The ambient drone of the building's climate control vanished, cut off as if a heavy acoustic dampening curtain had dropped over my ears.

"You are thinking about the northern sector guard rotation," a voice said.

It didn't come through the air. It came from the back of my own throat, echoing inside my sinuses with the terrifying familiarity of my own inner voice, yet the cadence belonged entirely to Nero.

I clamped my jaw shut, my hands locked at my sides, my fingers digging into the seams of my uniform trousers until my skin turned white. Get out, I thought, trying to build a wall of static, repeating my unit's gate code and call sign over and over in my head.

"A neat row of thoughts," the voice mused, now accompanied by the phantom smell of ozone and scorched copper. A cold pressure bloomed behind my vision, expanding outward until everything blurred into a gray smear. "But the thoughts point to coordinate 37, -122. And you are tired."

The pressure surged. I felt my own thoughts being pushed aside like a gear being violently stripped from a mechanism. My memory of the northern sector—the hidden guard post, the bilateral candidate I had been ordered to watch—did not feel like my own anymore. It felt like an inventory of gear Nero was systematically taking and tossing out of the room.

My eye began to twitch as a sudden, blinding ache flared behind the bridge of my nose. The telepathic intrusion manifested as physical leverage, mimicking a massive spike in sinus pressure that felt as though my face was being crushed outward from the inside. A sharp, white-hot pain locked my jaw in place. I wanted to scream, but the neural pathway governing my voice had already been seized, locked down under an immovable weight.

Nero finally shifted in his seat, the subtle rustle of his crisp service uniform the only real sound left in the universe.

"The sector will be cleared by dawn," Nero said aloud. His physical voice was quiet, cool and detached, contrasting sharply with the absolute, iron grip he currently held over my consciousness. "You may go now. You have done exactly what was required of you."

The pressure vanished so abruptly that I gasped, air rushing in as my frame buckled. I barely kept myself upright. The sounds of the briefing room returned in a deafening torrent—the hum of the display screen, the ticking of the wall clock. I looked up, my vision shaking and the dull throb behind my brow slowly receding, but Nero was already looking away, his attention returning to a tactical tablet, his mind already drifting toward a larger, more distant target.

I forced myself to step back, my footwear heavy on the polished floor. My body felt unresponsive, as if my flesh was dragging through thick mud, barely receiving the signal from my brain. The blinding ache below the bridge of my nose had subsided into a hollow, localized throb, but the silence inside my head was the worst part. It wasn't peaceful; it was empty, like a barrack room that had been systematically cleared of personnel and gear.  
Across the table, Nero hadn't moved. He didn't look up as I stumbled backward toward the security door. His hand simply swiped across the screen, drawing a slow, deliberate line right through the grid coordinate where my platoon was currently deployed.

I reached for the door handle, my grip clumsy and numb. As I gripped the cold metal, a sudden panic seized me. I tried to recall the radio emergency frequency—the channel to use if the outpost perimeter was compromised.  
Nothing happened.

I knew the concept of the frequency existed. I could remember the day I had practiced it during field training, the freezing rain on the training grounds, the specific black ink on the laminated card. But when I tried to view the setting itself within my own mind, my thoughts slid off it. In its place stood a smooth mass of absolute indifference. The memory hadn't been stolen; a layer of my mind had been paved over, buried beneath a dense stratum of Nero’s willpower.

"The guards will escort you to the transport," Nero said, his tone casual, almost conversational, though he remained focused entirely on the display. "Do not trouble yourself with the radio. The order has already been... adjusted."

The security door slid open, cleared from the outside by silent military police whose gaze held the same dull, glassy vacancy that I could feel settling behind my own brow. I stepped out into the sterile, fluorescent-lit corridor, the white structure pressing in on me. I wasn't bleeding, and I wasn't broken, but as the door shut behind me, locking Nero inside the clean room, I realized a terrifying truth: I was still moving, still breathing, but I was no longer commanding my own body.
