I know most people who aren't from the South dream about seeing a field lit up with lightning bugs. Running through the grass, cupping little flickers of light in their hands. I used to love it too.

Until they started getting brighter.

It began as innocently as you'd expect. My little sister and I ran around the backyard trying to catch them. I was sixteen and convinced I was too old for it, even though I was having just as much fun as she was. Mom and dad sat on the swing watching us, laughing and taking pictures.

We only went inside once it got too dark. They always whistled when it was time to come in. Every time I heard that whistle, I'd think about the scary stories our uncle used to tell us when our parents weren't around.

Looking back, I think that was the last normal night we ever had.

Over the next few weeks, Mom and Dad started arguing more and more. They always tried to keep their voices down, but they were terrible at it. 

They kept arguing about whether to let us stay outside past eight, since dusk didn't settle in until around nine. But no matter how heated the argument got, they always agreed on one thing.

Nobody was to leave the house after dark. At least, what we used to call dark. 

"The news says they're just a different kind of lightning bug. Some genetic mutation or something," Dad would say.

"Since when has the news ever been honest with anybody?" Mom shot back, pointing at the newspaper they'd stopped letting us read weeks ago. 

Every evening, the arguments started around sunset. Except the sun was setting... it just never seemed to get fully dark anymore. As the days passed, so did the brightness outside.

Then, a few weeks later, my little sister came running into the kitchen in the middle of one of their usual arguments. She was crying so hard she could barely get the words out.

"It's still light outside! I want to go play!"

The second my little sister started crying, Mom and Dad forgot all about arguing.

"We can't right now. The sun's been acting weird, and it doesn't want people outside at nigh-.. late in the day," Dad said, trying to brush off his mistake.

"I'll tell you what," Mom said, kneeling beside her. "If you go to your room right now, I'll come play Queen and Princess with you in a couple of minutes."

My little sister was a little different. Most little girls wanted to be the queen so they could boss the princess around.

She always wanted to be the princess.

We watched her disappear into the hallway.

The moment she was gone, so were my parents' smiles.

“What is going on?” i said angry 

For the first time since all of this started, they didn't brush me off. 

My mom looked at my dad and said “She’s old enough to handle it” 

My dad looked displeased but asked me “What do you know” 

While my only sources of information were my friends at school and the wind up radio in my room, I still tried to act like I knew everything.

"The days aren't ending," I said confidently.

Mom and Dad exchanged a tired look before they both sighed.

"We don't really know what's going on," Dad admitted. "They're saying the lightning bugs have some kind of mutation that's making them brighter."

Mom nodded.

"And they're breeding faster than normal. They used to spend about two years underground before coming out. Now they're saying it's only taking two weeks." 

“Okay? So why does that mean we can't go outside? My friends who have phones say it’s the government trying to kill us” i said 

"It seems like your 'friends’ have about as much sense as a screen door on a submarine," Mom snapped 

The anger left her face almost as quickly as it had come.

"We don't even know what's happening," she said quietly. "And honestly… It's scaring us too. The quilts over the windows. The aluminum foil just in case. It:s... too much."

I didn't know what to say.

I just wrapped my arms around Mom. She was on the verge of crying. Dad stepped in a moment later, and for a few seconds the three of us just stood there.

After that, I stopped asking questions. I just went along with whatever was happening.

As the days passed, not only did night stop coming, the days somehow grew even brighter.

School started earlier and only lasted a couple of hours. Mom and Dad were home more often than they were at work. Rolls upon rolls of aluminum foil covered our kitchen table, half used, while quilts slowly disappeared from the linen closet and ended up over the windows.

Weeks turned into months. Somewhere in the middle of it all, I turned seventeen. Nobody celebrated. I don't even remember if anyone said happy birthday.

The house, scratch that, everywhere was scorching. 

My little sister complained nonstop. The heat was bad enough, but what she really hated was never getting to see her friends from school anymore. 

Trying to sleep became miserable. We'd lie on our bare mattresses, leaving sweat marks on them because even a sheet was too hot to sleep under.

I knew it had took a turn for the worse when my dad, who was very against technology and social media, came home with a ham radio and a laptop. 

With the heat getting to me and my confusion i said “What the hell is this” 

Both mom and dad looked at me with utter disgust 

“How about we watch our mouth or were gonna wash it with some soap” mom said 

Then they both realized they were saying that to their 17 year old daughter and dropped the shock and disgust 

"It's getting worse," Dad said. "The light. The heat from the light. Water lines drying up, The government's saying they have everything under control and that we just need to adapt."

He looked around the kitchen at the quilts hanging over the windows and the rolls of aluminum foil covering the table.

"So... we're adapting."

Mom and Dad showed me how to use the ham radio once they got it set up. Dad spent hours talking with the neighbors, and whenever he or Mom needed a break, I'd take over and relay whatever the government broadcasts were saying.

The laptop was different.

They wouldn't even let me look at the screen.

More months slipped by.

The heat never got any easier to live with. If anything, it got worse. The water stopped running some days, then came back as nothing more than a trickle. Every week another window disappeared behind another quilt or another layer of aluminum foil. The government kept repeating the same message over the radio.

Stay calm. Adapt.

Eventually, it all started feeling normal.

My little sister stopped asking when she could go outside again. She stopped asking about her friends. Kids have a strange way of adapting to the world they're given. Before long, she hardly remembered what life had been like before the nights stopped getting dark.

I wasn't so lucky.

Every morning I woke up drenched in sweat. Every night, the light pouring around the edges of the quilts reminded me something outside wasn't right. I caught myself staring through the smallest gaps in the foil, wondering if I'd ever see a real night again.

Somewhere along the way, I turned eighteen. Mom wished me a happy birthday over canned peaches instead of cake. Dad apologized for not having a present. I told them it didn't matter.

It did.

By then, the ham radio had become the heartbeat of our house. It crackled from the moment Dad woke up until long after we went to bed. Neighbors traded rumors. Government broadcasts repeated the same reassurances they'd been giving for months.

Then, one morning...

It went silent.

In its place came something worse.

A slow, steady hum drifted in through the quilts covering the windows. It wasn't loud. If anything, it was almost comforting.

Until you realized it never stopped.

We stuffed cotton balls in our ears. It muffled the humming just enough to make it bearable.

My little sister never understood there was such a thing as too far. More than once, Mom had to dig a cotton ball back out of her ear with a pair of tweezers while she cried and promised she'd never do it again.

She always did.

The humming only got louder. We changed the cotton balls every day, but it got to the point where having them in felt more normal than taking them out.

The light, now accompanied by the constant humming, slowly drove all of us a little crazy.

Mom and Dad lost what little patience they had left. My little sister grew bitter, saying things like, "Maybe they aren't bad. Maybe we're the bad ones."

As for me, I snapped at everyone. Sometimes for no reason at all.

Every now and then we'd have a good day. We'd laugh, eat dinner together, and almost convince ourselves things were going to be okay.

Then we'd remember the light.

The humming.

And those good days became fewer and farther between.

Then one day, a voice came through the radio.

It was faint, muffled, and buried beneath static, but it was unmistakably a person's voice.

All of us rushed over, even my little sister.

Dad grabbed the microphone.

"Hello? Hello? Who's there?"

Nothing.

The voice came back, but it was nothing more than a jumble of words none of us could make out.

We stood there listening for what felt like forever, hoping it would become clearer.

It never did.

Eventually, we drifted away from the radio, more disappointed than before. Mom and Dad disappeared into their room. A few seconds later, I heard the laptop snap shut.

I couldn't sleep that night. Every few minutes I'd have to peel myself off the sweat-soaked mattress just to roll over, wiping sweat from my eyes before it could sting.

That's when the radio crackled to life again.

I waited for Dad to come sprinting out of his room.

He didn't.

I glanced toward my little sister.

She was sound asleep.

Slowly, I walked over to the radio.

The voice was louder this time. Clearer.

It sounded like a man. Not old, but definitely not a kid.

"Hello? Is anyone out there? Please... anyone."

My hands were shaking as I picked up the trucker's microphone and pressed the button on the side.

"H-Hello?"

I wasn't even sure this was real. My eyes burned, bloodshot from the heat and months of restless nights.

Then I heard him again.

"Oh my God... there's actually someone." He paused. "Who are you? Or... are you with the government?"

I had to think about that for a second.

"Would the government really answer with 'hello'?"

There was a pause before he replied.

"Uh... maybe. How am I supposed to know?"

I laughed.

It caught me off guard. It was the first real laugh I'd had in months.

That one awkward joke turned into hours of talking.

His name was Riley. He was nineteen. He told me his parents had slowly lost their minds before walking outside one day. He never saw either of them again.

I told him about my family. The light. The humming. The quilts. The cotton stuffed in our ears.

It was strange.

Talking to someone who wasn't family felt almost... normal.

Talking to a boy my age felt even stranger.

I'd forgotten what that was like.

Then I heard my dad's bedroom door open.

His footsteps came fast, making a beeline for the radio.

The second he stepped into the room, the voice dissolved back into the same useless jumble of static and garbled words we'd been hearing for months.

I couldn't explain why, but I suddenly felt like I'd been caught doing something wrong.

"Were you talking to someone?" Dad asked.

I hesitated.

"No."

I looked back at the radio.

"It's just been doing this. I was... just saying 'hello' to see if anyone would answer."

Dad stared at me for another second before turning his attention back to the radio.

"I got it from here. Go get some rest. You look like you haven't slept in a while."

I did exactly that.

Before long, it became our routine.

Mom and Dad would fall asleep, and I'd quietly slip out of bed to meet Riley on the radio.

We talked for hours.

At first it was just about surviving. Then it became everything else. The friends we missed. The lives we thought we'd have. What we'd be doing if the world hadn't fallen apart. At that point, our idea of what eighteen  and nineteen year olds were supposed to be doing came from movies we could barely remember.

He became my best friend without either of us ever saying it.

As the weeks passed, the cracks in my family only grew wider.

Mom and Dad spent more and more time locked in their room with the laptop.

My little sister would sit for hours, staring at the thin sliver of light that squeezed through the smallest gap in the quilts.

Sometimes she'd smile at it.

That scared me more than anything.

The only conversations that still made sense were the ones I had with Riley.

That lasted until one day they all seemed to break.

Mom and Dad spent hours repeating things they'd supposedly read online. They said the light wasn't hurting us.

"We're the problem," Dad insisted. "Our bodies just weren't made for it."

"We're the ones who have to change," Mom added quietly.

My little sister took it even further.

"We're not allowed in the light," she said, staring at the thin strip of brightness peeking through the quilts. "Not yet."

She smiled.

"But if we change with it..."

She looked back at me.

"...then we'll deserve it."

The first thing they did was take the cotton balls out of their ears.

They just sat there, letting the humming fill the room like it was the most natural sound in the world.

I begged them to put them back in.

They wouldn't.

It was like they weren't even listening to me anymore.

Only the humming.

I told Riley everything that night, hoping he'd tell me I wasn't crazy.

He didn't.

He didn't encourage it, but he didn't think it sounded as insane as I did.

They might just be... adapting," he said carefully.

That answer scared me almost as much as my family's did.

I kept hoping they'd reached the limit of how crazy they could get.

They hadn't.

Little by little, they started tearing the quilts off the windows.

Then the aluminum foil.

Every time I managed to hang one back up, all three of them would claw it down again. Eventually, they unlocked the front door and threw everything outside.

For the first time in over a year, the light poured straight into the house.

It was unbearable.

The heat grew so intense that the cable connecting the trucker's microphone to the radio began to soften and melt.

I barely noticed.

I couldn't stop staring at them.

When they came back inside...

Their eyes were wrong.

The irises had turned a dull gray, while the centers looked as if the black pupils had melted into them.

They didn't seem to notice.

Or maybe they didn't care.

They spent the rest of the day talking about the lightning bugs.

No.

Not lightning bugs.

"Evolved beings," Dad corrected me.

"They're evolved beings."

My little sister repeated it with the same certainty.

I wasn't even sure she knew what "evolved" meant.

hey kept trying to convince me, and it became harder and harder to talk to Riley as the cable connecting the microphone to the radio slowly softened from the heat, stretching a little more every day.

It finally boiled over.

After another one of their long speeches about the light and how they themselves needed to change, they finally decided to.

They completely ignored me. They didn't even acknowledge there was another person in the room. Mom stood beside my little sister in front of Dad.

A spoon and a screwdriver lay neatly on the table beside him.

I stood there, frozen.

He picked up the spoon and instantly forced it into Mom's eye, popping the socket clean out and leaving a blank crater. She screamed, a waterfall of blood pouring down her face, but she remained stiff, standing tall.

Then he grabbed the screwdriver and slammed it deep into her ears. Blood rushed out, meeting the crimson tracks on her chin.

The moment he turned toward my sister, I ran at him with every ounce of my being.

Next thing I know, I'm waking up…

The house was even brighter, My hands flew to my face.

My eyes.

Then my ears.

No blood.

I could hear a mixture of crying and laughing. It was too bright to make out their faces, but I could see the outlines of my family standing just outside the door, hand in hand.

I screamed.

I begged them to come back inside.

They never even looked at me.

Their laughter and crying drowned everything else out.

I ran to the last comfort I had left.

Riley.

I grabbed the trucker's microphone. It was white hot in my hands, the plastic beginning to soften from the heat.

I told him everything.

Not because I thought he'd have a solution.

I just needed someone to tell me I wasn't alone.

All I got back was:

"They're evolving.

You should too."

His voice slowly blended into the endless humming until I couldn't tell where one ended and the other began.

Something inside me broke.

I slammed the radio onto the floor. The plastic casing shattered, already half-melted from the heat.

If Riley couldn't give me answers...

Maybe the laptop could.

I opened it.

The only thing on the screen was a folder full of videos.

One for every day.

I clicked the first.

Then another.

Then another.

Every single one was the same.

Mom.

Dad.

My little sister.

Kneeling silently in front of the thin strip of light leaking through the quilts.

Each day they stayed there a little longer.

Each day they moved a little closer.

Each day they smiled a little more.

If you're reading this...

Stay away from the light.

I don't know how much longer I can.
