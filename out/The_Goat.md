I had one of the strangest dreams I’ve ever had last night.

It was set in the early 1900s, during that strange period where horse-drawn trailers still shared the roads with the first primitive automobiles. Outside the mansion, workers moved around carrying equipment and supplies while old cars sat parked near muddy roads lit by soft yellow lamps. Everything felt realistic, alive, and ordinary in the way dreams sometimes do before you realize something is wrong.

The mansion itself was enormous, but at first it didn’t feel threatening.

The rooms were properly lit.  
Servants moved naturally through the halls.  
People talked normally.  
Nothing felt eerie yet.

It was just an old early-1900s estate filled with workers and servants going about their routines.

Then you entered the dining hall.

The room was massive, with a dining table stretching almost the full length of the room beneath warm lighting and chandeliers. At first, everything still felt normal.

Until you noticed the object sitting in the middle of the table.

It didn’t belong there.

It looked like some kind of strange mechanical device — something completely out of place for the time period. Everything else in the mansion fit the early-1900s setting perfectly, which made the object instantly feel wrong the second you saw it.

Like it had been placed there specifically for you.

You picked it up.

On it were the words:

“Go to the goat.”

And the moment you read it, something changed.

Not physically at first.

Psychologically.

You immediately understood:  
this was no longer a normal dream.

It felt like you had discovered something hidden underneath reality itself, like you had accidentally activated the real version of the dream.

You left the dining hall and entered the next room.

The goat was standing there.

Black fur.  
Long horns.  
Perfectly still.

And the second you saw it, the entire atmosphere of the dream changed.

The lighting became dim and sickly.  
The mansion darkened.  
Shadows gathered in corners where they hadn’t existed before.  
The halls suddenly felt too long and too quiet.

The dream had turned into a nightmare.

After that point, every interaction felt wrong.

The servants were still there, but now they moved differently. Their expressions looked empty. Some stared too long. Others stood motionless in certain rooms like they were waiting for something.

And there seemed to be hidden rules controlling the mansion.

There was usually one correct person you were supposed to interact with, one correct direction you were supposed to go, but servants and people filled nearly every hallway and room.

And if you approached the wrong one, everything changed instantly.

A servant would slowly stop moving.

Turn toward you.

Their face would look subtly wrong at first — like something pretending to be human but not fully understanding how. Their eyes looked hollow and unnatural in the weak yellow lighting.

Then they would speak in a cold, calm voice:

“You’re not supposed to be here.”  
“Why are you here?”

And then they attacked.

The dream instantly became survival horror every time you triggered the wrong person or entered the wrong place. The servants moved unnaturally once they changed. Some smiled while attacking. Others looked completely emotionless. Every hallway became dangerous because you never knew which interaction would trigger another encounter.

The mansion itself started feeling alive.

Like it was watching.

Like the servants weren’t individuals at all, but extensions of the house itself.

The lighting made everything worse. Rooms were no longer fully illuminated. Entire sections of hallways vanished into darkness. Sometimes you could see figures standing at the far end of corridors without knowing whether they were real servants or something worse.

And the entire time, the dream carried this overwhelming feeling:

if you panic, it gets worse.

But instead of panicking, you adapted.

You fought back.

Somewhere later in the nightmare, after surviving through the mansion and interacting with the correct people, you came across shelves containing weapons and equipment that felt as though they had been left there for you to use.

Different melee weapons.  
Heavy tools.  
Even things capable of burning entire rooms.

But what disturbed you most was the realization that you had already survived nearly the entire nightmare without needing any of them.

It felt less like discovering loot and more like finding evidence that the mansion had expected you to need them eventually.

There were other people there too.

You never fully interacted with them or worked together directly, but somehow it felt like they had also been trapped inside the mansion, surviving the same nightmare system you had.

So you began taking some of the weapons and equipment, even grabbing extra things for the others.

And slowly, something inside the dream started changing.

The fear weakened.

The nightmare stopped feeling overwhelming.

The moment you realized you no longer needed to survive the mansion, the entire feeling of the dream shifted. It no longer felt like you were the one being hunted.

Instead, it felt like everything inside the mansion should have been afraid of you.

And that change broke the nightmare.

You remember thinking:

now this is going to be easier.  
now this is going to be fun.

You planned to go deeper into the mansion and clear out whatever remained hidden inside it.

But when you entered the next room, nothing happened.

Nobody attacked you.

Nobody turned hostile.

The nightmare feeling was gone.

The room still existed. The mansion still existed. People still moved through the halls. But the fear driving the dream had completely collapsed.

You had become too comfortable inside the nightmare for it to continue functioning as one.

So the dream changed again.

The mansion no longer felt threatening.  
The servants no longer felt dangerous.  
Everything became strangely casual and empty, like the dream no longer knew where to go next.

And while still inside the dream, you started explaining the nightmare to the people around you — describing how strange and terrifying it had been.

Then, suddenly, you realized something horrifying.

You weren’t awake.

You were still inside the dream.

The people listening to you were not real people.

They were part of it.

And the second you realized that, the atmosphere changed again.

The mansion became cold.  
The silence deepened.  
The feeling of safety vanished instantly.

It felt like the dream itself had noticed you becoming too aware of it.

And then everything collapsed into darkness.
