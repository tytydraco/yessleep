I'm posting this from my phone while hiding inside a supply closet on a floor of my office building that should not exist and I need someone to know where I am because my girlfriend is on the phone with me and she is trying to help and she cannot help and I need more than one person to know.

My name is Marcus Webb. I work on the fourteenth floor of the Calloway Building in downtown Pittsburgh. I have worked here for three years. I know this building the way you know any place you spend forty hours a week - not intimately, not every corner, but well enough that wrongness registers immediately. Well enough that when the elevator opened tonight on a floor that is not in this building I knew it was not in this building before I had consciously processed what I was looking at.

I got out anyway because the doors were closing and I panicked and stepped forward instead of back and that is the decision I have been replaying for the last forty minutes.

I need to describe the floor because the description matters.

It looks like the fourteenth floor. That is the first and most important thing - it is not some alien hellscape, not obviously wrong in the way of nightmares, not darkness and teeth and impossible geometry. It looks like a corporate office floor in a mid-sized Pittsburgh building at eleven PM. Drop ceiling with fluorescent panels, most of them off, two or three flickering. Industrial carpet in a shade of grey-blue that exactly matches the carpet on fourteen. Cubicle partitions arranged in the open plan layout I walk through every morning. A row of glass-walled offices along the far wall that look exactly like the glass-walled offices on fourteen.

The wrongness is in the details. The cubicle partitions are slightly too tall - a few inches above standard, enough to be unsettling without being immediately identifiable as wrong. The carpet has a smell that the carpet on fourteen does not have, something underneath the standard office smell of recycled air and old coffee, something organic and faintly sweet that I noticed the moment the elevator doors opened and that I have been trying not to think about since. The fluorescent panels that are still on emit a light that is the right colour but the wrong quality - too flat, no gradation, the kind of light that fills a space without illuminating it, if that distinction makes sense. Everything is visible and nothing casts a shadow.

And the floor number above the elevator doors, which should read 14, reads 13. Backwards. The digits reversed, as though the sign was installed by someone who had been told what the numbers looked like but had never seen them in the correct orientation.

I saw all of this in the two seconds before the elevator doors closed behind me and I turned around and hit the call button and stood in front of the doors for six minutes pressing the button every thirty seconds and the elevator did not come back.

I called my girlfriend Ren at nine minutes in. I want to explain why I'm telling this partly through what happened on that call because Ren is who I was talking to when the important things happened and her reactions are part of the record of this and I want the record to be complete.

She answered on the second ring, which is normal for eleven PM on a weeknight - she stays up late, she reads in bed, she is the kind of person who answers the phone. I told her where I was and what had happened and there was a silence of maybe four seconds and then she said, very carefully, in the voice she uses when she is frightened but does not want to transmit the fear: *"Okay. Don't move from the elevator. Keep pressing the button. I'm going to call the building's emergency line and I'm going to keep you on the phone."*

I told her the elevator wasn't coming. She said to keep pressing anyway. I pressed for another three minutes while she put me on hold and called someone and came back and said the emergency line had rung out and she was trying the management company. I pressed the button and listened to the flat wrong light hum above me and looked at the too-tall cubicle partitions and breathed the sweet organic smell and tried not to think about what was making it.

Then I heard the sound and stopped thinking about anything else.

It came from somewhere deeper in the floor - past the cubicle farm, from the direction of the glass-walled offices along the far wall. A sound that I am going to describe as accurately as I can because accuracy feels important right now. It was rhythmic. It was wet. It had the cadence of something being repeated with patience and purpose, a sound that suggested process rather than accident, and it was moving. Not stationary. Whatever was making it was moving through the space in a pattern I couldn't map from the elevator because the cubicle partitions blocked my sightlines past the first few rows.

I told Ren I could hear something. She told me to stay completely still and not make any noise. I stayed still. I did not make noise. The sound continued for approximately two minutes and then stopped, and the silence after it was the specific quality of silence that follows when something stops moving rather than when something stops existing. It was still there. It had simply gone quiet.

Ren came back on the main call. She said she had reached someone at the management company who was going to call the building's overnight security guard and send him up. She said his name was Gary and he had worked the building for eleven years and knew it better than anyone and he would be there soon. I told her about the sound. Another four second silence. Then: *"Gary is coming. Stay by the elevator. Don't go deeper into the floor."*

I told her I had no intention of going deeper into the floor.

That was before the elevator call button went dark.

The button didn't break - or it didn't feel like breaking, which has a particular quality, a sudden absence. It went dark the way a light goes dark when something cuts the power upstream, the whole panel losing illumination at once, the numbers above the door going out, the doors themselves becoming just doors with no mechanism behind them. I hit the button twelve times in quick succession and nothing happened and I told Ren and she said *"Gary is on his way, Marcus, Gary is coming"* in a voice that was working very hard to be steady and I appreciated the effort and I did not tell her that Gary being on his way felt significantly less reassuring now that the elevator was dead.

I want to tell you that I stayed by the elevator. I want to tell you I stood in front of those dead doors for the next twenty minutes pressing a button that didn't work and waited calmly for rescue. I did not do that. I did that for approximately four minutes and then the sound started again - closer this time, two or three rows of cubicles away rather than at the far wall - and I made a decision that I stand by even now which is that standing completely still next to a non-functional elevator while something wet and rhythmic moves toward you through the cubicle rows is not actually the safe option just because it feels like waiting.

I moved. I went left along the elevator wall, away from the sound, staying low beneath the top of the cubicle partitions, moving as quietly as the industrial carpet allowed. I told Ren I was moving. She said *"Marcus"* in a way that meant both *okay* and *please don't* simultaneously, which is a thing she does and which under any other circumstances I find endearing.

I found the supply closet at the end of the left wall, a standard locked door that was not locked, and I went in and pulled it shut behind me and I have been here since.

The closet is approximately the size of a large bathroom. Shelving units on three walls, the shelves holding the standard inventory of a corporate supply closet - printer paper, toner cartridges, cleaning products, the miscellaneous physical infrastructure of office life. There is no light source and I am using my phone torch which I am trying to use sparingly for battery reasons. The door has no lock on the inside. I am holding it shut with my back against it and my feet braced against the base of the opposite shelving unit and I want to be honest that this is not a position I can maintain indefinitely.

Ren has been talking to me continuously. She does not have useful information - Gary has not arrived, the management company is not answering callbacks, she has called 911 and they have taken the address and said they are sending someone and she does not know when. She is talking because silence is worse and she knows it and I know it and we have been together for four years and she knows exactly what I need right now which is the sound of her voice telling me ordinary things, so she has been telling me about her day, about the book she was reading when I called, about the restaurant she wants to try on Saturday, and I have been listening and the ordinariness of it has been the only thing keeping my breathing regular.

Fifteen minutes ago the sound passed directly outside the closet door.

I felt it more than heard it - a vibration in the door against my back, a change in the air pressure in the small room, the sweet organic smell intensifying until it was all I could smell and I had to breathe through my mouth to keep from gagging. It paused outside the door. I know it paused because the vibration became stationary, a sustained presence on the other side of a door I was holding shut with my body weight and my braced feet and nothing else. Ren had gone quiet. I don't know if I had stopped responding to her or if she had heard something in my breathing that told her to stop talking. We were both silent. The thing on the other side of the door was silent. The flat wrong light hummed.

It stayed outside the door for what my phone tells me was four minutes and nine seconds. It felt like considerably longer. Then the presence shifted and the smell began to thin and the vibration moved away down the wall and I breathed through my nose again and Ren said *"Marcus"* very quietly and I said *"still here"* and she made a sound that was not a word.

That was eleven minutes ago. I have not heard it since but the smell has not gone back to baseline and I do not believe it has gone far.

My phone is at twenty two percent. I have turned the torch off to save battery and I am typing in the dark with the phone face down against my leg between sentences so the screen light doesn't show under the door.

I want to say some things while I have the battery to say them.

Ren - I know you're going to read this. I know because you're going to want to know everything and this is everything. I need you to know that the four years have been the best four years and the restaurant on Saturday sounds genuinely great and I want to go and I intend to go and Gary or the police or someone is coming and this is going to be a story we tell at dinner parties for years in the way that terrible things become funny with enough distance and I am going to be okay.

I am telling you that because I need to tell you that. I am telling you that because the alternative is not something I am willing to type.

To whoever else is reading this - the Calloway Building, downtown Pittsburgh, fourteenth floor, there is a floor between twelve and fourteen that does not appear on any building diagram and I am on it and something is on it with me and I do not know what it is and I do not know what it wants but I know it has been here longer than the building has and I know the building was built around it rather than the other way around and I know this because of what I found on the shelves in this supply closet when I first turned my torch on and which I have not told Ren because there is nothing useful she can do with the information.

On the middle shelf, between the toner cartridges and the reams of printer paper, there are photographs. Dozens of them. Polaroids, mostly, some older print formats, spanning what looks like decades of photographic technology. They are all of the same subject - this floor, these cubicles, this carpet, this flat wrong light - and in each one there is a person. Different people, different eras, different clothing. All of them are in the same position. All of them are against the wall beside the elevator with their backs pressed to the doors and their faces turned toward the camera with the same expression, which is the expression of someone who has been looking at the same thing for long enough that looking has stopped feeling like a choice.

None of the photographs have dates. None of them have names on the back. There are forty one of them and I have counted twice and I put them back on the shelf exactly where I found them because something about disturbing their arrangement felt like a mistake I could not afford.

Fourteen percent.

The smell is getting stronger again.

Ren I love you. The restaurant on Saturday. I mean it.

I'm going to stop typing now and hold the door.
