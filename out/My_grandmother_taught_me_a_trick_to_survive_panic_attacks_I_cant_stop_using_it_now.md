Panic disorder is perhaps the only condition capable of fusing, in the same instant, the experience of near death and the delirium of pure psychosis.

It is as if tendons and synapses twist to the point of collapse, pouring all the logic of the human body into an invisible cataclysm, the indecipherable labyrinth of the mind.

And then everything ends with a single, nameable blow.

Shredding palpitations over thin layers of reddened skin.

Drops of sweat running like melted wax.

The ground sinks, the lungs grow heavy as stone, and imaginary branches begin to sprout and twist around them, suffocating, binding, turning the search for oxygen into a desperate, non-consensual struggle of everything or nothing.

Then, it comes…

The darkness.

Not just the absence of light, but a magnificent mantle of blinding blackness, without flaws, without gaps, without white points over a velvet background, a black so deep it devours the very concept of black until nothing remains.

Only the most serene, absolute and merciless nothing.

That is how the attack begins.

And it only ends when hands, human hands, warm, real, hold your wrist and remind you that you are not dying.

“Shhh, calm down, breathe, you are not dying.”

“Everything will pass.”

“Everything… will pass.”

That was what my grandmother always told me.

“In the end, there will be a cure.”

Cure… if there is something worthy of that name, it stops being unreachable and begins to become possible when a human being, just one, holds you in the world and reminds you.

The darkness is not a black hole.

It is a sentence. It is not death compressing your lungs.

It is just a trance, a passing fold, a moment among many others, so irrelevant that at the end of the day it blends into erased memories like a poorly remembered dream.

That was it.

Not a cure, but a reminder, someone real who guaranteed that the darkness would dissipate.

And for me, that someone was my grandmother.

But life, unfortunately, has a cruel way of fading even the brightest color.

And as I grew older, now at nineteen, I realize that the certainty of her presence has become nothing more than traces.

Maybe part of that is because I was the one who found her dead on my ninth birthday.

And now that I think about it, most of my memories involving my grandmother are not the bedtime stories she told me during sleepless nights, they are not her delicious savory pies, not the smell of roasted roses that came from her knitted clothes, much less her sky-colored eyes.

What remains, what persists the most, is that day.

I ran.

I ran through the neighborhood, dodging cars and broken sidewalks, as if the world were an obstacle made to delay me.

My grandmother had not shown up to my birthday, nine years old.

And she was everything I knew about safety, about love and warmth.

When I arrived at her house, the door was half open.

The cold air entered before me, carrying a metallic smell that I still did not know how to name.

The hallway floor was stained, with a dark red, thick liquid, sticking to my shoes. Each step made something crack that should not.

Then, I saw her.

My grandmother lying there, her body arched as if the world had twisted every bone on purpose. The red liquid had spread across the bed, staining the sheets, the carpet, the crochet pieces and even the walls.

A river of liquid iron, cold and alive, as if it had awareness.

And beside her were the four coins I knew so well, aligned between her fingers, now stained and deformed by blood.

I recognized the coins instantly. They had been a symbol of our small ritual of calm, of control, but now they were only a monument of death.

The police arrived shortly after.

One of them tried to set limits, saying it had been suicide, but every word that came out of their mouths sounded like a lie. I stared at them, and in my child eyes there was only revolt.

“It was not suicide, that is not possible,” I screamed. But they stepped away, cold, official and indifferent.

And it was in that moment that something inside me changed.

The little girl I was disappeared, and in her place came someone who swore to distance herself from death.

I swore to distance myself from all the darkness that could suffocate my lungs, from all the anxiety that could turn into poison, from blood, from horror and from cruel silence, but not from the memories, not from the coins.

“Alice, can you pass me your notes from the last class? I slept through the whole thing.”

My thoughts were cut by a dull, feminine voice, and I look over my shoulder.

It is Eleanor, my classmate from the last semester of nursing school, or rather, the technical course. The professor is behind his desk, his eyes sliding slowly over a pile of tests being corrected.

But there are few papers to analyze, far fewer than the real number of students present in the room.

I clear my throat, trying to keep focus, while Eleanor taps the base of my chair with her muddy sneaker. I hand over the notebook and receive a quick kiss of thanks.

“Didn’t everyone turn in their test yet?” I ask, in the most casual tone I can.

She shrugs, a small gesture that carries a reluctance she cannot hide.

“I don’t know, maybe they were absent,” Eleanor says.

She goes back to her position, but the movement is not entirely natural. Nothing there seems natural.

That is how we all are, trying to survive months that feel more like a labyrinth with no exit, many without any other perspective. Most of us because, whether we like it or not, this is the most competitive course in the city.

The least popular spots were in the night shift, but I did not care. I was never enthusiastic about the academic world, and when the opportunity appeared, I grabbed it tightly.

I dragged Eleanor with me, almost as an act of self-preservation.

And now we are here, close to 10 PM, waiting for the last bus at the stop in front of the institution.

It was too good to be true, except for… that.

There was a student in our class, a boy who was somehow both old and young, ugly and beautiful, nothing and almost everything. Everyone raised their hands when their name was called, but his name never came.

I used to wonder, did he really exist or was it something from my mind?

Until one day, a first semester student, an older man with a long beard, hit his desk and said:

“Sorry about that, man.”

The next day, the man was found in his apartment, suffocated, a piece of his own leg shoved into his mouth, eyes wide, mouth torn.

Since then, I know he exists. I know he is real.

I feel a sharp pain in my temples and force my eyes to open and close, shifting my weight.

I run into Priscila, beautiful, red-haired, tall, impossible to ignore, with an angelic smile and a hand extended toward me, holding a coin.

“Here, Ali, you dropped this.”

The small metal object lands in my palm and a thread of electricity runs through my body. I thank her, trying to rationalize.

Maybe it is just feminine envy or a passing scare, but something about that perfect face is too perfect.

Perfect, perfect, too perfect.

“Seriously, I don’t know why you keep those coins,” Eleanor says as she stands up when we hear the last bell, wrapping her arm around me.

“You never pay the driver with cash. If this anxiety technique were scientifically tested, I would use it. But sorry, it just feels like something to make you feel stupid.”

“Balancing four coins without dropping them? Ugh, impossible.”

I smile despite myself, until I notice the boy, the half-nothing boy, watching us as we leave the room.

“Hey, did you see that?” I say.

“See what? If it’s not a hot guy, I’m ignoring it. I just want to get home.”

That was another striking feature of the night shift.

An exaggerated, almost absurd number of beautiful people.

Men and women sculpted by a god or by a disturbingly skilled surgeon. Pale skin, perfect pores, perfect smiles, almost mathematical proportions that cornered you in every suffocating space of the classroom, even on cold nights.

Sometimes they talked among themselves, in whispering voices, contained laughter, in harmony as perfect as their faces, almost like lullabies, soothing just to wake you up with a jolt the next instant.

They were so beautiful it was impossible to look at them for too long. Not that Eleanor and I were ugly, it was just difficult to process a beauty that seemed not to belong to this world.

Some simply left the room whenever they wanted, others made strange noises in the back, imperceptible to almost everyone, but that stayed carved into my skin.

All of these actions were ignored by the teachers, but the normal students, us, received meticulous attention, and one by one began to disappear.

Empty desks, consecutive absences, names erased from school records.

Some vanished suddenly, like the short girl who asked to go to the bathroom in the middle of a presentation and never came back.

Others, like the bearded man, were found dead at home or on the street, always in grotesque, sick, inexplicable ways.

And now only the two of us remained.

The bus stop was well lit, and it was terribly long.

Every time I left at that hour and the cold wind moved my long hair, I felt the attack approaching. The darkness surrounded me like a predator stalking.

Quickly, I took the coins from my pocket and started the ritual.

It was simple, almost childish.

Align four coins on the ring finger, holding them with the middle finger, without moving and without letting them fall.

My grandmother taught me a week before she died, and maybe it was the only clear memory I had of her.

Over the years, I became a specialist.

I could do it with my eyes closed, with my non-dominant hand, even with smaller coins.

It always saved me.

Eleanor, as always, rolled her eyes and leaned against the long bench beside the trash can filled with rotten remains. I sat beside her, waiting for the cold sweat and palpitations to fade.

We did not speak in those moments.

We just stayed silent, staring at the ground.

During the day, the bus stop was normal, full of people, noise, buses coming and going, but at night… at night it transformed.

The silence was so absolute that we could hear the friction of our clothes when we moved our arms or the cracking of our tired bones when crossing our legs.

We were afraid of being robbed, yes, but deep down I knew that was the least of our problems.

At the end of the stop, there was a homeless man.

He did not look like a man, he looked like what was left of one.

His skin was gray, too thin, stretched over sharp bones, his beard gathered in hardened clumps full of crust.

Sometimes, I swore I could see a larva writhing between the strands before disappearing.

Beside him was always the dog.

If that thing could still be called that.

Its back was bent at a grotesque angle, as if its spine had been broken and healed wrong, its mouth permanently open, teeth worn and cracked down to the gums, its thick purple tongue dragging on the filthy ground.

The sound that came from it was not breathing, it was a steady leak, like wind escaping from a punctured lung.

They never moved.

Not in any obvious way.

But every time I adjusted the coins, the man seemed to blink right after.

Every time I changed the position of my leg, the dog dragged its paw a second later.

As if they were puppets tied to my body.

As if I were their rhythm.

“Don’t look, Ali,” Eleanor whispered without moving her lips, her eyes fixed ahead, her leg shaking in spasms.

“If you look, they will notice us.”

The coins trembled in my hands. I squeezed them until they marked my skin.

The silence became liquid, dense, sticking to our throats.

Then the dog lifted its head and blinked, not up and down, but sideways, with a vertical eyelid.

At that exact moment, the deep rumble of the bus engine broke the night.

Finally, it arrived.

We got on without exchanging a word.

The driver was the same as always, a fat man in a wrinkled uniform and a dead stare, avoiding looking at us as if carrying two passengers was a personal curse.

He always seemed to know more than he should.

He always seemed afraid.

The doors closed and we moved.

The interior of the bus was empty, except for us.

The yellow neon lights flickered with a low hum, illuminating rows of worn seats marked with scratches. Sometimes, I swore something moved inside those marks, as if the foam itself breathed.

Eleanor was not normal.

Her shoulders, usually relaxed, were rigid, hunched, and she bit her own lip until it bled, but did not seem to notice. The blood ran down the corner of her mouth and disappeared into her collar, as if absorbed by the fabric.

I looked at her from the corner of my eye, but quickly returned to the coins balanced on my finger. It was all I had, my shield.

Then she spoke.

“I’m getting off.”

“What?” I choked, tightening the coins until the cold metal burned my skin.

“But your stop is still far.”

She did not answer.

She just stood up and walked to the front, her dry footsteps echoing through the empty aisle.

The driver did not ask anything, did not look, just pulled the lever with his eyes fixed on the fogged glass.

The bus stopped somewhere I did not recognize.

A random avenue, no houses, no lights, just twisted trees like columns of smoke.

“Eleanor, wait.”

I stood up, the coins trembling in my hand.

She turned for just a second.

Her smile was too wide.

Too white.

A perfect crack.

“I have to go,” she said, and stepped out.

The doors closed behind her with a dry thud, and the bus moved again.

My heart beat in the same rhythm as the coins I forced to stay balanced.

I could not let them fall.

I could not.

Until, against my own instinct, I turned my face back.

And there, in the last row, he was sitting.

The half-nothing, half-everything boy.

His eyes fixed on me.

The coins slipped.

They hit the floor with a sound so loud it felt like lightning tearing the sky apart.

I bent down quickly, hands trembling, feeling my heart tearing through my ribs.

The coins were there, cold and scattered. I gathered them one by one, forcing them back into position, into the only possible balance.

I lifted my face, trying to prove to myself he was still there.

He was.

Then I felt it.

A warm breath, so close to my ear it moved strands of my hair.

And a voice came, slow, corroding from the inside.

“What a shame, Alice…”

“He is always above you.”

I don’t know how I got home.

I don’t remember getting off the bus.

Only my mother’s scream, shaking me, her phone trembling in her hands.

They had found Eleanor.

Crushed in a deserted street.

They said it looked like a bus accident.

That same night, the nursing school caught fire.

The flames consumed everything.

Teachers, classmates, every perfect and empty face from those rooms.

Everyone died.

Everyone… except me.

They offered me a spot in the day shift.

They offered me money for silence.

But nothing buys what remains.

And nowadays, I never let go of the coins.

I know I will get tired.

I know they will fuse into my skin.

And I will fail.

And I will end like my grandmother.

Because I saw him.

I finally saw him.

He was not a man.

He was not an animal.

He was stuck to the ceiling of my room, like an inverted spider, bones broken at impossible angles and still moving, each fracture functioning as a new joint.

His skin was too white, almost transparent, a loose fabric showing black veins pulsing like worms beneath the surface.

His face moved, sometimes on the shoulder, sometimes on the abdomen, sometimes between the bent legs, as if it had no fixed place.

And when the mouth appeared…

It did not speak.

It tore.

It opened on the forehead, and inside it coins fell endlessly, a metallic clinking, teeth of oxidized metal grinding against each other, like a safe that devours everything.

The eyes were scattered.

One on the arm.

Another on the ankle.

Another in the middle of the hand pointing at me.

All looking at me.

Always at me.

That is when I realized.

He was not on the ceiling.

The ceiling…

was him.

And I, small, fragile, balancing my coins as if balancing my own life…

was trapped beneath him.

And now I cannot let go of the coins.

Because if I do…

he might come down.
