# Part 2: The Files They Didn’t Expect Anyone To Read

I told myself I wasn’t going to open the drive again after I posted. For a few hours, I actually believed that. Writing everything out felt like enough, like maybe saying it out loud would put some distance between me and whatever happened at that clinic. That didn’t last. Within two days, three different parents contacted me through old intake emails I hadn’t checked in years. They didn’t know each other, and none of them mentioned my post, but every message sounded strangely similar. Each of them said their child had started talking about meeting other people who were “like them.” Not friends. Not classmates. They all used that exact wording, like they were repeating something they’d been told.

That was enough to make me open the archive again last night.

There’s a folder in the drive labeled Long-Term Cohort Social Integration. I skipped it the first time because I thought it was just routine follow-up notes. It isn’t set up like normal patient records. There are no doctor signatures, no legal disclaimers, nothing that looks like it was meant to be reviewed by families or regulators. The entries read more like observation notes, like someone was tracking patterns across multiple patients at once instead of focusing on individual care. Batch 731 shows up in those notes constantly, and the way it’s written makes it sound like they were watching how that entire group developed together, even when those kids were growing up in different states with different families.

One document talks about something called Spontaneous Group Convergence. Basically, it tracks patients from the same batch who ended up moving to the same cities around the same time once they reached their late teens and early twenties. The report includes maps showing clusters forming across the country. What made me uncomfortable wasn’t just the clustering. It was the way the report described it. It didn’t sound like they were surprised. It sounded like they were checking off milestones they expected to happen.

I looked up some of the addresses listed in the report just to see if they were real. A few of the apartment complexes mentioned used to be owned by companies connected to New Horizons’ parent organization. I can’t prove that means anything, but it doesn’t feel like a coincidence either.

The part that really made me sick was a section called Behavioral Incident Tracking. These weren’t official police reports or psychiatric evaluations. They were internal summaries of violent or disturbing events tied to specific patients. Most of the incidents either never resulted in charges or were explained away by outside investigations.

One case involved a nineteen-year-old male from Batch 731 who volunteered at a veterinary clinic. Over several weeks, small animals at the clinic started dying under what staff thought were equipment problems involving anesthesia. The police report closed the case as accidental malfunction. The internal clinic notes included a follow-up interview where the patient admitted he had been watching the animals during procedures because he was curious about how they reacted when they realized they couldn’t move. The summary described him as calm, cooperative, and emotionally detached. It also said he showed “strong compatibility with cohort behavioral markers,” which is a phrase I had never seen before and honestly wish I still hadn’t.

Another file described four Batch 731 patients who attended the same private university without knowing each other beforehand. According to school disciplinary records, they were investigated for hazing. The clinic’s internal report goes into more detail. It says the group singled out another student and spent several hours restraining and terrifying them as some kind of group exercise. The victim withdrew from school and refused to file charges. The clinic’s notes didn’t call it assault or abuse. They described it as evidence that the group showed coordinated responses under stress.

The deeper I went, the more consistent the pattern became. There were multiple entries tied to neighborhoods where family pets and small wildlife were found mutilated. Local authorities blamed stray animals or wild predators. The clinic’s notes tracked those same incidents by date and location, often adding comments about patients from Batch 731 spending increased time together within days of each event. The wording made it feel less like concern and more like they were documenting progress.

Earlier today, I received another call from a blocked number. I didn’t answer, but they left a voicemail. It sounded like the same woman who called before. She spoke slowly, like she was choosing every word carefully. She said reviewing incomplete files could lead to unnecessary panic and told me that a full archive still exists at an offsite storage facility that was never actually shut down. She gave me an address. I checked it against old employee directories and property records, and it matches one of the clinic’s former document warehouses. I never told anyone I still had access to these files, which means the only people who would know that are people who worked in administration when I did.

I wish the phone call was the only thing bothering me, but since reopening the drive I’ve noticed smaller things that are harder to explain. Two nights ago, I realized I kept rereading the same paragraph over and over without remembering what it said, especially sections describing group incidents involving multiple patients. This morning, I found three missed calls from a number I don’t recognize and a text message that only contained a timestamp. When I checked the timestamp against the archive, it matched the date of one of the animal mutilation reports connected to Batch 731. When I tried calling the number back, I got an automated message saying the number didn’t exist.

The worst thing I found tonight wasn’t even tied to patient behavior. It was a staff memo labeled Secondary Exposure Risk. The document listed employee identification numbers flagged for long-term observation after extended contact with Batch 731 patients or records. I checked the list twice because I thought I was imagining it. My employee ID is on that list, along with a note recommending monitoring continue even after staff left the company.

I don’t know if going to that storage facility is a terrible idea or the only way to figure out what they were doing, but I can’t stop thinking about what the man said at the end of his voicemail. He said I was closer to the project than I realized and that if I wanted answers, I needed to see the rest of the files in person.

I haven’t decided if I’m going to go yet, but I’ve already looked up directions more than once, and I can’t shake the feeling that someone already knows I’m considering it.
