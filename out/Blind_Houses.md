I know they say you should have a plan in place before enacting this sort of thing, this moment, when it comes. But I also know what they say about those who make plans. And I had endured enough by now to know what *really m*ade God laugh, or at least what was enough to make him look away.

So I wheeled the trash out to the curb that evening, later than usual, a backpack nestled between my shoulder blades, the rumble of the wheels behind me like suppressed thunder. I parked it on the curb, and stared down the uninterrupted row of cans that lined the street. It was recycling too, that week, but I didn't go back for the other can. Bringing both down doubled my risk of discovery, even though I had dissolved enough Carisoprodol in his last drink to knock out several men the size of our usual trash collectors.

I stepped off the curb and started walking.

The houses in our neighborhood were all dark. But it had been late, when I made my decision. The street seemed longer without a car. He had hid the keys from me a day ago, a late-coming reciprocity for a time early on in our relationship when I had hidden them from him at the bar. I had learned since then. I had been able to follow the rules, for a while. They changed so often. I had even prided myself on following them longer than most.

But no car, and no phone, now meant a longer way to go.

It was odd, I thought then, all those dark windows, all those blind houses. It was late, but I saw no evidence of the usual night owls, no graveyard shift workers starting their commute. No yawning garage mouths with someone tooling away at a work bench within.

I had walked ten, maybe fifteen minutes, before I heard a soft cry. It wasn't an infant crying, my first assumption, but a cat meowing plaintively outside one of the houses. I stopped on the sidewalk and watched a moment as she pawed repeatedly for entry, more and more frantic. Still, I couldn't place what was wrong, what had made me stop to take in what was surely a common enough scene, a cat wanting to be let in.

I realized the cat wasn't scratching at the front door of the home. Not because it wasn't in the right place to do so, there on the welcome mat. But because there was no door, for human or cat—just an uninterrupted stretch of siding that ran the length of the porch and disappeared around the corner. Startled, I glanced up, eyes mapping the exterior of the home. From garden beds to rooftop, the house was blank as a slate, with no doors nor windows to allow entry or exit.

The cat yowled suddenly and dashed off the porch, vanishing into the bushes with a lash of its tail. I started, my breaths ragged in my own head. I reminded myself to remain calm. Maybe I was only turned around, somehow on the backside of the neighborhood...?

I circled the perimeter, conscious of trespassing, of being in another's yard, of borrowed time ever running down, but those fears faded fast and were swiftly replaced when I realized I'd circled back around to the front of the house and all sides of it were the same.

No doors, no windows.

I started walking again, faster this time, heart thumping, legs pumping, to get to a destination that still remained amorphous in my head. *Bus shelter. Church.* It was suddenly more of an effort to keep myself from running, but I reined in my pace and tried not to jostle my backpack; I clutched it tightly by its straps just in case, like a hiker on her last stretch to the summit. I took in my surroundings again, eyes scanning for further discrepancies.

And I found them. Once-familiar houses bearing down on me as faceless as cubes, lifelessly mathematical, no doors, no windows; their garages, too, closed tight as bank vaults and tombs. No wonder the street seemed darker than normal, with no windows to pour their light, and not even a sliver of moon hanging in the sky. The only glow came from the soft effulgence of the streetlamps, and even those seemed more scattered than usual, the quality of their light muted and strange, like they were in too much danger of drawing attention to themselves.

I veered across the next lawn and mounted the steps to a different house. I groped for a doorbell and, finding nothing, hammered my fist hard against the outside. I held my ear up to the wall and listened, but heard nothing within. I was about to raise my voice to cry out to someone, *anyone,* then froze. There were other sounds: a thumping, and an overfull choking, coming from around the left side of the house.

A thrill of fear shot through me. I didn't have anything on me to use as a weapon, hadn't even thought about it. I had dissolved my last weapon in his drink. I had thought about money, alongside the most important thing I needed to take with me—but he had kept my keys locked away beside my phone, which was also my flashlight.

Cautiously I stepped down off the porch. I stilled and listened, like a deer alert to danger in a dark wood. The sound came again. *Gurgle, thump.* Was someone else outside the home? I moved slowly and peered around the side of the house.

At first I couldn't discern what I was looking at. No windows or doors on the west-facing side, as expected, but there was something there in the shadows all the same. A hooded light sticking out from the house hummed above a set of concrete steps that indicated an outlet used to exist there, a former door to a small paved path that connected the house to the garage. I drew nearer to have a look.

There was a face embedded in the side of the house: a man's, half-emerging. Beside it, fingers twitched and scrabbled like dying insects, sunk to their knuckle bones in inflexible wood. The shoe of the one leg that had made it outside shuffled like it was dancing, then kicked back against the wall in a desperate bid. *Thump. Thump.*

One of the man's eyes rolled in its socket to fix on me. His other eye was bulged and seemed severed at the nerve, a dead globe staring sideways out at his neighbor's lawn. He had been trapped mid-exit when his door vanished, probably on his way to take his own trash out. He gargled and choked. I recognized by his look the sound he made for what it really was: a scream.

I backed away, hands shaking. The choking became more pronounced. The man's exposed tongue wagged frantically, and I could see its root. Maybe he had been speaking, or screaming, when the house closed on him. I could see a glimpse of it, the house, and it was inside his throat. Unless there was something else, something hellish, keeping him alive, he would suffocate on it, but not soon enough.

A wind kicked up, stirring the discarded trash at his side. The man's undamaged eye rolled and fixed on something just behind me. Was he dying, or was something else making his pupil expand as if ruptured?

Then I *did* hear something. I could *feel* something, coming up the driveway, racing toward me, barreling toward me. I saw the grubs of the man's fingers spasm; they seemed to reach for my backpack, as if in an attempt to pull himself free. I leapt away. I retreated into the shadows of the next yard, turned, and fled without looking back.

Moments later I discovered I was wrong about the man. He *could* still scream.

It was when the scream cut off abruptly that I finally broke into a run. I began to seize and yank on the door handles of every parked car I encountered. All locked. By then I was sprinting, trying only half, and then none at all, down the unfinished assembly line, because the more I ran the fewer cars seemed to have any doors on them at all, as if something conscious in the world was only now addressing them as an afterthought, robbing them of function, or forgetting entirely what their function had once been.

And there was still something behind me, something huge, careening down the street in howling pursuit. But there was something ahead of me, too. The neighborhood playground loomed in dark carnival shapes, standing still as a graveyard, nothing like itself during the light of day, back when I was still allowed to visit. I pelted up the hill, sliding and falling to my knees as I scrambled, as the street lights popped and exploded behind me like glassy stars going out one by one. I found the structure I hadn't realized I'd been seeking and lunged inside—a doorless public restroom spiraling into itself like the whorl of a shell. I pulled my backpack around to my front and folded my body over it, cradling it close, shuddering.

Maybe the thing that was God or played God in that world, that was Behemoth and Minotaur among the shuttered streets, didn't recognize doors or entries. Maybe we were safe inside pregnable walls and maybe we weren't. Outside a tornado raged, a wild wind and lunatic roar, as the force pursuing us ripped and tore through reality, seeking but never finding.

I sat on that cold, damp concrete for a long time, staring at a grateless floor drain. I sat until I was certain daylight should have risen over the world, and by then my daughter was crying. I gently rocked us both. The cough medicine I had given her to make this all easier had worn off. Now she was cold, and hungry, and tears poured down the angry boil of her face.

How many hours had it been since we had hid ourselves away?

I pushed to my feet eventually, hugging her close, and went outside. It was still dark out. The playground had been torn apart: structures overturned, animal spring riders decapitated, the merry-go-round on its side still spinning like a wheel. The swings were a snarl of rusted chains that would never come untangled. And there were giant footprints, maybe claw marks, in the wood chips.

I wandered through the world for a long time after that, delirious. My daughter's wailing grew louder and more incessant. We both needed shelter, and sleep. A soft place to land. But there was nowhere. I couldn't think, couldn't plan. Should have had one all along. I walked. When my feet could go no further I stopped, swaying in place, and finally, I looked up. A rectangular seam of light beamed in front of me, only a few paces from the sidewalk I dragged us along. I recognized the door. I recognized the house it belonged to, where there was only one can out in front.

We were both so exhausted, and so afraid. We had been this way before, and survived.

*We went for a walk.* I practiced the words in my head as I climbed the steps, and prayed my voice wouldn't shake when the time came. *We couldn't sleep, we didn't want to wake you. We just went for a walk.* The backpack was gone, stuffed down deep beneath tomorrow's trash. I cradled my daughter and rehearsed. From behind me, I heard another sound—another mouth—crying to be fed. On the final step I turned, and spotted the cat. It sat on the sidewalk behind me, eyes gleaming, tail swaying; the tail seemed to curl into a question mark as our eyes met. I stood on the threshold a moment longer.

"You can't come in. It isn't safe," I told it, and shut the door.
