As a kid, I often wondered if Vincent knew that I watched his torment. 

There was about fifty feet between the attic window and his pitiful shack. At that distance, could he spot the glint of my eyes pressed into the glass, staring at him, day in and day out? I hoped not. He wouldn’t understand, and I never had a chance to explain. Growing up, my parents only allowed me out of the attic on my birthday. There’d be cake, a few quick laps around our four-story Victorian under the cover of darkness, and then it was back upstairs for another year. They seemed hellbent on minimizing the town's exposure to my *disfigurement*. Not that my scales were contagious or anything - just repugnant and a stain on the family name is all. 

With not much else to do, I’d stare at Vincent through the attic window. 

Sometimes he’d be pacing around his shack - smoking, twitching, muttering to no one - and he’d suddenly freeze, head snapping up in my direction. I’d duck below the windowsill, heart racing. Had he seen me? Surely not. I was small and mighty far away. He must have been staring up at the moon or the sun or maybe nothing at all. Still, catching his gaze made him feel more real, and the shame wouldn’t be far behind. In those moments, I wished I could beam a message into his mind. I was desperate for him to know that I didn’t savor his misery. I didn’t agree with the way they treated him.

I was just fucking bored. 

Recently, I haven’t really thought about whether Vincent was aware of my constant ogling. I just couldn't find the will to care anymore - apathy had dug its roots in deep. Then, one night, I saw his house beginning to burn, and for the first time in years, all I could think was:

Can Vincent see me watching?

His tall, paper-thin silhouette floated from window to window, but as far as I could tell, he wasn’t attempting to escape. 

I didn’t need to radio the sheriff’s office, right? 

No. Others had noticed the fire. I could see their shadows flickering in the pyre’s red glow. Shadows leaning over porch railings, shadows trudging across lawns, shadows lounging on curbs. One of those onlookers *must* have already reported it. They hated him, but they wouldn’t just let him burn, would they? 

Any minute now. 

I’d hear the wailing sirens curling over the mountainside any minute now. 

\- - - - -

Everyone - and I mean *everyone* \- hated Vincent. 

It was more than just a shared feeling, too. Their hatred was borderline performative, a sadistic circus where everyone had their own practiced routine. 

Elderly Mr. Peterson liked insults because he could sling them from bed. Whenever Vincent left his house, the rim of a megaphone would creep out of the old man’s bedroom window. There’d be a screech, followed by a flurry of \*“Rat Bastard!”\*s and \*“Flithy Degenerate!”\*s echoing across the long, suburban boulevard.

The Riley boys were more barbaric. They liked to smear dog shit all over Vincent's property when he wasn’t watching. Their family owned a trio of great danes, so ammunition was never in short supply.

Mom’s torment bothered me the most, because it seemed to be the most meticulous. She liked to take Dad’s empty beer bottles and shatter them across Vincent’s crumbling stoop. Then, she would spend at least an hour carefully rearranging the fractured glass. There was an artistry to it; a pattern most likely to find its mark. Mom often gushed about the mosaic of bloodstains adorning that slab of concrete. Guess she found her mark more times than not. 

Multiply that all by fifty, and you'll only have the vaguest idea of what Vincent was subjected to on a daily basis. Honestly, it seemed like more pain and humiliation than any one person could bear, yet, year after year, Vincent endured their festive wrath. He never fought back, never complained, never fled this wretched place, but he never broke, either.

Why did the whole town torture him? What unforgivable transgression had Vincent committed to deserve such treatment? 

Couldn’t tell you. 

One night, I mustered the courage to ask Dad why we all hate Vincent. He was sitting at the edge of my bed, forcing a smile as he patted my leg through the comforter. His sterile latex gloves released small bursts of powder with every robotic movement, as if the attic wasn’t dusty enough already. 

“Oh, don’t fret about the *whys,* bud. Just remember: life is a hard-hearted spectacle, and we all have our roles to play, including Vincent. Think about it - even the prettiest churches have floorboards for everyone else to step on, right?” 

I wanted to respond with something along the lines of: *You’ve never allowed me to leave the property, so how the fuck would I know?* At the age of eight, I didn’t exactly have the vocabulary to express that particular contempt. 

“Father loves our spectacle!” he cried, cocking his head up, clasping his hands together, staring at the ceiling as if he could peer through the rafters.

“And Father’s love is why we’re all so blessed. With nice, big, comfy houses. With perfect health and perfect happiness. With freedom.” There was a knowing cruelty in the way his tongue loitered on the word *freedom*; a lilt in his voice that betrayed satisfaction. 

“Could Father...fix me?” I asked, feeling the incessant itch of my jagged scales scraping against the cotton bedding.

Dad’s gaze drifted down until it landed on me, beady eyes digging into my skull. He rose from the bed, slowly reached over to my nightstand, and clicked the lamp off. 

The attic plunged into darkness.

“If we play our roles, Father grants us what we deserve. The weak-minded. The fornicators. The disfigured. All of us.”Floorboards moaned as he strode across the room, traversing the blackness.

“Even you...” He whispered, creaking the door open and stepping into the stairwell. 

“Even Vincent.” The door shuddered closed. One by one, Dad snapped the deadbolts into place. 

*Snap. Snap. Snap.*

*- - - - -*

Outside, the fire grew. 

Plumes of smoke leaked from the roof’s many unpatched holes. Vincent’s squat, battered home shimmered with rising heat. I squeezed the transmitter to my shortwave radio until my knuckles hurt. Everything was still. White starshine pushed down from the cloudless night sky, glinting through the mass of pine trees that encircled our remote slice of Appalachia. 

It was almost serene; very nearly, but not quite. 

A visible *pop* made me leap back. 

The front window exploded, flames lashing out into the night. I searched the street, hoping for movement, for someone to act. I pictured my neighbors dashing forward, a bucket of tepid sink water swinging in each hand, but the shadows stayed frozen. No one even flinched.

Darkness enveloped the street at both ends; no wailing sirens, no flashing lights, nothing. 

I tuned the frequency to the sheriff’s office and pressed the push-to-talk. Static hissed through the receiver. I paused, lips trembling, words catching in my throat. This was against the rules. I was only allowed to operate the radio if someone *in the house* was actively dying or already dead. Dad always warned that the consequences for misuse would be swift and severe. 

I felt a burst of heat behind my eyes; a shiver of rebellious indignation. 

Who *cares* anymore? 

Mom and Dad were gone. Buried deep. Dead for almost a month now. 

The rules died with them. 

“There’s a house on fire...” I said into the receiver, “The street is Stonecross Boulevard...please send help, over.” 

I released the button. A gruff male voice sizzled over the intercom. 

“This is Sheriff Donahue. Whose house? Over.” 

Vincent’s name lingered on my tongue. I swallowed it. 

“Uhh...well...my next-door neighbor’s...over.” He then asked for the *exact* address, of course. I hesitated. Provided my own instead. The runaround was futile, and his realization was palpable. 

“...I see. We’ll be there soon, over.” 

I set the receiver on the sill and turned from the murky glass. I couldn’t watch any longer. My whole life had been a goddamned spectator sport.

Behind me, the sheriff relayed one final message. 

“You best stay inside ‘till it’s time, Nate.” 

My heart pounded. Grim curiosity gnawed through my stomach. 

He knew my name. 

And time for *what?*

I couldn’t help myself. I turned back. 

My eyes widened as the center of Vincent’s roof caved in, forming a hellish casm of splintered wood, firelight, and smoke. A stew of warring emotions cascaded through my veins - disgust, anger, helplessness, shame, despair - the expected feelings. But there was something else, too. An emotion I didn’t understand. An emotion I failed to suppress. 

The image of Vincent burning flashed into focus. Charbroiled skin, black on bloody red, crackling like pig grease on a hot pan, fire eating him alive.  

And a tiny smirk slithered across my lips.

***Lamellar Ichthyosis*** **-**

That’s the technical term for my spontaneous, genetic defect. 

*Lamellar* is Latin for thin, metal plates. *Ichthyosis* roughly translates to “fish-like”. Put them together, and that’s how my parents saw me: a monstrously tall, landlocked halibut in an ungodly suit of armor.

For twenty-two years, I convinced myself it could be worse through one simple mantra. 

I might be covered in *fish scales,* every inch of me checkered with leathery, brittle plates, but at least I wasn’t Vincent. My skin might tug at my lips and eyelids, leaving every crease slightly open no matter how hard I tried to close them, but at least I wasn’t Vincent. I might be a pitiful outcast, a bottom-feeder locked away to protect my parents’ name, but at least I wasn’t Vincent.

God, I always assumed I had time. 

Time to prove to them that there’s a person imprisoned beneath the hard, dark flesh. A person who deserves the sun, just like everyone else. 

Then, a week before the fire, they died. 

Car crash. Mangled beyond recognition, according to a female voice that unexpectedly hummed through the radio one morning when I could have sworn the damn thing had been turned off.  

I set my book down on the battered desk, paused, and laughed. Not out of happiness, per se. Their deaths were just so sudden, so bizarre. The surprise of it all was amusing. 

But worry crept in quickly. 

I stood and started pacing the attic. The cramped, slanted walls were always claustrophobia-inducing, but now it felt like they were really closing in for the kill. Who was going to deliver food and water to me? The door was locked. The window was bolted shut. I was trapped. Entombed. My mind was spinning in frantic circles. Should I try to radio the voice back? Or radio the sheriff like I’d been taught? Break through the window and scream for help? Break through the window, slam my eyes shut, and leap out, headfirst? 

I don’t know why exactly I tried the knob. Dad never left the door unlocked, but panic makes you do funny things, I suppose. 

I flicked my wrist and felt my mouth go dry.

It turned.  

I stared down into the narrow, curling staircase and thought:

*Holy shit - I’m free.*

I wasn’t.

My cage had just gotten bigger.

As dawn broke, a fire truck puttered to a slow stop in front of the smoldering ruins. 

Drowsy firefighters slumped from the vehicle. They dragged hoses across patches of half-scorched earth and focused their efforts on the edges of Vincent’s property. Heaven forbid a stray ember gathered enough oxygen to ignite something that actually mattered. A cruiser marked *SHERIFF’S DEPARTMENT* rolled up behind the fire truck. A short, heavyset man pulled himself from the driver’s seat; presumably, that was the man I’d spoken to on the radio, *Sheriff Donahue*. He trudged across the lawn and disappeared into the wreckage. A firefighter with thick, bottlecap glasses and curly black hair followed close behind. 

They waddled from the ruins a few minutes later, body bag see-sawing awkwardly between them. Halfway to the waiting ambulance, the fireman abruptly let go. Some part of Vincent’s corpse slammed into the sidewalk as he reached into his pocket and procured a carton of cigarettes, absentmindedly sliding one out of the package and slipping it into position. Donahue stomped up to him and ripped the cigarette from his lips. The sheriff turned beet-red, screaming loud enough that I even caught a few expletives, shaking the cancer stick at him like it was a knife he was threatening to use. 

I chuckled; the whole scene was a bit slapstick. The sheriff jammed his paw into the man’s pocket, snatched the carton, and stuffed it into his own. The black-haired firefighter trudged away, head down, defeated. Donahue collecting an idiot tax, I figured. I reached for the blinds, ready to end the show, but something made my heart plummet. 

Vincent lived alone. Not once in my twenty-two years had I seen *anyone* else enter that home. Not a single, solitary time. 

So why were those two men stumbling back *into* the ruins, each carrying *another* body bag? 

My stomach twisted when they returned with a second corpse. The bewilderment was downright painful. 

But there was more.

Including Vincent, emergency services retrieved a dozen bodies from the ruins of that tiny hovel. 

\- - - - -

The night before the fire was thick with heat and moonless.

I stood in the backyard, staring at the forest, trying to coerce my feet over the property line. The chirping of crickets drifted through the air. Every day since my parents had died, I’d been venturing a little farther from the attic. I grit my teeth. Heart thundering, I channeled every ounce of myself into the ball of my right foot. No one was around to stop me; my parents were gone, torn to shreds by asphalt and metal. I could do it. 

I could just...leave. 

“Go,” I whispered through a clenched jaw, “Walk. *Fucking* move.” My fish-scaled feet refused to budge. Passing over the property line was against the rules, after all. 

I felt a sudden twinge of paranoia.

Was anyone watching me? 

I whipped around. All I saw was empty sidewalks, foggy streetlamps, and Vincent’s shack crouched among the imperial, multilevel homes that otherwise dominated Stonecross Boulevard. I sighed and faced the woods again.

Something new caught my eye. 

Ahead of me, an orange pinpoint gleamed in the grass, flaring and fading. Just a firefly, I figured. Something was wrong, though. Fireflies hover; the pinpoint was inert, deathly still. I squinted, breath catching once I saw the faint outline of a gaunt man lying on the ground *behind* the light. 

Vincent lurched to his feet. The lit cigarette fell from his lips.

“If you’re willing to heed the opinion of an undesirable such as myself, I think you’re on the right track.” His voice was low and wet. He took a long step towards me, paying no mind to the fallen cigarette, still burning a dim orange at his feet. 

“Get the fuck out while you can.” He grinned, gray, splintered teeth jutting from pale pink gums. I couldn’t hone in on the color or even the shape of his eyes. They seemed to be perpetually shifting around his sockets; a slow and steady churn of both form and hue. 

I *should* have been terrified. 

Moreover, he *should* have been revolted; my scales were very much on display. 

He held out a liverspotted, skeletal hand. There was a new cigarette in his palm. I shook my head. He shrugged, shoved it into the pocket of his grease-stained chinos, and sort of just collapsed, leaning backwards until he tipped over. Landed on the earth with a soft *thud* and instantly began rambling. 

“Speaking of which - you know what they should start calling us? *The Anathema*. It’s perfect! Every individual meaning of the word fits. Anathema as in someone who is vehemently hated by the parish; Anathema as in something that is cursed and separated from God; Anathema as in an offering that was created only to be destroyed. Expendable, star-crossed nobodies - that’s us! The purposefully loathed, the vermin that hang from these hallowed pines like Christmas ornaments, glittering and terrible and divine and - “

Vincent jabbered on, and on, and on, head resting in the grass, shifting eyes fixed on the sky. Not sure how long I stood there. An hour? Might have been longer. His relentless prattle was entrancing at first. There was a cryptic eloquence to the endless monologue. 

As he rambled, two details fought for my attention. 

First off, he was barefoot. My eyes traced the length of his heels. The skin was dirt-caked but smooth and relatively unblemished. I counted only one, crescent-shaped scar at the center of his left heel. Mom shattered beer bottles against his stoop every Sunday morning for as long as I’d been alive. She planted decades of improvised booby traps that seemed to draw blood more often than not. 

Shouldn’t he have *more* scars?

But the more pressing detail was the stone lying between my feet. 

It was a slender piece of sandstone, shaped like an arrow, tapering to a razor-sharp point. The rock was unnaturally smooth, as if it had been whetted. Heat congregated in my chest as I studied it.

Had it always been there? I couldn’t remember. 

All I knew for sure was that the universe was pleading for me to use it. 

Violent ambitions invaded my skull from somewhere high above. Pressure mounted. The heat in my chest roared. 

I needed to leave before I did the unconsciousable. 

I began backing away. 

The moonless sky came alive. It throbbed and contorted, as if something were trying to push its way through the black curtain. Crickets screamed from within the pines. My scales vibrated like live cockroaches were welded to my skin. 

I was about to turn and run. Away from that godforsaken house. Away from poor Vincent. No plan, no destination in mind, just *away*. This place was horrible. These people were disgusting and cruel. I just had to move my goddamned feet. It was that easy.

I hesitated.

Then, Dad’s memory whispered in my ear. 

He reminded me that Father blesses those who play their role. 

It was my time. I could be fixed. 

All I needed to do in return?

Offer a spectacle. 

A spectacle Vincent never asked for and didn’t deserve. 

“So, what’ll it be, son?” Dad cooed, louder than before. I could almost feel the icy wetness of his breath pressing against my ear. 

All the while, Vincent kept droning on, and on, and on...

Maybe he wasn't worth mercy.

But I was.

I scrambled forward, grinning wildly, dizzy with elated rage. I grabbed the stone, arced it over my shoulder, and leapt onto Vincent. The makeshift dagger punctured through his elbow with glorious ease. Thick globules of saliva overflowed from my corkscrewed lips. Sticky warmth trickled thin lines lovingly across my palm. 

The sky settled. The crickets stopped screaming. My labored breathing stood alone in the thick night air. 

I blinked slowly, heart relaxing, gaze locked on the spurting wound.

“Thank you, Nate.” There was a genuine sincerity in Vincent’s voice. 

Then, the sensation of falling. A *whoosh* that glided over my skin.  White light swarmed across my scales. The world dissolved. I sank under the surface of an incandescent tide.

I opened my eyes. An agonizingly familiar sight greeted me.

The attic ceiling. 

I sat up, clutching my head and wincing.

How the hell did I end up back in bed?

The acidic tang of copper flooded my nostrils. I yanked my comforter off and threw it to the floor. My right palm was coated in a delicate layer of dried blood, but the stone was gone. Thin rays of sunlight filtered through the blinds, threading strings of gold across the scales on my feet.

My brow furrowed; was there a slight haze rising from the tips of my toes? 

As it turns out, that was the first sign. A wispy harbinger of my reconstruction. 

Father was pleased.

He decided to fix me. 

First, though, 

Vincent needed to burn.

The night after the fire, my change started in earnest. The pain was excruciating, but the results? They were sublime. 

Clouds of steam billowed from my feet. My flesh audibly sizzled. I writhed in bed, failing to temper my wails. A dense, foul-smelling mist pervaded the attic; couldn’t see more than a few inches in front of me. I reached my hand down and ran my fingertips over my toes. 

I cried out. Not from the pain. Not from panic or confusion. 

The tissue was red-hot, but normal. 

*Normal*.

The scales along my toes were gone.

Steadily, my transformation ascended.

Toes. Feet. Ankles. Calves. 

I was happy; so transcedently happy. Happy enough that I’d forgotten all about the way I maimed Vincent, and the heaps of unexplained corpses that’d been retrieved from the ruins of his shack. 

He was dead. They were dead. 

What did it matter?

Vincent was expendable. They all were. 

Whoever they were.

Hours passed. Night fell. The steam had begun to feel suffocating. When the change reached my thighs, the pain had worsened exponentially.

I clenched my jaw. Chipped some teeth and swallowed the broken pieces. I felt my eyes bulge from their sockets. A new sound emanated from deep within both legs. A consistent, muted *crunch*, like shattered glass being pulverized under the heel of someone’s boot. 

It was unbearable.

I threw myself over the edge of the mattress, shrieking when my body hit the floorboards. I flexed my abs and squared my heels across the wood. My feet felt sore but usable. I swung my torso forward, planning on using momentum to get me upright. I almost stood, but crumbled as soon as I put pressure on my churning thighs.

God, what had I done? 

“I’m sorry, I’m sorry, I’m sorry...” The words spilled from me involuntarily. Through bleary eyes and the foul-smelling mist, I glimpsed the outline of my shortwave radio leaning against my desk. I dragged my steaming body forward. My lungs heaved. Breathing the mist felt like fire ants chewing through my chest. The crunching grew louder as the change approached my hips. I grasped the radio, slung it under my arm, and propped my back against the desk. I squinted, but couldn’t see the window. I knew where it should be, though. 

That was going to have to be enough. 

A guttural howl erupted from my throat as I swung the radio. There was a soft splintering when it connected. 

So I swung it again. And again. And again. 

*More.* 

*Faster.* 

*DIE. DIE. DIE.* 

A crystalline explosion permeated the attic. Fragments of falling glass tapped a silent melody against the scales of my forearm. Wind whistled over my head. I breathed deep and tasted cool, fresh oxygen mixed in with the stream. Slowly, the air cleared. 

That’s when I saw it. 

The street lamps were off, but the boulevard was bathed in moonlight. There was movement next door. Figures crawling in a neat, single-file line across the sidewalk. They were headed towards what should have been the creaking ruins of Vincent’s shack. 

But there were no ruins.

No trace of a fire whatsoever. 

Less than twenty four hours after it'd burnt down, Vincent’s home had been rebuilt. 

Wincing, I tilted onto my side, dug my nails into the wood, and pulled my body towards the stairwell.

His door was strange up close. 

It stood in stark contrast to the flimsy wood that formed the remainder of Vincent’s shack. Intricate webs of opal paint swirled and spiraled across a sea of ebony, forming a ring of hypnotic circles that grew sparser toward a black void at its center. The pattern was chaotic, yet rigid, as if every brushstroke harbored deep, unspoken intent. 

Was it beautiful?

The scent of the steam wafting from my breastbone pulled me back to reality. I doubled over, gagging, fresh bile dripping from the corner of my mouth.

How long had I just been standing there?

It didn’t matter. I fought through the nausea and forced myself upright. I gripped the bottle of my parent's favorite rum by the neck with one hand and reached for the knob with the other. 

I was going to burn it down, igniting that fire with the long-necked kitchen lighter that'd been used to ignite my birthday candles each year.

Would that halt the curse that was taking over my body? Maybe, maybe not. Whatever Vincent was, whatever his house represented, it was connected to my change, I was certain of that fact, even if I didn’t understand how. Doing nothing wasn’t an option. 

I couldn’t just watch it happen. 

With terror simmering at the base of my skull, I twisted the knob and pushed.

A gust of hot, humid air battered me as the door swung. I placed my foot over the threshold, grimacing when forehead knocked into the frame. Somehow, I’d misjudged my height. I bent my neck and pushed forward, glimpsing my childhood home out of the corner of my eye. A realization hit me like a sucker punch. 

I’d done it.

I'd passed over the property lines.

I lumbered across that forbidden barrier without even a flicker of hesitation.

It should have been a moment of profound triumph; I was free. 

So why did the accomplishment feel so empty?

The door snapped shut behind me. My eyes adjusted to the inky darkness. It was just a single room, barely larger than the attic. I glanced around, searching for the figures that I’d seen crawling in here an hour before. Silver moonlight trickled in through a pair of murky windows, outlining something human-shaped looming a few feet in front of me. My heartbeat grew wild and erratic. 

“Hello...?” 

No response. No movement. Just a light, consistent tapping. A sound like water dripping from a faucet.

I stepped a little closer, floorboards moaning, rum bottle shaking in my sweaty grip. I felt my transformation amplifying, growing hungrier with each passing second. The steam was rising from the middle of my neck. I pulled the lighter from my back pocket and pushed the trigger.

My jaw dropped. Abject horror curdled like months old milk in my gut.

It was Vincent. Not the man himself, but his visage; his tall, emaciated body immortalized in pearly glass.

A hollow, transparent statue, filled up to the middle of its neck with an unknown substance.

Pleading hands were clasped in front of its chest. Black liquid dribbled into a hole at the top of the statue from a massive, hanging beaker, which was strung from the ceiling with what looked like twine. An assortment of mundane objects lay at its feet, encircling the monstrous idol. A hairbrush. A spoon. A carton of cigarettes. A beige-colored square of hardened flesh. 

It was one of my scales. 

Panic exploded down my spine. I doused the statue in rum, emptying the bottle in a series of wild shakes. I pointed the lighter at the ungodly thing and pushed the tiny blaze towards it. My trembling hand froze inches from the shimmering glass. 

No.

No, no, no.

I couldn’t stop.

Why had I stopped?

I twisted my head and stared at my petrified hand. There was something curious imprinted on its scale-free palm, right above the wrist. A small, maroon blotch. Took me a moment to place where I’d seen it before. 

“God, please, no...” I whispered. 

It was a liverspot.

I’d seen it on Vincent’s palm when he offered me a cigarette. 

All of a sudden, my body relaxed. I felt the fear dissolve from my face in one unified wave. Terror continued to parade through my marrow, but I couldn’t act on it.

Not anymore. 

A muffled *thud* echoed through the room. I looked down. The sound had come from beneath me. I squinted. Steam rose from cracks in the wood. I bent over and dug my fingers between the floorboards. They were loose. I gripped the top of one and pulled.

The crawl space was impenetrably black. A soft, rhythmic wheezing curled into my ears. 

I tilted the flame into the hole and was greeted by a smile. 

The man below the floor had thick, bottlecap glasses and curly black hair that was falling out in clumps from his scalp. It was the fireman. The one Donahue had chewed out. 

Steam drifted up from the bridge of *his* nose.

Steam drifted up from the bridge of *my* nose. 

“Hello, Vincent,” he said, eyes churning in their sockets.  

“Hello, Vincent,” I replied. 

I lowered my head below the surface. 

Ten more steaming bodies lay scattered in the dirt. 

From behind me, I heard my Dad's voice, but I could not turn my head to look.

"Welcome home."

\- - - - -

For eleven days, I rest, and my brothers endure the spectacle in my stead.

On the twelfth day, I rise, ready to play my role. 

I step outside. Someone is screaming at me. I don’t bother looking down to avoid the shattered glass that’s on my stoop. The name-calling hurts. The cuts in my feet sting. 

But as twilight descends around me, I know it’s all okay. 

I lay in the grass and wait. There’s someone watching me from the window at the top of a nearby house. I don’t mind them. I’m staring past them, waiting for nightfall, waiting for the stars to come out. Once they do, I’ll be able to see Father’s grinning face, and I’ll know that he’s proud of us, all of us. 

He loves our spectacle.

And one day, he’ll get bored, and our role will be recast with a new batch of undesirables, fornicators, and low-lifes. 

I smile at the blazing sun as it begins to dip below the horizon, looking forward to that day. 

The day when I’ll be allowed to ignite our cleansing fire. 

The day when I might finally, 

trully
