He leaned a little closer and said, “Just don’t laugh, okay? I thought it was stupid too… at first.” I nodded, but something about his face already made it hard to take lightly. He wasn’t smiling. He started telling me how he used to sit in his room late at night, lights off, just scrolling on his phone. The only light in the room would be from the screen, and behind him, his shadow would fall on the wall. Completely normal. Something he had never even paid attention to.

Then one night, he moved his head slightly, just stretching his neck, and his shadow didn’t. I frowned and told him he probably just didn’t notice properly. That happens. Your brain misses things sometimes. He shook his head immediately and said he froze right after that and just stared at it. And then, slowly, it adjusted itself, like it was correcting a mistake. That part was strange, but still not enough to sound impossible, so I brushed it off.

He told me he checked again the next night. Same position, same setup. He moved his hand, and this time the shadow followed perfectly. For a moment, he relaxed. Then, before he moved again, the shadow’s hand twitched on its own. Not after him, before. That’s when he stopped treating it like nothing. After that, he started paying real attention, and he noticed something worse. If he moved quickly, everything looked fine, but if he moved slowly, there was always a slight delay. Just enough to feel wrong, like the shadow was copying him but thinking about it first.

By the third night, he said he already had this constant feeling that something was off in the room. Not in front of him, but around him, like he was being watched from places where light didn’t fully reach. That night, he got up to leave the room. His phone was still in his hand, casting that dim light behind him. His shadow stretched across the wall like always. He took a step, then suddenly stopped. He said he didn’t know why, it just felt wrong. So he turned around slowly, and his shadow was already there on the wall, standing straight, before he had even fully turned. I felt my chest tighten when he said that. Then he told me the part that really got to him. The shadow’s head tilted, not copying him, not matching him, just tilting on its own. He said he didn’t think at that point, he just moved quickly, almost jumping back, and instantly the shadow snapped back into place like nothing had happened. That’s when he knew it wasn’t just his mind playing tricks.

The next night, he decided to keep the lights on, bright, no darkness, no corners, nothing for shadows to hide in. He thought that would fix it. For a while, it worked. Then he looked down, and there was still a shadow. There was no light behind him that could have created it, nothing at the right angle, but it was there, clear as day, right at his feet. He slowly stepped back, and the shadow didn’t move. At that point, he said his body just went cold, like he already knew what was about to happen but didn’t want to accept it. He stood completely still, staring at it, and then the shadow lifted its arm. He didn’t move. It did. And then suddenly it shifted forward, not like a person walking, just closer, wrong, unnatural. He stumbled back, hitting the chair behind him, and at that exact moment the light flickered once. When it came back steady, the shadow was gone, completely gone.

He didn’t sleep that night or the next. When I asked him why he didn’t just leave the place, he went quiet for a few seconds, like he was deciding whether to say something he probably shouldn’t. Then he leaned closer again and said very softly, “I tried.” I asked him what that meant. He looked straight at me and said, “It didn’t stay in the room.” I didn’t know how to respond to that. Then he pointed down between us and said, “Look at your shadow.” I hesitated for a second, but I still looked. It was normal, exactly what you’d expect. I told him nothing was wrong. He didn’t argue, he just said, “Watch it carefully.” So I did. For a moment, nothing happened. Everything felt normal again. Then I noticed it, just slightly, just enough. My shadow moved, and I hadn’t.
