I know the voice because it's mine.

Not my voice as it is now, all ragged and cigarette-ruined and fifty years of shitty decisions worn into the grit of it all. But my voice from before. The voice I had when I was young enough to still believe that wanting to be a decent man was the same thing as being one. Younger me. Thirty-something me. The one who stood in this exact spot and made a promise to a door, and then broke it so completely that he forgot he'd ever made it.

He's been in here since then.

The thoughts all arrive the way the worst of them always do, as a fact you've known for a long time and have simply chosen to ignore. He's been in here since the last time. Since the time before that. Every version of me who ever walked through that door and drank the water and couldn't find his way out has been here, stacking up on the other side of that wood like sediment, like the gray crust on Levesque's septic tank, adding another layer every time the man on this side of the door decides to get back in his car and drive away and forget.

I did this.

Me. I did this to myself. Came back and came back and came back because I never actually left. You can't leave a place you never stopped running from, and I have been running from the quarry and the yellow kitchen gloves and the sound of the black water when it's swalloing something, and i have been running for over thirty years and the running looked like Nebraska and Iowa and a diner off Route 9 and another diner and another, and every time I ended up here. Right in this room. In front of this mirror. 

Looking at a man I almost recognize, but will never be able to.

"I'm sorry," I say. 

And it wasn't even to the thing behind the door. But to them all. To the version of me who stood here before with his hand bleeding from the stoplight lens and wrote *DON'T DRINK THE WATER* on a piece of paper and left it in a jacket, knowing he wouldn't remember why, knowing the next man in this room would look at it and hesitate and drink it anyway, because that is what I do. I read the warnings and then decide I know better. I decide my shit doesn't stink afterall.

To my wife, who has been lying awake for God knows how many years in a house I used to live in, wondering if the man she married ever existed or if she invented him out of loneliness or just sheer bad luck. 

To my son. God, my son. Who is... at least ten, and always will be in my head, who looked up at me from the backseat the last morning I was a father and said *bye, Dad* and I said something back, I must have said something back, but I cannot for the life of me remember what it was. 

I just hope it was enough. But I know it wasn't. 

The weeping on the other side of the door goes quiet. 

The light in here changes. Finally stops its flickering. Holds steady for the first time since I woke up on this floor with grout on my cheek and no memory of how I got here. The bulb burns clean and still.

And the door opens. 

It just swings inward on its hinges as if it had never been locked at all, like it was always only waiting for me to stop throwing myself at it and just *stand there*, and the air from the other side hits my face. That cool diner air. The specific smell of every American diner that has ever existed.

I stand in the doorway and look out at an empty dining room.

Empty booths. Empty counter stools. A ceiling fan is turning overhead in a slow, useless circle. Through the plate-glass windows, the parking lot is dark.

My car is there. Just where I left it, under the single working light in the lot.

I walk toward it.

My legs work, which surprises me. I expected them not to, expected the bad knee to give out in the doorway, expected to spend whatever's left of my life face-down on sticky linoleum, but the knee holds, and I cross the dining room, and I'm almost to the front door when my bad leg catches the leg of a counter stool I didn't see in the dark.

I go down hard.

Both hands out in front of me, pure reflex, and the right one, the broken one, the gray-purple zombie-looking one, takes the weight first and the pain that comes back up through my arm is the kind that wipes the slate clean for a second, nothing in my head but the sound of my own voice making a noise that isn't a word. I wrench sideways as I fall and catch myself on the edge of the counter instead, my good hand slamming flat against the formica, and there's something on it, a coffee mug someone left handle-out at the edge, and the handle catches the meat of my palm and opens it up clean, and I know before I even look that it's bleeding, I can feel the hot crawl of it in my wrist.

I lie on the floor for a moment. Both hands are ruined now. One dead, one bleeding. Probably some kind of poetry in that.

I push myself up with my elbows. Get to my knees and then my feet.

I look at my "good" hand. The cut is deep, running diagonal across the palm, already sheeting red. I press it to my shirt without thinking. Add it to the list of things to deal with in the morning. The list is getting really fucking long.

I push through the front door, and the night air hits me, and it is cold, and clean, and real.

I stand in the parking lot for a moment.

And I notice that I don't remember what I came in here for.

I needed to use the restroom, I think. Yes. Yes. Long drive. Needed to stretch my legs.

I look down at my hands. The right one is wrapped in what looks like an old shirt, tied off at the wrist, fingers swollen and the wrong color, and the left one has blood on it, seeping through where I'm pressing my palm to my jeans. I must have caught it on something on my way out.

I should get both of them looked at.

I should... I was going somewhere. I was driving somewhere.

I stand next to the car and try to find the shape of the thought, and it isn't there. There's just the hum of the highway somewhere beyond the trees and the cold and my own breath fogging the air in small, pale clouds. I had a reason for being out here. There was something I was thinking about on the drive, something important, something about...

A house I used to know. A boy.

I reach for it, but it slides away like trying to catch the drip before it detaches from the faucet. Gone.

I get in the car.

I drive.

I don't know how long. The radio plays something I don't listen to, and then static, and then something else. The dark opens up ahead of me and closes in the rearview and opens up again. Pine trees and the occasional light from a house set back from the road, lives I'll never know anything about, whole worlds running parallel to mine with no idea I even exist.

There's an exit sign I pass. There is, somewhere in me, an instinct that wants to take it, wants to drive to a house I may or may not still have a key to, wants to knock on a door at whatever hour this is, and stand there in the dark and say something to the woman who opens it. I don't know what. Something. Something that starts with her name and ends with *I'm sorry*, probably. 

But I pass the exit.

I'm tired. That's the thing. I am so profoundly, structurally tired that the tiredness has stopped feeling like tiredness and started feeling like whole-body fatigue. I need a drink, and then I need to sleep in a bed somewhere, and then I need to get these hands looked at in the morning, and then I'll think about that house. I'll think about everything in the morning when I'm not so tired.

I always do my best thinking in the morning.

I pull off at the next exit.

The diner is a quarter mile from the highway, the kind of place that exists in every small town all across the world, with its big neon sign in the window and gravel in the lot. A pickup truck I don't recognize. An old sedan. A closed sign in the window of the barbershop next door.

I sit in the car for a moment.

The name of the diner is something I don't look at. The neon in the window is a color I don't register. 

I need to use the restroom anyway. It's been a long drive.

I get out of the car.

Inside, it smells like everything I expected—beer and fried food and decades of spilled drinks absorbed into old wood, honest smells, the smells of ordinary people having an ordinary night. A woman at the bar turns to look at me when I come in. The bartender, young, with a face I probably won't remember by morning, looks up and nods. His eyes drop to my hands. A flicker of something. He doesn't say anything about it.

"Restroom?" I ask.

"Back of the house," he says, already looking back down. "Past the jukebox. Hang a right."

I nod and start walking.

The jukebox plays a song I recognize from somewhere I can't place. The hallway past it is narrow, the kind that forces you to turn sideways if someone's coming the other direction, and at the end of it, there are two doors.

One on the left.

One at the end of the hall.

The one at the end of the hall has a word on it.

**GENTS.**

I put my hand on the knob.

The cut in my palm presses against the cold brass. It stings, but I turn it.

The door opens.

And that was two days ago.

Or three. 

I'm not really sure anymore.

The light has been flickering, and the faucet won't stop dripping. My right hand has gone a color that hands aren't supposed to go, and my left one is crusted shut where the cut stopped bleeding sometime yesterday. The door doesn't open. I've tried the window. I've tried screaming. There's a jacket on the floor that isn't mine, some old denim with a patch on the shoulder that says something I can't read in this god-awful light, and in the inside pocket there's a folded piece of paper.

I haven't opened it yet. I don't know why I haven't opened it. I think I'm afraid of what it says.

Earlier today, I started hearing breathing from the other side of the door.

I'm posting this from my phone. The signal is bad and getting worse. I don't know if this will go through, but I don't know if it matters.

I'm so thirsty. There's a sink, and the faucet just started running on its own.

But I'm not going to drink from it.
