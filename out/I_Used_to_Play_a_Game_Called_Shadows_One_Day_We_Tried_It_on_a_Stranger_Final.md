My name is Perry.

I'm saying it now because I've already written two of these posts without saying it, and I'm done with that. If you've read the others, you know why I'm writing this. If you haven't, go back. This won't really mean anything without the context.

After I found the St. Vincent's story, I stopped sleeping in my bed.

Not a decision I made consciously. I just kept ending up on the couch, started as one night, then two, then a week, then six weeks, and at some point the bed became a room I kept the door cracked on but didn't go into. I ate standing at the counter. I let the plant on the kitchen windowsill go yellow, then brown, then past brown into something desiccated and done, and I left it there. My mother called four times, and I called her back twice. I told her I was fine, and she heard how my voice sounded, so she let it go, and I was grateful but also a little ashamed.

I kept going back to the story. I'm not sure if it was to find anything new or just because I couldn't stop. The parking lot, the curb, the October dark, me with my head down and him directly behind me, his head up, pointed at a window my sister wasn't even really looking through, and didn't mean to film

He was there while I fell apart.

Three months, and that sentence never loosened. Not the coffee shop or the Tulsa break room or the shadow on the grass—just that night, just that he was standing an inch from my back while I cried in a parking lot, thinking nobody could see me.

I started walking because staying in the apartment didn't feel safe anymore. I guess in my mind if he were here behind me, I'd rather it be in public than in private.

I had no real destination, just out the door and moving, usually when the light was going, sometimes after dark. My neighbor Harold caught me coming back one night sometime close to midnight, looked at me for a second, and said, *"You doing okay, Perry?"* and I said, *"Yeah,* *Harold, I'm fine*, *thanks,"* and went inside and sat on the floor.

My cat had stopped using her spots.

She had a rotation. The couch corner, the windowsill, the foot of the bed, years of it, predictable enough that I could've probably drawn you a map. She abandoned all of it. Started sitting at the front door instead, just present, facing it. Still.

I slept on the couch to be closer to her. I'm telling you because I said I was done hiding from shit.

Last night I ended up on Moreau Avenue.

A flat, indifferent sky above me. The air changed around me—hard to explain, some kinda of attention that moved through me—and both my legs went wobbly, and I had to grab the nearby STOP sign to steady myself. 

After a few seconds, I heard footsteps behind me.

I looked at the pavement. Took a deep breath. I stood there on Moreau Avenue for a long time before I turned around.

He was old.

Whatever I'd been carrying in my head, and I'd built a lot of versions across three months of bad sleep, the real version was older than any of them—late sixties, maybe seventies; white at the temples... a dark jacket with his hands in his pockets.

I knew him before he said a word.

Just the shape of him. Twenty-one years, and my stomach knew him before anything else did.

Neither of us spoke.

A car turned onto Moreau from the far end, passed between us, and kept going. Then it was just the gray sky and the bare trees and the two of us.

He said:

*"I probably did it longer than I should. I know that now."*

He had such a normal voice—the voice of someone's grandfather.

*"You were just always so good,"* he said. *"I wanted to know if you'd keep playing."*

He looked at me.

*"Good game, Perry."*

He put his hands back in his pockets and turned and walked north on Moreau Avenue. Same pace he'd always walked, probably. The same pace I'd matched at ten years old with the back of my neck gone hot, eight careful inches behind him, thinking *I* was the one playing the game.

I watched him reach the far end of the block.

I watched him turn the corner.

I stood there until he was gone and then a little longer, and my hands were shaking even then.

I walked home.

I know I did because I was there after, sitting on the floor of my living room with my back against the couch. I had my hands in my pockets the whole way. I focused on that—my own hands, my own pockets. I, moving my own body, moving through space.

My cat was on the couch when I got back.

Back in her normal corner. The one she'd abandoned before any of this, before the coffee shop video, before Renata said the word *around* on the phone and everything started falling apart. Curled up with her face tucked into her tail, asleep.

I sat on the floor next to her and put my hand on her back.

She exhaled. Small and warm. 

Rising and falling.

Outside, a car passed. Someone's door closed down the block. I sat there with my hand on her back and thought about a day in October and a parking lot. A summer when I was nine. A game I was good at. A stranger who turned around on a sidewalk near Lilac Park and looked at me for a long time and said two words I spent twenty years almost forgetting.

He said them again today.

I’m still sitting here.

I haven’t moved in a while; it’s easier not to.

My hands are in my pockets.

I keep thinking that if I stay still long enough, I might feel it.

The moment someone steps into place behind me.
