The floorboards didn’t groan, they screamed. There was a violent, heavy thud, like someone had dropped a bowling ball right outside my door. I remember almost drifting back to sleep, until I saw the Man in Red’s silhouette.

He was perched at the foot of my bed, crouched like a gargoyle. When the moonlight hit him, I saw blue eyes that didn’t blink and a face covered in third degree burns. 

My lungs hitched like an inflating balloon. I opened my mouth to let out a scream that would bring my parents running, but a cold, red hand clamped over my face.

"Shh," the Man in Red hissed. "Don’t scream, please."

I stared at his pale leathery skin and the dark circles around his eyes. He leaned in closer, smelling faintly of copper and old laundry.

"I know, I know," the man whispered. "I look a little different. But… I’m actually Santa Claus."

I made a muffled, skeptical sound against his palm.

"Look, I get it. I know I don’t look right, but it’s May, kid," the man said, his voice dropping to a confidential tone. "The beard gets itchy when it starts to get hot. I had to shave it, but I look so ugly without it, don't I?”

He tried to frown at me, but his broken face couldn’t.

“Now, if I let go, do you promise not to wake the whole house? Santa’s on a secret off-season scouting mission."

I stared for a long beat, then gave a tiny, hesitant nod before the man’s sticky red hand retreated.

"You’re not Santa," I whispered, my voice trembling. "Santa is fat." The Man in Red was tall and large, but he wasn’t fat. 

“I gain all that weight from all the milk and cookies I eat during Christmas time and then burn it all off as my new year’s resolution.”

I snapped instantly. "Prove it? What did you bring me for Christmas last year?"

The man’s head tilted. His eyes darted around the room, frantic and hungry. They landed on my half-finished lego castle. "Well, of course, I got you... a Lego set."

I squinted. "Maybe. What else?"

His gaze swept the walls, pausing on my Spider-Man posters and action figures. A jagged twitch tugged on his face. The man turned to me and suddenly jerked his wrists forward.

"Spider-Man," he said, making a "thwip" motion with his red stained fingers.

I cracked a small, shaky smile. "You really are him." I said. 

I know how this sounds. I was a gullible child, but it might have been what saved my life. 

"In the flesh," the Man in Red said, his grin widening. "Now, listen. Since I’m here, I can give you an early Christmas wish. Anything you want. What’s the one thing you need?"

My smile faded. I looked at my closed bedroom door. "Can you... can you make my parents stop fighting? They’re being really mean to each other lately."

The Man in Red's expression softened into something mocking yet final. He snapped his red stained fingers, and as he did, a few dark red droplets sprayed across my cheek.

"Already done, kid," he said. "They won't ever yell at each other again. Santa’s promise."

I wiped my cheek, looking at the dark smear on my hand. "What’s this? It’s sticky."

"Ketchup," the man said smoothly. "I was having a midnight snack in the kitchen."

I tentatively licked a bit of it off my finger and frowned. "It tastes like a nosebleed."

"Well guess what, that’s my favorite flavor of bubble gum," he replied without missing a beat.

"That’s a weird flavor to like, but who am I to judge, I like blueberry."

The man snorted. “How’s that weird?"

"I don’t know. It’s just what my brother Grant says, but I think it’s just because he doesn’t like blueberries. He's been really mean about it lately."

"Brothers," the man sighed, shaking his head. "Brothers are the worst, kid. They sure can stink sometimes."

"They really can," I giggled.

The man stood up, moving toward the open window.

"Wait!" I whispered. "One more thing for Christmas? For Grant? I broke his bike a while ago and he’s hated me for it ever since. Can you get him a new one? And... tell him I’m sorry? So he doesn’t hate me anymore? Please, Santa."

The Man in Red paused on the windowsill, the moonlight silhouetting his hunched frame. He looked back and gave a jolly wave.

"It’s already done, Paxton."

“Good night Santa.”

“Good night Paxton and Merry Christmas."  

I watched the Man in Red vanish out the window. 

Outside, a teenage boy was pedaling on by when he saw the Man in Red emerge from my yard. He must have not believed he was Santa Claus, because they found the teen’s body a block down the road in a ditch and his bike in my driveway covered in his blood with a bow and note on it saying it was for Grant. 

He never got to ride it and I never celebrated Christmas ever again.
