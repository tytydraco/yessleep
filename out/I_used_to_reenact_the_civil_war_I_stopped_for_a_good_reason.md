Before anyone starts freaking out, for the love of God, relax. I portrayed a union solider fighting with the 105th Ohio. Never wore grey in my life, but no matter what uniform I was or wasn’t wearing, some of the scariest shit I’ve ever seen in my entire life came from my time on the field. I can’t seem to forget about it, no matter how hard I try, and it’s hard to talk about with my family or friends, so I’m posting it here in case anyone has had a similar experience. 

Let me start by saying that reenacting and living history produce some of the best groups of people you could ever meet, as well as the most insufferable assholes who can’t help but talk about how smart they are for knowing the color of Sherman’s tie at the Battle of Resaca or something similar. I’ve met people from all walks of life, backgrounds, and political opinions, and trust me when I say that it’s not a monolith. For every “good-ol-boy,” there are two families that love portraying forgotten crafts and music, and for every hard-nose army dude who thinks he’s a badass for his “period correct kit,” there are groups of buddies who love doing nothing but shooting off black powder and smoking cigars. No matter the enjoyment or personal opinions of those involved, we all have one thing in common that unites us:

We’ve all seen some weird shit in our time. 

Everyone has a ghost story. Everyone has had something happen to them. We don’t talk about them a ton to people who aren’t in the hobby, mainly because I suspect more than half of them are bunk. However, the true stuff, the stuff that nobody really opens up about except to those who won’t call them crazy for saying it, those stories pop up too. Statistically, it makes sense, since most of our events occur in or near state and national parks. A lot of unexplained things tend to happen out that way. My case is no exception. 

I feel crazy for typing this, but I need to get it out. I’ll explain it as best I can, but I’m still coming to terms with the details. 

I was on the field at a siege battle, working a skirmish line. I normally did artillery, so I wasn’t the most graceful with a rifle, but I was having fun. It was late, and we were preparing a final charge over the battlements and into the earthen fort where rebel forces lay. We were all having a grand time, and it was always a hoot to go out en masse in a barrage. We were advancing, and our captain gave the command to charge, so we all broke and rushed the defenses, giving the audience a grand finale to all the shooting. 

I, however, did not get to charge. I slipped on a patch of grass thanks to the smooth soles of my new brogans and fell hard from the flank. No one came to check on me, so I figured they thought I was pretending to get shot, but that didn’t stop me from cussing up a storm. I fell hard and was left in the pine straw and leaves while rifles fired in front of me. I started getting dizzy, and after a little bit, all of the commotion seemed to fade away. Eventually, I pulled myself to my feet and tried to reorient, but something was off. 

It was dark outside. Pure, unadulterated night had encompassed the woods, and none of my unit was anywhere to be found. I also couldn’t see the fort. All there was were endless trees that seemed to stretch out in uncanny rows of pine and oak. I didn’t know what to do, so I shouted out for some of the guys I’d gone to the event with. I walked in circles, trying hard to find a sign of the campsite, or the visitor center, even a road, but there was nothing.

I started to panic. I didn’t have my phone on me to call for help, so I just started shouting. I was almost at the point of tears and hoarse from screaming for help before a voice came from a few yards behind me.

“Lost there, friend?” 

I turned to see another man in a blue coat with gear like mine, though most of his face and form were covered by the dark blue shade of the evening sky. Even in the moonlight, he was just a silhouette, but one that I was familiar with. I told him how glad I was to see him, then asked if he was with the Pennsylvania group we were camping with.

“No,” he told me, “I’m with the 3rd Kentucky.” That struck me as strange, since I wasn’t aware of a group under that name participating in the event. However,  I brushed it off quickly since I was still disoriented and confused by my loss of time. 

“Do you know where the others are?” I asked him. 

He nodded and pointed to the right of us. “We’re all still here, friend. All still here down in the hollow.” 

“The hollow?” I asked. There wasn’t any hollow I could think of near the fort, but the man didn’t pay any mind to my question. He simply turned and started walking away. 

“Follow me, friend, “ he told me. “Follow me, and we’ll all be back together where we ought to be.”  

I followed behind him from a few yards away, leaning on my rifle for support as I trudged. The woods grew thicker as we went, and I tried to ask him more questions, but all he did was keep walking. He only ever gave one response. 

“In the hollow is where we lay,” he said, “and there we remain until the earth forgives us for what we did to it.” 

I kept my distance as he muttered. I held a wary eye on the brush around us, trying to see if anyone else was around. Eventually, he stopped at a dip in the land and stared down with his back facing me. 

“Here,” he said, pointing into the rolling earth. 

“This ain’t no camp,” I said. “Where the hell is everyone?” 

The man didn’t have a chance to mutter a sound before a sabre flashed and his head tumbled from his body. Another man in muddy jean-cloth held the sword and stumbled forward as he fixed his grey hat. He’d emerged from the dip and continued with ferocious arcs of steel as he cut into the man’s body. I dropped my rifle and covered my mouth in horror. The rebel swordsman looked my way at the sound and rushed to his feet. 

“You ain’t supposed to be here,” he said to me. “You ain’t supposed to be here….” 

He raised his blade and started staggering toward me, and I started stumbling back. My legs were like Jell-O, and the man was still muttering, his savage face obscured by the shade of his wide hat. I fell back and scrambled as he approached. He was gaunt as a scarecrow and moved with horrible, jagged steps.

“You ain’t supposed to be-” 

His words were cut off by the blossoming point of a bayonet. Another bluecoat man was behind him, dragging him to the ground as more figures crawled out of the dip behind them. Forms of grey and blue, ragged and covered in mud, were bashing each other with the stocks of rifles and tackling opponents to the dirt with bowie knives in hand. I watched as they fought and dragged each other back into the pit. Each side carried their fallen over the drop, and the swordsman was no different. Two men took hold of his ankles and dragged him back to the hollow, the bayonet and rifle still stuck through him. The fighting continued, and I was half convinced I’d come across a group of crazies strung out on drugs. I was convinced that someone, anyone, had to have phoned the cops at the commotion, but no sirens came. Instead, I beheld one final gruesome sight that sent me to my feet and kept me sprinting into the dark. 

On the edge of the fall, standing limp, but attentive, was the headless form of the man who’d led me to that place. His shoulders turned toward me, and he pointed a finger down into the ditch. 

“The hollow is where we lay,” I remembered him saying, “and there we remain until the Earth forgives us…”

By the time I stopped running, a ranger’s flashlight was in my face, and my buddies from the 105th were crowding me. They got me loaded into an ambulance, and EMTs checked me over for damage. As far as they could tell, I had a nasty bump on the head and a possible concussion, but apart from that and the clear shock I was in, there wasn’t anything wrong. My buddies grilled me on what had happened. They told me that in the final part of the battle, I’d disappeared and had been missing for four hours. I kept telling them that I didn’t remember, and now, when any of them ask, I still tell them I somehow must’ve gotten lost. It’s an inside joke now with them. “Don’t get lost like-----”

I don’t reenact that much anymore, outside of a few festivals. I don’t like thinking about what I saw. 

There are plenty of stories from some of the older guys in the hobby that I may share around, too, but for now, I’m just glad I got this off my chest. 

I’ll update if anything new comes to light.
