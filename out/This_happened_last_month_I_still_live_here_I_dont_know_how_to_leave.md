I bought the place sight-unseen off a tax foreclosure list. Cheap, quiet street on the edge of The Valley, where the fog rolls in thick after dark and the streetlights buzz like they’re arguing with themselves. Realtor ghosted the walkthrough, left keys in a lockbox that reeked of old coins. I figured whatever—beggars can’t be choosers.

First night, I noticed the mailbox. No number. Just a blank metal flap, like it’d never been claimed. Previous owners probably peeled it off when they bailed. I slapped duct tape on it with “1427” Sharpied in big letters and crashed.

3:17 a.m. (I checked the clock because it mattered later), scratching. Slow. Deliberate. Like fingernails tracing the same line over and over on the porch wood. I peeked through the blinds.

Porch light was off—I swear I never touched the switch. Moonlight hit the mailbox. My duct tape was gone. In its place, fresh white paint: 1427. Exact size, exact spot. But the handwriting… the 4 had this weird little hook at the top, like it was questioning itself. Not my blocky scrawl. Someone else’s careful cursive.

I told myself sleep paralysis bullshit and went back to bed. Heart hammering, but denial is strong at 3 a.m.

Morning: number still there. Crisp. Professional. Like a sign company did it overnight. I checked the foreclosure listing photo on my phone—the mailbox was blank in every angle. No sticker residue, no ghost of paint. Nothing.

That evening I came home from work (it’s always dusk here by 5, fog swallowing the street). Porch light was on. Bright, welcoming, like the house was waiting. I hadn’t wired it yet. Bulb looked old-school incandescent, the kind that hums.

Inside felt… compressed. Doorways narrower. Ceiling lower by an inch or two. I measured later—same as listing photos. But it didn’t feel the same.

Landline rang. I hadn’t connected one. Picked up anyway because curiosity is a disease.

Woman’s voice, soft, polite, almost neighborly: “Is this 1427?”

My mouth went dry. “Who is this?”

Long pause. Breathing. “I used to live here. The address… it keeps moving. Slipping. I keep writing it down, but when I come back, it’s changed again. Please don’t change it back.”

Click.

Caller ID: –––––––– (just dashes, like redacted).

I unplugged the phone. Ripped the jack out of the wall for good measure.

Scratching returned that night. Same rhythm. But now from inside. Under the floorboards in the hallway. Slow drag, pause, drag. Like measuring.

Next morning, the mailbox had updated itself. Still 1427, but now the paint looked older—faded at the edges, chipped like it’d been there years. My duct tape was folded neatly inside the box, like someone had peeled it off politely and saved it for me.

I drove to the county assessor’s office. Asked about the property. Clerk pulled it up, frowned.

“1427… huh. System says it’s unassigned. No record of occupancy since 2011. But the tax rolls show payments coming in every quarter. Cash, dropped in the night slot. Weird.”

She showed me the map. My street ended at 1425. Then a gap. Then 1429. No 1427 on any official record. Like the house had been erased from the grid.

That night the phone rang again—even unplugged. I didn’t answer. It rang for seventeen minutes straight. When it stopped, the scratching started. Not under the floor now. Inside the walls. Close to my bedroom. Tracing the outline of the doorframe. Slow. Patient.

I stayed up with every light on. Around 4 a.m., the porch light flickered once. I looked out.

The mailbox read: 1427.

But below it, smaller, fresher paint: Please don’t leave. I finally found it again.

I haven’t slept right since. Every morning the number is the same, but the paint changes—sometimes fresh, sometimes cracked and peeling like decades old. The house feels… settled. Content. Like it’s finally been remembered.

Last week I tried to leave. Packed a bag, got in the car. Fog was thick. I drove to the end of the street, turned left toward the highway.

GPS said: Recalculating. Then: You are at 1427. Turn around.

I kept going. Street signs blurred. Fog got heavier. After twenty minutes of straight driving, the same porch light appeared ahead. Same mailbox. Same number.

I pulled in. The front door was unlocked. My keys were on the hook where I left them.

The phone rang as I walked in.

I answered this time.

Same voice. Calmer now. Almost relieved.

“Thank you for coming back. I was starting to forget the shape of the rooms again.”

I hung up. Unplugged everything. Sat in the dark.

The scratching stopped.

For the first time since I moved in, the house is quiet.

But this morning, when I checked the mailbox, the number was still 1427.

Under it, in the same careful cursive:

Welcome home.

I don’t know whose home this really is.

But it’s starting to feel like mine.

Update: The porch light just turned on by itself. It’s 2:47 a.m. I’m not going to look outside again tonight.

Wish me luck, The Valley. Or whoever’s reading this.
