You ever felt eyes on you? Like you could swear someone is watching. You turn to look and they are. Maybe it’s a friend across the parking lot, or the person behind you in the grocery line, hoping you’ll move forward. But somehow you were right. That’s not something you should be able to sense, right?



Every night as a kid, I felt that from my bedroom closet. Like there was some monster lurking in the shadows behind my winter coat. Something *was* there. I couldn’t be convinced otherwise even with the lights on. The feeling hung heavy in my gut and made my skin crawl. But I got older and we moved and I forgot all about the closet that watched me.



But nearly a decade later, that same feeling was back. Under the moonlit sky, I stood outside an ugly cement building I must’ve driven past a thousand times without noticing. There were eyes on me, I could swear it. And they felt like the same eyes. But despite the dozens of cars in the lot, there was no one in sight.



I’d taken a job at a place called Cosmeir labs. They had one of those tear-a-number flyers on a campus bulletin board. “$18/hr! Lunch Included! No experience! Any Major!” In Texas, with our $7.25 minimum wage, it seemed too good to be true, so I called to schedule an interview. They wanted me there at 9pm. Weird, but… okay.



Inside, fluorescent lights hung from the water-damaged ceiling and the carpet smelled like it hadn’t been washed in years. The old lady at the front desk stood with some effort with a smile that said she was waiting for me. Her shiny nametag read *“Georgia.”*



“You here for orientation?”



“Uh, just an interview, I think.”



“Matt?”



“Yeah.”



She let out a small chuckle. “Welcome to orientation, Matt.”



She walked me around the facility, monotoning about the duties. “You know what this place is?”



“Like a lab or something? You do psych experiments, right?”



She nodded. “Your job is to clean up after them. You know, sticky keyboards, butt-sweat in the chairs, vomit off the floors. You might even get a few dead rats out of cages.” She chuckled and I forced a smile. “It’s not all people you’re cleaning up after, but Felicity can tell you more.”



It was starting to sound like a real job and not like they were gonna have me door-to-door selling knives or something. I wasn’t sure I wanted to clean dead rats out of cages, but for $18 an hour, I’d give anything a shot.



Everything in this building seemed to be forty years old. The computer labs we passed had old CRTs on metal desks, great big computer towers on the floor beside them. There were chalkboards in every office and the smell of ancient sweat baking into the peeling wall paint. And… red alarm sirens placed high on the wall every so often.



What are those?”



“It’s a lab, kid. Just a safety precaution.”



Only when we came across a closed door did I realize every room we’d passed until then had been open.



“Matt.” Georgia slowed to a stop before the closed door.



“If a door is shut, you are never to open it. Even if you are assigned to clean that room. The assignment is a mistake.”



“Okay.”



“No matter what. You wouldn’t want to spoil an experiment.”



The rest of the orientation was dull. How to use the shop vac to clean butt sweat out of the seats, vomit-mopping techniques, stuff like disconnecting keyboards before cleaning them, mundane crap.



I filled out some paperwork and then Georgia gave me my assignment. The next day, I would report to room 1001 for my 9pm to 6am shift. That was fine by me. With almost no one else on campus, there was no point to keeping regular hours.



When I stepped back out under the moonlight, it was like a weight had been lifted. Maybe it was just the fresh air, but I felt like I could breathe again.



I went back to the dorm, which was mostly empty thanks to summer break, and slept like a baby.



I showed up the next evening, already tired from a day lounging in the empty dorm living room, and found room 1001 smelling of coffee, *thank god.*



The office was just large enough for 4. There was Terrence and Jacob, and then our supervisor, Felicity. I found out the guys had, like me, just finished freshman year, Terrence was an art Major, Jacob, Kinesiology. Felicity was a super-senior studying Ancient Languages, hoping to graduate in August after some summer classes.



After Georgia’s orientation, I’d expected to be mopping vomit until my arms ached, but the workload was much lighter. It was a long-ass shift, but we mostly just sat around talking, waiting for something to clean.



There wasn’t internet or cell reception and the Windows 95 computers were equipped with Solitaire and Word, so between tasks we were forced to make do with paper football, hangman on the chalkboard, or 20 questions. That is, with the exception of Felicity, who did god-knows-what on her computer.



An ancient fax machine the size of a small fridge beeped to life. Felicity sprung up from her seat and waited as it slowly printed.



Jacob and Terrence were crossing their fingers. I looked to them for explanation.



“No vomit, no vomit, no vomit,” Jacob whispered, a grin on his face. I crossed mine, too.



Felicity read the assignments from the fax. She gave me: “Monitors, Seats, Keyboards, and AC Unit. Room 3107. They’re adding a fourth floor, so the ac units up there pick up a lot of dust. Make sure to clean that out.”



“Yeah, course.” I had no idea how to open an AC unit, but I’d give it my best shot before asking for help.



I wandered the third floor with my cleaning equipment until I found 3107. It was written on notebook paper in sharpie. Out of curiosity, I lifted the paper. Beneath, a plaque was labeled 2107. Odd. Probably part of the construction.



The computer room was pretty clean already, just stale with age. I shop-vacced the seats, unplugged the keyboards before cleaning the food gunk from between the keys, wiped down the CRTs, and the front of the AC unit popped off easy enough. I dusted it and shook off the feeling someone was watching me. I tried to keep an even pace as I headed back to the office.



Felicity went off to a class while we ate lunch—dinner(?)—whatever you wanna call a midnight meal—in the cafeteria. Sure enough, it was all free, and the food was great. Like really great.



“So she really has a class at—“ I checked my watch. “Midnight?”



Jacob chuckled. “I dunno. That’s what she says.”



I noticed Terrence had just gotten one piece of bread. He threw away half of it by the end of the hour. I saw Jacob notice too, but he didn’t say anything.



After Felicity got back, there were a couple more jobs and several more cups of coffee, then we all went our separate, sleepy ways at 6am.

But as I lay there, the sunrise creeping through my dorm-room blinds, I got the feeling something was watching me from my dark closet. It was the same way I’d felt as a kid. Its cold, hungry eyes were crawling over my skin like centipedes. In my head, I heard a whisper of a thought: *I want your flesh.*



Of course, it was just my subconscious. I’d had a weird, long first day and the empty dorm building kinda freaked me out. All the silence punched a hole in my sense of ease. Without our senses being constantly bombarded, our minds make things up. It’s like staring at static on a TV for too long. Eventually you see shapes and maybe even hear voices. But it’s just your mind.



Despite the explanation I gave myself, I still pulled the blankets up over my head and turned to face the wall before drifting off.



Things at work were good for a while. Felicity would assign the tasks, file away the faxes, and we would go around to our assigned rooms, cleaning up whatever gross crap they had waiting for us. I always kinda wanted my room to be shut so I had an excuse not to smell like old butt sweat.



After lunch, we started roaming the halls. Felicity would be gone until 2am and she’d told us not to read the faxes while she was out, so we took the opportunity to try to figure the place out. Every day was different. Some rooms were full of Jazzercisers, in others people watched old TV on computer monitors, sometimes there were people in white coats performing experiments on questionable liquids in beakers.



I could tell the others felt the same sense of unease I did. We never talked about it, but it was in the way we would all glance around corners before heading down the next hall or sneak a look behind our backs. Even in the way we would laugh too loud when someone biffed a paper football kick or made a weird guess in twenty questions. It was all a game of pretend: to admit the place took a toll on us would make it real.



I didn’t want it to be real.



Terrence’s eyes were sunken and grey by the end of my first week. And he wasn’t eating at all anymore. But we worked weird hours. There wasn’t anything sinister happening.



“You’re not hungry, man?”



“Nah, I had a big dinner. I’ll eat later.”



But he had less interest in the games we played and I kept catching him muttering things under her breath. “I didn’t want to,” or “she made me.”



One day, while we were out watching a room of sword-fighting larpers, the ground shook violently. I’ve never felt an earthquake before, having grown up in Texas, but I don’t know how else to describe it.



The larpers stopped fighting.



Then the red alarms on the wall started blaring. So loud, I couldn’t hear my thoughts.



One of the larpers strode up to the door and slammed it shut. All down the hall, doors started shutting.



And then the hall lights flickered and failed. The only light from the spinning red alarms.



“LET’S GO!” I yelled. And we started running back in the direction of our office.



The stairwell sat at the far end of the hall. Whatever watched me in my closet was down those stairs. And its hungry, evil eyes ran chills down my skin.



But the office was down those stairs, too and we needed to get back. So I stepped into the stairwell and started down.



Jacob followed, but Terrence stopped at the edge and just stared down, his sunken eyes streaming with tears.



I waved for him to follow us. “Terrence! Come on, man! We gotta get back!”



“I didn’t want to open the door, but she made me!”



I went back up and grabbed him by the arm, but he sunk to the ground and curled up in a ball.



“Just leave him!” Jacob yelled.



“I’M NOT GOING DOWN THERE! I CAN FEEL IT’S EYES!” Terrence screamed at the top of his lungs.



I had no choice but to leave him.



Jacob and I made it back to the office. He closed the door and I shut off the lights. I don’t know what drove me to do that, but it somehow felt safer sitting in the dark.



The amber security light faded in and out, casting the room in an eerie glow.



The door must’ve had some heavy-duty sound-proofing because the alarms were barely audible now.



Everything was still as that dim amber light washed over us.



Me: “What do you think happened?”



Jacob: “Whatever they keep on the bottom floor got out.”



Me: “What do you mean?”



Jacob: “Felicity accidentally said something once. There’s something down there.”



There was a faint rumbling in the floor, like a heavy cart was rolling by outside. It stopped right outside the door.



I could barely breathe. It felt like all the joy was sucked from the air. My heart sunk into my stomach, my skin prickling and beading with sweat.



I could feel its eyes on me, even through the door.



There was a light scratching sound from outside. Jacob startled backward and knocked into Terrence’s desk where he sometimes did his art. One of his pencils started to roll.



I threw out my hand, just in time, and caught it.



The thing outside the door stopped scratching as if it was listening. As if it had somehow heard the pencil rolling. It knew someone was in here. I felt its eyes on me again. My blood curdled.



And then the floor seemed to sigh. The eyes turned away from me and the sound of it moving receded down the hall.



Long after the alarms stopped, Jacob and I sat in dead silence, waiting.



The door suddenly opened and the light flicked on. In walked Felicity. She froze at the sight of us curled up at the far corner of the room. “Umm… What are you doing?”



I tried to shrug it off like I hadn’t been afraid for my life just seconds prior, but when we finally got the story out, she nodded, understanding.



“I saw Terrence in the hall just a minute ago. He's fine.”



Jacob and I looked at each other with relief.



“But he asked to switch to the day shift. Guess that makes sense now. Sorry I wasn’t here for that.” She seemed frazzled. “You did everything right.”



The time ticked on. We still had four hours left on our shift.



3am.



4.



5:30. Jacob and I had mostly recovered from the scare, but it felt weird without Terrence there. I was struggling to stay awake, eyes on the analog clock as the second hand continued its endless journey around.



I jolted awake to the beeping of the fax machine, not even realizing I’d passed out. 5:57. Almost made it…



Felicity tore the paper free. “Only one assignment, who’s taking it?”



My eyes drifted to Jacob, who was still a bit shaken up. “I’ll do it,” I said begrudgingly.



“It’s a basement level, you been down there?”



I shook my head.



“It’s really the same as up here, just smells a bit worse.” She let out a humored breath of air. “Uhh… Room -1001. We have a floor zero, so that’s two floors down. It just says to wipe down the chalkboard. Should be quick.”



“Got it.” I pointed an okey-dokey finger gun at Jacob, who shot one back. “See ya,” then grabbed some chalkboard spray and an extra eraser in case the one in the room was missing.



The air was heavier as I descended the stairs and I felt the ever-present eyes on my back intensify. The fluorescent lights seemed weaker, too, adding to my sense of unease. But I shook it off and headed out into floor -1. It was almost the same as every other floor, except that most of the doors were shut.



*I just want to go home. Please let it be shut,* I thought.



\-1003, -1002, -1001. And sure enough! It was shut. I felt a smile creep across my face. *Thank god.*



I headed back upstairs, the air easing up around me.



“Oh, done already?” Felicity said, just leaving the office.



“Yeah, the door was shut, so—“



“So… it was locked?”



“Just shut. Georgia said if a door was shut not to open it.“



“Oh.” She chuckled. “She’s old. I’m sure she meant locked. If a door is *locked*, you don’t open it. That’s the rule.” She gave me a reassuring smile. “It’s okay, I promise. After that, you can head out.”



“Oh, okay.” I turned back around, hesitated for a moment. But headed down the stairs. Georgia had said *“shut,”* I was sure of it. If she’d meant locked, she would’ve said so, right? I replayed the orientation in my mind.



But it would be quick. Five minutes, tops.



The door stood before me Room -1001.



I knocked on the door.



It was quiet.



Then, someone said, “Come in.”



I turned the handle slowly. It was unlocked.



The room was an exact replica of our office. There was Terrence’s desk. Mine, Jacob’s, Felicity’s… and there was Terrence himself. He was cleaning the chalkboard.



He gave me a cheery smile. “Thanks for coming. I just needed help carrying this weight.” 



The door was spring loaded, so it shut behind me. I tried not to flinch. “Uh, yeah. I thought you switched to the day shift, man.” I pulled out my spray bottle and eraser and set up on the far end of the board.



“Oh, what? No, I don’t think they do day here.”



The chalkboard had been covered in a layer of chalk, but visible in glimpses beneath the white were symbols I’d never seen before, like from some ancient language.



I started erasing, feeling a chill down my spine. Terrence was freaking me out, too. Something was off.



“You wanna play 20 questions?” He asked.



“Uh, sure.”



“Okay. I have one.” Terrence said.



“Is it bigger than this room?”



He thought about it. “Yeah, you could say that.”



I erased faster, trying to get this done. The lights above were flickering and I did *NOT* want to be caught alone in the dark with him.



“Is it bigger than this building?”



“Yes.”



“Is it a tangible object?”



“Sometimes.”



“Is it an idea?”



“Yes.”



I didn’t want to ask any more questions, so I pretended to think as I wiped down the board. It stretched across the 30’ wall, but we were almost halfway done.



“Is it real?” I forced out.



“Yes.”



My heart was beating audibly over the electric hum of the flickering lights, but I felt like I wasn’t getting enough air, my fingers and face tingling. Something was wrong.



“I think the spray is messing with my head. I’m gonna go get some air.”



“Ask me if it’s in my head.”



“What?”



“Question six. Is it inside my head?” He raised his eyebrows as if actually curious.



I started toward the door. “I just gotta…”



“The answer’s yes.” He stared at me from the chalkboard.



I approached the door, praying it wasn’t locked.



“It’s inside your head, too, now.” A soft smile crinkled the skin around his eyes.



I pushed the door open and stumbled out into the hall.



“You left your supplies!” Terrence called.



But I was running already. Up the stairs. I didn’t even go back to the office, just found the exit and dove out into the fresh, morning air.



But the oppressive feeling wasn’t lifting like it had before.



*It’s not in my head*, I told myself—I’m still telling myself.



But the dorm building feels all wrong, like every shadow is full of eyes.



I think I let it out. I think it’s everywhere, now.



And I can’t get the question out of my mind…



“What is it?”
