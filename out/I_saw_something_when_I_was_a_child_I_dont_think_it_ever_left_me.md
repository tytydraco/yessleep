I saw a monster when I was young.

This might sound unbelievable, and for the longest time I thought it was in my head. 

I’m posting this now, because something happened recently… and I need to know if anyone else has seen or experienced something like this.

When I was a kid, my family and I relocated to a different country for my dad’s career.

He’d already been living there for a while, trying to set up a life for when we eventually came down to meet him.

When he was more financially stable and secured in his job, he finally sent for us.

The house he got for us was the biggest we’d ever lived in. I loved that about it.

It was a little secluded, not a lot of neighbors in the area. Except one.

The house—or shack—looked abandoned.

Wooden walls eaten through by termites, zinc roof rusted and broken in. A black tarpaulin was used to cover it up in places that had caved in. But all that just added to the eerie feeling of the house.

There were tall weeds all over the land, so tall they sometimes creeped over our fence.

But all these weren’t what scared me the most—what made me turn my gaze away from the house whenever my eyes fell on it.

It was the vultures.

Large birds sitting on her roof… simply watching. 

There weren’t any corpses around to explain their presence, yet these birds never left. Come rain or shine.

It always set my teeth on edge when I saw them. And I wasn’t the only one.

My mom had always been superstitious—a broken plate on a happy day: a bad omen. Heavy rainfall when the weather forecast showed otherwise: a bad omen. Vultures where there were no corpses: a *really* bad omen.

She would mutter prayers under her voice whenever we walked by the house. Asking for protection from evil eyes.

Her prayers told me we were protected. No evil weapons fashioned against us shall prosper.

So I believed her.

Until that night.

I still can’t remember what or why I woke up that night.

I remember walking through the dark halls of the house—there was a storm earlier that day, and it had taken out the power—gripping my teddy bear tightly in my arms.

I pushed through the door and walked into the garden, like I was on a mission.

What spurred me on? I don’t know.

Barefoot, I continued to walk, deeper into my mother’s well looked after vegetable garden. And that’s when I saw it.

A corpse.

Right in front of me, the body of our dog lay on the floor, blood seeping. But it wasn’t just the sight of our poor dead dog that made my blood run cold in my veins.

It was the birds.

Their bloody beaks pecking at the flesh, ripping away chunks of meat as they feasted.

There had to be ten of them. I never realized how big vultures were up close. These ones rivaled the size of turkey, and were just as ugly.

I stood, frozen to the spot and watched in quiet horror as the birds continued to feed. And just when I thought it couldn’t get any worse. The birds moved.

And that’s when I saw it, back hunched unnaturally over the half eaten body.

It wasn’t one of the birds. I know that much.

I can’t put it into words what it looked like—even now.

My blood ran cold and every instinct in me screamed to run away. So, I started to step back , because even at that age, I knew I had seen something I wasn’t meant to.

But I must have stepped on something. There was a slight snap sound—and everything froze.

And so did I.

Then slowly, the hunched back creature with tar like skin turned its head to look at me.

I couldn’t help it. I screamed.

That seemed to rattle it, and it rose to its full height, bones popping and cracking as its limbs stretched to unnatural lengths.

And then… I ran.

Heavy footsteps and bird calls followed me as I ran —my teddy bear long forgotten—into the house and straight to my parents room.

I remember shaking them awake, body trembling, as I told them about the monster I had seen.

My father—ever the realist—told me to go back to sleep. Just a nightmare, he said.

But my mom… watched me as I recounted the tale for her—eyes sharp and worried. But not disbelieving.

I was trying to convince my father to come se it. To get rid of it. I was at that age where I believed an adult could do anything. So it only seemed right.

My mother, however, thought it wasn’t wise. 

“We should wait for the sun to wake. We can’t go looking for monsters in the dark.”

I didn’t sleep the rest of the night.

I hid under the blankets, trying to keep my breathing calm, because I was sure any loud noise I made would call the monster back to me.

When daylight broke, I was already up.

I ran back to my parents, and finally my dad went out in search of the monster.

He came back a short while later to tell me he found our dog—intact—but dead. Not a drop of blood in sight.

I tried to tell him that wasn’t possible. I saw it. I saw the vultures.

He ignored me and came up with his own theory of how the dog died. 

Perhaps it was the storm. Or poor health.

It wasn’t the storm, and it definitely wasn’t due to health problems. 

We had just been to the vet earlier that week.

I turned to my mother, eyes pleading—because I knew, somehow that she would believe me.

“What about her teddy?” She asked my dad.

He frowned. “I didn’t see it.”

He seemed to ponder this for a second, but after a while shook his head—dismissing whatever doubt had creeped into his mind. He told my mom and I it would be somewhere in the house. 

Days after that, I still couldn’t sleep. Taken to hiding under my blanket, wide awake, with all the lights on. Praying that the monster wouldn’t come back for me.

Months passed, and finally everything seemed to be getting better. I was sleeping again.

Then one day, my mother and I were walking to the grocery store. We had just reached the end of the old lady’s fence when a shrill voice called to my mother.

It was the old lady.

That was my first time seeing her, and when we locked eyes, that feeling came back.

The urge to run. The urge to scream.

Everything I felt that night when the red eyes of the creature glared at me.

She was saying something to my mother, grinning widely as she hunched over—cane in hand.

My mother’s grip on me was almost bone-crushing. To the normal eye, she looked normal—smiling, nodding.

But I could see the tension in her shoulders. And I could feel her sweaty palms.

She was scared. 

“I have something. I think it’s yours.” 

She was speaking to me when she said this. Eyes glinting as she reached into her skirt and pulled out my ragged teddy. 

I took a step back, so did my mom—her facade breaking slightly.

After a tense moment, she slowly took it from her, thanking her for returning it.

The old lady turned to me. “Try not to lose it again.”

I lost all feelings in my leg then. But my mom’s grip kept me upright and moving.

We didn’t go to the grocery store again, instead we returned home. And headed straight for the garden.

There my mom placed the teddy on some dry kindle, poured gasoline all over and set it on fire, muttering prayers.

We both watched until only the ashes were left. And she packed those up too and threw it in the trash.

We left that beautiful house a few months later, and never returned. Even though it took a long time to sell.

I still see them sometimes. 

Not the creature. Just the vultures.

Sitting on rooftops.

They don’t gather like that for no reason.

And every time they do… I feel like something is waiting for me to come back.

Has anyone seen vultures gather like this… when there’s nothing there?
