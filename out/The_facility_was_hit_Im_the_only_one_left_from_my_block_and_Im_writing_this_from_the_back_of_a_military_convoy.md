In the pitch black, even the faint glow from my phone feels like a massive spotlight fixed right on me. I press the light against my chest, hoping the fabric of my t-shirt will soak up the radiance and keep me hidden; I hold my breath so I don’t give myself away. But the shaking... I can’t stop it. Every letter my fingers strike on the screen feels like a beat in the rhythm of the sobs I haven't been able to suppress since yesterday.

We are still on that endless road. The humming waltz of tires against asphalt echoes inside my brain. I’m exhausted—the backs of my eyelids burn as if they’re filled with sand—but I can’t sleep. If I sleep, I’ll go back to that moment.

When they brought us to what they called the "protected zone," I thought it was finally over. On the first day, lying on my bunk, I stared at the fine mesh on the windows. The other kids were buried in the sounds of games and laughter from videos. But I knew. I felt it. Those screens weren't there to keep mosquitoes out; they were there to keep us in and someone else out.

Then came the sound... sirens that tore through my eardrums and hammered against my ribcage. I can still see the guards outside flicking their cigarettes to the ground, sprinting into the unknown in a panic. I dove under my quilt, desperate enough to believe that a thin piece of fabric could hide me from the world. The others were crying, screaming for their mothers. And then... then the sky fell to the earth.

A terrifying roar, a cloud of dust that scorched my lungs, and that incessant ringing in my ears. When I opened my eyes, the wall next to my bunk was gone; half of it had collapsed outward, the other half inward. I was alive. But those voices—the ones playing games next to me—they had gone silent. I’m someone who never even raised my voice in a neighborhood scuffle, yet suddenly, I found myself standing among rubble and corpses.

I ran to the adult quarters where my parents were, but all I found was a massive pile of wreckage. "It’s a dream," I told myself, "it’s just a nightmare." But the cold concrete beneath my feet and the smell of gunpowder burning my throat were far too real. I couldn't jump the fences, so I started looking for an exit. There was no blood in the corridors, but the soldiers, the civilians... they were all lying there, lifeless. I tried to pick up a weapon next to one of them; my fingers touched the cold metal, but it was so heavy, it felt like I was trying to lift the weight of the entire world. I let it go.

Behind a cracked door, I heard that voice: "Report! From the top!"

I peeked inside. A commander stood before a line of soldiers standing like robots. "C12 strike," one soldier said, his voice trembling. "The mess hall, the barracks... they're all gone. The supply room is intact, but it needs repairs."

The commander’s voice was ice cold: "No need. Pack it up. We’re moving out."

I heard the soldier say, "Commander, the B5 variants... it’s not safe there." The commander’s curses echoed down the hall. "I didn't ask for your damn opinion!"

As I tried to flee, the door swung open. A hand grabbed me by the scruff of my neck, lifting my feet off the ground. When I was hauled before the commander, I was so certain I was going to die that I thought my heart would stop. But he... he just stroked my head. His hands were stiff, yet his touch was strangely soft. "This is much more than a terrorist attack," he said.

Now, I am part of this massive convoy. I'm tucked between jeeps and armored vehicles. I am in the commander’s vehicle, under that cold but secure shadow. Does this dull my pain even a little? I don’t know. I thought about running, but the outside... the outside isn't the world we used to know anymore.

Please, if you are reading this... do not forget what I’ve written. As I huddle here in this corner, dying a thousand deaths with every jolt of the road, all I want is to be remembered.
