My eyes were still locked on Thomas. He bent over, grabbed the legs of David, and stood up, waiting.

I stayed on the floor, not understanding at first, but Thomas shook David’s legs and motioned towards the body.

“Ye…yes,” I got out, my voice shaking. 

I took a tissue out of my back pocket and wiped the blood off my face. The dark red color almost made me gag. I threw the tissue on the floor and walked over to David’s body. His bright blue eyes now stared emptily into the tile ceiling. I bent down, but a tremble ran up my body before I even touched him. Thomas still held David’s legs, staring at me. I took a deep breath, walked over, and picked up David's wrists. Their warmth sent another shiver down my body. Thomas looked back and began walking out of my office.

The hall felt colder than usual. The ceiling seemed like it would fall any second. I tried to keep my eyes fixed on Thomas, ignoring what I carried in my hands. Thomas’s head was still turned back, making sure we were walking the right way. Around the corner stood the door to the preparation room. Thomas let one of the legs go and opened the door. The leg dropped down to the floor and echoed through the empty hall as it hit the floor. We carried the body in. The room still had hay lying on the floor and around the chair. I had to clench my jaw and look away from it. The thought of Samantha was still fresh in my mind.

“She, she dropped the flowers.”

He looked at me, his eyebrows furrowed.

“She dropped them, Thomas.”

“Yes.”

“What?”

“Yes, she dropped them.”

“But the Wall closed!”

He looked me up and down.

“Leave now. I’ll take care of the rest,” he said coldly.

I stood there for a few seconds, staring at him. Not even his pupils moved.

He motioned his hand towards the door, and I knew it was time to go.

My body moved across the Building. My steps echoed through the empty gray halls, walking from floor to floor. Thoughts and visions rushed through my head. David screaming, Samantha crying, her mouth full of hay and the bouquet, the bouquet. The image of it grew inside my mind. I stumbled to a bench and sat down, breathing, trying to feel my feet on the floor. Slowly, the images disappeared, and all I could see were the dark grey tile walls of the Building staring at me.

I got back up and began making my way down the hall, passing the brown wooden office doors. My eyes glanced over the names until I reached the stairs. I looked to my right, and the door read **"REV.**" **CHARLES KAMAS.** My feet stopped automatically. Charles Kamas took me under his wing during my apprenticeship, teaching me how to lead a prayer, set up the ritual, and teach people about the Wall. His office was all the way on the fifth floor. I didn’t even realize I made it that far. I dreaded walking up the flights of stairs back during my apprenticeship, but now, in my dazed state, I barely noticed walking this far up. 

Before I finished my thoughts, my hand was already knocking at the door. The familiar rattle, the nimble steps. The door handle moved, and the face of Reverend Kamas appeared, looking just as I remembered him: the sunken brown eyes, large nose, balding head, and protruding chin. He opened his eyes a little when he saw me, but they immediately went back to being half-closed. 

“Preacher, please come in.” 

“Thank you.” 

His office hadn’t changed. Large wooden shelves stood around the room, populated with history and law books. Opposite the door was a dark red mahogany table and chairs with leather seats. His table was clean as always, with only his notebook, a pen, and a small iron table lamp. I sat in the chair. I could feel the cold leather even through my clothes. Rev. Kamas sat opposite me and put his hands in his lap.

“Working on the ritual’s history.” He smiled and pointed to his table. “To what do I owe the visit?”

I looked around the room and took a deep breath.

“Samantha. This morning.”

“Oh yes.” He nodded his head.

“I heard. Terrible. I’m sorry for you, Preacher. This rarely happens. People like the ritual, but every once in a while, someone cracks. I wouldn’t expect it from David. Going that far. Good thing Thomas was there to help you.”

I grabbed onto the armrests, clenching my jaw. How did he already know?

“You’re not expected to take the Sunday service. I’ll lead it for you. Go rest. ” He began getting up out of the chair.

“No. Something else.”

He narrowed his eyes and sat back down.

“Don’t think about it, Preacher. David was crazy.” 

Memories of David and Samantha talking lovingly about each other flashed before my eyes.

“Samantha dropped her flowers.”

“What?”

“She dropped them before the Wall closed.”

He paused and looked deep into my eyes.

“Did you not have the black cape? How did you see it?”

“It was before I put it on.”

“Did others see it?”

“No. They had theirs on.”

“Did the Wall close?”

“Yes.”

“Good.” His word cut through me.

“Reverend, she dropped her flowers. The ritual broke, but the Wall closed.”

“The Wall accepted it. It was enough.”

“But the ritual. It states she needs to hold the flowers or else.”

“The Wall closed. No one saw the flowers. People are happy again. They feel safe. Look out.” He pointed out the window. Children ran around the town square, couples sat on the benches, holding hands.

I stared out the window. What was he saying? Did Samantha give her life up for nothing?

“But Reverend.”

“You’ll understand.” The Reverend said and stood up. He walked to the door and opened it. “Take your rest. I’ll take the Sunday service.”

It felt like the whole room was closing in on me, the shelves falling, the books burying me. 

“Preacher?” the Reverend asked, pointing at the door.

I shook my head and quickly got up, almost running out the door, but he stopped me with his hand.

“I’d keep it to myself,” he said, patted my back, and closed the door behind me.

Children’s screams could be heard in the hall. I walked closer to one of the windows. The sun outside glistened. Everyone looked happy. I could feel a smile form on my face. Did it really matter? 

“The purity dress, the transient hay,” echoed through my head. Then the image of Samantha flashed, her in the chair, hay stuffed in her mouth, tears rolling down her cheeks. It made me gag. No. I had to know more. I couldn’t let it go.

There was still an hour until the Reverend would leave. I locked myself in the bathroom at the end of the hall and waited.

After an hour and a half, I walked out, turning my head in each direction. As I walked to the Reverend’s office, it suddenly felt like each of my steps echoed through the entire Building. I sped up and stopped at his door, putting my ear to it. Silence. I knocked. Nothing. The handle twisted in my hand. It felt like my heart would beat out of my chest. I peered through the door. The room was dark, empty, quiet. I slipped quickly into the room and shut the door behind me. I made my way to the Reverend’s table, turning on the small iron lamp on his table. His notebook sat on his desk. I took a deep breath and grabbed the corner of the notebook.

Did I want to know? 

Yes. There was no doubt.

I grabbed the corner and opened it.

As I flipped through the pages, I couldn’t find anything of interest. It was mostly scribbles and notes, nothing coherent. Then I stumbled onto something that looked like a record sheet.

In the left column were names, and in the right column were small descriptions.

Sally Duran. Civil clothes - the Wall closed.

Susan Meril. Refused dress - the Wall closed.

Mary Abigail. No flowers - the Wall closed.

My eyes darted over the records. The Wall closed each time, no matter who or what was in it.

Then my eyes stopped on the last name.

Samantha Buro. Dropped flowers - the Wall closed.

I put my head into my hands, breathing hard. 

Did Samantha die for no reason? Did all the others, too? Was it a lie? Was it all a lie? I looked out the window, trying to collect my thoughts. A wave of coldness washed over me. In the window’s reflection stood a figure. One that I knew too well. Its face was cold, expressionless as always. 

Thomas. 

I turned around, ready to do something, anything! A dull pain vibrated through my head. My body tumbled to the ground. The room began to spin. The shelves began to fall again, burying me in a pile of books. Then everything went dark.

I woke up in the morning, my mind banging, expecting to see the dullness of the preparation room, but I saw white walls and a small desk in the corner. It was my room! My clothes were still on, but someone took off my shoes and laid them neatly beside the door. There was a strong taste of iron in my mouth. I slowly got out of bed. My head spun immediately. I stumbled a few steps and grabbed onto the table. 

The sun shone bright outside. The square was empty. In the middle stood a wooden structure. My eyes darted upwards. A platform. A crossbeam. And a noose. The carefully tied noose. My head spun again. I almost fell to the ground. Running to the door, I grabbed the handle, but it was locked. There was no point in trying the window. What would it even be for? My room was on the 7th floor of the building.

I hear steps coming down the hall. Two people. One’s steps were light and nimble. The other’s steps were dull and loud. Thomas and the Reverend have come. They’ve come to take me down, down to the gallows.
