Not the first, not the last. Was supposed to be married before the last harvest, but my fiance was stolen from me. Apparently a pattern in these parts.

It was the cold season in the Pacific Northwest, when the rains return and the fog settles low among the cedars. Fields had gone soft with mud. The last of the crops were being brought in before winter. Nights stretched longer with each passing day, and sound carried strangely through the valleys after dark.

That was when the singing began.

Three weeks before the wedding, I woke sometime past midnight to the sound of a fiddle out beyond the woods behind my property. Slow playing. Uneven. The sort of music that makes a man uneasy without rightly knowing why.

My fiance heard it too.

I know because she sat upright in bed staring toward the window as if somebody stood outside calling her name.

After that she changed little by little.

I would wake before dawn and find the back door cracked open though she swore she had not touched it. Some evenings I’d catch her standing at the edge of the trees staring into the fog without speaking. Once I found mud on the floorboards beside the bed as though she’d gone wandering barefoot during the night.

And always she hummed the same tune beneath her breath.

Then one night she whispered a name in her sleep.

“Cotton-Eyed Joe.”

I thought it nonsense then.

An old song. A dream.

I know better now.

She vanished six days before the wedding.

No tracks. No horse missing from the stable. Her belongings untouched. The house still locked from within save for the back door hanging open to the rain.

And somewhere deep among the cedars, the fiddle played.

The sheriff searched. So did neighbors from town. They found nothing but wet earth and my own footprints.

Three days later, a Salish man named Tseepah came to my property unasked. Quiet old fellow from farther north along the river. Folks around here claimed he knew the old stories too well for comfort.

He walked the edge of the woods in silence before finally asking:

“Did she hear the singing?”

I told him yes.

When I spoke the name my fiance had whispered, he shook his head slowly.

“Joh,” he corrected me.

Not Joe.

Joh.

The way he said it made the hairs rise on my arms.

I asked him what Joh was.

For a long while he fed cedar bark into my stove without answering. Then he said:

“Not spirit. Not beast. A grief.”

He told me there were stories older than the towns here. Stories of brides vanishing during the cold months after hearing music in the woods. Their husbands and lovers would go searching for them and never truly return themselves.

Some claimed they glimpsed the missing women afterward.

A pale face between the trees.

A reflection standing in river water.

A figure in white beyond the fog.

Always distant.

Always watching.

Always just far enough away to follow.

The men searching for them became consumed. They abandoned farms. Families. Church. They wandered deeper into the wilderness each passing season chasing voices through the rain and dark.

Eventually the searching hollowed them out until nothing remained except longing.

That was Joh.

Not one man.

A curse passed from one grieving soul to the next.

“The settlers changed the name,” Tseepah told me. “Made songs from it so they would not remember.”

I asked him how to get my fiance back.

He looked at me for a long while before answering.

“You do not.”

I near struck him for saying it.

Instead I demanded he tell me where to search.

At this, Tseepah grew quiet.

Then he warned me:

“When she calls to you from the woods, do not follow.”

That was two days ago.

I followed anyway.

The first time I heard her voice beyond the cedars, I nearly wept from relief. She sounded frightened. Cold. Calling my name somewhere out in the fog.

I took my lantern and went after her.

I searched till morning and found nothing.

The second night I saw her standing among the trees in her white wedding dress. Pale as river mist. Smiling faintly.

I ran toward her.

She vanished deeper into the woods.

Every night since, she appears farther away.

And every night I follow.

Folks in town have begun avoiding me now. They say I look sickly. That I speak to myself when I think nobody is listening. Yesterday I found my boots soaked with mud beside the bed though I cannot recall leaving the house.

And tonight, while sharpening my knife by the stove, I caught myself humming that tune.

The fiddle song.

The one from the woods.

Tseepah visited once more yesterday evening. He stood in the doorway studying me with a sorrowful look I did not much care for.

Then he asked:

“When you find her… will you still know her from the others?”

I demanded he explain himself.

But he only shook his head and said:

“Joh never understands what he becomes until it is too late.”

Tonight the singing has returned again.

Closer than before.

And though every sensible part of me knows I should bar the doors and stay by the fire…

I reckon I shall follow it once more.
