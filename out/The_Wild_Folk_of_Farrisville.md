What's the first thing you think of when you hear about men living in the mountains of Appalachia? Your mind probably wanders to an age-old depiction of a bunch of hillbillies who looked like they'd crawled out of a cartoon or maybe an old episode of The Beverly Hillbillies. Folks who wear worn-out overalls while wielding a double-barreled shotgun, or perhaps an inbred clan of troglodytes that live in log cabins. In reality, the stereotyped mountain folk you see in films and television are just a fictionalized amalgamation of hateful stereotypes to make rural Appalachians seem subhuman. But there is a glimmer of truth deep down in the hatefulness of these portrayals. Mountain men, true mountain men, are real, and they are *nothing* like this, as much as I wished that they were. Wild folk are a different breed of human altogether. I would've given anything to deal with a bunch of inbred hillbillies who drink moonshine and wield archaic weaponry.

Let me backtrack a second and introduce myself. My name is Marcum, and I used to be a police officer in Farrisville. It's a small Appalachian town, and I've had the pleasure of being an officer for nearly twenty-eight years. I'm getting up in my years, and truth be told, I'm aiming to retire early. The force has plenty of good, much younger police officers than me, and I'm totally fine leaving the town to them. I've seen many scenarios during my tenure as an officer. Stand off, drug-fueled rampages, domestic abuse, and other unpleasantries, I'm sure y'all can figure out. It's a rough field, but I managed. Well, I managed until about a few months ago when the mountain men came into our lives.

It was summer, if I recall, and we were responding to a case where a woman called in a suspected drug addict in her yard. She said that she saw a woman crawl into her backyard from the wooded area behind her house on all fours. She was completely naked, filthy, and had matted, unkempt hair. Her legs were hairy, and so were her forearms; there was a heavy amount of peach fuzz on her face, too, somewhat beardlike, but not quite. The family dog barked at the intruder, and the naked woman galloped toward the dog and began to savage beat and bite it. When we arrived, we saw no sign of the woman, but we found a trail of blood leading into the woods that stretched on for miles. The owner of the house shared surveillance footage that was caught on a security camera mounted towards the back yard. The woman, after taking a few bites out of the dog's soft, hairless underbelly, bit down into the dog's pelt and carried it back with her into the woods like a coyote carrying off a rabbit.

Days passed, no matter how many times we enhanced the image and shared it on social media, no one came forward to say that they knew the wild woman. She seemingly appeared out of nowhere, attacked the dog, and carried it off back to wherever it was. The force said we'd look into it, but we just felt like there was no proper response for how to deal with this type of situation. We're a very small town, and sending all of our officers on a manhunt into the woods seemed like a bad idea. We told the owner the situation, and thankfully, they understood. They didn't like it, but they understood. Weeks passed, and we got another call from a man on the complete other side of town. Complained about a drug addict on his front lawn in his apple tree. It sounded familiar to the case we had to drop, and we drove as quickly as we could to the scene.

We arrived, and this time we weren't late. There, in the front lawn and underneath the tree, sat a large naked man sitting crisscrossed, devouring a small pile of apples. My partner Liam and I exited our vehicles and approached the wild man. He had a huge head of hair that sprang out in several different directions, a massive beard that hung down to his belly button, and there were thick strands of hair on his arms, legs, and back. He was taking huge bites into the tiny, green apples as the juices spurted out of his mouth and dribbled down his chin. I could smell him from all the way back from the street, a pungent stink that assaulted the senses. It made my eyes water as I approached him. He had large, calloused hands, but the rest of his body could've been described that way. Large and callous. He looked up at us with two beady, unflinching eyes that seemed vacant of intelligence.

"Sir, do you know where you are?"

No response.

"Do you have a name?"

Again, silence.

Liam looked to me and shook his head, whispering,

"I don't like this Marcum."

I nodded in agreement. This whole thing had me on edge. I cleared my throat and put on my 'authoritative voice' and told the slack-jawed wild man,

"Sir, we are asking you to leave the premises. This is private property."

He got to his 'feet,' and by this I mean on all fours, standing in an ape-like position, gawking at us with those beady, blue eyes that began to grow wider. He bared his teeth, crooked, yellowed, but the canine teeth were larger than the average man's, almost overtly predatory. It began to give out this guttural whooping sound. Its pursed lips were weathered but cracked. It inched forward, continuing it's whhoping warning towards us. Liam slowly withdrew his taser, and when it galloped at me, he shot it. The prongs stuck it in the neck, the shoulder, the right pectoral, and in the side. It winced in pain, but it did not go down.

It turned toward Liam's direction, eyes furious, mouth baring its teeth, and lunged at him. I scrambled for my pistol as the large man mounted him and began clubbing his chest with his massive arms. From afar, they looked barely defined, but in motion, they revealed a terrifying strength. Liam couldn't scream; the wind was being pounded out of him, and the only noise that emanated from his throat was a rattling wheeze. Liam tried to shove him off, but the wild man took his hand and bit off three of his fingers. It sounded like someone biting into a carrot, and they tore away from the hand so easily. I finally sobered up, drew my gun, and fired into the side of his head. The hulking man collapsed on top of him.

I radioed in to the station to send an ambulance. An officer was injured, and a man was dead. The homeowner came out and saw the carnage and yelled that he would call an ambulance. Before I could tell him that I'd already made the call, he retreated into his home to make the call. Liam was clenching his teeth as he gripped his hand, screaming curses in agony, and I don't blame him. I'd probably say worse if I'd lost three of my fingers too. I was walking towards him when I felt a sledgehammer strike my chest, an awful, sharp pain. I thought it was just a palpation, but then it came again, and again. I doubled over, clutching at my chest, and said a silent prayer. I could hear Liam calling my name,

"Marcum? Jesus, Marcum, you okay? Marcum?! MA-"

I awoke in a hospital bed surrounded by family. My wife Melissa was holding my hand, and my sons brought their families to see me, too. I was so relieved to see my grandchildren that I wept in joy. Evidently, weeks passed, and I was out for a long time, according to my wife. The doctor said it was a heart attack, a deadly one. In fact, he said my heart stopped, but if it weren't for Liam giving me CPR, it was nothing but helpful as he walked me through life adjustments I'd need to make in order to not have another incident like this. After a few more check-ups and visits from folks I knew, I was released a few days later.

It's funny, I woke up in the hospital and felt scared that I'd come so close to death. Yet, it became this joyous occasion where everyone I knew showed up to check in. It was the last time I felt any joy in Farrisville, because what followed would be the darkest chapter in our town's history.

I called in to the station as I was eating some low-sodium ham that my wife had cooked up for me for breakfast. It was answered by Jessie, who was a good stand-by and always filled me in on the latest. We talked for a small bit about the little things before I asked about Liam,

"Liam is doing fine. He wanted to visit you in the hospital, but he...I don't know, I think he's just got bad nerves since that attack. I hear he's going to step away from the force. From what I hear, he's having a difficult time adjusting to his hand."

"That's a shame, I ought to thank him for saving my life. He's a hero for that, and I owe him."

I paused for a second and followed it up by asking,

"And...what about the John Doe I shot? Does he have any family? Any...next of kin?"

Jessie cleared her throat and warily spoke,

"Uh....I don't know how to explain this to you, Marcum..."

Not only was there no next of kin, but there were also no DNA matches; someone was completely out of the system. Yet, that wasn't the only strange thing.

"Marcum, there's more of them."

Since I was hospitalized, there have been dozens of cases of 'wild people' around the town. They come from the trees, the mountains, and all of them were different from one another. This wasn't just a random case of coincidence where two strange folks just wandered into people's yards. There were so many more, different heights, skin colors, hair colors, and they were all naked, and brutish. It had been seemingly constant, with the last call happening just yesterday, the woman on the line simply said,

"I've got one of them wild people rooting through my trash."

As I was recovering from my stay in the hospital, more and more cases transpired. It had gotten so bad that a city council meeting was held. Everyone met in the town rec center, and folding chairs were aligned on the basketball court to accommodate the crowd. There were some officers there who greeted me and asked how I was doing. I thought about attending in uniform and lending a hand, but this time I just observed as a citizen of Farrisville.

When the doors opened, people poured in. Most times, a city council meeting had sparse attendance and the occasional question from those in attendance. Tonight though? There were so many that the folding chairs were taken up, and other folks had to just stand there on their own two feet. When the city council members filed in, they didn't discuss anything else; they went straight for the jugular.

"It seems our town is having a bit of a strange epidemic," the mayor started, "These wild people are invading our town, and I just wanted to let everyone know that we are working on it."

From how the officers eyed each other with sarcastic glances, I could tell this couldn't be further from the truth. In fact, I think the whole crowd smelled this bullshit before the meeting even began. As the council passed bills and motions, the crowd stirred, mumbling under their breaths. The council was halfway through a discussion about repairing the city parking lot when a man named Ted Hollister chucked a soda can at the council members. They can burst and spew soda over them. The officers apprehended him, and the council quickly dismissed the meeting. It was a bad idea; it didn't take a genius to see that. It caused a bunch of shouting, throwing, and even some folks tried to charge the leaving council members. I slipped out during the chaos.

The straw that broke the camel's back came a day later. A woman named Willow Jean heard her baby crying in the middle of the night and prepared to fix a bottle like she did every other night. Then the crying stopped. She dropped the baggie of formula and bolted for the baby's room. Silhouetted by moonlight, she saw a wild woman crouched over the crib and holding the limp body of the child in her hands. She screamed, and the wild woman screamed back in retaliation. The Willow went to attack her, but the beastly wild woman galloped away with the body scooped in her hands.

The manhunt started the following morning.

I squeezed into my uniform, brought my good flashlight from home, and reported to the station. Along with nearly fifty volunteers, the police department enveloped a point of interest along a brush of woods east of town. The search would have to be on foot; the woods were far too dense to drive through, and we weren't allowed to hack down a path to get to wherever these things lay their heads to rest.

We formed an almost line as we trudged over uneven ground, riddled with wet leaves, mud, and mosquitoes. There were so many trees that the sunlight was blocked out almost entirely, except for a few beads of light that shone on the forest floor. A man to the left of me named Eddy Birch panted and wiped sweat from his brow and wheezed to me,

"We gotta be close, right? I mean, if there's a group of one, we gotta be seeing one soon."

I kept on and spoke back,

"You ever hunt?"

"Yessir."

"What do you hunt?"

"Deer."

"Lemme tell you something, how long did it take for you to track and hunt one?"

"Lord, I don't know. Sometimes it was days."

I didn't speak, I just gestured to him as if to silently say 'there ya go, you answered your question'. He didn't like it, though; he grimaced and panted through the woods.

Another hour passed, and the sun was starting to get low. Through the leaves above, I saw that the bit of sky that was visible to us was waning into darkness. But that's what the flashlights were for after all. I took a breather on a log, and as I did, I caught a whiff of something. It was smoky like a bonfire. I turned to the radio to the other officers,

"I smell a fire over here. Should I go in or wait for backup?"

I waited a few seconds before I heard a voice reply,

"Hold up, Officer Dawson will join you in a bit, he's headed your way right now."

"I'll be sittin' on a log, standing by."

I sat there and took a twig to pluck the dirt from under my nails as I waited for Dawson to arrive. The smoke smell did not diminish; in fact, it just got more and more potent. In about five minutes or so, I see Dawson sprinting my way. Dawson was a rookie but a well-trained one. In another life, he would've been in the military. His big glasses rested over his nose, sweat pouring from his face and staining his uniform.

"Goddamn, you can get lost in here," he said breathlessly,

"Many folks have." I said, "Thankfully, there's a lotta people here scattered."

"Where are the folks that were with you?"

"They've gone on forward, but I want you to smell the air. You smell it too, right?"

The rookie sniffed the air and nodded,

"Like a campfire."

"Exactly."

I got to my feet and began following the smell. I went over stumps, hilly areas, and what I came upon made my hair stand up on end.

It was the entrance of a cave. Trickling from the roof of its mouth was white smoke, which almost looked like a sort of dragon you'd see written about in fairy tales. Maybe that's what this all was, a big, scary tale made to scare children. But the grip of the rookie's hand upon my shoulder snapped me back to attention. This was reality, and I had to come to grips with the fact that sometimes evil things simply existed without rhyme or reason. I looked into the abyss and then back at the rookie with an expression that seemed to worry him. I guess I was having a hard time masking how afraid I really was. I cleared my throat and asked,

"We going in?"

He nodded.

"Radio to the others, he turned away to speak into his mic, but all I heard was silence. I turned around in confusion and saw he was staring up into the trees. I nudged him,

"C'mon, radio the others this is-"

Then I saw what I was looking at.

Above perched in the trees were four wild men. Hairy, naked, and slathered in mud that made their skin as crackly and dry as the bark of the tree they were resting on. We were frozen in place. These wild men stared down at us with wide, terrifyingly intelligent eyes. I grabbed my shoulder mic and spoke close and quietly into it.

"This is Marcum." I whispered, "We have found..."

My eyes saw the rookie reach for his weapon. Before I could seize his hand, he had already withdrawn the pistol and fired into one of the wild men in the tree. It slumped backward and fell awkwardly to the forest floor. The others were already upon us. They leaped down from the trees and galloped towards us. I withdrew my pistol, but I heard the cave behind us erupt with noise. Whoopings, screeches, and braying sounds filled the darkness of the cave. More were coming. Something struck the back of my head, and I was out.

I awoke in what looked like hell. An enormous cavern with a roof that seemed to stretch on forever. There was a massive bonfire in the center, and the wild people surrounded it. When this little invasion started, folks theorized that there were maybe a dozen of these wild folk.

There were hundreds.

I reached for my pistol, but there was nothing there. They somehow knew it was something dangerous to them. Their pale, naked bodies danced around the flame. In the dark, I could see buildings illuminated by the flickering inferno. Along the cave walls looked to be old buildings of sorts; they looked like derelict facades of what used to be a civilization. The decor almost looked to be colonial. There was a bank, a church, businesses, and homes. There were the tattered remains of an old Betsy Ross version of the American flag, or at least that's what I thought it was. The old colonial structures were built into the rock and encircled the cavern; perhaps where I was caged up in front of the inferno was what remained of their town square. I examined the cage and saw that it was an amalgamation of the bones of several different animals and hide. I kicked at the cage expecting it to break loose, but to my shock, it held tight. They were so much smarter than we expected.

To my right, I saw the rookie, his hair was damp with blood. His eyes met mine; they were tired. He shouted over the thunderous roar of the bonfire,

"I TRIED TO CALL THE OTHERS. NO SIGNAL DOWN HERE. EVEN THE COMMS DON'T WORK."

Tears filled his eyes, and he shouted again,

"I'M SORRY."

Four large wild men approached his cage. One grunted, and the others opened the cage. They stripped the clothes off him. He weakly resisted, but it was met with violence. One of the men bit into the nape of his neck, and he suddenly stopped moving. He slumped to the ground, but I saw that his eyes were darting around. He was still alive, paralyzed, but alive. I wished that he had just died then and there. They brought a large stick with a massive pointed end, and they began to stick it inside of him. He didn't scream. I don't think he was capable of it. But he was in pain. Tears rolled from his eyes, and he emitted a groan like the beginnings of a child's cry.

I saw the skewer go deeper inside of him, and I saw that blood had begun to trickle down the wooden post. With one last push of effort from the wild men, they thrust the post into him one last time, and I finally saw the tip of the skewer poke through his shoulder blade. They hoisted him over to the bonfire. Around the fire were circular holes where they placed these skewers. There were some surrounding the fire that had the corpse of a deer, and one that had to have been one of the missing dogs from Farrisville. They placed the skewer into the dug-out groove in the cave floor, and as it sank into the earth, the wooden spear burrowed deeper into the rookie and opened the puncture protruding from his neck even wider. Blood ran down his skin, bathing him in a dark red.

I watched him cook for hours and wondered when my time would come. I saw hair burn away, the skin bubble and droop off the body in thin droplets of melted flesh. His body sagged on the spit as his flesh seared and tenderized against the flames. The wild people looked on in anticipation, they licked their lips, and a child crawled towards the body and pinched a piece of meat from the thigh. It came off so easily, like brisket off the bone. I felt sick, and I turned away from the sight of the wild folk delving into their meal. The sounds of the ripping, squelching, and wet chewing were torment. I could hear them moan in joy with every bite that they took.

After the feast, they returned to the husks that used to be this subterranean town. I didn't have anything to cut my way out of the small boney cage. I couldn't sleep. I just looked at the pile of gore and bone that used to be Dawson. I began to pray, and I didn't stop for a good while. My prayer was interrupted by a finger poking my back through the cage. I turned to see one of the young wild children. It had dirty blonde hair and yellowish eyes. It had an innocence to it as it curiously looked at me.

I am not proud of what I did next. I was desperate and scared.

He was so small and fragile, I thought maybe he was nimble too. That maybe he'd scurry away from me before I could get a hold of me. But with one fierce grab, I seized his throat. It began to yelp and cry, and I brought it close to me. I screamed out,

"DOWN HERE, YOU SICK FUCKS!"

The horde emerged from their houses, whooping and grunting. What I assumed to be the mother shrieked at the sight of me gripping her child's throat. It was held back by another wild person, almost looking like she was being consoled. A few wild men approached, and when they did, I gripped the child's throat tighter. It whimpered in pain, and they stopped in their tracks. I used my other hand to grip the cage. I shouted,

"OUT! I WANT OUT!"

They, of course, didn't understand me. I gripped the cage made of bone and shook it. I screamed as I did it.

"OUT! OUT! I WANT OUT!"

To my shock, they cut the cage open. I stood to my feet, legs feeling like jelly, but my grip was still razor tight. I scooped the child up with my other hand. And walked backward towards an entrance that I saw on the other side of the cavern. It was the only place where the light of the fire couldn't illuminate. The wild people watched on in terror, the mother wailing in panic while some of the other followed me. None dare sneak up behind me because even if they bit into my neck, I'd break the child's neck. I walked backward into the dark, feeling where the breeze was blowing into the cave. The wild men and women stalked me as I walked, the mother watching on with large, terrified eyes.

I walked backward for a long time, and as I grew closer to the exit, I heard chatter from behind me. Voices of folks I recognized, I shouted,

"Anyone out there?!"

Dozens of flashlights flooded the corridor, like a bunch of spotlights washing over every smooth surface of the cave. The light then found the wild people and me. A voice shouted out,

"Here! Down here!"

I didn't turn around, but I heard dozens of voices chattering, and then the sounds of weapons being readied to be fired. Another voice spoke up,

"Just back up, Marcum, if they move, we'll fire."

I inched backward with trembling feet. I could feel the air against the sweat of my neck. I could hear the sounds of nature and the murmurings of the search party.

The mother broke through the other wild folk and ran towards her child, arms outstretched. I wish I could tell you that I did something noble, that I gave the child back to her mother, and both parties were satisfied enough to go their separate ways.

I broke its neck in my hand.

Some delicate and small, it just twisted out of instinct, or at least that's what I've been telling myself. The more I think back on it, maybe I did it out of malice. I let the child drop to the cave floor as I sprinted towards the exit. The mother dropped to her knees and began to wail. The other wild folk looked on in shock, and they were in disbelief. I turned and ran towards the search party and saw that there were dozens of armed men at the front of the cave, nearly thirty or more. Their shotguns, rifles, and pistols all aimed at the cave corridor.

When I was outside, I gasped for fresh air, my heart thundering in my chest. I was sure that another heart attack was coming for me. Then, I heard the gunshots. They kept firing over and over again. When the symphony of violence was over, it was deathly still. An officer, whose name escapes me at the moment, slung a large duffel bag off his back and zipped it open to reveal tear gas grenades. He pulled the pin on it and slung it down the cave. The sounds of screams and cries were heard as it exploded down in the dark. The other onlookers and officers joined in, withdrawing grenades and throwing them down in the dark. I lost count of how many they threw down into the cavernous city, but it was thirty-something. The cavern was smothered in tear gas. One of the officers shone his flashlight into the corridor, but was met with nothing but a wall of white smoke.

We walked back, and as we did so, I told the others about Dawson and said his body was still down there. The other officers told me to stay home and rest up. They assured me that tomorrow, if there were any left alive, they were taking a party down there to destroy whoever remained. Last I heard, there were none alive because they could smell the bodies from miles away. Dawson's remains were buried in the town cemetery, the remains of the wild people were burned in the subterranean city, and the cave entrance was bricked up and smoothed over with concrete.

I still think a lot about the wild folk. I retired shortly after, and I haven't looked back since. I wake up every night drenched in sweat, dreaming about the little child I held in my grip, and the muffled crunch of bone I felt in my palm. I could still envision an entire civilization being smoked out like rabbits and choking in the dark. I could still hear their screams; they may have been wild, but there were people after all. It feels wrong to pity them because everyone shakes my hand and tells me about how much of a hero I was, but I don't feel like one. They lived in the dark for God knows how long, and then they got a taste of sunlight, and they loved it. They wallowed in fresh grass, tasted the fruits from trees, but their carnivorous nature forged by desperation painted them as monsters. We exterminated them. They were rabid, they were brutish, they were animals, but aren't we animals too? Are we so unique that we make an exception for extermination? They were intelligent, maybe even cruel, but are we no different? They were out and about looking for food, maybe even a new home to rest their heads, but hasn't humankind done worse for less? Did they rub their cruelty onto us, or did we rub it onto them? All I know is that I can't look my grandchildren in the eyes anymore; the child I held looked just like my youngest granddaughter.

Sometimes, I sit in the backyard and hope I see something. Maybe there was a chance for these wild folks to escape and wander off to some new cave system or a new forest to rest up and start life again. It's a foolish dream, but I'll keep dreaming it. Perhaps one day I'll see one of the wild folk running through the woods behind my house, kicking up the leaves as he does. Maybe, just maybe, I can dream that there's still some wild folks in Farrisville.
