The first night my phone vibrated on the nightstand, it stopped before I could touch it.

That should have been enough to wake me fully. Instead, the smell arrived.

Cold air and pennies, sharp and metallic, like a hospital hallway after midnight when the floors have just been mopped and no one is supposed to be there.

I lay still, counting my breaths. The dark felt crowded, like it had learned where my body ended.

I live alone in a ground-floor apartment tucked behind a thin line of pine trees. Cheap. Quiet. I moved in after a breakup because I wanted silence and walls that didn’t know my name yet. At night the branches scrape together like knuckles. You get used to it.

That night, nothing felt used to me.

My phone buzzed again. One short pulse. I grabbed it so fast my wrist cramped.

No notification. Battery full. No missed calls.

I rolled onto my side, but the smell stayed. The room felt incorrect. Not dangerous. Just wrong, the way a room looks after someone has stood in it too long.

I turned on the lamp.

Everything was where it belonged. Bed. Dresser. Closed bedroom door. I stared at the handle longer than I meant to. It looked darker than the rest of the metal, like it had been warmed by hands.

I slept eventually.

I dreamed I was standing in the pine trees outside my window, watching myself sleep. When I woke up, my phone was face down on the floor.

The footprint appeared the next morning.

It was on the inside of my bedroom door. Faint but unmistakable. A bare foot pressed flat against the wood. Toes down. Heel smudged.

Someone had stood there from my side of the room.

I stood in my pajamas with coffee cooling in my hand and tried to explain it away. Sleepwalking. I’d never done it before, but bodies lie all the time. I wiped at it with a towel.

It didn’t smear. It wasn’t moisture. It was oil. I washed my hands twice and still smelled pennies.

At work, I felt evaluated. I kept catching my reflection in dark computer monitors, elevator doors, anything glossy enough to show me myself. Every time, I expected someone else to be standing just behind my shoulder.

When I got home, the smell was waiting for me inside the apartment.

Pennies. Cold air.

I checked every room. Windows locked. Bathroom empty. Closet empty. Under the bed, dust and a sock I’d lost months ago.

No sign of anyone else.

The smell faded after an hour. I made dinner. Ate it standing up. Fear is a hobby the brain picks up when it has too much time.

That night, I left the lamp on.

I woke up at 3:12 a.m.

Breathing came from the other side of my bedroom door.

Slow. Patient. Not hiding. It was the sound someone makes when they’ve been standing very still for a long time and finally remember they need air.

I sat up, heart hammering hard enough to make my vision pulse. I watched the thin line of light beneath the door.

The breathing stopped.

It didn’t fade. It ceased, like a machine losing power.

Then my phone buzzed.

The screen lit my hands white. A text from an unknown number.

You moved the lamp. It makes the room feel safer.

My fingers went numb. I typed before I could think.

*Who is this?*

The reply came instantly.

*Please do not open the door.*

What scared me wasn’t the warning.

It was the politeness.

Whatever was out there had preferences. Expectations. It believed I would respect them.

I didn’t open the door.

Morning leaked through the curtains like diluted milk. When I finally stood, my legs shook so badly I had to grab the dresser. I opened the door.

The hallway was empty.

Centered on the carpet was a wet footprint. Bare. Toes pointed toward my room. Heel toward the living area.

Someone had been standing there facing me for a long time before stepping away.

I stayed with a friend that night. I told her I thought someone had broken in. The text thread was gone from my phone. Completely erased.

She joked about carbon monoxide. I laughed too loud.

When I returned the next day, the smell was gone. The footprint was gone.

I bought a camera. I set it up facing my bedroom door. I needed proof that I wasn’t inventing things just to survive them. That night, I slept in my own bed.

At 3:12 a.m., my phone buzzed.

I opened the camera feed. The screen showed my bedroom door from the hallway side. Nothing happened for a long time.

Then the handle turned.

Not fully. Just enough to test whether I was still willing to lie to myself. The door opened an inch. A black wedge cut into the frame.

Something pressed into the gap.

Not a face.

A shoulder, maybe. Something with weight but the wrong proportions, like it had been assembled from memory instead of anatomy.

It stayed there.

Listening.

The smell flooded my room.

My phone buzzed again.

*You see why I asked.*

The feed glitched.

For a single frame, the shape turned toward the camera.

I didn’t see eyes.

I saw skin that looked like it had learned how to be skin by watching people from far away.

Then the feed went black.

I don’t remember leaving my bed. I remember locking myself in the bathroom. Sitting in the tub with my knees to my chest.

Footsteps moved through the apartment. Slow. Careful. Like whoever owned them understood walls were listening.

My phone buzzed until it died.

I moved out two days later. Sold the furniture. Changed my number. I told myself it had just been curious.

I told myself curiosity doesn’t last forever.

Last night, in my new place, my phone vibrated once on the nightstand.

I didn’t turn on the lamp.

I lay very still and listened to the familiar breathing on the other side of the door.

Then the phone lit up.

One new message.

*May I come in yet?*
