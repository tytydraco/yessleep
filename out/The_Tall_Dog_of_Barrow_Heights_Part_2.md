Pastel lines shimmer into existence all around me.  
  
They're two-dimensional, stuttering like animation frames. The shuddering brown outline of Barrow Heights rises in the distance, its windows empty black squares. There's a pulsing scribble of green bushes in the foreground, vibrating with childish imperfection.

I can't move.

My body is fixed in place, locked to the edge of the frame like a thumbtack pinning me to the margin. I can turn my head, barely, but my feet won't obey. The drawing has borders apparently, a white void where the crayon stops, and I'm outside the scene, pressed against the boundary like a face to glass.

The image holds.

It's nighttime. The brown rectangle of Barrow Heights is dark except for one window on the ground floor, where a yellow scribble of light flickers. Below it, a stick-figure man descends the front steps. He's drawn larger than the building's entrance, his proportions wrong in that way only a child's hand can make them. Head too big. Arms too short. Legs represented as straight lines that end in L-shaped shoes. 

Behind him, almost hidden by the oversized front door, a smaller figure watches.

Pink triangle dress. Circle eyes wide with curiosity.

Florence.

She follows him down the crayon street. Past rectangles that are meant to be parked cars. Past a smudge of orange that might be a streetlamp. Into an alley where a woman waits, her cigarette a single hot dot. Green rectangles change hands. Money.

Florence stops at the alley's mouth. Her thought bubble appears in shaky, misspelled letters:

*Whar do yu go?*

It holds there. The animation freezes. Father, daughter, and the woman between them, all locked in a single panel that a six-year-old drew because she didn't have the words for what she was seeing. And I know whatever happens next can’t be good. 

I try to reach for her on instinct. My arm doesn't respond. I'm a passenger here.

All I can do is watch.

The drawing holds. And holds.

Then, in the scribbled shadows beyond the streetlight, something moves. A shape. It rises, tall and hunched and scarecrow thin, ears hanging past its patchy shoulders. 

It wasn't in this drawing before.

It's in it now.

A word scrawls itself above the shape, letter by letter, in handwriting that isn't Florence's. Then the word multiplies until it’s stamped across the sky, across the buildings, across Florence herself—until the alley is gone and the panel is nothing but black crayon and letters echoing into the dark.

MINE

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

The stairwell hits me like a slap.

I'm on my knees, one hand white-knuckled around the railing, the other crushing Florence's drawing against my chest. My lungs are heaving with deep, ragged pulls of air that taste like mold and old paint and the real, three-dimensional rot of Barrow Heights.

The paper is crumpled in my fist. Just a drawing. Just crayon on paper.

But my hands won't stop shaking.

*'You don't understand.'*

The voice drifts down from above. I look up, still gripping the railing, and there she is — leaning over the banister from several floors up. A woman.

Agnes Miller.

She looks like death. Her ashen hair hangs in thin, brittle strands. Her face is sunken, skin pulled tight over jagged cheekbones. An IV stand rattles beside her, two clear tubes snaking into her nostrils, feeding her oxygen in soft gasps while her legs tremble with the effort of standing.

She looks every bit her age. 

*'It's his fault I lost my sister,'* she rasps, voice wet and labored. *'My worthless father. He took her. But I'll get her back. I'm close, you see, closer than I’ve ever been.’*

I release the drawing, let it flutter to the stairs. ‘What are you talking about?’

*‘The deal. The deal I made. This is the last cycle. The Tall Dog get the Copeland boy, and Barrow Heights is freed. Freed! Florence finally comes home and—’*

‘Your sister's dead, Agnes. She’s been dead for a century.'

*‘No…’* Agnes's nostrils flare. ‘*I spoke with her not two days ago. And besides, I’ve given the monster too many lives, paid too much in my tenants’ blood for it to deny me now.’*

‘And if it still does?’ I ask. ‘If it still asks for more?’

Agnes's face twists with grief and rage and something worse than madness. Her hands shake on the railing, IV tubes swaying. Her mouth opens. Then shuts. It’s like she’s trying to answer a question she never once considered. 

‘Tell me how to access the basement,’ I say, my voice level. ‘I’ll get your sister back. I’ll kill the Tall Dog. It’s what I do.’

She laughs, but it’s a weak, wheezing affair. 

*‘You can’t kill it.'* She pushes off the railing, shuffling backward into the shadows of the 10th floor. *'Nothing can. All we can do is barter with it. Buy a little more time. Spare a few more lives… before the next child comes due.'*

Her voice echoes down the stairwell as she retreats. I surge after her, taking the steps three at a time, boots slamming against warped treads. But the geometry is working against me. 

*'You've seen the boy's Bad Ending, I'm sure,’* she calls from somewhere distant. ‘*Don't you wonder about yours, Mr. Jhune?'*

I pause on the next landing, catching my breath. I’m not as young as I used to be. That’s when it catches my eye, movement outside the vertical slash of a window. There’s a man outside. He’s kneeling in the alley below, head bowed, shoulders slumped in the moonlight. 

A hiss cuts through the air. His shoulder jerks backward. Once. Twice. He's being shot, over and over, silenced rounds punching through him with clinical precision.

*Thwip. Thwip. Thwip.*

He crumples, blood running between his fingers. Then falls backward into the spreading pool of red, motionless, his face gazing blankly at the sky. 

That face.

It belongs to me. 

I give a skeptical shake of my head, pulling back from the window. If this is my Bad Ending, it doesn’t make a lick of sense. 

I’ve never met a cosmic horror that needed a gun to kill. Something isn’t adding, but I don’t have time to connect the dots. I’ve got a landlady to catch. 

I throw myself up the remaining flights, lungs burning, legs screaming. By the time I hit the 10th floor, I'm drenched in sweat and gasping for air.

The hallway stretches like a fever dream.

It extends a kilometer, maybe more, perspective warping at the far end where the rusted steel jaws of the elevator shaft gape open like a waiting mouth. To my left, a woman screams in apartment 10-A.

*'It's your fault! You made me do this! If you'd just* listened—!'

The hallway throbs as I charge forward, doors rippling past me—10-B, 10-C, 10-D—each one leaking sounds of domestic horror. Then I find what I’m looking for. 

Apartment 10-R.

The door sits ajar, smoke curling into the corridor in long, lazy tendrils. I push it open with my food. The space inside is a hoarder's wet dream.

Newspapers are stacked to the ceiling. Boxes overflow with yellowed photographs and moth-eaten clothing. Narrow pathways have been carved through the debris like game trails through a jungle, and there, at the center of it all, seated behind a desk buried in medical equipment—

‘Agnes,’ I say coldly. 

She's surrounded by machines that beep and hiss and click, keeping her tethered to life through sheer mechanical stubbornness. IV drips. An oxygen concentrator. She doesn't look up at me. Just sits there, staring at something on the desk I can't see.

*'You're persistent,'* she croaks. *'I'll give you that, Mr. Jhune.'*

My pocket watch throbs against my ribs. My fist closes around it, the message vibrating directly into my skull. 

**ENTITY DETECTED**

**CLASS 1: WHISPER**

A smirk tugs at my lips. That’s barely more than a mean ghost. 

'It's checkmate, Agnes.' I step into the apartment, hand resting on my revolver. 'One way or another, you’re going to tell me how to access that basement.’ 

She reaches for something on the desk: a cigarette, ember glowing in the dimness. She brings it to her throat. Sucks in through the tracheostomy scar with a weak wheeze. 

Smoke pours from her mouth.

*'So arrogant,'* she whispers. ‘*Just like father was.’*

*‘He loved threatening people, too, you know?’* Another drag. *‘My mother, mostly. But when she’d put up a fight, he’d take it out on the whores instead.’* The cigarette's ember flares brighter, turning deep crimson, like a coal pulled fresh from Hell's furnace. *‘You remind me of him. Pathetic. Annoying.’*

**ENTITY PROMOTED TO—**

‘*My father…*’

Agnes is rasping. Agony traces her every word. 

‘*My father died before he could be punished, you know. Before I could give him a taste of the suffering Florence endured.’* Her mouth falls open. Her teeth. They’re moving inside her gums, squirming like starving maggots. *‘I see his same cruelty in you. His same failure.’*

My back up comes against the wall. 

An uneven smile splits her lips. ‘*Your screams should sate me just as well.’*

My hands. They’re shaking. 

How long has it been since they shook like this? Years. Not since the first time I died. Not since the first time I stepped out of a coffin and into a life I wasn’t built to survive.

COSMIC DISTORTION DETECTED

ENTITY MERGING WITH ENVIRONMENT

Kent doesn't miss a beat. *'Sounds like Ellison’s condo.'*

The squad chuckles. 

I try not to throw up. 

Alvarez nudges my shoulder. *'You good, Juno?'*

Let’s see—I’m spiraling, my heart’s hammering my ribs, my breathing’s gone shallow, my thoughts racing in circles I can't control. I’m thinking about Abby back at home waiting for me to walk through the door with her birthday cake, but I'm here instead, on my first mission as a Pale, about to breach a house that killed an Inquisitor 1st Class.

An *Inquisitor.*

The best of the best.

Dead.

‘Yeah,’ I say, voice cracking. ‘Never better.’

The house looms larger with every step.

It *twists* as we approach, the boards warping, angles shifting in ways that make my eyes hurt. The structure creaks and groans like it’s breathing. The windows tilt, panes catching the moonlight almost like it’s following our movement.

We fan out into position: Ellison and Kent peeling off toward the back entrance, Alvarez and I moving left toward a ground-floor window, Stevenson and Shirley taking the front door.

Kent's voice filters through the armor's psychic link:

*'Careful. It’s watching us.'*

Shirley snorts. *'Figures a haunted house would be a peeping tom.'*

Scattered laughter crackles through the team. 

*'Save the stand-up for Jhune’s funeral,'* Stevenson cuts in, voice flat and cold. *'We've got work to do. Alvarez, Jhune—first floor sweep. Ellison, Kent—clear the top floor. Shirley and I take the second, provide support if either team needs extraction. Priority is Torsen’s Red Book. Pocketwatch is a bonus. Leave the body.'*

*'Roger that,'* Ellison confirms.

He and Kent surge forward, disappearing around the side of the house in perfect sync.

Alvarez and I move toward our window.

Stevenson and Shirley are already at the front door, stacking up for breach. We pass them, boots crunching on dry dirt, and Alvarez presses his back against the outer wall beside the window frame. I mirror him on the opposite side.

My rifle is shaking in my hands.

Alvarez looks completely relaxed.

*'Deep breaths, Juno,'* he says quietly. *'They help.'*

'Really?' 

He shrugs, then smashes the window. Glass explodes inward, tinkling across hardwood floors inside. *'Dunno, but that's what the manual says.'*

I can't see his face behind the white mask, but I can hear the grin in his voice.

*'Hey—do me a favor. If something kills me in there, kill it back, would ya?'*

Before I can respond, he vaults through the window.

My heart *stops*. For three seconds there's nothing but silence and the hammering of my pulse in my ears.

Then: *'All clear, Juno.'*

I exhale hard and follow him through, landing in a crouch on broken glass.

Alvarez is already moving deeper into the house, and the way he moves is like watching a ghost. Soundless. Fluid. His boots barely touch the floor, while mine creak and groan with every step like I'm stomping through a minefield.

We pass through a den. A library. A dining room.

Everything looks normal.

No overturned furniture. No blood spatters. Not even a crooked picture frame on the wall.

It’s making my skin crawl. 

*'Eerie, isn't it?'* Alvarez mutters, sweeping his rifle across the empty dining table. *'Almost too quiet.'*

I open my mouth to respond—

Another voice cuts through the comm. Ellison.

*'Bishop—it’s Bravo Team. We've found Torsen. Third floor, end of the hall. Private study. Door's open.'*

Stevenson’s voice crackles through. *‘Hold position, Bravo. Shirley and I are en route for backup.'* Stevenson's tone sharpens. *'Charlie squad—cover the first-floor exit. Things are quiet, but don't let that fool you. And Ellison—'*

A beat.

*'—grab that Red Book.'*

Silence.

*‘I’m not sure that’s necessary.'*

*'Why the fuck not?'*

*'Because…’* Ellison voice wavers, confused, unsettled. ‘*Because* *Torsen isn't dead.'*

My blood runs cold.

*‘What do you mean she isn’t—’*

The scream that erupts through the comm isn't human. It's *piercing*, a shriek that carves straight into my brain, vibrating through my skull like a lobotomy drill. I collapse, hands flying to my head, but the sound is inside me, inescapable.

My armor writhes.

It peels back from my face, retreating down my neck, bubbling and shuddering like it's in agony. Beside me, Alvarez drops to one knee, his armor doing the same—recoiling, afraid.

The scream cuts off.

Silence rushes back in, thick and suffocating.

*'Jesus—'* I gasp, ears still ringing. *'What the hell was that?'*

Alvarez doesn't answer.

I look over.

He's staring up at the ceiling—at the direction of the third floor—with an expression I've never seen on his face before. His armor crawls back up his neck to cover his face. He bolts for the stairs. 

*'Alvarez—'* I scramble to my feet. 

*'The armor hasn't reacted like that since Kilton,'* he calls back without stopping. *'Stay. Here.'*

Kilton.

The Pale I replaced.

The one who got torn apart by a Class 4 entity.

The one whose coffin I'm using.

*'Are you saying—'* I start, but the words die in my throat.

Because I already know the answer. 

Ellison is dead.

And we’re next.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

I hit the bottom of the elevator shaft like a meteor strike.

The impact detonates the concrete, a shockwave of dust and debris exploding outward. Pain erupts along my spine, white-hot and blinding, and for several seconds I can't breathe, can't move, can't do anything but lie there gasping like a fish on dry land.

My lungs finally unlock.

I drag in a ragged breath, then another, world spinning as I force myself upright.

My hand goes to my neck instinctively checking for damage, and I feel it there:

My armor.

Hardened into a rigid shell across the back of my skull and down my spine, a protective exoskeleton that absorbed the impact and kept me from turning into human mulch.

It shudders at my touch.

Trembling. Exhausted.

'Thank you,' I whisper.

The armor bubbles in response, then slowly recedes, crawling back down my neck and disappearing beneath my collar. Back into hiding. Back into waiting. I stand, legs shaking, and tug at my jacket. Dust cascades off the fabric in gray clouds.

I cough, eyes finding my worst nightmare. 

It’s lying on the ground a few feet away, barrel bent at a sick angle, a fissure running through the steel. My revolver. Must've landed on it during the fall. 

Shit.

And I still had another bullet left.

Now it’s useless. 

I shove the gun into my jacket with a bitter sigh, arming myself instead with my flashlight. A dark, particulate cloud shifts and swirls in the beam of my light, stirred by air currents. I crouch down, scoop some up between two fingers.

Bring it to my nose.

It smells acrid. Burnt. Like the aftermath of a fire.

'Ashes,' I murmur. 'The elevator shaft is full of ashes.'

A low growl echoes from my right.

I wheel around, flashlight cutting through the dark.

There; a narrow brick corridor leading out from the base of the shaft. Ancient. Crumbling. The kind of passage that shouldn't exist according to any architectural blueprint. And at the far end, barely visible in the distance is an angry, red ember. 

*Here, Inquisitor…* 

The voice skitters like insects across my eardrums, each syllable wrong and broken and stitched together. My pocket watch convulses against my chest, violent and urgent.

*Come and get it.*
