I've been trying to put this into words for weeks now. I type something out, read it back, delete it, start over. Because part of me knows that once I post this, it's real. It's not just something that loops in my head at 3 AM anymore. It's something that happened.

My name is Marcel Dean. I'm thirty-one. Up until September of last year, I was a reporter at the Pioneer Press in St. Paul, Minnesota. I say "was" because I wrote an investigative piece about officers in the Ramsey County sheriff's department burying evidence in an assault case. The piece was solid. Every word checked out. But the paper wasn't interested in solid — they were interested in safe. My editor, Glenn Marsh, a man who looks like a thumb and sweats through his shirt whenever someone says the word "lawyer," called me into his office on a rainy Tuesday and told me the paper was "moving in a different direction" and that I was no longer part of that direction. He said this while looking at his desk. Not at me. At his desk. They gave me two weeks. I cleared out my things in a cardboard box and walked to the door and the security guard — a guy named Earl who I'd maybe said ten words to in three years — said "good luck, Marcel" while staring at my shoes.

Nobody else said anything.

A week later, my grandfather died.

Henry Dean. Eighty-one years old. Heart attack in his sleep. Alone. In his house in a town called Grinnell, up in Pine County, northern Minnesota. The doctor who signed the death certificate — Dr. Lyle Engström, a seventy-something-year-old who serves three towns and drives forty miles a day to do it — said it was quick. Said he didn't suffer. That's what they always say and I don't know if anyone actually believes it.

The funeral was on a Thursday. Late October. Cold. I was there. Pastor Jim Tallqvist was there — old guy, yellowed fingers, been running the Lutheran church solo since '74 apparently. And a woman from the township office named Donna Przbylski — she pronounced it for me three times and I still couldn't get it right — who said she was "administratively required to attend." Not required to pay respects. Not required to offer condolences. Required to attend. Three people in a small cemetery behind a white church with chipping paint, burying a man who lived eighty-one years and couldn't find more than that to see him off.

My dad didn't come. I didn't expect him to. Him and my grandfather hadn't spoken since 1979 — the year my dad married my mom. My mom is Black. My grandfather was a man from a different generation in Minnesota. I'll leave it at that.

My brother Kevin texted me: "send me a pic of the grave." I didn't send him a pic.

My mom called me that night and said: "God rest his soul. Don't forget to ask about the papers." The deed. She meant the deed to the house. Because Henry Dean, despite everything, left the house to me. Not to my dad. To me.

I still don't know why.

---

Grinnell isn't really on the map.

I mean — it exists. You can find it if you look. A dot between Sturgeon Lake and a pine forest that stretches north until it hits nothing. Seven hundred people according to the 1990 census. One main street with a grocery store called Dale's, an Amoco gas station, a post office, and a bar called The Walleye with a busted neon sign that only lights up "The Wall." Two churches — Lutheran and Catholic. An elementary school. A public library the size of a bedroom. That's it. After that it's forest and dirt roads and farms spaced far apart and quiet.

I got there two days after the funeral. Four and a half hours from St. Paul in a rented Honda Civic with 120,000 miles on it and a ticking sound in the engine that started after Hinckley and never stopped. Past the last real town, the highway shrinks to two narrow lanes between walls of pine. No radio stations. Almost no cars. Just me and the road and a low gray sky that looked like it was about to collapse.

My grandfather's house sat at the end of a dirt road that branches off County Road 7 and runs half a mile through the trees. One-story. Wood frame. White paint peeled halfway off, showing gray timber underneath. A narrow front porch with two crooked steps. Black shingle roof buried under a layer of pine needles. Small windows with the curtains drawn.

I parked and got out. The air up here is different — cold and damp and it smells like wet soil and timber. No sounds except wind moving through the pines and a bird somewhere far off.

Then I heard something on the porch. A small movement. I looked.

The dog.

He was sitting by the front door. Medium-sized — thirty, thirty-five pounds maybe. Brown-gray fur, thick, with small tangles around the neck. Ears up. Face somewhere between a German shepherd and something else I couldn't place. Brown eyes. He looked at me when I got out of the car and didn't bark.

Donna Przbylski had mentioned him at the township office when she handed me the key. "Dog's name is Buddy. Henry fed him, let him in and out. Quiet dog. Doesn't cause problems." She said it like she was reading off a checklist and wanted to get to the next item.

"Hey, Buddy."

He looked at me. Didn't move. Didn't wag his tail. Didn't come over. Just sat there and looked. After a few seconds I gave up and unlocked the door and went inside.

The inside was what I expected. Old heavy furniture — a brown couch worn thin at the arms, a small dining table with a plastic floral tablecloth that had faded to almost nothing, a Zenith TV with a curved screen and a framed photo on top of a man and woman I knew were my grandparents but who looked young in a way that felt wrong. Small kitchen — a white fridge humming a sick hum, a four-burner gas stove with one dead burner, a sink with rust stains. One bedroom with a double bed made up tight — nobody had touched it since he died. A narrow bathroom with a mirror tilted slightly to the left. And a short hallway connecting the living room to the bedroom.

I'll come back to that hallway.

The place smelled old. That mix of damp wood and unwashed fabric and settled dust. Normal. Just a house that's been closed up.

I was carrying in my second bag when I realized Buddy was inside.

I didn't leave the door open. Or maybe I did — honestly I don't remember. But he was there, sitting in the living room near the couch, watching me with those quiet eyes.

"Guess you let yourself in."

Nothing.

"Sure. Make yourself at home."

I let him stay. He wasn't bothering me. And honestly, having another living thing in that house felt better than I expected. I fed him from a can of beans I found in the cabinet — I didn't have dog food — and he ate slowly, quietly, then went back to his spot by the couch.

That first night I slept in the bedroom. The bed was more comfortable than I thought it'd be — old mattress but thick, and the pillow smelled faintly of my grandfather's soap. Ivory, maybe. I fell asleep fast.

---

I woke up at 4:30 AM.

No sound. No nightmare. Just — awake. That thing where your eyes open and you're fully conscious before there's any reason to be. I looked at the bedroom door. It was open. I hadn't left it open. Or maybe I had. New house, tired, who keeps track.

Buddy was in the hallway. Sitting. Facing the bedroom.

"Buddy. Go to sleep."

He stood up and walked away. I heard his nails click on the wood and then nothing. I went back to sleep.

---

The first few days in Grinnell were just me trying to function like a normal person who moved to a new place.

I went to Dale's and bought supplies — milk, bread, eggs, a can of Folgers, and a bag of Purina because I felt guilty about the beans. The man behind the counter was Dale himself — Dale Koskinen, sixties, tall and thin with a long face and thick glasses. He looked at me when I walked in with that look I'd get used to.

It wasn't hostile. I don't want to exaggerate. But when you're the only brown face in a town of seven hundred white people in northern Minnesota in 1991, you learn to read looks fast. It was a calculating look. Sizing me up. Putting me in a box.

"You're the one who inherited Henry's place."

It wasn't a question.

"Yeah. His grandson. Marcel."

"Welcome, Marcel. Henry used to come in every Friday. Bought the same things every time. Coffee, bread, and a can of Campbell's tomato soup." Pause. "Quiet man."

"Yeah. He was quiet."

"You look like him."

I didn't know what to do with that. My grandfather was a white man with white hair and blue eyes. I'm not white. But Dale said it and moved on like it was a comment about the weather.

I paid and left. On my way to the car I passed two men standing by a blue Ford pickup. One of them — short, stocky, red beard — was talking loud about something, then saw me and stopped. The other one — taller, wearing an orange hunting cap — looked at me, then looked at his buddy, and they went back to talking quieter.

That's what Grinnell was for me. Clipped smiles and conversations that got quieter when I walked by and polite questions that didn't really ask anything. "Minnesota Nice," they call it. I have another name for it but that's not the story I'm telling right now.

---

Buddy in those first days was just a dog.

I need you to understand that. There was nothing wrong. He ate from the bowl I put out twice a day. He went outside in the mornings and disappeared for hours — roaming the woods, I figured — then came back before dark and sat on the porch until I opened the door. He slept in the living room by the couch. Never barked. Not once. Quiet in a noticeable way, but I figured that was his temperament. Some dogs don't bark much. My grandfather was quiet and the dog picked up his owner's energy. That's what I told myself.

I talked to him sometimes. Loneliness does that — you start talking to anything that breathes.

"Long day, Buddy."

He'd lift his head and look at me.

"Yeah. Me too."

He'd put his head back down on the floor.

Normal. Ordinary. I need you to remember this because what came after needs you to know what the beginning looked like.

---

About a week after I arrived, I started hearing about it.

I was at The Walleye on a Saturday night. I hadn't planned on going but the walls of the house were starting to close in and my head was full of thoughts about my dead career and my shrinking bank account and a future with nothing clear in it. I walked in and sat in the corner and ordered a beer from the bartender — a woman in her thirties named Cheryl Moberg, blonde ponytail, tired eyes, small feather tattoo on her wrist.

"You're the new guy. Henry's grandson."

Everyone knows here. Seven hundred people. News moves faster than wind.

"Yeah."

"Sorry for your loss."

"Thanks."

She set the beer down and walked off. Then came back a couple minutes later. "You hear about what happened out at the Holloway place?"

"No. What happened?"

She glanced behind her. The bar was half full — ten, fifteen people, mostly men in work clothes, a few women. A group playing pool in the back. Normal Saturday night in a small town.

"Karen Holloway. Lives out on County Road 7 — same road as your place, actually, about two and a half miles further down. Been living alone since her divorce from Bill. Anyway — she's gone."

"Gone?"

"Two weeks now. Nobody's seen her. Sheriff Mike Dalquist went out to check and found nothing. Door open. Car in the driveway. She's not there." She looked at me. "Then they found her clothes on the road. About a mile from her house. Torn up."

"Animal attack?"

"That's what they're saying. Wolves or bears or whatever." She paused. "But they didn't find — they didn't find anything else. You understand? No body. No blood. Nothing. Just the clothes."

A voice from the other end of the bar — a big man in a red and black flannel sitting on a stool, name turned out to be \*\*Tom Gunderson\*\*, works in logging, voice like an empty barrel.

"Cheryl. Quit scaring people."

"I'm not scaring anyone, Tom. I'm telling what happened."

"Nothing happened. Woman left. People leave. Not everyone who leaves has a story behind it."

"Leaves and abandons her car and her clothes are torn up on the road."

Tom drank from his glass and didn't answer. Cheryl looked at me, shrugged, and went to serve someone else.

I didn't finish my beer. But the thing in my head — that reporter part — woke up.

---

Next day I went to the town library.

One room in an old shared building next to what used to be a community center — half of it's locked up now. The librarian was \*\*Ruth Lindgren\*\* — maybe sixty-five, short white hair, round face with real kindness in it. First person in Grinnell who looked at me without doing math behind their eyes.

"You're Henry's grandson."

"Yes ma'am."

"I'm sorry about him. He came in every Tuesday. Borrowed books about World War Two. A new one every week. Read everything I had on the subject." She smiled sadly. "Last time he came was a week before he passed. He looked tired but he didn't complain."

"Did you know Karen Holloway?"

Something shifted in her face. Not fear. Sadness, maybe. Worry.

"Karen came every week too. Always borrowed two books — a novel and something about gardening. Sweet woman. Quiet." She paused. "Last time she came in was about a week before she disappeared. Only borrowed one book. And she was different."

"Different how?"

"Jumpy. Kept looking at the door. I asked if she was alright and she said yes but..." Another pause. She looked out the window. "She said she'd been hearing animals around her house at night. Said dogs. Dogs coming around at night, walking around the house."

"Stray dogs?"

"There aren't many strays around here, Marcel. And her nearest neighbor is two miles out. Whose dog is walking two miles in the dark to circle her house?"

I didn't have an answer.

"Anyway—" Ruth opened a ledger in front of her. "If you want to borrow anything, you're welcome to. I can set you up with a card."

I understood she was done with the subject. I got a card, borrowed a book I never read, and left.

---

On my way back I stopped at the sheriff's office. Small brick building between the post office and a closed-down auto repair shop. Walked in. Deputy \*\*Linda Cowell\*\* was behind a desk covered in papers, a coffee mug, and a police radio crackling softly.

"Afternoon. Can I help you?"

"Marcel Dean. Henry's grandson. I heard about Karen Holloway and I have a background in journalism so I thought—"

"Journalist." Her look changed. Not the usual Grinnell look — this was someone closing a door.

"Former."

"Mr. Dean, the case is open and we don't comment on active cases. Sheriff Dalquist is available Monday if you have something official. Otherwise — thanks for your concern."

She smiled. Minnesota smile. Door closed.

I left.

---

That night I started writing.

I bought a yellow legal pad from Dale's and sat at the kitchen table and wrote down everything I'd heard. Karen Holloway. County Road 7. Torn clothes. No body. No blood. Dogs circling her house at night. I wrote questions in the margins: Who saw her last? Did she report the dogs to anyone official? Where's her ex-husband Bill?

I was writing not because I planned to publish anything — I had nowhere to publish — but because this is what I know how to do. It's the only thing I'm good at. Ask. Record. Connect.

It was about ten at night. Buddy was lying by the couch in the living room. Quiet. Normal.

Then we heard it.

Footsteps outside. On the dry leaves around the house. Slow. Heavy.

I looked at Buddy.

He had lifted his head. Ears forward. Looking at the back door — the kitchen door that leads to the woods. He didn't bark. Just looked, his body slightly tense.

"Buddy?"

The footsteps stopped.

I waited. One second. Two. Five.

Then they started again. But further away this time. Moving off. Until they were gone.

Buddy relaxed and put his head back down.

Deer, I figured. The woods are full of deer up here — I'd seen three on the road my first day. A deer wandering around the house at night. Normal.

I went back to writing.

---

Next morning I found something on the porch.

A field mouse. Dead. Small. Placed by the door.

I looked at Buddy. He was sitting in his usual corner of the porch.

"This from you, Buddy? Brought me a present?"

He looked at me. Didn't move.

I smiled. Picked up the mouse with a paper towel, tossed it into the trees, washed my hands, and forgot about it.

Dogs do this. They hunt and bring things back to their owner. Normal.

---

That same week I decided to drive out to Karen Holloway's farm.

I didn't go inside — the yellow police tape was still strung across the driveway. But I stood on the road and looked. Small white house. A red barn that had lost half its paint. A garden on the side with dead summer crops — wilted tomatoes and dried-up bean poles. Her car — a brown Chevy pickup — still sitting in the driveway. Everything looked normal except that the place was empty in a way you feel in your chest. That kind of empty that comes when someone leaves a place fast.

I noticed one thing: the ground around the house — especially under the windows — was covered in tracks. Lots of them. Animal prints. More than one animal. Circular patterns — like something had been walking around the house over and over. I couldn't tell the species from where I was standing but they were bigger than coyote and smaller than bear.

I wrote it all down in my pad and drove back.

---

Week two. The gifts kept coming.

After the mouse came a sparrow. Then a rabbit. Every day or every other day, something new on the porch. All dead. All placed the same way — on their side, right by the door. And all clean. No bite marks. No blood. This didn't bother me at first — I figured he was breaking their necks with his jaw. Quick and clean. But the rabbit made me think. The rabbit was completely intact. Fur clean. No wound, no break, no mark of any kind. Just dead.

"Buddy, how do you do this?"

He looked at me and tilted his head. That thing he does — tilts his head slowly. Cute. Makes you think he's trying to understand you.

I smiled and tossed the rabbit and went inside.

---

That same week something happened that changed how I looked at Buddy.

I was sitting on the porch one afternoon reading — not the library book, the yellow pad, going over my notes — when a car came down the dirt road. An old white Buick. It stopped and a man got out. Medium height. Early forties. Thinning brown hair. Red face — from sun or booze or both. Carhartt jacket, jeans, work boots.

He walked toward the porch. "Hey. You the one living here now?"

"Yeah. Marcel."

"\*\*Bill Holloway\*\*."

The name stopped me. Karen's ex-husband.

"Sorry about Henry. He was my neighbor, kind of. His place is the closest house to Karen's." He looked around. "I came because I heard you've been asking about Karen around town."

"Yeah. I'm a journalist — former — and I—"

"I know. Cheryl told me." He looked at the porch, then at the door, then at me. "I want to tell you something but—"

Buddy came out of the house.

I didn't hear him coming. Didn't hear his nails on the wood. He just appeared — in the doorway — and looked at Bill Holloway.

Bill took a step back.

"That dog. That's Henry's dog?"

"Yeah. Buddy. He's harmless—"

Buddy came down the two steps. Slowly. Didn't bark. Didn't growl. Just walked toward Bill in measured steps — one, two, three — and stood between him and the porch. Looking at him.

Bill looked at Buddy, then at me. "Man, that dog—"

"Buddy. Come here, boy. Buddy."

He didn't move.


Bill took another step back. "Look — what I wanted to say — Karen was calling me before she disappeared. Twice. First time she said there were animals coming around her house at night. I told her to put out rat poison or call Fish and Wildlife. Second time..." He looked at Buddy again. "Second time she said something weird. She said: 'Something stands at my window at night and watches me.' I asked her what she meant and she said: 'I don't know. It just stands there and watches. It doesn't move.' Three days later she was gone."

Silence.

"Did you tell the police?"

"Mike Dalquist doesn't listen to anybody. Said it was animals. Period." He looked at Buddy one last time. "Anyway — take care of yourself, Marcel. The woods around here... there's things people don't like to talk about."

He got in his car and drove off.

I looked at Buddy. He was still standing in the same spot. Looking at the road where the car had disappeared. Then he turned and looked at me. And walked slowly back up the steps and into the house.


That night I wrote in the pad:

"Something stands at the window at night and watches. Doesn't move."

And underneath:

"Dogs. But not dogs."

Then I went to bed. Or tried.

At three in the morning I opened my eyes. No reason. Just opened them. I looked at the bedroom window.

The curtain was cracked open slightly. And outside it was dark — that rural dark that doesn't look like city dark. No reference points. No light. Nothing.

But as I lay there looking, I felt something. Not a sound. Not a movement. Just a feeling. That feeling that makes the hair on your arms stand up for no clear reason. The feeling of being watched.

I got up and closed the curtain.

In the morning I went outside and looked at the ground under the window.

Tracks. Same kind I'd seen at the Holloway place. Circular. Bigger than coyote. Smaller than bear. Lots of them.

And among them — one that was different. One print. Bigger than the rest. Much bigger.

It didn't belong to any animal I knew.

Buddy was sitting on the porch. Watching me look at the tracks.

He wagged his tail. First time since I'd arrived — he wagged his tail.

And for the person who asked about Buddy — I'll say this once. If you ever see a quiet dog that doesn't bark, that just sits and watches you, that makes you feel like it's known you forever — don't trust it. Don't feed it. And don't let it in your house.
