I BURIED MY BROTHER IN THE WOODS. HE WON’T STOP TALKING

The snow in Novgorod province lay crust-deep and blue under the moon, and it creaked like old bones when Pyotr walked. 

He dragged the sledge behind him, rope burning his palms through the mittens. Inside the sledge, wrapped in burlap, was his brother Aleksei. Dead three days. The frost had kept him stiff and clean. 

“Just to the treeline, Pyotr,” his wife had said that morning, her breath fogging the low doorway. 

“Father Grigory won’t bury him in the churchyard. Not after what he said about the Tsar.”  

“Father Grigory won’t bury anyone who freezes,” Pyotr had answered. “The ground’s iron. We wait for spring.”  

“Then the wolves take him. Or worse.” She crossed herself, twice, quickly. “The woods at least will hide him.”

The forest swallowed sound. Pine needles held the cold, and when he exhaled it hung in front of his face, thick as milk, before tearing away into the dark. 

The only other noise was the sledge runner scraping, and somewhere far off, a branch snapping under weight that wasn’t wind. 

He stopped at the first large birch and started digging with the hatchet. The topsoil gave way, then the earth turned to stone. 

Each strike rang up his arms and into his teeth. Sweat froze in his eyebrows. After an hour he had a shallow trough, maybe two hands deep. Not enough. 

“Brother,” he said, because the silence was worse than the cold, “you always said God was a rich man’s lie. Maybe you were right.” 

The burlap didn’t move. But the air changed. It got thicker, wet, like the moment before a thunderstorm, except the sky was clear and full of stars so sharp they hurt to look at. Pyotr smelled copper and old meat, though Aleksei had died of fever, not violence. He looked over his shoulder. 

Nothing. Just trees, black and endless, and his own breath rising. 

He bent to lift the body. That’s when he heard it. A voice, right against his ear, low and amused, using Aleksei’s mouth but not Aleksei’s words. 

“You dig so hard, Pyotr. For what? The worms are sleeping. The church is empty. Even the wolves have gone south.”

Pyotr dropped the burlap. It hit the snow with a soft thud. The shape inside didn’t move, but the voice kept talking, unmuffled by the cloth. 

“Put me back in the house. Let Mother see me at the table. She still sets a bowl for me. She thinks I’m late from the mill. She’ll keep thinking it if you let her. Isn’t that kinder than truth?”

The cold was in his lungs now, in his blood. Pyotr grabbed the hatchet again, not to dig. “You’re not him.”

“No,” the voice agreed, and the burlap shifted, though no wind blew. “I’m what’s left when the prayers stop. I’m what lives in the space between your brother’s teeth and the sky. I’m what you’ll hear when you’re old and alone and the fire goes out.”

Pyotr swung. The blade bit burlap, then flesh, then wood, then earth. He didn’t stop until his arms gave out and the sledge was kindling and the body was pieces. The voice laughed the whole time, never louder, never softer, just there, like a second heartbeat.

When he finished, the forest was quiet again. The copper smell was gone. The stars were the same. He stood, spat blood from where he’d bitten his tongue, and looked at the mess he’d made. 

He understood then. There was no Aleksei. There had never been Father Grigory, not really. There was only winter, and the need to put things in the ground, and the sound a man makes when he realizes the ground doesn’t care, God doesn’t care, and the voice he hears is his own, waiting for him to finally listen.

He left the hatchet. He walked home without the sledge. His wife was asleep. He didn’t wake her. He sat by the dead hearth and watched his breath until the sun came up, and it didn’t warm anything.
