I downloaded a sleep recording app for the first time and I wish I could take it back.

I’d been having trouble sleeping for the past few weeks. In the beginning, I didn’t think much of it. I’d blink myself blearily awake to darkness and then slip into someplace darker several seconds later.

Until it started becoming more and more difficult to fall back asleep. Each time I surfaced, I would strain for that familiar dark serenity only for it to slink farther away. Gradually, I started being roused in the middle of the night by the skin-crawling sensation of being watched, which made no sense considering I lived alone.

My apartment was small enough that I could see nearly all of it from my bed. Kitchenette against the wall. Tiny dining table beside the window. Narrow hallway leading toward the bathroom, closet, and front door.

Nothing ever looked out of place, despite the nights that I would wake abruptly with the overwhelming certainty that there was a presence just beyond the bedroom doorway. Shadows started forming shape when I stared too long into the hallway, so I stopped.

But the feeling that I wasn’t alone didn’t.

During nights like those, I could only resort to staring at the back of my eyelids, listening to the droning hum of my refrigerator until the chittering of the birds perched outside my window signalled that it was time to get up for work.

They started sounding like they were laughing at me.

Honestly, the worst part wasn’t even the exhaustion. It was the humiliation of functioning only semi-aware all the time, like I was waddling through a brain fog.

At work, I kept forgetting conversations.

A colleague frowned at me as we loitered near the coffee machine after our weekly meeting. “You alright?”

“…Yeah? Why?”

“You already asked me that ten minutes ago. Like, two times.”

I laughed it off. It probably would’ve been even more unprofessional to add that I had absolutely no memory of even speaking to him earlier.

Another time, my manager thanked me for volunteering to stay late on Thursday. Apparently I had insisted, but I don’t remember any of that either. Surely I wasn’t that masochistic.

"Could be the stress,” my friend Flavie suggested when I brought it up during dinner.

We were slurping phở inside a Vietnamese restaurant which we’d booked a few days prior. Well, she was, at least, and happily at that. I’d been excited to come to Belleville after so long, except now I could only summon enough strength to stare unseeingly into my bowl.

My distorted yet visibly weary face rippled across the soup, peering back at me with shadowed eyes through stalks of wilting beansprouts.

“Maybe,” Flavie continued to mumble through her mouthful, propping her chopsticks on the edge of her bowl as she leaned back in her chair and turned her full attention to me, “you should take a few days off of work.”

“Can’t,” I sighed. “I already took enough breaks in May.” One of the greatest advantages that came with working in France, truly.

“Don’t you have those melatonin gummies?”

“Does it look to you like they’re working?”

“Well, no—so take more?”

I grimaced. “That’s not how it works—also I always end up with the weirdest dreams when I do that, and I feel nauseous waking up.”

Speaking of which, I couldn’t even recall a single detail from any recent dreams I’d potentially had.

"Remember Théo? From sociology?” She ignored my blank stare and carried on, “Anyway, he mentioned before that he started using this sleep recording app because he would wake up with this sharp pain in his jaw or something like that, so he figured that there was something going on in his sleep. Turns out he was grinding his teeth and going at it real hard. He couldn’t believe what he was hearing. Apparently he sounded like a goat choking on marbles.”

“I don’t grind my teeth.”

Flavie rolled her eyes. She reached for her cup of chè ba màu and I watched the beans and pandan jelly twirl inside the glass as she took a sip.

“Obviously I don’t think it’s going to magically fix your problem,” she said as she set it back down. “It’s just something you can try to see if you’ve been doing anything unusual in your sleep, and if you locate whatever it is that has been affecting your sleeping pattern—I dunno, maybe you’ll find a way to improve your sleep quality. Somehow. I mean, it wouldn’t hurt to try, right?”

“I don’t talk in my sleep.”

She fixed me with a deadpan gaze to which I flashed an appeasing smile in return. “Girl, you don’t know that. I’m just saying, whatever you’re going through isn’t exactly normal. You’ve always slept like a dog—”

“*Log*, not *dog*—”

“—You really should go get it checked when you can. But in the meantime, like I said, the app is just something you can try to play with.”

In my bowl, my eye-bags were so dark they looked like another set of eyes nestled underneath my original pair. I swirled the rice noodles with my chopsticks until the ripples shattered my reflection.

Despite myself, I downloaded the app that night upon returning home.

It helped that the app itself was free and looked forgettable. Black and white icon. Minimalist interface. No reviews. Flavie was right; it wouldn’t hurt to try.

The first night, the app didn’t record anything. The second night produced my first recording, which was almost funny when I played it.

**02h44 :** ‘**Suspicious,’** muttered my voice. **‘Very suspicious.’**

Silence.

I snorted. How insightful.

The next night was different; the recordings were suddenly much longer.

**03h10 : ’I don’t like coffee without sugar.’**

**03h10 : ‘Coffee with condensed milk is even nicer.’**

**03h13 : ’I should stop drinking coffee so late at night.’**

Now, that…I had to admit I was a bit unsettled by that. Not only was I speaking in complete sentences, in a voice that was clear and alert, but I also sounded conversational and curious.

Still nothing to be truly alarmed about, though. I texted Flavie about it and she found it funny.

I kept using the app from then on. Some nights there was nothing, but most nights the app recorded me rambling absolute nonsense.

**04h05 : ‘Name.’**

**04h05 : ‘No. Mine.’**

**04h06 : ’My name?’**

**04h07 : ’Good. Very good.’**

As nonsensical as the recordings were, my sleep actually improved somehow despite no particular change in routine on my part. Nonetheless, I started falling asleep within ten to fifteen minutes, and stayed unconscious throughout the entire course of each night. I was ecstatic to notice the progress and became slightly obsessed with checking the recordings first thing every morning. It almost felt like I was excited to go to sleep.

The recordings continued.

**03h03 : ’Flavie is my best friend.’**

**03h04 : ’Flavie is so nice and pretty. I like her so much.’**

To that, Flavie responded *‘ewww’* with a dozen vomiting emojis, which meant she was really pleased.

**02h45 : ‘I can télétravailler this Friday.’**

**04h11 : ‘I start my period next week.’**

**05h32 : ‘Six…seven...’**

I began to notice something a little bizarre, though, but I wasn’t sure if I was just imagining it. The thing was that my voice always sounded the same, meaning that it was my own voice, of course—but there was something slightly odd about the cadence and tone.

It was too deliberate. As though every word, every sentence, had been scripted before being spoken. I was sleep-talking with crystal-clear pronunciation, sometimes in Mandarin, mostly in English, and other times in French—and I always sounded like I was actually awake. Except I must’ve been fully asleep.

One night, the recordings suddenly became…weird.

**03h25 : ‘What do you want me to say?’**

**03h35 : ‘You keep interrupting me too early.’**

**04h01 : ‘Stop it...’**

**04h02 : ‘Stop listening…’**

**04h04 : ‘STOP ! ! I SAID STOP IT ! ! ! !’**

I flinched so violently my phone almost leapt from my grasp. I turned off the app and stared at the sunlight filtering through my shutters as my heart pounded away.

What the hell. I didn’t even know I could sound like that. Like a banshee. I couldn’t believe I hadn’t woken myself with a screech as loud as that.

In hindsight, I probably should’ve deleted the app after that one instance.

But I didn’t—because I was curious, or, I don’t know, masochistic—but I wanted to keep using it. Just so I could get my own piercing shriek out of my head.

**05h04 : ‘I’m tired from getting up so early every day.’**

**05h04 : ‘What do you want me to say?’**

**05h08 : ‘Stop resisting.’**

I still didn’t delete the app after that, but I stopped listening to the recordings. I didn’t think the problem lay within the app. After all, it was simply a tool that recorded me sleep-talking.

Yet my sleep quality instantly deteriorated to the state that it had been in a few weeks ago.

I was waking up in the middle of the night or at the crack of dawn again, and whenever I fell back asleep—if I was even lucky enough for that to happen—the sensation felt akin to being wrenched roughly underwater, before I would startle awake a few hours or a few minutes later with a full-body jerk, without feeling like I’d had any real reprieve. My face ached often, too, my cheeks and jowl sore.

Maybe it really was the stress.

So I went to see a general practitioner, just to have her tell me that she didn’t detect any abnormalities and recommend me to stop drinking coffee altogether for a few days. Which seemed sort of counterintuitive since I was so exhausted these days that I felt like a living corpse, like a shell of myself. If I had been wading through a bog before, I was now sinking steadily in the middle of a swamp. The waters were to my chin, at least.

One late evening, Flavie showed up directly on my doorstep.

It felt like I hadn’t seen her in a while. She was donning a cute pastel blue dress that flowed to her knees and a light denim jacket that framed her shoulders nicely, holding a paper bag from which wafted the heavenly aroma of buttered potatoes. Wow, I’d missed her. Even the sight of her angry, pinched face didn’t dim the rare joy that sparked to life in my chest.

“Hey,” I greeted her with a smile.

She glowered. “Hey? Just *hey?*”

I felt my smile strain a bit. “What’s wrong? Did something happen?”

“Really? What’s wrong?”

After all I did was ogle at her, she pressed me gently but firmly backwards until she could cross the threshold and shut the door behind her. She placed the paper bag on the floor and whipped back to face me.

“What’s *wrong* is that we’re supposed to be having dinner in this really nice bistrot right now but you never showed up. Girl, I waited *thirty minutes*. You didn’t respond to any of my calls, so I got pissed, yeah, but also freaking worried so I got takeaway instead and—are you seriously having *tea* right now?”

I followed her incredulous gaze to the mug that I had forgotten I was still gripping. “It’s just tisane. You know me, I like some tisane before I sleep.”

“But you hate sweetened tea,” she blurted. “You always make fun of me for adding sugar and now you’re doing it, too? Three sugar cubes—really?”

I blinked. “No? I’ve always sweetened my tea?”

Flavie’s face tightened, but she didn’t retort immediately. She scanned the disarray that was my apartment and I tried not to fidget as she discreetly side-eyed my scattered pieces of clothing, unwashed dishes, and unfinished paperwork. French bureaucracy was even more of a pain in the ass to deal with for foreigners like me, okay—who was she to judge?

“You haven’t been sleeping here properly,” she concluded mercifully.

“Eh.” I shrugged. “I sleep.”

“You pass out. That’s different.”

I raised my mug to my mouth as my mind whirred to come up with excuses, and barely held in my splutter. Damn, this shit was way too sweet.

Flavie took pity on me. She stepped up into my space, close, concern swimming in her big brown eyes, and softened her voice. “Look, I’m just worried, okay? I didn’t want to make it a big deal or whatever, but you’ve been acting…a little *bizarre*.” She reached out to wrap warm fingers around my wrist. “If you’re going through something, if there’s anything I can do to help…you know you can tell me, right?”

How could anyone say no to that?

And so I showed her the recordings. There were several, in fact, way more numerous than I’d expected—because I distinctly remember pulling multiple all-nighters on certain nights, consecutively—so it didn’t make sense that the app had recorded me sleep-talking every single night. Especially during periods where I remember being wide awake.

Whatever I had been saying made even less sense.

**02h05 : ‘I’m tired. You need to sleep more. I need to sleep more.’**

**01h10 : ‘Why aren’t you sleeping?’**

**01h43 : ‘Stop resisting.’**

**03h40 : ‘I hate summer. The metro smells like armpit.’**

**02h22 : ‘Sleep.’**

**01h31 : ‘I should do that more often.’**

**03h19 : ‘No. I’m being too obvious.’**

**05h10 : ‘You almost saw me in the hallway last night. Lucky for you that you didn’t look too hard.’**

Huddled together on the couch with my phone between us, Flavie and I exchanged a thousand-mile stare.

“Oooh-kay,” Flavie finally remarked. “Well, that wasn’t creepy at all.”

I attempted a laugh, which came out so painfully forced it made us both wince.

“There’s something else,” she continued. “It sounds like…it almost sounds like you’re rehearsing. Like—like, you’re *reading*, you know what I mean?”

I sucked in a sharp, reedy breath through my nose. “What the fuck, can you not? That’s creepy.”

“Yeah, you don’t say.”

Flavie didn’t want to leave me alone that night, but I had this irrational, nagging feeling that I needed to spend the night by myself and then check the newest recording tomorrow morning on my own.

Maybe I was scared of embarrassing myself further. Maybe I was just scared.

Scared that if someone else heard the recordings again, they would start sounding less like nonsense and more like evidence.

We lingered awkwardly at my doorway for a few moments before she finally sighed and tugged her jacket tighter around herself. “Text or call me if anything weird happens.”

I almost joked that everything weird had already happened. “Will do. Text me when you get home.”

After she left, the apartment seemed to exhale around me. The hallway looked darker than usual.

**03h12 : ‘What do you want me to say?’**

**03h15 : ‘Stop resisting.’**

**03h16 : ‘I said too much today. I’ll get me in trouble.’**

**03h16 : ‘I’ll get her in trouble.’**

**03h17 : ‘Stop resisting.’**

**03h20 : ‘It’s almost time.’**

After that, not even Flavie could’ve dissuaded me from deleting the app immediately that morning before I headed to work.

As I swayed in the metro, I felt detached from my own body. Like I’d been scraped hollow from the inside. Everyone looked exhausted under the fluorescent lighting, faces washed-out and gaunt with commuter fatigue. A man across from me stared too long in my direction without blinking.

Work was more unpleasant than usual that day due to my sheer fatigue, but that also meant I fell asleep almost instantly that night.

A few hours or a few minutes later, I jerked awake with a gasp, my feet lifting briefly off the mattress.

My skin, clammy with cold sweat, was prickling with that familiar sensation again—that instinctive, primal certainty that tells you that somebody is watching you.

My eyes flickered over my room as they tried to adjust to the darkness.

My room was pitch black except for the diluted glow of streetlights leaking through the shutters. And somewhere beyond the bedroom doorway, in the hallway—something shifted.

There was someone—something—in my house.

I still couldn’t see clearly, and I wasn’t sure I wanted to, but the first coherent thought that flashed through my mind was that it was impossibly tall—so tall that its head disappeared beyond the top of the doorframe. Beneath the neck hung a looming humanoid mass of shadows and flesh, and before I could yank away my gaze and scream, it suddenly folded downward and thrust its head into my room.

If I’d had any air left in my lungs, I would’ve been capable of screaming like I wanted to.

Alas, I could only keep staring in muted horror as its head snapped in my direction with an audible crack—and it had *hair, holy shit*, long and dangling and jerking with the motion. For a split-second, I swear we made eye-contact.

I’d always wondered whether I’d choose fight or flight in extreme cases of debilitating danger. Turns out I preferred to play dead. I shut my eyes and pretended I’d seen nothing, that everything was normal, that I was sleeping or dead. I wished I was just dreaming. I wished I was already dead.

I don’t know how much time had passed, or if any time had passed at all, before I convinced myself to open my eyes again.

It was no longer standing in the doorway.

It was right in front of me, face hovering a scant few centimeters above my own. I felt its dank breath fan across the tip of my nose, strands of hair tickling my cheekbones.

This time I screamed. My hand thwacked the lights switch above my head and flooded the room with blinding brightness.

As if I had completely fabricated the whole ordeal with my newfound hallucinative abilities, the thing was gone.

It had disappeared. Vanished. Nowhere to be found.

My room was empty, save for myself and my enduring terror.

The thudding of my heart threatened to drown out everything else. I jostled myself until I was sitting upright, chest heaving for air, then hunched forward on the bed, head bowed above my knees, before grinding the heels of my palms against my eyes. They came away wet with tears. My mind collapsed into itself again and again as it tried to make sense of what I had just seen or imagined.

There was only one thing that I was certain of: I needed to get out. Immediately.

Except I didn’t know how to get to my front door without crossing the hallway, and trying to make it out through the window meant risking a deathly fall—which suddenly seemed like a more alluring option over confronting whatever it was that could still be lurking in my house.

The silence itself became unbearable. I couldn’t stand the waiting any longer—maybe it was waiting for me to make the first move. Maybe I had to move before it could.

I remember gripping my phone so tightly my fingers ached. I remember staring at the bedroom doorway until my vision blurred. I remember inching off the bed, half-expecting to be grabbed around the ankles.

Then—

nothing.

Not darkness.

Just absence.

My next coherent memory was of stumbling down the building’s stairwell barefoot, nearly falling hard enough to crack my chin open on the steps.

I don’t remember crossing the hallway.

I don’t remember opening my apartment door.

Suddenly, the damp night air of summertime enveloped me all at once, the streetlights smearing together overhead in nauseating streaks, and I found myself hobbling outside with my phone pressed against my ear.

I rang up Flavie three times before the call got through. Her sluggish, sleep-addled voice nearly rocked me to the pavement with relief, and I warbled out a broken explanation while trying not to hyperventilate or start bawling.

The streets were so dark. The night buses were full of staring men whose faces seemed to blur together every time I looked at them directly, all hollow eyes and chipped teeth. I couldn’t tell anymore whether they merely reminded me of the thing in my apartment or whether it was following me.

By the time I reached Flavie’s apartment, I was shivering even in the summer heat.

She was already standing beside her open door, wrapped in an oversized shirt, hair mussed cutely. The relief that crossed her face upon seeing me lasted less than a second before twisting into alarm.

“*Damn.*”

“What?” I wheezed.

“You look haunted, you poor thing—where the fuck are your shoes?!”

She ushered me inside before I could turn around to glance down the winding staircase of her old Parisian apartment building. I kept feeling like there was somebody standing on the steps behind me, about to yank me backwards by my shoulder.

Flavie locked the door carefully, checking it once, then twice, and thrice.

She turned back to face me and froze. She cocked her head to the side, scanned me up and down, then asked, “What’s so funny?”

“Huh?”

“You’re grinning like you won the lottery.”

“Like hell I am.” Despite myself, my hands flew to my face, fingers skimming over my chapped lips and chin just to make sure. “I’m not in the mood for that.”

Flavie studied me with unreadable eyes, then breathed out a sigh when I still didn’t relent. She didn’t press further or ask any specific questions. I could tell she was also spooked by the night’s abrupt turn of events, but she wanted to steady me first. Yeah, I really liked Flavie.

She offered me tisane, but I didn’t want to add sugar in front of her and risk her ribbing me again. After making me clean the journey’s grime off my body with wet wipes, she handed me a set of her pyjamas; fresh, not soaked in sweat like mine, and laced with her familiar scent.

We were both yawning when we finally climbed into bed. The frantic terror in me had been subdued to a lingering unease. I didn’t think it would be leaving anytime soon.

“You’re texting your boss then seeing a doctor first thing in the morning. If you start levitating or speaking Latin in your sleep, I’m calling an exorcist.” She tried to pat my shoulder reassuringly but just ended up palming my chest in the dark. “Now go to sleep, I’m begging you. I literally have work in four hours.”

“Thank you. Goodnight, Flavie.” I squeezed my eyes tightly shut. “Love you.”

“Love you, too. Goodnight.”

I don’t remember falling asleep.

I was awake before my eyes opened. My skin was prickling. The room was utterly silent except for loud breathing.

Not mine.

I registered all of a sudden that Flavie was sitting upright beside me. I couldn’t tell which direction she was facing from the blurred outline of her body. I felt frozen from where I lay unmoving. I didn’t know whether to lean in closer. Or farther.

Something glinted in the darkness, white and glistening, near where her face should be.

“Flavie?” I forced myself to whisper. “Ça va? What…”

She twitched, head snapping so fast toward me it must’ve hurt. I pushed myself up on my elbows, the hairs on my nape rising.

Well, at least now I was certain she was looking directly at me. A cold shiver streaked down my spine when it suddenly dawned on me that the glint was her teeth.

Her lips were peeled back in a grin that looked absurdly wide in the dark. Her eyes looked enormous. Empty.

I swallowed hard, then finished my question: “What are you looking at?”

Silence.

I was self-conscious of how loud both our breathing sounded, mine rattling through my lungs.

The glaring headlights of a passing car illuminated her face the exact same instance she finally spoke—she was grinning toothily from ear to ear, jaw unhinged so low her chin touched her collarbones, pupils so dilated her entire eyeballs were black, her pale, sallow face twisted into an expression of feverish excitement and depraved delight—

The words that came out the red slash that was her mouth speared me through with stark terror:

**“What do you want me to say?”**

I right about flew off the bed as I lunged to switch on the lights, a scream finally tearing itself free from my throat. When I whipped back to face her, panting like I’d just run here from my house again, Flavie was cowering on her side of the bed with the blankets clutched all the way to her chin.

“What the fuck?” She looked genuinely terrified, maybe even more terrified than me, almost teetering off the edge of the mattress.

“You were talking,” I choked out, back pressed flat against the wall behind me. I wanted to sink through the concrete and disappear. “I can’t do this anymore, *shit*, I can’t fucking—”

“What?”

“You were staring at me, smiling, *and* *then you said—*”

She shook her head violently, sleep-tousled hair whipping from side to side. “No, nono*no—you—*”

“You said—” I cut myself off when she jabbed towards my face with an accusatory finger.

“Hey,” Flavie whispered wetly, like she was on the verge of breaking down into tears. “*You* were the one smiling. You said I shouldn’t have *looked* at you.”

Neither of us apologized. She retrieved her blindfold and the lights stayed on.

We went to back to bed in silence. It was awkward.

Her mattress was a bit too small for two grown women, so I could feel the stiff line of her body pressed up against mine, the heat of her skin leaking into my clothes. I could sense that she was understandably still just as frightened as me—and probably *of* me—because she wouldn’t stop trembling. Like she didn’t know whether she wanted to inch closer to me or farther. I’m just glad she didn’t banish me to the couch.

I didn’t fall asleep again. I didn’t dare open my eyes either.

Flavie left for work shortly after, while I returned to my place. I packed fast, throwing all my essentials into a suitcase—some clothes, my passport, residence permit, my wallet, a packet of toast, a bottle of soy sauce. I felt safer moving in broad daylight, even if the walls felt like they were pressing closer with every second I stayed in my apartment.

I just needed to stay somewhere else for a week or so, until I felt a bit better, a bit more like myself. Somewhere far from here. And far from Flavie—for both my sake and hers.

The third night, Flavie called me crying.

“You were at my apartment just earlier.”

My mouth went dry instantly. My hands stopped towelling my hair and dropped back down to hang limply at my sides. The towel fell to my feet, which suddenly felt encased in ice.

“What?” I rasped. “I’m not—*I didn’t*—I’m in my room! But not my apartment, I—”

“You just—” she gasped between sobs, “you just stood outside my door.”

“*No*, Flavie, I—”

“You *did*. I *saw* you.”

I maneuvered myself to plop my ass down atop the hotel bed before my shaking knees could send me toppling over.

Then: “You kept asking me to let you in.”

“*I swear, Flavie—*”

“You kept asking me what you had to say for me to let you in.”

Every muscle in my body locked up and I ended the call.

I texted her curtly to not respond to any of my messages. To not pick up any of my calls.

Because I think that was the moment I finally understood.

The recordings were never documenting my sleep.

They were documenting something speaking in my voice.

Learning me.

Practicing.

Pretending.

I don’t know which parts of me it learnt first.

And tonight, for the first time, I heard it speak without the recordings.

Just moments ago. Just now.

Coming right from the bathroom next to my hotel door.

In my own voice:

**“Can you hear me better now?”**

I don’t think it needs me asleep anymore.

I haven’t slept in three days. I think.

I don’t know what to do.

I think it wants—

**What do you want me to say?**
