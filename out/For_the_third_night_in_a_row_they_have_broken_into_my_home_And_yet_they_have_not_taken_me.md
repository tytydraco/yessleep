“Robert…I have known you for many years.” Father David begins. “You have served this fellowship with a selflessness that has inspired everyone.”

“I get that from my mother…” I choke out. “…something I’ve realized today.”

A loud, vibrating sound causes me to jump in place as my eyes race over to the back of the church, my hands literally shaking so violently that I can hear the tremble. I notice that the pace of my breath matches the pace of the organ, and it’s just the first of many songs tonight that has startled me in this moment.

He scans my face and places a hand behind my neck.

“The demons that are seeking you cannot harm you here.”

He also believes he can keep me safe. He also wouldn’t. But, that doesn’t matter now.

“How long must one repent until someone’s sins are wiped clean?” I whisper out.

“Until they are willing to give confession. Then it is done.” He responds.

I open my mouth to speak seven times, but it closes each time. I pause for a second as a sister walks by with the offering basket. I reach over and stuff it with everything in my pocket, and as I l lean back, I feel a bit lighter.

I feel like it’s time.

“I…was asked to keep a promise to someone. Someone I loved very much.” I say, feeling some of the weight lift from my shoulders.

“And you broke that promise?”

“No…worse.”

I watch as his eyes narrow.

“What could be worse than keeping a promise?” He asks.

“…keeping it.” I say, a dry gulp fighting its way down my throat as my head shakes back and forth.

“How can maintaining a vow be a sin?” He presses softly.

His words echo through my brain, repeating over and over, like my entire life is encapsulated in such a simple statement.

“I…had to leave someone behind that I loved very much.”

“And you promised you’d go back for them, but you haven’t? Is that it?” He inquires.

I hold my eyes closed as I see the entire dream playing in my head. The running. The fear in my mother’s eyes as she stared out the window for a husband who would never return. The knowing that she had, no, that we both had, even then, that if I waited, that I too would follow them into the dark.

“No…” I said, the weight lifted from my shoulder for the first time in twenty-seven years.

“…I promised that I wouldn’t.”

There is a silence between us, the first moment of complete quiet I’ve felt in nearly three decades.

“Then you are forgiven.”

“Can it really be that simple…?”

“We are all worthy of salvation in the eyes of God, Robert.”

“…what about in the eyes of men?”

As Father David is about to speak, a blinding white light forces its way through the stained glass, muting i’s beauty, piercing through with only one intention.

I have led them here.

They have come.

I hear a banging on the door and screams echo through the corridors of the church. Pastor David rushes off to help, and I can hear the screams of panic through the congregation. I look for my wife, and she is not here, yet.

I understand fully in this moment the sacrifice my mother had made that day. I choose to accept the forgiveness that has been given to me. And, I make a choice – the same choice that she made – to spare others from the same pain that will befall me.

I run full speed towards the front door, and just as I’m about to reach it, it bursts open. The one with the red eyes is standing there, the bright white light shining around him, obscuring everything else around him beyond a silhouette.

I run directly into his arms, and I feel my body encircled in light.

Suddenly, I am weightless, floating, as I am laid horizontal.

Taken by them. After all these years. Like my mother and father before me.

I'm validated, terrified, and somehow, relieved. At least it's finally over.

But then, I hear something. Words so familiar to me, words I’ve spoken so many times, but this time, they aren’t coming from my lips.

“I have a family. I have a kid. I have mouths to feed.”

I suddenly remember why I’ve resisted for so long. Instinct drives my body to push back, trying in vain to escape. I try to swing my arms free, and I feel my bracelet torn from my wrist, the legacy of my family scattering into the street. I try to reach out to save even one, but they use their ways to bring my body to stillness, and my mind into darkness.

I awaken in a place without light.  A place where the only sound is human suffering.

Groans, grunts, wails, despair.

Here, we are all the same, despite how we would have presented before.

There’s no reason to me to fear the aliens anymore, I realize. Only I knew the truth.

I WAS the alien all along.

I find my feet off my makeshift bed, packed close to the hundreds of souls that have been stripped of their lives that share this space.

Faces with the same despair I’d seen at dawn this morning.

No, somehow, worse. Much worse.

I hear a greeting, out of my left ear, and I don’t even flinch.

That’s how well I’ve trained myself after twenty seven years of being someone else.

I hear the greeting again, in the same tongue I had ignored for so long, and realize that I don’t have to pretend anymore.

“What’s your name?” he says, in his native dialect.

“It doesn’t matter now.” I stammer back, in English, realizing that I can no longer speak my own native language.

“Doesn’t it? Doesn’t it matter who you are?” He asks.

“I haven’t been truly myself in a long time. I don’t even know who that is.” I respond.

“Do you at least know where they’re taking you?”

“Yes. But, I’ve never been there, at least, not since I was a child. I don’t know anyone there, I don’t speak the language. I won’t even know how to begin.”

“Won’t your people come looking for you? Don’t you think they will help?”

“Eventually, but I told them that aliens were after me.”

“Why? They couldn't have believed you?”

“They didn’t, but that wasn’t the point.” I begin. “I said it to keep them safe. Sure, they worried about me, but they never had to worry that THIS would happen. And somehow, that was the best I could to do to both stay vigilant and spare them from the same fear I’ve lived with my entire life.”

“I’m sorry that it has ended this way for you.”

“I’m not.” I say, as I become overwhelm with emotion.

They were the best years of my life.”

I feel a tear run down my cheek and I choose not to fight it.

“I was proud. I was proud to be an American.”

The tear drips off my chin, and I catch it on one fingertip, rubbing it against my thumb. For some reason, I raise it to my nose and inhale.

It makes me calmer, just like…

A distinctive howl suddenly echoes through the halls of this damned place.

The same sound that THEY make.

That quaking, wailing scream that’s awoken me three nights in a row.

I realize as I approach that as I have nowhere to run, he will have no reason to hide. I will finally meet him, face to face. My pursuer.

As I inch closer and closer, there is just a small window that separates us. When I peer through, I recognize him immediately, even though his face looks entirely different. He still has those red eyes - swollen, dark, wet. And, as I look closer, I see they are also…

…sad?

I realize that before, he was simply wearing a mask. Without it, we aren’t much different at all. I watch as he leans his head against the wall, staring down at something in his hand.

He’s holding a picture that has a small puddle forming on it, and I watch as he wipes his tears off As he turns, we lock eyes, but we do not share words.

There is nothing left to say in a place where individuals matter less than agendas, where families are second to prerogative. We are both trapped here, bound by a system that requires sacrifice. The compassion I’d hoped for in this moment has been sold for dollars and cents.

I just nod as I look again at the photo.

He has a wife. He has a son. He has mouths to feed.

He puts his mask back on, shrouding who he is underneath. As he straps on his vest, a barrier between our humanities, the words across his chest define what has brought us both to this pivotal moment.

Immigration. Customs. Enforcement.
