I’ve been a detective for almost twenty years now, and after enough time doing this job you stop believing things can still surprise you anymore.

Most cases become paperwork eventually.

This one didn’t.

I was called into the Hollows County Sheriff’s Office early that morning regarding a suspicious package. It had been placed neatly against the front entrance doors, No return address. No markings, no indication of where it came from. Just… left there.

Bomb squad was dispatched immediately. They arrived at approximately 07:42 hours. I stayed outside while they approached it. Standard procedure.

Over the radio, one of them confirmed:

“The package is clear. No explosive components.”

“There’s… something inside.”

A few minutes later, they opened it and inside was a small object. That’s how they described it at first. Just an object. At 07:51, one of the bomb techs walked it out to me.

It was a USB drive, black, no branding, no markings.

There was a tag attached to it. Handwritten.

It read:

“Sins of the Lamb.”

The tech handed it to me and gave a half-smile.

“Last thing I remember, the Unabomber didn’t send USBs.”

He laughed; I didn’t. I nodded once and muttered “asshole” under my breath. He either didn’t hear it or pretended not to.

I took the device from his hand and headed toward the department. Every time I walked into that building, the statue of Lady Justice near the entrance caught me off guard. Six feet tall. Tarnished bronze. The air around it always carried this copper smell that tasted like blood.

Beneath it was an epigraph:

“Let every person be subject to the governing authorities. For there is no authority except from God…” — Romans 13:1–3

It always made me uneasy. I wasn’t chosen by God. I was chosen to fill in the spot of a man who drank himself to death, according to the other officer. 

I walked straight to Chief Brennan Johnson’s office. Johnson was already there. He always was. Family man on paper, respected. Clean record. But there was something about him that never sat right with me. He was ambitious in a way that made people dangerous. Always wanting more, more cases, more recognition, more control.

I placed the USB on his desk.

He stared at it for a second longer than he should have.

Then looked at me.

“What is it?” he asked.

I didn’t answer immediately.

“Something someone wanted us to find.”

I grabbed it from his desk and walked to my office on the corner of the department. My office was far from the other; I didn’t mind, I didn’t want to be bothered, I used one of the department’s isolated systems to open the drive.

One folder.

That was it.

“TALBOTT”

I stood up so fast my chair slammed into the wall behind me.

My name is not “Detective,” not “Case File.” Just Talbott…at first, I thought it was a prank. It had to be. But nobody should’ve known this would land on my desk. Nobody should’ve known I’d be the one opening it. So how did they?  Inside the folder was a single video file:

“Trial\_One”

I stared at it for a long moment before finally hitting play. At first, nothing happened. A black screen, with no sound, no movement. I almost wrote it off as corrupted until the screen flashed white at exactly five minutes and thirteen seconds. Just for a split second. I replayed it. Once the length of the video got to five-thirteen, there it was, another flash. I leaned closer. Something about it felt deliberate. So I played it again and started writing the flashes down.

Short.

Long.

Pause.

Short.

By the time it ended, I already knew what I was looking at. Morse code. I translated it.

EPHESIANS 5:13

I leaned back in my chair and rubbed my eyes.

“A Bible verse. Great.”

I looked it up on my phone.

“But when anything is exposed by the light, it becomes visible.”

I stared at the USB sitting on the desk.

“Exposed by the light...”

So I grabbed my flashlight and held it over the drive. Nothing, I tried different angles. Still nothing, I was getting irritated by that point. Then sunlight came through the blinds, I noticed it in the reflection of the monitor… Faint numbers, they were barely visible, coordinates. I stood up slowly and moved closer to the screen. Yeah, definitely coordinates! I copied them down before the light shifted away. Then I plugged them into the system, the map loaded. A pin dropped outside town, it was the middle of nowhere, but there was still a faded label attached to it.

“Frosty Pines Ice Cream”.

I remembered the place immediately. It closed years ago. Nobody went out there anymore, it has been abandoned for a long time, which meant whoever sent this knew exactly where they wanted me to go. I printed the map and headed straight for Johnson’s office. My shoes squeaked against the wooden floor the whole way there.

I dropped the paper onto his desk.

“That’s where it points.”

Johnson glanced down at it.

Just a glance, but I caught it. It was Interest, real interest. It was gone a second later.

“What is it?” he asked.

“Coordinates from the video file.”

He leaned back in his chair. Calm. Too calm.

“And you’re sure about that?”

“Yeah,” I said. “I’m sure.”

Silence. A few seconds too long.

Then he nodded.

“Alright.”

Another pause. 

Then:

“You’re gonna sit this one out.”

I laughed once, thought he was joking, He wasn’t.

Talbott “Excuse me?”

“I’ll send a team to check it out.”

“ A team?” I stepped closer to the desk. “I did the work for it.”

“And now you’re done with it.”

Something in me snapped.

“No,” I said, “I’m not fucking done with it.”

He didn’t even blink.

“Watch your tone, Talbott.”

“Don’t tell me about my tone,” I shot back. “You had me working this all day and now suddenly I’m out?”

“That’s not your call.”

“Then whose is it?”

Silence again, that same measured look, like he was deciding something. I grabbed the map off his desk and crumpled it in my fist.

“ Fine,” I muttered. “Send whoever the hell you want.”

Then I walked out without looking back, I didn’t go back to the station after that. Didn’t see the point.

The drive home was quiet. No radio. No calls. Just the road and the sound of the engine. I don’t remember half of it, Just pulling into the lot, Turning the car off. I sat there longer than I should have.

My apartment was on the third floor. Same as always. The hallway lights flickered when I stepped inside. Nobody fixed them, Nobody complained either. Complaining meant listening to the landlord ramble for twenty minutes while cigarette smoke leaked from under his door. I walked past the same apartment numbers. Same silence behind them. 814.I unlocked the door and the hinges screamed loud enough to wake the dead.I kept telling myself I’d buy WD-40. Never did, I stepped inside. Nothing, No sound, No movement, No reason to say anything out loud. I shut the door. Locked it. My keys hit the counter and echoed through the apartment. Next to them sat a photo frame face down. Same place it had been for months. I didn’t touch it, didn’t need to.I tossed my jacket toward the chair. Missed. I left it there.

The bathroom light hit me like a flashbang. God, I hated those lights. I stepped toward the mirror and stared at the man looking back at me. I Didn’t recognize him. Forty-two years old. Gray spreading through my hair and mustache. Eyes swollen with bags underneath them so dark they looked painted on. I looked like somebody had been feeding me bad news for twenty years straight. I turned the shower on and cranked the water as hot as it would go. Steam swallowed the mirror almost immediately. Good. I liked not seeing him. I stepped into the shower and let the water hit me. Didn’t move. Just stood there while the heat burned against my skin.

Tried washing the day off. The case. The feeling. Didn’t work because it wasn’t dirt, it was something else. The feeling faded somewhat under the hot water, but it was still there. Like embers buried under ash. I grabbed whatever I could find to put on, and I walked to the living room. I leaned back into the couch. My eyes felt heavy enough to sink through my skull. I closed them. Just for a second. That’s all it took. Darkness, not the room. Something else, a road. Wet pavement reflecting red and blue lights. Voices somewhere far away. A cardboard box soaked through with rain.

Then a voice:

“Don’t let him—”

Gone.

Cut off by the sound of my phone ringing. Sharp and violent. I grabbed it before the screen light could burn my eyes out and answered without looking.

“Yeah.”

Silence.

Then:

“Talbott.”

My stomach tightened immediately.

A second passed.

Then:

“It’s me.”

Johnson.

And just like that—

The embers survived

The phone was still against my ear. Johnson breathed on the other end for a second before speaking again.

“What the hell was that back there?”

I leaned further into the couch cushions and rubbed my eyes.

“I already left Johnson.”

“That’s not the point.”

Silence.

The TV kept talking to itself somewhere across the apartment.

“You done?”

Johnson let out a tired breath through his nose.

“You don’t get to walk into my office, disrespect me in front of the department, then storm out like some pissed off teenager.”

“Could’ve fooled me.”

Johnson didn’t say anything just stayed silent, I could hear papers shifting on his end. Car door shutting somewhere in the background.

“Any progress on Frosty Pines?”

Johnson hesitated too long.

“Not yet.”

That answer bothered me more than if he’d said nothing at all.

Then:

“Look, just cool off for the night.”

“I’ll try...”

“Talbott listen to me for once in your life, I’m your chief like it or not…” 

I cut him off mid sentence

“You calling to bitch me out or you got something useful to say?”

Another silence.

Then the line clicked.

Dead.

I stared at the phone for another second before tossing it onto the couch beside me.

I threw my head back and I was staring at the discolored ceiling 

About forty minutes later, dispatch called.

“Detective Talbott?”

I was halfway through a beer I didn’t even remember opening.

“Yeah.”

“We got reports of a disturbance at Beer Tab Bar just outside county limits.”

“Patrol busy?”

“Nearest unit’s over twenty minutes out.”

I looked at the clock.

2:11 AM.

“What kind of disturbance?”

Dispatch shuffled papers.

“Intoxicated male. Threatening customers. Bartender says he may have a firearm.”

I shut my eyes for a second.

“Of course he does.”

“You’re the closest detective available.”

I grabbed my keys.

“Yeah. I’m on my way.”

The Beer Tab Bar sat off the highway like somebody forgot to demolish it. Half the neon sign was burned out. Rainwater dripped from the roof into muddy potholes outside. Three pickup trucks in the parking lot. One motorcycle. I stepped out of the car and immediately smelled cigarettes, wet pavement, and stale beer leaking through the walls. Country music played low inside. The kind you feel more than hear. I pushed the door open. The bartender spotted me instantly and pointed toward the back corner.

“Bout damn time.”

I looked over, big guy. Late forties maybe, Heavy beard, Hunter jacket still dry so has been here for a while, Wade Crest... He sat alone beneath a wall full of mounted deer heads, staring into a whiskey glass like it owed him money. Two men nearby kept their distance. One woman looked ready to leave.

“That him?”

Bartender nodded.

“Started screaming at some guy over not knowing how to gut a buck.”

“Sounds serious.”

“Then he threatened to put a hole through the jukebox.”

“That’s more serious.”

I walked toward him slowly. Wade didn’t even look up at first.

“Evening.”

Nothing.

“You Wade Crest?”

Wade finally lifted his eyes toward me. They were bloodshot and cold. The kind of eyes that never fully stopped watching the woods.

“Who’s askin’?”

“Detective Talbott.”

Wade snorted softly.

“Course you are.”

“You’ve been causing problems.”

Wade took another drink.

“Bartender talks too much.”

“You got a weapon on you tonight?”

Wade smiled at that, it was not friendly.

“Depends.”

“On what?”

Wade finally stood up. Jesus...Big bastard, taller than me by at least half a foot.

“Depends if you’re askin’ polite.”

The room got quieter.

I could feel people listening without looking.

“Alright. Put your hands where I can see them.”

Wade stared at me for another second, Then laughed it was low and broken.

“You got tired eyes, detective.”

“Hands.”

Wade stepped closer instead. Beer and cigarette smoke rolled off him.

“You ever shoot somethin’ and still hear it breathin’ after?”

“Last warning.”

Wade’s smile twitched.

“That stays with a man.”

Then suddenly—

his hand grabbed the front of my jacket. It was fast. Too fast for a drunk man. Instinct took over. I drove my knee straight into his groin. Wade folded immediately with a sound somewhere between a cough and a growl. The entire bar winced. He dropped to one knee holding himself while I shoved him against the nearby pool table.

“Hands behind your back.”

Wade laughed again, actually laughed, even while gasping for air.

“There it is…”

“Shut up.”

“Mean son of a bitch…”

I slammed cuffs onto his wrists. Wade looked up at me through wet strands of hair hanging over his face.

Then quietly—

almost too quiet to hear—

“You got a fire in you, officer.”

Something about the way he said it bothered me immediately. Not drunk. Not rambling. He recognized something and it rubbed me the wrong way . I pulled him toward the door.

Outside, rain hammered the parking lot harder now. I threw dumbass in the backseat, and slammed the door shut behind him. Wade laughed to himself in the backseat while rain hammered the roof hard enough to drown out the highway.I got into the driver’s seat and pulled away from Beer Tab. The storm had gotten worse. Way worse. Rain slammed against the windshield in crooked sheets while the wipers fought a losing battle trying to keep up. Dispatch crackled over the radio a few minutes later.

“Units be advised, County Route 14 is currently blocked due to flooding near Mile Marker 22.”

Perfect, That was my route back into town, I rubbed a hand down my face.

“Any alternate routes open?”

Static.

Then:

“Backroads are still accessible for now.”

For now, I glanced in the rearview mirror. Wade sat leaned against the window with bloodshot eyes half-open. Smiling.

“Something funny?”

Wade looked out into the rain.

“Storms make people stupid.”

“You’d know.”

Wade chuckled quietly to himself. I took the turn onto the backroads about ten minutes later. Trees swallowed the road almost immediately. No streetlights, No houses. Just wet branches twisting in the wind while the headlights cut through rain and darkness one miserable foot at a time.

Then—

something burst from the trees.

A deer.

I slammed the brakes hard enough for the cruiser to skid sideways across the wet road.

Wade’s shoulder slammed against the backseat door. The deer froze in the headlights. Huge thing. Its eyes reflected bright white against the storm. It just stood there staring directly at the car while rain rolled down its body.

“Move!”

The animal twitched slightly, then bolted back into the woods. I let out a breath and gripped the steering wheel tighter. Behind me, Wade started laughing again. Quieter this time.

“What now?”

Wade leaned forward just enough for the cuffs to rattle.

“Missed your shot.”

Something cold crawled up the back of my neck at the way he said it. I looked back toward the road. That’s when I saw it through the trees.A faded sign barely hanging from rusted chains.

FROSTY PINES

The old ice cream mascot beneath the letters stared out from the darkness with half its face peeled away. I slowed the cruiser as the sign disappeared behind the trees. Frosty Pines sat farther back from the road than I remembered. Most of it was swallowed by darkness and overgrown weeds. The storm made the whole place look like it was trying to sink back into the woods. That’s when I noticed the car, only one, it was parked crooked near the front entrance. A black sedan with a license plates of the department. Rain hammered across the hood while the headlights cut across it for a second. It was Johnson’s car. He didn’t send a team it was just him. 

“What an asshole, I thought. 

I frowned.

“The hell are you doing out here…”

Behind me, Wade shifted in the backseat, the cuffs rattled softly.

“Told you storms make people stupid.”

I kept staring at the building. The front sign buzzed faintly in the rain. One of the old letters flickered weakly before dying again.

FRO\_TY PIN\_S

The place looked dead. But Johnson’s car was still warm enough that rain steamed faintly off the hood. Meaning he’d only gotten there recently, my stomach tightened.

“Johnson?”

Nothing, just the hum of electricity somewhere deeper inside the building. I followed it down a narrow hallway. The lights flickered harder down there. One of the freezer doors hung halfway open. Inside looked wrong immediately.

Medical equipment, old monitors wires hanging everywhere, and in the center of the room an industrial steel mixing vat. Big enough to fit a human body.Water dripped slowly from the rim onto the floor beneath it.

Then I saw him.

Johnson.

Laying on the floor beside one of the machines, motionless.

“Johnson!”

I rushed forward and immediately pulled my gun. Something covered his face.

A mask, dark green, glossy in some places, cracked in others. The expression carved into it looked wrong. It didn’t express pain, It was want need. I stepped closer carefully.

Gun raised. I’d be dumb if I didn’t. The entire room hummed softly around me.

“Johnson…”

No response, I crouched beside him slowly. Rain thundered somewhere outside the plant. I reached toward the mask, then something slammed into the back of my head. Pain exploded through my skull. The room tilted sideways as I hit the floor hard. For half a second, I saw it standing over me. A figure dressed in black robes. Then darkness…

Fluorescent lights buzzed overhead. Everything felt cold, I sat at a metal table staring at my hands. Rainwater still dripped from my sleeves onto the floor beneath me. Somebody was talking behind the glass. It was muffled I couldn’t hear them. My ears still rang.

Then—

The sound of a drip, I looked towards the sound slowly. A cardboard box sitting on the table across from me. It was dripping; the box was collapsed inward in some places. Something dark soaked through the bottom corners and dripped onto the floor. It was Blood. My breathing stopped.

No, No, no—

I couldn’t look away from it. Then a voice: It was soft, calm, almost gentle.

“Open your eyes.”

The lights above me flickered once. Twice. Then everything went dark.
