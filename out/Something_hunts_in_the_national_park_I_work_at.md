  
So, I work for the Department of Wildlife Conservation in my country; usually, the worst thing we come across on patrol out here is other people, given the uptick in poachers. I would be unlikely to see many of those, since the elephants cleared out of this area in the late 90's mainly because of those murderous little pigs, so now they only come to slaughter the Buffalo or deer for their horns.

But a little over a week ago, I was on patrol with another Ranger(for anonymity, let's call him Malik). At the time, I was on a small foothill, and Malik was back at the pickup, calling his wife. Then the smell hit me, iron and rot, something died nearby, and descending the foothill, I saw exactly what had.

I had worked that job for years at that point, so kills were common to find, but this was one of the stranger ones. A bull buffalo around 600 kilos, or at least what was left of one, was a relatively fresh kill, couldn’t have been made more than a day ago, given no scavengers were on it yet. Obviously fed on though, belly ripped open, the innards and parts of head were consumed and gone, but the flanks, legs and belly meat which most predators large enough to take down a buffalo would eat were untouched, the bull's left legs were damaged at the ankle as it is when it falls in an incline this steep, right flank torn in five jagged lines, as if something had slashed at it while knocking the buffalo down. But here's the thing: the largest predator in the country couldn't knock something this heavy down. When Malik eventually came down to check on me, he saw the kill too. He was confused as I was, but he pointed out something I hadn't noticed; along with most of its scalp, the thing's horns were also missing.

I took photos and noted the location. When we got back to the Northern entrance to the reserve, where most of our official infrastructure and accommodations were, I reported the kill as something either a leopard or a poacher did, along with attaching copies of the photos to the report as additional evidence. But I knew it couldn't really be either, Leopards had no use for horns, poachers had no use for organs, and neither had the power to tip over half a tonne. I looked into species profiles and activity areas, but came up short; it had to be a poacher trying to kill the local wildlife for their own gain, something that had always made me a little heated. And that was it for a week, went home on Saturday, saw my parents and cousins, then came back to work on Sunday, the kill having slipped my mind almost completely, until I went on patrol the next day.

Malik had used up his vacation days to go on a beach trip with his family, which was all well and good except for the fact that I was alone on my rounds now. It was around midday when I saw it hanging from a Banyan tree, shiny and white, undeniably eye-catching in the dense mesh of greens and browns that color the canopy. I stopped the truck and got out. The walk was uphill but not too difficult, given the sparser undergrowth of the area. When I eventually reached the tree, it took me a few seconds to realize it was a large pair of horns, definitely from a buffalo. The surface was clean, meticulously polished like expensive ivory. I took more pictures, made more reports, and got more silence from management.

Finding the horns had reignited the fire in me, and at this point, I was convinced it was a person who had likely used a trap to tip the Buffalo, and used the claw-like cuts to make it seem like a leopard kill? I wasn't quite sure about the semantics of it, but I knew for certain it wasn't an animal's doing. So I requested to spend the next night posted out in one of the small field observation huts in the area. All I'd need to do is prove that people were here, then the police could get involved and figure out the rest.

That area of the reserve rarely gets night patrols, given how inland it was and the steeper terrain. "A poacher couldn't get in without going through the patrol of another ranger first, and even then, he's more likely to get hit by lightning than to get out with his payload." That's what one of the senior officers told me when I asked about it. So it wasn't a surprise when the hut was ever so slightly overtaken by vines on the outside and caked in dust on the inside. The layout was simple and small, and three of the four sides had a large window with a layer of bars to stop anything from getting in, and the fourth was concrete where the door was. Inside was a desk, a chair set against it, and the ever ubiquitous bucket. I set the things I brought with me down in the corner by the door. It was the basics for something like this: my issued rifle and ammo for it, a utility knife, a high-power flashlight, and my radio, along with a bag of trail mix and a large bottle of water.

I went on three patrols that night, not a single thing out of the ordinary, except maybe for the Sambur. Sambur, like most prey animals, is polyphasic, so they don't sleep for long, but usually they rest more frequently at night. The herd I saw from my patrol was all up, closer together, twitching at any small noise. But even as I watched for a longer time, no signs of what was making the sambur like this revealed themselves. So I decided whatever had been after wasn't here, at least not that night.

It was around 2 A.M when I heard it, the scampering crescendo I was familiar with, chaotic and uneven, exactly how a herd scattered when something lunged. I immediately grabbed my rifle and flashlight and called in the sound with the radio.

The sound had died down when I got there. Now the woods seemed quiet, but not the same quiet that precedes a predator attacking, nor was it the silence that the night sometimes gave way to; this was different, absolutely nothing, hell, not even the wind rustling leaves, as if the wild had been strangled. I made it back to the clearing where I first saw the Sambur. The herd was gone, all except one.  
It was a Buck, openned, scalped and emptied the same way as the Buffalo was, it was still warm, couldn't have been more than 20 minutes since death, but this time it was dispatched with a long set of puncture wounds that carved canyons that were a quarter as deep as the neck was wide, there were five claw marks this time, splayed out like raccoon tracks. I didn't even know what I was looking at anymore, even with my years of apprenticeship and time on the job. But I did know that I didn't want to be around it, especially not when the corpse was this warm.

I was on a trail, halfway back to the hut, when the canopy rustled, not like when a monkey moved, but heavier and more intentional. I turned to the noise, even with the high power flashlight, the distance and the morning fog now rolling in made it hard to distinguish anything but the large silhouette and the light that hit its eyes, reflecting as green glowing spots from up in the canopy. I was frozen; it was still, for a moment, just observing each other.

The moment broke quickly as I finally overcame my lizard brain enough to run. flashlight clattering down from my grip as I did, the rustling didn't follow. As I rushed back, I twisted my ankle from tripping over a rock, but I kept going, adrenaline keeping me from feeling the pain. Eventually, I was able to climb back up to the hut, where I was greeted by the familiar flash of headlights and a concerned and confused junior officer who wouldn’t believe me if I told them what happened. But honestly, who would?

It's been a week since then, and the Department of Wildlife Conservation put me on paid leave for a while till my ankle healed. But I asked Malik to go check on the Banyan tree again. Yesterday, he called, said there's a pair of polished Sambur antlers hanging from the branches now.
