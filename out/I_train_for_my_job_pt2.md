So, let’s talk about Jesse.

Jesse was another guy on the team. Latino, in his 40’s. He’d been a brick layer most of his life. I don’t know much more about him. We didn’t talk much before Taylor, we didn’t talk much after Taylor. However, I did know he was a good guy. He was actually the captain of our crew, which is a strange thing to say after I just introduced him that way.

We’d say “good morning” on the ride down, eat quiet lunches at the same table, and we worked next to each other. He was in the 6th stall, I was in the 7th stall. We had a routine.

Right! The stalls. As we entered our “position”, that’s what we call our place on the floor with the procured materials. Let’s call “procured materials”—  “transitioners”. We set up our plastic sheeting between our work areas. How does this isolate us? How does blood just not pool on the floor and into each other’s stalls? I’m glad you asked.

You know how those  “zero depth” sinks operate. The one that looks like a flat surface, but the water flows into a hidden channel? Theres a slight opening where I’ve noticed the blood draining in  between the stalls. But to the casual observer, it just looks like the blood avoids crossing underneath the plastic sheets.

This is how we usually collect the blood. If we just put the materials in the machine without draining it, there’d be a heck of a mess to clean up. Trust me. You don’t want to work overtime, dismantling and cleaning the interior of these machines. It took me 3 hours to clean up my first mess.

Do the transitioned still get parted, sorted, and shipped when full? Yes. But they have to be cleaned before they are. Blood has a way of going foul— way before the parts do.

Back to Jesse. The company allowed us to transition the materials through any means that doesn’t damage, destroy, ruin, or foul parts. You can’t just take a bolt gun and pop them in the heart. The head is usually the best bet to efficiently start the procedure. We call this, “starting the clock”.

There’s a time limit to these things. Both in acquiring and shipping materials. We don’t just send them around America, we ship internationally. Seeing as we’re the first of our kind of facility, that means we produce all the parts for waiting customers and we need to be quick about it. So, some people skip to step two to “boost proficiency”, “up their numbers”, “exceed expectations”.

Jesse liked to get straight into blood acquisition.

With a steadying hand and the flick of a long knife, that’s how he “started the clock”. The problem with that is, the body knows it’s dying.

It struggles, it fights, even when it’s pointless. Especially when it’s pointless. It’s worse when they wake up during “transition”. It happened to me once. I don’t use knives to transition. I stick to the gun. It’s more… palpable to me. This is a job. I follow the Standard operating procedure, SOP, I don’t leave things to chance and I’m not cruel. But lord help me… as I pressed the cold steel to its head, it woke up… it made a small sound… confused. Then I pulled the trigger.

Jesse didn’t talk about it. Jesse didn’t complain. Jesse was doing his job… just doing it his way. But past the blurry, plastic sheeting, under the clear face mask, I could tell, he was smiling as he worked. Making the “transitioning”  into the “transitioned”— shaking until there wasn’t a drop of blood to shake out of its parts.

Jesse had a big one come in on the sixth month of working together. Transitioning parts came in all shapes and sizes, usually fit with high cholesterol, only the finest of parts we could acquire. But Jesse was a “professional” now. We all were.

But Jesse had a system within a system. He was the top operator so far, and we looked up to him because he was the oldest, strongest, and most efficient. We didn’t think twice about his “Transitioner”, even if the parts were more… muscular than normal. Maybe it used to work together as a a line backer, or in the military. We didn’t get personal details like that. The plastic wrap made sure that parts didn’t flail and kept everything nice and tidy before being fed into the machine.

Jesse set up his station, “started the clock” and somehow, on the 4th or 5th spasm, the legs kicked off the Velcro restraint and the transitioned fell onto Jesse. Jesse let out a gurgling moan of pain as we rushed to his station.

Non slip shoes parted the Red Sea, hands tore away plastic sheeting. 250 lbs of meat had fallen, spasming, squirming as Jesse laid under it.

“Ghlep— m”, the words were wet on Jesse’s lips as we grabbed the transitioned, and lifted it off our coworker.

The long knife had slid between the two. Slicing skin at an angle, piercing between the ribs, into a lung, maybe puncturing the heart. We didn’t pull the knife out. It didn’t matter if we did or not. The thing about this work area is that… we’re in a sterile environment. We weren’t allowed to open the doors. We had four others transitioned waiting to be processed. Mine was already half empty.

Jesse understood… he was our quiet captain. Our leader. He talked to Bernadette about concerns we never brought up to her. Jesse asked for a monthly team dinner when he noticed our… lowering morale. Jesse was a private man with a family. Jesse loved his children and his wife.

I’m actually tearing up while writing this. We didn’t talk much, but I knew him, and I will never forget how he pulled that knife out and mumbled, “gek ack u wark”

We did.

Thanks Jesse. I don’t think any of us had the strength to do that to him… we would’ve let everything spoil… and then he would’ve died anyway.
