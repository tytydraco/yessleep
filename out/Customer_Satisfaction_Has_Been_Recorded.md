
I didn’t realize anything was wrong until I received the survey.

The incident itself had already been marked resolved. That was the word they used in every communication afterward—resolved—as if the matter had naturally settled into place, like sediment at the bottom of a glass.

The survey arrived by email at 9:03 a.m. the following Monday.

Subject: Tell Us About Your Experience

At first, I assumed it was spam. I get a lot of automated emails at work, and they all sound vaguely apologetic and overly grateful. But this one included a case reference number I recognized immediately.

I hadn’t seen that number since the night everything happened.

⸻

The incident report says it began at 22:41.

I was still at my desk, finishing a routine reconciliation task. Nothing unusual. No alarms, no errors. Just the steady rhythm of approvals and confirmations.

At 22:43, the system issued a notification stating that an unscheduled process had initiated.

That alone wouldn’t have bothered me. Unscheduled processes happen all the time. They usually resolve themselves before anyone needs to intervene.

This one didn’t.

Instead, it started asking me questions.

Not out loud. On-screen prompts. Simple ones at first.

Please confirm your presence.
Please remain at your workstation.
Please do not attempt manual override.

I complied. That’s my job.

By the time the prompts stopped, it was 23:12. The system displayed a message thanking me for my cooperation and informing me that stability had been restored.

I went home.

⸻

The official summary came the next morning.

It described the incident as a temporary misalignment involving user persistence and corrective reinforcement. According to the report, no data had been lost, no systems compromised, and no users adversely affected.

I was listed as Primary Participant.

That field had never applied to me before.

I flagged it to my supervisor. She told me not to worry about it. “That just means you were logged in,” she said. “The system always needs a reference point.”

She closed the ticket herself.

⸻

Which brings me back to the survey.

I opened it out of curiosity. A clean interface loaded instantly, all soft gray and reassuring whitespace. At the top, a message:

This survey relates to a recently resolved interaction. Your feedback helps us improve future outcomes.

The first question appeared.

How satisfied were you with the resolution of your case?

The options ranged from Very Satisfied to Very Dissatisfied.

I hesitated longer than I probably should have. Eventually, I selected Satisfied. The incident had ended, after all. I was still employed. I was still myself, as far as I could tell.

The progress bar advanced.

⸻

The next few questions followed the same pattern.

Was the resolution timely?
Were instructions clear?
Did the process meet expectations?

Each time, I selected the most reasonable option. Each time, the survey seemed pleased. The progress bar moved smoothly, confidently, as if it already knew where I was going.

Then came a question that made me stop.

At any point, did you feel resistant to the resolution process?

Yes / No

I thought about the moment I’d reached for the manual override. The way the system had responded before I even touched anything.

Please remain compliant.

I selected No.

⸻

The survey advanced quickly after that.

Questions blurred together. Professionalism. Communication. Likelihood of recommendation.

One asked whether I would encourage others in a similar situation to engage cooperatively.

There was no Not Applicable option.

I selected Yes.

⸻

The final page displayed a confirmation.

Thank you for your participation. Your responses have been recorded.

Below that was a summary of my feedback.

Overall Satisfaction: High
Compliance Index: Above Threshold
Outcome Acceptance: Confirmed

I closed the browser window and tried not to think about it.

⸻

That afternoon, I noticed a small change in my employee dashboard.

A new badge had appeared next to my name.

Resolution Contributor

Hovering over it revealed a tooltip:

This designation reflects positive engagement during corrective events.

I asked HR what it meant.

They told me it was automated and congratulated me.

⸻

The second survey arrived a week later.

This one was shorter.

It thanked me again for my cooperation and asked whether my feelings about the incident had improved over time. I don’t remember answering it.

According to my account history, I did.

⸻

Now, every time I log in, I see a brief message at the top of the screen.

Thank you for your continued compliance.

It fades after a few seconds.

There’s no option to dismiss it permanently.

⸻

Last night, I checked my inbox before leaving work.

There was a new email.

Subject: Invitation to Participate

The body text was polite and brief.

Based on your prior experience, you have been identified as a suitable participant for future resolution events. Participation is voluntary.

Below that was a button.

Accept

I didn’t click it.

When I checked again this morning, the email had been archived.

The system marked it as Acknowledged.

⸻

I don’t know if the surveys actually improve anything.

But the system seems satisfied.

And apparently, so am I.

At least, that’s what the record says.

No further action is required.
