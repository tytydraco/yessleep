He was standing upright now, a disgusting man so tall he had to lower his head a little to fit underneath the doorpost. He took one step into the room and stopped. I could see his cheap catsuit caked in new layers of dirt, grime, and stains of dark liquid. The same type of dark liquid that covered his mouth and lips and was smeared across his cheek and stained his dirty blonde beard. I could see his limp member hanging between his legs in a grotesque mockery of decency. Worst of all, I could see the purposeful malice in his pale blue eyes, which were fixated on mine. Human and animalistic at the same time. He stood still as a statue in the middle of his living room. The stillness of a tiger ready to pounce.

“Back for more?” I growled defiantly. I knew I had locked myself in with the beast. I knew I couldn’t possibly outrun it, not with my sprained ankle. But I didn’t need to. Seeing this thing and being reminded of how it put its disgusting paws on my family brought back the rage I felt that evening. I had a feeling I was going to need it.

Tibbles moved. A slight bend of the knee to generate force. Then, with predatory purpose, he shot forward. And I was too slow.

Tibbles crashed into me, tackling me against the ground and pinning me down with its full weight. The bat clanked to the ground, outside of my reach. I coughed, air being pressed out of my lungs. I gasped for new air and was immediately greeted by the rank smell of sweat, blood, and decaying breath. Tibbles began growling. A deep, rumbling sound like rolling thunder. I tried to push him off but the thing was too strong and too heavy. It then raised itself up, sitting on top of me. The relief that I felt was short-lived as I saw Tibbles raise its claws. I shielded my face and torso with my arms as he began scratching me with long, dirty, yellow nails. They weren’t as sharp as a cat’s, but they were long, and the force behind them made them dangerous weapons all the same. I gritted my teeth as I felt Tibbles’s nails tear through the fabric of my pajamas and then through my skin. I was losing the fight. And I had to do something. In a lucky move, I managed to get ahold of Tibbles’s wrists, which allowed me to interrupt his scratching. At least for now.

Tibbles growled, trying to wrestle his arms from my grasp. Its pale eyes bulging, face a mask of animalistic fury. Then, it managed to wriggle its wrists from my grasp. Enough to grab my wrists instead. It pinned both my arms to the ground next to me and brought its face down to mine. I could feel its hot breath on my face as our faces practically touched. I knew what came next. I had watched my fair share of nature programs. Enough to know how most felids kill.

It brought its mouth to my neck. And bit down hard.

I wanted to cry out in pain, but no sound came out. Just a dry wheeze. I could feel warm blood rising in my throat, bubbling up into my mouth until it was filled with a coppery taste. It slipped through the gaps between my teeth. We lay there for a few moments. Me, the prey. Gurgling, feeling the life drain from me. Tibbles, the predator, using its canines and incisors to crush my throat. It let out another growl. Not a loud, angry, feral growl. But a softer one. Filled with self-satisfaction as if knowing the battle was already over. And the slightest hint of a chuckle.

And then, in a very human display of arrogance, it loosened its grip on my wrists ever so slightly. And it was enough for me to find the last bit of strength in my body and use it to reach for my keys…

...and jam the blade into the eye of the man in the catsuit.

Tibbles released his jaws and yowled in agony. The pressure on my throat and body was released, and I gasped for air. My body was grateful for whatever snippets I could inhale through my tattered throat with gurgled breaths. I grabbed the open wound with my free hand, feeling warm blood and pain. I pressed the wound closed as best as I could. I crawled back against the counter. In the living room, Tibbles was grasping its bleeding eye socket while thrashing, jowling, and hissing. Its flailing limbs knocking over furniture, its nails scratching across the floor. With my free hand I grasped the counter and pulled myself up, scanning among the flailing limbs and toppled furniture for my one salvation.

The bat.

I found it lying in the corner. I shuffled over to it. One hand still on my bleeding throat, I picked it up. Then, I shuffled towards the man in the catsuit. Tibbles was in the corner, clutching its wounded eye. Its shrieks and yowls had subsided into an agonized whimper. I towered over it, bat raised overhead. The man in the catsuit noticed me. He looked up to me, giving me a one-eyed glare filled with as much hate and malice as I had ever witnessed any creature possess, human or not.

I swung the bat against its head. It procured a sickening crack. I raised it and swung again. And again. And again. Until it stopped moving and then a few times more for good measure. Just to be certain.

When I was sure the man in the catsuit was dead, I rested my tired arm and let my weapon slip through my fingers. It landed on the floor with a dull clank. In a daze, I staggered to my phone and called 911. I was hardly able to communicate with the state of my throat. But luckily the person on the other side of the phone was savvy enough to understand that my gargled words and gurgling, wheezing breaths meant it was urgent. As I heard the sounds of sirens approaching from the distance, I sank to the ground. The fight was over, and my body was slowly beginning to catch up to this fact. I passed out.

I wish I could tell you that this story ends happily. Unfortunately, you don’t go through these sorts of events mentally unscathed. Starting with the physical damage to my throat and vocal cords. For the remainder of my life, I can only speak in raspy whispers.

When I got by in the hospital, Holly was by my side. I could see she was happy I was okay, but her face also revealed concern. Who could blame her? For years I had been her rock. One of the pillars that held up the stable life we had carved out for ourselves and our child. With everything that happened, that certainty was now taken from her. The accident that landed me in the hospital was ruled a psychotic episode. It didn’t explain my torn throat, but at this point, I knew explaining what really happened would be pointless. Whatever it was that decided to torment me was meant for me and me alone. Of course, they never found Tibbles when they arrived. Just me, unconscious and bleeding on my living room floor. 

After I left the hospital, I was checked into a mental facility. My willingness to accept and go along with any treatment they suggested made me the perfect patient. If Tibbles was really something my mind had created, I wanted it out. And I was willing to try any type of therapy or medication to make it happen.

I was fired from the hospital after only a few weeks.

I now sit at home, feeling slightly groggy from the new medication I’ve received. My wife is downstairs playing with Jenny. The project of getting our daughter a pet was put on indefinite hold. Getting things to return to normal is taking time. Time, work, and learning to trust my senses again for all three of us. I feel isolated. Coming face to face with terror is bad, but not being able to share my experiences with others and experience comfort from that is even worse. But I’m trying. Bit by bit, day by day, I am getting my family back. I’ll do what it takes. I’ll take my medication. I’ll attend every appointment with my therapist. I’ll ignore the dead rats I keep finding in front of the door. Rats with large chunks bitten out of them, far too large for any normal cat.
