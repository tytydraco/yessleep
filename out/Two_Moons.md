I never intended to end up working on a cargo ship.

Since I was sixteen, I'd worked in kitchens. Spending months away from home surrounded by nothing but ocean wasn't exactly how I imagined my life turning out. But when COVID hit and restaurants started shutting down, a job posting for a chef aboard a cargo vessel felt like a lifeline.

Surprisingly, I adjusted to life at sea pretty quickly.

The routine was simple. The crew was small but good company. Well, the only company for hundreds of miles in any direction, but good company nonetheless.

Everyone else onboard had been sailing for years by the time I arrived. They helped me settle into my new life, especially a deckhand named Tim.

We developed something of a ritual. Every evening after I'd finished cleaning the galley, I'd grab a pack of cigarettes and head out to the smoking area. More often than not, Tim would already be there waiting for me. He looked exactly how you'd imagine a sailor would look. Weathered face, scruffy beard, and enough tattoos to fill a sketchbook. While we smoked, he'd tell stories.

Stories about storms that appeared out of nowhere. About ships that vanished without sending distress calls. About strange lights moving beneath the water at night. Most of them sounded completely ridiculous.

I was pretty sure he just enjoyed messing with the new guy. Still, I liked listening. One night, after a particularly miserable shift, he told me about the two moons.

"Ever hear about sailors seeing a second moon reflected in the water?"

I laughed.

"No. Can't say I have."

Tim didn't laugh back.His expression remained completely serious.

"My grandfather sailed tankers," he said. "He used to say if you ever see two moons reflected in the water, you go inside."

"Why?"

Tim took a long drag from his cigarette and stared out into the darkness beyond the ship.

"Because it means something underneath is looking up at you."

I chuckled nervously.

"I'm serious."

I'd heard him tell dozens of stories before, but this was different. There wasn't even the slightest hint of a joke in his voice. I promised I'd follow his advice. A few minutes later, Tim finished his cigarette and headed back inside.

I wish I had done the same.

Instead, I sat down, lit another cigarette, and watched the moon hanging above the ocean. Tim's story kept replaying in my head. Eventually curiosity got the better of me. I stood up and walked to the railing. Maybe I wanted to prove the story was nonsense. Maybe I just wanted to ease my mind. Either way, I leaned over the edge and looked down. My blood ran cold. There were two moons reflected in the water. Not a trick of the light. Not a distorted reflection. Two distinct moons floating side by side on the dark surface of the ocean.

They were separated by only a few feet. I stared at them for several seconds, trying to make sense of what I was seeing. Despite everything Tim had just told me, I pulled out my phone and took a picture. When I checked the image, only one moon appeared in the water. The second was gone. That was enough for me. I shoved my phone into my pocket and practically ran back inside.

That night I woke to the sound of footsteps outside my cabin. At least, I thought they were footsteps. The sound was wrong somehow. Heavy. Wet. Each step landed with a dull thump against the metal floor. I checked the clock beside my bed. 3:07 AM. Grumbling, I pulled on a pair of shorts and opened the door. The corridor was empty. But through a nearby porthole I could see someone standing outside on deck. It was Tim. At least I thought it was. He was standing perfectly still near the railing. Not looking down at the water. Looking up at the sky.

I watched him for a few moments. Part of me considered going outside and telling him what I'd seen earlier. Instead, I decided it could wait until morning. I went back to bed. The next day Tim was gone. No one had seen him. At first we assumed he'd fallen overboard during the night. But that explanation didn't make much sense. Tim had spent most of his life at sea. He wasn't careless.
The captain reviewed the security footage. Nothing. No one had gone outside during the night. No one. I told them I'd seen Tim standing on deck around three in the morning.

The footage showed otherwise. There I was opening my cabin door. There I was staring out through the porthole. There I was standing there for almost a full minute. But nobody was outside. Nobody at all. After that, no one knew what to think. Weeks passed. People settled on whatever explanation made them most comfortable. Accident. Suicide. Some bizarre misunderstanding.


Life aboard the ship continued. I couldn't move on so easily. I never went outside alone at night again. When we finally returned to port, I handed in my notice. Maybe that sounds ridiculous. Maybe it was. But I didn't feel safe on that ship anymore. Eventually I put the whole experience behind me. Years passed. I stopped thinking about Tim. Stopped thinking about the second moon. Stopped thinking about that night entirely.

Until today. Tonight I was washing dishes when I happened to glance down into the sink. The basin was filled with water. Reflected on the surface were two moons.

Not one.

Two.

I looked out the window immediately. There was only one moon in the sky. I know what that means. I know something is looking up at me again.

If you're reading this, then I was right.

Please, be smarter than I was.

If you ever see two moons reflected in the water.

Leave.
