I moved into the apartment at the end of October, right when Cleveland starts to feel like it’s been drained of color. The wind off the lake had that sharp, metallic bite that made every walk outside feel shorter than it should’ve been.

The building was wedged between a laundromat and a used bookstore with a hand-painted sign in the window. Across the street was a coffee shop that stayed open late, the kind with one cracked neon sign in the window and chairs that never matched. A corner carryout sat on the other side of the block, and every night the smell of fried food drifted down the sidewalk like it was trying to hide something.

I took the apartment because it was cheap and close to work. That was all there was to it. The building was old, the hallways were dim, and the front door stuck unless you pulled it hard enough. The landlord, a man named Mr. Havel, kept the keys on a ring so large it looked like it belonged to a prison guard.

My apartment was 4C, at the end of the hall. Across from me was 4B.

The lease said it was occupied, but I never saw anyone go in or out.

When I asked Mr. Havel about it, he waved a hand like I’d asked about the weather.

“People keep odd hours,” he said.

That was the first time I should’ve left.

# Day One

The first night passed without incident. I ordered a sandwich from the corner carryout, listened to traffic drift through the cracked window, and fell asleep with the TV still on.

The next morning, I opened my door and saw that 4B was open.

Not wide open. Just enough for a narrow strip of darkness to show through.

I stopped in the hall and stared at it for a while. The building was otherwise quiet, except for the radiator ticking and the faint hum of the light above the stairs. I waited for someone to come out. Nobody did.

By the time I got home that evening, the door was shut again.

I told myself I’d imagined it.

# Day Two

The second day was normal in the way ordinary things are always normal right before they stop being normal.

I grabbed coffee from the shop across the street, sat through a long day of emails, and came home after dark with my hands numb from the cold.

When I stepped into the hall, I heard three knocks.

Slow. Measured. Right across from my apartment.

I looked up.

4B was closed.

No light. No movement. Nothing.

I stood there for a few seconds, waiting for the sound to repeat itself, but it didn’t.

That night, at 3:17 AM, I woke up to the smell of wet dirt.

It was strong enough to pull me fully awake. Not mold, not sewer gas — dirt, like someone had just dug something up in the middle of the room.

Then came the knocking again.

Three times.

This time it was from the wall beside my bed.

I sat up so fast I hit my shoulder on the headboard.

The knocking stopped.

I stayed awake until sunrise.

# Day Three

The next morning, I went downstairs and asked a woman who worked at the bookstore if she knew anything about the apartment across from mine.

She gave me a strange look and said, “The one across the hall?”

I nodded.

She frowned for a second, then said, “That unit’s been empty for a long time.”

I told her I’d seen the door open.

She didn’t answer right away. Then she looked past me toward the building and said, “Don’t stand in the hall after dark.”

I asked why.

She just shook her head and went back inside.

That evening, I checked the door myself.

4B was closed, but when I bent down, I saw dirt packed into the seam at the bottom.

Fresh dirt.

I don’t know why that bothered me more than the knocks. It just did.

I went to bed with every light in the apartment on.

# Day Four

At 3:16 AM, the air in my apartment changed.

That’s the only way I can describe it. The room felt colder, and the smell of dirt came back, stronger than before.

At 3:17, someone knocked on my front door.

Three times.

I didn’t move.

Then I heard a voice from the hallway.

Very quiet. Very tired.

“Wrong one,” it said.

I got up and looked through the peephole.

The hallway was empty.

But 4B was open.

And standing in the doorway was a woman in a gray cardigan.

She had her back turned to me.

She stood still for a long time, like she was listening to something inside the apartment. Then she turned her head just enough for me to see part of her face.

Pale skin. Dark circles under her eyes. No expression at all.

“You took too long,” she said.

# Day Five

I should have called the police.

Instead I stood there in the dark, unable to move, listening to the building make its usual noises — pipes, floorboards, the old elevator groaning somewhere below me.

Then I heard something behind me.

A soft scrape from inside my apartment.

I turned.

My bedroom door was open.

I knew I had shut it.

The room beyond it was dark except for a thin line of light under the closet door, and for a second I thought maybe I’d left it open.

Then I saw movement.

Slow.

Careful.

Like something trying not to be noticed.

I backed away toward the front door, but the hall was no longer empty.

Three people stood there.

Then four.

I hadn’t heard anyone approach.

They were all looking at my apartment door.

Not at me.

At the brass number plate.

I looked down.

It didn’t say 4C anymore.

It said **4B**.

I don’t remember opening the door.

I only remember standing in the hall barefoot, staring at my apartment behind me.

It was wide open.

The inside was wrong.

The furniture was covered in sheets. The walls were older. The air smelled like wet earth and something sweet underneath it, like flowers left too long in a sealed room. The hallway beyond stretched farther than my apartment should have allowed, disappearing into darkness.

Then I heard knocking from inside.

Three slow knocks.

I took one step back.

That was when the woman in the cardigan appeared in front of me.

I didn’t see her move.

She was close enough that I could smell the dirt on her coat.

She looked past me into the hall, then back at my face.

“You’re in the right place now,” she said.

Behind her, my apartment door began to close.

I ran.

I don’t remember the stairs, or the lobby, or how I made it outside. I only remember ending up on the sidewalk in the cold with no shoes on, staring up at the building like it had always been empty.

I didn’t go back that night.

I spent the night in my car near the coffee shop across the street, and when I woke up, my phone had thirteen missed calls from Mr. Havel.

I didn’t call him back.

The next morning I checked into a motel outside the city and tried to convince myself the whole thing had been a stress blackout.

Then the desk clerk handed me my room key.

It had a brass tag on it.

**4B**
