Have you been on a dementia ward? For me it started a few weeks ago. 

I work as a painter and i had just came back from a holiday in turkey newly engaged. Honestly, I was high on life so when my contract manager asked me to work night shift at Green Leaf care home I didn’t think twice. 

Our job for the next few weeks was to redecorate the hallways that connect all the rooms. It was only one floor, the third floor and it was on the dementia ward. I’ve worked in this care home before during the day, painting the staircases and I never had any issues with the residents. 

The first night working we were all told by the staff to always keep arms distance away from the residents as sometimes their moods can be unpredictable and they’d hate to see us get hurt. So of course I’d always make sure to keep my distance even if that meant I’d have to paint around them at times. Most of them were harmless and just wanted to steal our brushes only to drop them a meter or so away. But I quickly grew a soft spot for one.

She’s called Barbra. I first met her when she was humming a tune I love and after a while she showed interest in me. It started small, like she would call me handsome and tell me I missed a spot just to wind me up. I felt kind of sorry for her, nobody would visit her. No family, not even her kids. I was slightly confused as to why she was on this ward because she seemed pretty normal to me, compared to all the other folks who would walk around aimlessly and always looked so confused.

This was until I was walking down the hall to get more paint and I saw her approaching quickly towards me. Before I had time to think she had already grabbed my hands. My mind was racing and I wanted to gently get out of this situation until she started gently singing. 

She sang ‘you are my sunshine’ and before I knew it my colleagues were around me joining in. Mocking me. Honestly, I thought it was sweet. She must have been in a moment in time where she sang this with a lover or some memory that was bliss for her. I kindly said “thank you” and resumed back to my duties for the rest of that night. It left a warm feeling in my chest like I helped someone come back for even a moment. 

The thought stuck with me and I wanted my fiancée to think back on lovely times like that when she’s just as old. So, I started singing ‘you are my sunshine’ on quiet weekend nights under the low lights of the kitchen while we slowly swayed together, sipping on a glass of red. 

The next time I saw Barbara was into the second week of me working nights. She seemed a little more lost and confused than normal so I just assumed she was having a bad day. But it felt more than just that. You see, she started watching me. Not just to admire the painting like everyone else, it was like she was studying me. During this week I realised why she was on this ward as she would mumble random things especially to us painters. One night she approached me and said “you’re my husband” and in the next breath she went to my colleague and said “you’re my husband.” I teased him by saying “why is my Barbara cheating on me with you.” I got a giggle out of him and we carried on with the night as usual. 

The next night Barbra seemed unsettled. And each time I went to the staircases (which was keycard locked) Barbra would stand at the glass and tap gently. Some nights it would unease me and I’d stay on the stairs just a bit longer until she left. But she never did, there was nothing behind her eyes but they would always find me. 

At this point I’d try to avoid her on her more confused days as I didn’t know how she’d act. I was midway through painting a skirting board when I had that burning feeling that someone was watching me. As I turned around, I saw Barbra staring at me,eyes wide, from a corner at the bottom of the hall. Before I could process it she disappeared. At this point it was 2:30am and I was due to leave at 3 so I just started to pack my equipment until I saw a shadow looming onto the wall just above my head. I spun around quick nearly dislocating my neck and standing right above me was Barbra. 

I confidently said “Hiya barb” masking my unease. She smiled “you were so romantic proposing to me on that white sanded beach in that lovely hot country y’know.” my gut dropped and all I could muster up was a low “what.” She said “don’t you remember silly, it was the best night of my life. We ate fish and drank a lot of wine then you took me to the beach when it was dark so nobody was around. You knew me so well, that I didn’t want an audience for such a delicate moment.” 

My heart dropped to my feet and I was speechless. How did she know this? And then it clicked, my colleagues. So I kindly excused myself from Barbra and stormed to where the other painters had been slacking off. “Why’d you put a vulnerable woman up to a sick joke? It probably would’ve been funny in any other circumstance but do you not think this is sorta crossing a line?” 

Their reaction was just pure confusion and when I explained the situation they just chalked it up to me reminding her of a past lover and how it’s common to get engaged on a beach. But the details were too precise, I didn’t believe them. 

I lay awake until Laura’s alarm at 6am went off. She knew something was up the moment she woke up so I told her the whole story and how unhappy I was with my colleges. Her reaction was to try and wind me down so she said “I don’t want to be competing with some other woman now.” That got a genuine smile from me and I got out of bed to listen to her about her days at work while she got ready for the day. Before I knew it I was back at Green Leaf care home getting everything ready for the long night. 

Only a few hours into the night I felt a familiar presence lurking in the corners of the hallway. She was dead set on me but too shy to approach. I thought this time I’d keep looking over my shoulder so she wouldn’t catch me off guard this time. Every time I peeked behind me she’d shift closer until she was only a few metres away. My gut told me to get out of there so I slipped into the stairwell. This time she didn’t tap, she closed her fist and slammed the window once. Then again. And again. Until both hands were slamming the window. Her eyes began to stream and she screamed “why did you leave me?” “come back to me!” Just before I ran downstairs she started slamming her head along with her hands against the window, creating a stream of blood running down the freshly painted door. By the time I got to the front door I could hear nurses helping her and before long an ambulance arrived. 

I was there when they rolled her out, I was standing alone in the dark night smoking a cigarette. The paramedics gently pushed her to the ambulance, stones popping underneath the weight of the carrier. Her eyes locked onto mine and with a sorrowful and  empathetic face quietly said to me “I’m sorry we couldn’t have our own children.” 

By the time the ambulance had left my eyesight the end of my cigarette had burnt out. I stood in silence. Nobody knew that me and Laura couldn’t have our own kids. Not even the other painters. 

Nights went by and Barbra was nowhere to be seen. Obviously I felt uneasy around her but I still wanted to know if she was alright. I kept most of this to myself as I didn’t even believe it Nevermind trying to explain it without sounding like a paranoid. So, the nights went back to normal and I had the usual bystanders watching me paint. 

A week or so went by and we were wrapping up the rest of the work. We had one night left and then it would be back to the day shift. Towards the end of the night I saw a familiar face wrapped carefully in bandages with a big bright smile and she started shuffling towards me. “Hello handsome” she said. With a big smile I replied “hello beautiful, how’s the head?” She tapped it gently and for a second the recognition of what happened crossed her face “ I’m sorry honey I didn’t mean to scare you, it’s just ever since you died 20 years ago seeing you again in your painting overalls just made me want to cross over into eternity with you.”

And that’s when it clicked.

Laura Barbra Shirley.
