Everyone’s gaze was in a different place.

Bryce’s was nowhere. Staring vacantly forward into the air with his eyes full of fear and jaw slack in confusion. The screams of Mindy shrieking from the GoPro had stopped almost 10 minutes ago, but I had no doubt they echoed through his ears still, locking him in place like a deer in headlights, hoping that if he just remained still, nothing in this nightmare we’d become enraptured in could hurt him.

Lacey’s eyes had fixed back on her brother’s bloodstain. The one that was now buried beneath bits of pale flesh and oily puddles of bile. Despite this, I knew she could see it clearly. Its shape and image was undoubtedly seared into her brain upon seeing it for the first time. She may not have been there for Casey’s death, but now she’d gotten to see from a like perspective just how horrifying it was for him.

Carly had fully turned in her seat to stare out one of the tiny basement windows, the late afternoon sun shafting its beams through the murky glass and illuminating the dust particles dancing there. She almost stared with a sense of longing. A desire to climb through it and run far, far away from the Manor. From the monsters within, from the curse it had placed on us, and from the painful burden of everything we now had to carry.

Me; my eyes were where they wouldn’t stray from anytime I was in the basement after that day. Fixed squarely on the red door. Another hour was approaching, and every passing second I anticipated it swinging open again. Unleashing another unfathomable horror onto us that we would have to put down before it managed to steal away and erase another one of our friends from existence.

How had Mindy sealed it, I wondered? How had she managed to make it stay shut in the video? The image of the strange gestures she made over the knob flashed through my mind over and over again as I stared at our reflection in its black mirror, wondering if I could just recreate it, would that be enough? Or was there another component needed? A secret locked away in the depths of the labyrinth beyond that she had found in her time trapped there?

There were eyes in every angle of the room, all of us unsure what else to do, but the one place we wordlessly swore off looking was at Kait. The girl just stared tearfully down at the camera in her hands, clutching it tightly as if it was the most important thing in the world. In many ways, it was.

The camera clearly held answers. The key to our escape from this mess. Clues to what the door might be and what sort of evil lay behind it. Hints to what might have become of Casey, and why the monsters took him some place we couldn’t find. Right now, it was all we had to guide us forward; a direction that was harder to move the more we became beaten down and thrashed in our hope.

None of that was what made the camera important, though. What made it important was that it was one of the last surviving relics of Mindy; the only proof to the world that she had at one point made marks across its cruel surface.

The sickening part was, even with this proof, if we showed it to somebody, their brains wouldn’t comprehend it. Their minds would fog over and expel the information right back out, leaving a hole where the poor girl had once been.

I think that’s why Kait was clutching it with white knuckles. This had already happened to her once. She didn’t want to let it happen again.

“I… I knew her…” she finally spoke, her tone barely audible. “She knew me somehow, and I just forgot…”

“Kait, it’s not your fault,” Carly softly offered, “This place did this to you—to all of us. If she knew you, maybe she knew us too. Maybe… maybe we all forgot.”

It was meant to be a reassurance—to take some of the pain off Kait—but the thought only made us all feel more ill.

“M-Maybe she was talking about a different Kaitlynn,” Bryce said, “It’s a common name—it could have been a coincidence.”

“Don’t,” Kait pleaded softly, “I appreciate it, but…” Her words fizzled out, and she worked at her cheeks with her palms to dry them, “It’s not a coincidence. It can’t be. It all adds up. We were trying to figure out how I knew about this place, and, well… Now we do.”

That set a silence over us, as well as a wordless implication that pressed heavy on Kait. She had brought us here. She blamed herself. I may have been the one who opened the door, but in Kait’s mind, the door would have never risked being open if she hadn’t suggested the red manor in the first place.

The worst part was, though we didn’t know what sort of silent voice was talking to Mindy in her final moments, the context between her words was evident. The girl had thought she had won by sealing the door, but the power that lurked in the dark seemed to taunt her with a backup plan. Someone else who it could lure in to break the seal once more.

Kait.

Her voice broke as she went to speak again, the weight finally crushing her, “She said that I wouldn’t do it… she didn’t think I’d be stupid enough to come here. I let her down. I don’t even know who she is anymore, and yet she had all that faith in me and I—I—”

“Kait,” I started firmly, trying to draw her eyes to me. It worked briefly, and I continued, “Carly is right. You didn’t know. This place made you forget Mindy, and by proxy, any sort of red flags you could have known about this place. Even if she had warned you in some way before this, I don’t think you would have remembered. None of this is your fault. This place is messing with us.”

They weren’t my most reassuring words, but it seemed to at least stop Kait from hyperventilating. Give her new thoughts to turn over before she succumbed to total guilt once more. There were a lot more thoughts than just those that she needed to consider, though—we all did.

“What did she do to that door?” Bryce asked after giving Kait some room to breathe, “She did something to it in that video, and it stayed shut—did you all see that?”

“Yeah,” I agreed, eyes returning to the black, shiny eye of the sinister red presence.

Carly spoke next, but her words were unsteady and ended before they finished, “Do you think she…”

We all turned to her, showing that she had our attention to continue, but her face went a little sheepish.

“Do we think she what?” Prodded Lacey.

“I-I’m not sure—it just sounds so crazy, but…”

“C, with everything we’re dealing with, nothing sounds crazy.” I told her.

Carly nodded, “It looked like she… cast some sort of spell or something? The way she moved her hands over the knob and was touching the door—it just seemed too intentional, you know?”

“There were no words or anything, though,” I noted, “And she didn’t put anything on the knob—wouldn’t a spell take more than just hand gestures?”

Carly raised a brow, “What, are you a wizard now?”

I lightly tossed my hands in surrender, “No, I guess not.”

Lacey eyed the door warily, “Well, if we could figure out how she did it, it looks like we could keep that door shut for good.”

The girl was only half right in a sense. Yes, Mindy’s odd gestures did something to the barrier to keep it locked up against the force that wanted out, but what followed was a clear indicator that even if we did figure out the odd ritual the girl performed on the door, something on the other side wasn’t going to go down without a fight.

It had found a way to get it back open once before, and it could do it again…

The discomfort that tainted the air showed that we all knew this, but Bryce tried to dispel it with unfounded hope, “Maybe we won’t have to. Maybe that second monster was the only other thing haunting the door.”

It didn’t work. The lion creature had been on the more feral side, and though its avian brother seemed to be a little more calculating, it hadn’t displayed an ability to communicate. Maybe it had been the thing talking to Mindy in the dark before it pounced on her, but the fact of the matter was, there was another presence in the hall that we hadn’t yet seen, which only meant more threats the next time the clock decided to give a death chime.

I did my best to explain this to my friends in a way that wouldn’t tank our spirits any deeper than they already were, but most of them had already assumed this for themselves. Hearing me say it aloud only made their anxiety worse.

“Well, then, figuring out how to seal the door it is,” Carly sighed with a shudder.

Lacey gently set her hands over Kait’s and spoke softly, covering the camera in the process, “Can I see this, Kait?”

Kait’s gaze didn’t stir as she nodded softly and released the device.

The girl began playing the beginning half of the last recording once again, watching for the exact method Mindy had used to keep the red door closed. I saw Kait wince at her roommate’s gentle utterances of reassurance to herself, so certain that she would succeed in her goal…

The rest of us watched over Lacey’s shoulder as Mindy began her gestures again, stepping out of the hall into the basement just enough to interact with both sides of the knob. As she drew circles over the black porcelain and weaved fingers through the air, we tried to commit it to memory, Carly even practicing the motions for herself alongside her.

It went on this way until Mindy started to shut the door again, at which point Lacey quickly restarted the video so that Kait wouldn’t have to hear the finale all over again.

Bryce spoke as the second loop began, “Where did she even learn to do this? Do you think she somehow knew before, or did she find something beyond the door while she was trapped?”

“It had to be in the halls,” Carly shook her head, “If she’d known beforehand, why would it have taken her so long to do it?”

Bryce shrugged, “Well, to be fair, when she first showed up, she didn’t really seem interested in closing it. It was what she was looking for, so obviously she wanted something inside. If she knew enough about the door to seek it out, though, she might have already known how to close it as a safety. Maybe she kept that information somewhere.”

It wasn’t a bad theory by any means, but Kait finally decided to speak, and when she did, her words hit us all like a sucker punch.

“None of it matters,” she announced coldly, fresh tears glistening across her cheeks.

We all exchanged a glance, and Bryce decided to take the lead, “Kait, of course this matters. If we can figure out how to seal this thing, then—”

“No, it doesn’t.” Kait cut him off, once again a dour tone haunting her voice, “We can figure out how to do whatever ritual she did to seal the door, but in the end it wouldn’t matter. You all are forgetting a very important step in the process.”

“What do you mean?” Carly nervously asked.

“Mindy could have left, but she didn’t,” my friend told us, swallowing hard and looking to the ceiling. “She was right at the exit, but instead of going, she stayed to do the ritual, then went back in. You all saw the video before that—if she was so eager to get out, why would she have not just sealed it from the outside?”

We all knew where she was going with this, and none of us liked it.

“Maybe something behind the door still had a hold on her. It compelled her to stay,” Lacey offered desperately, “She was in some sort of trance when she first arrived, and at the end she was talking to something that wasn’t there.”

Kait shook her head, “No, if that was the case, she wouldn’t have been boasting to it about winning. Come on, guys, let’s not pretend here…” Her red, puffy eyes finally lifted from the ground to meet ours, and there was pure pain behind them, “If we want to lock away whatever is doing this to us? Whatever it is that took Casey and killed Mindy? Somebody is going to have to seal the door from the inside…”

The silence that followed was palpable; thick as the stench of death from the rotting corpses in the corner. We had come here to kill the monster that had hurt Casey and to bring his body home, but part of our goal had also been to stop whatever curse had been laid over us and the town.

This was quickly proving to be a much deeper sickness than could be cured by shotgun slugs and swings from an axe, but even with knowledge of a cure, Kaitlynn was right. It was a terminal sickness.

Whatever force lay beyond the door, it needed external influence to free it in the first place. I may not have known anything about how the accursed thing worked, but by its own twisted logic, it made morbid sense that the door would require a sacrifice to shut it once again.

Unless we were willing to give up one of our lives, we were now stuck watching over this door, lest the things on the other side escape and wither the world beyond away.

I was prepared to do it. Staring once again at the chipped sanguine boards, I could have jumped up in that moment and given my best recreation of whatever spell Mindy had cast. It was my fault the damn thing had been opened in the first place; if there was anyone who deserved to be sealed inside, it was me.

Two things halted me, however. The first was that we didn’t have all the puzzle pieces yet, and I doubted flailing my hand around the knob was going to do much. The second was that I suspected I would be tackled to the floor by my friends before I could make it even two steps. Hell, Lacey might even shoot my legs out from under me if it meant stopping my advance. She had already lost her brother; she wasn’t about to lose the only other family she had left to this maw into hell.

That only left me with one option; the same we’d condemned ourselves to so far. Press on. See if there was a light at the end of this dark, floral-lined tunnel.

I stood slowly, and absently moved for the door, to which Kait confirmed my theory from moments ago. Her hand shot out so fast and gripped my wrist so tightly that I thought she might tear through the tendons.

I turned back and slipped my hand over hers, “Hey, it’s okay—easy,” I softly reassured, “Sorry; I’m not trying anything. I just want to look at it.”

“Jessie…” Carly warned in agreement with Kait.

I put my non-viced hand up in further submission, “Bryce is right. Whoever Mindy was, she knew about this door before she arrived here. That means there must be more clues as to what it is hidden somewhere.”

Kait gave me a skeptical look, then slowly loosened her grip. I gave her a tiny, grateful smile, then moved to the door. My fingers traced over the slick ebony sphere, heart thudding steadily against my chest as I challenged the force living the red-coated knots of oak.

“The spell she sealed it with can’t be the only way. She was panicked in her videos, which means that her solution was probably a desperate one. If we dig deeper, I’m sure we can find another way out of this.”

“Where should we start?” Carly asked softly, “If there're answers, they have to be in those halls, and there’s no way in hell any of you are going back in there.”

“We don’t have to,” I told her, “This door is inside of this house, and I doubt that the Manor was just set dressing.”

“The front door,” Kait said softly, doing her best to ride the hope I was trying to supply, “it was carved up with a lot of mythological imagery. Whoever lived here had to have had some interest in the supernatural.”

“That’s not even mentioning the library,” Lacey added.

“There’s still Mindy too,” Bryce offered, speaking into his palm as he thought aloud, “We may not be able to see what she did behind the door, but we have her car still. Maybe there’s more than the camera in there that we could work off of. We might even be able to figure out where she came from before… well… you know…”

I could tell he was trying not to put Kait on the spot, but the way his eyes darted to her finished his thought loud and clear.

‘Where she came from before the whole world forgot her.’

As hard as it was to stomach, he had a point. Given her disheveled state at the beginning of her final logs, Mindy had been torn up over the Red Manor for some time before coming here. If we could find any sort of fossils from her past—relics like Casey’s old room that were now pockets of time halted in human history—there might be more answers.

The idea made that now all-too-familiar sick feeling begin gnawing at my stomach once again. I thought of Mindy’s mom, whom she’d called for in her final video, not even remembering that she had a daughter whom she’d raised for over twenty years. I hoped that Mindy had moved out; I couldn’t stomach the thought of her family moving past her vacant room from day to day with blissful, unaware smiles on their faces.

That’s when the thought struck me like a bolt of lightning. Mindy didn’t live with her parents—she had a roommate. Her friends had mentioned that in the first video that we’d seen. It could have just been a coincidence, but the information felt important, and when I turned around to look at my friend, her eyes still watery and looking at me with pure grief, my brain connected the dots.

“Kait, you used to have a roommate, right?” I asked her.

She hesitated, then fixed me with a quizzical look, “N-No, I’ve always lived alone, ever since I moved out.”

I pursed my lips, uncertain of how to break the news I was about to give her, “I… I don’t think that’s true—I remember you telling me that you weren’t going to be able to afford to live on your own with how much you were making when you moved out. That was why you asked me to—”

The words crammed up in my throat as Kait’s dazzling, melancholy eyes fixed mine. There was already too many terrible thoughts plaguing the cold basement, and I didn’t think either of us wanted a reminder of that night at the school playground… besides, my words seemed to spark recollection in her.

“Those people in that video,” I continued, “the first one at the mall; they had asked where Mindy’s roommate was and why she hadn’t invited her. The roommate was an urban explorer, like her, from the sound of it.”

Kait eyed me carefully, her brain trying to play catch up, but once it did, her eyes widened, “N-No, that’s not—she couldn’t have been…”

I didn’t like pressing salt harder into her forgotten wound, especially if she might have lived with the girl, but all the pieces seemed to line up.

Kaitlynn must have been Mindy’s former roommate.

I continued to just stare at her somberly, hoping that she wouldn’t make me sink the dagger deeper by spelling it out, and thankfully she conceded. Burying her face into her hands, Kait let a shuddering, weak breath filter past her fingers.

“Oh, God…” the girl whimpered, “How could I forget someone I lived with? She seemed so kind; how could she just fall out of existence?”

Lacey scooted closer and slipped and hand over her back, “Kait, remember what we told you. You couldn’t have fought this. None of us could. I… I would have even forgotten Casey if the circumstances had been any different. We don’t know what this is, and we can’t blame ourselves for it.”

The mention of Casey seemed to remind Kait that while she was feeling lost in the moment, Lacey was still mourning her twin, and it prompted her to try and be strong. The girl sat up and gulped down her shudders, resting her hands on her thighs and doing her best to look poised. We all gave her a beat to compose herself, and once she had, she was back to the usual, fierce Kaitlynn I knew.

“I need to go home,” she said confidently, but the way her eyes fixed the floor told me she was terrified, “If she used to be there with me, I need to see her room for myself. Besides, if answers are anywhere, they would be there.”

Lacey nodded in support, “Of course. Whatever you need to do.”

I agreed with her, but couldn’t help but quickly cut in, “Not alone, though.”

Their eyes turned to me, and I went sheepish, looking down at my axe handle, “Whatever this place did to her, it was able to do it even with the door shut. That means it could affect her from your apartment. If there’s something lingering around there, I don’t want it getting to you too.”

Kait seemed to weigh my words carefully, her eyes quivering as she looked me up and down. She nodded slowly, then asked, “Will you come with me?”

I felt a lump in my throat and a deep pang in my chest. A nauseating sense of déjà vu overwhelmed me; Kaitlynn asking me the same question she had so long ago. The context was incredibly different from then, but the desperation in her voice was all the same, and this time, I wasn’t going to resist its draw.

“Of course.” I told her.

Bryce shifted nervously, “But… the door… what if it—”

We all jumped as, right on cue, the clock chimes rang out. I hefted my axe in my hands, then took a few cautious steps back, ready to see the red barrier unlatch itself and gust open. We waited one chime, then two, but by the fifth, I lowered my weapon.

Only when they stopped completely did I dare breathe a sigh of relief.

 Bryce did the same, then finished his thought, “What if the next time, something else comes through?”

“We’ll handle it,” Lacey said confidently, clutching the gun tight, “We don’t even know if there’s more for sure, and if there is, we put down the last ones with only a few of us. This could be important. Jess, Kait, you guys go see the apartment and look for answers there. We’ll stick here and check the library.”

I nodded and turned to Kait, who stood and spoke, “We’d better get moving. We have a long drive.”

I agreed, but there was one last matter of business to address before we departed. I turned to my three comrades, whom I hated to leave here alone, hoping to offer them the smallest extra bit of hope. I didn’t know exactly how to put it, so I did so bluntly.

“While we’re gone, keep an eye out for a little girl. She may be able to help you.”

It was a good reminder how poor my social skills were. Carly looked at me like I’d just spoken another language, “What?”

I looked back at the door, then explained to everyone what I’d seen back in the halls; my little ghost friend who had helped to guide us back to safety, and the vision that had come along with her. On any other day it would sound like I was going crazy, but we were far past that threshold by now.

“Holy shit…” Carly muttered.

“Do we know if she’s safe?” Bryce questioned, “I mean, sure she helped you, but this house clearly knows how to mess with people. What if it’s luring us into a false sense of security? Trying to get *all* of us into the halls?”

“She might be,” I told him, “But right now, she’s the only friend we have in this place. She may have looked scary, but… She seemed scared herself. I don’t think Mindy was even close to the first person who’d been trapped here.”

My words landed somberly over the crowd, and they lingered that way until Lacey changed the subject, “Jessie, do you know what triggered that vision? That’s weird that we were all together, but it only happened to you.”

She meant it with concern, but the question was a little poorly timed. Given we were only moments ago talking about strange forces of the Manor affecting the mind, I didn’t want my friends thinking I was succumbing to some sort of influence from the house.

Then again, maybe I was…

It only gave me all the more reason to get out of there in that moment and far, far away, so I gave my most reassuring smile, “We can figure that out when we get back. One mystery at a time.”

Nodding at Kaitlynn, we both ascended the stairs, eyeing our friends through the cracks in the steps until they were fully out of sight.

 When we reached the yard, I tried to hurry Kait along so that she wouldn’t spend too long staring at Mindy’s abandoned car in the driveway. I’m sure she was trying to imagine what it might have looked like parked in her own back home.

When we got int my truck and I started the engine, it dawned on me that I didn’t actually know where Kaitlynn lived. I knew the city, but I had never gotten the chance to go visit her like I’d hoped…

She told it to me vacantly while I punched it into my phone, as if the words and numbers that made up her dwelling were nothing but a bitter, broken lie. Once we began off, the cab was quiet save for the sound of the engine’s drone.

I wanted to search for words to comfort her, but I knew that it was a pointless endeavor. I’d known Kait my whole life, long enough to know when she just wanted silence. Her thoughts were already full, and she didn’t need me trying to cram more into them.

Instead, I lost myself to the road and the circumstances we were tangled in, thinking about the Red Manor and the horrible abilities it had, and Mindy’s screams and the way Casey’s eyes had met mine in the final moments.

They were all heavy in my mind, but in that moment, my empathy toward Kait was too strong, and my thoughts were continuously drawn back to her. Oddly, it wasn’t toward the current situation, though. It was long before that, a curse that had befallen us many years before we opened the scarlet door.

It had been a rainy day outside, and I sat in the back halls of the gymnasium wing of our school, waiting for Kaitlynn. When the bad weather came, it was always the place we met up to smoke since the school roof would leave us drenched. There was a back door we could pop open and puff out of—much riskier in regards to getting caught, but it was always worth it for the short reprieve together.

Around 10 minutes passed without my friend showing, however, and I began to worry that Kait may have gotten caught cutting class or something. Meandering back down the halls, I casually passed by her classroom door and glanced through the window, finding her seat empty. It wasn’t like her to no-show without telling me, so I immediately knew something was up.

Unsure where else to look, I moved back through the school until I reached the usual door we met at; the back side of the cafeteria. Pushing through, I pulled my coat higher onto my shoulders and hugged the wall, moving along it until I reached the space for the dumpsters.

Putting my foot on a milk crate that the lunch ladies probably set on for *their* smokes, I vaulted onto the recycle dumpster, then planted my foot on the concrete wall concealing the trashes. From there, it was easy to climb the remaining distance to the rooftop.

There she sat in our usual place: hugged up against the wall of the gymnasium and trying to keep her cigarette alight against the blowing rain. I stepped softly toward her, not wanting the good students below to hear the phantoms that stalked at fourth period. Her sensitive ears still heard me over the patter of rain, and she turned to me, eyes tired but warm.

She scooted, and I silently sat beside her, pulling my jacket off and swooping it over her head. She didn’t have one herself, and she was already soaked in her jeans and short-cut grunge tee, but I figured I could at least spare her from more punishment. She accepted it awkwardly, eyes to the floor, but once I was beside her, she tented it over both our heads, then scooted close so that we’d both fit.

She handed me the cig, and I took a puff before speaking, “You had me worried.”

“Sorry. I had a feeling you’d know where I was if I didn’t show, though.”

“What brought you up here? I don’t know if you noticed, but it’s raining.”

Kait smirked and took the cigarette back, “Really? I hadn’t, actually.”

“Everything alright?”

“Yeah. Just didn’t want to be down there right now.”

I nodded, understanding the solemn implications behind her words and realizing this was one of those times Kait wanted silence. I nuzzled closer to her, just so she knew I was there, then gave it to her.

Together, we watched as a raven appeared on the far side of the roof, perching on the lip and cocking its head curiously at the two of us.

‘You’re not supposed to be up here.’ It seemed to say.

Soon after, another one stopped to perch beside it. Two pairs of beings silently staring at one another, getting soaked in the rain.

Apparently their silence made Kait realize she was over the concept, and she spoke after passing me the next drag.

“So how was it?”

I turned to her with a curious look.

“Sleeping with me. Everything you hoped for?”

My face probably went red as a stop sign as I met her flirty eyes, and I don’t recall how long I was sitting in stunned silence before I could answer.

“H-Huh?”

Kait snickered and looked forward, speaking sarcastically, “You know, the crazy sex we had in the school locker room last month?”

I still couldn’t properly articulate words, “What are you—? We never—”

Kait saw me nearly having a stroke, and she let out another laugh, finally sparing me from the embarrassment, “Don’t worry, hun. I can also ask Matthew D. He and I did it in the diner parking lot just last Monday. And Brian K. when we met in the park the other night, and Jonas, and Kyle, and Marcus and…”

Kait shut herself up with the butt of the cigarette, staring off at the birds as she let her unspoken list continue on.

I breathed a deep sigh (half out of relief now that I knew what she was talking about) and shifted awkwardly, “They’re still spreading shit about you, huh?”

Kait nodded, then snickered darkly, “I don’t get what I did wrong, Jess. I keep to myself half the time, and when I am a bitch to people, it’s when they start shit first. I feel like no matter what I do, they just hate me. Just because my mom—”

Kait thought better about finishing her thought, then shut herself up again with a drag.

I waited for her to finish, then stole it from her, pulling nicotine into my lungs and joining her gaze with the ravens, “You haven’t done anything wrong. I don’t know if you’ve noticed, Kait, but people around here are dumb. And I say that as someone *from* around here.”

She laughed at that; a warm, gentle giggle that made my tired bones feel young with new life. My eyes met her warm pools, and I smiled at her.

“You’re not dumb, Jess.” She told me.

I eyed her hand cautiously as it rested on her thigh, then moved for it, engulfing it with my own and squeezing it tight, “You’re different, Kait—in the best way possible. You rebel, you’re artistic, you don’t buy into the bullshit that everyone else does in this shitty town. You’re different, and people around here don’t like different. They like the same old boring shit.”

Kait smiled, and nodded, but bit her cheek after a moment, “Thanks, Jess, but I think it’s a little more complicated than that…”

I knew she was right, and I knew what she was referring to, but I didn’t deny it. I just nodded and squeezed her hand again, “I’m sorry you have to deal with that.”

“It’s okay,” Kait shrugged. “I… I love her, Jess, y’know? I love her so much, and I know she does it because it’s how she keeps us fed, but…” Kait looked up past the edge of my jacket toward where the rain spilled from the clouds, “It’s just so embarrassing. I hate that I resent her for that, but I do.”

“I get that.” I told her with an empathetic nod, “You know how my dad is. I think you have a right to feel that way.” She met my eyes, and then it was her turn to squeeze my hand. I continued, “You shouldn’t be getting shit for something you have no part in.”

Kait let out an amused snort, “It’s just funny, you know? Did you know that Mr. Lancaster was at my house the other night? A married man, by the way. He comes over for a night of adultery—and this somehow gets out to the town—but somehow my mom gets more shit for it than the man that paid to see her. How does that make any sense?”

“Well, maybe if your mom employed a third of the town…” I joked.

“And if she were a *man*.” Kait scoffed.

“Yeah, that might help,” I sighed with a pull from the cig, “I bet Lancaster is getting high fives at work right now.”

Kait chuckled, but it faded fast, “And since nobody here can give her shit, I guess that makes me the slut by proxy in everyone else’s eyes. Go figure, right?”

I pursed my lips and side-eyed her, “They’re really saying we’re sleeping together now, too, huh?”

Kait nodded, “They say we’re lined up to be the next teen pregnancy. Don’t think we’ll make it till senior year before dropping out.

“Well, we’d be living up to our parents' names,” I smiled.

“We at least get a cute nickname,” Kait smirked back, “‘*Beauty*’ and the Beast.”

“Wow, that’s rude. They call you ‘ the beast’?” I said with a raised brow.

Kait released my hand and smacked me, which got a hearty chuckle. I admittedly felt a little sad that her palm was no longer slipped into mine, but she made it up to me by setting our spent cigarette butt in the empty can beside our ledge, then locking her arm into mine, fully snuggling close.

Silence came again, but it was different this time. Content and peaceful, like a sigh of relief. Our eyes once again fixed on the dark birds that sat pruning each other among the rain, each movement of their razor-sharp beaks so gentle and careful toward their partner. Eventually, they noticed us staring, and as if we’d disturbed their sacred dance, the first bird took off, flapping its strong wings and carrying itself into the misty clouds that loomed above the endless forest. Soon after, the second followed.

When Kait spoke again, her voice sounded lighter than before.

“I’m going to get out of here, Jess.”

“Yeah?”

Kait nodded, “I’m starting to think Stillwater is a trap. Most people who are born here never leave, and anyone who moves out here gets stuck and can’t.”

She shook her head and regarded the space in the clouds that the birds had escaped through.

“I can’t be mad at the way people around here treat us. It’s all they know. The world outside doesn’t exist to an animal born in a cage, and that’s all Stillwater is. Just a bunch of lost animals in a cage growling and fighting and breeding more lost animals that’ll grow up the exact same way. And that’s fine, you know? It’s shitty, but—it is what it is. Not much you can do to change it.”

Kait lifted her head from me, and I turned to face her. Her eyes were latched to mine like a man adrift at sea might cling to a raft. Even beneath my coat, I couldn’t tell if the water streaking her cheeks was rain dripping from her bangs, or the weight of her words pouring out of her eyes.

“But if I stay here long enough, I’m afraid it’s gonna’ make me that way too. And I don’t want to become an animal, Jess…” she squeezed my hand tightly, “I don’t want to see *you* become an animal. You or anyone else.”

Her words didn’t warrant it, but I couldn’t stop the faintest of smiles from teasing at my lips. I loved them; those words. Kait had a way of speaking so eloquently and restless—such tragically taboo poetry compared to most around Stillwater that it only validated her previous statement more.

Even if her words were dripped in edge and dramatics, Kait meant them with her heart, and it was that passion behind them that never made them silly to me. The girl could speak a made up language, and my heart would still be pulled toward her like the weight of the tide beneath the moon. It was one of the reasons that Kaitlynn was so special. It was why, whether she stayed in Stillwater or not, I could never imagine her allowing herself to diminish into anything lesser.

My hand found hers again, and I leaned in, closing my eyes and pressing my forehead to hers. She leaned in too so that the tips of our noses touched, her breath slow and choppy and tight with emotion. I let us linger like that, thinking only thoughts of pure adoration for the girl as if it might transpose the power of my pounding heart into hope to fuel her.

“You won’t become an animal, Kait,” I reassured her softly with all that I was, “You could never.”

They were simple words, not nearly as poetic as hers, but they seemed to land on her all the same. She swallowed, then nodded, breaking a small laugh from her lips as if overwhelmed by the awe I held for her.

I dared to open my eyes, and saw her smile melt away, leaving her lips parted ever so slightly—almost expectantly. I felt that gravitational pull in my chest once again, and as she shifted her cheek subtly to tilt her head, I nearly leaned in deeper. I wanted to so badly. I knew she wanted me to, too. It was a spark that flickered often between Kait and I, but it was never acted upon.

If there was ever a time, it was then, together alone on the roof among the rain and reverence for one another. There was a pull in my head that was stronger than my heart, however, and it was a force so much less kind than Kaitlynn. Something old and nasty and unwelcome, yet there to stay.

I didn’t have the heart to let Kaitlynn know that maybe I had already become an animal. Maybe I was too late to be saved.

So I didn’t lean in. I just sat there like a fool and let the raindrops pass us by.

This didn’t seem to hurt her, though—I’m not sure she even noticed. The girl eventually just slipped her cheek past mine to nest into my shoulder, wrapping her arms around me as she went just to rest. And rest we did.

Me not kissing her wasn’t the only mistake I made that day. Looking back, I can’t help but kick myself over my response to what she’d told me. She wanted to leave, but I hadn’t encouraged that. I had simply told her that if she stayed, it wasn’t possible that she would falter.

Maybe if I had, things would have turned out differently. Maybe that night, much later on the playground, when she’d asked me to escape with her, I should have gone. Because as we drove on down the highway toward Kait’s tainted apartment over an hour away from Stillwater, I began to think that Kait was wrong.

Stillwater wasn’t a trap, it was cursed.

More cursed than the red manor and its door. More cursed than the twisting halls beyond and the creatures living within. Hell, maybe it was the town that had bore all of that in the first place.

Kaitlynn had tried to leave it—to escape its shanty neighborhoods and decrepit streets. And in return, she had been punished. Stillwater had taken someone she cared about, then it dragged her back kicking and screaming to be locked in a basement for as long as she could manage to survive.

I wondered in that moment what it took to break that curse. If me supporting her decision to leave and ultimately going with her would have been enough to shatter it. The rebellion of two souls in unison spitting in the town's face as we left and never looked back.

Maybe then we would have never gone up the mountain. Maybe then we would have never found the red door.

But we had, and now we were trapped again. I could see the pain of it on Kait’s face as we neared her apartment that would no longer be her home. I may have damned us by choosing to open the door, but that decision was already sealed by my cowardice many years before.

I felt nothing but regret for it as I took Kait in, reaching across the seat to take her hand in mine. She looked at me with those teary, pained eyes, and in them I could see a slight comfort come over her, as well as a small gratefulness.

I don’t think she would have felt such things in that moment if she’d known the truth behind my eyes. That no matter what we found in this apartment, I was only looking for one thing. An answer to the riddle we’d been given by Mindy’s video.

All I needed was the missing key of how to lock the door. Once I had it, if things came down to desperate times, I wasn’t going to hesitate to seal the curse I’d let loose.

Kait had blamed herself for everything earlier, but the fact of the matter was, the blame was on me, and I would make things right before I let another beast lay a claw on anyone I loved.
