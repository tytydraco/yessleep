The night the water came, my father locked the door twice.

That should have scared me. It didn’t. I was still trying to understand why the city sounded wrong.

Mumbai is never quiet. Even at midnight, there’s noise leaking through the walls, pressure cookers, arguments, traffic, someone’s TV playing too loud. But that night, the sound thinned out slowly, like someone turning down the volume on the world.

My father came home before sunset, drenched in sweat, breathing too fast. He didn’t explain anything. He pushed a chair under the door handle and turned on the TV.

Every channel showed water.

Streets gone. Cars floating. People on rooftops waving cloth at helicopters that never landed.

“The storm’s moving west,” he said quietly. “They’re saying it’s not stopping.”

My mother stopped humming in the kitchen.

That’s when I got scared.

We packed our bags. Rice in plastic. Two bottles of water. A flashlight that worked only if you hit it. The power went out before midnight. The building made a long, hollow sound, like it had sighed for the last time.

The wind outside carried a smell that didn’t belong in the city.

Salt.

Metal.

Rot.

By morning, the streets weren’t streets anymore.

From the rooftop, I watched water push into the lanes below like it had been waiting its turn. Cars stalled. People slipped trying to run through currents that reached their knees. Someone near the pan shop fell and vanished under the surface.

A few minutes later, he came back up.

Not like someone swimming.

Like something lifting him.

His head rose slowly from the brown water. His eyes were open. His mouth moved, but no sound came out. He didn’t look at the people screaming around him.

He looked straight up.

At me.

And he smiled.

The water pulled him back under before anyone else noticed.

I kept staring at the spot where he disappeared.

The surface went still again.

Then I noticed something I wish I hadn’t.

The water wasn’t reflecting the sky anymore.

It was reflecting the buildings.

As if the flood wasn’t showing what was above it…

but what was inside it?

I grabbed my father’s arm so hard he winced. “Did you see, ”

“I saw,” he said. His voice had gone flat. “Don’t look down again.”

That’s when we heard it.

A deep sound that didn’t belong to wind or thunder. Something heavier. Like the earth itself being dragged across concrete.

My father whispered, “It’s here.”

The wave appeared between buildings like a black wall. It didn’t look like water. It looked like the horizon had been erased and replaced with something moving. The first building it touched collapsed. The second cracked in half.

Ours was next.

We grabbed the emergency rope ladder and started climbing.

We weren’t fast enough.

The water hit the building so hard I felt it in my teeth. The rope snapped. I fell into freezing water that forced its way into my mouth and nose before I could scream.

Under the surface, for a split second, I saw faces.

Not floating.

Looking back at me through the water.

Hands grabbed me.

My father dragged me onto what was left of the roof, a slab of concrete barely above the flood. His face was bleeding. One eye was already swelling shut.

My mother never came back up.

We found her drifting a little distance away. She had hit her head. She was still warm when I touched her fingers.

My father didn’t cry.

He didn’t say anything at all.

By evening, the water stopped moving fast. That was worse. Bodies drifted between buildings like trash. Smoke rose where gas lines had burst. The air smelled like plastic, blood, and sea salt.

The city was quiet.

Too quiet.

Then, from somewhere below, I heard someone calling my name.

Soft.

Close.

I stepped toward the edge before my father grabbed my shirt and yanked me back.

“Don’t,” he said.

“I think someone’s alive, ”

“No,” he said sharply. “That’s not someone.”

That’s when we heard voices from the rooftop across the flooded alley.

Three men. Wet. Thin. One of them had a knife tucked into his waistband.

“Anyone alive over there?” one of them called.

My father stood up immediately. He grabbed a rusted pipe from the ground and pulled me behind him.

“Supplies?” another man shouted.

I wanted to answer. I wanted to believe they were like us, scared, hungry, just looking for help.

But my father leaned close to me and whispered something I will never forget.

“Don’t trust anyone who asks for supplies.”

The men didn’t wait for a reply.

They started crossing over to our roof.

And that was when I realized something that still makes my skin crawl:

The flood didn’t just drown the city.

It brought something with it.
