# Chapter One: The Cracks in Echo Falls 

Echo Falls was the kind of small town that seemed lifted straight from the silver screen, a living embodiment of the American Dream. Picture perfect, with its white picket fences bordering manicured lawns and a Main Street where the Stars and Stripes fluttered gently in the breeze. Hill's Bakery filled the air with the comforting scent of sugar and yeast, a sweet counterpoint to the nostalgic charm of Marla's Antiques across the way. Neighbors gathered for potlucks in the town square, kids rode their bikes down tree-lined streets, and the loudest drama was usually the high school football game on Friday nights.

Ivy had been as much a fixture of this comforting scene as the weathered benches outside the town hall. With her warm smile and easy laugh, she was the kind of person who knew everyone and was known by all. Her days were spent just across the street from Hill's, at "The Daily Dish," the cozy soup, salad, and sandwich shop that had been her parents' legacy. Running it now, Ivy poured the same heart and soul into the daily specials as her folks had, remembering regulars' quirks – old Mr. Grafszynski always wanted extra pickles on his tuna melt, Mrs. Susie-May Hooten preferred her tomato soup with a pinch of cayenne – and anticipating their usual orders. It was a life built on connection and familiarity, a comforting rhythm that had always felt as natural as breathing.

Her two-story house on Sunset Avenue, a charming relic from the early 1900s that she had lovingly restored to its original state, was a testament to her roots in Echo Falls. The wide, welcoming wrap-around porch, with its peeling paint meticulously touched up each spring, was her sanctuary. Mornings often found her there, a steaming mug of coffee warming her hands as she watched the town slowly wake, the newspaper rustling in the gentle breeze. Evenings sometimes brought impromptu visits from neighbors, settling into the old wicker chairs for a chat as the fireflies began their nightly dance in the twilight.

The old landline phone in her living room, a heavy, beige rotary model that had somehow survived countless technological advancements, was as much a part of the house's history as the creaking floorboards and the faint scent of lavender that always seemed to linger in the upstairs hallway. In Echo Falls, landlines weren't quaint relics; they were a necessity. Cell phone service was spotty at best, a frustrating dance of finding the "sweet spot" on the porch or driving up to Krillin's Ridge, which overlooked the breathtaking Crystal Waterfalls, to get a decent signal. So, the old rotary, with its reliable connection, remained the lifeline to the outside world, a simple invitation to conversation.

But six days ago, that familiar ring had taken on a sinister new tone. The first call cut through the quiet of her living room like a scream in the night, its shrill jangle so piercing Ivy felt it could be heard on the other side of town. A heavy silence followed, broken only by a distinct breathing on the other end – close to the receiver, almost excited, a throaty rasp that sent a shiver of unease down her spine. Then, a faint whisper, undeniably her name.

“*Ivy…”*, before the silence returned, the caller seemingly content just to listen. 

“Yes? Can I help you,” Ivy said tentatively. 

The second call came barely ten minutes later, identical in its disturbing nature. After that, the phone continued its relentless assault, ringing five more times before the first hint of dawn. Ivy, heart pounding, refused to answer. *Just a wrong number,* she told herself firmly before finally retreating to bed, the unsettling shrieks of the phone  echoing in her mind.
