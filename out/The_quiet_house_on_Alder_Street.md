The house on Alder Street wasn’t abandoned.  
That’s what made it worse.  
Everyone in town knew it *should* be empty. The previous owners—the Whitakers—had left in a hurry almost ten years ago. No one ever said why. The grass never grew too high. The porch never rotted. And every now and then, late at night, the porch light would be on.  
People noticed.  
They just didn’t talk about it.

Maya noticed it the most.  
She worked the late shift at the diner on Maple, which meant she walked home around midnight, cutting through Alder Street to save time. It shaved five minutes off her route—but more importantly, it meant she passed the house.  
At first, she only glanced at it. A habit, like checking your reflection in a window.  
Then one night, she saw someone inside.  
Not clearly. Just movement behind the curtains. A shadow crossing the living room.  
Maya stopped walking.  
The porch light flickered on.  
And then—  
The shadow stopped moving.  
It was facing her.  
She couldn’t explain how she knew. It was just a shape behind fabric, barely human in the outline. But it *felt* like eye contact. Like something had paused mid-step because it realized it was being watched.  
Maya hurried home after that.  
But she kept thinking about it.

She told her brother, Eli.  
He laughed at first, but not in a dismissive way. Eli was the kind of person who found things interesting before he found them scary.  
“Maybe someone moved in,” he said, stirring his coffee. “Quietly. Off the grid.”  
“Then why no cars? No mail? No lights on except sometimes?”  
Eli shrugged. “Some people don’t want to be noticed.”  
Maya leaned forward. “It *noticed me*.”  
That part made him pause.

Two nights later, Eli walked her home.  
They slowed when they reached Alder Street.  
The house stood the same as always—still, silent, watching.  
“No lights,” Eli said.  
“Wait,” Maya whispered.  
There—second floor window.  
A faint glow.  
Like someone had just turned off a lamp.  
Eli squinted. “You saw that too?”  
Maya nodded.  
The porch light snapped on.  
Both of them flinched.  
“Okay,” Eli said, voice quieter now. “That’s… weird timing.”  
“Let’s just go,” Maya said.  
But Eli didn’t move.  
He stepped closer to the fence.  
“Eli,” she hissed.  
He turned back with that same half-smile he always had when something got under his skin in a way he couldn’t ignore.  
“Come on,” he said. “We’ll just check the yard. If someone’s living here, they might need help. Or maybe they’re hiding for a reason.”  
“That’s not a good reason to go *toward* it.”  
“It’s not a good reason to leave it alone either.”  
Maya hesitated.  
Then followed.

The gate creaked open.  
Too loud.  
The kind of sound that makes you instantly aware of how quiet everything else is.  
They stepped onto the property.  
The air felt… wrong. Not colder. Not thicker. Just *aware*.  
Like the house knew they were there.  
The porch light flickered again.  
And then—  
The front door clicked.  
Unlocked.  
Eli froze.  
Maya grabbed his arm. “We didn’t touch it.”  
“I know.”  
The door slowly drifted open.  
Just an inch.  
Then two.  
Darkness inside.  
Waiting.

They should’ve left.  
They both knew that.  
But curiosity isn’t always loud. Sometimes it’s quiet and reasonable.  
*Just a quick look.*  
*Just to be sure.*  
Eli pushed the door open.

The inside of the house was… normal.  
Dusty, sure. Unused. But not abandoned.  
Furniture still in place. Photos still on the walls. A faint smell of something clean—like someone had wiped surfaces not too long ago.  
“Okay,” Eli whispered. “Someone’s definitely—”  
A floorboard creaked upstairs.  
They both looked up.  
Slow.  
Deliberate.

“Hello?” Eli called.  
No answer.  
Another creak.  
A step.  
Then another.  
Someone walking.  
Maya’s voice was barely sound. “We should go.”  
Eli nodded.  
But neither of them moved.  
Because the footsteps stopped.  
Right above them.

A shadow passed across the ceiling.  
Not fast.  
Not hiding.  
Just… moving.

Eli swallowed. “We’re leaving,” he said, louder now. “Sorry for coming in.”  
Silence.  
Then—  
A voice.  
Soft.  
From upstairs.  
“…don’t.”

Maya’s stomach dropped.  
That wasn’t fear in the voice.  
It was… pleading.

Eli stepped forward. “Are you okay?”  
“…don’t leave.”  
Maya shook her head. *No.*  
But Eli was already moving toward the stairs.  
“Eli—”  
“Just stay here,” he said gently. “I’ll check.”  
He started up.  
Step by step.  
Each creak louder than the last.  
Maya stood frozen in the living room, staring at the staircase, listening.  
Halfway up—  
The voice again.  
Closer now.  
“…please.”

Then—  
Silence.

“Eli?” Maya called.  
No answer.  
“Eli?”  
Nothing.

She waited.  
Five seconds.  
Ten.  
Thirty.

Then she heard it.  
Not footsteps.  
Not movement.  
Just—  
Breathing.  
Behind her.

Maya turned slowly.  
The hallway behind her was empty.  
But the front door—  
Was closed.

She hadn’t heard it shut.

Her chest tightened.  
“Eli?” she whispered.  
Still nothing.

Then—  
From upstairs—  
Eli’s voice.  
“…Maya?”  
Relief flooded her. “Eli! I’m here!”  
“…don’t come up.”  
Her relief twisted into confusion. “What?”  
“…just leave.”  
“That’s not funny.”  
“I’m not joking.”  
His voice sounded… wrong.  
Flat.  
Careful.

“Eli, what’s going on?”  
A pause.  
Then—  
“…there’s no one here.”  
Maya frowned. “Then who was talking?”  
Another pause.  
Longer this time.

“…Maya,” he said slowly, “when I got upstairs…”  
Her heart started pounding.  
“…I found a room.”  
“Okay…”  
“…with a bed.”  
“…and?”  
“…and photos.”

Maya swallowed. “Photos of what?”

Silence.

Then—  
“…of you.”

Everything inside her went cold.  
“That’s not—Eli, stop.”  
“…you. Walking home. Sitting in the diner. Sleeping.”  
Maya’s breath caught.  
“…from inside the house.”

Her mind reeled. “That’s not possible.”  
“…and there’s more.”  
“Eli—”  
“…photos of me.”

The floor beneath her felt unsteady.

“…but not from tonight.”

Maya’s voice broke. “What do you mean?”

“…they’re old.”

A pause.  
Then—  
“…years old.”

Maya’s stomach dropped.  
“That doesn’t make any sense.”

“…Maya,” Eli whispered, voice shaking now, “there’s a mirror in the room.”  
“…okay?”  
“…but it’s not showing me.”

Her heart stopped.

“…it’s showing someone else.”

Before she could respond—  
A hand touched her shoulder.

Maya screamed and spun around.

No one.

But the breathing—  
Closer now.  
Right behind her ear.

“…don’t leave.”

Maya bolted for the door.  
She yanked it open and ran into the night, not looking back, not stopping, not thinking.

She didn’t see Eli come out.

The police searched the house the next morning.  
No one was inside.  
No signs of forced entry.  
No hidden rooms.  
No photos.  
No mirror.

Just an empty house.

Eli was never found.

Maya moved away.  
Far away.  
Different town. Different job. Different life.  
She never walked alone at night again.  
Never looked into dark windows.  
Never talked about Alder Street.

Until—  
Five years later—  
She got a letter.

No return address.  
No stamp.  
Just her name.

Inside—  
A single photograph.

Maya sitting in her new apartment.

Taken from inside the room.

Behind her—  
A reflection in the window.

A man standing in the dark.

Smiling.

It wasn’t Eli.

And on the back of the photo—  
Written in careful, familiar handwriting—

**“You weren’t supposed to leave.”**
