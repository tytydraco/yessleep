I need to say this up front because it matters.

I am not someone who scares easily.

I’ve spent years reading psychological evaluations tied to violent crimes. I’ve reviewed competency reports, risk assessments, mitigation packets, post-conviction evaluations. I’ve read about childhood torture, sexual sadism, dissociation so severe people lost years of their lives. I’ve read transcripts where people describe murder like they’re describing grocery shopping.

I don’t spook.

That’s why I’m writing this.

I go by Bee. I’m thirty-two. I work remotely reviewing discovery and psychological records for defense attorneys. I don’t work for the state. I don’t evaluate people. I organize, analyze, flag inconsistencies, build timelines. I’m the person who reads 3,000 pages so the attorney doesn’t miss the one paragraph that changes a case.

It’s quiet work. Isolated work. Just me, my laptop, PDFs, and redacted trauma.

I’ve been doing it long enough that I know what standard formatting looks like. I know how clinicians structure their impressions. I know what boilerplate language looks like.

That’s why I noticed immediately that something was wrong.

⸻

The File

The case came through encrypted transfer like they all do.

Folder labeled:

STATE v. HENDERSON

Psychological Evaluation – Competency & Risk

Normal. Routine.

The evaluation was 47 pages. Standard forensic template. Identifying information redacted except for the defendant’s initials: J.H.

I skimmed the first few pages while building my issue list.

Then I hit the demographic section.

Subject: White female

Age: 32

Occupation: Legal consultant / discovery review specialist

Education: Master’s degree in Criminology

I stopped scrolling.

Plenty of people have master’s degrees in criminology. Plenty of 32-year-old white women work in legal fields.

Coincidence.

I kept reading.

Subject reports being a single mother who completed graduate education while working full-time.

My hand actually left the mouse.

Still coincidence.

Statistically possible.

Then:

Subject has a documented history of obsessive pattern recognition behaviors, particularly involving violent case material. Reports difficulty disengaging from case narratives once exposed.

I have never been evaluated for that.

I have never told a clinician that.

I kept reading.

My heart was beating harder now, but not from fear — from irritation. This felt like sloppy redaction. Like someone accidentally merged two reports.

Then I reached the behavioral observations section.

⸻

Behavioral Observations

The clinician wrote:

“Subject demonstrates high verbal intelligence and controlled affect. Maintains composure while discussing violent subject matter but displays microexpressions of distress when discussing personal vulnerability.”

That is forensic-language specific.

That’s not generic.

Then:

“Subject exhibits hypervigilance in domestic environments. Frequently checks door locks before sleep. Reports sleeping lightly due to prior traumatic experience involving unexpected threat.”

No.

No, I didn’t.

I do check my locks.

But I never reported that to anyone.

I’ve never been evaluated.

I’ve never sat in a forensic interview room.

I don’t have a criminal case.

I kept scrolling.

My palms were sweating now.

⸻

The Offense Summary

There was no named offense.

Instead, it read:

“Alleged incident occurred in subject’s residence during early morning hours. No signs of forced entry. Scene presentation initially consistent with self-directed behavior; subsequent review suggests third-party interaction remains possible.”

My apartment felt smaller reading that.

I live alone.

No signs of forced entry.

Third-party interaction remains possible.

I scrolled faster.

⸻

Risk Assessment

The evaluator used HCR-20 language — structured professional judgment.

And then I saw this:

“Primary risk factor appears to be perceptual sensitivity to embedded stimuli. Subject reports noticing auditory irregularities in otherwise normative recordings.”

I physically stood up from my chair.

Embedded stimuli.

Auditory irregularities.

I don’t work with audio.

I work with documents.

But three weeks ago, I did review deposition transcripts where multiple witnesses described “hearing something no one else heard.”

I flagged it as credibility inconsistency.

I wrote a note: “Possible shared delusion vs. suggestibility contamination.”

I remember that phrasing exactly.

The report continued.

“Subject demonstrates above-average detection of environmental anomalies. This trait was noted as both adaptive and destabilizing.”

Adaptive and destabilizing.

Who talks like that unless they’ve been studying you?

⸻

The Date

I should have checked it earlier.

I didn’t want to.

The evaluation date was listed as next Thursday.

Today is Monday.

The evaluator’s signature block was signed.

Dated.

Stamped.

Next Thursday.

⸻

The Last Page

I don’t know why I kept reading.

I think because if I stopped, the thing would still exist.

The final page was Recommendations.

Most forensic reports end with treatment suggestions, court advisements, competency conclusions.

This one ended differently.

It said:

“Subject does not meet criteria for incompetency at time of writing. However, continued exposure may accelerate threshold breach.”

Threshold breach.

That phrase again.

Then, last paragraph:

“Monitoring phase appears complete. Subject now demonstrates full perceptual access. Further concealment unlikely to succeed.”

Monitoring phase.

Complete.

I work from home.

No one monitors me.

I don’t even have clients yet this week. It’s been quiet.

Too quiet.

The final sentence was not formatted like the rest.

It wasn’t clinical.

It wasn’t professional.

It was single-spaced at the bottom, like someone added it after.

It read:

“She notices what others don’t. That’s why she was selected.”

Selected.

⸻

The Metadata

Here’s the part that made my stomach drop.

I checked the PDF properties.

Author: Bee Wilson 

That’s not uncommon.

Wilson is common.

My last name is Wilson.

But here’s what wasn’t common.

Creation time: 2:14 AM

Last modified: 2:14 AM

I was awake at 2:14 AM.

I remember because I got up to get water.

I checked my phone.

2:14 AM.

I didn’t log into my case portal until 8:32 AM.

The file was already there.

Uploaded.

Stamped.

Signed.

Dated next Thursday.

⸻

The Part I Didn’t Tell You

There’s one thing I haven’t mentioned yet.

When I first opened the encrypted folder, before I opened the evaluation, there was a second file.

Just one line.

No extension.

No preview.

Just a text document labeled:

Audio Pending.

I assumed it was mis-uploaded.

It wouldn’t open.

I ignored it.

After I finished reading the evaluation after the “selected” line I went back to the folder.

The file was gone.

In its place was a new one.

An MP3.

Timestamped 2:14 AM.

Length: 00:32.

Thirty-two seconds.

I haven’t pressed play.

I don’t know what’s on it.

I’m writing this instead.

Because I’ve read enough forensic summaries to know what comes after “monitoring phase complete.”

Escalation.

Interaction.

Confirmation.

The report says “continued exposure may accelerate threshold breach.”

What if writing this counts as exposure?

What if reading it does?

I keep thinking about the line:

“She notices what others don’t. That’s why she was selected.”

Selected for what?

Evaluation implies observation.

Observation implies watcher.

I live alone.

But since finishing the report, I’ve noticed something.

When my laptop screen goes dark, I can see the reflection behind me.

Just my couch.

My door.

My hallway.

Normal.

Except a few minutes ago, when I thought I saw movement near the doorframe.

It could’ve been lighting.

It could’ve been my eyes adjusting.

But I don’t think it was.

The evaluation says the incident occurs early morning.

No signs of forced entry.

Third-party interaction remains possible.

Next Thursday.

That’s four days from now.

If something happens, I want this documented.

If nothing happens, I will delete this and pretend I had a stress response to too much violent material.

But I don’t think that’s what this is.

I think the monitoring phase is real.

I think I’ve been evaluated without knowing it.

And I think the audio file is the next step.

I’m going to sit here for a few minutes.

I’m not going to play it yet.

If something changes — if I hear something — I’ll update this.

If I don’t update, and you somehow hear about a case involving a 32-year-old legal consultant found in her apartment with no sign of forced entry 

Just know this:

I noticed.

I just don’t think noticing was the point.
