The first thing you learn on the graveyard shift is that silence has texture; there is the heavy, suffocating silence of a tragedy, and then there is the hollow, synthetic silence I walked into that Tuesday night, a silence that felt like the world had been paused.

I was three months into my tenure with the county sheriff’s department, responding to a 911 hang-up from a property out on Blackwood Ridge. Dispatch noted the call was just static, but the GPS pinned it to a mid-century modern house at the end of a long gravel driveway.

 When I rolled up, the place looked manicured to an obsessive degree. The grass was cut to military precision, the hedges were geometric shapes, and the front door was standing wide open, spilling warm yellow light onto the porch. It looked like a stage play waiting for the actors to arrive. I unholstered my sidearm, announced my presence, and stepped across the threshold. The air inside didn't smell like a home; it didn't smell like laundry, or cooking, or even old wood.

 It smelled like absolutely nothing—a vacuum of sensory input that made the hair on my arms stand up.

I began clearing the ground floor, moving room to room. The furniture was upscale—leather sectionals, glass coffee tables—but everything was positioned with mathematical rigidity, facing the television which was currently off. In the kitchen, I saw a bowl of fruit on the counter.

 I reached out to touch a Red Delicious apple, and my finger slid off hard, cold plastic. I frowned and opened the stainless-steel refrigerator. It was packed. rows of milk cartons, eggs, vegetables. I grabbed a carton of milk to check the expiration date, and it was light as a feather. It was a prop. Empty cardboard. I checked the eggs; ceramic. The entire kitchen was a facsimile of domestic life, stocked with hollow replicas. 

My boots crunched too loudly on the hardwood as I moved to the hallway. I found a bookshelf and pulled a thick novel from the spine. It didn't open. It was a solid block of wood painted to look like a book. A cold sweat began to prickle under my Kevlar vest. This wasn't just a model home for real estate; someone was living here. There were indentations on the couch cushions and a glass of water on the side table—real water, half-empty. 

I made my way to the master bedroom. The bed was made, but atop the duvet sat a spiral-bound notebook. I shone my flashlight on the open page. The handwriting was jagged, frantic, and phonetically written. It was a script. 

'SCENARIO 14: GREETING. SMILE: TEETH VISIBLE, EYES CRINKLED. SAY: HELLO OFFICER, IS THERE A PROBLEM? PITCH: LOW, CONFIDENT. DO NOT BLINK TOO MUCH.'

I turned the page. 'SCENARIO 15: GRIEF. SOBBING NOISE. HANDS TO FACE. SAY: HE IS DEAD, HE IS DEAD. SHAKE SHOULDERS.' 

The ink was fresh. I wasn't clearing a house; I was walking through a training simulator.

A floorboard creaked directly above me. The house was a ranch style, but there was an attic access pull-down in the hallway ceiling. The hatch was already hanging open, the ladder extended. I gripped my weapon with both hands, my breathing shallow, and ascended. 

The attic wasn't dark. It was lined with soundproofing foam and illuminated by a ring light setup. In the center of the room, facing a full-length mirror, stood a man. He was naked, his skin pale and hairless, his posture rigid. He was staring at his own reflection with an intensity that made him look like a wax figure. 

'Sheriff's Department!' I shouted. 'Show me your hands!'

The man didn't flinch. He didn't look at me. He kept staring at the mirror. Then, his jaw unhinged slightly, opening wider than seemed comfortable. A sound came out of his throat—not a voice, but a perfect, high-fidelity recording of a police siren. *Whoop-whoop.* It was indistinguishable from the cruiser outside. 

He slowly turned his head toward me. His face was slack, devoid of any genuine muscle tension. He looked at my gun, then at my face, analyzing. 

'Show me your hands!' I repeated, my voice cracking.

The man’s lips moved, and out came *my own voice*, recorded from seconds ago, perfectly mimicked in tone and pitch: 'Sheriff's Department. Show me your hands.'

He smiled then—a wide, horrible expression that exposed too much gum line, exactly as described in the notebook.

I didn't wait to arrest him. I backed down the ladder, keeping my gun trained on the square of darkness, and radioed for immediate backup. I held the perimeter from behind my cruiser until two other units arrived ten minutes later. We went back in. The attic was empty. The soundproofing foam was there, the mirror was there, but the man was gone. The only evidence left behind was a digital voice recorder sitting on the floor. 

Back at the station, we played the last track. It was a recording of me calling for backup, followed by the sound of my car door closing, and then the sound of me breathing as I sat in the driver's seat waiting for them. He hadn't fled into the woods. He had been in the back of my cruiser, silent, listening, studying, before slipping out just as the other units arrived. I still check my backseat every time I get in, but I know it doesn't matter. He knows my voice better than I do.
