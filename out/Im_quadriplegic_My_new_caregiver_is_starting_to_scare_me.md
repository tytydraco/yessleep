I've been a prisoner in my own body for eighteen years. That's a long time for anyone. Especially for someone like me.

I was sixteen when my older brother became successful.

Hanry was eight years older than me. As the firstborn, my parents always expected a lot from him. And to his credit, he delivered.

At twenty-four, he started a home renovation company with a few of his friends. To everyone's surprise, they became successful within six months. Bigger contracts started rolling in. The work was brutal, but the money was good.



That's how I ended up owning a shiny silver Yamaha R3 at sixteen.

Technically, the paperwork was in Hanry's name. But the bike was mine.



It didn't take long for me to become popular at school. My brother was making serious money and helping out my parents, and me, in every way he could.



Who wouldn't want a rich new friend?



I remember that night.

Every detail of it.

Amy invited me to the beach. Almost everyone from my class was there. Everyone who mattered. And of course, what else was a sixteen-year-old kid supposed to ride to a beach party where the hottest girl in school personally invited him?



The Yamaha.

I never made it there. One bad line through a turn. A little too much speed.

What sixteen-year-old boy thinks about danger when his whole life is ahead of him and the prettiest girl in school wants him at her party?

I drifted into the oncoming lane and hit a family SUV head-on.

  


Thankfully, nobody was hurt except me.

The doctors told me that if I hadn't been wearing my helmet, I would've died.

Sometimes I wonder if that would've been better. I was in critical condition anyway.

Almost every bone in my body was broken or fractured. I had internal bleeding. Then the doctors determined that my C4 spinal cord had been damaged so severely that I would never move again.

Not ever.



The whole process lasted about a year and a half. Treatments. Rehabilitation. Specialists. Doctors. Therapists. Everyone trying to force life back into a body that no longer wanted to cooperate. Into the muscles and bones of a teenage boy who couldn't move.



The best they managed was a slight amount of movement in my right shoulder. Just enough that I can turn my head a little and look in either direction. That's it. The whole thing was hell.

My mom cried constantly. My dad was always quiet. And Hanry… Hanry never came to see me. Mom always told me he would. That he just needed time.

But I don't think he could bring himself to look at me. I don't think he could meet my eyes and tell me he was sorry. I hated him back then.

Not just him. Everyone. The whole world.



I was a helpless cripple whose father still had to bathe him and carry him to the toilet while he was almost eighteen years old.

I wanted to die. I was depressed. I had violent outbursts.



And honestly?

It would've been better if I'd died on that winding road leading to the beach.

  


But that's not what happened.

The world doesn't work like that. Some people deserve to die and keep living. Some people deserve to live and end up dead. And then there's me. The guy who would've chosen death if he'd been given the option. The years passed. My parents got older. And eventually, Hanry finally worked up the courage to face me.

I still remember the first time he walked into my room. The look on his face. The way he fought back tears. He just stood there like a stranger. No hug. No words. Nothing.



I thought he was disgusted by me. By what I looked like. By what I'd become.

After that, though, he started visiting once a month.



He was always dressed well. Every year the suits got nicer. Even his cologne smelled expensive. I hated him. I genuinely hated Hanry. 

Why did he get to be the successful one? The rich one? The businessman with a future?

While I was left with this. With misery.



Mom always defended him whenever I lashed out.



She'd remind me that Hanry paid for my treatments. Everything. The devices that made my life a little easier to live.

He even hired caregivers so my aging parents wouldn't have to carry me around like dead weight.



But I didn't care.

Maybe Hanry cared about me. Or maybe he just didn't mind spending money on me.

Back then, I couldn't tell the difference. That angry, bitter version of me lasted well into my twenties.

Some days were worse than others.

  


There were times I was impossible to deal with. Times when I refused to eat because I hoped maybe I'd finally die.



Nobody ever let me. Eventually, I accepted things. Or at least, I accepted them as much as someone like me can.



I started seeing psychologists and therapists. Hanry paid for those too.

And slowly, I started getting better. One therapist told me something that stuck with me.

If I couldn't change my body, maybe I should focus on changing my mind.



That was still mine. So I started reading.

With help, obviously, but I educated myself. Eventually I found psychology. I devoured everything I could get my hands on. And because of my situation, I ended up specializing in trauma psychology.

I earned a degree. But I didn't want to stop there. I started working toward a doctorate.



And then my father died.

It hit all of us hard. Not just me. I could see it in Hanry too. Especially because Dad never got the chance to meet his grandchildren. By then, Hanry had married a wonderful woman named Rose.



Sometimes he'd tell me about her. About how happy she made him. How much he loved her. And despite everything… I was happy for him. Bitter. Jealous. But happy. He deserved happiness. What happened to me wasn't his fault.

  


But the person my father's death hit the hardest was my mother. And God, I'm ashamed of myself for this… I hated myself.



How could I have been so blind?



Mom got worse and worse after Dad was gone. And Hanry wasn't just paying for my care anymore. He was paying for hers too. 

I tried finding work myself so I wouldn't have to depend on him forever. By that point, he was supporting his own family and, on top of that, taking care of my mother and me.



Finding work wasn't exactly easy in my situation.

Sometimes I'd write psychology articles. Other times I'd provide online counseling for patients dealing with severe trauma. But none of it paid enough for me to contribute much.



Then Mom died too. It didn't take long. She followed Dad. And just like that, I was alone in the family house. Or at least I would've been. Hanry hired a live-in caregiver for me.



Sarah was a kind forty-two-year-old Filipino woman. She'd come to America with her husband and their three children.



I never would've thought someone could be genuinely happy about helping a guy like me use the bathroom, but Sarah was grateful to finally have steady work.



And Hanry paid her well.

Sarah and I… Well, we got along.

Even with the language barrier. Her English wasn't great, which occasionally led to some interesting misunderstandings, but she was always patient and polite.



Even on my bad days. Because no matter how much you learn about yourself, no matter how much therapy you go through, everyone has days when they sink into a dark place. Even normal people.



After my parents were gone, Hanry started visiting less and less. I understood why.

He was busy. He had another child. His company had grown far beyond what he'd originally started, and he always seemed glued to his phone.

Sometimes he and his family would come visit. Those visits were always awkward.

Especially when Sarah wasn't around and I had to ask them for help with basic things.

It felt like they weren't the guests. I was. Eventually, Hanry and I settled into a routine.

  


If I needed something, I'd call him. Or we'd just talk for a few minutes.

Most of the time, though, he barely had time for that. Sometimes he'd only stay on the phone for a minute or two.

Sometimes he wouldn't call me back for days. And little by little, I started feeling that familiar emptiness creeping back into my life. Like none of it mattered. Then came the final twist of the knife.

One day, after five years, Sarah told me she was leaving town. Her husband had been offered a good job in another state. And just like that, I was about to be alone again.

  
  


"Kiddo..." Hanry said, finally looking up from his phone. "Sarah was good, yeah. But she's not the only caregiver on Earth."

  


I hated when he called me that. Kiddo. As if we were still children.

I was thirty-six years old. A helpless quadriplegic with a degree in psychology.

Not some teenager.

  


"Yeah, Hanry, I know." I sighed. "But I'm stuck in this house like a ghost. And now the only person who actually spent time with me is leaving."

  


I saw something twitch beneath the lines on his face.

Guilt, maybe. Then he pulled himself together and typed something into his phone.

  


"I spend time with you." Hanry patted my numb shoulder as he walked past me. "But listen. I already found you another caregiver. This one's a guy. Around your age, I think. You'll probably get along."

  


"Hanry..." I glared at him. "I don't need a buddy."

  


"I know. I know." He shook his head impatiently. "But hold on. I have to take this."

  


Just like that, the conversation was over. He walked into the kitchen, already answering his phone.

I rolled my eyes. As decent as Hanry tried to be, I always had the feeling that deep down he saw me as a problem to manage.

Nothing more. A few minutes later he hurried back into the room.

  


"Kiddo," he said, already heading for the front door. "I can't stay and wait for the new caregiver. Something important came up. I forgot about it. You'll be okay?"

  


"I'll survive," I said flatly.

  


Hanry gave me a thumbs-up and hurried out without another word. Leaving me alone in the large, empty house. I let out a long sigh.

Then I slowly turned my head toward the small table in the living room.

  


"Alexa," I said. "Let's go with the usual Radiohead."

  


"Yes, Derek," Alexa replied in her familiar voice. "Playing your usual Radiohead playlist."

  


The opening notes of How to Disappear Completely had barely started when someone rattled the front door.

  


"Ah..." I groaned. "Alexa, stop."

  


The music immediately cut off.

A moment later, whoever was outside knocked again. Waiting to be let in.

  


"It's open!" I shouted. "Come in!"

  


I heard the front door creak open.

Then the sound of hard-soled shoes clicking across the floor as someone made their way toward the living room. And when the person finally appeared...

I never would've guessed in a million years that he was supposed to be a caregiver.

  


A tall, thin figure stood beneath the archway of my living room.



His messy blond hair stuck out in every direction, as if he'd just come running in from a game of tag. His narrow, bony face wore a cheerful expression, though his skin was so pale and gray it looked like he hadn't seen sunlight in years. His blue eyes sparkled with playful energy.

The ugly brown overalls hanging from his frame didn't fit him at all. Not just because of the colors. They were too short, exposing his ankles completely. As if that wasn't enough, he wore a brightly striped shirt underneath.

The whole thing gave me the uncomfortable feeling that I should be taking care of him.

Not the other way around.

  


"Howdy, Derak!" the lanky man announced, extending his hand for a handshake.

  


"I can't move," I said with an awkward smile.

  


"Oh, craaap!" The strange man smacked himself in the forehead. "I forgot already. Sorry, Derak."

  


"Derek," I corrected. "And it's kind of weird that you forgot. The wheelchair isn't exactly hard to miss."

  


"Hah! Right!" he said cheerfully. "Jeez, I gotta get myself together."

  


I didn't answer. I simply nodded as much as I was capable of.

  


"Okay then." He straightened himself up. "Let's start over proper-like. I'm Mr. Happy, your new caregiver. Nice to meet ya!"

  


"Is this some kind of joke?" I asked flatly. "Is Hanry messing with me? Or is he just trying to spice up my boring life?"

  


"Uhhhh..." Mr. Happy scratched at his messy hair. "I dunno. But I got the caregiver job. And I'm very good at caregivering!"

  


"Yeah... no." The words slipped out before I could stop them. "Absolutely not. Call Hanry. Right now."

  


"Derek." Mr. Happy suddenly looked almost hurt. "I really just wanna make being here a little happier. I don't wanna cause trouble for ya. Just... y'know... a little change. Something different."



I studied him suspiciously.

For the first time since he'd walked through my door, it felt like he'd stepped out of the role of the goofy clown he'd been playing.

  


"What is all this?" I asked. "What's your real name?"

  


"Mr. Happy. That's what everybody calls me." His face went completely blank. "And I told ya. Things need a little fun in 'em so every day ain't all gray and boring."

  


Mr. Happy smiled at me cheerfully, but his eyes didn't reflect what his face was showing.

The smile felt practiced. Like he'd spent years teaching himself how to do it.

The thought crossed my mind that maybe he was autistic. I'd met people before who struggled with facial expressions and social cues. Maybe that was all this was. 

  


"I see," I said slowly. "So that's actually your real name?"

  


"Finally, Derak." Mr. Happy nodded enthusiastically. "Now we're gonna have fun.”

  


"I guess I'll show you around the house..." I said, feeling reluctant about the whole thing.

  


Mr. Happy, however, already seemed far too comfortable. He had started wandering around my living room, inspecting everything. Like some kind of analysis robot, he'd stop in front of every little trinket and stare at it for minutes at a time. Every photograph, certificate, and porcelain figurine received the same intense examination.

All of them had belonged to my parents.

  


"Would you mind helping me?" I tried prompting Mr. Happy, who was currently studying a childhood vacation photo as if it were an eighteenth-century oil painting.

  


Mr. Happy didn't react. He continued examining the picture with great concentration.

  


"Sir?" I called louder. "Could you come over here?"

  


Still nothing. He was completely absorbed in analyzing the photograph. I let out a long sigh and rolled my eyes.

  


"Mr. Happy?" I tried again. "Could you help me?"

  


"Oh?!" The man finally looked up. "Sorry! Didn't hear ya."

  


"That's a pretty serious problem," I said dryly. "No offense, but are you sure you can do this job? I can't move. I require twenty-four-hour care. If you're already not paying attention to me, that's going to become a very big issue."



Mr. Happy's expression darkened slightly. He took a deep breath and dropped onto the living room couch.

  


"Derek," he said. The tone was completely different. "People like you. Folks who end up in situations like this. They're prone to long-term depression. Just like you are. You know everybody tried to help you, but you'd rather sit in self-pity and push everyone away. That's the ice I'm here to break."

  


I stared at him.

  


"The Derek whose life is overshadowed by suicidal thoughts? I'd like him to stop being that Derek. Understand? That's my specialty."

  


For a moment, I couldn't say a word.

This strange, disheveled man had stumbled into my house acting like an autistic ten-year-old, and within five minutes he'd summarized twenty years of my psychological self-analysis.

  


"So, Derek?" Mr. Happy said. Just like that, the goofy smile returned to his face. "We gonna keep the fun goin'?”

  


My house wasn't particularly large. It had been my parents' house, and eventually it became my inheritance. Hanry had given up his claim to it, so I stayed. Mr. Happy and I toured the first floor together. The kitchen, the living room, the foyer, the dining room, and a small patio that opened into the backyard. Mr. Happy politely pushed me through the house. No jokes. No clowning around.

I couldn't see his face from where I sat, but I had the feeling he was actually paying attention. Listening.

  


"And this is the way upstairs." We stopped in front of the staircase. "As you can see, there's a lift system that can carry my wheelchair up. It's voice-controlled too. Gotta love modern technology."

  


"Ohhh." Mr. Happy stared at it in amazement. "What funny things people got nowadays."

  


"Yeah," I agreed. "Parts of the house are automated. Thankfully Hanry paid for all of it. Makes life easier."

  


"I see." Mr. Happy nodded and immediately leaned forward to inspect the lift mechanism.

  


Like a curious child. He got so close that he practically pressed his nose against the machinery.

  


"I think we should head upstairs," I said, watching him with confusion.

  


Mr. Happy immediately returned to his duties. He pushed my chair onto the platform, and after I gave the command, the lift began carrying me upward with its usual mechanical hum. By the time I reached the second floor, I was about to ask him for help getting off the platform. He was already standing behind me.

Before I could say anything, he was pushing me down the hallway.

  


"There are a few more rooms up here," I explained. "The first one is mine. The room next to it will be yours. Bathroom and shower are at the end of the hall. Speaking of which... didn't you bring any luggage?"

  


"Ah, no." Mr. Happy waved a hand dismissively. "Nope. Won't be needing any."

  


I glanced back at him as much as my limited range of motion allowed.

What was that supposed to mean? Did he not know what luggage was?



We continued through the rooms.

Mr. Happy barely spoke. He looked at everything with the same fascinated wonder a parent has when their child proudly shows them a drawing they made at school.

Eventually I asked him to take me to my room. I had a video session with a patient coming up. Mr. Happy happily complied, wheeling me over to my desk. Then he stopped beside me. And stared.

  


"You can go for now..." I said awkwardly.

  


Mr. Happy continued standing there. Watching. Not speaking. Not moving.

  


"You know..." I continued, feeling increasingly uncomfortable. "This conversation is private. People trust me with very personal things, and I'd like to keep it that way. Maybe you could... I don't know... make lunch or something?"

  


"Yeah..." Mr. Happy muttered absentmindedly. "Yeah. Yeah, I make lunch."

  


Then, as if he'd simply been waiting for someone to give him instructions, he walked out of the room. Or so I thought.

A few moments later, I noticed his reflection in my monitor. The door was cracked open.

And he was still standing there. Watching.

What the hell is wrong with this guy? The thought crossed my mind immediately. Or maybe he's worried I might need something and he won't hear me?

Mr. Happy remained there for a while longer. Then, in a sudden movement, he disappeared.

A moment later I heard his shoes clattering down the stairs.

Strange guy. Hopefully he knows how to make something edible for lunch.

  


By the time I finished my consultation, I could already smell lunch drifting through the house.

I wouldn't exactly say I'd enjoyed being fed and cared for by other people over the last eighteen years. But for some reason, my stomach growled anyway.

  


"Mr. Happy!" I shouted, making sure he could hear me downstairs. "Could you help me? I'd like to come down and eat."

  


The door opened the very second I finished speaking. I looked up, surprised. Mr. Happy stood there smiling pleasantly, his pale lips stretched into a friendly grin.

For a moment, I considered asking how he'd gotten upstairs so quickly. Then I dismissed the thought. He was probably already nearby.

  


"Can I ask what's for lunch?" I said as Mr. Happy maneuvered my wheelchair onto the lift.

  


"You'll see, Derek," he replied cheerfully. "But I'll tell ya one thing." He leaned in slightly. "Everybody loves it."

  


The lift slowly carried us downstairs.

I waited quietly until we reached the first floor, and Mr. Happy immediately pushed me toward the dining room.

The table had already been set.

There was a pitcher of water, silverware, and a single plate covered by a silver cloche that I had no idea where he'd found. Only my place was set. That felt strange.



Sarah and I had always eaten together. Or at least she'd sit with me while feeding me. One bite for herself, one bite for me. I missed Sarah.

  


"And here we are, champ!" Mr. Happy announced happily as he pushed me up to the table.

  


"Thanks," I said, less enthusiastically.

  


But instead of helping me, Mr. Happy simply walked to the opposite end of the table.

Then he stood there. Smiling. Watching me.

Like he was eagerly waiting for me to discover what he'd prepared.

Several painfully awkward seconds passed.

I couldn't tell if we were waiting for something...or if he'd forgotten, once again, that I couldn't move.

  


"Could you help me?" I finally asked.

  


"Oh gosh, right!" Mr. Happy gasped dramatically. "You can't take it off yourself."

  


He immediately sprang to his feet and bounced toward me with an exaggeratedly silly walk, as though lifting his legs required tremendous effort.

Then he hopped beside me. With one smooth, almost elegant motion, he whisked the cloche away from the plate.

And for the first time in years, I genuinely thought I might fly into a rage.

Because underneath wasn't lunch.



It was two sour gummy worms. Carefully arranged.

As if they were the signature entrée of some expensive fine-dining restaurant.

  


"Are you kidding me?" I asked angrily. "What is this?"

  


Mr. Happy stood beside me holding the silver cloche. It was obvious from his expression that he was struggling not to burst out laughing.

  


"Your lunch!" Mr. Happy finally exploded with laughter.

  


I stared at the thin man as he doubled over, laughing so hard he could barely stand.

For some reason, though, I didn't find the joke particularly amusing.

It felt childish. So I simply sat there and waited for him to finish.

  


"Oh, that's good." Mr. Happy wiped tears from his eyes. "What's wrong, Derek? People usually laugh at this."

  


"It's not funny," I said flatly. "It's childish."

  


The smile slowly vanished from Mr. Happy's face. I watched his blue eyes search mine, almost desperately looking for reassurance.

As if maybe I was joking too. But I wasn't in the mood for jokes.

Truth be told, I hadn't been in the mood for jokes in a very long time.

  


"But... Derek." Mr. Happy carefully set the cloche on the table. "I don't understand. People like this one. People laugh. I... I don't know..." He hesitated. "Different joke?"

  


"What?" I stared at him in confusion.

  


"I'm sorry, Derek," Mr. Happy said quietly. He suddenly looked lost. "I put this away."

  


As though nothing had happened, he picked up the plate with the brightly colored gummy worms and carried it into the kitchen.

The silly bouncing walk was gone. The exaggerated cheerfulness was gone too.

He moved like a waiter clearing away a dish that had been sent back. Less than a minute later, he returned carrying another plate.

This time there was no joke. Even from across the room I could see steam rising from roasted chicken and mashed potatoes. So that was the smell I'd noticed upstairs. I stared at the meal. Then at Mr. Happy.



What was the point of that joke?

If it had even been a joke at all.

  


Mr. Happy stood beside me the entire time, like some kind of servant, carefully cutting the chicken into small pieces and feeding me one bite at a time.

He didn't say a word. Not a single one. Eating like this always felt uncomfortable. As if I were some nobleman being waited on by staff. Or worse, some catatonic old man who simply needed to be stuffed with food.



Sarah had always sat beside me while I ate. We'd shared meals.

Mr. Happy, on the other hand, delivered each bite with perfect precision, but never once looked at me. Instead, he kept glancing out the dining room window with a gloomy expression, as though he were waiting for someone to arrive.

  


"Everything okay?" I asked after swallowing a spoonful of mashed potatoes.

  


"Yeah..." Mr. Happy muttered sadly as he raised another bite toward my mouth.

  


I watched the thin blond man. His shoulders were slumped.

His expression miserable. He simply held the spoon out toward me and waited for me to take the bite.

Honestly?

I felt sorry for him. Or maybe I pitied him. I wasn't sure.

What I did know was that I couldn't understand why my reaction to his childish prank had affected him this much.

  


"Mr. Happy, would you sit down here for a minute?" I asked thoughtfully.

The man mechanically pulled out a chair and dropped into it. He continued holding the spoon toward me exactly as before. "Tell me a joke."

  


"What?" Mr. Happy looked up miserably.

  


"Tell me a joke," I repeated. "Let's see what you've got. I'd like something to laugh at."

  


Mr. Happy immediately brightened.

He jumped out of the chair I'd just convinced him to sit in and ruffled his bird's-nest hair excitedly.

  


"You ready?" he asked, his eyes practically sparkling.

  


"Sure."

  


"Why is the psychopath's wife crying on the couch?" He grinned from ear to ear. Before I could even ask why, he blurted out the punchline. "Because the chicken crossed the road!"

  


Mr. Happy nearly exploded with laughter. His face turned red. He slapped the edge of the table while laughing like an overexcited child.

As stupid and bizarre as the joke was, I found myself smiling anyway. Mostly so he'd feel better.

  


"Was it goooood?" Mr. Happy wheezed through his laughter. "Was it a gooood one?"

  


"Hah... yeah," I said, forcing a smile. "It was."

  


"Then listen to this one!" Mr. Happy suddenly straightened up and adopted a more serious expression. "Ahem. Ahem. Khrrf."

  


He cleared his throat and coughed like an old smoker.

For a moment I honestly expected him to spit a wad of tobacco onto the middle of the table like some cowboy from an old Western.

What happened next genuinely surprised me.

Most people know the distinctive call of a Northern Cardinal. That sharp series of whistles and chirps they make.

But this wasn't some amateur imitation. It sounded exactly like the real thing.

I stared in amazement. Mr. Happy's mouth barely moved, yet somehow the sounds coming out of him were indistinguishable from an actual songbird. The whistles. The chirps. The rhythm. 

Everything. It was perfect.

  


"How are you doing that?" I asked before I could stop myself.

  


"Urrgh. Hrm." Mr. Happy cleared his throat again. Then he smiled. "I know lots of things." His blue eyes lit up. "You'll see." Then he grinned wider. "And we'll laugh together.”

  


After lunch came the part of the day I'd rather not talk about.

The early afternoon. That's when I needed help with... well, everything. My caregiver emptied my catheter, helped me use the bathroom, and then cleaned me up afterward like I was an infant.

Not exactly a thirty-six-year-old psychologist's proudest moment. And yes, it was humiliating.

But after eighteen years, I'd gotten used to it. What worried me was having to start over with someone new.



Sarah already knew exactly what she was doing. She never rushed, but she always finished quickly enough that the process never became more uncomfortable than it already was.

What surprised me, however, was that Mr. Happy finished almost as quickly. Possibly even faster.

It was obvious he had a great deal of experience. Every unpleasant detail was handled professionally and respectfully. The less said about those details, the better.

But throughout the entire process, one question kept repeating in my mind.



Who is this guy?



He felt like several different stages of a person's life all mashed together. Sometimes he acted like an obnoxious, immature teenager. Other times he reminded me of a developmentally delayed grade-schooler.

Then he'd behave like a baby discovering the world for the first time, fascinated by every tiny detail. And then, suddenly, he'd become a mature adult who knew exactly when a joke was appropriate and when it wasn't.



Once he finished helping me, he wheeled me out onto the back patio. I liked sitting there.

It always helped me relax.



The garden wasn't nearly as beautiful as it had been when my mother cared for it, but it was still pleasant enough. Hanry paid a gardener to keep it maintained. The grass was cut regularly, and the bushes stayed trimmed.



I sat quietly by myself while Mr. Happy cleaned the house. I could hear the vacuum running inside. Meanwhile, I couldn't stop thinking about the way he'd perfectly mimicked a songbird in the middle of my dining room.

  


"You done?" I asked when I heard the patio door slide open.

  


"Yeah," Mr. Happy muttered, sounding almost tired.

  


"Come sit with me." I tried to sound friendly. "Let's talk for a bit."

  


Instead of grabbing a chair, Mr. Happy simply crouched down on the steps leading into the yard like a little kid. He stared out at the green grass without saying anything.

  


"Where are you from, Mr. Happy?" I asked after a few moments of silence.

  


"Here. There." He shrugged.

  


"What does that mean?" I asked. "Where's home? Where were you born?"

  


Mr. Happy didn't look at me. He seemed to think about the question for a very long time.

As though answering it required real effort. Finally he spoke.

  


"I don't know."

  


I frowned.

  


"You don't know where you were born?" The therapist in me refused to let it go. "Were you... maybe an orphan?"

  


"Orphan...?" Mr. Happy repeated quietly. Then he echoed the word again. "Orphan."

  


"So you never knew your parents." I nodded slowly, sensing that this was a difficult subject for him. "My parents were good people. Kind people. It took me a long time to understand how much they sacrificed for me."

  


"Parents?" Mr. Happy repeated like a parrot. Then he tilted his head. "Linda... Paul..."

  


"What?" I blinked. For the first time, genuine surprise crept into my voice. "How do you know their names?"

  


"I saw them on the papers inside," Mr. Happy replied coolly.

  


"I see." I turned my attention back toward the garden.

  


From what I could tell, Mr. Happy was a traumatized person. Someone hiding old wounds behind awkward humor and childish behavior. Maybe some kind of Peter Pan syndrome.

Maybe something else. Whatever the case, one thing was certain. He was not an ordinary caregiver.

  


It had been five days since Mr. Happy moved in.

I wouldn't say he'd filled the hole Sarah left behind, but he was decent company sometimes.

Sure, he had his moments. At first, I'd chalked them up to dissociative episodes. It was almost as if he occasionally slipped out of himself and wandered someplace else entirely.

Not that I considered him dangerous. Quite the opposite. If anything, he was the best caregiver I'd ever had.

Everything was done on time. Exactly the way I asked. Never late.



Most mornings, by the time I opened my eyes, he was already walking through my bedroom door. Long story short, Mr. Happy was good at his job. Just a little strange.



Sometimes he'd ramble nonsense. Sometimes he'd try way too hard to be funny. One night I caught him standing in my doorway after putting me to bed. Just standing there. Waiting.

When I asked what he was doing, he said he'd forgotten what he wanted. Then he left.



Hanry hadn't visited since then, but I was used to that.

Honestly, over the past few days I'd actually started feeling better around Mr. Happy.

Even his jokes seemed to be improving.

  


"What do you want for dinner?" Mr. Happy asked after closing the front door behind us.

  


Lately we'd been taking evening walks before dinner. Well...

Mr. Happy walked. I sat in my chair.

  


"I think I'll stick with mac and cheese tonight."

  


"Excellent choice, boss-man," Mr. Happy said cheerfully. Then his eyes lit up. "Can I tell a joke while I cook?"

  


"Let's get it over with," I said with a grin.

  


Mr. Happy immediately perked up.

  


"What did the macaroni say when the cheese hugged it too tight?" He didn't even give me time to answer. "You're gonna mac me squish!" Mr. Happy burst into laughter.

  


Actual tears formed in his eyes. Meanwhile he'd completely forgotten I was still sitting by the front door.

  


"Mr. Happy?" I called after him.

  


"Oh crap!" The blond man spun around. He grabbed my wheelchair and hurried me into the dining room, parking me in my usual spot at the table. "Be right back!"

  


Then he waddled off toward the kitchen in one of his intentionally ridiculous walks.

I sat there waiting. A few minutes later he returned carrying a steaming bowl.

  


"Derek, sir," he announced dramatically. "Dinner is served."

  


Like always, he tied a small bib around my neck so I wouldn't spill food on myself. Then he reached for a spoon.

And froze. Completely froze.

  


"Mr. Happy?" I frowned. "You okay, buddy?"

  


That's when I noticed something running from his nose.

Blood.

At least I thought it was blood.

The second I looked at him, he flung the spoon across the length of the dining table and clapped a hand over his face. Then he staggered backward.

Like a drunk.

  


"Mr. Happy?" I watched him stumble toward the living room. "Y-you okay?"

  


"Jsht... clry... m'self..." The words came out mangled. Barely human.

  


Then he hurried out of the room. I sat there staring after him.

What exactly was I supposed to do?

I was paralyzed. If something was wrong, I couldn't exactly run after him.

  


"Mr. Happy?!" I shouted as loudly as I could.

  


No answer. I waited. Listened. The house sat in silence. Then I heard a voice.

  


"Wheeeere aaam Iiii?" An old woman's voice.

  


Somewhere in the living room.

I never thought I'd be capable of feeling that kind of fear again. If I'd still been able to run, I would've been halfway down the street.

  


"Whaaaat iiiis thiiis?" The voice creaked. "Whaaaat a loooovely hooouse..."

  


"Oh! This one's new!" Another voice answered. Mr. Happy. Cheerful as ever. "You like it?"

  


"Neeeew hooouse?" The old woman rasped.

  


Panic tightened my chest.

What the hell was happening? Who was this woman?

I couldn't see anything. No matter how hard I tried turning my head, my body wouldn't cooperate.

  


"Look!" Mr. Happy said excitedly. "There's all kinds of stuff here. That's the living room. That's the foyer. Over there is the kitchen."

  


"Ooooh." The old woman growled softly. "Aaand thaaat?"

  


"Oh, that's the dining room." Mr. Happy lowered his voice. "And in there..." A pause. "...that's Derek. My new friend."

  


"Frrriiieeend?" The old woman suddenly sounded angry. "Deeeereeek?" "Frrriiieeend?"

  


"Um... yes!" Mr. Happy sounded nervous now. "But hey, you know what? I'll introduce you!"

  


My heart skipped a beat.

Whoever that woman was, I had absolutely no desire to meet her. Especially after hearing the way she'd reacted to my name.

The door leading into the living room creaked.

Slowly. Like the door of an old barn.

My mouth went dry. I couldn't decide whether I should look or squeeze my eyes shut.

The door opened wider. 

And wider.

But nobody stepped through. Another creak.

The gap widened. A little more.

Then— BANG.



The door flew open.

Mr. Happy launched himself through it. His blond hair was sticking up in every direction.

His face was smeared with something red.

  


"AAAHHHH!" I screamed. If I'd still had control of my bladder, I probably would've pissed myself.

  


"Hahahaha!" Mr. Happy nearly collapsed laughing. "Derek, holy crap!" He doubled over. "You should've seen your face!" Then he pointed at me. "You looked like you were about to die!"

  


"What the FUCK is wrong with you?!" I shouted. My voice echoed through the dining room. "What the hell are you doing?! Who were you talking to? Who's the old woman?! Say something!"

  


"Just a joke..." Mr. Happy mumbled. His smile started fading. "Different joke."

  


"That wasn't funny!" I yelled. "Do you understand me?!" "Who were you talking to?"

  


"Just voice..." Mr. Happy sniffled.

  


"What the fuck ever." I was shaking with anger. "Screw dinner." I glared at him. "Take me upstairs."

  


Mr. Happy didn't argue.

Head hanging low, shoulders slumped, he quietly wheeled me away.

  


I didn't speak to Mr. Happy again until the next morning. When I woke up, he helped me bathe, but I didn't say a single word to him.

He didn't say much either.

The gloomy silence hung over him like a raincloud. It was as if the strange, playful spark in his blond hair had somehow faded overnight. After the bath, he got me dressed and took me downstairs to the dining room.

He didn't ask what I wanted for breakfast. He didn't start the day with one of his ridiculous jokes. The whole house felt strangely quiet. Strangely sad.

In the end, Mr. Happy made me scrambled eggs. Ironically, that was exactly what I would've asked for.

  


"Mr. Happy?" I stopped him as he held another forkful in front of me. "Let's talk about this like adults, okay? Sit down." Mr. Happy dropped into the chair beside me like a sulking child.

"These jokes of yours..." I took a breath. I searched for the right words. "Sometimes they're really strange. And that last one was awful. I wasn't laughing, Mr. Happy. I was scared." I watched him carefully.  "Do you understand why that's a problem?"

  


Mr. Happy sat completely still, staring at the tabletop. It looked like he was struggling to process what I was saying.

  


"The joke was the problem?" Finally he spoke.

  


"You don't get it, do you?" I sighed. My tone came out harsher than I'd intended. "Just like you don't really understand the jokes themselves." Mr. Happy kept staring at the table. For a moment he looked so lost, so helpless, that I actually started feeling sorry for him. "Do you understand why I'm angry?" I asked again.

  


"Because of the joke." Quietly, he nodded.

  


"More or less," I said. I let out a long breath. "And I'm sorry for yelling at you. It was just a bad joke."

  


"Are we okay now?" Mr. Happy asked. The question came out with almost childlike sincerity.

  


"Yeah." I found myself smiling despite everything. "Yeah, we're okay." Then I pointed a finger at him. "But only if you're sorry too."

  


Mr. Happy frowned. The expression looked genuinely confused.

  


"Sorry...?" He tilted his head. "For the joke?"

  


"Sure. Let's go with that." I couldn't help it. I laughed under my breath. Mr. Happy visibly relaxed. I nodded toward my breakfast. "Now come on." I opened my mouth for the next bite. "Let's continue.”

  


And for the first time, I found myself thinking that this might be the beginning of a friendship with Mr. Happy.
