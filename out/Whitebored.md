I bought my brother a whiteboard for Christmas. He said he needed it for brainstorming his novel.

I should’ve known something was wrong when he got rid of all the markers.

To start at the beginning, Davey’s imagination always impressed me. He had a way of coming up with such unique ideas that were so… him. Like his mind was free, unconforming to the same, tired cliches you’d see over and over. And he wasn’t just like this with his storytelling, but in real life situations, too. He would think critically about things and problem solve like it was nothing. I looked up to him a lot.

Anyway, I’m telling this story for multiple reasons. One is I just need to make sense of it all. The other I need to save for the end.

Everything was normal the first several days. We shared an apartment, just the two of us, and had similar work schedules, so we’d often see each other in the evening. We’d usually play games in the main room, but I’d occasionally come into his room to chat or show him funny videos. I remember seeing the whiteboard with a mess of words on it, detailing different plot points for his story. I always read them with glee, even though Davey would scold me for “spoiling myself”.

This wasn’t a big whiteboard. It fit on his nightstand, where he kept it facing outwards which meant I could immediately see it when I entered his room.

For about a week it seemed like just book stuff on there, but one day I noticed a break in the pattern. This is what I saw on the whiteboard:

9 AM - Breakfast: cereal  
1 PM - Lunch: hot dogs  
5 PM - Dinner: canned soup

It only struck me as slightly odd at the time. Sure, what was depicted was exactly the type of food he’d eat, but Davey wasn’t the type to schedule his meals. He just threw whatever he was feeling at the moment into the microwave. But hey, who am I to judge? New Year, New Him, right?

Thinking back on it, he did have cereal that morning, but the first red flag was when we went out around noon with our mutual friend, Kira.

Davey always had a bit of a crush on Kira. Nothing super serious. It was the kind of crush that lingers in the background for years. But he always made sure she was entertained when we hung out. Always put on a show for her. And he always predictably folded whenever she wanted to do something.

But not today.

“I want a burger so bad,” Kira groaned next to me.

“I’m down,” I replied.

When I suggested a place, Kira got really excited.

“Yes! Those! God, my mouth is watering now. Drive faster.”

“Do they serve hot dogs?” Davey asked from the back.

“Ummm,” I said, “I don’t think so…”

“Who cares? You really want some crappy hot dogs over the best burgers in town?”

“I need hot dogs.”

“Why? You really craving them *that* bad?”

“Anything else would be boring.”

Kira gave up on trying to convince him after that. I didn’t blame her. What he said made no sense. In hindsight, however, it was also terrifying.

“Okay,” I said, “I’ll swing through the drive-thru for our burgers, then we can find a place with hot dogs.”

“No time. Pull into there.” Davey pointed towards a grocery store coming up next to us.

“Pffft, okay, sure.”

“DO IT!!!”

He was dead serious. I exchanged a concerned glance with Kira. The wideness of her caramel eyes were amplified by her glasses. Davey always complimented her eyes to me the few times we spoke about his crush. I knew he didn’t like giving basic compliments like that, so he really must’ve thought they were pretty. Kira’s mom would also point out how pretty her daughter’s eyes were, mainly because they reminded her of Kira’s dad who’d recently passed. Davey hadn’t brought up her eyes since that happened.

I pulled into the snow-patched parking lot. Lights reflected off the damp asphalt. The moment I parked, Davey jumped out and headed into the store.

Davey never yelled in Kira’s presence. He and I had our fair share of screaming matches in our life, but he remembered his manners around her. This is when I knew something was up with him.

When he returned, he only had a pack of hot dogs. No buns. No ketchup or mustard, although it was possible we had the sauces at home. Neither me nor Kira said anything. I just took us straight to the burger joint.

Perhaps it was a coincidence. Or maybe fate. I don’t know. But as soon as we left the drive-thru, I checked the clock in the car, and it had just turned from 12:59 to 1:00. At that very moment, Davey opened his pack of hot dogs, pulled one out, and began scarfing it down like it was the best thing he’d ever eaten.

It was unsettling enough that Kira didn’t even roast him for it.

A week later, I’d almost forgotten about the incident. I hadn’t been in his room for a while, and work was more demanding than usual. Davey started work about an hour before me, so he’d leave earlier and come home earlier.

On this day, after he left, I noticed the markers that came with his whiteboard were all thrown into the trash can in the kitchen. At first I was offended. I paid a good twenty bucks for that gift. Then I wondered if he got rid of the whiteboard, too. I took a peek into his room and it was still sitting on his nightstand. I even glimpsed what was written on it.

Leave Them Open

I assumed this was just a reminder he left for himself for something. Didn’t put too much thought into it. As for the markers, it would make sense if one was defective, but all of them? Maybe he just liked using different ones. Whatever.

Kira called me at work, asking if she left her glasses at our place. We’d hung out the night before, but it was still a stupid question.

“What? You didn’t even take off your glasses.”

“I dunno man I was just checking. Jesus.”

She had a bad habit of losing things, and I guess her glasses finally paid the price. I wasn’t too worried, though. It’s not like she was helpless without them. But she was supposed to wear them when she drove and she didn’t like taking the risk if she could help it.

When I arrived home, I went to the kitchen for food. My heart jumped. The fridge door was left open. And the microwave. And the dishwasher. I stood in shock, listening to the hum of the refrigerator fill up the otherwise dead silence of the apartment. I knew Davey had to be home, but it still felt so… empty.

“What the fuck!?”

I shut them all and marched right to Davey’s room. He was sitting on his bed, reading a book. I snatched it from him.

“Why the hell did you leave all the shit open? In the kitchen.”

“Was it fun?”

“What?”

“Was it fun to watch?”

I was dumbfounded, and ran my hands through my hair.

“No, Davey. It wasn’t fun to watch our electric bill go up in real time. What’s wrong with you?”

“Oh.” His whole face dropped in disappointment, as if the idea his little stunt wasn’t fun ruined his day.

“Why? Is this… is this a prank? Were you fucking with me? Filming somewhere?” I glanced around the room, half expecting to see his phone attached to a tripod, laughing at me. Nothing. Just Davey, his furniture, his now-blank whiteboard, and his book I was throwing back at him. “Don’t pull shit like this again. It’s not funny.”

Then he said something very ominous as I was leaving the room. Something I ignored because, frankly, I was fed up with him at that moment.

“They like it.”

The next day after Davey left for work, I immediately remembered the whiteboard. The message to leave them open before he actually did leave everything open. He was clearly messing with me, or so I thought at the time. I decided I would check what it said today. After all, I wanted to know what to expect from him this time.

And I gotta say, this one made my hair stand on end. Just a little. Just enough to linger with me through the work day:

Let Us Watch Him

I don’t know if it would be embarrassing to admit at this point, but I was looking over my shoulder all day. The cars in my rearview mirror. The people in the office. I was paranoid of any cameras that could be used to film me. Was I overreacting? I don’t know. Sure, it could just be a prank, but Davey doesn’t act like this. He never has. Honestly, I entertained the conspiracy that the CIA had kidnapped my brother and replaced him with a double. Whatever was going on, I didn’t see anyone watching me that whole day.

It was a relief when I got home and shut myself in my room. The day was long and I wanted to retire early before the weekend. I probably fell asleep around 9 PM.

Then, in the dead of night, I woke up to light spilling into my room.

Everything else was pitch black. My bed is positioned so that I’m not able to see out the door, so all I could see was the light. And half the silhouette at the foot of my bed.

At first I froze. My brain hadn’t processed anything besides that someone came into my room and was watching me in the middle of the night. My silent breaths hitched, as if I was choking on my own heart. My eyes were locked onto the figure, terrified of what it might do if I looked away. I studied it for any movement, but there was none. One would think I was simply mistaking a piece of furniture for a person, except I had none of that shape or position in my room.

The shadow in my room had its arms up, like it was holding something. I could only partially see what it was, but I could see corners. It still didn’t move.

Finally, the logic part of my brain kicked in. I concluded this had to be Davey. Rage replaced my fear and I quickly switched my lamp on. Sure enough he was standing there, holding his whiteboard up facing me. It was blank… as was the expression on his face.

“Davey, what the fuck are you doing!?”

He didn’t respond. He just stared at the wall behind my bed. Right before I tried again, his mouth started moving. It was like he was talking but no sound came out. And it didn’t stop. His mouth moved faster and faster, like he was reciting something. I didn’t even realize I was shrinking back in the bed.

Then Davey abruptly turned ninety degrees and walked out of my room, whiteboard still held up.

I didn’t confront him. And I didn’t sleep for the rest of the night. He acted like a robot. Or a puppet. This wasn’t my brother.

The next day, Saturday, I called Kira. I told her everything that Davey has been doing.

“No way.”

“Yeah. I don’t know what’s going on or what’s up with him.”

“Should we check him into a mental facility or something?”

“We can do that?”

“I dunno. Look it up.”

We couldn’t do that.

“Maybe we should just get him outside,” she suggested, “he might be cooped up at work and the apartment too much.”

“I don’t think he has cabin fever, Kira.”

“I’m not saying that. Just… touching grass could be helpful.”

“Well, it’s freezing outside so maybe he can touch some bowling balls instead. Are you free tonight?”

“No. But maybe tomorrow.”

After the call, I packed a change of clothes, toothbrush, deodorant, and pajamas into a small suitcase and poked my head out of my room. I called Davey’s name. He responded from his room. His door was closed, so I was sure he couldn’t see my suitcase. I told him I was going out. He didn’t ask where.

I went to our parents. They put me to work changing my dad’s oil, but that was fine. I was just happy to be away from Davey right now. I needed to recover from last night. And get some good sleep. They asked me how he was, and I told them he was a little stressed from work. Then I asked them if I could sleep over and they were fine with it since it was the weekend.

I waited for Davey to call or text asking where I was.

He didn’t.

On Sunday morning, when I came back, I didn’t see Davey in the main room, and he didn’t answer when I called. I slowly opened his bedroom door to see if he was sleeping. He wasn't there. My gaze drew to the whiteboard. It was turned around on the nightstand. I found that a bit unsettling. What was Davey trying to hide?

I decided I should just go in and look at it. The curiosity would eat me alive, especially since I was hanging out with him and Kira today. I grabbed it and flipped it around:

Don’t Stop For It

This one didn’t scare me like the last one. I was just confused. Then I got a mischievous idea. I figured I’d scare him a bit. I was gonna erase his stupid message and write one of my own. I wiped my hand across the whiteboard. No change. I did it again several times. The message didn’t go away. I even tried scraping it off with my fingernail, but it all felt smooth. I grabbed a sharpie from my room and pressed it to the whiteboard to scribble over it. It didn’t do anything. I didn’t know how that was even possible, but eventually gave up and returned the whiteboard to its original position and shut the door behind me.

Later on, Davey agreed that bowling sounded fun. Kira demanded we pick her up because she still hadn’t found her glasses.

“I can drive,” he said.

“Okay.”

When we arrived at Kira’s apartment, she got in the car and I could tell there was a hint of awkwardness behind her smile. It didn’t manifest in her voice, though. “Thanks for picking me up. Ugh, I need new eyes.” She continued to make conversation like normal. Davey did, too, which helped both of us relax a bit.

He behaved just fine at the bowling alley. Well, besides the usual trash talk when he got a turkey, but that was earned. Kira was having a great time and, I admit, I was, too. When Davey was bowling his final frame, Kira leaned over and whispered to me.

“See. Told you he just needed some fun.”

“Yeah.”

Of course, I knew there was more to his behavior. There had to be a deeper problem. But, at least for right now, he was my brother again.

Davey drove slowly through the icy complex to drop Kira back off at her place. We were joking and laughing like normal. For a second, I forgot how weird Davey had been acting. But just for a second.

A shadow limped into the road. Looked like a small animal. It stopped and looked at us.

“What the hell is that?” I said.

“Awww,” Kira said, leaning forward to see better, “looks like a wounded kitty.”

Davey didn’t react, and the car was getting uncomfortably close.

“Bro, stop.”

“Yeah, Davey.”

We got right up on it.

“Davey, STOP! What the hell!?”

The car bounced slightly as he slowly ran over it. It wasn’t until Kira smacked his face from the backseat that he stopped.

“Why the fuck did you do that!?” She had tears falling down her cheeks.

His response was monotone. “You should’ve had a front row seat.”

The back door opened and Kira climbed out. “Fuck you.” She slammed it.

I climbed out after her. She was looking down below the vehicle.

“Is it under there?” she mumbled.

“Probably. Kira, it’s definitely dead.”

“Davey has some serious fucking problems.”

“Oh, you think? That’s what I’ve been saying.”

She wiped her tears and flipped around. “I can’t do this. I’m going inside. Get the hell out of here.”

I tried going after her to calm her down, but she wasn’t having it. Eventually, I gave up and let her go inside.

When I swiveled back around towards the car, Davey was standing by the trunk. He had a very pronounced frown. I genuinely couldn’t decide if it was because his crush probably hated him now or if it’s because we didn’t like his macabre “show”.

We didn’t speak a word on the drive home. I wasn’t going to try and reason with him. I know it sounds crazy, but I was convinced at this point that the whiteboard was telling him what to do. I don’t know if it's some kind of demon or black magic or cursed object or whatever the hell you call it. All I know is it was evil and had to go.

Monday morning, I waited for Davey to go to work an hour before me, as usual. I was going to break the whiteboard into many pieces and dispose of it where he could never find it. I sat in my room, listening for his footsteps. Thump, thump, thump. They were uneven. No rhythm at all. Almost inhuman. Still, I waited. Eventually, the door swung open and shut. I rushed out of my room to look out the window of our apartment. I could see the parking lot where his car was parked, and I waited until he got inside and drove off. I wanted to make sure he didn’t catch me.

Excited, I approached his room. I grabbed his door handle and turned. The door inched open. I was going to get my brother back.

Then I saw it on his bed.

Being newly dead, I couldn’t smell any decomposition, but I still felt the need to hurl. He’d brought the cat home and laid it on the bed. I looked over at the whiteboard, which was turned sideways to face the dead animal. As I approached, I noticed a grimy tablespoon next to the cat’s corpse. You can probably guess what he did with it, so I won’t go into detail. I don’t like remembering it, anyway.

Then I looked over at the whiteboard, and my heart dropped:

Kira Needs New Eyes

The first thing I did was call Kira. She ignored me, obviously still upset from last night. I cussed. This was not the time for that. I texted her that if Davey shows up at her place, to not let him in. After several minutes of being left on read, she responded with ‘No shit’.

I figured I would have an easier time just calling the police.

I told them I had reason to believe someone was going to harm Kira and to send someone to her address. They asked me where she lived.

I’m going to be honest with you. I consider myself a person of average intelligence. At the very least, I’m not stupid. But gun to my head I couldn’t tell you Kira’s address. I put it into my GPS many times until I had memorized the way to her apartment. I got so used to driving by memory that I never even needed the address. Pair that with the fact that I just found out my brother may want to kill her and, well, I was drawing a blank. I didn’t even know the name of her complex.

I did my best to describe how to get there as I grabbed my keys, the whiteboard, and slid on my shoes. I gave them other details about Kira, including her phone number, and they assured me they would attempt to locate her via cell.

I hung up and texted Kira that I called the cops and that they were on their way over to keep Davey away. She was probably fuming because my phone was buzzing endlessly as I rushed to my car. I answered.

“Kira! Don’t leave your apartment.”

“Hmm.”

I was thrown off. She was calling and texting me furiously, so I expected yelling. But the other end of the line was eerily quiet.

“Kira?”

“Hmm?”

“Are you at your place?”

“Mhmm.”

“Is Davey there?”

“He left.”

My stomach dropped. “You didn’t let him in, right?”

“He left… them open.”

“What?”

“I see.”

“Okay I’m going to be there soon and we can talk about all this.”

“I see.”

The whiteboard was blank as I drove over.

When I arrived, I rushed up to the door and almost broke down. It was wide open, and Kira was standing in the middle of the room. It was like she was forcing herself to stand. Her knees were buckling and her arms hung lifelessly. But her face. Her smile. She was beaming at the ceiling like a child on Christmas. Her eyes, however, were covered by a shadow.

“Kira?”

She was giggling.

“Are you okay?” I moved in closer, but not too close. Honestly, she was scaring me.

“I see.”

“What do you mean, you see?”

“I see… you sleep.”

I froze. “You what?”

“He’s right.”

“Who? Davey?”

“It’s fun to watch.”

I sucked in a breath, hoping it would help me feel some bravery. It didn’t, but I moved closer to Kira. Close enough that I could see her eyes.

What I saw could only be described as an aberration. And she continued giggling about her newfound sight until the cops arrived.

Kira was in the hospital, last I heard. I haven’t seen my brother since. I destroyed the whiteboard the first chance I got, but honestly, I might have been too late.

I get the feeling whatever was in the whiteboard, telling Davey what to do, finally got what it wanted. Maybe it’s in Kira now, I don’t know. I don’t know if she’ll survive. I don’t know if Davey will ever be normal again. Maybe he is, and is just hiding from the cops. Or his guilt. I just hope he doesn’t do something to himself.

Even though he’s the prime suspect, the police have been suspicious of me as well. That’s the other reason I’m writing this. I’m documenting what happened so you people will know the truth.

I don’t have much more to say. I just hope Davey comes back.

Sometimes I think I see him in my room, but I probably just miss him.
