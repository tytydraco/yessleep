Today’s date is April 28th, 2026. Yesterday was April 27th, 2026. I remember because it was my dad’s birthday and I texted him, getting only a thumbs up emoji in response. My phone’s calendar says April 28th. My PC says April 28th. The scrolling signboard on the highway entrance ramp says ROAD WORK BEGINS APRIL 28TH, PLEASE USE ALTERNATE ROUTE. So why has it been April 28th for four days now?



I work as a freelance audiovisual engineer for various corporate events and meetings. If you need screens, projectors, lights, microphones, whatever for your sales kickoff summit or medical townhalls, my company gets the call. For a few months now, I’ve had a steady contract with a medical group as they hold meetings to discuss clinical trials for a new drug. By chance, I have been taking the old version of said drug for memory dysfunction after a car wreck I was in a few years ago. The doctors said that it would help stimulate my synaptic connections or something and as long as I kept at it with therapy I wouldn’t lose any more memories or motor function. I don’t think I can name the company or either drug since it’s still in development and I would prefer to keep my job, but I’m beyond freaked out at this point. I didn’t go into work today. I’m hoping a change in the routine will make something happen, so I’m telling you my story in the hopes that someone can help me.  




I woke up the first time on April 28th at 6:15 AM, snoozed my alarm twice, then showered, ate breakfast, a chocolate chip muffin and an iced green tea lemonade, fed my cat Pterodactyl, and drove to work. My next-door neighbor Jerry was taking his morning walk as I left, and he called out to me saying, 



“Mornin’, champ! Beautiful day out, huh? We definitely needed that rain yesterday!”  
I waved back, replying in the Midwestern ritualistic way, 



“Yep, sure did. You have a good one, boss man.” which always got a deep chuckle out of Jerry. I was late to work today because I forgot about the construction on the highway on ramp, forcing me to take the back way in. I turned on the projectors, sat down at the tech table in the back of the room, and prepared for a long, boring day of medical jargon and endless doctors who act like they’ve never seen a microphone in their lives when it’s their turn to speak. 



This first day, I couldn’t tell you a single thing they talked about. You learn to only have one ear open and listen for cues when one speaker is wrapping up so you can switch powerpoint presentations or turn off a mic pack. Lunch was an italian sub from the hole in the wall sandwich shop across from the conference center. The meeting ran long, they always do, and I left just before 5:00. I fed Pterodactyl again as I got home, reheated some pizza from the night before, doomscrolled on my phone for a bit, took my medicine and did my cognitive exercises, then watched The Truman Show and two and a half episodes of The Boys before passing out. I know you don’t care about my daily routine. I just need you to understand that if I describe everything that happened to me that day, you’ll know that I’m not making this all up. 



Everyone thinks they would figure out that they’re in Groundhog Day immediately. It seems obvious to them, there’s so much variance in even a short span of time, if your day is *exactly* the same, it's weird. You’d notice. I didn’t notice for longer than I’d care to admit. I didn’t notice the date hadn’t changed on my phone. I didn’t notice there were still two muffins left in the pack, even though I definitely ate one the previous morning. I have more or less the same conversation with Jerry every morning, since he always takes his walk when I’m leaving for work, so that didn’t seem out of the ordinary. I remembered the construction had started, since it made me late yesterday, or I guess today. I didn’t even notice that the opening speaker for the meeting was the same doctor giving the same dry, rehearsed speech since I was barely listening the first time. I went to the same sandwich spot for lunch, got a roast beef sandwich this time since I had an italian yesterday, or today, and didn’t think anything was amiss when the meeting ran late again and I left just before 5:00. The only thing I cared about was filling out the invoice for overtime since they only had me booked 8-4.   




I’m embarrassed to tell you the first sign of something being wrong I noticed was the stupid TV show. I was catching up since some of the guys at work were talking about the new season and I hadn’t seen it. It was season two, episodes two and three that I had finished last night, and got tired during episode four. My TV was telling me I had only watched up to episode one. But hey, I work with technology every day, glitches and weird stuff happens all the time. I took my medicine again, did my exercises, and went to bed. 



They say that after a traumatic head injury, routine is the best thing you can do to help you recover. Patterns, repeatable things that give you structure in a day so you aren’t disoriented and can let your brain retread the paths it’s created before. Day 3 was when I really began to get scared. Two muffins still in the pack. Jerry in the same navy blue shirt. Traffic. Dr. Daniels, as I learned his name was, constantly leaning away from his microphone. I paid attention to the speeches today. Some patients, they said, had been experiencing confusion, circadian rhythm disruption, and bizarre regression in otherwise healthy recovery times. Dr. Daniels posited something about paradoxical metabolization, and more jargon I didn’t really understand. I was starting to panic at this point. Was this why I was feeling this way? Was my brain just feeding me the information from April 28th as I went through the motions, despite it being April 30th?



At the afternoon break, I wanted to approach the doctor and tell him what I thought was happening to me. But I hadn’t really been paying attention the first two days of this meeting, it could have been totally distinct content. I don’t understand a lot of the terms and data they were showing on the slides, there wasn’t a way to know for sure. Plus, if they thought I was losing it, I’d get taken off my shift, and I needed the money. Their drugs weren’t cheap, and my health insurance was kinda shit. I just kept to myself and rode out the rest of the meeting, hoping my foggy brain would sort itself out in the meantime. 



When my leftover pizza was still in the fridge when I got home, that’s when I really started to spiral. April 26th. It had been April 28th for three days now. I know I’m not crazy. I get confused sometimes, sure. I had my skull practically cracked open like an eggshell in the accident. And I know progress isn’t linear. The doctors kept drilling that into me in the first few weeks of recovery. Some days would be better than others, some more lucid as my brain healed. But if I’m not in a time loop, are my eyes lying to me? My ears? Hearing and seeing the *exact same things* three days in a row? It’s beyond coincidence now. The movies always make it out as some colossal event, the fate of the world or your loved ones is at stake if you can’t figure out how to break the loop. But I get to be tortured by an endless, regular day at work. 



I didn’t take my medicine last night. I called off of work too. It’s day four as I write this, I think. It’s still April 28th. I don’t know what to do.
