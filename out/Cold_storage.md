The first thing you learn working in a morgue is that the dead are heavy in ways that have nothing to do with weight.

I don’t mean emotionally, though that’s true too, but physically. The silence presses down on you. The air hums with refrigeration units that never quite stop breathing. Even alone, you’re never alone. The bodies have a presence. A gravity.

I’d been working nights at Greenridge County Morgue for almost three years when the problem started. Graveyard shift, four nights a week. Less paperwork. Fewer families. Fewer questions.

Fewer living people, anyway.

My job was intake and prep. Tag the bodies, log identifying marks, assist with autopsies when needed, clean, catalog, store. Routine. Methodical. Respectful. I liked the predictability of it. The dead didn’t argue. They didn’t flirt. They didn’t ask how my day was or whether I planned on smiling more.

They just lay there.

Still.

The first time something moved, I told myself it was temperature contraction.

A toe twitched on a Jane Doe we’d pulled from the river. Waterlogged, bloated, early stages of decomp. I was alone in the prep room, documenting lividity patterns. Her foot jerked—sharp, fast, unmistakable.

I froze.

Then I exhaled and laughed at myself. Muscles can contract postmortem. Happens sometimes, especially with temperature changes. I’d learned that day one.

Still… it hadn’t felt like that. It felt intentional.

I finished my notes quickly and zipped her into the body bag, more unsettled than I wanted to admit.

The second time, I didn’t laugh.

It was three nights later. A man in his late forties, heart attack at home. Clean case. I was transferring him from gurney to table when his jaw snapped shut.

Not sagged. Not shifted.

Snapped.

The sound echoed in the tiled room—teeth clicking together hard enough to make me jump back.

I stared at his face. His lips were pale blue. His eyes sealed shut, lashes resting peacefully against sunken skin. He smelled faintly of antiseptic and old cologne.

“Residual nerve activity,” I whispered aloud, because saying it made it feel true.

But when I stepped closer again, his mouth slowly opened.

I backed away.

That’s when I started documenting things off the record. Times. Names. What moved. How. I told no one. Morgue techs don’t report “weird feelings.” You report facts or you lose your job.

By the end of the month, I was sleeping with the lights on.

Then came Cold Storage B.

Cold Storage B was the overflow unit. Older. Louder. The temperature controls were finicky, and management avoided using it unless we were slammed.

We were slammed.

A multi-car pileup on the interstate brought in six bodies in one night. By the time intake was done, Cold Storage A was full.

So I wheeled the last gurney into B.

The door groaned when I opened it, metal shrieking softly like it hadn’t been used in weeks. The air inside was colder—sharp enough to sting my lungs. Rows of steel drawers lined the walls, most empty, a few occupied.

As I slid the gurney alongside an open drawer, the lights flickered.

I muttered a curse and bent to transfer the body.

Female. Early thirties. Blunt force trauma. No visible facial damage. Eyes closed. Hands folded over her abdomen by the EMTs.

As I pulled the sheet back, her fingers twitched.

Not a spasm.

A curl.

I dropped the sheet.

My heart slammed against my ribs. I waited. Counted my breaths. Nothing else moved.

I told myself—again—it was nothing.

When I reached for her wrist to tag her, her hand grabbed mine.

Her grip was cold and wet and strong.

I screamed.

I yanked back, stumbling into the gurney behind me. By the time I looked again, her arm lay limp at her side, fingers relaxed.

I ran.

I didn’t clock out. I didn’t shut off lights. I just ran all the way to my car and sat there shaking until dawn.

I took a sick day. Then another.

On the third night, my supervisor called. Staffing shortages. I owed shifts. If I didn’t come in, I’d be replaced.

I didn’t tell him why I was scared. I couldn’t. Instead, I told myself maybe stress had finally gotten to me. Sleep deprivation. Burnout. Hallucinations.

I went back.

The building felt wrong the moment I stepped inside.

Too quiet.

Cold Storage B’s door was ajar.

I hadn’t left it open.

I followed the sound of breathing.

It was faint, rhythmic, coming from behind the steel drawers.

I should have left. Instead, I walked toward it, flashlight trembling in my hand.

One drawer was partially open.

The tag on the handle read my name.

I laughed nervously. Someone’s idea of a joke.

I pulled the drawer open.

Inside was me.

My body lay pale and slack, eyes sewn shut, lips slightly parted. A deep purple bruise bloomed around my throat. My hair was damp, strands stuck to my cheeks.

My tag listed cause of death as unknown.

I staggered back, bile burning my throat.

Something grabbed my ankle.

Hands—dozens of them—burst from the surrounding drawers, fingers gray and peeling, nails blackened. They clawed at my legs, my arms, my torso, dragging me down onto the floor.

The breathing grew louder.

I screamed until my throat tore.

They pulled me into an open drawer, metal biting into my shoulders, my hips. The cold was unbearable—like being packed in ice and razor blades. Fingers forced my arms to my sides, folded my hands over my abdomen.

I thrashed, but the drawer slid shut with a final, ringing clang.

Darkness.

Then pressure.

Something heavy pressed down on my chest.

A face loomed inches from mine—my own.

Her eyes opened.

They were wrong. Milky. Filmed over.

“You left us alone,” she whispered, breath frosting the air. “Now you stay.”

Pain exploded through my throat as her hands closed around it.

I woke up screaming on the morgue floor.

Lights blazed overhead. Cold Storage B was sealed. Quiet. Empty.

My throat burned.

When I touched it, my fingers came away purple with bruises.

Deep, finger-shaped bruises.

HR let me go two days later. Said I’d had a panic episode. Unsafe behavior. Liability.

I never went back.

The bruises faded after weeks, but the scars didn’t. Sometimes I wake up gasping, convinced I can hear drawers sliding shut.

And sometimes, when I pass a refrigerated truck on the highway, I swear I see my name stenciled on the side.

Waiting.
