Throwaway account because I don’t want the Thompson’s (fake name for privacy reasons) finding out about this.

About six months ago, I (25M) found a nice home in the north side of my dream city (which city I won’t mention) for a steal. Two stories with a finished basement, four bedroom, three bath, and a massive yard with a shed. It had been on the market for weeks and I was able to afford it on a paralegal’s salary. I couldn’t sign fast enough. The seller wouldn’t look me in the eye, but I just assumed they were an incredibly rude person.

In hindsight, I should have asked more questions about the history of the house, but I wasn’t about to look a gift horse in the mouth. A prime piece of real estate that I could sell whenever I wanted for a massive profit? I thought the seller was a moron or insane for selling the house for dirt cheap. 

I’m sure the Thompson’s thought that about me too.

It was early October when I finished settling in. The house was everything I could ever want: a bachelor pad that I wasn’t embarrassed to bring someone back to, a three hour drive from my parents, and large rooms with enough space for my cat Tux to make it his own.

Things were quiet for about a week.

After that, the occurrences started pretty tame. A quiet knock in the middle of the night, footsteps on the roof, etc. It was just enough to make me second guess what I had heard. But every time, I just shrugged and covered myself once more with the comforter and pretended that I heard nothing.

It was around the third week that it started getting difficult to ignore. Faint laughter stirred me from my sleep one night; I looked outside to see if the neighbors were outside or something, but I found no one.

Suddenly, a soft tapping sound echoed from the bottom of the stairs. I opened my bedroom door and squinted through the darkness, convincing myself that I wasn’t terrified to see what it was. 

The tapping ascended, drumming faster and faster as it grew closer and closer. As it reached the top of the stairs and turned, the taps started sprinting to my bedroom door. Panicked, I flipped the hallway light to confront whatever was coming. I stood staring at an empty hallway, no tapping sound anywhere.

A few more similar occurrences happened in the following weeks, and I started to assume that whatever was in this house just wanted to mess with me. 

Then they killed Tux.

By now it was mid-December and I was running a little late one morning, so I didn’t stop to think about the fact that Tux wasn’t asleep next to me in bed. I followed my usual routine: shave, brush, shower. I was buttoning my shirt as I rushed down the stairs; I hadn’t turned on the living room lights before my foot caught a patch of something slick. My legs split and I tensed in pain.

I flipped the switch and was greeted by a dark crimson pool, a perfect circle interrupted only by the streak made by my heel. I leaned closer, unsure of this strange substance on my hardwood floor.

\*drip\*

A single splash broke the surface of the ruby lake, splattering a tiny speck onto my face. It was then that I distinctly remember noticing the smell of copper. I eased back and stared until another drip broke the silence; I followed its path to the ceiling.

A scream caught in my throat and silent tears burned my shuddering cheeks.

Tux was hanging from the ceiling by his tail. His eyes were glossy black and his mouth was slack, mimicking an almost human cry.

My mind ran circles around itself, trying to understand what or why. My only friend in the city was displayed like an absurd Christmas ornament. At that moment, I knew that something was watching me and it wasn’t God.

I buried Tux in the backyard, another tidbit of info I failed to mention to the Thompson’s. 

That night, I tossed and turned, subjected to a chorus of choked meows and hisses coming from outside my window. The rooftop footsteps thumped louder and the tapping inched closer along my wall. I hid under my sheets and prayed that I survive until the morning.

When I awoke, I immediately put the house up for sale at a price far lower than what I bought it for.

As I waited for a single bid to come in, I started documenting the occurrences and learned that everything starts at 9:47 pm; unfortunately, I was sleeping in the shed by this point, so I have no idea what time it ends in the morning.

As weeks passed, I learned more and more. The tapping sound would follow you throughout the house during the night, but only in the dark. As long as you were surrounded by light, the tapping would leave you be.

Furthermore, the footsteps on the roof would follow the exact same pattern every night at random intervals: It started from one end of roof then tromped across to the other side, the same route every time. In the mornings, a patch of grass was browned beneath the end of the footsteps’ path. It regained its color by noon.

But there was one aspect of the house that I wouldn’t investigate. One aspect that I should have warned the Thompson’s about.

The basement.

Throughout my stay, the door to the basement remained closed. I never bothered checking it out after the tour.

But it smirked at me.

After 9:47 every evening, it called to me. Soft cries and gentle murmurs coaxed me closer. It begged me to listen. I almost reached for the knob one night, but I fought the impulse, instinctively knowing my possible demise lay behind that door. I withdrew my hand.

And it howled at my refusal, beating back at me in frustration. The floor pounded under my feet and I retreated to the safety of my shed that evening.

I cried when I learned that the house had sold. Some poor suckers had been roped in, just as I had. It was a young couple, fit and attractive. Full of hope and happiness. They droned on about how thankful they were to find a house in their budget. How nice it was that their kids had a large yard to play in.

I wanted to warn them. I wanted to tell them to keep the lights on. Keep your kids away from the basement. But I didn’t. I wanted the house to sell and I didn’t want them asking questions.

I couldn’t even look them in the eye as they signed the paperwork.

I shook Mr. Thompson’s hand, standing on the rug that hid Tux’s bloodstain. I forced a smile, continuously glancing at the clock that ticked 5:22 pm. Their daughter’s shrill laughter gripped my heart as she chased her puppy around the yard. Yet I made idle chatter, watching as their young son crept closer to the basement door, nodding his head as if talking to a friend.

With a pleasant nod, I bid farewell and made for the foyer. And whatever sort of bastard lies in wait behind that basement door, it thanked me as I left.
