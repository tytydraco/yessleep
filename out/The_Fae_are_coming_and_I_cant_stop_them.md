The Cù Sìth howls 3 times. The first howl lets you know the hound has found you, its prey. The second howl means it’s close. The final howl takes you. Most predators will kill their prey by their own hand, claw, fang or beak. Not the Cù Sìth. Legend says that its howl is so terrifying that fear is what kills you.



Harbingers of death in mythology are a dime a dozen, but the Cù Sìth is slightly different. Not only does it kill, but sometimes it drags its victims into the Faerie Realm instead. I’m honestly not sure which fate is worse. I share this because I’ve experienced the Cù Sìth, and I need to get this out there.



When I was young, my grandmother would often try to scare me with stories of the fae, warning me of creatures like the Selkie.



“Seachain na cladaichean, a ghràidh!” she would sometimes rave at me, “avoid the shores, my dear!” When I told her I didn’t identify as man nor woman, she would cry out that the fae had taken her grandson and replaced me with a changeling. As much as it hurt me to hear back then, looking back I only care for the good times. I say all this to express that this stuff was commonplace to me when I was growing up.



When I grew up, I stayed as close to home as I could, keeping the memory of the clan close to my heart. I never could settle, though. I lived alone for a while, my own cabin that I built by hand, a small garden with all the veg I’d need. It was a peaceful life, a quiet life. I had grown accustomed to the quiet. The call of a raven would carry across the moors and the sound of wind would sing through the trees. 



One night, I was out and about, strolling by a nearby loch. The water was glassy and reflected the constellations and moon like a perfect mirror. Then, off in the distance, I heard a deafening howl. Like a wolf speaking with the voice of a lion, echoing through a great valley. The sudden noise violently pierced through my comfortable silence. It rumbled through my ears, and made my hairs stand on end.



I froze, listening carefully for anything. I almost convinced myself that I had imagined it, a trick of the isolation. The human mind is funny like that, it can invent sensations when deprived of experiences. But then I looked out over the loch and I saw ripples caused by nothing. My shoulders tightened and then I looked at the opposite shore, every memory from my gran flooding through my head. Kelpies running across the lochs, Baobh-shìth tricking men, The Ghillie Dhu guarding his trees, and more. Each story flashing through in an instant, colourful memories and gran’s smoker’s voice.



Then I saw a shadow, shifting on the shore. The size of a bull, and the origin of the ripples. It was then that I recognised the one story my gran would tell with seriousness beyond any other. The Cù Sìth.



“He’ll howl at ya,” she would warn, “and when he does, that first howl tells ya to run. Run on home and lock ya’ door. Put the crudha over the door, and try to sleep.”



The first howl means you’re still safe, but you don’t have long. The thoughts had gone through my head, and my legs were running before my mind could tell them to move. As soon as I realised what was happening, I heard it again.



Another howl. Louder, closer, deeper. Echoing in itself, vibrating through me and outside of me. I looked behind me, and I saw the grass shifting. It had cleared the loch in seconds, and it was stalking me. I saw dark green fur, either matted by filth or slicked by the loch, a braided tail swaying back and forth, and each step was silent. That was the worst part. It wasn’t walking lightly, but it made no sound. Not a growl, not a bark, not a whine. A silent hunter.



I ran faster than before, my legs burning more as I forced them to push off with more and more force. I saw my cabin up ahead, the light of my campfire outside beckoning me closer, the promise of safety. It filled me with hope, excitement, and peace. I was almost at the boundary to my sanctuary.



Then one more howl. My heart skipped a beat. A cold river of sweat ran down my neck. My breath caught in my throat, and my hands began to tremble. Yet I wasn’t dead.



“When ya’ hear the third howl, it’s too late. The heart stops. The blood won’t flow. The fear will grip ya’.” Gran would warn.



Then I heard more. A growl. The snarl of a hunter, about to rip and tear its prey apart. My body froze, ‘why haven’t I dropped dead?’ I thought. Then I felt a sharp pain in my leg.



The sharp pain of dog teeth ripping into my calf. The heat of blood trickled down towards my foot, and the heat of pain stabbed into my bones. I cried out, screaming in agony. Then I felt a jolt, as I started getting dragged by the hound. My face fell straight into the dirt, I could feel my nose dig a hole on impact.



Grass and dirt rushing past my eyes, blurring any chance of seeing what was happening, all I had was sound. The rushing of wind, the growling and panting of the dog. Then suddenly, I felt water. Drenching me to my core, the searing pain of water rushing into my lungs. I’m not sure if I was crying or not, but I think I was. I truly thought I was about to die.



Then there was nothing. It's difficult to explain. As humans, existence and “stuff” is all we know. I used to think I could comprehend the concept of “nothing” but I was wrong. I could see the void, and I knew that's what I was seeing because I couldn't see. I could hear the silence, and I knew that's what I was hearing because I couldn't hear. I could feel nothing, and I knew that's what I was feeling because I couldn't feel.



Then suddenly I was warm. I couldn’t feel the crushing pressure of the waves above me anymore, I couldn’t hear anything again. Then I heard birds chirping, as if it was the middle of a summer’s day. The pain in my leg had vanished. I sat up and looked around. The closest way I can describe what I saw is a swamp, but wrong. Trees with leaves of blue, purple grass that grew inversely. Streams running a greenish-orange hue. I scanned the area around me and that’s when I noticed a throne.



Great and made of stone, adorned with gems and engraved knotwork. Smaller thrones sat beside it, smaller but equally as majestic. On them, sat creatures.



They appeared like humans, but perfect. Their skin was soft and clear, with eyes that could stop anyone in their tracks. Their hair flowed like the purest of streams and shone with the brilliance of stars. Deep emerald green cloaks adorned their bodies with crowns made of the brightest gold.



“Human from Sgùrr Fhuaran. Young child from Alba. I humbly ask for your name.” The voice echoed within it and within me. Deep and groaning, coursing through my veins. The voice was everywhere and nowhere, vibrating through my bones, rattling my thoughts. My gran’s warnings flooded my head.



“No… No, you may not have my name…” I shuddered, my mind scanning through all names I knew. Lying to this Fae wouldn’t be smart, but neither would saying my name. My mind landed on when gran called me a changeling, “but you may call me Lachlan.” Saying the name felt like a dagger between my ribs, the name that I had buried long ago. Gran used to say that our true name isn't necessarily the one our parents gave us, so all I had was hope.



The creature shifted, although no face was there to convey the emotions, I could sense its frustration.



“You were Lachlan, once. But that is not your true name… You know our rules, our games,” the voice creaked through my ears, “but do you know the truth?”



“I…” I took a deep breath, “I do not claim knowledge, only that I have been given information.” A long pause followed, the blank faces shifting slightly as if weighing my words.



"You speak with caution, human. Will you share your thoughts, so you might be relieved of their burden?" The voice chirped and clicked, like a dolphin searching for prey.



A shiver ran down my spine as I stared at the creature, and the sound of the hounds growling softly started behind me.



“I will not… My thoughts are mine alone,” I swallowed a lump of anxiety, “but I will tell you I desire to return home. I tell you to make the fact known, not shared.”



“Are you repulsed by us, human, or do you embrace us?” The voice trickled down my spine, yet travelled up to my ears.



“Neither. I recognise the knowledge that you exist.” I spoke with confidence that I never knew I had. Dancing around each word like it was a landmine, primed to blow.



“What do you wish for, human?” it softly whispered. I took a deep breath and thought for a moment.



“I do not make any wishes here, but I ask for the knowledge of who I am speaking to.”



“You desire for us to gift that knowledge to you?”



“No!” I cried in frustration, “I do not want a gift nor do I want you to give me something. I merely ask for the knowledge to be explicit.” I swallowed, aware that a single word could tip the balance.



A laugh came from the fae. I know it was a laugh, but I don’t know how I knew. It sounded like the ground shifting under the weight of a herd of mammoths. The faceless head smiled at my outbreak, like it was tasting alcohol after years of abstaining. Like a starving shark smelling blood in the water.



“We are known by your kind as the Unseelie Court. But our names are ours to keep, and only yours to wonder about.”



My gut fell to the bottom of my torso. My face fell as I shivered before the court of the redcaps, of the sluagh. I stared in fear-struck awe at the court of mischief and pain and death.



“You react to the knowledge,” the voice purred, “you know of us, which is why you speak with such care. I acknowledge the caution in your words.”



“Than-” I cut myself off. I stopped and thought. Would thanking it for the acknowledgement count as accepting something from them?



I looked around again. The trees swayed against the wind, and the birds chirped in conversation. The place around me was idyllic. It was peaceful beyond peace. Almost intentionally too peaceful.



“Do you accept my praise of you, Lachlan?” it asked.



“I acknowledge, but do not accept it.” I said defiantly.



“Very well. You desire to leave this place, and we can grant you that. But we have conditions.” It tilted its head as it stared me down.



“What are these conditions?”



“You must never return. You must abandon your mountain, and your home. You must leave the land of Alba, and never step foot in its borders again. Do you agree to these conditions?” The voice wrapped around me like vines grasping up a fence.



“I agree to the conditions, on the grounds that it allows me to leave at this moment.” I said each word carefully and distinctly. Suddenly, I heard a crack of lightning behind me. I could smell the air of the loch, and I could feel the wind flowing through. I turned to leave, and saw a crack floating in the air before me. I stepped forwards, when the voice beckoned me once more.



“Do you give your word?”



“Yes-” I stopped dead in my tracks as I felt like I had been winded, the word itself being ripped from my lungs. That same, deep laugh rolled across the earth before me, causing the trees to shiver in fear. The blades of grass quivered in anxiety. Suddenly, the crack in the air before me rushed towards me, leaving me stranded. I gasped, inhaling the air, the taste of freedom flooded me with relief. I went to laugh.



Nothing. 



No sound came out. I tried to speak, I tried to tell myself it was okay. My mouth opened, I could feel the muscles in my throat pulling and pushing air, but all I could hear was the air escaping.



I had given it my word.



I looked around the hills, scanning to get my bearings. It was dark out, but I immediately knew the landscape looked different. The treeline had higher peaks and lower depths. The air bit into my skin through my coat. I continued to scan, when I saw my cabin. It was darkened, but it was my cabin. The darkness crept out of it, but it still calmed me. It's strange, once you've seen evil, the horror of mundanity ceases to exist and the unease of life brings comfort. Although “evil” isn't the right word, they were evil in the sense a storm wiping out a farm is evil. I tell myself that makes it easier, but it doesn't.



I approached my cabin hoping to have just a brief bit of respite, and then I noticed a tattered piece of paper stapled to the door.



My face looked back at me, with the word “missing” in large bold letters above the picture. I breathed deep and entered my house. The door creaked as I had to use strength to open it, and when it finally shot open, a storm of dust kicked up. My eyes slowly adjusted to the dark as I looked around. I could make out spiderwebs, shining silver in the moonlight, coating my walls. I took a deep breath, choking slightly on the hurricane of dust, and pulled my phone out of my pocket.



The last time I checked my phone, it was 9pm, October 12th, 2019.



6 years had passed in a matter of minutes.



But there’s something worse. I hear small chittering sounds around the house. I know what they are, somehow deep inside me I know the Brownies are here, cleaning what they think is a mess. I know there are 3 Kelpies at the Loch near my house, waiting for children to want to pat the horse. I know there is a Shellycoat at a nearby river, getting ready to pretend it's a drowning child.



I also know they’re planning something. I didn't escape because I “won,” I escaped because they thought that would be more interesting. They took my voice, but I won’t let them silence me.



Carry iron, guard your name, and if you hear a deafening howl in the middle of the night, don't wait for a second.
