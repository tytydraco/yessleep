The devil followed me my whole life and only now knocked on my door. I know I sound crazy, but give me the benefit of the doubt.

It was a night like any other. I was eating with my daughter Laura. I had made meatloaf.

Until I heard a slow:

Knock, knock.

My house filled with a particular smell. I thought a gas valve had opened, that I had left something in the oven.

But it was his smell. Smell of gas, sulfur, your favorite food burned. That’s his perfume. It follows me, and he knows I hate it.

I went as fast as I could.

I was assuming the worst.

The smell was getting stronger.

I reached the peephole. It was a woman.

I had seen her before.

Years ago, I used to go to a club. Around 2 in the morning, every now and then, a woman would appear.

The world would stop, slow down, grow darker — and not because it was night.

Slow songs would start playing. Jazz, blues, and the like.

She would ask the worst men to dance.

Sexual abusers, wife-beaters, thieves… and, from time to time, ordinary people from my town.

Slow rhythms played because she wanted them to savor what little they had left. But I cannot conceive the idea of a kind devil.

I watched her dance. Slowly. She clung to the men, squeezed them, kissed them, made them hers. She looked at them with those blue eyes.

These men looked into her eyes and saw heaven. How contradictory she is.

Couldn’t they smell it? Didn’t they think it was a dream? That it was too good to be true?

Then she looked at me. As if she saw something special. As if she knew I could see her toys. As if I had a gift. As if I were the only one who could see her in her playground.

She smiled at me.

It wasn’t a long smile — those disturbing smiles people describe.

It was a calm smile. Like someone drugged. Like someone you don’t know whether they’re happy or dying of sadness.

I don’t understand why.

Maybe because she knew that someday I would sin. That someday I would be hers. That someday she would ask me to dance.

Or simply out of pride, for being the queen of the night. Just for knowing that the only person capable of detecting her couldn’t do anything.

He tortured me that way.

Then she took them.

So they would never return.

That’s why now it was my turn.

He is patient. He doesn’t act impulsively. He is reason and the unreasonable.

He knows he could break the door without a key. He knows I know him well. He knows I know.

That’s why he waits.

One wrong move and the show would be ruined.

I took my daughter upstairs. I told her to play Mario. That I would come up in a bit.

I went down the stairs as fast as I could.

I didn’t want to look through the peephole, but I did.

It was my wife.

My wife had died from an overdose. She had problems with substances.

He knows I can’t sleep because of the memories. He knows she died on that same couch, from her own vomit.

He knows that if I hadn’t fought with her, that wouldn’t have happened. I would have been with her to save her, to take her to a hospital. So that today she would be having dinner with us.

He simply knows. He plays with my memories.

He came to collect.

He’s surely smiling, staring at me. Waiting for a reaction, for my tears. He wants me to kneel, to ask for forgiveness. He wants to see me resigned.

He just wants to crucify me, hammer the final nail.

But I won’t give him that pleasure. I won’t cry. I know him better than myself.

I snap out of the trance.

I dared to look through the peephole again and saw darkness. He was gone. I suppose that’s what he is. Darkness.

But I could see something else.

I strained my eyes — more than I ever did in eye exams.

All those poor men whose lives were taken by him were there.

They were standing, stiff as stone. Watching his best show. They were there to see my last dance.

I looked away.

I was hyperventilating. Breathing in his perfume, which kept growing stronger.

Until I heard my daughter’s voice upstairs:

— Dad… do you want to come play Mario with us?

The sound of a saxophone and piano started playing. I hear it upstairs.

He wants me to go up.

What should I do?
