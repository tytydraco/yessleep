I don’t know how I know before I even open my eyes in the morning, but I do. There’s a pressure in the room, like being watched from somewhere that shouldn’t be able to see me. Slowly, I open my eyes. The bedroom is dim, lit only by a thin stripe of streetlight, and across from the bed is the dresser mirror. At first, everything looks normal; the bed, the curtains, the open doorway behind me. And then I see her.

Samara is standing beside my bed.

I don’t turn. I don’t move. I only see her in the mirror. Her head is tilted slightly, and she’s smiling. It’s too wide, too still, like it’s something that’s been attached to her face rather than something she feels. My throat tightens. “Samara?” I whisper.

The reflection doesn’t answer.

Very slowly, I turn my head.

There’s nothing there.

My heart stutters. I look back at the mirror.

She’s closer now. Right beside my shoulder. Still smiling.

“I was watching you,” she says.

Her voice isn’t coming from the room, it’s more like it’s wrapped around me. I spin around again. Nothing. When I turn back, the mirror is empty.

I wake up with a start and tell myself it was a dream. I watch Samara closely that morning at breakfast.

She sits nonchalantly, with a mini pancake in one hand dripping syrup down the the side of her arm, and a crayon in the other. She sighs deeply and says like it’s nothing “You look different in the mirror.”

I freeze. What did she say? “How?” I ask, struggling to keep my voice from shaking. She shrugs, still coloring. “Sometimes you don’t match.”

A cold weight settles into my chest. “What do you mean?”

She presses harder on the crayon until it snaps. “Sometimes you’re smiling in there when you’re not out here.”

I force a laugh, but it sounds wrong. “That’s not possible.”

She looks up at me then, completely serious. “I don’t like it when you look at it for too long.”





That night, I wake again. This time I don’t look around the room. I look straight at the mirror, and I wish I hadn’t. In it, I’m sitting up in bed.

Except I’m not.

I’m lying down. I can feel the mattress under me, the weight of the blanket. But in the mirror, I’m upright, head slightly tilted, smiling. Slowly, the reflection of me raises its head and looks not at me, but at Samara, standing beside the bed again.

“I told you mommy,” Samara whispers, her voice small and tight, not looking at me… But looking at the mirror. “Don’t look at it for too long.”

My heart pounds so hard it hurts. I force myself to stay still, feeling my heartbeat in my ears. In the mirror, the smiling version of me begins to turn its head. Too slowly. Too deliberately. Toward where I’m lying.

I squeeze my eyes shut.

I don’t remember falling asleep after that. But I wake again in the morning. Everything looks normal. Light spills into the room. The mirror shows exactly what it should.

Samara is sitting on the edge of the bed, watching me. No smile this time. Just fear.

“Mommy,” she says softly.

I sit up carefully, avoiding the mirror. “What is it?”

Her voice shakes. “You keep getting up at night.”

My stomach drops. “No, I don’t.”

She nods quickly. “You do. You stand by my bed.”

Something cold crawls up my spine. “What do I do?”

She swallows. “You just look at me.”

A long pause. I can barely breathe.

“And you smile.”

I shake my head, but it feels weak. “That’s not me.”

“That’s what I thought too,” she whispers sadly, and stalks off looking defeated.

That night, I wake again.

I don’t move. I don’t breathe. I look at the mirror.

And I see myself standing beside the bed.

Looking down at where I’m lying.

Smiling.

Slowly, carefully, the reflection-me raises a hand and points directly at me on the bed.

Behind my reflection, something shifts.

Something tall.

It leans closer, its shape barely visible, like it’s made of shadow stretched too thin. Its hand rests on my reflection’s shoulder. Not heavy. Not forceful. Just… there. Guiding.

Like it belongs.

Like it’s helping.

I can’t stop myself. I turn to look… And there’s nothing beside the bed.

No one.

My breath catches as I turn back to the mirror.

My reflection is no longer standing.

It’s lying down.

Back in the bed.

Exactly where I am.

Perfectly still.

Eyes closed.

But the smile is still there.

I feel it then.

Not in the mirror.

On my own face… A tugging at my lips.

Sidward. Upward.

From the doorway I see Samara , eyes wide, her voice breaking, “That’s not you anymore.”

I try to move. I can’t. I try to speak. Nothing comes out.

All I can do is stare at the mirror, at the version of me staring back… Eyes now open, wide and aware, smiling wider than the normal stretch of skin allows, as the long shadow leans down close to its ear.

And in my own voice, perfectly calm, perfectly familiar, I hear it whisper:

“Look, now we match.”
