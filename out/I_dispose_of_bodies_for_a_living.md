 It’s really not as grim as you might think, at least for me. You see, I used to work at a mortuary before I was fired for certain “indiscretions”. Finding a new job with my lack of experience in anything else, not to mention the substantial black mark on my record, proved to be quite impossible. With homelessness breathing down my neck, I ended up in my current line of work, and I was pleasantly surprised at how easy it was. There aren’t as many psycho murderers out there as you might think, so most of my gigs were with organized crime guys; assassinations, gunfights and all that.

Unfortunately, no job is perfect. Sleeplessness, chemical burns, bloodborne diseases and the disapproval of the government are among the downsides of my work; but the worst one has to be “the weirdos”. “The weirdos” is jargon for people who leave behind bizarre and often scenes and then call someone to clean it up, at least in my area. See, the aforementioned psycho murderers don’t contact people like me because they want their handiwork to be found. Unless something goes wrong, they want nothing to do with us. When people realized the odd nature of these jobs, they coined the term “weirdo”.

 I don’t get to socialize with my “co-workers” much, but I heard through the grapevine that these “weirdo” jobs have been popping up in the area for as long as anyone has been in business, and some people even quit and left the area entirely because of them. At first, I was skeptical, but once I had my first taste of a job like that, I myself considered a change of scenery.

I can’t give you an exact date for obvious reasons, but it was a cold winter day when I got the call. The voice on the other end was the audio equivalent of those ransom notes made by cutting up letters from magazines and whatnot. Not exactly common practice, but I wasn’t experienced enough to know that at the time. The cut-up voice on the other hand told me a time and a location, and that I would be paid a hefty amount on site. I really needed the money, so when the time came, I packed my tools and drove my shitbox out over an hour to a rust covered building with busted windows. It was late night by time I got there

My first impressions of the scene were mild. The building was an old toy factory, producing all kinds of dolls. The absence of windows left the inside of the building cold and damp. The place smelled like rust and concrete, with a familiar hint of copper pennies that I had learned to ignore. I walked between rows of old doll parts, left abandoned whenever the factory was shut down. 

There was a strange absence of anything unusual and before long, I was uneasy. I could smell a body, but not see it. Nothing about this place felt right. It was too inconspicuous to be the scene of a crime; it was more akin to place to hide a body. I searched for the corpse for a long time, and panic set in the longer I searched. Was this a set up? Would the police burst in through the door and arrest me? Was I playing some sick game?

It was still pitch dark outside when I found it. Hidden under one of the shelves, a hatch rusted nearly shut, the smell of decay wafting from within. The hatch had a ripped piece of paper taped firmly to it. On the paper, written in a delicate hand, was the word “hello”, alongside a smiley face. Though unsettled by the note, I relaxed a little, thinking the body would be down there and I would finally be free of this place. Lifting the heavy iron hatch, I shone my hand crank flashlight into a wooden basement. Without thinking, I slid down through the hatch, only for the rot eaten wooden flood to give way.

I landed with a wet thud. The floor beneath me was cold, wet and stinking like nothing I had ever experienced. I clicked my flashlight to life, and saw what I was standing on. The floor was completely covered in rotting, oxidized, brown meat; put through a meat grinder. I was near hip deep in the foulness, and I realized with sudden terror that I was not alone in there. Something was crawling towards me, like a shark beneath murky waves. I began to scramble for a way out, desperately grasping at the rotten floorboards above me. As the thing moved closer, it sucked up the refuse that had gathered on the floor, spraying a terrible, smooth puree. I pulled myself up to the rotten pit, desperately praying that the boards wouldn’t let me die.

I threw myself through the hatch, rolling onto the cold factory floor half trying to catch my breath and half trying not to vomit. Covered in rotting, almost liquefied meat and shivering, I saw through teary eyes a rotting, gangly arm, made from the same stuff in the flesh pit I had just escaped, reaching out through the horrid basement. It groped around the floor for a time, only to grab the hatch with its long, slender fingers and gently pull it shut.

Mouth agape, I must have sat on the cold floor for at least half an hour before I could move again.

Around a month later, I received a letter in the mail. The envelope was damp, plump, and stank of rotten meat, bringing back awful memories. Written on the envelope, in the same delicate hand, were the words “See you soon”. A smiley face was drawn next to it. I ripped the fat envelope open with my nail, only to find twenty thousand dollars in cash.

I haven’t received a similar job since, but I don’t think I’m rich enough to be turning down jobs quite yet.
