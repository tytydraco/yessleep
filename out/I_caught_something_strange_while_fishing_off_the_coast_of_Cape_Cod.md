It all started as a simple father-son fishing trip. Rent a cottage by the water, wake up early for the best catch, and bond over shared boredom turned peacefulness. 

It was my son Dylan’s final summer before college, and we never exactly had a textbook relationship. 

He was a shy kid, tucked so deep inside his shell that getting him to shed that shield was difficult for my ex-wife (his mother), and near *impossible* for me. 

But, at the end of the day, I loved him more than anything—and if it was quiet he wanted for our last weekend together, well, I knew exactly where to take him. 

From the second we stepped out of the van, the deafening silence of the Cape in the offseason was apparent, hitting us even before the salty smell of brined algae. 

Even the gulls were hushed, the dense fog cutting their squawks, reducing them to whispered reminders of the feathered world above. 

“Head on down there,” I nudged my son, gesturing towards the beach, “Scope out the area while I unpack". 

I received no verbal confirmation, but with an earnest nod and an AirPod pop,  Dylan set forth to the sea. 

I tracked his stride, the uncoordinated side-step of a teen too tall for his own good, until he was nothing more than a lanky outline, impressed in the coastal fog. After it swallowed my son whole, I grasped both of our duffels and turned to face the cottage. 

It was *ancient.* Midcentury, I think, and as I dropped my load in the entryway, I realized how uninhabited it had been since then. 

A cloud of dust billowed up from under the bags, filling my lungs with a musty plumage. There must have been heaps of sand mixed within it, because my throat began to feel violently raw, the coarse pellets scraping down walls of pink flesh, chafing it red. 

Finally, once my trachea felt like a pulpy mess and my vocal cords a shredded curtain, the shards of sand dropped into my stomach acid the way a penny plunks into a wishing well. My wish was for the pain to stop. 

Coughing, I stumbled back and shut the door, wondering how someone could rent it out in that condition. The cottage surely hadn’t seen a guest in years- and a maid in decades. 

I brushed it off, however, because if it truly was as abandoned as it looked, the fish population would be thriving. Nobody would have even set foot in these waters, let alone cast a line. 

*More for us*, I thought. 

“Hey Dylan,” I shouted hoarsely as I meandered down to the ocean, rods in hand, “This is prime real estate”. 

“What?” he called back, palming his right AirPod and squinting up at me through the fog. 

“The beach. Nobody’s been here for ages. We have our choice of the catch!” I tossed Dylan a rod, and he caught it clumsily. 

Looking back, I could tell Dylan wasn’t the biggest fan of the sport. I could tell, yes, and I chose to ignore it. Pretended I couldn’t see how feigned his enthusiasm was. 

After all, it was a staple for my father and me, and just as important for his father and him. 

I thought even if Dylan couldn’t appreciate that, he would at least understand the tranquility. 

There’s a serenity to the sea that calms even the stormiest heart. A magical property that draws even the unwilling to it. 

This is how I justified bringing him- but there is no justifying what happened next. 

We should have just gone to the movies. Or played a video game. Or done whatever the fuck else teenage boys like to do. 

But there we were, lines cast towards the open ocean, when I felt a tug. 

Dylan and I watched as the pill colored bobber was ripped under the surface with a force so tremendous it almost took the rod with it. 

I gripped tightly, digging my bare toes into the sand to anchor myself, but whatever was on the other end of the line was no fish. It was only when I heard Dylan’s yell that I realized that I wasn’t reeling it in; it was the other way around. 

“Dad!” As I skimmed through the shallows like a waterskier attached to a jetski, Dylan clasped my arm with one hand and the rod with another. 

“Dyl,” I shouted over the sound of rumbling clouds, creeping over the increasingly choppy ocean. “I need you to *reel*”. 

Letting go of the knob, I swung my arm around to Dylan’s back, stabilizing us in the sand as he grabbed the knob, twisting like a madman. 

“I’m getting it,” Dylan clammered, the sea spraying his face as the waves began to crash. 

The ocean was angry, but my son held fast—and I held him. 

Suddenly, with a deafening crash, the sea parted around our catch, and we were launched backwards. 

My back slammed against the sand, tearing my breath from my lungs. Disoriented, I could hear a sound next to me, a wailing of some kind. 

Dylan. 

I jolted up with electric urgency and swivelled around only to see my Dylan… laughing. I hadn’t seen him so much as *smile* in a really long time. 

“Nice catch,” he wheezed in between giggles, holding up the rod. 

Dangling from the hook was… a shell. 

A typical, run-of-the-mill conch shell, its off-white color shimmering as rays shone through breaks in the ominous clouds. 

They were parting. It only took one more look at the petite shell responsible for all the commotion before I, too, crumpled onto the ground laughing. 

We lay there on the beach for a while, listening to waves crash. 

The sun had emerged completely and proudly wrapped the entire shoreline, swaddling Dylan and me in a blanket of gold as we stared towards the heavens. 

“I’m sorry, bud,” I told him regretfully.

“For what?” he responded, genuine confusion plastering his tone

“That we didn’t catch a single thing…” I stopped myself. “That we didn’t catch a single *living* thing all day”.

Dylan scoffed, and I sat up inquisitively. 

“What?”

“You still think I give a shit about fishing, don’t you?” he answered sardonically. 

There it was. The usual Dylan mockery. *Sometimes I wonder why I even bother*, I thought to myself, as I hoisted myself up from the earth and started walking back towards the cottage.

“Hey Dad,” Dylan called from the sand, without making eye contact. He was still looking straight up at the sky.

“Yeah?” I replied.

“Today was a good day”. 

I smiled at him.

“Grab your loot and let’s head inside”, I said. I could hear him jump up from the beach, his footsteps pattering closer until he had caught up. 

“You know, they say you can hear the ocean in those things,” I noted, pointing at the glistening conch in his hand. 

“Bullshit,” he laughed, raising the shell to his ear. “I don’t hear any-”. He stopped midsentence.

“Told you,” I mused. “It’s cool, right?”

No response.

“Dylan?”

Confused, I turned to look at him, and my stomach dropped. Dylan had stiffened with the conch still fixed to his ear. 

He was still—not alert-still, but frozen-still, like he couldn’t move even if he wanted to.

“Dylan,” I spoke gravely, approaching him and waving my hand in front of his eyes. 

“What’s going on?” 

His eyes began to slowly bulge out of their sockets, gelatinous orbs pushing through shields of skin, revealing snaking veins and popped blood vessels. 

I yelled his name again, as if that would change anything, then began to shake him. It was like trying to shake David after Michelangelo’s careful chiseling—no matter how much he looked like a man, he’d still feel like a fortress. 

*A seizure*, I thought, *it must be*. 

*What were you supposed to do during a seizure again? Lay them on their side?* 

Dylan wasn’t even on the ground to begin with. 

*A stroke, then? Yes, it must be a stroke.* 

I peered into his bugged-out eyes, which had gone from glassy to milky.  

In an attempt to wake him, I tugged on his shirt, tried to lift him onto my back, and even launched into an earsplitting yell towards his left ear. None of this worked, of course, but it drew my attention to the shell that remained gripped over Dylan’s right ear. 

His fingers had gone white from the pressure and his dry knuckles had cracked, leaving thin red lacerations splintering across each nub. 

I took one last look at his protruding eyes before whipping out my phone and dialing 9-1-1. I paced the gravel driveway as it rang, gnawing my thumbnail to a jagged stump, not taking my eyes off my son. 

And that’s when he moved. 

A cold, calculated motion. 

A removal of the shell from his ear. 

“Dylan!” I dropped the phone and ran up to him, watching as his eyeballs receded into their oval alcoves. I bombarded him with questions: 

“What just happened? Are you okay? You looked like you were having a stroke!” 

He responded simply: 

“I’m fine.” 

And it wasn’t my first time hearing that word. 

“How was school?” “Fine.” 

“How’d that test go?” “Fine.” 

It only took a couple of playground bullies and failed exams to realize that “fine” was a veil, draped over an intricate accumulation of anguish and fervor, heightened complexities only a young heart could harbor. 

I didn’t give a fuck about what words came out of his mouth, no matter how reassuring—something was wrong. 

“9-1-1 is on its way,” I said, redirecting my gaze to the phone that had slipped from my grip, cracked but still dialing. “We’re gonna figure out what just happened to you.”

“No,” Dylan snapped sharply, eyes darting between me and the phone with a squirrely speed. “I’m fine, really. I leave for college in 2 days, and I don’t want to spend it with stethoscopes and syringes. I want to spend it with you. I want to spend it here.”

In that moment, it didn’t matter to me whether or not Dylan was hiding his pain. You have to understand, I waited 18 years to hear my son say that, and I’d be damned to ignore it.

Reaching for the phone, the sound of the operator’s confused voice rose in volume. 

“Hello? Sir? Are you in any danger?” I glanced at my son and couldn’t deny the fact that he looked completely healthy. Whatever momentary blip had occurred, it was over. I relented.

“Hey, sorry. My son gave me a bit of a scare, but he’s claiming to be okay now”. I watched Dylan exhale, his shoulders collapsing in relief. At least I could give him that. 

“Sir, you do know that calling this number without an actual emergency is a federal-” My finger met the red circle to silence her spiel, and I turned to my son. 

“You're welcome,” I stated, and then directed my attention to the object in Dylan’s hand. 

I wasn’t certain on the matter, but another reason I had caved so easily was my working theory that whatever had happened wasn’t a result of Dylan’s health. It was a result of the shell. 

Maybe a jellyfish, one with paralytic venom, had found itself trapped within the moist cavern of calcium. 

Maybe there was a very laughable explanation, like a simple sting. 

Maybe.

“But Dyl, if you’re going to refuse examination by officials, I’m gonna do some digging of my own. Hand me our catch of the day, would you?” Dylan grew abruptly pale and clasped the conch tightly between both hands. 

“*No.”* It wasn’t a yell, nor a whisper, but it carried the firmness and breathlessness of both: a guttural wheeze of… terror? Desire? 

I wanted to know- no, I needed to know what possible reason he could have for keeping the shell so close to him. 

I could push, but as I learned in a parental counseling course during a really rough period of Dylan’s life, pushing has the same effect mentally as it does physically: it creates distance. Dr. Howard told me that if my teenager is having a hard time, I need to let him come to *me* instead of barraging him with questions. Fuck Dr. Howard.

“Why not,” I asked firmly, extending my open palm to Dylan, hoping he would drop the shell (and the front). 

He said nothing, simply opened the door to the cottage, and shuffled inside, shell clutched towards his chest like a child’s tattered security blanket. I followed him inside.

“Dylan!” I called, trying to muster an angry and intimidating bellow. The squeal that left my mouth, however, wasn’t angry at all. It was sad. I heard my son slam a bedroom door, and let my shoulders slump in a tortured exhale. 

The rental guide had urged against taking anything from the cottage, but after a few minutes of cabinet sweeping and the discovery of a bottle of Jack Daniels, I thought: *to hell with it*. 

The living room’s single linen chair chafed my exposed legs as I sank into it, but glass in hand, I hardly cared. 

I thought about our day. How, for a second, it felt like Dylan was finally opening up to me. Did that have to end? When would he be finished walking on eggshells, retreating into his silence? 

I remember thinking when and not why, because I knew why. And my ex-wife knew too. I filled my glass again. 

When Dylan was younger, I had hurt him.

 It was a one-time thing: a single, *stupid* mistake. 

The night of my wife’s birthday, I had had a little too much to drink with dinner. Some nice Italian place. 

After, as I was getting ready to cut the cake I had ordered for her, Dylan reached for it, trying to nab a piece of frosting from its intricately designed surface. 

I yelled for him to stop, that he’d ruin it, but he didn’t, so I grabbed his arm and pulled him away. 

Not hard, or at least I didn’t think it was— but a soft crack, like that of a twig underfoot on a hiking trail, proved otherwise. 

I was mortified, but my apologies were drowned out by my wife’s shrieking as she ran over to cradle our little Dylan’s limp arm in her hands. 

She left me later that night, and for that, I don’t blame her. 

And though I harbor plenty of resentment because of it, I don’t blame her for the custody agreement either. Though I vowed from that point on to never lay a finger on my son again, my alcoholism was out of control, and because of that, a weekends-only schedule was for the best. 

But that’s why I needed this last trip to be perfect. We didn’t have a week before he left for college; we had 2 days. 

2 short days for the ocean to fix everything. 

And for one cloudless moment on the sand, I felt like it had... but now it’s all fucked. 

At this point, I ditched the cup and started drinking straight out of the bottle. 

*What happened?* I wondered. *What happened to our day?* 

One second, my son was a lighthouse, his smile brightening the foggy day for the first time in a long cycle. The next, he was completely closed off, more so than ever before. 

*Was it the shell?*

It had to be. 

The shell that somehow had resistance in the ocean, a pull stronger than an inanimate object, a pull stronger than even a *fish*. 

The shell that caused my son to freeze up with it fixed to his ear like a distraught family member receiving the dreaded yet inevitable phone call regarding their elderly relative. 

The shell that he wouldn’t let me take… I just wanted to look. 

*I need… to go… look*—but before I could finish that thought, the empty bottle of Jack Daniels fell from my hand and made a toast with the hardwood floor as I said cheers to the sweet surrender of sleep. 

I was awakened by the sound of a creaking door and a soul-sucking pit in my stomach. An excruciating sense of dread that only intensified when my gaze groggily swung to the front door, which was hanging wide open, letting the night’s darkness creep into our temporary home. 

“Dylan?” I called hoarsely, staggering up from my chair while clutching a nearby table for support. 

No response. 

I didn’t even need to check his room… that unbearably bad feeling told me I wouldn’t find him there. I turned my attention to the open door, and without thinking twice, stumbled out into the twilight zone. 

My head pounded, blood beatboxing to each ear in a defening crescendo. It was the tail end of a buzz, and the rearing head of a hangover, but I had to keep moving. The fog had fallen once again, but as I bumbled closer to the shore, I began to make out a silhouette.  

Squinting, I could see Dylan pacing towards the water, and though I couldn’t make out what he was holding through the mist, I didn’t need to. I launched into a clumsy sprint.

“Dylan!” As I expected, there was no response, and the closer I got, the more of the shell by his ear I saw. It was glowing. 

*A trick of the moonlight?*

“Dylan!” I caught up with him just as one of his bare feet touched the sea. “Dylan, you have to stop, you’re going to drown!” 

He continued walking, his movement rigid and wooden, a stroll akin to an uncanny puppet rather than a real boy. None of my feeble attempts to hold him back made any difference. 

He may have been skinny, but Dylan had reached an age where he could overpower me. Which was, at first, frightening on an existential level, but in this scenario, it scared me in a more immediate fashion. 

“Dylan. Stop. Fucking. Moving!” I grunted in between heaves as I gripped his arm and tried to pull him back, but it was just like the weird seizure earlier. He wouldn’t budge in any direction his legs weren’t already taking him.

I turned my attention to the conch and remembered my drunken train of thought earlier that evening. Whatever was happening, it was because of that shell. I needed to get it off him. 

By now, we were waist-deep in the chilling surf, and my knees began to buckle with numbness. I grabbed the conch and attempted to pull it out of his hand, but his frozen grip felt less like a handle and more like a lock. 

The water crept up as we plunged deeper, and once it reached the midsection of my son’s body, I began to panic. 

I tried one finger at a time, endeavouring to peel each up and free the shell, but it felt like the harder I pulled, the tighter they latched. 

By now, waves were splashing onto our necks, and my feet lost contact with the reef’s bottom for an instant. I didn’t know how long Dylan had, so I pulled harder and harder on his index finger, exerting all my force until: 

*\*Snap\*.* 

It was a sound I was familiar with. 

It was a sound I had heard on my wife’s birthday, many years ago. 

It was a sound I had vowed never to hear again. 

It was the sound of my son… breaking. 

I let out a choked cry of shock. Dylan’s finger extended up off the shell, still as stiff as ever, but this time facing the sky. 

For a split second, moonlight illuminated his exposed bone, a shattered stake jutting out of the pale flesh of his knuckles, but that view was soon obstructed by a surge of bright crimson. 

Blood squirted up, spraying Dylan’s unchanged face. His mouth remained shut, but I watched as a single tear slid down his cheek, leaving a streak of transparency on his now otherwise scarlet skin. 

He continued to walk. One rigid foot after the other, the marching of a soldier towards a trench that he knows he’ll never march away from. 

I grabbed Dylan’s middle finger, tears streaming down my own face, and since I knew he couldn’t, I screamed. 

“Please forgive me.” 

*\*Snap\*.* 

The blue sea lapped at my chin as a red sea spewed from Dylan’s new wound. The first had been a clean break, but this fracture was crooked; arteries were twisted the wrong way, spurting fluid sporadically like a garden hose with a knot in it. 

I thought about that day with the cake. I remembered how Dylan looked at me after, the same way he has looked at me every year since: like he was frightened of me. Maybe he was right to be. 

“I… can’t,” I choked out, staring at his bloodied hand, but the gravity of the situation returned to me courtesy of an aggressive splash right into my open mouth. I coughed at the salty taste, and gagged once I acquired a sinister note, a metallic aftertaste that could be nothing other than my own son’s blood. 

That’s when I saw his head go under. 

In that moment, I forgot all about the taste. I forgot all about the reason for my wife's and my separation. Hell, I even forgot about her altogether. As the water rushed over my head too, I realized what I was feeling was fight or flight—and I sure as shit wasn’t leaving my son. 

\**Snap\*.* 

Even in the muffled abyss, the crack was just as audible as the swirling streams of maroon bubbles were visible. For a second I almost joined the bubbles, drifting upward towards the surface, but I grabbed Dylan’s shirt to anchor myself. 

That’s when I noticed something I couldn’t explain then, and certainly can’t now. While I kicked and struggled to stay underwater, Dylan was walking *effortlessly*, feet to the sand as if his soles were 50-pound weights. 

Somehow, in the face of all common sense, rationality, and physics, my son was casually strolling down the ocean floor. 

My bewilderment quickly turned to fear, which I had no choice but to translate into anger and tug. *Snap*. Dylan’s ring finger tore completely off in my hand, adrenaline having unlocked a strength I wasn’t, up until this point, aware of. 

I shouted in disgust and terror, well aware only the fish could hear me now, and flung the finger as far away from myself as possible—not stopping to watch its red contrail unfold dizzyingly through the deep. 

Black splotches began to smudge my vision, and my head began to pound from oxygen loss. Kicking against the night tide, I gripped the lonely pinky. 

I had never cried underwater before. It's strange—since both liquids are so salty they’re virtually indistinguishable. Is there even a difference at all? If you were to ask me now, I’d tell you the entire ocean is tears. 

\**Snap\**. 

Dylan loosened, and I watched as the shell drifted out of his mangled hand. His eyes sank back into his skull, and I could tell whatever trance he was in was over by the gradual floating of his feet from the sand. 

I extended my arm out for Dylan to grip, and, holding each other close, we kicked our way up out of the clammy abyss. 

My vision was swirling as we breached the expansive surface, and even after a gulp of oxygen, the moonlight’s reflection appeared to me as shapes, dancing across the periodic waves. But whatever enduring damage the air deprivation had done was not nearly as important as my son.

“Breath for me, Dyl, breath.” I watched his chest rise and fall as we treaded water, hard and frantic enough to send ripples out across the sea. I patted him down as best I could, splashing around to confirm he was conscious. 

“Dylan. Are you okay?” I wanted him to tell me he was going to be alright, to explain to me what was happening to him, what it all meant. But instead what I got was

“I’m fine.”

“God*damnit,* Dylan,” I cried, shaking him, trying to knock sense into him, or even just jolt words out of him. 

“You have to fucking *talk* to me.” 

I thought about all of my failed attempts to get him to open up, all of the times I sat beside his bed silently, ears open, just *waiting* for him to speak. And the fact that he wouldn’t was all my fault! The fact that he couldn’t—I did that to him! 

“Dylan, I’m sorry. I’ve said it before, but I’ll say it again. I’ll say it to you every day for the rest of our lives. But I need you to understand that all I ever do, all I’ve ever done since that day so many years ago, has been to help you. To shield you from harm—by me, those bullies in the 4th grade, or anyone else who would ever accidentally or intentionally bring you pain. And I don’t know what you’re caught up in right now, but whatever it is, it *doesn’t* come with good intentions, and I *do*. So please, Dylan. I’m sorry, but I’m begging you. Talk to me.” 

Dylan said nothing. 

But then he paddled over, as fast as a boy could with a broken hand, and folded me into a hug. 

I wrapped my arms around him too and we floated there, still numb from the freezing water, but warm inside. 

“It’s the shell,” Dylan muffled through tears, and I nodded solemnly, pulling away.

“Tell me everything.” 

And he did, in fact, tell me everything—but no matter how vast the term may be, nothing in our natural world could have prepared me for what this scenario’s “everything” encompassed. 

“There’s a voice,” Dylan uttered, still hoarse from seawater and stolen gasps. “A beautiful voice, coming from inside the shell. And it sings to me. A song so sweet all I want to do is listen to it”. 

I looked at him like a war-stricken veteran, dumbfounded, but expressing only a blank forward stare. 

“She would sing about the things she wanted me to do. And I would want to do them.” Dylan paused, as if reminiscing, then continued, “It felt so nice to listen”. 

“Dylan,” I stated sternly, still confused, but feeling a biting urgency that wasn’t there before. My treading became more intense. 

“What did she tell you to do?” 

“She told me she wanted to meet me, but she couldn’t do it in the daylight”. 

I stared in horror at the shore, barely visible beyond the twilight fog. It looked so far away.

“She told me to come back at night”. 

Suddenly, before I could even process what was happening, my son was pulled under. 

I froze for a split second as the event replayed in my mind: Dylan’s head disappearing beneath the surface like a fishing bobber, signaling that a catch was made. 

And with the initial shock still stinging my neurons, I screamed.

“DYLAN!” 

Splashing frantically, I swiveled my head around, looking for any sign of resurfacing, but all that remained was a ripple where my son once was. 

I dunked into the freezing sea and peered past choppy waves and slinking shadows. Salt screeched at me to close my eyes, but I responded only by opening them wider… 

And that’s when I saw it. 

Far beneath me, the ocean began to look less like something of this earth and more like a kind of galactic void you’d see up among the stars. Except there were no stars, only darkness. 

And within that darkness, I could make out… a tail. 

A thick, scaled tail sweeping side to side in a motion so hypnotic, I didn’t even notice what was being dragged beside it. 

Not at first, at least. 

But soon enough my eyes adjusted to the silhouette of my son’s limp body, and everything went dark.  

The next thing I remember is coughing. My coughing. 

I was beached on my side, being hovered over by two men in red uniforms. 

They mumbled to each other about oxygen as I continued spewing boughts of water, followed by miscellaneous pieces of seaweed and sand. 

It was there, sitting in a tidepool of my own making, that my memory of the night returned to me. 

“Dylan,” I grabbed one of the red men’s ankles and gripped, resisting the urge to shake him violently. “Where’s my son?” 

“That’s exactly what we were about to ask you,” another man said, stepping down onto the beach. 

This one was wearing a uniform too, but his was black. 

Sharp sunlight glinted off the scintillating badge on his chest, leaving me feeling like an ant under the loving care of a little shit with a magnifying glass. 

I watched as my ex-wife stepped out from behind him, holding the empty bottle of Jack Daniels, and harboring an expression that burned harder than the sun’s scalding reflection.

“What did you do to our boy?” she choked through tears, taking her attention away from the bottle and gesturing to my hands. I held them up and squinted at the darkly crimson residue caked beneath my fingernails. 

“You don’t understand,” I said, attempting to stagger up, but failing to catch my balance.

“I didn’t… it was…” 

“You’ll have plenty of time to explain at the station,” the officer said, as rings of cold hard steel fastened my hands behind my back. 

I *could* take another 5000 words to explain to you how the trial went, but my free hour is almost up, so I might as well just tell you where I’m writing this from: a cell at the Barnstable County Correctional Facility. 

It has been 6 months since Dylan’s disappearance, and the authorities are going public with the story of a presumed drowning. 

Even with a cancelled 9-1-1 call earlier that day, a BAC of 0.30, and a previous offense to my son on record, I am being released tomorrow on account of no body being found, therefore clearing me of a manslaughter charge. 

My ex-wife wouldn’t talk to me for the first month, but eventually she came to believe my tall tale. I think maybe believing in monsters was easier for her than believing her son was dead. And I’m grateful for that. 

I still don’t know what really happened to Dylan, if he’s even alive or not. But with the state dropping the case, there’s only one way to find out. 

I had my ex sweet-talk the guy at the evidence locker, and upon release, there’ll be something waiting for me at home. 

Neither of us knows how it’s gonna play out, but I’m at the end of my rope here… so tomorrow I’m going to do a series of things. 

I’m going to walk out of this prison, and into another one: a house without my Dylan. 

I’m going to remove the conch shell from it’s ziploc evidence container, carefully placing it to my ear. 

And then I’m going to need all of *you* to do something for *me*… 

Pray.
