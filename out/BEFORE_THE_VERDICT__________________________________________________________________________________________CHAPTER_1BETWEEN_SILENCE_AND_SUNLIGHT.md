# Chapter One: Between Silence and Sunlight

The interrogation room in Saanjhpur Central Police Station was small, square, and airless. The paint on the walls had begun to peel in tired flakes. A single tube light flickered above, humming like a restless insect. The metal table at the center bore scratches—some from impatience, some from fear.

Ayaan Rahmani sat on one side of that table.

He was shaking.

Not the kind of trembling that comes from cold—but the kind that rises from somewhere deeper. His hands quivered against the steel surface. His eyes were lowered, but not empty. They were filled with something no one in that room could confidently name.

Guilt.  
Revenge.  
Mercy.  
Grief.  
Or something beyond language.

Outside the room, **ACP Raghav Suryavanshi** stood with his arms folded.

“Today’s generation,” he muttered, voice edged with irritation. “They don’t think. They act impulsively and destroy their lives forever.”

Beside him, calm and composed, stood **DSP Ira Sen**.

“Every generation is the same,” she replied evenly. “The difference lies in who is watching them.”

Raghav exhaled sharply and pushed open the interrogation room door.

He stepped inside.

“So, boy,” he said in a loud, harsh voice, the word *boy* landing like a verdict. “What have you done?”

Ayaan continued shaking.

His silence was heavier than confession.

Ira entered moments later, her eyes briefly meeting Raghav’s—an unspoken request to lower his tone.

She pulled a chair slightly closer to Ayaan.

“Don’t worry,” she said softly. “No one here is judging you. We’re here to understand. Tell us… what did you do to her? And why?”

Fifteen minutes passed.

Silence did not break.

Only the tube light hummed.

Eventually, both officers stepped outside.

Raghav rubbed his forehead. “No normal human—especially a teenager—could commit something so brutal.”

Ira’s gaze remained steady. “We cannot say anything until we know the full story. Didn’t you see? He’s terrified.”

“This boy isn’t scared,” Raghav replied coldly. “He is scary.”

A junior officer approached hurriedly. “Sir, we found something.”

“What?” Raghav snapped.

“The girl… she was his girlfriend. They’ve been in a relationship for the past two years.”

Ira froze. “What?”

She took the file from the junior officer’s hands.

Inside were photographs.

Four images.

# The Photographs

**First Picture:**  
Under the golden evening sky of Saanjhpur, in the courtyard of *Aarohan Public School*, Ayaan stood behind Meher Anand’s wheelchair, pushing her gently. Both were laughing at something unseen. His head was thrown back in careless joy. Her eyes—usually guarded—were open and bright.

**Second Picture:**  
In the school garden, beneath a wide **banyan tree** whose branches stretched like protective arms, they sat at one of the stone tables placed around its trunk. Ayaan held a lunchbox, offering her a bite. Meher leaned forward, smiling—an expression rare and soft, like sunlight breaking through monsoon clouds.

**Third Picture:**  
Ayaan had drawn something silly on his cheek with a sketch pen, pretending to be a villain. Meher was laughing uncontrollably, her wheelchair tilted slightly back as he exaggerated a dramatic pose.

**Fourth Picture:**  
No laughter. No theatrics.  
Just the two of them sitting close. Quiet.  
Ayaan’s hand resting gently on the wheel of her chair.  
Meher looking at him—not with excitement—but with trust.

The kind of trust that doesn’t come easily.

Raghav looked at the images and shook his head.

“Now do you believe this boy is scary,” he said, voice sharp, “or should I say… a monster?”

Ira took a long breath.

The photographs did not scream violence.  
They whispered intimacy.

And intimacy complicates everything.

# --------------------------------------

The classroom of *Aarohan Public School* was bright, noisy, and alive.

Sunlight streamed through tall windows. Posters of the human digestive system and nervous system hung unevenly on pale yellow walls. A faint smell of chalk dust and freshly mopped floors lingered in the air.

The biology teacher, Mr. Deshpande, stood near the blackboard, passionately explaining cell division.

At the third row, Meher Anand sat in her wheelchair, her notebook perfectly aligned on the desk attached to it.

Behind her sat Ayaan Rahmani.

Restless.

Alive.

He leaned forward slightly and tugged her hair playfully.

Meher tried to maintain composure, eyes still on the board—but a small smile betrayed her.

“Stand up!” Mr. Deshpande’s voice cut across the room.

Ayaan stood, lowering his head, but smiling.

“Did you write what I just taught?”

“No, sir,” Ayaan replied innocently.

“And why not?”

Ayaan glanced toward Meher and smiled knowingly. She always shared her notes.

“I didn’t have to, sir.”

The class burst into suppressed laughter.

“Get out of my class!” the teacher snapped. “Useless, insincere student! Because of students like you, sincere students suffer.”

Ayaan walked out dramatically, pretending to wipe invisible tears. Meher shook her head, hiding her laughter.

# Recess

The school bell rang.

Ayaan rushed back inside.

He gently took hold of Meher’s wheelchair.

“Madam,” he bowed slightly, “shall we proceed to the royal wash area?”

She rolled her eyes—but let him push her.

He washed her hands carefully before lunch. Not hurriedly. Not awkwardly. As if it were the most natural thing in the world.

They moved toward the garden.

Under the massive **banyan tree**, whose roots touched the earth like ancient fingers, several stone tables were arranged in a circle. Students gathered there daily, sharing food, secrets, and laughter.

Ayaan opened his lunchbox proudly.

“I cooked for you,” he announced.

This wasn’t new. He had done it many times before.

He fed her a bite.

Meher chewed.

The food was, as always, disastrously bad.

Too salty. Slightly burnt.

But she smiled.

“I want to eat all of this,” she said. “I don’t want to share it with you.”

As always.

Ayaan narrowed his eyes suspiciously. “Why didn’t you tell me the food is bad? I didn’t cook it well.”

She looked at him.

Soft. Certain.

“Food is bad but not you. I love you not food.”

And she smiled.

The sunlight filtered through the banyan leaves, casting shifting patterns over them.

Ayaan stared at her for a moment longer than usual.

Then, as always, he did something ridiculous—crossed his eyes, made a dramatic chef apology speech, and pretended to faint from heartbreak.

Meher laughed.

Not politely.

Not softly.

Fully.

Color returned to her face when she laughed like that.

# Back to the Present

The interrogation room in Saanjhpur felt even darker now.

The laughter from those photographs seemed impossible in this space.

The boy who once turned burnt food into comedy now sat motionless under flickering light.

In another part of the city, outside **Naina Kunj**, media vans had begun gathering.

And somewhere, perhaps at **Sakoon**, silence had replaced warmth.

Inside the police station corridor, Ira closed the file slowly.

“Stories don’t begin where we discover them,” she murmured.

Raghav looked at her. “And where does this one begin?”

She glanced at the closed interrogation door.

“Not here.”

**End of Part One**
