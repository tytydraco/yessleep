Part one

  
To preface, I'll state that I grew up in a small farm town. I lived there until I ran away at age seventeen, and even now that I'm an adult I live in another that is, technically, even more destitute. 

When I ran away, I thought that leaving it would make me forget, or at least let me come to terms with my past. It didn't. 

There are certain things you notice as a child that begin to slip your memory as you age. Maybe you get small flashes here and there, but with those you must hold tightly, because there is no way to create new ones. These memories can be as varied as watching an anthill, lying on your stomach in the grass, to the mornings of your father leaving for work. You can never get them back simply because the small instincts of your childhood are gone, and in turn, it's impossible to create new ones from the same perspective. 

I didn't think anything of those moments when I was a kid. None of us did.  
  
But I keep remembering this one summer, and I can't tell if it was actually strange or if my slowly developing frontal lobe has simply been altering the memories since they happened, or if I simply didn't understand what I was looking at back then. 

I guess I'm posting this because I want to know if anyone else has seen anything like it, and this is far from my only weird experience. I have many more if anyone is interested, i just figured this would be a good one to start with.   
  
It started with the deer. 

There were only a few kids in my neighborhood. Most of the people there were as old as the houses that lined the street, so when Kaitlyn and Calvin moved in, we were eager to meet them.

The day we finally got to was in mid-July, the kind of heat that softened the tar snakes holding the cracked road together. My sister and I picked at them while we waited, pulling up thin black strands and rolling them between our fingers until the smell stuck to our skin.

We had knocked on their door a few minutes earlier. We’d seen them moving in all week, but our mom made us wait before going over. Said it was polite to give people time to settle.

The waiting had felt like watching grass grow, and now that their mother had told us they’d be out soon, it somehow felt even slower.

We stayed near the road, keeping an eye on their front door. When we heard a car coming, we both moved without thinking, stepping off the pavement and into the ditch as it came down the hill faster than it should have.

It wasn’t something we talked about. We just knew to move.

We lived at the bottom of a long slope, and drivers didn’t always slow down in time. It was easier to get out of the way first and think about it after.

The car passed in a rush of sound and heat. As it disappeared around the bend, I looked up the road to make sure there wasn’t another one behind it.

As I looked, I noticed something:

It wasn’t obvious at first. Just something out of place—a glint, maybe, or a darker shape against the road. I told my sister to wait where she was and started up the hill.

Even paved, it was a rough climb. The asphalt was hot under my bare feet, and by the time I reached the spot my legs were burning.

It was a frog. Or maybe a toad.

The front half of it was still intact, its skin damp and shining in the sun. The rest was gone. What was left trailed behind it in a dark red streak that stretched down the road.

I remember feeling a kind of hesitation before I got too close. It wasn't fear, not exactly. Just the sense that I should stop where I was.I didn’t.

My sister called up to me from where she was squatting on the shoulder in the rare shade of a tall tree, asking what it was.

“A dead frog,” I yelled back.

I crouched down anyway, close enough to smell it. It was fresh. Most of the ones we found out there were dried out by the sun, flattened into something thin and brittle, like a mummy. This one still looked like it had been moving not that long ago.

I thought about moving it, maybe pushing it off the road with a stick or burying it, but I didn’t. It didn’t feel right to touch it, and burying half of something felt worse than leaving it where it was. So I stood back up and left it there.

When I got back down the hill, my sister was still watching the road. We didn’t talk about it again. At the time, it didn’t feel important.



Kaitlyn and Calvin, as we came to know them, interrupted our idle play nearly an hour later. By then we’d grown bored of the road and moved back into the soft grass of our yard.

I was up in the short lilac tree, stretched along one of its thicker branches the way I imagined a cat might be, watching without really being seen.

Kaitlyn came over first.

She was scrawny and freckled, her hair bright red in a way that didn’t look real in the sun. She seemed shy at first, but not enough to stop herself from walking straight into someone else’s yard.

“Hi,” she said, loud enough that she didn’t have to repeat herself. “I’m Kaitlyn.”

My sister stood up from the grass, brushing her hands on her shorts.

“I’m Avery,” she said, then nodded up toward me. “That’s Eric.”

Kaitlyn followed her gaze, squinting a little through the branches. For a second, nobody said anything.

So I dropped down.

I landed harder than I meant to, my feet sinking into the cool grass.

“Do you have a brother?” I asked, before anything else could get said. “We thought we saw a boy when you were moving in.”

“Yeah,” she said. “Calvin. He’s inside. My mom’s making him eat first.”

I nodded, already looking past her toward the house.

The girls kept talking—about where she moved from, what grade she was in, things I didn’t really care about. I drifted a few steps away, closer to the edge of the yard, watching the front door across the street.

It took longer than it should have. Long enough that I started wondering if he just wasn’t coming out at all.

All at once, he came out fast, like he’d been waiting just inside for a chance to leave. He stopped at the bottom step of the porch, looking around until he spotted us.

Even from that distance, I could tell he was about my age. He looked like Kaitlyn, just sharper somehow– his jaw, his nose, his elbows skinny and pointed like they would pop through the skin at any moment, his shoulders the same. 

He didn’t wave, he just saw us and ran. 

He cut across the yard and into the road without stopping, not even bothering to look both ways, shoes slapping against the pavement as he came straight toward us.

“You guys live here?” he called out, like there was any other reason we’d be standing there.

“Yeah,” I said.

He slowed down when he got closer, looking between us like he was trying to figure something out.

“I’m Calvin.”

“Eric.”

That was enough for him. He nodded once, like something had been decided.

“You wanna do something?”

I shrugged. “Like what?”

He glanced back toward the road, then up the hill for a second before looking at me again.

“I don’t know. There’s not much out here.”

“There’s stuff,” I said.

I don’t know why I said it like that, there actually weren't many things to do, I just didn't want to admit the shame of living in a boring neighborhood. 

He didn’t question it. Just smiled a little, like he understood anyway.

Behind us, Kaitlyn and my sister had stopped talking and were watching.

“What kind of stuff?” he asked.

I looked toward the road without really meaning to.

“Just stuff,” I said again.

For a second, none of us moved, then he nodded.

“Okay.”

By a week later, their introduction felt like it had happened a long time ago.

Our mom had a strict rule about not being inside during daylight hours, so we kept ourselves busy outside. Kaitlyn and Calvin joined us mostly because they had nothing better to do. That was enough. We were friends by then, all of us, and so had decided to walk to the lake as our adventure of the day.

None of us knew exactly how to get there, so we packed for it like it mattered. Our supplies ended up being a handful of rhubarb we’d stolen from the shaded side of my house, and my sister’s pink plastic water bottle, which we shared between the four of us. 

Calvin brought Pokémon cards. He and I sat in the grass before we left, flipping through them while he pointed out the ones he swore would be worth thousands someday. I didn’t understand how something that thin could be worth anything at all, but I liked looking at them anyway. He said I could pick out a water-type one to play as when we got to the lake, and the idea excited me.

It was midday by the time we finally started walking, following my sister's lead because, despite the days we've spent before at the lake, I never bothered to learn the path to it. Avery, on the other hand, older and more aware of her surroundings, could swear she remembered the way. 

The sun was weaker than it had been the past few days, dulled behind a thin layer of clouds. I’d brought flip-flops because my mom made me, but I carried them looped around my wrist instead of wearing them. The road was cool enough, and I felt faster without them.

Calvin ran ahead with me while our sisters trailed behind.

It took us longer than it should have. By the time we found the road that led to the lake, my legs ached and my shirt stuck to my back. I kept thinking about the water—how cold it would feel, how it would suck the heat right out of my skin. 

I wasn’t paying attention when Calvin stopped.

I ran straight into him.

“Why’d you stop?” I said, pushing at his shoulder.

He didn’t answer.

I pushed him again, harder this time, but he barely moved. When he turned, it was only with his body. His head stayed fixed forward, like he didn’t want to look away.

“Are they dangerous?” Kaitlyn asked from behind us.

Her voice was quieter than usual.

I turned, half-annoyed, but the look on her face stopped me. She and my sister were standing a few steps back, both staring past us, so I looked too.

A group of deer stood in the road ahead, blocking the turn into the lake’s parking lot. There were more than I’d ever seen in one place before, and, for a second, I just felt irritated. Like they were in the way of something that mattered more.

“They’ll move,” I said. “They always do.”

My sister reached out and caught my wrist before I could keep walking.

“I don’t…” she started, then stopped.

The silence stretched and I felt an impatient growl build in my throat. 

“I don’t think so,” she said finally. “We’ve never seen this many.”

The deer didn’t move.

They weren’t grazing, or shifting, or even looking around. They just stood there. Watching, almost mirroring us.

I didn’t notice anything strange about that at the time. Just that they were being stubborn and blocking my path to our destination.

I pulled my wrist free and nudged Calvin forward again.

“Come on.”

He hesitated, then followed.

As we got closer, some of the deer turned their heads toward us. The movement was smooth, almost identical, like they had all decided to do it at the same time. It made me think of the mind control Calvin had told me some of the pokemon from his cards could do. The rest kept staring past us, back at the others.

We got close enough that I could see the details in their faces. Their eyes looked darker than I expected, almost glazed. 

“Why aren’t they running?” Calvin asked.

He sounded unsure, but I just shrugged.

“They usually do,” I said. “As soon as they notice you.”

I shifted the flip-flops off my wrist and stepped into them, balancing myself against his shoulder as I did.

“We normally only see one or two at a time though,” I continue, steadying myself again on my own two feet. 

When I turned back around, I waved at our sisters.

“Hurry up!” I called.

My voice sounded louder than it should have, like all the birds stopped to listen too. None of the deer reacted.

I pushed my way through the deer, the space between them tighter than it should have been.

I had never been that close to one before. I used to try, sometimes—moving slowly, holding out my hand, hoping one would let me get near enough to touch.

This wasn’t like that, it felt different for some reason. 

Up close, their fur looked wrong. Greasy in places, with clumps of shedding winter down still hanging along their sides. For a second, I had the strange feeling that if I touched one, it would stick to me—like a cactus, or the burrs that would dig into my feet. 

Or worse, that they would all startle at once.

There were enough of them that I couldn’t see past the bodies. I remember realizing, suddenly, how small I was standing in the middle of them, so I didn’t reach out.

We made it through without anything happening, and the lake was just past the bend, exactly where it should have been.

I stepped into the cold water first, watching the squelching dirt rise from my footsteps and settle onto the foam of my flip flops. Calvin came in after me. 

We tried to play for a while– we splashed around, ducked under, tried to catch minnows in our hands. It didn’t feel the same. I couldn’t have said why. I was upset a little, blaming my sister for it, glaring at her from time to time as she and Kaitlyn sat silently on the cracked pieces of an old concrete platform, a few feet away from us. They didn't bother to follow us in and they weren’t talking either.

I remember noticing that more than anything—that they weren’t even whispering to each other. Just sitting there, looking out at nothing in particular. I wondered what made them so upset, if it was because of the deer, or if it was just how older kids acted sometimes, like they were suddenly too cool to play. 

Calvin didn’t seem to care. Or if he did, he didn’t say anything about it. We kept trying to catch minnows. 

I managed to trap one in my hands after a few tries. I called out to him, and he held it for me while I went up to my sister and asked for her water bottle. She handed it over without looking at me. That should have felt strange. I think I expected it to snap her out of whatever she was doing, but it didn’t; so in my final rebuke against her sudden angst, I dumped the water out in front of her. It didn't get the reaction I had hoped for either. I went back down to the lake and filled it again. Calvin dropped the minnow inside.

We stood there for a while, watching it swim in frantic circles.

Eventually, our sisters told us we should go.

None of us had thought to bring towels, so we were still wet when we left and the air was cool enough now to make it uncomfortable.

I wasn’t thinking about the deer anymore, but when we reached the curve in the road, I heard my sister let out a long breath behind me.

Relief.

I turned to look and the road ahead was empty.

There were no deer. Not even tracks in the dirt where they’d been standing. The underbrush on either side looked untouched, like nothing had passed through it at all.

I remember feeling a sudden urge to find one again, to get closer this time– maybe even to touch one.  I looked around but saw no stragglers, and I was cold, wet and wanted to go home, so I put no effort into searching for one. I didn’t say anything about it. We walked the rest of the way home in silence, me and Calvin shivering side by side.
