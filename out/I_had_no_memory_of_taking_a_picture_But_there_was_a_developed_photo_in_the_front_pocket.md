&#x200B;

The snowstorm hit somewhere between Billings and the Wyoming border, and by nine o'clock that night, I couldn't see past my own headlights.

&#x200B;

My name is Dev. I've been running long-haul routes across the northern Rockies for eight years — long enough to know black ice from shadow, long enough to read a sky that's about to drop six inches before the weather app figures it out. I've slept in my cab on the shoulder of roads that most people don't know exist. I've been comfortable in the dark for a long time.

&#x200B;

That night changed that.

&#x200B;

I pulled my rig off Route 212 onto what used to be a Forest Service access road — the kind of road that shows up on old maps but not on GPS. The snow was coming down sideways. Visibility was near zero. Company policy allowed a two-hour rest stop if conditions made driving unsafe, and conditions had been unsafe for the last forty minutes. I killed the headlights, drew the thermal curtains across every window so the cabin was completely sealed — dark inside, dark outside — and stretched out on the sleeper bunk with my phone.

&#x200B;

I planned to lie there for an hour. Maybe ninety minutes.

&#x200B;

I was awake for about ten before I heard it.

&#x200B;

Footsteps.

&#x200B;

Not the sound of wind moving through pine trees, not the truck settling on cold ground. Footsteps. Deliberate. Heavy enough to compress the snow with each step, the soft crunch of it carrying through the total silence outside. Someone was walking toward my truck from somewhere deep in the tree line.

&#x200B;

My first instinct was to dismiss it. A deer, maybe. A branch falling. But I lay still and kept listening, and the sound kept coming — evenly paced, unhurried — and then it stopped.

&#x200B;

Right beside my cab.

&#x200B;

I didn't move. Didn't breathe. Didn't reach for my phone. I lay in the dark with every nerve in my body tuned to that same three feet of space just outside the door, and I waited.

&#x200B;

Nothing happened.

&#x200B;

No knock. No voice. No second footstep.

&#x200B;

Whoever was out there had simply... stopped. And was standing there. In a snowstorm. In a forest. At nine-thirty at night, miles from any town, beside the cab of a truck with its lights off.

&#x200B;

I kept waiting.

&#x200B;

The wind came and went. Snow ticked against the glass. The engine creaked as the metal cooled. But underneath all of it, underneath everything, I could feel the presence of something standing right outside that door, and it was not moving, and it was not leaving, and it was completely calm about all of this in a way that made my skin feel wrong.

&#x200B;

Fifteen minutes passed before the door handle moved.

&#x200B;

Not rattled. Not shaken. Moved — the careful, deliberate way you'd try a handle if you were testing whether it was locked. Slow pressure. Then release. Then a pause of about thirty seconds. Then slow pressure again.

&#x200B;

I had both doors locked. I knew that. I had checked before I killed the lights. But knowing that did almost nothing for me, because whatever was methodically testing my locks didn't feel like it was improvising. It felt like it was patient.

&#x200B;

It stopped after the third try, and the silence came back.

&#x200B;

I waited for footsteps. Retreating footsteps, going back the way they came. I needed to hear them go. But there was nothing. Just the snow and the wind and the sense that something was still there, standing in the dark, two inches of insulated door between us.

&#x200B;

Then the radio turned on.

&#x200B;

No station — just static, the low hiss of AM white noise filling the cab. My radio hadn't been on. My radio couldn't have turned on. There was no one near the controls. I was lying flat on the bunk six feet away.

&#x200B;

I stared at the static on the display for a long moment before I realized the scratching had started.

&#x200B;

Fingernails — or something that moved the way fingernails would — slowly dragging along the metal side of the cab. Starting at the rear corner. Moving forward. Methodical. Almost gentle. The sound of someone tracing the outline of the door from outside.

&#x200B;

And then a child laughed.

&#x200B;

Not a scream. Not a shriek. A child's laugh — high and clear and completely out of place, the kind of sound that belongs in a backyard on a summer afternoon. It lasted about three seconds. Then it stopped.

&#x200B;

I stopped breathing.

&#x200B;

And then a voice — soft, close, right outside the glass — said: "We can see the curtain moving."

&#x200B;

I don't know how long I lay there after that. Long enough that my legs went numb. Long enough that the radio static cut out on its own. At some point I realized the scratching had stopped, and the presence — whatever it was — seemed to have shifted. I could hear, faintly, the footsteps moving away from the cab, moving around to the front of the truck.

&#x200B;

Moving away.

&#x200B;

I told myself to think. I'd been parked here long enough for the snow to build up around my rear axle. If I didn't move in the next twenty minutes the truck would be stuck until morning, and I had a delivery window to hit. I had a walkie-talkie. I had a plan. I just needed to move while they were in front of the truck, facing away.

&#x200B;

I slid into the driver's seat without turning on any lights, pulled the curtain back in one motion, and started the engine.

&#x200B;

The headlights came on automatically.

&#x200B;

There were three of them, maybe thirty feet ahead in the road.

&#x200B;

A man. A woman. A child.

&#x200B;

They were standing in the snow with their backs to me, and something about the way they stood — too still, too close together, no hats, no coats in the kind of cold that was registering at eleven below — made me press the accelerator before I could get a good look at them.

&#x200B;

The tires spun, caught, spun again, and the truck groaned sideways on black ice and dropped into a shallow ditch at the road's edge.

&#x200B;

I hit the brakes. Looked up.

&#x200B;

They had turned around.

&#x200B;

The headlights caught them fully now. The man and woman were dressed in clothes that didn't belong to this century — heavy wool, old-fashioned cuts, the kind of thing you'd see in a photograph. Their faces were pale in a way that wasn't pallor. Their eyes were open but they were not blinking. There was no expression on either face — not anger, not fear, not anything. Just attention. They were looking at my truck the way you'd look at a door you'd been waiting a long time to open.

&#x200B;

The child was looking directly at me.

&#x200B;

And he raised one hand and waved.

&#x200B;

Not a threatening gesture. Not a beckoning gesture. Just — goodbye. A small, slow, patient wave, the way a child waves to someone they know.

&#x200B;

I worked the accelerator for the next four minutes in a cold sweat until the rear axle finally bit and the truck lurched free. I didn't look at them again. I stared at the road ahead and I drove until the tree line broke and a gas station materialized out of the snow like a hallucination, fluorescent light bleeding into the dark.

&#x200B;

The mechanic who straightened my rear bumper — a large, quiet man named Glenn who ran the shop with his adult son — didn't ask me what had happened when I walked in. He asked me where I had come from.

&#x200B;

I told him. The Forest Service access road off Route 212. The old one.

&#x200B;

He and his son exchanged a look. Not a you're crazy look. A different look. The kind two people share when they've had this conversation before.

&#x200B;

Glenn poured me a coffee without asking whether I wanted one and set it on the counter in front of me.

&#x200B;

"There's a house back there," he said. "About a mile into the trees. Nobody's lived in it for a long time. People come through here sometimes, talk about seeing things on that stretch of road." He stopped and wiped down the counter. "We don't go up there."

&#x200B;

He didn't tell me anything more than that. Didn't explain. Didn't fill in the details. I got the sense he didn't want to, and I didn't push him.

&#x200B;

When they got my truck onto the road, I thanked them, paid in cash, and turned the heat up as high as it would go. I drove the rest of the night without stopping.

&#x200B;

Three weeks later I was going through a box of old gear and found the point-and-shoot camera I'd had in my bag that night — one of those old instant-print cameras from the early 2000s. I had no memory of taking a picture. But there was a developed photo in the front pocket.

&#x200B;

It showed the inside of my cab. The sleeper bunk, the dash, the curtains drawn across the windshield. I recognized it immediately.

&#x200B;

In the driver's seat — where I had been lying flat on the bunk when I took it, where I had definitely been alone — there was a child.

&#x200B;

He was facing the camera.

&#x200B;

And he was waving.

&#x200B;

I still drive the Rockies. I still park on the shoulder when storms get bad.

&#x200B;

But I don't use that road anymore.

&#x200B;

And I always, always leave the radio off.
