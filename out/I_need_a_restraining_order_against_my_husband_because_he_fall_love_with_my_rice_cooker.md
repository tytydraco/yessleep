Okay, I know the title sounds terrible. But I'm sitting in a motel filled with the smells of cigarettes and shattered dreams, my phone battery at 23%, and I can't think of a better title right now. And I'm almost certain my husband will kill me over a kitchen appliance. It's true. This is my life right now.

Let me start from the beginning.

Three days ago, my rice cooker broke. That's it. The nightmare began. Not because of cheating, not because of secret gambling debts, not because he told me he'd been a spy all along. Just because of a damn rice cooker.

It was old, probably ten years old. I bought it when I was in college, living in a run-down apartment, the bathroom tiles must have been covered in talking mold. It was a Zojirushi, one of those high-end Japanese rice cookers, ridiculously expensive, but the rice it cooked was so delicious you'd want to write a poem in its praise. For ten years, it had silently and dutifully cooked perfect rice three times a week, never complaining, never asking for anything in return, except for the occasional descaling.

Then last Sunday , it… broke down. It broke down halfway through cooking. I came home from work to find an error code on its screen, which, according to Google, basically meant "I'm dead." I poked it, unplugged it, plugged it back in, and even threatened it like it was a dead person, saying I'd beat it if it didn't start. Still no luck.

I was, of course, frustrated. It was a really good rice cooker. But you know what? Things break. That's life. I learned from Queer Eye to accept impermanence and be prepared to move on.

So, I did what any sensible person would do: I went to Amazon, found the exact same model, thank goodness they were still in production, paid for overnight shipping, and planned to throw the old one in the recycling bin.

Then, I made a big mistake.

I should have asked my husband first. Perhaps, like my childhood goldfish, I should give it a funeral. I should have noticed sooner that the way he sometimes hit the rice cooker after it beeped was definitely something wrong, even a little eerie.

The new rice cooker arrived next Monday morning. I opened the box, took out all the desiccant packets labeled "Do Not Eat," and placed it on the kitchen counter, exactly where the old one was. Even the rice scoop was different. It looked exactly the same. Because they were originally identical. Same model, same color, everything is same.

I put the old rice cooker in a garbage bag and left it by the garage door, intending to dispose of it later.

My husband, Carl, came home from work around six. Everything was normal, the commute was normal, and he greeted me warmly as always: "Honey, I'm home!" He walked into the kitchen, looked at the counter, and had an expression on his face I'd never seen before. It was like seeing someone realize their dog had died, but the emotion was much stronger.

"Where is she?" he asked.

"Who?" I asked, my mind already racing, wondering if I'd forgotten what I'd promised his mother.

"Grace," he said, his voice choked, truly choked, as if he were about to cry.

"Who's Grace?"

He pointed to the rice cooker. The new rice cooker, exactly the same as the old one.

"Our rice cooker," he whispered.

I laughed. I really laughed, because I thought he was joking. "Oh, honey, the old one broke. But I bought the same one! Look, exactly the same."

"Where's Grace?"

He wasn't joking.

This man wasn't joking.

"I… the old one's at the garage door," I stammered. "It's broken."

He ran off. He rushed toward the garage door like a madman, and I heard him sob, that heartbreaking sob. I followed him and saw him carrying a garbage bag containing the old rice cooker, shaking it back and forth like he'd just rescued a child from a burning house.

"We can fix it," he kept saying. "We can fix it, it's not broken, we can fix it."

"Carl, it's just a rice cooker."

The way he looked at me. My God, the way he looked at me. It was as if I had just told him love didn't exist.

"This is Grace," he said slowly, as if explaining to a silly child. "The rice cooker we bought in 2015. The one that was there the first night we moved into your apartment. The one we used to cook rice for our first dinner party, our Grace."

"Okay, but… I bought the same one, the new one is exactly the same as the old one."

"That isn't Grace!"

He took the old rice cooker back. He locked the office door. I stood in the hallway for twenty minutes, trying to figure out what had just happened. Finally, I went to bed alone. He slept in his office, the rice cooker right next to him.

I told myself, this is just… I don't know, nostalgia? A strange emotional attachment? My mom still has her first microwave, bought in 1987. and she cries every time we suggest she get a new one. People always have an attachment to things. It's nothing. It really is nothing.

The next day, Thursday, Carl called in sick. He stayed in his office all day, the door locked. I could hear him talking softly inside. Once, I pressed my ear to the door and heard him say, "I know, I know, but we'll get through this together."

To a broken rice cooker.

To Grace.

I went to work because I'm not a coward, and because I need dental insurance to make ends meet, just in case I need a psychiatric evaluation. All day I thought about how to handle this. Maybe he needs therapy? Maybe we both need therapy? Maybe I should let him process this inexplicable sadness, and then he'll get over it on his own?

When I got home, his office door was open. He was gone, leaving a note saying he'd gone to see a "small appliance repair expert who knows old-fashioned appliances." The rice cooker was gone too.

My curiosity finally overcame my reason.

I'd never really looked at Carl's office closely before. It was his private space, and I respected that; we had healthy boundaries, and so on.

I started with his desk. Just ordinary things. Bills, work documents, and the novel he'd been "writing" for five years. Then I opened the bottom drawer.

Photographs.

So many photos.

Photos of Carl… with the rice cooker.

Not just our rice cooker. Several rice cookers. Different models, different colors, different eras. He posed in various ways, like he was taking a picture with someone. For example, he was hugging the rice cooker, smiling at the camera. There was a photo of him standing on the beach at sunset, holding a rice cooker, looking at it like someone looking at their fiancée in an engagement photo.

An entire photo album. My hands trembled as I flipped through it.

Carl and Grace at the Grand Canyon

Grace's first snow

Grace meets her mother

Really? A Christmas 2019 photo, our rice cooker wrapped in a little scarf, placed on his mother's sofa, his mother smiling at it.

My legs suddenly gave way, so I sat down in his office chair.

Then I noticed the filing cabinet.

I opened it.

The first drawer was crammed with receipts. All receipts for rice cookers. Dozens, the earliest dating back to 2008. We hadn't even met then. Different brands, different stores. Some receipts had names written in his handwriting: "Amber," "Emily," "Hope," "Charlie ."

He named them all. He named all the rice cookers.

In the second drawer was a diary.

I opened it.

I shouldn't have opened it.

The first entry was dated March 2008.

Today at Target, I found the prettiest appliance. Stainless steel, six-cup capacity, with a steamer. I decided to name her Amber because her 'cooking' indicator light is a warm yellow. I brought her home. She's perfect. I can't wait to see what we can make together.

Well. A little strange. But maybe he was just being impulsive? People do that, right? Give their things cute names?

I flipped to the back.

April 2008 Amber cooked jasmine rice perfectly. I told her it looked beautiful when she steamed it. I think she knew it."

June 2008:Amber broke today. My heart is broken. I tried to fix her, but I think the heating element is faulty. I don't know how to say goodbye to her. We had three wonderful months together."

July 2008:"Found a new model at Bed Bath & Beyond. She has a special quality that captivated me. I call her Emily. She's a Panasonic. More modern than Amber, but she has her own unique beauty."

And so it went for several years.

Several rice cookers. Several "relationships." Each one carried this… this longing. This romantic language gave me goosebumps.

Then I saw the diary entry from October 2015.

I visited the girl I met at a social event, the one who works at the marketing company. She's a nice person and seems very intelligent. But then I saw her. In the kitchen. A Zojirushi NS-TSC10 rice cooker. The most beautiful thing I'd ever seen. Pearl white casing. Digital display. Perfect proportions. She just sat there quietly on the counter, and I swear she sparkled. I asked her about it. The girl said she'd just bought it. Had barely used it. I knew then I had to see her again. This rice cooker. I needed to get to know her.

That girl was me. He started dating me to get close to my rice cooker.

November 2015:Third date with her. She suggested we cook dinner at her place. Finally, a chance to use Grace—I've always called her Grace, and the name suits her so well, the way she steams things, the way the indicator light comes on, the little tune that plays when the rice is cooked. I think I'm in love. Clara's great too, I think. It's so easy talking to her."

December 2015:Cooked rice with Grace again. Told Clara I really loved her cooking. Actually, I just wanted an excuse to use Grace. Clara suggested going to her house for dinner, and I was so excited. Was that wrong? Grace was there."

March 2016:"Clara and I were getting more and more serious. She mentioned maybe we'd live together someday. My heart was pounding, not because of Clara, but because it meant living with Grace. Was I wrong? Should I tell Clara? No. She wouldn't understand. Nobody would understand."

I had to put down the diary. My hands were shaking so badly I could barely hold it. I married this man. I married him, and all he'd ever loved was my rice cooker.

Wait, there's more to come.

At the very bottom of the filing cabinet, there was a book wrapped in velvet, like that damned Necronomicon.

It looked like a Bible. Leather binding, gold-embossed pages, everything.

I opened it.

The title page read:The Book of Grain: The Testimony of Rice and Revelation

The rest… I simply don't know how to describe it. It was Bible verse. Genuine Bible verse. But someone had replaced some keywords with words related to rice cookers.

"In the beginning was the Grain, and the Grain was with the Cooker, and the Grain was the Cooker." (John 1:1. obviously)

"For the Cooker so loved the world that it gave its steamed rice, that whoever partakes of it shall not hunger but have everlasting satisfaction." (John 3:16)

"The Lord is my rice cooker; I shall not want for rice. (Psalm 23)

There were hundreds of such notes. Some people spent hours writing them. The handwriting was neat. There were also annotations in the margins, such as, "This verse really touched Grace's heart" and "Remember this when she fails to steam her broccoli."

One entire chapter was called "The Book of Cooking," filled with... instructions on how to cook rice correctly, written like the Ten Commandments.

"Thou shalt not open the lid during cooking; it is an abomination."

"Honor thy rice scoop and measuring cup, that the grains may be distributed evenly."

"Thou shalt not covet thy neighbor's rice cooker, unless it be truly excellent."

I found a bookmark in the middle of the book. It was tucked into a passage of scripture that had been repeatedly highlighted and underlined:

"Husbands, love your rice cookers, just as the cooker loves the rice and gives itself for it, cleansing it with water and descaling solution to make it holy." (Ephesians 5:25-26)

Next to it was another line: "My vow of grace. Forever."

I closed the book, went upstairs, poured myself a glass of wine, then another. Then I picked up the bottle.

My husband and the rice cooker have been in love. For ten years. Our marriage is a complete lie. I'm nothing more than the mistress of the kitchen appliance.

Carl came home around eight o'clock that evening. He was humming a tune. Really humming. He walked past me into his office, and I heard him say, "Don't worry, Grace. They said they can fix you. They just need to order one part. You'll be as good as new."

I followed him into his office.

He was placing the rice cooker on the table, gently resting it on a small towel.

"Carl."

"Hmm?" He didn't look at me. He was busy adjusting the rice cooker's position.

"We need to talk."

"Can we wait a moment? Grace has been through a lot today."

"No, we can't wait."

He finally looked at me. "What's wrong?"

"What's wrong? What's wrong? Carl, you've fallen in love with a rice cooker!"

"Huh?"

"I found the diary, the photos, and that damned rice cooker bible!"

His face went from pale to flushed, then turned a strange gray I'd never seen before.

"You went through my things."

"You dated me just to sleep with my rice cooker?"

"No, we didn't, we were Platonic." He stood up, trembling. "You don't understand. You'll never understand."

"Then explain it to me! Explain how this relationship turned into you using me to get close to my kitchen appliance."

"Grace isn't just an appliance," he shouted. "She... she's perfect. She's always been there for me. She never judges me, never criticizes me, never demands I change. She just... cooks rice, and always cooks it perfectly. Do you know how rare that is?"

"It's a machine. It was programmed to do this."

"And you threw her away..."

"It broke."

"She can be fix !"

We stared at each other. I was crying. I didn't even know when I started crying, but my face was wet, my nose was running, and I might look like a madman, but you know what? My husband was clinging to a broken rice cooker and maintaining a relationship with it, so I guess it was understandable that I looked a little deranged.

"Go," I whispered. "Get out. Tonight. Pack your bags and go."

"I won't leave Grace."

"I don't care about Grace, take Grace, take them all, take your weird rice cooker harem, get out…"

He looked at me with hatred. Pure, unreserved hatred. As if I were the madman. As if I were the problem.

"Okay," he said. "But this isn't over. Grace and I… we're not done yet."

He left the office. I heard him go to our bedroom.

I stood there, trying to process what he had just said.

Then I heard the closet door open. That closet contained a toolbox, camping gear, and…

Oh, no.

Oh, no, no, no, no.

I ran into the bedroom.

Carl stood by the bed, holding our camping axe. That big axe, the one we bought when we went to the mountains to chop wood for our anniversary. Now that I think about it, he probably took some pictures of Grace and me there.

"Carl?"

He looked at me. His eyes were empty.

"She hasn't finished cooking yet," he said softly. "Grace still has to cook. And you want to take that away from her."

"Put down the axe."

"You can't abandon your loved ones just because they're broken," he said, taking a step closer to me. "You have to heal them. You have to respect them. Grace taught me those things."

I stepped back to the doorway.

"Do you respect Grace?" He took another step forward. The axe wobbled slightly in his hand. "Did you thank her for ten years of dedication?!"

I ran.

I fucking ran.

I rushed downstairs, grabbed my wallet and phone from the kitchen counter (the new rice cooker lay there quietly, looking completely innocent, utterly unaware that it had triggered a marital disaster), and sped to my car in the garage.

I heard Carl yelling something about betrayal, a steamer, and that I'd never understand true love behind me.

I drove off the driveway as fast as I could, leaving tire tracks.

Now I'm here. Motel 6. room 237.

So… any advice?
