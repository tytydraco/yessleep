The oncologist had a picture of a dog on her desk. I kept looking at it while she explained things. The dog was caught mid-run on a beach somewhere, tongue out, ears back, apparently thrilled to exist. The Doctor had the careful voice of someone who had given this particular speech before, and I tried to pay attention to what she was saying. Stage 4. Highly aggressive. Need to be realistic about options.

I wasn't scared of dying.

I was scared of what comes after.

I was sixteen years old when my grandmother died. Her name was Ruth and she smelled like flowers and medicine and made the best meatballs I've ever had. She was seventy eight and her heart had been giving out for two years. It wasn't a surprise when it finally happened. My mother cried. I held her hand at the service. I thought I was doing okay.

Then they lowered the casket.

I heard her voice. It wasn't like you hear in a dream, or the way grief plays tricks on your mind with a familiar sound. It was clear, coming up from the ground. She was saying something I couldn't make out at first, and I thought I was having some kind of mental break, some grief response. I looked around at my family. My uncle had his arm around my mother. My cousin was picking at the hem of her dress. Nobody else seemed to hear anything.

My grandmother's voice got clearer. She was saying she was cold. She was asking where everyone had gone. She was asking why it was so dark.

Then the other voices started. People crying, yelling for their loved ones. I ran from the funeral, and spent the next ten minutes hyperventilating and throwing up what little I had for breakfast and trying to make sense of it. 

Afterward my mother asked if I was okay and I said yes, I had just been overwhelmed, and went home and sat in my room for a long time trying to decide if I was losing my mind. I decided I probably was.

I went back three days later. To visit her grave. To see if it happened again.

I walked through the older section on the way in, the part with tilted headstones and worn down names. I walked past a tombstone gone gray with age and heard a man's voice.

"Esther."

That was all. Over and over. It was neither a moan or a whisper, but said the way you'd say something if saying it was the only thing left to do. I stopped and looked at the stone. Johan, it read. Born 1851, died 1890. Thirty-nine years old.

I stood there a while thinking about Esther. Who had she been. A lover? A sister? A daughter? I would never know.

I didn't visit my grandmother's grave that day. But I went back, and I kept going back, because when it's summer and you're sixteen and you don't have anywhere else to be and you have to know if it's real or in your head, each time telling myself it would be the last. It never was. Some part of me kept hoping I'd walk in and hear nothing, that it had been a fluke, something all in my head.

What I figured out, piece by piece, was mostly practical. The voice was loudest when I was standing directly over a grave and thinned out toward the edges of the plot. If I kept moving and kept my eyes forward I could cross most cemeteries without stopping, carrying ten or twenty voices at once like static between stations, none of them quite resolving into words I had to think about. It helped to have somewhere to be. Somewhere to look.

The old graves, the colonial-era ones with the skull carvings and the sandstone faces worn to nothing by two hundred years of weather, those were almost silent. A murmur at most. Whatever was left in them had run down to almost nothing over the centuries. I tried not to think about what that meant.

Everyone else was not quiet.

There was a grave I passed once, the stone reading Joan, Beloved Wife and Mother, 1921 to 1958. She was saying it was dark. That she had been good, all her life she had been good, and they had told her heaven would be bright. She said it with this bewildered patience, like she was still adjusting to a mistake that would sort itself out. 

I've thought about her family a lot over the years. What they told her when she was dying, what she'd believed, how certain she must have been. Her children are probably dead now too. 

A few years later I was cutting through a different cemetery near my apartment, a route I tried not to use if I wasn't in a hurry, and I got careless near a section I didn't usually walk through. The stone was small, made of black granite. His name was Jeremiah. It had his rank and a flag emblem. He'd died in 1987. He wasn't calling out for anyone, he didn't sound confused or scared. He was just repeating himself, quietly. The only part I could make out clearly was "I can't sew these men up in the dark." His voice was gravelly. He'd been sixty three.

I have a rule about the children's sections. I don't go near them. The infants only cry. No words, no names, nothing to catch and hold onto. Just crying from underground that no one is ever going to answer. I learned the rest the hard way, early, and I don't want to describe all of it. 

I'll describe one, the first one.

Ethan. It was a small stone with a football and toy car carved into it, and the dates put him at seven years old. He was saying he'd be good. He was saying he wanted to go back to the hospital. He was asking his father to please, please take him back to the hospital.

Thirty years, and he was still asking for his dad. He'd been in the ground for thirty years waiting for someone to come. 

I left at a run. I sat in my car in the parking lot with my hands on the wheel. I wasn't thinking about God or fairness. I just sat there until my breathing evened out, and then I drove home, and I didn't go near that part of the cemetery again.

I couldn't stay away but I hated the voices of the dead. They sounded hollow and distant.

I found the columbarium by accident. There was a small marble structure near the path with niches in the wall and brass nameplates and urns set behind frosted glass panels. I'd seen them before but always given them a wide berth, the same as everything else.

This time I was in a hurry and cut close, and I braced myself, and there was nothing.

I stopped. Stood directly in front of the niches. Close enough to read the names, close enough to touch the plates.

Quiet. Total, genuine quiet.

I stood there for ten minutes, maybe more. I read the names. I did the math on the dates. People who had died recent enough to have known the internet, people who had died in the eighties, in the seventies, and heard nothing. Not even a murmur. I stood in it the way you stand in front of a fireplace when the rest of the house has gone cold.

The only quiet graves I'd ever encountered before were the ancient ones, where whatever was left had thinned down over centuries to almost nothing. These people weren't ancient. Some of them had died in the last decade.

The difference, as best I could figure it, was the body. What takes centuries for time to do, fire does quickly.

I thought about that for a long time on the walk back to my car. I've thought about it, in one form or another, ever since.

The Doctor was still talking when I pulled my attention back to her office. She was explaining a treatment center two hours away, Hospice, support resources. The dog, a golden retriever, grinned from its frame on the desk.

I asked her to repeat the part about prognosis.

She did, carefully. I nodded. I asked practical questions about timelines and symptoms, about what the next few months would look like. I think I seemed calm. I was calm. I'd been afraid of this for a long time, in the abstract, and there's a particular relief that comes when a thing you've dreaded finally arrives. The uncertainty stops.

On the way home I called my sister and then I called my best friends Xavier and Eddie. Those calls were hard in the way those kind of calls are always hard. I don't need to describe them.

That evening I sat at my kitchen table with a legal pad and wrote down what I needed to arrange. There was a lot of it. There always is.

At the bottom of the list I wrote: Cremation. No burial. Spread the ashes on grandmother's farm.

My sister pushed back. She was insistent about the family plot, about having a place to visit, and I understood what she was really asking for. She wanted somewhere to come and feel close to me. I said it was what I wanted. I almost told her why. I thought about what her face would do if I did. I said I was sure, and she let it go.

I haven't told anyone why. What would I say. I hear the dead and they can't hear me, and I've spent twenty years walking fast through cemeteries, and the only quiet ones are the ones who burned. It doesn't make for an easy conversation.

I'll spend whatever time I have. There are people I love and things I want to finish. I'm not rushing anything. But I know how it ends, and I've made my arrangements, and the thought of the quiet columbarium garden is the closest thing to comfort I have right now.

Quiet is enough.

I've looked up the cremation process more than once. The temperatures, the duration. How fast it is, at those temperatures. How efficient.

I hope I can't feel the flames when I burn.
