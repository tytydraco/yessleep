I’m an entomologist, which means I concern myself with the study of insects. Discussing my line of work tends to bore people to tears, but children are absolutely enthralled by what I do; which is why I started providing educational talks for schools visiting the local natural history museum. I loved inspiriting youngsters about the most fascinating biological class of the animal kingdom.

Fascination and terror are not mutually exclusive, as I learnt six months ago, and I’m starting to wish my disinterested friends and family were correct.

It would be better for all of us if there truly were nothing extraordinary about insects.

Following one of my museum talks in late summer, a schoolboy waited until his teacher was distracted by his classmates, then he peeled away from the class to timorously tell me about his discovery of a new insect: one with wings coloured a ‘weird white’ and making a ‘whispery buzzing noise that you only hear if you pay close attention’.

He wasn’t the first excitable child (or even adult) to mistakenly proclaim himself the discoverer of a new species, but he was certainly the first *frightened* one. His fear piqued my curiosity, so when he told me to go underneath the pier at the local beach and see the white-winged insect for myself, I did just that.

I was a good hundred yards away when I first clocked the white-winged bugs. They were emitting a nauseatingly intimate buzz, like paper crinkling in my ear canals, as if they were nearer to me than they seemed to be. I told myself they must be glasshouse whiteflies, but such sap-feeding bugs would have been entirely out of place beneath a ramshackle wooden pier.

Besides, the truth of the matter was that they didn’t look like whiteflies.

They were about four times too large, and their shade of white was like no shade of white I’d seen before. ‘Weird white’, the boy had called it. Still, I dismissed that as a thought born of my sleep-deprived mind, much as I dismissed the alien crinkling sound, like no insect I’d ever heard. *A whispery buzzing noise that you only hear if you pay close attention*. The boy had been right again. I’d walked the pier dozens of times and probably never heard the little bugs, despite walking right overhead. It was only now, as I was looking and listening for them, that I had clocked them.

Even if I were to dismiss those many oddities, there was no dismissing their method of retreat: the bugs dove into the sea.

I must have paled. I’d never seen anything like it. *Suicidal insects?* I hypothesised, but I went over to the water lapping the shoreline and saw not a single drowned bug floating atop the surface. The flying bugs had swum underwater and vanished.

My colleagues laughed about my flying aquatic insect, and I found myself laughing with them. Disturbingly, the memory was already muddying in my mind. It was a type of lethargy like none I’d ever experienced before. I honestly believe, had that little boy not revisited the museum with his parents a week later, my brain would have erased all knowledge of what I saw and heard.

The parents chortled as their son talked about this ‘brand new’ insect. They said he had an overactive imagination, and I gave a slight smile in agreement, but then the boy said something which chilled my flesh.

“You’ve already forgotten, haven’t you? That’s what they do.”

His father scoffed. “They’re just bugs, kiddo. I’m sure Dr Farrow would be able to identify the species.”

The parents apologised for wasting my time and scooted their son away, but his words had dredged up that obscured memory of my visit to the pier. I remembered hearing a sound impossible to describe. I remembered seeing bugs, of an impossible colour, dive into the water and disappear.

Of course, as a scientist with beliefs still grounded in reality, I believed I might be having a psychotic break. The only way to be sure was to revisit the pier later that day.

I went to the beach, stood at my ‘safe’ distance of a hundred yards, and watched the people walking the length of the elevated pier walkway. Below, there were no bugs; only a woman standing with her face craning upwards, so she could peek through the spaces between the pier boards overhead. She was flailing about erratically, steering stiff limbs and popping goldfish-like lips, as if having a soundless conversation with someone. I followed her eyeline up to a lady on the pier; she was conversing with her friends and gesticulating enthusiastically with her arms.

The woman below was imitating the woman above.

I decided it was like watching a baby learn from an adult, only this was a grown woman learning from another grown woman. *Is it a woman?* came a horrid thought as I eyed the mimicker below the pier, and that brought out an involuntary whimper of terror from the pit of me. It was only a quiet sound. Too quiet for someone a hundred yards away to hear. Yet, the imitating woman immediately froze in position, as if playing the children’s game of musical statues.

No more than a second later, she snapped her face my way.

Her eyes were entirely white; only sclerae with *no pupils*. It was an impossible white, like the shade I’d seen only once before. And when pieces of the woman’s skin flaked off, carried by the wind towards me, I realised they were bugs.

I realised *she* was bugs.

I high-tailed it to the beach car park, too winded to scream. However, when I turned to look behind me, there was nothing but a concerned passer-by, likely wondering why I’d been in such a panicked hurry to get back to my vehicle. There were no white-winged insects. There was no woman.

From that day, I made a concerted effort to avoid the coastal town and pretend none of it had ever happened. Forget the bugs. Forget the boy. Return to ignorant bliss, contrary though that would be to my scientific principles. My fear overrode my curiosity.

Forgetting worked, to an extent. The mysterious sludge returned to my brain, drowning my memories; a defence mechanism of the bugs, I have always believed.

Nevertheless, while driving through a nearby town a few weeks later, I saw her crossing the road: the woman from under the pier. As I stopped, so did she, and her head turned to show me her eyes, which were no longer white voids. They bore brown pupils atop the white. She looked human, and she moved like a human, and I was sure she would sound like a human too. But I saw the falsity of those eyes and the smile below them, and when I really listened, I heard it again: that paper-crumpling sound, worming its way through the crevices of my car’s bodywork to drown the interior of the vehicle.

The maddening hum of the white-winged water bugs seemed almost to carry, hidden among the static, a warning; seemed almost to carry words.

*See you*.

I thought, for a moment, she would hurl her body onto the bonnet. My throat swelled and nearly closed up in terror as I waited for her to do so, and she certainly communicated that want with her eyes. Thankfully, she finished crossing the road, so I floored the accelerator pedal, believing I’d escaped her unscathed yet again. Then I looked into the rearview mirror and let out a terrified sob.

Blood was trickling out of my ears.

Her whispered, papery words had cut me.

I managed to forget again, for the black memory sludge was a blessing as much as it was a horror. But there was no forgetting forever. I think, like that little boy in the museum, I had opened Pandora’s Box. No matter how many times I buried the bugs and the woman, something would ladle out the sludge to dish up the truth once more. That sound of crinkling paper pursued me, and whenever I sought the source, I would always see somebody who didn’t look quite right: off-white sclerae and strangely practised movements.

That was when I stopped hiding from it. I recorded audio of the strange crinkling sound in public, and I covertly snapped photographs of strange-eyed strangers. I pestered the other scientists about my findings, and it took only a week for the institute to let me go, prompted by a supposed ‘concern for your mental wellbeing’.

Some of my colleagues promised, apologetically, that they weren’t being obstinate or oblivious. They were similarly unnerved by my data on this unclassified species of insect, but a ‘higher-up’ at the institute had threatened them to leave it alone or be sent out the door alongside me.

One colleague even mentioned he’d done a little digging and learnt of similar incidents at other scientific institutes. “My advice? Stop digging now, Dr Farrow. Count your blessings. I’ve heard of researchers meeting worse fates than a forced resignation.”

I felt vindicated. Of course it wasn’t only a little boy who knew about the bugs.

Powerful people are covering this up.

People who, I fear, might not even *be* people.

You see, for the past five months, I’ve been burning through my savings on a quest for answers. I’ve been travelling from continent to continent, country to country, city to city, and town to town. I’ve been studying members of the public for hours each day: recording them, photographing them, and verging on harassing them, I’ll admit. I’ve barely slept. Barely ate. And after collating data from 36,794 ‘people’ over the course of 189 days, do you know what I’ve found?

3707 subjects had off-coloured eyes or, when I really listened, emitted that paper-crinkling hum; and they were always the ‘people’ who regarded me with unyielding smiles and the highest suspicion. Many would stop in their tracks and whisper terrifying threats with their paper-crinkling voices, slicing my ear canals and causing blood to trickle free. Once or twice, the more daring of them even charged at me, and I scarpered in fright.

I found 3704 human-presenting non-humans.

10%.

10% of people come from the sea as white-winged bugs, mimic our behaviours, then morph into us.

10% of people aren’t people at [all](https://www.reddit.com/r/dominiceagle).
