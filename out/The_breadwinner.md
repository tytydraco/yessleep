When I first saw him in the restaurant, I thought he was one of those men who only know how to dress well and state the obvious with a confident tone; the kind of people you unconsciously listen to, even if what they are saying is wrong.

​His name was Ernest. He wore a dark coat, his watch was gleaming, and when he smiled, it felt as if everything in the world temporarily made sense.

"I just believe everyone should know their role in life," he said.

I gently placed my spoon on the plate and asked, "For instance, what is a woman's role?"

​Without a hint of awkwardness, he replied, "A woman can be strong too, of course. But does being strong always mean fighting outside? You don't always have to crush yourself just to prove you've become independent. I like the man to shoulder the outside work, while the woman maintains the peace of the home. I don't see it as demeaning; it's a division of labor."

​It was the kind of talk many dislike hearing, but coming from an attractive, calm man, it seemed oddly bearable. He wasn't one of those aggressive men who mock feminism by shouting, nor one of those detached intellectuals who pretend there is absolutely no difference between men and women. He simply spoke with certainty.

​I said, "So if a woman doesn't want to just stay at home...?"

​His smile faded slightly. "Then it just means I might not be the right match for her. That's all. Not everyone is meant for each other."

That night, we talked until midnight. About money, work, marriage, children, and why people like to turn love into a battlefield. I would disagree, he would explain. I would grow sharp, he would grow calmer. And every time I thought he was defeating me, he would say a sentence that softened everything.

​Like this: "I'm not afraid of a strong woman. I just don't like the person I love exposing herself to hardship for no reason."

​It touched me deeply.

​Our relationship began right there. Calm, normal, even pleasant. For someone like me who had spent years around people who either lied or were afraid, a man who spoke his mind with such clarity was like a breath of fresh air.

​Of course, I was lying too. I had secrets that absolutely no one could ever find out about, just like any other human being.

​A year passed since that night. Our relationship deepened, and we moved in together. It turned out he wasn't entirely bound to his own philosophy, because whenever my attention drifted from the house, he would immediately occupy himself with chores. But a heavy shadow of suspicion loomed larger over our home day by day. Ernest had noticed my suspicious behavior: sudden disappearances in the middle of the night, silent phone calls, and returning home at dawn looking disheveled and shameful. Most bizarre of all were my expensive purchases—oh my god! Without paying attention to this simple detail, I was bringing home the finest silk dresses and the most luxurious delicacies without spending a single cent of his money or telling anyone anything about my job.

​His suspicion was turning into certainty that I was earning money through illicit means. But he was suspicious too; he behaved exactly like me. He did everything to avoid conversation and prevent anyone from entering his private space. Like me, he came home late. Our life was truly becoming agonizing with all this secrecy. The heavy burden of this secret in my heart was poisoning our romantic relationship. I am exhausted; one day I must confess everything, and he must do the same for me.

​On the night of our anniversary, I decided to give him the real gift. Taking his hand under the pretext of a special surprise, I led him to an abandoned building on the dark outskirts of the city—my paternal home, where no one had lived for years.

​Upon seeing my father's house, Ernest looked at me, terrified. I could read it in his eyes.

But trying to keep himself calm, he said, "Anya, what is this place?"

With a smile, I replied, "It's a secret I have to reveal."

​A heavy silence hung in the air. Like a terrified child trying to hide his fear, Ernest walked quietly behind me. We passed through dusty hallways and damp rooms until we reached the end of one of the corridors. The door of the room we stood before was different from the rest; it was cleaner, sturdier, and looked newer. With pride and composure, I threw open the final door and switched on the surgical light hanging from the ceiling.

​Ernest's mouth dropped open in shock when he saw that wounded old rapist—whom the police had been desperately searching for for three days—bound hand and foot to the bed. With a fear I could completely sense in his tone, he asked, "Anya, what is going on here?"

​That reaction again! These foolish, cowardly men are all the same. Just like in the movies, he's surely terrified after seeing my secret. If he makes one wrong move, I can slit his throat with the knife I'm holding!

"This is my secret, something I’ve wanted to tell you for a long time. And it's in your best interest to tell me what's going on in that mind of yours and what your secret is!"

​He said nothing; he just kept staring at the old man.

The moment the old bastard laid eyes on him, he started screaming even louder, thrashing and weeping! For just a single second, my attention drifted to him, and I failed to notice the hand coming towards me.

​I was fast, but he was faster. He lunged forward, squeezed my throat, and whispered in my ear: "Didn't I tell you that the woman who lives with me has no right to work outside the house?"

​I was taken aback, but completely involuntarily, I nodded in obedience within his grasp, looking at him innocently and telling him I was sorry.

​Then he took my knife, threw me out of my cleansing room, and locked the door behind him.

I was pounding on the door, begging. His hands were shaking; he was ruining my handiwork!

​My god, why are men like this?
