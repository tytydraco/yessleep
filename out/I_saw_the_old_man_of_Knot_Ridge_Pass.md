My name is Alexander Patterson. I like to go camping as a hobby. I take my gear and head out on my faithful old beater, an old 90's Tacoma, and spend a few weeks out in the wilderness. I'm not stupid, I let people know where I am, and to call the police if I don't come back. 

I guess I didn't prepare enough that night. I took my old rust bucket down to this town at the base of somewhere called Knot Ridge Pass. Small, quaint little town with its own superstitions. I stopped at this diner for some grub, and when this waitress heard where I was going, she just grabbed my arm. "If you get snowed in, look for the old man." 

For context, Knot Ridge Pass is situated right between two huge mountains. It's big enough to camp in, but it was winter, and snow could trap me in the Pass. I brushed off her concern anyway. "I'll be fine." I mean, I was an experienced camper. What could go wrong? 

After dinner I took the car into the Pass and set up my camping site. Beautiful place, really. The towering mountains overhead combined with the dense forest made it look ethereal. Weather forecast looked clear for the week so I was settling down for a comfortable evening. I guess I was so tired that I didn't remember my head hitting the sleeping bag. 

When I woke up the next morning, the ground was covered in a thick blanket of snow. Local news confirmed I was snowed in. Not a problem, anyway, because I am an experienced camper. A bit of winter snow is well within my repertoire. 

So around that point I decided I needed some food. Since the Pass was free game as far as hunting officials were considered, I grabbed my rifle and went looking for some deer. It was unethical, but I did need to eat. It was a process I had repeated dozens of times before. Find the deer, aim, shoot, dress, and carry back. 

That routine was soundly interrupted. I found my target, an adult doe, and shot it as usual. It did not panic and run as the gunshot wound through its side bled black instead of red. It looked right at me, uttering a low groaning sound like wet boulders shifting faintly underneath the earth. Then it pounced. 

I have training for wildlife attacks just to be clear. But this thing was not what I trained for. Clubbing it over the head got it to release its toothed grasp on my arm. I panicked like a dumb idiot and ran, partly because of pain, and partly because of fear. That's when I saw the Cabin. 

The absolute strangeness of seeing a cabin in bum fuck nowhere didn't stop me from running up to it. I tried the door, locked. My ceaseless banging on the wood continued as whatever that creature was kept getting closer. To my relief, the door swung open, and I was allowed passage. 

Time felt strange in that cabin. Like entering a movie theater in the morning and exiting at night. The old man's eyes greeted me from across the single room. He looked too old to be human, face worn like prehistoric granite. The old man's eyes saw right through me. 

In all my adrenaline I had forgotten about the creatures bite. It was jagged and uneven, reeking of a putrid odor and oozing black pus. The pain and shock that washed in after the adrenaline left overtook me and I passed out. When I woke up, it was dark again. 

I was on the rooms only bed with thick bandages wrapped around my arm. The putrid smell was replaced by a distinctly leafy odor, like fresh pine trees. An old chain festooned with antique trinkets of various origin was wrapped around the dressing, resisting my attempts to remove it. 

The old man is gone now. There's a pot of what looks like meat and vegetable stew on the old wood stove. I  hesitantly had some and warmth and vigor instantly flooded through my veins. Now the door is locked from the outside and I can't open it. The snows won't clear for another few weeks.
