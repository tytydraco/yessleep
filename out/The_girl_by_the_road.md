I know this sounds cliché, but this story did not happen to me. In fact, I wouldn’t have remembered it if I hadn’t met with some friends a few days ago and *The Road* hadn’t come up in conversation. The story isn’t even that creepy, but knowing that someone I actually know, someone I trust, says it really happened makes me shiver a bit. Even her dad says it happened, too.

*The Road* is a five-mile stretch between two towns that neighbor my hometown. The first time I heard about it, I was coming home for Christmas. I had lived 700 miles away from my hometown for most of my adult life, and at the time, I was trying to make an effort to reconnect with my family. The only person I had spoken to for the majority of that time was my little brother.

Going home to spend Christmas with my family was going to be the first time I took such a long road trip, so my little brother flew to the city I was living in, stayed with me for a few days, and then we drove back together. The idea was to take turns driving so we wouldn’t have to stop too often to rest.

Initially, it was supposed to be a 12-hour drive, which meant that if we left at 5 AM, we could be there by 5 PM. However, there was a landslide that kept pushing the ETA further back. By the time it was 10 PM, we were still two towns and 60 miles away. My brother had been driving for the last two hours and was tired, so we stopped at a gas station, filled up, and I took over.

When we passed the first town and headed for the second, he said, “Go slow here. The curves are dangerous, and animals can jump in front of you out of the blue. Also, if you see someone on the side of the road trying to get you to stop, don’t.”

“Why not?” It was a dumb question. It was late, and picking up a hitchhiker wasn’t very smart, but it felt off that he needed to say it.

Then he said, very nonchalantly, “Because they’re not real.”

I almost slammed on the brakes he caught me so off guard. “What did you just say to me?”

“What?” he replied, just as unbothered. “Everyone knows that. This road is famous for it. You know, seeing things that aren’t there and people in black. How do you not know this? I thought you liked horror!”

“I like horror from the comfort of my own home, drinking tea, with headphones on; not like this!” He smiled, so I said, “You’re screwing with me, right?”

He cackled a little. “I’m laughing, but I’m not screwing with you. This is a real thing that happens. I’ve seen it a couple of times.”

“Nah, you’re bullshitting me.”

“Okay, if you say so. Just… you know, don’t stop if you see anything.”

I didn’t see anything that night. We got home about 45 minutes later, and I completely forgot about it for about two years. A year after that trip, I moved back home. My father had kidney failure, which meant he needed a lot of care, and I didn’t want something to happen to him while I was 700 miles away. Three months after moving back, I met an amazing woman, and we started dating. Her family is originally from one of the towns connected by *The Road*—the first town I passed through on that road trip, actually. It was while visiting one of her relatives that I heard the story for the first time.

My girlfriend’s cousin was celebrating her son’s first birthday. There was barbecue, beer, and children running around, so a few of us sat in a far corner and began talking about nothing in particular. The conversation slowly shifted toward road trips and traveling because my girlfriend and I enjoy a bit of adventure biking, and we talked about how annoying it is when something on the road changes your whole itinerary. I mentioned what had happened to me with the landslide, how it forced me onto *The Road* at 10 PM, and how my little brother told me people just knew spooky things happened there.

“Oh, please. Superstition!” said the cousin’s husband.

“No, it isn’t! If you travel between towns often enough, you know something’s wrong!” said my girlfriend’s cousin.

“People are gullible, man,” he added.

“It’s real, honey. I know it firsthand,” she replied.

“Oh, sure, babe.”

“No, for real. I remember when I was like 15. And mind you, this happened at 2 PM, with the sun blazing. I was on vacation. I was tagging along while my dad worked and I was riding shotgun in his pickup truck. He had to make a delivery, some sound system for a party or something, and we had to drive through the road. We were approaching that big curve, about two miles before the gas station? You know the one? When we saw this girl. She was 16 or 17 and walking along the side of the road. She was wearing a blue top and gray jeans. She looked tired, so my dad stopped next to her and asked if she needed help. She said she just needed to make a phone call to let her mom know where she was. My dad offered her his phone. She was kind of guarded at first, but seeing a young girl with him probably convinced her he wasn’t a predator or something, so she asked if he could give her a lift to the gas station where her mom could picked up.”

She took a sip of her beer, cleared her throat, and continued. “Anyway, my dad gave her his phone. She made the call, and I could hear everything her mother was saying: ‘We were so worried. Where are you? We’re coming to get you,’ right? They said their goodbyes, ‘I love you, Mom,’ and ‘See you in a while.’ During the remaining two minutes it took us to get to the gas station, she was quiet and seemed to be falling asleep. She was also sweating, a lot, like she’d just been dipped in a pool and had her clothes put back on afterward, which was weird because it was a really cool day. We finally got to the gas station, and my dad offered to stay with her while she waited for her parents, but she said it was okay and that she was safe now. She thanked us, and we kept driving. We made some deliveries, and when the sun began to set, we went home.”

At this point, she began to breathe heavily, like she was struggling to find the words to finish the story. “When the 7 PM news started, we heard it. A 17 year old girl who had been missing was found by her mother near a creek behind the gas station. She was wearing a blue top and gray jeans. It was her, I knew it before I even saw the picture on TV. She’d been missing for almost a day and had been killed and dumped by that creek that morning. That means that by the time my dad and I picked her up, she had been dead for five hours.”

We all looked at each other; some of us genuinely creeped out, others pretending the air didn’t feel heavier somehow.

My girlfriend’s cousin finished her story by saying, “I don’t care if you believe me. This happened to me and my dad. Go ask him if you want, but it happened. I had to sleep with the lights on for weeks.”

Needless to say, I did ask her dad. He corroborated it. And he added that as soon as that girl got into the pickup truck, he felt unbelievably sad and cold, and he couldn’t understand why.

They never caught whoever hurt that girl.

I don’t travel down that road much. It's not that I'm always thinking about that story, I barely remember it now. But there is something primal about not wanting to go near that 5 mile section. I think I’ve gotten used to avoiding it by default; even during the day.
