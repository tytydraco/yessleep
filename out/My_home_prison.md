There is a specific gravity in being an impostor—a density measured not in steps or breaths, but in millimeters of discrepancy. My jailer is a man of meticulous habits; he uses no chains, only a geometric patience. He watches me inhabit this space with the coldness of one who has designed a masterpiece and now oversees its maintenance, ensuring that nothing of my true nature ever cracks the surface of his creation.

He says this structure is a victory, a legacy of straight lines and proportions that I should honor with the silence of the devout. He does not understand that I am not the owner of what I see; I am its forced occupant, an intruder desperately seeking an exit between walls that bear my name but not my thumbprint. Every morning, he polishes the boundaries of my prison and compels me to recognize myself in an order he calls 'destiny' and I call 'occupation.' I do not inhabit a place. I inhabit a calculation error.

The room where he receives me every afternoon has no windows, only darkened mirrors that return an echo of shadows. It is an environment ruled by symmetry: the walls are clad in a gray stone, cold to the touch as judgment, and the air tastes of something sterile—a void that has forgotten the wind. Here, every piece of furniture is arranged according to a logic that admits no deviation.

He sits in a dark velvet armchair, elbows resting on the armrests to form perfect right angles. He watches me enter, his gaze a spirit level seeking the flaw in my posture. I want to ask him why he is so obsessed with geometry, why he feels the need to constrain me within this marble perimeter when the space outside is so vast, so fluid. But I merely obey.

He rises, moving with a grace I find obscene. He approaches and places a hand on the wall, right beside my head. I feel the cold of the stone transfer through the boundaries of my prison, a mineral shiver running through my foundations.

"Vastness is chaos," he whispers, with a hint of pity. "Without these walls, without this magnificent armor of angles and weights I have preserved for you, you would be nothing. I am not holding you back. I am defining you."

He remains motionless, a breath away from my facade. His eyes trace the lines of my prison with the pride of an architect admiring a flawless perspective.

"If I allowed you to spill out," he continues, "you would destroy the beauty of this project. You are the steward of a perfect form. Do not allow disorder to inhabit it."

I do not answer. I cannot. I remain still under the weight of his benevolence, while in the darkness of my basements, far from his eyes, the fire I am fueling finally begins to lick the ceiling.

When the door closes and the sound of his footsteps is swallowed by the hallway carpet, the silence that remains is a thick poison. I am left alone with the architecture he has chosen for me. I move toward the center of the room, where the light falls vertically, pitiless, like an indictment.

For years, I believed my prison had iron doors and deadbolt locks. I was wrong. My cell is made of this perfect symmetry he calls 'beauty.' It is in the muscles that tense with a strength I did not ask for, in the curve of the jawline that cuts the air like a knife turned against me.

I approach the polished surface on the back wall. It is not glass; it is a silver abyss that does not lie. My fingers, long and knotty—tools for a labor I do not wish to perform—tremble as they touch the contours of my exile. I feel the heat of rebellion pressing against the cold logic of my tissues. He says he is defining me. I say he is erasing me beneath a shroud of unwanted perfection.

I extract the only tool I managed to hide from his architect’s gaze. It is small, cold—a fragment of metallic truth.

As the first line is drawn, I feel no pain. I feel only the wonderful sound of armor breaking.

My jailer would call it suicide. Or perhaps mutilation. He, who spent twenty years praising the sturdiness of this chest, the density of the hair on my face, the gravity of a voice that never stopped vibrating in my throat like an insult. He called me 'son' as if it were a noble title, never realizing that to me, it was only the name of a solitary cell.

But metal does not lie. As it slides over the skin he considered sacred, my prison finally reveals its nature as a fragile vessel. There is no basement. There is no secret room.

There is only a man who never existed, except in the eyes of the one who sired him.

And as the blood bathes the floor, warm and free from all geometry, I am not dying. I am simply stepping out of the host that has held me prisoner since birth. I am ceasing to be his masterpiece of masculinity to become, at last, a heap of rubble.

The most beautiful rubble I have ever seen.
