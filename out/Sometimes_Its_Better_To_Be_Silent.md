I study astrophysics.

Not the kind people romanticize. No telescopes on rooftops or making wishes on shooting stars. My work is mostly numbers, graphs, and long hours staring at data that doesn’t care whether you understand it or not.

I’ve always preferred it that way.

Data doesn’t lie.

People do.

There’s a question that follows you in this field, whether you want it to or not.

If the universe is this big… why is it so quiet?

We call it the Fermi Paradox. You’ve probably heard of it.

There should be signals. Civilizations. Something.

But there isn’t.

At least, that’s what we thought.

Here are some theories that I’ve come up with:

1. Silence might be intentional:

We assume the universe is quiet because no one is there.

But what if it’s quiet because everyone learned not to make noise?

If something out there reacts to signals—radio waves, light emissions, anything detectable—then the smartest civilizations wouldn’t broadcast.

They would go silent.

And the only ones making noise…

Would be the ones that don’t know any better yet.

Like us.

2. The “Dark Forest” idea:

It goes like this:

The universe is like a dark forest.

Every civilization is a hunter.

No one knows who is friendly.

No one knows who is dangerous.

So the safest move?

Stay quiet.

And if you detect something else—

Eliminate it before it detects you.

No warnings.

No communication.

Just silence.

3. If something was obvious, we would have noticed it… right?:

We assume that anything big or dangerous in space would be detectable.

But that assumes we know what to look for.

Every discovery in science has one thing in common:

We didn’t see it until we knew it existed.

So ask yourself:

What if there’s something obvious out there—

Something massive.

Something active.

And we’ve been looking right at it…

But our brains just don’t recognize it as unusual?

All of these theories… they don’t add up to what’s happening right now.

It all started that day…

Where it all began.

It was a sunny day.

Well, of course it was—it was summer.

I’m not really into the idea of vacations or outdoor activities, especially on busy days like that. I don’t want to sound bitter about not taking breaks, but I’ve always been the kind of person who prefers work over everything else.

So I stayed.

While I was at work, the first anomaly didn’t even reach my desk.

It came in as a minor report from a monitoring station assigned to the asteroid belt. Routine scanning. Object tracking. Nothing worth escalating.

It was labeled as “unidentified drift mass.”

That’s a polite way of saying, we don’t know what this is, but it’s probably nothing.

I only saw it because Mara forwarded it to me with a single message:

“Does this look wrong to you?”

I opened the file expecting debris.

What I saw… didn’t behave like debris.

“Pull up the trajectory again,” I told her the next morning.

She already had it ready.

“It doesn’t follow any known path,” she said. “No orbital pattern. No momentum loss.”

I stared at the screen.

The object wasn’t tumbling.

It wasn’t spinning.

It wasn’t even drifting the way objects should in that region.

It was moving with intent.

“Maybe it’s a fragment from a collision,” I said, even though I didn’t believe it.

“Fragments don’t correct their direction,” Mara replied.

She was right.

Every few hours, the path adjusted slightly. Not enough to be obvious. Just enough to stay aligned.

“With what?” I asked.

She didn’t answer immediately.

Then she zoomed out.

Pluto.

“…that’s not a coincidence,” I said.

“No,” she replied quietly.

“It’s not.”

It took six days to reach Pluto.

We tracked it the entire time.

No heat signature.

No emissions.

No visible propulsion.

Just movement.

Steady. Patient.

When it got close, we expected impact.

That’s what usually happens. Objects collide, they break apart, they scatter…

That’s not what happened.

“Where is it?” someone asked.

We had visual confirmation seconds before.

Then…

Nothing.

“Did we lose the feed?” I asked.

Mara shook her head slowly.

“No… it’s still there.”

“Where?”

She pointed at the screen.

Pluto’s surface.

“It didn’t crash,” she said.

“It… landed.”

The word felt wrong.

Things don’t just “land” on Pluto without consequence.

We switched to thermal imaging.

Nothing.

Infrared.

Nothing.

“It’s like it isn’t there,” someone muttered.

“But it is,” I said.

Because something had changed.

The surface readings weren’t stable anymore.

Subtle shifts.

Pressure variations.

Tiny distortions in the data that shouldn’t exist on a body that cold and that still.

“Is it moving?” I asked.

Mara hesitated.

“…I think so.”

We enhanced the footage.

Frame by frame.

At first, nothing.

Then—

A slight change.

Not the object itself.

The ground.

Like something was pressing down.

Then dragging forward.

“…that’s not possible,” the analyst whispered.

It stayed on Pluto for two days.

Then it disappeared.

No departure.

No debris.

No trace.

“Buried?” someone suggested.

“Or moved,” I said.

We didn’t know where.

Not yet.

But the silence in the room that day felt heavier than usual.

Because we all knew something had just happened.

And none of us understood it.

The next anomaly came from Neptune.

This time, it wasn’t labeled as minor.

“Atmospheric distortion,” the report said.

But Neptune’s atmosphere is chaotic by nature. Storms, winds, constant movement.

Distortion shouldn’t stand out.

Unless it’s wrong.

“Show me,” I said.

They pulled it up.

At first, it looked like a storm system.

Swirling gases. Shifting colors.

Normal.

Then I saw it.

A section that wasn’t moving with the rest.

“…pause it,” I said.

The image froze.

“Zoom in.”

The edges of the distortion didn’t blend.

They didn’t dissipate like gas should.

They held shape.

Like something solid.

“Track that region,” I said.

They did.

It moved.

Against the wind.

Slow.

Deliberate.

Mara leaned closer.

“…it followed the same path.”

I didn’t need to ask what she meant.

Pluto.

Now Neptune.

“Inward,” I said.

It stayed there longer this time.

Four days.

During that time, Neptune’s energy readings fluctuated.

Not dramatically.

But enough.

Like something was… taking.

When it left—

We didn’t see it go.

We just saw the absence.

Uranus was next.

Then Saturn.

Then Jupiter.

Each time—

The same pattern.

Arrival without entry.

Presence without visibility.

Movement without explanation.

And every time—

Energy readings dropped.

Not enough to trigger alarms.

But enough to notice.

By the time it reached Jupiter—

We stopped pretending it was coincidence.

“This is a progression,” Mara said.

I nodded.

“Outer system to inner,” I added.

“Why?” someone asked.

No one answered.

Because the answer was already forming.

And none of us wanted to say it out loud.

“Where does it go next?” the analyst asked.

I looked at the orbital map.

There was only one direction left.

“…the sun,” Mara said.

That’s when the Pentagon called.

The meeting felt smaller than I expected.

Not physically.

Just… emotionally.

Like the room already knew what we were going to say.

The general didn’t waste time.

“Something’s wrong with our sun.”

“We know,” I said.

That got his attention.

We showed them everything.

The asteroid belt.

Pluto.

Neptune.

Uranus.

Saturn.

Jupiter.

The path.

The pattern.

The energy loss.

And finally—

The sun.

The footage played.

The sun flickered.

No—

It disappeared.

For less than a second.

Then returned.

“That’s not a malfunction,” the general said.

“No,” I replied.

“It’s the same thing.”

Silence.

“It moved inward,” Mara said.

“And now it’s here.”

“And doing what?” the general asked.

I took a breath.

“Feeding.”

We were sent to space two days later.

Not to confirm.

But to see.

The closer we got—

The worse I felt.

At first, it was mild.

A headache.

A pressure behind my eyes.

Then nausea.

“Are you okay?” Mara asked.

“…yeah,” I said.

I wasn’t.

It got worse the closer we moved.

Like something in my body—

Didn’t want me there.

The pilot glanced back.

“You’re not the only one,” he said quietly.

That didn’t make me feel better.

The sun filled the observation window.

Blinding.

Massive.

Normal.

For a moment.

“Zoom in,” I said.

The image sharpened.

And then—

We saw it.

Something on the surface.

Not separate.

Not behind.

Attached.

Spread across the sun—

Like something alive.

“…oh my God,” Mara whispered.

It moved.

Slow.

Dragging.

And wherever it moved—

The light dimmed.

Not instantly.

Gradually.

Like something being drained.

“It’s the same thing,” I said.

“Pluto. Neptune. Jupiter…”

“It didn’t leave,” Mara said.

“It grew.”

That’s when it turned.

Not physically.

But attention.

I felt it.

That same pressure—

But deeper.

My stomach twisted.

I dropped to my knees.

“I can’t…” I gasped.

“What’s wrong?” the pilot shouted.

“It knows we’re here.”

I didn’t know how I knew.

I just did.

The thing moved faster.

Across the surface—

Like a parasite adjusting to disturbance.

“A mosquito,” I whispered.

“What?” Mara asked.

“It’s like a mosquito…”

“Landing. Feeding. Moving.”

“But that’s the sun,” she said.

“Yes,” I replied.

“That’s the problem.”

Then the sun blinked again.

But now—

We saw it clearly.

The thing pressed deeper.

And the light disappeared beneath it.

Not gone—

Hidden.

Drained.

We left after that.

No discussion.

No hesitation.

Back on Earth—

Everything looks normal.

But it isn’t.

The light feels… thinner.

Weaker.

And sometimes—

For less than a second—

Everything pauses.

Like something is… adjusting.

Mara doesn’t sleep anymore.

She told me something yesterday.

“I think we misunderstood the silence,” she said.

“How?” I asked.

She looked at the sky.

“It’s not empty.”

“It’s quiet.”

“Because whatever is out there…”

“…moves slowly.”

“And by the time you notice it…”

“…it’s already too close.”

The sun blinked again last night.

Longer this time...


And in that moment—

I felt it again...

That nausea...

That pressure...

Like something—

Is still feeding...

Still growing..

Months past after the mission....Presidents all of over the globe  had a major announcement that made the world scared and hopeless..

"As we are experiencing this catastrophic event...we all know that something is wrong with our sun..we are not alone in the universe..and as I am here standing ..speaking..The sun is getting drained by an unknown entity..I suggest that you enjoy the remaining days,weeks with your love ones..there's nothing we can do to prevent this...may God be with you.."


I was in disbelief that were just here...waiting...waiting for it all to end..

Each day I could feel the suns temperature slowly dropped..some Asian countries experiencing...cold weather even if it's summer...there's no such a thing as summer anymore...

The light gets dimmer and dimmer as the days go by..

"Is this the end?" I ask to myself

"Is there nothing we can do to stop this?"

"Waiting for our demise...until the day the sun faded?.."

We knew the consequences of sending signals out there...And yet we still insist on doing so...

We were all wrong..


We are not alone.
