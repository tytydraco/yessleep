And I think I am starting to notice it too late.

Except for the factory. The Redbrook Milk Factory. It rises above everything. Not just tall — heavy. Like it is pressing down on the entire street. Steel silos. Industrial pipes. Sections that look newer than others, as if it is constantly being changed. Rebuilt. Adjusted.

At night, there is a sound. A low hum. Constant. Steady. It never changes. And once you notice it, you cannot stop hearing it. Even when everything else is silent.

My grandfather worked there when it first opened. At least, that is what my family said. He never explained what he actually did there. He never spoke about it properly. Until one day, when I was eleven.

It was raining. Not heavy rain — just steady. The kind that makes everything feel quieter than it should. We were sitting in his living room. The television was muted. Through the window, you could see the factory in the distance.

I asked him what it was like working there. He did not answer straight away. He just stared forward for a long time, like he was listening to something I could not hear. Then he said: “They told us it was dairy production.” He paused. “But it was not.”

“There are levels under the ground,” he said. “They are not on any plans.” Then he added something I never forgot: “People went down there. And not all of them came back the same.”

After that, he never spoke about it again.

Years passed. Life stayed normal. Until people started disappearing.

At first, it did not seem connected. A teenager. A man walking home late. People vanish sometimes. That is what everyone said.

But then it got closer. Students from Chequers Lane Primary School. A boy after chess club. A girl after football practice. No witnesses. No signs. No explanation. Just gone.

Every case had one thing in common. The path behind the factory.

One night, I went there with my friend Prince. Just after midnight. The air felt wrong immediately — cold, heavy. My phone lost signal. So did his.

The deeper we walked, the quieter everything became. No wind. No traffic. No sound except something low in the distance. The hum.

We found a metal door in the ground. It was not old. Not abandoned. It looked maintained. But scratched deeply. Some marks looked like they came from inside.

Prince said we should leave. I opened it anyway.

The door slammed shut behind us. No lock. No handle. Just sealed.

Then we were inside. Concrete corridors. Pipes overhead. Flickering lights. The hum was everywhere now — inside the walls, inside the floor, inside everything.

My phone turned on for a second. Then died again. That should not have been possible.

We kept walking. Then we saw rooms made of glass. People inside. Standing still. At first, I thought they were not real. Then one blinked. Slowly.

They were alive. But wrong. Like something could not keep them stable.

A man in a lab coat walked past. He did not look at us. But he said: “Younger subjects stabilize longer.” Then he kept walking. Like we were not there.

We went deeper. We should not have.

A massive chamber. Machines everywhere. And a tank in the centre. Inside it… bodies. Suspended in pale liquid. Floating. Still.

Then I saw my grandfather. His eyes were open. And they moved when they saw me. He recognised me.

Everything stopped. Then alarms started. The facility reacted like it had become aware of us.

We ran. But the corridors changed as we moved. Doors appeared where there were none. Hallways shifted behind us. Like the place was correcting itself.

Then I saw him again. Behind glass. He pressed his hand against it. And mouthed: “Do not.”

We escaped. Or at least, we thought we did.

Outside, everything looked normal again. Our phones worked. The street was quiet. Like nothing had happened.

But things did not stop. They continued. Small inconsistencies. Repetitions. A man walking the same route twice. A child freezing too long at the school gate, then continuing like nothing happened.

And always… the hum.

Then it started happening at 3:17 AM. Every night. No alarm. No reason. Just waking up at the exact same time.

Prince noticed it too. He said it was not repeating anymore. It was continuing. Like something was still building the world. Adjusting it. Fixing it.

Then I went back alone. The path was gone. But I could still hear it. The hum.

My grandfather appeared again. He said it was not a factory. He called it a correction system. Something that records reality. Then rebuilds it. Better each time. More stable. Less flawed.

He said I was still seeing errors. Because I was one of them. An unstable element.

And once I stop seeing them… the correction will finish. And I will not notice anything wrong again. Ever.

Now I am writing this because I do not know how much time I have left to notice it. Because things are getting smoother. Too smooth. Too consistent. Too perfect.

If you are reading this… and something feels slightly off… just small things… repeating moments… delayed reactions… sounds you cannot place… then you are still seeing it too.

But it will not stay like that forever. Because the Redbrook Milk Factory is not a place. It is a process.

And eventually… everything becomes part of it.

Even this. Even me. Even you.
