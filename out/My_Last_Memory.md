>We always call them coincidences—those tiny, ordinary moments that feel slightly off. You’re sipping your coffee and the air turns brittle, a sudden frost that has nothing to do with the weather. You blame the draft. You always do. But it wasn’t a draft. It was the room getting crowded.

Here is one such incident which when I look backward, guess it was not just any coincidence, or may be I am thinking too much.

**December 18, 2016**

It was a bitter winter night. I was hunched over in my chair, the blue light of my laptop screen bleeding into the darkness of the room. I was trying to force a horror story into existence; the atmosphere was perfect, but the pages remained stubbornly flat. There was nothing creepy about my writing—at least, not yet.

I reached for my orange copper bottle, but my hand recoiled for a second. It was thick with tarnish, a dull, dark film covering the metal that had been polished clean only hours before. I could even smell it—that sharp, metallic scent of old pennies. *Maybe the air is too humid,* I told myself, a weak excuse for how fast the metal had soured. I ignored the intuition tightening in my chest and turned back to the screen.

"Mamma, can you fill this for me? I’m in the middle of something."

No reply.

"Mamma? You there?" I raised my voice, but the house didn't offer so much as a floorboard creak in response. It was a heavy, suffocating kind of silence.

*She’s just watching TV with the door closed,* I reasoned. But a cold thought nagged at me: even with her door shut, I should have heard the muffled drone of the news or the flicker of a sitcom. Instead, there was just... nothing. I stayed in my chair, laziness acting as a shield against the growing unease.

I managed to hammer out two whole pages. Suddenly, the story was flowing, turning dark and visceral—so much so that the hair on my arms began to stand up. But the goosebumps didn't come from the words on the screen.

It was the sound.

A rhythmic, splintering creak, like heavy boots pressing into old wooden floorboards. It was unmistakable. I kept typing, my fingers trembling slightly as I tried to rationalize it away. *My mind is just playing tricks because of the story,* I thought. *This house is all marble tiles. There is no wood here to creak.*

But the sound didn't stop.

The thirst became unbearable, a dry ache in my throat that forced me out of my seat. I grabbed the tarnished bottle and stood up.

"Mamma, seriously, what the hell is going on?"

I walked toward the door. It was hanging slightly ajar, revealing nothing but a sliver of the pitch-black hallway outside.

“Wait, the hallway is too dark. Let me just... turn the lights on.”

I was talking to myself. I never talk to myself unless I’m trying to drown out a silence that feels too heavy. I flicked the switch for my room’s main light. For a split second, as the bulb hummed to life, I saw it—a smudge of absolute black in the corner, tucked behind the heavy folds of my curtains.

“Just an illusion,” I whispered. “Pareidolia. The brain is just a pattern-recognition machine playing blind chess with itself. It sees a face in a cloud; it sees a ghost in a shadow. Nothing more.”

I clung to that reasoning, even as a sharp, cold tightness clamped down between my lungs.

But then I looked at the door.

My room was flooded with light, yet the hallway remained a solid, impenetrable void. Even with the door wide open, not a single photon seemed to cross the threshold. It was as if the darkness outside was hungry, actively devouring the light before it could escape the room.

I tried to force my brain back into its comfort zone. *Paranormal phenomena don't exist,* I told myself. *Science has an answer for everything.* Maybe it was high Electromagnetic Interference (EMI) causing a localized hallucination? Or perhaps the air in the room had become so incredibly dense that I was witnessing a freak occurrence of Total Internal Reflection (TIR), trapped within the boundary of the doorway.

But the logic failed as soon as it formed. For TIR to occur at that scale, the air in my room would have to be thick enough to crush my chest. I wouldn't be able to breathe, let alone stand.

In that moment, I realized I wasn't just rationalizing. I was lying to myself to keep from screaming.

“Alright, Avinash! It’s nothing. It’s literally nothing. Just get into the hallway, turn the lights on, navigate to the kitchen, and fill the bottle. That’s it. Maybe I’ll check on Mamma on the way back.”

I forced myself across the threshold. The darkness didn't just surround me; it felt heavy, like I had dived into the ocean and accidentally stirred up the silt on the seabed. In that kind of murky void, you can’t see your own hand in front of your face. You just have to trust the safety rope. My "rope" was my proprioception—my brain’s internal map of my own home. I didn't need eyes to find the switchboard; my muscles already knew the way.

My fingers found the cold plastic of the switch. I flicked it.

Nothing. The hallway remained a solid block of black.

“Ah... wrong switch. My mistake. Let me try again.” I heard myself laughing—a jagged, high-pitched sound that was halfway to a scream. I was making as much noise as possible, trying to suffocate the terrifying silence of the house.

I flicked the next switch. Still nothing.

“I guess the bulbs are fused. They’re old... that’s perfectly natural.” I was talking faster now, my breath hitching in my throat. “Just as natural as the temperature. The hallway is just... naturally colder than the rest of the house. Thermodynamics. It’s all just physics.”

I kept laughing, but the sound felt hollow, echoing off the marble walls like it belonged to someone else.

Suddenly, I felt it. A plume of ice-cold breath against my right shoulder.

Every single cell in my body began to scream, but my throat was a desert; I couldn't make a sound. My heart wasn't just racing—it was hammering against my ribs like a trapped animal trying to break free of its cage. I began to back away, one trembling step at a time, retreating toward the sanctuary of my room.

I sped up, my heels clicking frantically on the marble, when a hand—dead-cold and impossibly strong—clamped around my left wrist from behind.

"Help! HELP!"

The scream finally tore out of me. I lunged forward, wrenching my arm away with such violence that I felt the skin tear. Long, sharp nails raked across my flesh. I didn't stop to look back. I dove into my room and slammed the door, fumbling with the lock until the bolt clicked into place.

I slumped against the wood, gasping. I looked down at my hand. It was a mess; hot, dark blood was already dripping from my fingertips, spotting the white tiles. This wasn't a trick of the light. This was physical.

I scrambled toward the window, desperate to see a neighbor, a car—anything that belonged to the real world. I tore back the curtains, expecting the familiar glow of the streetlights.

Nothing.

The world outside was a void of absolute, ink-black darkness. There were no vehicles, no streetlamps, not even the silhouette of the house across the way. It made no sense. Yesterday had been a full moon, but tonight, the sky was a prison. Thick, unnatural clouds had swallowed the moon whole, engulfing every last drop of light as if the universe itself had been extinguished.

A sharp, rhythmic knock sounded against the wood. I spun around, my breath catching in my throat, only to see the impossible: the door I had just bolted shut was hanging wide open.

It wasn't just a hallway out there anymore. The darkness was pouring into my room like a physical tide, thick and ink-like, swallowing the floorboards. I scrambled backward, my hands frantically searching my desk for anything—a pen, a lamp—something to use as a weapon. But before I could grab a thing, the bulb in my ceiling flickered once and died.

The blackout was absolute.

The only thing I could hear was the wet, ragged sound of my own breathing and the heavy *thud-thud* of my heart. Then, I remembered my phone in my pocket. My hands were shaking so violently I nearly dropped it, but I managed to thumb the screen and hit the flashlight icon.

The beam of light cut a jagged hole into the blackness. I swung it around the room—nothing. I turned back toward the window—nothing. Then, I felt it.

Two hands, cold as grave-dirt, clamped down onto my shoulders from behind.

I spun the light around, the beam catching a face inches from my own. It was a jagged, gaunt nightmare—skin stretched like grey parchment over a skull, eyes sunken into rotting pits of shadow. It didn't scream; it just stared, its mouth pulled back into a wide, lipless grin.

The world tilted. The light dropped from my hand, and everything went black.

I woke up to the sterile, sharp scent of antiseptic and the steady *beep-beep* of a heart monitor. My head was heavy, wrapped in thick layers of gauze. According to Mamma, she found me sprawled in the hallway, unconscious and bleeding out from the gashes on my arm. She had heard nothing—no screams, no footsteps, no knocking. Just the sound of my body hitting the marble floor.

The contrast is what haunts me. To Mamma, the scene was mundane, almost boring. No screams, no struggle, no impossible darkness. In her version of reality, I simply slipped on the polished marble and hit my head—a routine accident caused by a dark hallway and a bit of clumsiness.

But as the antiseptic sting fades, my scientific brain is already discarding her theory. It’s searching for a new hypothesis to explain the data. If I rule out the paranormal as "unproven," I am left with a much more clinical terror.

*Schizophrenia.* The symptoms fit with terrifying precision: auditory hallucinations (the wood creaking), visual distortions (the "hungry" darkness), and tactile delusions (the cold breath, the hands on my shoulders). Perhaps the gashes on my arm weren't from nails, but from my own frantic, unconscious scrambling against the furniture.

I am at a crossroads of fear. One path leads to a world where ghosts are real and the laws of physics can be broken. The other leads to a world where my own mind is a traitor, a "prison of darkness" that I can never escape.

I’m not sure which one I was praying for.

I was discharged two days later. The psychiatrist was clinical, professional, and entirely unimpressed. After a battery of tests, she found my brain was functioning within perfectly normal parameters.

“It’s not Schizophrenia, Avinash,” she said, scribbling on her clipboard. “And it’s certainly not ghosts. You’ve been consuming too much ‘horror junk’ while writing your story. When you slipped and hit your head, your brain entered a state of partial consciousness. You were dreaming while awake, fueled by your own creative adrenaline. Your mind just filled in the blanks with the monsters you were already thinking about.”

I wanted to believe her. Scientifically, ghosts have no measurable mass, no energy signature, and no place in the laws of thermodynamics. If they aren't proven, they don't exist.

That evening, my friend Abhinav dropped by to check on me. I told him everything—the darkness, the scratches, the psychiatrist’s dismissal.

“You must have done something to offend them,” Abhinav said, his voice low. “Whatever is in that house... you should just ask for forgiveness.”

I laughed, though it sounded forced. “Ask forgiveness from whom, Abhinav? My own synapses? If I apologize to a shadow, I’m just training my brain to believe the lie. Science hasn't proven ghosts, so I refuse to acknowledge them.”

Abhinav left shortly after, looking worried. I went to bed early, clinging to my psychiatrist's words like a life raft. *It was just a dream. A concussion. Horror junk.*

But that night, the darkness returned.

It didn't come with knocking this time. There was no cold breath on my shoulder, no desperate struggle at the door. It was much worse.

As I lay there, I felt the mattress beside me sink. It was a slow, heavy indentation, as if someone—or something—had just sat down right next to my hip. I could feel the radiant cold through the blankets. I stared at the empty space on the bed, my heart vibrating with a terror that no textbook could explain.

In the crushing silence, a memory of my Physics professor floated to the surface of my mind, a warning I had dismissed months ago:

*“Just because something hasn't been proven in science, it doesn't mean it has been disproven.”*

I realized then that science wasn't a shield. It was just a map of the things we understood. And I was currently sharing my bed with something that wasn't on the map.

By the way this was the last episode that happened to me and back to 2026, there are no more such episodes. But this story of mine as I send this to my friend who literally published this on this platform, literally gave me a way out.

*As Abhinash, I ignored them too much, and they were tired of that. They wanted to prove there presence to a rude scientific dude.*

***But anyways, thanks for reading my story, though may be I will meet you soon if you don’t mind someone lying on your bed by you side.***
