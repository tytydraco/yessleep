I was 13 years old when I attended the funeral of a sweet old lady that lived across the street.  


She used to be the town librarian, she was unmarried and had never had children but she was the most caring person you could ever have the pleasure of meeting. I had never seen her with anything but a smile on her face, her dainty figure overflowing with kindness and good intentions. You would hear her melodic voice travel down the street as she strolled through town and all your troubles would melt away.   


One day, a year before her funeral, for the first time in my life, I felt a chill go through me as she passed by. She was scowling and had the demeanor of a person tormented by a dark cloud floating above their head. She looked harried and distracted, her eyes darting around, filled with suspicion and dread. I offered up a greeting and she seemed to jump out of her skin in fright. Then she grabbed my head and searched my eyes with hers. Whatever she saw, staring into my soul like that, made her break out in tears.

"Are we alone? Is it just you and me left?"

The sound of footsteps coming around the corner made her scurry off, and that was the last time I saw her face to face until the funeral. Everyone said that she was losing her mind. That her age had caught up to her. She would scream relentlessly when anyone came near her door, horrific shrieks that would terrify me as a child. Late at night I could sometimes see her through my window. Her front door would open ever so slightly, and she would peek out, so subtly that only her nose and eyes were visible. She would survey the area, those pained and tired eyes sweeping from one end of the street to the other then back again. After a few minutes of this, she would emerge, a stooped old woman shuffling silently in the dark, all her belongings strapped to her back in thick layers such that she looked like a mushroom. 

She would walk up or down the street and vanish from sight until several minutes later she came hobbling back, falling over and scattering old photos and furniture and clothes everywhere. Yelling and pleading as she picked up as much as she could, every time a neighbor came out to assist, she ran off leaving the road filled with broken heirlooms. Back at her house, having barred the doors and windows shut, she would scream until dawn, begging whoever or whatever it was that tormented her, to just let her leave. That all she wanted to do was leave. That year, we did not go on our family vacation, we did not visit grandma and grandpa, we did not go on any school trips. I did not realize it at the time, but I could not leave either.

One day, my parents woke me up early at dawn, teary eyed and heavy hearted. 

"Miss Rosebud is dead" they said letting the turbulent news settle.

"The funeral is in a few hours Virgil, you need to get dressed."

Two hours later I walked into the town parish in my ill-fitting suit, a few sizes too small. The whole town was there in that great ominous domed church, dressed in black and dead silent. The chaplain stood at the pulpit looking over a platform beneath him, on which lay an austere coffin. It was from this coffin that the only sound in the church emanated. It rattled ceaselessly as everyone looked on unbothered, like a nest of crows saying grace over a wriggling worm. The chaplain spoke of her service to the community, her kindness and pure heartedness, and the cruel disease that ravaged her in her final year, all the while sobs and grunts and whimpers escaped the coffin and echoed through the great church hall. 

At last we got the chance to view the body, I was equal parts terrified and curious, but it appeared that nobody that passed by seemed to note anything remarkable by the muted, respectful expressions on their faces. When it was my turn, I had to tiptoe to look into the still rattling casket, and I nearly fell over at the sight of the bound and gagged old woman, strapped to the floorboard of the coffin. Deathly pale, emaciated like a corpse and wasting away but alive in every other way. She stopped struggling as we locked eyes, she shook her head while staring into my soul one last time then I was pushed along and that was the last I saw of her.

As the casket was lowered into the grave later in the day, she seems to have managed to bite through her gag. She begged for forgiveness, for mercy, for her life.. and at last, she begged to die. She yelled and pleaded so loudly that the first few clumps of soil shoveled into the grave leapt off the casket, but nobody seemed to hear a thing. 

A month later, "Miss Rosebud" returned. Smiling, laughing and singing as she skipped through town. Nobody seemed bothered by the fact that she had supposedly died and been buried a month prior, and so I did not ask. It has been 13 years since that day. I have never left my hometown, and I never plan to try. I believe I am the only one left.
