*\[Michael O. was\] A skeleton encased in loose, faded skin. Whose hair separated in blocks at every twitch of the neck. Eyes of a sickening yellow akin to jaundice, though with a slight blue undertone. His lips were split as if by teeth.*

\-*Foxglove Hill Newsletter 15 Feb 2026, on the recovery of a missing person from the Foxglove Ridge Winery.*

Every day since my last delivery, I constantly checked my phone. I knew the object would kill. Yet, no news had ever come.

I had taken to spending any free time in Hilltop Museum rather than in my housing. I was around people there, and distanced from the sickening judgement of my walls. Some of the staff even asked me how I was. Such simple, meaningless gestures filled my heart and warmed my bones.

I had stopped trying to ask about the museum since I failed to transport Sherry. Curiosity was a lower power than fear. I did keep walking by her division in hopes she would return. Instead, I felt an increasingly strong aura of suspicion from the employees of the exports department. Was it so bad for me to be near them? Regardless, I had not received an order for a week after my last delivery─in contrast to my previous daily orders.

While one would expect this to be a good thing, it instead built a silent dread. It was likely they were taking so much time to prepare my next order out of caution. Unfortunately, I was right.

The Representative had found me and guided me to its office. It sat at its desk and waved me over. The chair was comfortable─until it locked my wrists and ankles in place with rusty shackles. The door slammed behind me. I heard locks engage. I didn't bother struggling, in spite of my anxiety. The Representative leaned forward. I swore its hands were distorted and its eyes were too bright.

"You have been spending a lot of time near staff." Its voice was never normal, but its tone then made me question if it was even speaking.

"I didn't think that was against my contract." My mouth responded before I could.

"It is not, but it is possible you are doing so to learn what you are not allowed to." It was like there was an old radio stuck in its throat.

"It... helps me. To be around others." I couldn't recall the last time I told someone this.

"Leave it alone. Your next order is an escort, regardless." A tinge of excitement broke the fear. "The difference is you must go to the location and bring them back to the museum." That should have meant this person was not a horrible product of this place.

I was wrong.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Foxglove Ridge was known to be a slum. As my tires struggled against the rural marshlands, I noticed how dilapidated all of the buildings were. Rotting wood and leaky walls. The market and drugstore had sinking roofs. None of this ever made it to newspapers. The only thing concerning Foxglove Ridge I had ever seen was about this poor soul who was trapped in the winery by a murderous owner.

Once I reached the coordinates provided, I was hardly surprised when I looked at the entrance gate and saw "Foxglove Ridge Winery." It should be under new management. The thought brought little comfort─my arms and legs trembled all the same. Upon stepping out of the car, I felt a weight drop onto my body that broke my posture. Its appearance was deceptively benign.

A memory crept to my attention: my first passenger working here, Caleb M., had said the wine was the only good part about Foxglove Ridge.

I walked inside, disobeying my survival instinct. An old woman greeted me. I did notice that one of her hands had seven fingers and the other only had three─I repressed it.

"I'm the escort from Hilltop Museum. I was ordered to drive someone from here back to the museum?" The tremor in my voice betrayed my false confidence.

"Ah, yes. It's being moved into your car now."

"How did you know I was here?" A familiar feeling rose up the back of my throat.

"Cameras. Since the previous owner unknowingly created a folktale, I've had too many bored kids sneaking around." The logic and normalcy of her response relaxed the tension in my body.

"Wait, didn't you say 'it' was being moved?" I studied her face for a reaction, to no avail.

"Yes, the passenger." Questions crowded my throat. Right when they neared my mouth, my chest ached as it had after it was crushed by the boot of the museum. "It's ready. Thank you, sir." She ushered me outside.

I decided to get into the habit of not looking into the car from the outside. When I sat in the driver's seat and started the car, I steeled myself and looked into the rear view mirror.

I understood why the winery manager said "it."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

I didn't want to be the first to speak. I pulled out of the winery and began the drive in silence. My eyes were distracted. I was certain it was dead, until its bruised eyes met mine. Each motion of its neck shook the grey skin flaps encasing its skeleton. The numerous fissures bled as they were stretched. 

I could see the tendons in its neck struggle to carry the weight of its skin. When it spoke, its voice was dampened by bloody mucus that it hacked up.

"What do I smell like?" My bond to this monster was different from the bond to the Representative or the passenger with rusty lips. It was not dead or absent. Instead of a threat or a chain, it was akin to a thin guitar string─cutting the fingers and leaving calluses. How many times were its fingers cut? Regardless, I focused my nose.

"Apples." His expressions were hidden by the drooping skin of his scalp and cheeks.

"Can you be more specific?" 

"Sour apples. It reminds me of green apple candy, almost." The passenger sighed and hacked again.

"Wine." The word it struggled to speak made my gut tense. It drew attention to its tongue and teeth, both with parts missing. 

"What is your name, sir?" I didn't want to call him "it" anymore.

"Orlie." The words leaving his mouth were muddled. The string tethering us to one another contracted.

"I'm Michael. I haven't heard of an Orlie yet. Are you from here?" His breaths were struggling against the weight of his skin, his lungs could barely exert enough force.

"Y..e..s." Sweat rolled from my forehead onto my creased brow. The corners of my lips pulled down, much like his skin flaps. Orlie was not a monster meant to kill. He was already killed by one.

When we arrived, a new employee that I had yet to see was waiting. He was like the Representative─indescribable. Space warped around him, or at least warped my vision. The tension in my eyes dredged tears up. Orlie struggled to push the door open. I began to open my door to help, but the new man stopped me. 

I would guess that it took Orlie three minutes. As soon as he exited, three staff members in uniforms I did not recognize took him away. The back of their vests, however, did say "Hunting Department."

The odd man walked behind them. His fingers were far too long.  

The Representative met me as I exited the car. 

"You did not ask too many questions." I nodded. "Tomorrow's passenger and cargo will tempt you more. Do not draw the Hunter's ire."
