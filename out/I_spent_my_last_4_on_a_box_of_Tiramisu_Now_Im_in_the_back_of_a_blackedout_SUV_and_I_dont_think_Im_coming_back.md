The heavy glass doors of the hospital swung open, and a sharp, biting wind rushed in to greet me. Outside, the world felt like it was in a state of decay. Pale, yellow leaves surrendered to the pavement, dying quietly under the grey, overcast sky. The chill brushed against my knuckles, a cold reminder that time was freezing over.

The doctor’s ultimatum was still echoing in my skull like a repetitive tolling bell: “One week, Liv. One week before the shadows become permanent. One week before she loses her sight forever.”

I pulled my coat tighter, my fingers brushing against the crumpled $10 bill in my pocket—the last of my strength. As I reached the corner, the faint, roasted aroma of sugar drew my eyes to a small, weathered bakery. It hit me then—it had been months since Grandma had tasted Tiramisu. It was her only vice, her only escape from the sterile walls of the nursing home.

I looked at the sweat-dampened bill. $10. After the hospital's hidden consultation fees, it was all I had for the week. With a trembling hand, I traded $6 for a small white box. As the baker handed me the change, the weight of the coins felt mocking.

I was left with exactly $4.00.

Back at the nursing home, the scent of bitter cocoa and sweet cream pierced through the medicinal smell of the room. As I guided a spoonful of the dessert to my grandmother’s lips, a fragile, ghost-like smile flickered across her face.

"How is it, Grandma?" I whispered, my throat tight with a swallowed sob.

"It’s sweet, my child... just like your words," she murmured, patting my hand.

In the dim light, I truly saw her. I saw the deep canyons that time had carved into her skin. Beneath that mask of age, I caught a glimpse of the vibrant woman she once was. But as I felt the soft, paper-thin skin of her hand, a cold dread settled in my chest. Life was slipping through those wrinkled fingers like sand. My only wish was that her final memories wouldn't be lived in total darkness.

That wish was the catalyst. It pushed me toward the one door I had always been terrified to open.

That night, in my apartment that smelled of damp wood and termite-eaten dreams, I flipped open my dust-covered laptop. I bypassed the standard web and dived into the murky depths of the Dark Web. On a flickering, encrypted site, I offered the only thing I had left of value: my expertise in massage therapy.

I signed a 'Life-Debt' contract. The terms were chillingly simple—immediate funds for the surgery, but in exchange, I would be transported to an undisclosed location to work until my debt, and its mounting interest, was paid in full.

I didn't even read the fine print. I just pressed 'Enter.'

As soon as the confirmation screen flickered, my phone buzzed. No "Welcome" email. Just a text from an unknown number: "The SUV is outside. Your shift begins now."

I’m writing this from the back seat of a blacked-out Suburban. The driver hasn't looked at me once. My GPS is dead. I have $4 in my pocket and a contract that probably owns my soul.

If anyone is reading this... don't look for the 'Trust-Worth' app. Whatever you do, don't sign the contract.
