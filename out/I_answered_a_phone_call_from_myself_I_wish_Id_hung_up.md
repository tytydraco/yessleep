I got a voicemail from myself telling me to hide the murder weapon.

I don't know if the police will believe any of this. Honestly, I don't expect anyone will.

But if you're reading this after what happened in Conference Room B, at least you'll know I wasn't lying. 

It started three days ago. I was in Chicago for work. Nothing exciting. Quarterly meetings, client presentations, bland yet expensive hotel food. The kind of business trip you forget before your plane even lands.

Around midnight my phone buzzed on the nightstand. Unknown number. I glanced at it, rolled over, and went back to sleep.

When I woke up the next morning, I had one voicemail. I didn't bother listening to it. I figured it was spam. 

That morning, on my way to my first meeting the elevator stopped between the sixth and fifth floors. Nothing dramatic like in the movies. No loud crash or sparks flying from the ceiling. It just stopped.

I sighed, pressed the lobby button again, then the door open button. Nothing.

I hit the emergency call button. A bored woman answered almost immediately. She told me maintenance was on the way.

So I waited.

Hotels have a strange talent for covering every empty wall with advertisements. This elevator had one framed beside the buttons.

"WELCOME TO THE ASHFORD HOTEL

Leave a message for yourself at any time.

Dial #318"

I stared at it for a second. Leave a message for yourself? Some marketing gimmick, I assumed. Maybe guests could record reminders that played through the room phone later. Who knows. 

Also, the wording made me laugh. Not "anytime," but "any time." It sounded less like an advertisement and more like permission. 

I pulled out my phone. Why not?

I dialed #318.

An automated voice answered.

"Please enter the time you wish to call."

"...Seriously?" I entered 11:59 PM.

The automated voice continued.

"Please enter the destination number."

I entered my own cell phone.

"Please record your message after the tone."

I grinned. "If anyone actually hears this...take the stairs."

I hung up before I could laugh at myself.

A few seconds later I remembered the voicemail I'd ignored.

Curious, I opened it.

Unknown Number.

11:59 PM.

I pressed play.

Static crackled for a moment.

Then my own voice.

"If anyone actually hears this...take the stairs."

Every hair on my arms stood up. I listened again. Same voice. Same words. Exactly the way I'd just said them. I checked the timestamp. 11:59 PM. Last night. Not two minutes ago.

The elevator lurched back to life. The sudden movement almost knocked me over. The doors opened into the lobby like nothing had happened.

I stood there for several seconds before convincing myself there had to be some explanation. Delayed voicemail. Phone glitch. Someone spoofing my number. There had to be one. There always is. Right?

Anyway, I spent the entire day in meetings.

By dinner I'd almost forgotten about it.

Almost.

I was walking toward the hotel bar when my phone rang. Unknown number. The same one.

My stomach tightened.

"...Hello?"

Silence.

Then my own voice again. This time it’s slurred. "You...don't have too many drunks...or you'll get drinked."

The line disconnected. I stared at my phone. It was me. Drunk enough to barely form sentences.

I looked through the glass doors into the hotel bar. My coworkers waved me over.

"There he is!"

"You buying the next round?"

I almost turned around and went back upstairs. Instead I walked inside. Maybe hearing myself drunk would make sense after a couple drinks. It didn't. 

I ordered two beers and forced myself to drink them. When everyone started ordering shots, I stood.

"I'm calling it."

The table erupted with boos.

"You just got here!"

"You've gotten boring!"

"You seriously flew to Chicago to go to bed?"

I smiled.

"I've got an early meeting."

They laughed.

I left.

By ten-thirty I was asleep.

No hangover. No embarrassment. No drunken voicemail.

As I lay in bed, one thought kept returning.

If those calls really were from me, then I'd just prevented something. That meant, maybe I could use it. Maybe I could make something happen.

The next evening I had an idea. 

I checked the baseball scores. Red Sox 7, Cubs 3.

I dialed #318.

The recording answered immediately.

This time I didn't hesitate. I selected tomorrow morning. Entered my own number. After the tone I said,

"Bet the Red Sox to win seven to three."

Before I could put my phone down it rang.

Unknown number. I answered immediately. My voice spoke without hesitation.

"Yankees over the Angels. Five-four."

That was it.

The next morning I placed the bet.

That night the Yankees beat the Angels. Five to four.

I made more money in one game than I'd made all week. I finally understood. The messages weren't predicting the future. They were completing it. Future me had already lived through the game. He called me. Now I'd become that future version by sending the Red Sox result backward.

A perfect loop. No paradox. No changing history. Just information moving through time.

I laughed the entire walk back to the hotel.

This was incredible. Tomorrow's lottery numbers. Stock prices. Sports. Anything.

I reached for my phone. Might as well leave another message.

The automated voice interrupted before I could record.

"You have exceeded your daily message limit."

Daily limit?

I frowned.

"Please try again tomorrow."

One message a day. Fine. Honestly, that was probably enough to become rich.

The following morning was my last day in Chicago.

My phone rang before my alarm. Unknown number. I answered immediately. I expected another score. Maybe another winning bet.

Instead I heard myself whispering. Breathing hard. Like I was hiding.

"Hide the knife."

Nothing else. Then the call ended.

I sat frozen on the edge of the bed.

What knife? I don't own a knife.

I almost laughed. This one had to be wrong. Maybe future me was joking. Maybe… I don’t know. 

But it couldn’t be. Every previous message had been real.

I spent the entire morning distracted. 

The presentation wasn't until ten. By nine-thirty I was sitting alone in the conference room waiting for everyone else. I tried convincing myself everything would be fine. Then I opened my briefcase to grab my laptop.

There was a kitchen knife inside. Its blade was covered in drying blood. 

I slammed the briefcase shut so hard the room echoed. Someone outside asked if everything was okay. I couldn't answer. Because for the first time I wasn't wondering whether the messages were real anymore.

I was wondering whose blood was on the knife, and did I put it there.
