My contract ended a few days ago. Twenty eight  days in saturation as a three-man team, working a deep water rig in the North Sea.

I’ve done routine repairs for a few years now. The physical toll of nonstop maintenance work, however, has narrowed down what I can still do. For reference, I’m twenty nine.

For this contract I signed on as a bellman.

My job is to monitor lifelines from inside the diving bell while the guys do their work. I make sure the umbilical tethers, gas, and safety conditions are good so the workers can go home to their families. I do this sitting down, in a damp, cold box, for 12 hours at a time.

We breathe heliox, which is a mixture of helium and oxygen, since regular air becomes toxic at this depth.

Breakfast came through at 0600. Scrambled eggs, a couple of sausages, and coffee. Not Michelin star by any means, but it comes fully cooked, through a med lock three times a day. The mixture of heliox in the bell makes it taste pretty bland. Coffee doesn’t fare better either. Still, whatever we can get that even slightly distracts from our situation is always a plus.

I get a lot of questions about what it’s like when you’re further down than a high rise is tall. What types of weird stuff I’ve seen or heard, how isolating or maddening it must be to be so far away from civilization.

The reality is that it’s painfully boring.

Most of the time I’m listening to comms and feeding umbilical line as the crew needs it, as well as monitoring their heliox levels. I rarely look up from my monitor or my hands the entire shift.

There is one tiny port near my station, which allows me to see the breathtaking sight of pitch black water. Along with the occasional flicker of a weird ass fish swimming in front of the floodlight.

One thing you’d think would get tiresome is everyone around you talking like chipmunks. Never gets old. Breathe in helium and say every curse word you know, then recover from laughter. Really, the hardest part is getting along with everyone in such a confined space for such an extended period of time. Luckily everyone I’ve met at this job site has been the most confident, easy going person I’ve had the pleasure to work with.

You’d think you’d get some sort of cabin fever being holed up in the dark for a month, but when you have a group of divers depending on you, you learn to squash any beef as quickly as it comes up.

Harrison is a bit like me. Introverted. Keeps to himself mostly, but is on top of his stuff. He’s in his early 40s. We can generally get along even though we don’t have a lot in common aside from sharing the same toilet.

Some people are a bit challenging to work with though. Specifically this one welder.

We’ll call him J. He’s an older guy, about 48.

J has his own way of doing things. Despite being the oldest on the team he carries himself like that doesn’t matter. Just endless amounts of energy. Even after my third cup of coffee I still need a good hour and a half to feel somewhat lucid enough to give a shit, and I’m 32.

Not him. I swear I’ve seen him sleep 30 minutes at most at the end of a shift. Every time I wake up, no matter what time it is, he’s either already been up, or hasn’t slept to begin with. Since I’m directly responsible for every diver that leaves the bell it becomes an issue when I only know what he’s telling me.

He does his job though, very efficiently might I add. Hasn’t really done anything unsafe to give me any reason to tell the topside supervisor about it.

With everyone else, I can shoot the shit. Talk about whatever stupid video our kid made us watch before we left, trade stories about what got us into this, whatever. J is either working, or he’s not. He does not socialize. The one time I asked him what he gets up to while the rest of us are asleep he just told me:

*“Dolphins.”*

“What like, the animal?”

*“Yeah.”*

“They swim this far down?”

*“Sometimes, if it’s worth it for them.”*

“So what, you just stay up and watch?”

*“No.”*

“Alright then.”

Riveting conversation.

None of the other guys seem to pay him much attention though. When you’re constantly exhausted the only things on your mind are sleeping and eating, with a little bit of mental energy set aside to make sure you remember the bathroom exists.

Still, just being a bit of a weirdo doesn’t necessarily mean anything bad. We all decided to make our living at the bottom of the ocean, so go figure.

What concerns me is his indifference. Nothing about what we do fazes him, and if I can’t tell what’s wrong that means he’s deliberately not telling me.

Even the toughest SOBs I’ve ever worked with had something to complain about, and if they didn’t say it outright I can usually tell and drag it out of them. J always looks the exact same. From the moment he goes out to weld to the moment he comes back.

Sometimes I’ll pull up his helmet feed just to see what he’s up to.

“Everything good, J? How much longer you think on that support beam?” I say.

*“Thirty five and a half minutes.”*

“You timing yourself or something?”

*“Not exactly.”*

“….Okay, well — Topside is asking for a sitrep.”

*“Second support beam should be done at exactly thirteen hundred. Visibility is minimal. Single cetacean is circling around my weld.”*

“A what? Tilt your helmet up I wanna see.”

*“Bottlenose swam right up to me.”*

The camera lifts up to reveal…nothing.

“You know, the word dolphin still exists. Anyway I think you just saw a grouper. You see where it went?”

He stays silent. His helmet light illuminating only the support beam and a bit of the sea floor, with the pitch black stretching to fill beyond it.

He goes back to welding.

*“Bell, dive control. I say again:* ***status check***, all divers, how copy?”

I answer back.

“My bad dive control. Bell here. Diver one is now on the second support beam. ETA thirteen hundred for completion. Diver two is finishing up bolt replacements at the manifold flange. No issues. Gas readings nominal.”

*“Copy bell. You see that dolphin on J’s feed? Never seen one follow this far down.”*

“Repeat your last?”

*“Dolphin. Y’know, like Flipper. Actually, that show might be before your time.”*

“Where at? I’m not seeing anything on my end.”

*“Nevermind dive bell, disregard.”*

“Ten-four,” I say.

The next 30 minutes go by without a hitch. I feed the line, monitor the gas, check both J and Harrison’s camera feeds. No sign of that dolphin. A few small fish and an isopod skittered around but that’s about it.

J is finishing up the rest of his weld. I switch the camera to check on Harrison and the surrounding area of the dive bell. Lots of nothing. Miles upon miles of nothing.

I look down at my hands. I’m feeding line and winding up any slack, when I realize that J has stopped welding.

I feel four strong tugs on the cable. The reel hasn’t changed. Same feed, same tension. I pull up J’s feed. Still at the weld.

“J, dive bell. Status check?”

*“Dive bell, J. You’re cutting out. Say again?”*

“Status check. You good?”

*“Good here. Should be coming home in two-zero.”*

I watch him on the cam. Weld is sparking. Slag dropping onto the silt.

“Copy. You didn’t signal four just now?”

*“Say again?”*

“You didn’t pull four times on your line.”

*“No.”*

On comms J calls topside to break the welding circuit.

*“Topside, J. Open the knife switch and kill power.”*

**“Copy that, J. You’re cold.”**

His hand comes up into the cam. He clips the electrode holder to his harness. The cam swings, and I briefly lose him. It settles and he’s stowing the rest of the rods in his quiver. The cam tilts down and his glove comes up and runs along the weld.

*“Dive bell, this is J, heading home.”*

“Copy that. Winding cable back now.”

The camera shakes a little as he starts walking. The feed shows shrimp scattering in front of his feet, schools of minnows darting sideways past the helmet light. He passes the first rig support. The glow at the base throws shadows up the column while his camera adjusts.

All I can hear now are footsteps and breathing. One step after the other. I check the clock. He’s been walking for about forty five minutes. Passing the same line of support beams. In front of him, the schools of minnows and tuna crabs are gone. Aside from the silt his boots are kicking up, nothing.

As the final support beam disappears from the camera, J steps toward the diving bell. Its floodlights are just faint white pixels slowly creeping into the foreground.

“J, dive bell, status check. How copy?”

No answer.

“J, **status check.** How copy?”

Still nothing.

He’s still walking. Sediment kicking up around his feet and the lights getting closer with each step.

“J. I say again, status check.”

“Topside, dive bell. Loss of comms with J. Can you raise him?”

**“Roger that diving bell. J, status check how copy?”**

Right as topside says that he stops and turns the camera to face an empty patch of the water column.

He hangs there for about a minute before finally responding.

*“Topside, J. Back on you.”*

**“Copy that. Have you been receiving comms from dive bell?”**

*“Yes.”*

I interject before they can respond to him.

“Topside, this is dive bell. Note for the log. On my end I count three unanswered status checks.”

“Ten-four dive bell.”

“J, dive control. Radio check, how copy?”

*“Dive control, J. Loud and clear.”*

“Dive bell, J. Radio check, how copy?”

“J, dive bell. Loud and clear.”

Harrison is seating the last replacement bolt of twelve at the manifold flange. Both gas panels read nominal. Each diver kicking up their own separate silt clouds.

“Dive bell, this is topside. Prep hydration break for J.”

“Copy that topside.”

J reaches the diving bell and climbs into the trunking; the airlock between the bell and the water.

He got stuck with the only helmet without a drinking port, so I have to hang out with him in the interim for the next fifteen minutes.

We share barely a cursory glance once his helmet comes off. Topside is monitoring gas levels for Harrison. The chatter of their back and forth helps make the next ten minutes of silence a bit easier.

Cell service is nonexistent. Luckily my obsession with long form video essays and audiobooks has kept me company. For J, he’s content with drinking from the camelbak and staring directly ahead. In the middle of the comms transmissions I hear the faintest attempt of getting my attention through my headphones. J’s voice barely carries, even in such a tight space.

*“He is calling.”*

I pop one ear of my headphones off.

“What?”

*“A pod of four.”*

Back to staring at the wall. Headphones on.

The rest of the hydration break passes. J dons his helmet, and climbs down from the trunking and into the dark.

*“Dive bell, Harrison. Walking it back home now, how copy?”*

“Ten-four, H. Got your feed front and center. ETA?”

“Uh, probably about fifteen, give or take.”

“Copy.”

His lights come up on the feed exactly fifteen minutes later. A small cloud of tuna crabs scurries past, darts backwards, and vanishes.

My shoulders relax. Harrison climbs into the trunking. We give each other a quick nod and I hand over the camelbak. The cable on J’s reel is paying out further than it was at the first beam. Twenty five more feet on the gauge. Almost halfway to the other worksite.

On the cam, his helmet light attempts to cut through a silt cloud just a few feet in front of him. It catches the reflective glint of a tail fluke pushing the sediment around in a swirl.

The view pivots to follow the direction of the shape. One step after the other. His breathing quickens. The cloud and the sea hang still where it was, and within that stillness J stands too.

Two or three minutes pass and my monitor doesn’t change. Three minutes. Cable is starting to run out.

J is now one hundred and fifty feet away from the worksite, standing in open water. The sound of his breathing now absent. Me and Harrison are both watching the feed now. Topside raises me for a status check.

**“Bell, topside. Status check all divers, how copy?”**

“Topside, bell. Harrison is getting ready to head back out. Standby for J.”

“Copy.”

There’s zero movement. No idle camera sway or shake. At first I check each connection. Outside bell camera is fine. Second camera shows me sitting and Harrison in the trunking. The feed is not frozen. Little particles of sediment float past and I can see that the tuna crabs have returned.

“J, dive bell. Status check, do you read?”

Silence.

“Topside, can you check comms connection for all parties?”

**“Bell I’m seeing no issues here. Are you getting any audio feedback on J’s end?”**

“Negative aside from background noise. Standby.”

“Copy.”

Gripping the line with my right hand, and bracing the reel with my left, I give it two strong pulls. The line goes slack and kicks up sand that blocks the feed almost immediately.

The view clears enough for me to see the same as before. No movement or sound from J. I give the line four pulls. Resistance at the end of the line turns into an intense vibration, as if an electric current was flowing through it.

“J, status check. How copy?”

I wind the cable onto the reel. The gears tick with each crank of the handle. More dirt swirling blankets the beam of the headlamp. Still no answer.

Topside radios back.

“Dive bell, can you confirm the status of your first diver?”

“Negative. Diver one is unresponsive to both hand and audio communication. Am I clear to lockout for investigation?”

**“Affirmative but make sure you’re covered. Switch with Harrison and have him double check comms.”**

“Copy that. Getting ready now.”

He takes off his gear and sits in my chair. I put on my suit and helmet and clip the cable to my back, slip through the trunking and into the dark.

J’s cable runs parallel to the dive bell on the sea floor. I bend down to pick it up. It stretches out toward the mass of columns and pipes that fade into the dark past the edge of the rig. Visibility is minimal but I can see just barely beyond the first row of support beams.

Dive control and Harrison are monitoring. One foot after the other. I run my hand along the length of the cable, slowly closing the distance a few steps at a time. It’s been fifteen minutes since my walk began. My footing is very unsteady. Every step I take almost ends my walk before it’s even started. Thirty more feet left to go. Topside radios in a status check. Twenty five feet left now.

As I’m pulling on the cable I can feel weight dragging at the end with every step. The tuna crabs and minnow schools dart past my helmet giving me a second to pause. J’s silhouette is just up ahead, no less than fifteen feet from me. One foot after the other.

Comms are silent. Only my own breathing fills the helmet. Coming up on J now. He’s standing motionless, bent down as if trying to find something dropped on the sea floor. His right hand up and shielding his visor from something I can’t see.

“Topside, dive bell, coming up J’s position now, how copy?”

**“Loud and clear.”**

No movement or any sign of life. Just standing motionless. I wave a hand in front of his face. Nothing. With the tether in my other hand, I pull four times. A slight deviation from what my hands felt on the cable a few minutes ago, but still nothing from him. The resistance is much, much lighter.

His shoulder feels spongy. Like memory foam. I watch my hand print leave an impression, then spring back into place and disappear.

On his front, the inside of the helmet is pitch black until my light hits the visor. Small flashes of scales and millimeter sized fins drift inside the glass.

Topside is trying to get a sitrep from me.

“Unresponsive.” I radio back.

That was a few days ago. As I’m writing this there have been a few attempts to send an unmanned ROV down there to recover what I’m told are his remains. Any time one of them gets even remotely close to that suit it dies.

My company is chalking my dive up to mismanagement and a mix of nitrogen narcosis. They’re medically retiring me effective immediately. Memory loss and psychogenic absent seizures.

All I want to do is look at the dolphins.
