I’ve been on dozens of road trips across North America. Neither of my parents likes to fly, but they love to travel, so they load up the Subaru and drag me up to Maine, or down to the swamps of the Deep South, or out to the Sonoran Desert, or anywhere else on this wide continent. At 15 years old, I’ve been to 42 states, Canada, and Mexico. We live in Kansas, so we’re in the center of everything. Sometimes, we’re visiting family. Sometimes we’re going to National Parks. Sometimes I think my father just picks a random spot on the map and decides to drive there, hoping for an adventure. I don’t mind it. My hometown is small and boring, the kind of place where people either move to the big city after high school or they fall down a long, dirty spiral of meth addiction, criminality, and despair. 

In my short life, I’ve grown to love traveling. The open road fills me with wonder. I can’t help but imagine what it would be like to live somewhere else, from the red-rock beauty of Sedona, Arizona, to the cozy, alpine ambiance of Helen, Georgia. My parents wanted me to see a whole world of possibilities from an early age. And I lapped it up. I’ve always wanted to be a truck driver when I grew up, someone out roaming the countryside. Never tied down. Forever free. A rolling stone that gathers no moss. But things changed recently. My travels have taken a very dark turn. And it all began at a rest stop.

I’ve probably visited more rest stops in my short life than most adults. I’ve seen every kind, from elaborate state-sponsored welcome centers to sprawling truck stations with diners and convenience stores, to the basic, unmanned ones that pepper all the highways and byways of this strange land. My story begins two years ago at one such small, unassuming rest stop nestled in the Appalachian Foothills. We’d been on the road for four hours straight, and my bladder was about to burst, so we made a short pee break at a rest stop. After relieving myself, I decided to stretch my legs and take a walk around the heavily wooded property. The endless forests surrounding the area were dark and vine-choked. It was the dead of Summer, the air as thick as molasses and choked with gnats and mosquitoes. I imagined what it would be like to live in such an impenetrable wilderness. Supposedly, there were still some moonshiners deep in the mountains, living off-grid. That’s when I got the strangest feeling, almost like deja vu. The hairs on the back of my neck shot up. And then, I turned around and saw him for the first time. 

The boy looked a lot like me. He was short, pale, and freckly with light brown curls atop his head. He was a few years younger and wore a cool T-shirt with a Velociraptor on it, my favorite dinosaur. The design was from the original *Jurassic Park*, which my father showed me when I was way too young, sparking my love of the franchise. 

“Hi,” I said, waving. 

The boy didn’t respond. He just stared at me for an awkward beat before running off towards the parking lot and disappearing behind some cars.

*Huh. Weird kid,* I thought. And that was the end of it. The whole encounter was brief but unremarkable. In fact, I would’ve forgotten all about Velociraptor T-shirt (as I called him) had I not seen him at a different rest stop in Kansas a week later. I spotted the boy while perusing the vending machine for a Dr. Pepper. He was by himself again, standing on the outskirts of the parking lot, staring at the endless cornfields on either side of the highway. He wore the same Velociraptor T-shirt. I started to walk towards him, but my mother called me back to the car, “Dash! Let’s go!” As I ran back to the Subaru (Dr. Pepper-less), I felt that same electric shock on the back of my neck. I didn’t turn around, but I knew what was happening. The boy was watching me again.  
   
—  
   
Summer ended. I went back to school. Hung out with my friends. Joined the soccer team. Played video games. Life went on. I forgot all about Raptor T-shirt. Was it weird that I saw the same boy at two different rest stops, so many states apart? I suppose. But maybe his family had just taken a similar route on a similar road trip. Stranger things have happened. I didn’t dwell on it much. No, that’s a lie. I didn’t dwell on it at all. Not until the holidays came around. That’s when everything changed. 

We traveled to Michigan to visit my grandparents for Thanksgiving. It was blisteringly cold. Snow falling in blinding sheets. On such trips, we limited our breaks to rest stops with large indoor complexes, restaurants, and convenience stores. It was too cold for the small ones with their (mostly) open-air facilities. We visited a mega truck stop on our way up to Ann Arbor. Inside, I passed a big bulletin board. It was stuffed with all sorts of ads and banners (*Visit Sleeping Bear Dunes*, *Buy Penzoil*, *Help Stop Human Trafficking*). But one poster caught my attention above all else–  
 

**MISSING PERSONS**  
 

It took up half the bulletin board and showed dozens of faces. Names. Ages. Height. Weight. Last known location and date of disappearance. I’d seen countless posters like it at other rest stops across the country and barely glanced at them. But this one made me stop dead in my tracks. There was Velociraptor T-shirt, smack dab in the middle of the poster. His name was Michael Winkins, aka “Mikey.” Age 13. Last seen at a rest stop outside Phoenix, AZ, on Oct 13^(th), 2009.

“Holy shit!” I said, moving closer to examine the entry. The poster included an age progression, showing Mikey with wrinkles and thinning hair. *He’s a ghost,* I thought. It was the only possible explanation. *But why would a ghost focus on me? Who or what killed him? And was that same danger headed my way?*

“Dash!” 

I nearly jumped out of my skin. 

Dad grabbed me by the shoulder. “I’ve been looking all over for you. Your mom grabbed us a table at Maggie’s Diner. C’mon.” He had to tear me away from the poster.

“What are you looking at it? The Missing Persons?”

“I was just checking it out,” I said sheepishly.

“Why? You recognize someone on there?” My dad asked.

“No,” I said after a slight pause, hoping my dad didn’t realize my lie.

“Well, if ever you do, just tell me, ok?” Dad said. “I’ll handle it.”

 “Yeah. I know.” My parents were always trying to get me to open up more. They said I spent too much time inside, scrolling Reddit on my phone, or playing Minecraft on the computer. It was another reason why they brought me on so many trips. To get me to meet new people. Try new things. Open up. Truth is, I liked being alone. I daydreamed about running away to live in almost every cool or interesting place we visited on our road trips. And in each fantasy, I was living on my own. I don’t know why I felt this way. I guess I envied the freedom of a lone wolf. Probably because my parents helicoptered over me so much, never letting me spend the night at my friends’ houses, calling every hour to see when I’d be home. Tracking my social media.

“There you are!” My mother stood up from the booth as Dad and I entered Maggie’s diner. She looked worried. “We’d been looking all over for you. You need to be careful at big rest stops like this,” she said. “It’s easy to get lost.”  
   
—  
   
After that day, I’d decided I would approach Mikey if I ever saw him again. If he were a ghost, there had to be a reason I could see him. *Perhaps he wanted me to solve his murder?* Solving a cold case would be awesome. My friends and I all listened to true crime podcasts. We were especially obsessed with any unsolved ones because we could act like detectives. We’d spend hours spinning elaborate theories about potential murderers, alternate causes of death, or people vanishing on purpose. Six months later, I got my wish. My family was on another summer road trip. Mom wanted to visit sunny SoCal, see LA, Disneyland, the San Diego Zoo, and so on. We were on the way there when we took a break at a massive truck stop smack-dab in the middle of the Arizona desert. There were so many big rigs around the place that you couldn’t even see the facilities until you were right on top of them. My dad gassed up the Subaru while I washed the car with one of those squeegee things, scraping 500 miles worth of bug guts off the windshield, front grill, and bumper. Afterward, I told him I was gonna hit the restrooms.

“Ok, but be back quick,” my Dad said. “We’re losing daylight, and we wanna be in Phoenix before nightfall.”

I nodded and kept going. The rest stop was huge and packed with travelers, mostly big, greasy truckers or harried parents, struggling to keep their toddlers in check. There were multiple fast food restaurants, a convenience store, and plenty of restrooms. They even had ones with showers. As I left the men’s room, I saw Mikey standing by some floor-to-ceiling windows, staring at the endless desert beyond. 

“Mikey?” I said, as if he were some old friend.

The boy didn’t respond. 

I stepped closer. Close enough to reach out and touch him–

Mikey spun around to see me, surprised. I got that tingly feeling on the back of my neck again.

“Do you need help?” I asked.

Mikey opened his mouth, but stopped himself. He looked frightened and perhaps a little confused. Then, he muttered two words in a hoarse voice, like he hadn’t spoken in years: “Go away.”

“What?”

The boy didn’t respond. Mikey ran outside the building.  

“Hey.” I chased after him.

“Don’t follow me,” Mikey called out as he sprinted through a maze of big rigs parked on the sprawling lot. The boy weaved through them like he was following a secret trail only he knew. He moved around objects like he was real, ducking beneath the truck trailers, squeezing between parked SUVs. But no one seemed to notice him. No one except me.

“Why are you following me?” I asked, still chasing Mikey. “You keep showing up at these rest stops.” I started to fear I was the victim of some elaborate prank. 

Mikey didn’t respond. He just kept running, trying to lose me. But his short strides were no match for my long legs. I finally caught up with the boy amid a series of boulders on an island in the parking lot. Someone had set up a small camp in the area. There was a tattered tent, an old folding chair, and a discarded cooler. The campsite was hidden from the rest stop, a secret canyon amid all the chaos of travelers. I grabbed my knees, sucking in air. Ghost or not, this boy was fast.

“What do you want?” Mikey asked.

“What do I want? What do you want?” I asked. “You’re the one who keeps showing up wherever I go.”

“I don’t mean to,” Mikey said. *What the Hell does that mean,* I thought. “Are you a  
ghost?” I asked. “I saw your missing persons’ poster.”

“Not quite,” Mikey said. “Look. I can’t explain right now. You should leave.” 

*Was I dreaming? Was this a heat-induced hallucination?* “Why do I need to leave?” I asked.

“Because it’s not safe around me,” Mikey said. “You should be with your parents.”

“No one out there saw you except for me,” I said, trying to wrap my head around the situation.

“Just drop it,” Mikey said. “Go back to your Subaru. Continue on your trip to LA. Forget you ever saw me.”

“Wait. How do you know that?” I said. “You are following me!”

Mikey pushed me back. “I said LEAVE!” 

I almost toppled over, catching myself on a nearby boulder. *I guess the boy wasn’t a ghost after all.* “Are you spying on me?”

“I’m…” Mikey’s eyes suddenly bulged. He stared at something behind me. I felt cold. Icy cold. Then a hand clamped over my face, muffling my screams for help. The hand was massive, covered in a rubber glove that smelled of dirt and engine oil. I never saw its owner. I didn’t see anything after that… Darkness.

—  
   
When I awoke, I was lying in the back seat of a sedan. I think it was a Volvo. The first thing I saw was him, sitting behind the wheel.  

“I’m sorry,” Mikey said. “I tried to warn you.” 

I shot up, adrenaline pumping. “Wha–Did you kidnap…” I didn’t wait for an answer. I just opened the side door, which was left unlocked. It was nighttime outside. A full moon illuminated the desert landscape. “MOM! DAD!” I cried out, running through the parking lot. 

There were dozens of police cars parked at the rest stop. But my Subaru was gone. “They left me?” I said aloud. “They LEFT ME?” 

I ran to the nearest officer, a burly man who was checking out the vending machines. “Hey. I’ve been kidnapped. There’s this boy, and someone grabbed me and…”

The cop didn’t so much as glance in my direction, no matter how loud I spoke. I tried grabbing his arm, but my hand just passed right *through* him. The officer continued speaking with his fellow cops about a missing boy named Dashiell Bowman.  

“Dashiell? Hey! That’s me!” I said, jumping up and down! 

Still no response from the police. Apparently, there was a state-wide search for me. My family was down the road at the Hopi Motel, where the police had set up a base of operations. I pulled out my cellphone and tried calling my Mom, but the thing was just a glass brick. No power. Nothing. 

“HELP!” I screamed to no avail. 

Since none of the bozo cops were paying attention to me, I decided to run to the Hopi Motel myself and reunite with my parents. But as soon as I got a few feet from down the dusty highway, there was a bright flash—

And I was suddenly running towards another rest stop in a heavily wooded area of the Northeast. A nearby sign proclaimed 10 miles to Burlington. *Burlington?* I’d been before. It was in freaking Vermont. I’d just traveled hundreds of miles in a millisecond. There were a few cars at this new rest stop, some families on a road trip. I yelled at them for attention, but they ignored me just like the cops did. 

“It won’t matter!”

I spun around. 

Mikey was right behind me. I no longer got that tingly feeling when I saw him. But my mind burned with rage. “What the Hell did you do?” I shoved him backwards, just like he did in Arizona. 

Mikey fell flat on his ass. “I tried to warn you,” he said, teary-eyed.  

“What the Hell is this? What’s happening?”

“You’re stuck here,” Mikey said, wiping his nose. “With me.”

“What do you mean, stuck? At this rest stop?” 

“At all rest stops,” Mikey said. “I’ve been here since ‘09. Sometimes with other kids. Most of the time, by myself. I don’t know who came up with the name, but it’s older than my time here. We call this place ‘The Waiting.’”

“The Waiting?”

“Yeah. You can only visit rest stops. Any time you try to leave one, you’ll just end up at another one somewhere else in the country. Sometimes, it’s the next stop down the road. Sometimes, it’s clear across the country, like here.” 

I took a deep breath, hoping beyond all hope this was just some horrible nightmare. I pinched myself. Slapped my face. But I didn’t wake up. Then, I asked the question that had been burning a hole in my brain for the past fifteen minutes. “Am I dead?” 

“I don’t think so,” Mikey said.  

“'Cause with a name like ‘The Waiting’ it sure sounds like I’m dead. Waiting for the great beyond,” I said. 

“It’s not as simple as being a ghost,” Mikey said. “Here. Let me show you.” He brought me to the rest stop building where a series of vending machines lay. The boy pressed a few buttons, and an ice-cold Coca-Cola tumbled into the collection bucket. Mikey grabbed it and twisted the top. Fizz. He sipped some of the Coke, then offered it to me. “Try it. You can eat and drink anything you want. No one ever seems to notice when we take something.” 

I took the Coke and made a tentative sip. It tasted just like the 20,000, or so I’d had before. “Did you pay—?”

“Nope. It’s all free,” Mikey said, grinning. “All you can eat. All you can drink.” 

I gave the Coke back to him. “So we can still interact with this world, just not any people?” 

“Yep,” Mikey said. “If the weather’s bad at one rest stop, you just go to another where it’s better. You can eat and drink whatever. There’s always a working restroom nearby.  You never grow fat or old. It’s kind of like Neverland.”  

“A fucked-up Neverland,” I said. Then, my eyes fell upon an ancient device I’d never even bothered to notice before. There were very few left in the world, but the fact that one was at this rest stop seemed to be a sign… It was a Pay Phone. I ran over to it and grabbed the receiver. But when I put it to my ear, all I heard was an ear-splitting shriek of static. “Oww!”

“You can’t call anyone,” Mikey said. “Believe me, I’ve tried many times, and this was back when pay phones were everywhere.” He chuckled. 

I hung up the phone, sighing. And then I remembered what had happened the moment before I passed out. That giant rubbery hand that grabbed me from behind. “Mikey?” I asked. “Who put us here?” 

Mikey’s smile vanished in an instant. “Don’t worry about them,” he said. “I won’t let them touch us again.” 

“Them?” 

“The Red Gumbys,” Mikey said. 

“The Red what?”

“Red Gumbys. We called them that because they wear these thick, rubber suits that make them look like Gumby, only red. Blood red,” Mikey said.  

“Is that who grabbed me? A guy in a red, rubber suit?” I shivered. “What did he do?”

“Nothing. They only touched you, but I pulled you away before anything else could happen.” 

"Anything else? What do you mean by anything else?”

Mikey didn’t answer. “You’d already passed out once they touched you, which meant you were now part of this world. But I could still keep us safe. I’ve gotten really good at losing the Red Gumbys over the years. I pulled you into a nearby car and drove from one rest stop to another, then another, and another, and so on, until I was certain they’d given up the chase. By then, I’d ended up right back where I’d started.”

“So that’s why I woke up in the back seat of a car?” I asked. 

“Yeah,” Mikey said. “Driving’s the best way to lose them.” 

“What if they come back?” I asked. 

“We’ll know if they’re coming,” Mikey said. "The air gets really cold, and you see this black tanker trunk. No one notices the truck or the Gumbys, except us. And they’re the only ones who can see us in The Waiting. When you see that truck coming, Dash, run. Don’t stop. Don’t look back. Just get to the next rest stop, as fast as you can.” 

“What will they do if they take us?” 

Mikey didn’t respond. He just stared at the horizon. “Let’s go somewhere down South. Somewhere warmer.”
