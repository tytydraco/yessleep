I had just turned 19, and nothing particularly important was happening in my life at the time. I had a part-time job at a supermarket, I was studying film, and I was enjoying life. I was very aware that youth passes quickly, so I took advantage of every weekend to really live it.

In the middle of all that, an opportunity came up. My dad, who I only saw once a year for Christmas or New Year’s, asked if I wanted to move in with him in San Diego, CA. He had left when I was 10 chasing a job and eventually ended up running his own business, one that was moderately successful. Enough to live comfortably.

My mom had her doubts, and my first instinct was to say yes immediately. I mean, I’d be right next to Los Angeles while studying film. We’re talking about the year 2000, it was a completely different world from the one we live in now. It was a golden opportunity. But accepting it also meant leaving an entire life behind, family, friends, and the security of being in familiar territory, in my comfort zone. I liked my lifestyle. I was having a good time. This opportunity meant change, and there was no way to know exactly what would change.

I told my dad I needed to think about it and asked for a week. I talked it over with friends and, of course, they said, “Dude, get on the plane and get the hell out of here NOW.” Two days later, I called my dad to tell him I accepted, that I’d move in with him. When I told my mom, she started crying, but she gave me a hug that felt like she was saying, "You’re doing the right thing".

Damián and Sofía, two of my best friends, insisted that before I left we had to have one last night out. I didn’t know when I’d be able to visit again, it was a reality, so they organized everything.

It was the night before my flight. The night had three parts. And a quick note for my American friends, nights out in Argentina start at midnight at 12 at the earliest and end at… well, whenever you run out of energy. Nightclubs close at 6 a.m., and people usually stay until the very last minute.

Back to the three parts of the night.

First, a small gathering with close friends at Sofía’s place. She had her own apartment with a big backyard and a grill. The second stage was going to a nightclub, one that was a bit more laid-back compared to the ones we usually went to. It was a bar that turned into a club after 1 a.m. The third part? Having breakfast burgers at Tito’s stand, where all our nights usually ended.

The day arrived. I went to Sofía’s with three packs of beer and two bottles of fernet with their respective Coca-Colas. Damián was in charge of the barbecue, as always, our go-to grill master. Before the rest of the group showed up, the three of us, the original crew, made a toast.

“To the best night of your life,” Sofi said.

“And the last,” I added before clinking glasses.

Little by little, everyone else arrived, all carrying bottles. Francisco showed up with two people we didn’t know. That annoyed me a bit, it was supposed to be just the usual group. But they were already there. What was I supposed to say? It was a guy and a girl. His name was Nicolás, and hers… I can’t remember. Maybe Belén? Honestly, the memory is blurry, probably because of the industrial amounts of alcohol I drank that night.

The guy was quiet but normal. I mean, if you’re at a gathering where everyone knows each other and you’re the only outsider, it can be awkward. Unless you’re as outgoing as the girl, let’s call her Belén, even though I’m not sure that was actually her name.

She had no problem acting like she’d known us forever. The karaoke idea was hers, which resulted in a pathetic performance from me but hysterical laughter from everyone else. I guess Sofi noticed this girl was sticking pretty close to me, because every now and then she’d look at me, smile, and raise her eyebrows, basically signaling that the girl was clearly into me. Subtle hints, or not so subtle in this case, that I usually miss, and Sofi knew that.

Around midnight we started organizing taxis, who was riding with whom and how many people could fit in each car. Forty minutes later we were on our way, sharing a taxi with Damián, Sofi, and this girl Belén, who insisted on riding with us because “we were the most fun.”

We went from noise and laughter to the awkward silence of the taxi ride. Belén broke the ice by asking if I was afraid of flying and whether I had ever been on a plane.

“Oh, Francisco told you I’m leaving, right?”

She shook her head, smiling.

“No, I’m psychic. I know everything about you,” she said, laughing.

We arrived at the bar/club. As soon as we got in, we ordered several drinks and bottles. I was already pretty drunk, but after another hour of drinking like crazy inside, I reached a whole different level. I remember dancing close with Belén, feeling her hand on my back, her face very close to mine… and the way she smiled.

Fuck. What a smile.

After that, mental gaps and time jumps, something that used to happen a lot when I drank. Well… when I used to drink.

I know we went to Tito’s stand, but I didn’t have an appetite. I don’t know if I ate. I remember Sofi telling me the night was over, that they were calling a taxi to take me home. I didn’t want to go to sleep, I wanted to keep going. Or rather… I wanted to finish the night with Belén.

I got into a taxi with her. I clearly remember looking out the window and seeing Sofía’s disapproving expression. She knew I was about to screw up. I was way too drunk to make good decisions.

Not even two minutes later, I looked to the side and saw Belén doing a bump of coke with her apartment key. She dipped the tip back into the little bag and offered it to me. I had tried it before and didn’t like it. It feels incredible, sure, but the last time I did it, I ended up in the ER with twelve stitches in my head after getting into a fight with two guys who obviously beat the hell out of me.

But I was too drunk, and in that state I don’t make the best decisions. So I took a bump too.

Belén took off the leather jacket she’d been wearing all night, and I saw a fairly large tattoo on her shoulder. It looked like some kind of logo, something I’d never seen before.

A dagger, piercing what looked like an eye. All inside a crest, like a soccer club emblem or something. Of course, much darker in style.

I asked her what it was. “It’s the religion I practice,” she said without looking at me.

I was confused. I think the taxi driver was too, judging by the look he gave us in the rearview mirror.

“It’s right here, mid-block,” she said, and the driver slammed the brakes. He wanted nothing more to do with us, the situation was getting weird.

I didn’t even know we were going to her place. I must have agreed happily enough, but I didn’t remember. The coke had sobered me up a bit, but I had drunk too much, so every step felt like walking through a minefield.

We went into her apartment and… it was chaos. Actually, there weren’t many things. A stained mattress, a black-and-white television, and not much else. The walls were eaten away by dampness, and the smell of rotten food turned my stomach. My body was telling me to run… but I stayed.

She started taking off her clothes without saying a word. It wasn’t erotic at all, it felt like a chore, something we had to do rather than something she wanted. That’s when I started noticing scars on her thighs, around her stomach, and her lower back. Bruises too, quite a few.

I asked if she was okay, and she laughed. “Yeah, don’t worry. I fall a lot. I’m clumsy.”

She started undressing me and asked why I didn’t have any scars. I shrugged, it was a strange question.

“I get it. You’re a good boy. I’m not good… I deserved a lot of these,” she said, touching her scars.

By this point I was paranoid, from the coke, from what Belén was saying, from her bruises and scars, and from the wrecked apartment. I could hear my own heartbeat in my head.

She pushed me onto the mattress and climbed on top of me. She started kissing me, and I relaxed a bit. I closed my eyes for a few seconds, trying to focus… to give a decent performance.

I heard her feeling around near the mattress with her hand while kissing me. I assumed she was looking for condoms, and I hoped she was, because I didn’t have any.

She stopped kissing me, and I opened my eyes.

She was running a knife along her neck, then slowly sliding it down her abdomen until it reached mine. I could feel the cold blade on my stomach. She smiled and said:

“So… did you enjoy your last night?”

She raised the knife above her head. It was a split second. I just reacted.

What would you have done?

I punched her in the mouth. She fell sideways, onto her own knife, straight into her abdomen. It didn’t take long for the bleeding to start… and it was a lot.

I didn’t know what to do. I looked at the watch on my wrist. 8 a.m. My flight was in five hours. I didn’t think. I acted. I got dressed and ran out of there.

The drunkenness disappeared instantly. I got home in shock, took a shower, got dressed, grabbed my luggage. Mom woke up, hugged me tight, cried again. This time I cried too, but for different reasons.

We went to the airport, where I met Damián and Sofi. They asked what happened with that girl. I told them I started feeling sick, threw up in the taxi, and she just sent me home. They laughed and believed me.

I asked where she had come from, how Francisco knew her.

“She’s in his band. That punk one that sounds awful,” Damián said.

“Oh yeah, the knife and eye one? He once tried to give me a T-shirt. I didn’t know how to say no,” Sofi added.

Later Sofi told me it was actually a good thing I hadn’t done anything with her. Apparently Francisco had hooked up with her before, and while they were eating at Tito’s after I left, he said she had some really weird fetishes.

My blood pressure dropped. I felt pale. I think they noticed, because they asked if I was okay. I said yes, that it was just nausea and a bad hangover.

But I was far from okay.

I was defending myself, at least that’s what I thought. What was I supposed to think? She had a fucking knife.

Me? I got on that plane and never returned to Buenos Aires. I never called anyone again, never answered any calls except my mom’s, and I never saw her again either. I didn’t even go to her funeral.

I don’t know what happened to that girl. I don’t want to know.

Even though I haven’t had a peaceful night’s sleep since, and guilt has been eating me alive for 25 years.

I can’t go back home.

I don’t know what’s waiting for me there.
