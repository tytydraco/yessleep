It's been nearly a year since I revisited this memory. I apologize for my silence, but it couldn’t be helped. Shortly after I posted the last part, the police arrived, one inspecting the property despite my protests, while the other asked me a series of questions. The conversation between us was brief; I hardly managed to recall the events of the night to them through snotty tears and constant sobs, but strangely enough, I didn’t see any disbelief or understanding across his face, just a blank stare as I choked out the story. Telling me they would investigate further in the morning, he told me to gather at least a week's worth of my things, and we were off to the police station. 

My Pa had grown to be a part of the community, and his best friend, Deputy Noodin, was the one who ran to my side as I arrived at the police station, embracing me and trying to convince me it’ll be ok. I knew it wouldn’t be. I had no mother, no father, and only God knew where the rest of my family was. The deputy knew this, and after taking my statement, he filed the necessary paperwork, and as of now, his trailer has been my home for the past year.

As we drove away from the police station to my new home that night, I could only think of one thing. 

“Deputy, please, you have to find what that was back there, you have to believe me! I wasn’t seeing things, I swear. You know me. You beli-” He cut me off quickly.

“It’s not your fault, kid. I know what you’re thinking: why couldn’t I have scared off the wolf? I could’ve done more, couldn’t I? All of those. I’ve heard them before, and you have to know you were scared, scared as anyone would’ve been. It was a freak incident, thats it.” I couldn’t believe what I was hearing 

“It wasn’t a FUCKING WOLF! I’m not blaming myself either, I NEED ANSWERS, NOT COMFORT.” I roared as he pulled to the side of the road. 

“I know you think you saw something out there, but you didn’t. We already had two of the deputies do a sweep of the area, and they couldn’t find anything.” He said calmly. “You have to believe me, kid, and I know what you went through is raw and painful, and you need some sort of reason, and I assure you we will do our best to find anything, but right now there's nothing. Your father was a great man, and this hurts me too.” 

I cried. A man shouldn’t cry, but I couldn’t hold it in anymore. It hurt so bad. It still hurts now. 

We pulled into his driveway, a gravel road leading to a small trailer dwarfed by the void of midnight. I noticed as we drove up, the countless number of dream catchers, all bearing a similar structure. A small black box suspended by a spiderweb of strings. I hardly took note of it; they were just dreamcatchers to me. Just dreamcatchers. 

Through the next few months, I started to readjust to my life without Pa. It felt empty, and the deputy did his best to amend this, but without answers, nothing helped. I became obsessed with finding the solution for myself. The trail started cold and grew frozen as I went on. I attempted to revisit the farm multiple times, but the deputy insisted that I couldn’t go back. He said it was being cared for by some friends of his, and it was mine as soon as I became an adult. Without even an opportunity to revisit the scene, how could I find an answer? 

I gave up for a while. I listened to the deputy and got a job in town, finally trying to build myself back up with hard work. It was simple enough to get hired at the grocery store, a small building called “Hastings”. After school every day, I would help restock the shelves, then wait in town until the deputies' shift was finished. It was a small, empty shell of a town, with little to do in it, but just outside, there was an array of abandoned buildings that I was a frequent visitor to. 

The buildings consisted of a church, some houses, and one Indian residential school. The deputy had given me a brief history of the school in particular, a school built in the 1930’s to “properly integrate” Native Americans into society. He had an obvious detest for it, but told me it wasn’t worth exploring out of anything. 

I listened to him up until yesterday. 

I got cut early from work, and per the usual, walked the mile out to the row. I was heading to the church, my favorite of the buildings. I had made the choir loft into a comfortable hangout spot and got decent enough service to pirate a couple of movies on while I waited for the deputy to text, letting me know he was done with his shift. 

As I was making my way to the church and walking past the school, I smelled it again. No other scent but the pungent, sickening smell of blood. Vomit instantly swelled up, an acidic splash hitting the ground immediately. It was the same as that night, the exact fucking same. 

I wiped the bile from my lips with my sleeve, trembling. I knew it was more than a risk to go into that place; I would certainly die. I had my phone and a drawstring bag with my work clothes. If it was that… beast again, I didn’t have a clue as to what to do. 

Despite my crushing fear, I started to creep up to the outside of the structure, scanning the grounds for any signs of entry. I couldn’t see any tracks, and the door, which had been boarded shut years ago, was just as it had always been. 

“I should wait for the deputy,” I said, my words being carried away into the empty wind.

But what help would he be? He had denied me the opportunity to investigate anything and thought a wolf had been responsible for Pa’s death. I was still running through my next course of action in my head when I heard it from inside. A long, deep exhale, and then splashing. What the fuck? 

There was a number of busted-out windows from other lonely wanderers, allowing me entrance into the school. I had to find out what had made that noise. As I hoisted myself through the window and turned on my phone's flashlight, I couldn’t help but notice them. Infesting my field of view were hundreds, if not thousands, of dream catchers. They hung from the ceiling, were littered across the floor, nailed to the wall, everywhere. And all of them had a small, black box dead center. 

“Little weird.” I breathed, full of fear but attempting to disguise it to the world. 

They were the same as Noodin’s, but I couldn’t think about that now. I needed to find what made that sound. As I weaved my way through the amalgamation of strings, I found myself in a small classroom, desks piled up in the corner and the wall… missing? Through the darkness, I saw a void of black where the wall should’ve been between the small hallway and what I assumed to be another classroom on the other side. 

I crept slowly up to the edge and shone my flashlight down. The edge sloped and continued on. It appeared to be a large pit, one I was not inclined to enter. I started to gain a footing on the decline, but noticed a series of holes in the walls of the pit. At this point, the smell of blood was polluting my nostrils, so pungent I could taste it. It was coming from the middle of the pit, and I was determined to find its source.

About to start the descent, I suddenly heard a scuffling from one of the holes.

“Shit, shit, shit.” I breathed, clambering back up to the classroom. 

A creature emerged. I could only make it out due to a glow that followed from the hole. It came clambering out, the same leech-like mouth and dead, black eyes. The claws were magnificent, larger than any knife I’ve laid witness to, and it slowly stomped towards the center of the pit. At the entrance of the hole, I saw out of the corner of my eye a hand and a lantern, but I could only attach my gaze to the beast. 

It stopped near the center of the room, and I could finally see the source behind the scent. A large, crimson pool. Before I had time to process this, the beast started. A large, dry heave, deep and penetrating, emitted from its mouth, and a rush of blood followed. From its mouth streamed pure red, entering into the pool with a chunky splash. It expelled from its stomach the lifeblood of some poor victim until it was empty. I had to stifle a mix of vomit and noise that my mouth longed to produce. The creature, finished with its sickly ritual, turned towards the hole and followed the light out. 

I sat there, sick with fear and filled with a sense of dreadful wondering. What had I just borne witness to? I wanted to leave, but before I could, I had to at least see the pool for myself. I slowly crept into the pit once again, listening intently for any sound in the halls, but the air was filled with a terrible silence. I reached the dusty floor and walked across the rock towards the center.  As I grew closer towards the edge, I saw something in the center of the pit, where the pool was. I knew what I would find before the light revealed its form. 

A large, black cube, surrounded by a lake of blood. 

Never have I felt such a sense of dread. I stared at the cube. The cube stared back.

I was outside within a minute, attempting to vomit up the contents of an empty stomach. I don’t know how long I was in that pit, as it was now dark out. It couldn’t have been more than a few hours since the deputy hadn’t been by yet, but it had only felt like a few seconds. I walked along the road, trying to comprehend what I had seen in the school. Wandering back into the church, my eyes locked onto the frail form on the crucifix, and there my eyes lay at rest. 

Rubber stirring gravel jerked me out of my daze. The deputy had finally arrived to get me out of here. I walked out, doing my best to mask any trauma or emotions linked to my encounter earlier. He waved me into the passenger side of the truck, and after throwing my bag in the back. I hopped in. 

“Sure are dusty today, aren’t we?” The deputy said with a trickle of humor in his voice.  

I looked down. I hadn’t realized it, but I was caked in dry dirt from the pit. 

“Oh yeah. I tripped on my way out here today.” I replied lamely

“Well, I got dibs on the shower first. I got pretty dirty myself, was out on call all day.” Glancing over, I noticed how he had little bits of rock and dust everywhere, even in his hair.

“You got it, boss.” I was eager to stop talking, but didn’t want to explain to the deputy why. 

As we pulled up through his driveway and made our way to his trailer, I felt a light in me die. The dreamcatchers. They had become another day-to-day view at the deputy's house, and I hadn’t paid much mind to them until now. With sick realization, a wave of recognition washed over me. A black box sat dead center of every dream catcher, suspended in the air, encircled by thin winding lines. 

“Hey, you listening?” The deputy seemingly repeated, snapping me out of my dread trance.

“Y-yeah. Of course.” I responded, not entirely sure what to 

“Well, we can eat once we’re both done showering. Let’s go.” He got out and slammed the door behind him, while I crawled out the opposite. 

My head has been racing since. Inside now, I’m writing this as I wait for the shower, sure of what I have to do but trying to prolong it for as long as I can. I’m going to go back to the house. I’m going to go back to Pa. I know, I’m sure that I must’ve missed something, or that some clue will be there to help me with all of this. If I can make it out of this, I will update you ASAP, but until then, please pray for me.
