Riley sat at their desk, fingers hovering over the keyboard, scrolling through the endless gray of the feed. Each post passed silently, like leaves drifting over still water in a light breeze. Occasionally, the tap of the keys “haha so funny” echoed through the room, breaking the quiet in a small, hollow ripple. The mouse wheel whirred softly, the only constant movement in a world of stillness.


After hours, Riley decided to make a post. Fingers trembling slightly, they hit “send.” The screen blinked, then… nothing. No likes, no comments, no reactions. Just the quiet gray feed stretching out, indifferent. A pit opened in their stomach. Why did everyone else get engagement? Why did their words disappear into silence? Was their voice worthless? Was anyone even there to hear it?


The room seemed to press closer, the quiet deepening, and Riley refreshed the feed, searching for a spark that never came.


Riley’s eyes darted across the feed, desperate for a pulse of life, a single notification to prove they weren’t shouting into a void. But every post was still. Every reaction was silent. Even the little “haha” taps they’d made earlier seemed to vanish the moment they landed. It was as if the room had swallowed sound itself.


Then they noticed it; small, almost imperceptible patterns. The comment sections were filled only with smiles and applause; the usual cruelty had vanished entirely. Every so often, a comment would appear, but the words didn’t make sense. “Good day, floating circles,” one read. Another: “Your thought smells like blue.” Riley frowned. Who typed these? Were they bots? Were they people? Or echoes of people long gone, repeating fragments of themselves? 


A shiver ran down their spine. The feed wasn’t empty. It was… broken. Half alive. A ghostly approximation of connection. Riley’s heartbeat thudded in rhythm with the silent scroll, and suddenly, the room didn’t feel like a room anymore. It felt like the walls themselves were listening, judging the emptiness of their post, the lack of engagement, the futility of attention.
Their own post blinked quietly in the void, unnoticed. And then, for a fraction of a second, Riley thought they saw movement in the screen’s corner. Like a cursor nudging itself, a reply forming… and vanishing before their eyes.


Silence again.


Riley blinked, staring at the blank screen. The silence of the feed had seeped into the room itself. The hum of the computer no longer felt mechanical; it was a pulse, subtle but insistent, like the heartbeat of something watching. They reached for their coffee cup, but it was empty, and even the clink of ceramic against the desk was swallowed before it could echo.
Outside the window, the world was still. Cars glided silently along streets that seemed too wide, too empty. Neighbors walked past with their heads down, scrolling phones, tapping out “likes” that never appeared. Riley realized with a pang: it wasn’t just the Internet. It had spread. The silence had spilled over into reality, turning everything into a muted simulation.


Panicking, they refreshed the feed. One post shimmered, briefly alive. A dance video, a girl spinning, laughing, but the comment section was gone. No one. Just gray. "Be the first to comment!"


Then another post blinked, and Riley swore they heard a faint whisper from their speakers: “You shouldn’t be here.”
The cursor moved. Not by Riley. A sentence typed itself: “Do you see us now?”


Rileys fingers hovered, frozen, because replying felt like speaking to the void, and maybe, worse, letting the void speak back. The room seemed to lean closer, walls bending, pixels stretching toward them like tendrils, and the feed scrolled on endlessly, indifferent, alive, and terrifying.


They wanted to scream. But even the scream was gone before it left their mouth.
