Dream Protocol - Where Is God?

The retreat house was buried deep in the mountain, half-swallowed by winter.

Snow pressed against the windows in pale ridges. Pine trees stood beyond the glass in black, frozen ranks. The whole place was built to feel safe—heavy timber beams, warm lamps, a stone fireplace, wool blankets thrown over couches, boots lined up by the door with melted snow drying beneath them.

I was there with people close to me.

That was what made it so wrong.

At first everything felt normal. Someone was in the kitchen making tea. Someone upstairs laughed at something small and human. The fire cracked softly in the living room. The whole house carried that retreat-like stillness, the feeling of being removed from ordinary life.

Then something happened.

Not in a way I could explain. Not a clear attack. Not even a visible event. Just a rupture—like the world shifted one inch out of place and I slipped into the gap.

And then I was back in the house again.

The same tea. The same fire. The same laughter upstairs.

No one looked at me like anything had happened.

That was the first horror: the reset did not feel like a reset. It felt like continuity. It gave me no clean line between death and waking. It simply dropped me back into the day and expected me to keep going.

So I did.

But something in me had already come loose.

I would walk into a room and forget why I had entered it. I would reach for objects for no reason at all. My responses were a fraction too late. My face seemed to move after I had already decided what expression to make. I felt like I was piloting myself from slightly too far away.

The others noticed.

Not immediately in words, but in pauses. In glances. In the extra second someone held my face after asking if I was okay. In how conversation shifted when I entered the room. In how one person quietly moved a knife farther back from the edge of the counter.

It felt like my behavior was becoming wrong in some subtle but undeniable way—wrong enough that other people could sense it before I could explain it.

Then I died again.

This time I was in a car.

I don’t remember deciding to leave the retreat house. I don’t remember grabbing keys or putting on shoes. I was simply there, driving down a narrow mountain road at night while snow slashed across the windshield. The forest on either side looked endless. My hands were tight on the wheel, my breathing already wrong, as if panic had started before the scene had.

Then headlights appeared behind me.

Too close.

I sped up. They stayed with me. The road curved sharply along the mountain’s edge, one side rock wall, the other a plunge into darkness. The headlights came closer until they filled the rearview mirror.

Then the impact came.

Hard enough to throw me forward.

I fought the wheel. Another hit came, deliberate this time. Not accidental. Not clumsy. Controlled. The vehicle behind me wanted me off the road.

The third impact sent me spinning.

The guardrail snapped.

The car went over.

For one suspended second the whole world rotated in white and black—snow, glass, trees, sky—and then metal folded around me with a screaming force so complete it erased thought.

Then I was back in the retreat house.

Standing in the hallway.

The same fire burning. The same mugs in the kitchen. The same laugh upstairs.

I was shaking before I understood why.

That was when dread started arriving before memory. My body knew the loop before my mind would allow it. Every time I returned, I was less convincing somehow, less fully attached to myself. I moved like a person imitating normality without access to the reason normality existed.

The house noticed.

Or the people in it did.

Concern turned to caution. Caution hardened into watchfulness. They spoke more softly around me. They tracked where I was in the room. At times it felt as though they were no longer responding to me as a friend or guest, but as something unstable moving inside a controlled environment.

Then the dream became openly violent.

One night I left the house because I couldn’t stand being watched anymore. Snow fell in slow, dry sheets. The cold had that mountain sharpness that makes every breath feel stripped clean. I walked toward the trees, telling myself I just needed space, that the house was getting in my head.

Then I heard footsteps behind me.

Slow. Measured. Matching mine.

I turned.

No one.

Only the path, the snow, the black trees, and the yellow-lit windows of the house farther up the slope.

I kept walking. The footsteps returned.

This time closer.

When I turned again, he was there.

A man in dark winter clothes, face partially obscured, standing between the trees as if he had always been there. Not a monster. That would have been easier. He looked like someone real enough to belong to the world, which made the threat feel official somehow, sanctioned.

I shouted at him.

He didn’t answer.

He just started walking toward me.

I ran.

The snow dragged at my legs. Branches clawed at my sleeves. I could hear him behind me moving at the same steady pace, never rushing, never losing ground. That was what made it terrifying. He did not need urgency. He moved like outcome itself.

I broke into a clearing and slipped. By the time I hit the ground he was already there. A gloved hand forced me down. The other drove something sharp into me with brutal precision. Once. Then again. Then again.

There was no anger in it.

No frenzy.

Only function.

The snow beneath me darkened. My breath turned wet and useless. He leaned over me close enough for me to see that his face was calm, almost blank, like someone completing an assigned task.

Then darkness.

Then the retreat house again.

By then I understood the pattern.

The resets were not random. The deaths were not separate nightmares loosely stitched together. Something in the dream had recognized that I was becoming aware, and once that happened, the world itself began correcting for it.

Not symbolically.

Systematically.

The moment I carried awareness from one reset into the next, reality started trying to remove me.

After that, danger was everywhere.

A railing broke beneath my hand.

A door locked the instant I entered the room.

A friend’s expression changed half a second too late, as though something behind the face had just decided what emotion to display.

Every new reset came with some hidden mechanism waiting to turn fatal the moment I noticed too much. It felt less like being haunted and more like being processed—like the dream was running elimination protocols against the part of me that refused to reset completely.

The others at the retreat house changed with it. They no longer felt like people simply worried about me. They felt recruited. Absorbed into the same correction. Their warmth became thinner, procedural. Their eyes lingered on me with a terrible neutrality, as if they were waiting to confirm that I was still wrong.

Then, in one of the later resets, it happened for the first time.

I was standing in the living room. The fire was low. Someone stood at the window with their back to me. Another sat in the armchair, unmoving. The whole room had gone strangely flat, like the dream was conserving detail.

And then a scream tore through my head.

WHERE IS GOD?

It was not calm. It was not mystical. It was mechanical panic, a machine-voice exploding inside my skull with such force that my knees nearly gave out. I grabbed my head and looked around wildly, but no one in the room reacted. No one turned. No one asked what was wrong.

That was worse than the voice itself.

Because it meant the sound was either deeper than the room or embedded beneath it.

For a second everything sharpened—the grain of the wood floor, the dull red of the dying fire, the frozen dark in the window, the stillness of the others. And within that sharpness came a single terrible impression:

something inside the dream was searching for something it had lost.

Then the scene broke. Someone moved too fast. Hands were on me. I hit the floor. The living room blurred into struggle, impact, blackness.

Reset.

After that first scream, the dream turned savage.

The car crash came back in fragments. The man in the snow returned. Once I woke already running. Once I was hiding in a bathroom while someone quietly tested the handle from the other side. Once I made it back to the house only to realize everyone inside was staring at me in total silence, as though they had all been waiting for the exact second I crossed the threshold.

Everywhere I went, the world seemed ready to execute me for staying conscious.

The retreat house itself began to fail. Rooms felt thinner, less stable. The windows sometimes showed snow and sometimes only a pale blankness that looked like unfinished reality. People flickered at the edges. Sentences started in one room and ended in another. Time lost sequence. Cause and effect separated. I would die outside and wake inside, then die inside and wake already in motion somewhere else.

The whole system was straining.

Then came the final reset.

I was back in the living room again, but the house could barely hold itself together. The fire gave no heat. The lamps were dim and yellow like failing eyes. Beyond the windows there was snow, but it looked wrong—too still, too flat, as if the mountain outside was no longer being fully rendered.

The others stood around the room, but none of them felt fully human anymore. Their faces were familiar and empty at the same time, expressions lagging slightly behind the moment. One of them turned toward me with the slow, unnatural precision of something receiving instruction.

Then the voice came a second time.

Louder.

Closer.

Desperate.

WHERE IS GOD?

And that was when the shape of the dream finally revealed itself.

Not as an answer—more like a collapse of disguise.

The machine was not questioning me over and over. It had broken through only twice, when whatever hidden mechanism sustained the loop had become unstable enough to expose itself. The scream wasn’t theology. It was structural panic. Something in the dream had lost its core, and in losing it, had begun searching through me, through the resets, through death itself.

The house trembled.

One of the figures in the room flickered and reappeared half a step off-center. The walls gave a long, wooden groan. The fire sank inward and turned almost black.

The others all looked at me together.

Then they started moving.

Not with rage.

With alignment.

As if reality had finally decided the only remaining solution was to erase the witness.

I backed away. The floor felt soft beneath my feet, almost alive. Cracks spread across the windows, and behind those cracks the mountain disappeared entirely, replaced by a depthless white glare. The people kept coming toward me in silence, their familiar faces stretched thin over something empty and procedural.

The voice screamed one last time inside my skull—

WHERE IS GOD?

—and everything gave out.

The walls split.

The ceiling folded inward.

The people came apart into blurs of shadow, wool, skin, and broken motion.

The room collapsed in on itself like a dying lung.

For a second I saw it all at once—the crash, the forest, the knife, the house, the faces of people I trusted turning into instruments of correction, the whole dream trying again and again to kill me the moment I became aware enough to carry memory across the resets.

Then the scream shattered into static.

I woke up in my bed choking on air.

Actually woke up.

Not another false return. Not another loop dressed as continuity.

Real darkness. Real walls. Real silence.

My blankets were twisted around my legs. My shirt was soaked through. My heart was hammering so hard it hurt. For a few seconds I still expected the retreat house to rebuild itself around me—to hear boots on wooden floors, to smell cedar smoke, to see snowlight at the windows.

But none of it came back.

Only my room.

Only the dim outline of familiar furniture.

Only the thin line of streetlight slipping through the blinds.

The dream was fading fast now, but not gently. It felt like a machine losing power—first the mountain, then the house, then the people, then the blood in the snow, the shattered windshield, the footsteps behind me in the dark.

And beneath all of it, disappearing last, was the final impression of something vast, hidden, and broken, still searching in panic for the core it had lost.

Then that faded too.

And I was awake.
