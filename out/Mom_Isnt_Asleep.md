My dad passed away suddenly, leaving only Mom and me.

The doctor said she’d been traumatized and hadn’t been sleeping well, so I should keep an eye on her.

I moved back home. Every night before bed, I’d listen outside her door, only daring to go back to my room once I heard her breathing steadily.

That night, I worked overtime until early morning. I opened the door quietly. The night light in the living room was still on.

Mom was sitting on the sofa, her back to me, completely still.

“Mom? Why aren’t you asleep?”

She didn’t turn around. Her voice was soft, as if drifting from far away:

“I’m waiting for your dad.”

I walked over to help her back to her room. Her hands were ice-cold, her knuckles white from gripping tight. Her eyes were fixed on the shoe rack by the entrance.

Dad’s slippers were still there, right next to Mom’s.

“Mom, Dad’s gone,” my voice trembled.

She slowly turned her head. Her face was blank, her eyes terrifyingly empty.

“He didn’t leave. He comes home every night. When you’re not here, he still talks about you.”

My hair stood on end.

“Mom, don’t talk nonsense.”

I led her back to her room, then lay down in mine. I tossed and turned all night, feeling as if someone was watching me through the crack in the door.

The next morning, I was making coffee in the kitchen when I heard her muttering to herself in the living room. I peeked over and saw her talking to thin air, cooing like she was talking to Dad:

“Stop scaring the kid. He’s easily frightened.”

I pretended not to hear, ate breakfast quickly, and left for work. I was on edge all day, as if someone was breathing right next to my ear.

That night, I was too scared to go to my room and curled up on the living room sofa.

At two in the morning, I was woken by rustling sounds.

Mom was standing in front of me, holding Dad’s old black-and-white checkered scarf, wrapping it around my neck, loop after loop.

“Mom! What are you doing?!” I struggled and pushed her away.

She stared blankly, then let go, murmuring:

“Your dad said you were cold… told me to wrap this around you…”

I coughed violently, annoyed.

“Mom, are you out of your mind?!”

I couldn’t stand her mental state anymore. I called a nursing home to pick her up the next day and checked into a hotel myself.

To be honest, that house gave me the creeps.

At 3:00 a.m., the front desk called:

“Sorry to disturb you, Mr. Zhou. There’s an elderly lady here who says she’s your mother. She’s waiting for you at the front desk with a scarf.”

What? A scarf?

That crazy old woman! I’d had enough.

I hung up, stood up angrily, got dressed, and opened the door to go downstairs.

As I waited for the elevator, it suddenly hit me:

I had locked Mom inside the house. How did she get out?

I took out my phone, opened the home security app.

The live feed showed Mom leaning on the sofa, facing the TV, motionless, her back to the camera. I couldn’t tell if she was asleep.

If Mom’s still at home… then who’s downstairs?

A cold chill ran down my spine. I called the front desk again.

“Hello? This is the guest in Room 816. You said a lady claiming to be my mother is waiting for me?”

“Yes, sir. But she said she couldn’t wait and was coming up to see you. We couldn’t stop her. We called your room, but you didn’t answer…”

I watched the elevator numbers flicker: 6… 7… 8… finally stopping at my floor, 9.

Ding.

I held my breath, frozen with fear.

The elevator doors slowly opened.

No one was inside.

Just as I began to relax, I faintly heard a familiar song in the distance.

It was the lullaby Mom used to sing to me when I was little.

“Son…”

A voice came from my phone, the security feed. I almost dropped it in terror.

On the screen, Mom, who’d been sitting on the sofa with her back to me, slowly turned her head—without moving her body. Her head twisted 180 degrees, staring straight at the camera in an inhuman position.

“Son… Mom hasn’t fallen asleep yet.”

Her eyes were pitch-black and hollow, absolutely horrifying.

“When are you coming home?”

In the empty hotel corridor, the lullaby grew louder, drawing near.

Along with it came the unique sound of Mom’s slippers shuffling on the floor.

“Son… Mom and Dad are both home. Come back soon. Let’s be a family again.”

The security feed turned to static.

The slipper sounds and the singing stopped abruptly behind me.

My eyes widened in terror. I stiffly turned my head and slowly looked back.

There was nothing in the corridor—

only Dad’s old black-and-white checkered scarf, lying quietly at my feet.

Uncontrollably, I bent down, picked it up, and wrapped it around my neck…

loop by loop.
