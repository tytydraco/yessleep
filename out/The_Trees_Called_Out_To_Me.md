It was a warm Saturday night in September. Looking out at the leaves I could tell that they would redden and fall soon, but for now, they were gorgeous and lush. The tall trees wore the light of the quickly setting sun like orange, burning halos. I was driving back to my college dorm. I had spent the majority of the day in a coastal Maine town, salty air blowing through my hair, exclamations of happiness and joy from children on the beach, it was a great day.

I had on my swimsuit top under a tank top. I still have a sarong around my hips, which I meant to replace with the sweatpants in my backseat, but I got caught up in leaving. I was just meeting some friends from a different college for a little beach day. I take my sunglasses off. It’s dark enough now that I don’t need them. It’ll only get darker.

My name is Sylvia. I’m a college student. It’s the first weekend of the semester. I have about two hours left in my drive. I don’t love that the majority of it will be in the dark, but I will manage.

BANG

My thoughts are interrupted by a loud noise. All of a sudden I smell burnt rubber and I feel my control over my car getting worse. My tire must have popped. I gradually slow down, looking for a level place to stop my car. I eventually make my way to a flat, leftwards-curving section of the highway. I put my car into neutral to check if it rolls, and when it doesn’t, I put my car into park and then just sit there for a second.

“I’ll make sure to get a tire for you before you leave.” 

My brother’s words echo through my mind. He was driving my car a little over a week ago and he popped a tire trying to drift. All of a sudden, it was time for me to go to college and there was no time to get a replacement. I cursed myself for dragging my feet on this issue.

I open my calendar app and schedule a note saying ‘bitch get a replacement tire and swear at Ian’ for tomorrow. The smell of burnt rubber was eventually too much to bear. I step outside and walk around my car. It was the back left tire that popped.

“Damn it.”

I look up and down the road but I don’t see headlights. I look around the road to see that I’m in the middle of a field, with a line of trees maybe a mile away. I hear… a strange droning coming from the field… like a cat resting in the right keys of an organ, except far away.

I instinctively feel myself walking towards the field, stepping down the steep decline past the guard rail next to the highway, walking into the grass. The grass was about hip height, though I am taller on average. I reach down as I slowly pace towards the trees, each tall blade and reed passing through my fingers with the deliberate unpredictability of a lover tracing the lines of my pam.

Each step felt… right. The soil wasn’t dry, but my feet didn’t sink into it. The grass didn’t fall down as I passed, but they didn’t push too hard against my thighs, shins, and feet. The sound, the droning sound, that melancholic chord, it resonated in my heart and my brain, it quickened my breath, it made my stomach sink, it waltzed up and down my spine, it danced the length of my leg, from my toes, past my shin, around my knee, up my thigh—

“HEY!”

I whip my head around. A man stands by my car. He’s pulled his truck behind my white sedan.

“IS THIS YOUR CAR!?” He yells at me.

“YES, SORRY, I BLEW A TIRE!” I yell back, now incredibly aware of how far I walked into this field.

I walk to him. He holds out his hand to pull me up the decline. I take it. Now that we’re on an equal plane, he uses the helpful hand to shake mine.

“I’m Walt.”

“Sylvia.”

“You got a last name Sylvia?”

“None relevant.”

“None relevant… I like that answer.” He smirks, continuing, “well, you got a spare?”

I inform him that I don’t to which he looks less than impressed.

“Well shit, young lady… I mean, do you want me to take you down the road to a mechanic to see if they have a tire that fits?” He asks.

“Um…” 

Damn it. I don’t want to leave my car, even more, I’d rather not get into the car with this strange man, but I guess I don’t actually have any other choice.

“Sure, I guess. I’ll get a couple things from my car.”

“You got pants?” He asks. I look down at the sarong, which you can all too visibly see my swimsuit under.

“Oh you’re j— yeah, yeah I do.”

As Walt respectfully turns around, I walk over to my car and grab my phone, dropping a pin on my location in my navigation app. I took the sarong off, put on my sweatpants, picked up my bag from the backseat, shut the side door, and walked to my trunk. I opened it, rooted through my hiking pack, took my knife, and discreetly put it in my bag. Locking my car and making sure my headlights are off, I walk to Walt’s truck, open the passenger side door, and step up into it. We start driving.

I glance over at Walt, an older man, maybe sixty, maybe more. His hair is greying and short. His face looks like he’s never put sunscreen on. His flannel is red and black, and his jeans are blue. His truck smells faintly of cigarettes. There are cans rattling by my feet. Some old sounding rock song is softly playing on the radio.

“So what’s a pretty girl like yourself doing out in this town at this hour?” I can’t tell if he’s a creep or just old.

“Uh, I was visiting some friends by the coast.”

“You a college student?”

“Yes.”

“Whatcha studying?”

“Politics and music.” The answer came out faster than I could think about it. I try not to tell people in the rural parts of Maine that I study politics. It has gotten me into a couple shouting matches. 

“Mhm. What kind of music?” Thank god he didn’t mention the former.

“Harp, I’m a harpist.”

“Well isn’t that something? Got them quick fingers, huh?” Leaning more towards creep.

“Yeah, I guess.”

“We’re about ten minutes away from the mechanic, so we've got some time together.”

I remembered the noise I heard. 

“Hey, did you hear that noise before you called out to me?”

“Can’t say I did… what, uh, what’d it sound like?”

“Like a chord of some sort, like it was coming from an organ, it was constant dron—”

“Droning, entrancing, huh?” Walt cuts me off.

“Yeah, it was, what was that?”

“The trees. They sang to you. It’s a phenomenon that happens ‘round here. Sometimes, kids like you walk a little too far into ‘em, and, well, we don’t exactly know what happens then. They never come back. Probably get eaten by bears or coyotes or something.”

“The trees?”

“Ayuh.”

“They sing?”

“Ayuh.”

“What the hell does that even mean?”

“Couldn’t tell you. I ain’t never heard ‘em. But it’s the legend ‘round here. Honestly, I probably wasn’t going to stop for you ‘til I noticed you waltzing your pretty ass into the field there. Clearly there’s some draw to those trees.”

“Yeah, I see. Well, I’m glad you called out to me.”

“Ah, well, I might have done it even if you weren’t in the field. I can’t give you all the answers, but the mechanic, Jerry, he might be able to tell you a little more about the trees.

The rest of the car ride was ice breakers and silence. We eventually make it to the mechanic’s place. There was a light on in the tiny attendant’s shack. A man walks out as we pull up.

“Alright, let’s talk to Jerry.” Walt pulls over and we step out. I sling my bag around my shoulder.

Jerry is a very average looking man. Coveralls with a ‘Jerry’ patch, curly, dark hair. Looks like he hasn’t stepped foot in a shower in days. Similarly old, like Walt.

“Jerry.” Walt acknowledges the man.

“Walt. What’ve ya got here?” Jerry asks, revealing a heavier Maine accent than Walt’s.

“This girl here popped a tire out on the highway, didn’t have a spare.” Walt clarifies. Jerry extends his right hand.

“Sylvia.” I say, shaking it. His handshake is strong but precise. Probably very well suited for car parts.

“Mhm. Well, I can’t be sure I have what you need, but I’m good with a few brands ‘round here, what’s the make ‘n model?” He asks.

I tell him, to which he thinks for a second.

“Well, lucky for you, I’m good with that brand. In fact, the city up north has a dealership. Come to think of it, one of their mechanics owes me a couple few things. Now I’ve an excuse to haul my ass up there.” He says.

“Well, great!” I say, glancing at Walt, who smiles.

“Perfect, tell you what, I’ll leave Sylvia with you, why don’t you go up the highway, pick up her car, and keep it here. After you’re done, Jerry, take her to Captain’s Inn, I’ll drive up and tell Martha, after all, she owes me one.” Walt says.

“You all owe each other a lot, huh?” I remark.

“Don’t think anyone’s paid for anything in this part of Maine, honey.” Jerry says, playfully slapping my shoulder.

“Ok, I’ll be going now. Oh, Jerry, tell her about the trees on the drive.” Walt adds while starting to walk back to his truck.

“God, Walt, she heard ‘em?”

“Ayuh.” Walt gets in his truck, starts it, and drives off.

“He’s— ah whatever. Ok, let’s head out. Stay here a sec.”

He walks to his garage and he drives out a tow truck. I got in the passenger side. We drove off, down the road the opposite way Walt and I went.

“So, uh, what are you doing out here?” He asks.

“I was visiting some friends on the coast. We hadn’t seen each other since last semester.”

“Yeah. Ok. Got a boyfriend?” 

“What?”

“Or girlfriend? I guess, I mean, I don’t mean to be rude, but it’s a little new for me ‘n you college kids are doing whatever these days.”

“No, I, sorry, what? I’m single.” I didn’t really know how to respond to that.

“Yep. My wife and I met down by the coast. High school sweethearts. She went to college in California, studied Marxism and Socialism. Gotta say I never really understood it. But she came back. I never pushed her, she said she’d fancy a life in local politics. Who was I to say no to the whip smart tanned bombshell at my door?” He laughs. “She did law. I worked my trade. Jerry and Jenny. We, uh, we had a kid a while back. Tim. He was the most beautiful baby boy. Looked exactly like his mom. Had her smarts, too. But he had my eyes. He was a high school senior. Played football like his old man. Martha, his girlfriend, was the lead cheerleader. Just like Jenny. After homecoming, his team had a party out in that field. The one your car is parked by. The grass wasn’t always so tall. His teammates said he was acting weird, he kept telling Martha about music he was hearing. He walked to those trees and no one saw him again. There was a search party. Half the team also went missing. Now, we don’t talk about them trees. Not often. I take it you heard that song?”

“Yes.”

“Well. Let’s just get you out of town.”

“I’m sorry for your loss.”

“Yeah, can’t quite change much now.”

The rest of the drive was silent. The truck smelled like oil. The sun was just barely peeking over the horizon. Probably five or so minutes of sunlight left. There was no one on the road. I could see Jerry scanning the side of the road, probably looking for deer. We eventually pulled up to my car. A fox was sleeping on top of it. It woke up as we arrived, and darted into the grass. Jerry pulls up in front of my car.

“Ok, I got this. You stay right here.” He says, getting out.

As I sit in the car, Jerry hitches my car to the back, pulls it up, and gets back in. We drove to Captain’s Inn silently. I felt like if I said something, made a noise, even breathed too hard, that one of us would break into tears. The tension felt like a bodybuilder flexing his biceps, with me in between them. I had never heard silence so deafening.

We eventually pull up to the Inn. We both got out. I walked around to the back of my car and grabbed a small suitcase from my trunk. I’ve learned my lesson one too many times about unexpected multi-day trips.

“I’ll tell you what, I’ll have your car all ready at around 11:00 am. If ya walk the rightwards down the road, I’m a mile away. You’re young, you can handle that. Martha’s right over there. Have a good night.” He says.

“Thank you, you too.”

“Yup.”

“I mean really, thank you, you and Walt, you’re lifesavers.” I insist.

Jerry pauses for a second.

“Make sure you get a good night of sleep.” He gets back in the truck and drives my car off.

I walk over to Martha. The attendant’s booth at the motel is cozy. Lit with warm lights. Martha tells me that Walt was just here. She gives me a room key, 210. Is she the same Martha that Jerry mentioned? I walk over to room 210. Almost as soon as I enter the room I realize how tired I am. Like there are weights tied to every one of my limbs. I brush my teeth and text my roommate that I wouldn't be home tonight. I take the swimsuit top off and put a baggy shirt on. After that, I crawl into bed and go to sleep.

4:13

The tiny clock on the wardrobe perpendicular to my bed is the first thing I see. The red, analog numbers cut through the darkness, I can still make out the rest of the room, the door, the carpet, wildlife pictures. Then I see them.

At the foot of my bed, staring at me, is a person. Shock rushes through my body, it feels like pins prick every single pore on my body. I try to move. I can’t. I can’t move my arms, my legs, my fingers. It’s like I’ve been frozen in a block of ice. I’m lying on my back, my head propped up, facing the two people. I try to scream, to talk, to make any noise, but all that comes out is a pathetic whimper.

The figure, a tall, nondescript man, starts slowly reaching towards my face. He’s not wearing clothes, but its too dark to make out any part of his body, like he’s wearing a skin tight dark-colored outfit. Before his hand reaches my face, it stops at my shoulder. His arm seems impossibly long. He curls his fingers under the sheet that I had pulled up to my chest. He starts pulling it back. 

The fear felt like a bomb going off in my heart. It felt like the neighboring rooms could hear my heartbeat. It felt like my ribs were going to burst from how strongly my heart was beating. Further, it felt like my stomach sank into a pit so impossibly deep that I’d never feel normal again.

I went to bed with just a pair of underwear aside from the shirt. As the sheet receded, I felt the cold night air gently constrict on my skin. My stomach, legs, and arms were covered in goosebumps. He slid the sheet off the bed.

Gingerly, he brought his finger to my toe. He moved his hand up, past my shin, around my knee, up my thigh. If I could flinch, I would— if I could kick, I would. Onto my stomach,, over my sternum, my neck, around my chin, up the bridge of my nose, and stopping at my forehead.

He takes his hand back. I still feel the line he traced up my body. Out of nowhere, he holds up what looks like a scythe in one hand and a bundle of wheat in the other. I start imagining how easily the metal could cut through my skin. Instead, he laid it on my stomach. The cold steel feels like pure ice, even through the shirt. He placed the wheat on the scythe.

I jolt up in bed, throw off the sheets, and check my stomach. There’s nothing. I lay back, defeated. Checking the time, it’s 8:04. I still feel the traced line and the cold steel. My heart still feels like a danger to my ribcage. I roll over and pick up my phone. I watch videos for about an hour.

I eventually get out of bed, get dressed, and brush my teeth. Even though I brought the small suitcase, it didn’t have a full set of clothes in it. I was wearing the sweatpants, tank top, and swimsuit top again. 

I walk over to the attendant’s booth to talk to Martha.

“Hi, doll, how was your sleep?”

“Oh, it was… fine.”

“You’re a terrible liar, but it’s ok.” She said, smiling.

“Well, I guess that makes me a more moral person?” I smile back.

“Makes you bad at a lot of other things though. You know, I’m about to go on break to head into town ‘n get some breakfast. You wanna come with?” She asked.

“Sure, thank you. All the people in this town have been so nice to me.” I replied.

“Too bored to be rude, I guess.” She grabbed her keys and walked to the door, beckoning me to come with her.

We walk to and get in her car. The drive to town was pretty short. We listened to some 90’s Icelandic pop via cassette tape. Martha’s car was old. It had that nostalgic old car smell I’m not sure the origin of.

We stop, walk into a diner, and sit down.

“Martha, I was talking to Jerry last night about—” I lowered my voice, recognizing that what I’m about to say might be a sore subject, “about the trees.”

Martha has a serious expression on her face.

“He mentioned a Martha, one that was dating his son. Is that you?” I ask.

“Yeah. I dated Tim in high school. Up until his disappearance. Knowing Jerry, I probably couldn’t tell you much more than he already has. Tim… well, honestly, I don’t know what happened. I probably won’t ever know. He walked into those woods and he never walked out. Police ain’t do much. Ain’t much for them to do. I still wonder sometimes. Where’d he go? But, there ain’t much I can do now. I try to live life for myself. Now. Let’s have some waffles, huh?”

Our conversation shifted from the trees to life, college, gossip, and our day-to-day lives. I eventually realized that it’s 10:43. I told Martha that I had to be at Jerry’s around 11:00. She asked if I wanted to hitch a ride, but I told her that I would love to stretch my legs a little.

She drives me back to the Inn. I grab my stuff from my room and head off down the road. The sounds of bugs and birds combined in a chorus around me. It was a hot day. The trees didn’t do much to shade me. I eventually made it to Jerry’s to see my car with all new tires. Jerry walks out of his shed and over to me.

“Well, here ya go, Sylvia.” He said as I put my bags into the trunk.

“Thank you so much! What do I owe you?” I asked.

“Ah, nothing. Walt paid me a little earlier, and, well, he’s done a lot for me. Especially yesterday. I guess you’re off, huh?” Jerry looks weirdly shifty, like he’s unsure about something.

“Yeah. I have to get back to college. I’ll make sure to come back, though, to experience town in a better circumstance.” I said

“Yeah, that’d be nice.” Jerry says. 

He pauses a moment before extending his left hand for a handshake. It was a little odd to me, he used his right hand last time. Without investigating further, I take his hand and start shaking it while looking at it. All of a sudden, I feel his grip contract on my hand, he slightly pulls me closer. My eyes shoot to his face, only to see his right fist shooting towards my face. I flinch—

Black.

Appearing to me through the black— the scythe. It pulses in the monocolor landscape.

The pulsing of the scythe eventually transfers to my brain. It feels like my diaphragm has been transplanted to my brain. Pain shoots throughout my face.

The blackness is replaced by overpowering beams of sunlight beating down on my face. It takes me a second to acclimate to my surroundings. My arms are above my head, bound at the wrist. I’m being dragged on my back. I look up. Jerry holds the rope that binds my wrists. He’s dragging me with some extra length of rope.

I look around me, we’re back in the field. I hear that music again. I start to scream, to freak out, to jerk wildly at the restraints. Jerry notices.

“I’m sorry Sylvia. But you heard it. No one else hears it. I need to see where he went. You understand.” He says, still pulling.

My answer wasn’t coherent, but it was loud. The fear I felt manifested in warm tears.

“No one’ll hear ya. No one’s out here.” He says.

After what felt like an hour, we eventually made it to the trees. He pulled me a little bit past the tree line, where he stood me up. I tried to run, but he jerked the rope and I was pulled off my feet. He pulled me to a tree where he tied the rope around a branch. He gave me enough slack so that I could rest on my knees. 

“Why are you doing this?” I asked, out of breath.

“I need to see what happened to him. I’m not a bad guy, I just want to see who —or what— took him.”

I glance around. I can see my white sedan waiting at the side of the highway. He noticed.

“Yeah I took your car. Tell you what? I’ll even give you your keys.” 

He reached into his pocket and pulled out my keys. He underhanded them to my head. With my arms above me, I couldn’t block them as they hit my face and fell to the dirt in front of me.

“Well, now we wait. Do you hear it?” He asks, sitting down.

“Yes.” My answer is dripping with resentment, but it’s the truth.

“Ain’t that something?” He says.

We sit and wait. I notice the music start to… fade. It gets quieter.

“You got a family, Sylvia? I don’t think you’d understand my action if you don’t— the fuck?”

Jerry cuts himself off, suddenly standing up and looking around.

“God damn it in all my life I never— Sylvia, I hear it! The music!”

He turns back to me. I don’t have an answer. My throat is dry. A combination of the screaming and the buckets of sweat I lost, probably.

“Is this what you heard?”

He tries to mimic the chord with his voice. I stare behind him. Just when I thought I couldn’t get more scared, I saw him again. The man from my dream in the inn. He’s peeking out from behind a tree. My stomach falls into that same pit as it did in the inn. Jerry sees me staring behind him. He turns around and he stops talking. He freezes.

“Tim?”

Jerry screams as the figure jolts forward. Not just forward. Through. He runs through Jerry. His scream rings out through the trees. Jerry is gone. The figure returns to a standing position. He walks to me, the scythe in his right hand.

It put the scythe under my chin, making me look up at its face. It raises the scythe high above its head. I close my eyes and turn my head away. I hear the scythe cut through air. My arms fall to my knees. I hesitantly open my eyes. He cut the rope tying me to the branch. I carefully untie the rope around my hands, grab the keys in front of me, and look up. The figure is no longer there. I don’t waste a minute. I burst out of the treeline.

I run harder than I ever have before. I don’t let the vastness of the field stop me. I get to my car in what feels like only two minutes, though I’m sure it was longer than that. I check the trunk. My bags are still there. I slump into the driver’s seat. My mind races to who I tell about what just happened to me. No one would believe me. I eventually decided to just go back to my dorm, be thankful I haven’t sustained any injuries, and deal with the mental impact later.

I open my phone to put my dorm into my navigation app only to be met with a calendar reminder.
