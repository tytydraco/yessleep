Winter in Moscow.

Minus 20 degrees.

I check my watch: it’s almost 6:09 PM.

That’s how I usually walk peacefully from the office where I work to my apartment. The entire route shouldn’t take more than four blocks. As the icy wind of the approaching night grows harsher, I notice a passageway leading exactly toward my apartment, which is strange, since I’ve taken this route countless times and have never seen it before... though “passageway” isn’t really the right word. It’s more like a street. It’s surprisingly wide, even paved. Its only unusual feature is being squeezed between two enormous buildings, but...

As I stare at the path, a violent gust of wind blows straight toward me, forcing me to take shelter inside it.

Now somewhat protected from the blizzard, I notice two signs attached to the walls beside me, one on each building, displaying their names: Maxim Building on my left and Sofia Building on my right...

But weren’t those buildings connected to each other?

My friend Ekhaterina lives in the Maxim Building and once complained that she didn’t even have a side window because the neighboring buildings were pressed right against it...

The moment I turn around to head back, the snowstorm grows even stronger, and I realize my only option is to keep moving through this strange alley that I’m almost certain shouldn’t exist.

After ten or fifteen steps, I spot a restaurant called “Laci’s.” I’m surprised, since I hadn’t seen it from the entrance of the alley, so I immediately head toward it. It’s probably the perfect place to warm up...

Locked.

I turn the handle the other way... Is that even possible?

Still locked.

I knock on the door several times, but nobody answers. This place should be open by now. Or at least someone should be inside. Wednesday is a workday too...

Back to square one.

So I keep walking.

After some time, I hear faint squeaking noises coming from inside what looks like an ordinary trash can. Thinking it’s probably just a rat or something, I pick up my pace to avoid trouble, but since when could such a narrow alley stretch on for this long?

It’s impossible for a place like this to go on for hundreds of meters in the middle of Moscow.

I continue walking until I see another trash can.

Another sharp squeak comes from inside it.

Another Laci’s restaurant...

Wait.

Another Laci’s?

That makes no sense...

I knock on the door again, and once again nobody answers.

Immediately, I start running as if my life depends on it — maybe it actually does — but every couple hundred meters, it’s always the same thing:

A Laci’s restaurant.

Then, a few steps later, a squeaking trash can.

I can’t take it anymore.

 I want to go home. 

I want to throw myself onto my bed and forget all of this!...

After countless loops — which, strangely enough, never exhausted me — I finally make the dreadful decision I’d been avoiding since the beginning:

I’m going to open the trash can.

That thing is driving me insane... what kind of rat never shuts up!?

With every step I take toward that cursed metal container, my heartbeat grows faster and louder, but what choice do I have? It’s my only option left...

I grab the handle of the trash can, and the moment I crack it open just a few inches, something bursts out of it.

Soft. Freezing cold.

No mouth. No eyes. No nose. Nothing.

That thing spills out of the trash can and nearly buries my entire body beneath it. Its color resembles nothing I’ve ever seen before.

Nothing.

I scream and scream and scream, but nobody hears me. No protagonist comes to save me. And with every passing second, I feel the pressure crushing my chest grow stronger. I can hear my bones cracking, as if I’m being constricted by a giant snake...

Until I finally give up.

Maybe being devoured — or whatever this thing wants from me — is the only way out of this place...

Just as I begin to see a blinding light approaching me, I hear a voice repeating my name several times.

When I open my eyes — which, in my mind, had stopped working long ago — I see a brown liquid with the smell of coffee, the taste of coffee, and the warmth of coffee, along with an old man in tears shouting my name.

My face soaked in cheap coffee, still trying to process everything that had happened over the past few minutes — or hours? — I ask the old man who he is, and discover that he’s Laci, the owner of the restaurant in that alley...

I thank Mr. Laci for helping me, and, afraid of becoming even more involved with that place, I notice the storm has weakened.

I stand up and continue on my way.

No fatigue.

No pain.

Nothing...

The rest of my night is peaceful, and I even wake up feeling unusually well. I follow my normal route to the office and pass by the Maxim and Sofia buildings.

They really are connected to each other...

That can’t...

be possible.

I sit down on a bench beside the buildings and search on Google:

“Laci’s Restaurant Moscow.”

There aren’t many results.

No location. No website.

Just a blurry image of a newspaper article from 1963, uploaded to some online forum in terrible quality...

According to the article, a woman had been found dead in front of Laci’s Restaurant under circumstances never seen before. All 206 bones in her body had been fractured, and because of the incident, the alley where the restaurant stood was sealed off in 1964 and eventually became part of a new extension of the Maxim Building.

I turn off my phone, leave it beside me, and decide not to go to work today.
