
I’ve always been a creature of the night. Some people are driven by the sun, but I’ve always felt that the world only makes sense when the rest of the population is dreaming. That’s why I took the job at "The Rusty Anchor," a 24-hour diner tucked into a jagged corner of the industrial district in Gary, Indiana. It was the kind of place where the air always felt heavy with the smell of old fry-oil and the distant, rhythmic clanging of the steel mills.
 
My coworkers were ghosts in their own right. There was Old Pete, who had been flipping burgers since the Kennedy administration and whose skin looked like cured leather. Then there was me—a college dropout trying to find meaning in the bottom of a coffee pot at 4:00 AM.
 
To pass the time, we’d tell stories. Usually, they were about the truckers who’d gone missing on I-65 or the "Lady in White" who supposedly haunted the walk-in freezer. But one night, during a particularly brutal thunderstorm that turned the sky a bruised purple, Pete leaned over the stainless-steel counter.
 
"You ever hear of the Hash-Slinger?" he asked, his voice a low rasp.
 
I chuckled, wiping a smudge of mustard off my apron. "You mean the SpongeBob story? The Hash-Slinging Slasher? Come on, Pete. I’m twenty-four, not six."
 
Pete didn't smile. He didn't even blink. He just stared through the front window at the rain-slicked parking lot. "The show didn't invent that name, kid. They sanitized it. They took a story that used to keep men out of the kitchens in the fifties and turned it into a gag for kids. But the real one? The one they based it on? He wasn't a cartoon."
 
Pete told me about a man named Elias Vane. In 1954, Elias was the fastest short-order cook in the state. He worked at a diner just like ours. One night, a freak accident with a faulty meat grinder didn't just take his hand—it took his arm up to the elbow. The diner’s owner, a greedy man with no insurance, fired Elias while he was still in the hospital.
 
The story goes that Elias came back a week later. He had "repaired" himself. He’d taken a heavy, industrial-grade spatula and bolted it directly into the bone of his stump. He didn't want his job back. He wanted a "final order."
 
"The signals," Pete whispered, leaning closer. "The lights flicker. The phone rings and nobody’s there. And the walls... they start to sweat. Not water. Not grease. Something dark. Something that smells like the grave."
 
I laughed it off. I had to. If I didn't, the shadows in the corner of the diner would have started to look too much like a tall man in a chef’s hat.

 
Two weeks later, Pete didn't show up for his shift.
 
It wasn't like him. Pete was the kind of man who would show up even if he were dead. I was left alone to man the grill. The diner was empty, save for a single flickering neon sign that buzzed like a trapped hornet.
 
At 3:00 AM, the atmosphere changed. The air became thick, almost pressurized. I felt a pop in my ears, the way you do when you’re descending in an airplane. I looked at the clock. The second hand wasn't moving. It was twitching—back and forth, back and forth, as if time itself were trying to move forward but was being snagged on something.
 
Then, the lights began to flicker.
 
It wasn't a power surge. It was rhythmic. On. Off. Long pause. On. Off. It felt like a heartbeat. I walked over to the fuse box, my heart hammering against my ribs. "Just old wiring," I muttered. "Gary has terrible infrastructure. It’s just the storm."
 
But there was no storm. Outside, the air was dead still. The puddles in the parking lot were like black glass, reflecting nothing.
 
I turned back to the kitchen, and that’s when I heard it.
 
Ring.
 
The payphone by the restrooms. It was an old-school bell ringer. I hadn't heard that phone ring in the three years I'd worked there. Most people didn't even know it worked.
 
I walked toward it, my boots sticking to the floor. The linoleum felt... soft. Like I was walking on something organic. I reached for the receiver.
 
"Hello?" I said. My voice sounded tiny in the vast, empty diner.
 
At first, there was only static—a white noise that sounded like the rushing of a distant ocean. Then, a sound emerged from the fuzz. A wet, rhythmic thud.
 
Thwack. Squelch. Thwack. Squelch.
 
It was the sound of a heavy blade hitting something soft. Something saturated.
 
"Is this a joke?" I asked, my voice cracking. "Pete? Is that you? It’s not funny, man."
 
“Order… for… Vane…” a voice whispered. It didn't come from the phone. It sounded like it was being spoken directly into my inner ear, a cold vibration that made my teeth ache.
 
I slammed the phone down. My hand was shaking so violently I could barely keep it steady. I ran back to the kitchen, intending to lock myself in the office and wait for the sun. But when I reached the grill, I stopped dead.
 
The walls.
 
The white subway tiles were weeping. A thick, dark-green sludge was oozing from the grout. It didn't drip like water; it pulsed, moving in waves down the wall. It smelled like a mixture of copper, burnt hair, and the stagnant water you find at the bottom of a dumpster.
 
The third signal.

 
I backed away from the grill, my breath coming in short, ragged gasps. I looked toward the front windows, praying to see a police car, a trucker, or even a stray dog. Anything that belonged to the world I knew.
 
Instead, I saw the bus.
 
It shouldn't have been there. There were no bus routes that ran past the industrial district at 3:30 in the morning. It was an old, rounded-edge transit bus from the 1950s, painted a dull, rusted cream color. The windows were opaque, covered in a layer of grime so thick it looked like skin.
 
The bus didn't have a driver. It drifted into the parking lot without the sound of an engine, stopping perfectly in front of the diner doors.
 
The doors of the bus hissed open. Pshhh-kooh.
 
A figure stepped out.
 
He was impossibly tall, his head nearly touching the top of the bus door frame. He wore a chef’s toque that was yellowed with age and a double-breasted tunic that was missing most of its buttons. His apron wasn't white; it was a patchwork of stains—black grease, brown blood, and green slime.
 
He walked with a heavy, mechanical limp. Step. Screee. Step. Screee.
 
He wasn't carrying a weapon. He didn't need to. His left arm ended in a stump, and protruding from the scarred, necrotic flesh was a massive, rectangular spatula. It wasn't a kitchen tool anymore. The edges had been ground down until they were razor-sharp, and the metal was pitted with rust and dark, dried matter.
 
He pushed through the front doors of the diner. The bell above the door didn't chime; it let out a dull, dead clunk.
 
I scrambled behind the counter, grabbing the largest carving knife we had. "Stay back!" I yelled. "I have a weapon! I'll call the cops!"
 
The figure didn't stop. He didn't even acknowledge me. He walked straight to the grill. He moved with a terrifying, jerky motion, like a marionette being operated by a drunkard.
 
He reached out with his "hand"—the spatula—and scraped the flat-top grill. The sound was deafening, a high-pitched metallic shriek that made my ears bleed. He wasn't cleaning it. He was preparing it.
 
He turned his head toward me. He didn't have eyes. Where the sockets should have been, there were only two deep, puckered scars. His mouth was a jagged horizontal line that didn't seem to have lips.
 
“Shift… change…” he rasped.
 
 
I realized then that the "Slasher" wasn't here to kill me. Not in the traditional sense.
 
He reached into the pocket of his filthy apron and pulled out a stack of order tickets. He began to skewer them onto the order rail, one by one. Thousands of them. They weren't made of paper; they looked like dried skin, translucent and brittle.
 
"What do you want?" I cried, cornered against the industrial fridge.
 
He pointed the rusted spatula at me. “You… work… the… graveyard… shift…”
 
The world around me began to dissolve. The diner walls faded away, replaced by an infinite, dark kitchen that stretched into the void. There were millions of grills, millions of fryers, all manned by silent, tall figures in stained white hats. The smell of burning meat was overwhelming.
 
I looked down at my own hand. My left hand was beginning to turn a sickly, translucent grey. The skin was tightening, the bones underneath shifting and cracking. I felt a white-hot pain in my wrist, as if a saw were slowly working its way through the marrow.
 
"No!" I screamed, swinging the carving knife at the figure.
 
The blade passed through him as if he were made of smoke. He lunged forward, surprisingly fast, and pressed the flat of his spatula hand against my chest.
 
It was the coldest thing I had ever felt. It felt like my heart had been dipped in liquid nitrogen. My vision began to blur, the blue light of the diner replaced by the flickering red glow of a thousand distant grills.
 
 
I woke up on the floor of the diner at 6:00 AM.
 
The sun was coming up over the steel mills, painting the sky in shades of orange and pink. The morning cook, a woman named Sarah, was shaking my shoulder.
 
"Hey! Wake up! Did you pull an all-nighter on the floor?" she asked, laughing. "The place is a mess. What happened to the grill?"
 
I sat up, my head spinning. I looked at the grill. It was covered in a thick, black carbon—as if someone had left a dozen burgers to burn for hours.
 
"I... I must have fallen asleep," I stammered.
 
I looked at my left hand. It was fine. No grey skin, no missing fingers. I breathed a sigh of relief so deep it hurt my lungs. "Just a dream," I whispered. "A hell of a dream."
 
But then I saw Sarah’s face. She wasn't laughing anymore. She was staring at the floor.
 
"What's that?" she pointed.
 
Leading from the front door to the grill was a trail of wet, dark-green sludge. And sitting on the counter, right where the "Slasher" had stood, was a single, rusted spatula. It was bolted to a piece of what looked like human bone.
 
I quit that day. I didn't even go back for my final paycheck. I moved three states away, took a job in an office, and vowed never to step foot in a diner after midnight again.
 
But it’s been six months, and the dreams haven't stopped. Every night, at 3:00 AM, my bedroom lights flicker.
 
Last night, my cell phone rang. It was an unknown number. When I picked up, all I heard was the sound of a blade hitting a cutting board.
 
Thwack. Squelch. Thwack. Squelch.
 
And this morning, when I looked in the mirror, I noticed a small, circular scar forming on my left wrist. It looks like a bolt.
 
The bus is coming back. And I think this time, the order is for me.
