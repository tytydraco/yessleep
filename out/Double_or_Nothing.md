The air in the Kafana Ruski Car in Belgrade was a thick soup of Balkan tobacco and desperation. 

It was 1934, and the Kingdom of Yugoslavia was a powder keg wrapped in silk. King Alexander had been assassinated in Marseille only months prior, and the streets felt like they were holding their breath.

I, however, was breathless for a different reason. I had a "Dead Man’s Hand" in a game of ajnc, and my lungs were burning.

I’ve always played the odds. I survived the Great Retreat through Albania in 1915; I survived the Spanish Flu. I figured I was owed a winning streak. But the man sitting across from me didn't look like he belonged to this century, let alone this decade.

He wore a pristine šajkača cap, but his suit was sharp, black, and smelled faintly of ozone and damp earth—like the air right before a lightning strike on the Avala mountain.

"You’re betting the deed to the timber mill, Marko?" he asked. His voice sounded like gravel grinding in a mortar.

"The mill and the soul of the man who owns it," I spat, pushing the paper into the center of the table. "Deal the last card."

The crowd in the kafana had gone silent. Even the Roma violinists had stopped their mourning songs. 

The stranger didn't use a standard deck. His cards were painted on thin slabs of dried bone.

 The first card: A skeletal soldier.

 The second card: A weeping willow.

 The third card: My face.

"In our folklore," the stranger whispered, leaning over the rakija-stained wood, "we speak of the Vampir, the Drekavac, and the Krsnik. But people forget the Mora—the one who sits on your chest and gambles with your breath while you sleep."

I felt a sudden weight on my ribcage. The room dimmed. The electric lights, a novelty in Belgrade’s center, flickered and died. I looked at my hand. I had a perfect total. The odds of him beating me were mathematically impossible. I had the king, the ace, and the ten.

"I win," I croaked. "The mill stays mine. My debts are cleared."

The stranger smiled, revealing teeth that looked like rows of unpolished marble. He didn't flip a card. Instead, he reached out and touched my wrist. His skin was ice-cold.

"You played against the odds of the living, Marko. But you forgot where you are."

He gestured to the window. Outside, the streets of Belgrade weren't filled with the 1930s Ford Model Bs or the gendarmes in their blue uniforms. The street was a river of gray mist. People were walking backward, their faces featureless masks of wax.

"You didn't lose the game tonight," he said, finally turning over his final card. It was blank—a mirror. I looked into it and saw not my face, but a charred, mangled version of myself.

"You lost the game in 1915," he whispered. "On the frozen slopes of the Prokletije mountains. You died in the snow with the rest of your regiment. This 'life'—the mill, the 1930s, the King’s death—it was just the fever dream of a dying man trying to bargain for one more minute of warmth."

The kafana dissolved. The smell of tobacco turned into the smell of stagnant frost. The "Ruski Car" was gone. I wasn't sitting at a table; I was lying in a trench, my fingers frozen around a deck of wet, discarded playing cards.

The stranger wasn't a gambler. He was the cold.

"Double or nothing?" he asked, as the last of the light left my eyes.
