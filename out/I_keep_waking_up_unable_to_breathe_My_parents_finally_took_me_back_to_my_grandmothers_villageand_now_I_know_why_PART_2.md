Who would have thought things could get worse?

After what I saw last night... and the burnt piece of cloth we found in the morning, I couldn’t relax. I don't know if I was becoming hyperaware or if I was overthinking, but I started noticing things I hadn't paid attention to before. 

Strange things. 

That morning, my grandmother dished out three plates of porridge. 

There were only two of us. 

She carried the third plate to a room that was always locked. I watched carefully this time—she unlocked it, slipped inside, and locked it again when she came out. 

That's when it clicked. 

She had done the same thing the night before with dinner. 

I didn't ask. 

Something told me not to. 

I had woken up very early that day, even though I hadn't slept at all. What confused me was that everything was already done. 

First, it was the breakfast. 

Then the house.

It was spotless. 

Not just clean—perfect. The kind of clean that didn’t feel normal. The floors looked like they could reflect light. 

After we ate, we sat outside. 

That's when I told her something had been bothering me for years. 

The first time I had ever come to this village, I was ten months old.

I know how that sounds. 

But I remember it. 

I told her about the sitting room. 

About how I had been moving around... and then suddenly froze. 

Because I felt something. 

Not just like I was being watched. 

Something stronger. 

Something that made my whole body go still. 

She didn't believe me. 

She just smiled and said it was my mind trying to create a memory from when I was a baby. 

So I dropped it. 

Then I told her about the recurring dream.

The one I had a year ago. 

In it, she was tied to a bed inside a small shack in the woods. She kept begging me to come help her escape from something... something that had deceived the people she loved. 

I told her I used to make my father call her every time I had that dream. 

Just to make sure she was okay. 

This time, she didn't smile. 

She just stared at me. 

Then she said:

"It was just a dream."

Nothing more. 

After that, she said she needed to go into the forest to collect wood for a ceremony.

My initiation. 

I offered to go with her.

She refused. 

Before leaving, she went into her hut and came out with a large divination bag—it was larger than normal.

Then she left me alone. 

I tried calling my parents. 

The signal was terrible, but I kept trying until I found a spot where it worked. 

I called my mother. 

It rang. 

And rang. 

No answer. 

I called my father. 

Nothing. 

No ringing. 

No connection. 

I told myself it was just the network. 

But something about it didn’t feel right. 

I went back inside and lay down. 

At some point, I fell asleep. 

When I woke up, the room felt darker. 

Too dark. 

As I reached under my pillow for my phone, my eyes caught something. 

Someone. 

Standing by the wardrobe. 

I froze. 

It was him. 

The man from outside—my great-uncle. 

But this time... his neck was straight. 

And there was something in his forehead. 

A nail. 

Driven straight through it. 

He didn’t move. 

But his mouth opened. 

"Help." 

His lips didn't move. 

But the voice kept coming. 

"Help." 

"Help."

"Help." 

It got louder. 

Sharper. 

Like it was inside my head. 

I screamed. 

And then...

I woke up. 

My grandmother was standing over me, her hand on my shoulder. 

"You were dreaming," she said. 

She had returned from the forest. 

Later, she called me to eat. 

The table was full. 

Samp.

Chakalaka. 

Golden brown chicken. 

Fresh dumplings. 

The smell filled the entire house. 

For a moment, everything felt... normal. 

I ate like I hadn't eaten in days. 

She served two plates. 

And the third went to that room again. 

I didn't look at it this time. 

After we ate, she took the leftovers to her hut. 

"For the ancestors," she said. 

Outside, I noticed the wood she had brought back. 

It was too much. 

Way too much. 

There was no way she could have carried all of it alone. 

I asked her. 

She dismissed it. 

Said she was used to this life. 

That night, we arranged the wood into a pyramid. 

It was already past seven. 

I reminded her. 

She told me not to worry. 

Whatever she had done in the forest... it was keeping things away. 

For now. 

Then she told me to take my clothes off. 

All of them. 

Something in her voice made it impossible to refuse. 

By the time I looked up...

She had already done the same. 

Before I could say anything, she forced a sour liquid down my throat. 

And just like that...

I lost control. 

My body started moving on its own. 

Dancing. 

Around the fire. 

I had never learned this dance. 

But I know it. 

Perfectly. 

The fire grew higher. 

Higher. 

Higher. 

And then...

We weren't alone anymore. 

Figures moved in the darkness. 

Dancing with us. 

Watching me. 

Then I saw him. 

My great-uncle. 

Standing on the other side of the fire. 

Holding a hammer. 

And a nail. 

Everything stopped. 

The figures turned toward me. 

Then he ran at me. 

I couldn't move. 

I couldn't scream. 

I hit the ground. 

Something held me down. 

Too many hands. 

Too strong. 

He placed the nail on my forehead. 

Raise the hammer....

And then my grandmother stepped in. 

She stood over me, reading from a book wrapped in animal skin. 

Everything went black. 

When I opened my eyes...

I was in the hospital. 

My parents were there.

Crying. 

Smiling. 

And next to me...

was my grandmother. 

My real grandmother. 

She was locked in that room. 

The one I never questioned.

The other women?

She was a witch. 

The one who had been turning people with strong spiritual gifts like me...

into umkhovu. 

Something like my great-uncle. 

I'm alive because someone stronger stopped her. 

The one who initiated her into being a traditional healer. 

But my great-uncle...

is not free. 

It is too late for him. 

And now...

I understand what I am. 

I completed my initiation. 

I'm a traditional healer now. 

And if she ever comes back...

I'll be waiting.
