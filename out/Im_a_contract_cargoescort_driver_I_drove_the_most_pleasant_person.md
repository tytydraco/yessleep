*If you make an irreversible mistake of sufficient magnitude, we will call the Hunter.*

*-Final clause of the Hilltop Contract for Michael F., cargo/escort driver.*

[Previous](https://www.reddit.com/r/nosleep/comments/1s9ptg8/im_a_contract_cargoescort_driver_my_passengers/)

I had come to expect all of my cargo/passengers to be related to some horror. All of mine so far followed this trend. So, when I entered my vehicle, I was somewhat surprised at the well-kept woman in the backseat.

She said she was Sherry L., the exports department lead.

"I know there's some irony to having you export the exporter, but I can't drive!" Her voice was light.

"Where are you from, Michael?" She raised the pitch of her voice at the end of questions.

"Cinnani, about forty-five minutes from Foxglove Hill." I have never been asked this question.

"I was born and raised in Foxglove Hill. Now that I think about it, I've never met someone from Cinnani."

"Good place. I can't think of the last time I heard of a crime."

"Well, that's true for anywhere close to here." I rubbed the steering wheel with my thumbs. I could feel my heartbeat in my ears.

"What was it like growing up in the utopia?" Sherry chuckled. Was I a funny person?

"Oh, the city may be utopia, but the people seldom are. Because everything is in excess of quality, expectations are high. My parents, in fact, were not utopian." I looked away from the rear-view and down to my dashboard.

"Oh, uh, sorry." Sherry laughed, again.

"Relax! They're not dead or anything. Just dicks. It was sorta weird; they didn't care too much about grades like other typical strict parents. They cared much more about the way I answered or observed things." I looked into her eyes from the rear-view mirror as she continued.

"Like my dad would randomly point at something and ask me to describe it. A bluejay? I'd say it was a blue bird. I'd get smacked and told to try again. So I'd say it was a bluejay. Smacked. This would go on until he got sufficiently frustrated and gave me the answer. You know what that was? 'A blue avian appearing of reproductive age, likely nesting. It was feeling stressed as it could not find branches on the ground, or any it could break off the trees.' What a load of shit." I laughed, surprisingly.

"A blue avian, Sherry!" We shared laughter over the ridiculous language of her father. Maybe I was funny.

"When I was a teenager, he only became more picky. Once, we were at a park. He saw someone feeding geese with bits of bread. In an all-too-familiar fashion, he pointed and asked me to describe it. 'An old lady feeding geese?' Smacked. The answer ended up being 'As a subject throws white bread in small chunks onto the ground, the geese respond by flapping their wings and approaching the bread before eating it. They don't stay for longer than fifteen seconds without bread.' I was thirteen, not a bird trainer!" How did her father expect her to get that answer?

"Was it always birds with him?"

"No, but they were the most common. Something like 'their behaviors are different from yet similar enough to people' or whatever." I began shaking my left leg.

"What was the strangest thing he asked you about?" Sherry's smile softened.

"He did ask me about some strange stuff, but it was my mom, actually, who asked me about the strangest. They both were in agreement on this weird training, mind you. Anyway, we were at the museum. Instead of meandering around the newest collection, she rushed me towards an older one that I hadn't seen before. Have you actually toured the exhibition floor?" My face flushed as I shook my head no. She giggled.

"No need to be embarrassed. I guess you wouldn't know it, but this one object was the Indigo Microphone." She shook her fingers and moved her hands like she was displaying a glittery banner. "Creatively named, considering it was an indigo microphone. She had me watch a guest use it before requesting that I describe what I saw. It was just a guy giving a presentation to a group of twenty-five or so. I said as much. Smacked." She clapped her hands.

"The short version was that she tested me for forty-some-odd minutes on this thing. I didn't even get close to the answer she told me. Can you guess what she wanted me to say?" My lips curled upwards on their own.

"That the presenter had to shit the whole time?" We laughed in harmony.

"That may have been true! But, no. 'He was speaking before he turned the microphone on. We heard his voice perfectly clear despite our distance. He said good night at the end, even though it was only noon. There must be consequences for not saying it and/or for turning the microphone on before speaking.' Admittedly, I did notice those things, but didn't think they mattered." The stress in my neck migrated to my forehead.

"It sounds odd. Why would he need to do those things?"

"I don't know, especially because this could've just been a one-off. Mom didn't think so. I think my right cheek is still callused from that lesson." Hurting Sherry was unimaginable.

Conversation had reached a lull. I looked at the GPS. Only six hours remained.

"So, uh, what's your job like? I have no idea what it's like to actually work inside the museum." I broke the silence.

"It's okay. As you could imagine, my parents were less than pleased when I became interested in shipping and management. I spend most of my time putting out fires and writing for the Representative."

"Writing what? Things for him to tell me to do?" She smiled.

"Sometimes. I also manage imports. So info about new exhibits and/or discoveries usually reaches me before anyone else." The face of the man with rusty lips rushed to my attention.

"Ah. Say, you probably know the Rusty Lung?" Sherry's smile faded. Her eyes tensed.

"Yes, that's one of the newer ones. It has a pretty interesting story." The story faded into murmur. I noticed a neon green car had been behind us for quite some time.

More importantly, it was the only other car on this usually busy highway. Its headlights were off.

I drifted to the nearest exit. It followed.

The car turned on a police siren. I pulled over.

"Were you speeding, Michael?" Sherry was worried. For me.

I shook my head. "I've yet to see a neon green police car." Her eyes pinned and lips quivered. A speaker from the car ordered us to leave the vehicle. So we did.

A flash stole my vision. A boom deafened me, but Sherry's screams shook me. My senses overloaded. I fell to my knees.

Sherry was gone. No corpse or blood. In her place was a man wearing a maroon trench coat, holding a purple gun.

He was wrong. His face was human, but not the soul behind it. The skin of his face and hands was too smooth. His breaths were shallow.

He walked towards me, gun still drawn. I kept replaying Sherry's terror.

A leather badge was shoved into my vision. My upper body heated up and the rest went cold. Numbness followed after, shocking my nerves. My breaths were ragged.

It had the crest of Hilltop Museum on it.

Tears burst from my eyes. I broke a rule.

“Foolish.” The mere presence of this man was maddening, but his voice forced my thumb back against itself. Pain flared white-hot. Then came the crack, sharp enough to drag a scream out of me. When I looked down, it was pointing the wrong way.

He glared at me in disapproval.

My body turned against the driver’s door. My head struck it once, twice, then again. Long past when I should have blacked out, I kept hitting until a patch of flesh had been beaten away. With my good hand, I touched the exposed bone. My chest could not decide whether to race or stop.

He kicked me down into the dirt. Sour rain began to fall, burning my bloodshot eyes. His face hovered centimeters from mine.

“Hmm.”

Then he turned and walked back to his car, leaving me in the dust.

I crawled back into the driver’s seat, smearing blood behind me. The mirror showed a broken body, but a man who missed Sherry. I had grown used to seeing her smile behind me. That was what I wanted to miss. I think I just missed the bond.
