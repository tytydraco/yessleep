The Null Line



The mail arrives the way it always does.

Slid under the door while he isn't paying attention.



He picks it up from the floor without thinking. Shuffles through it standing up, still in yesterday's clothes, the city outside doing its usual impression of a city.



Institute of Advanced Physics. On the first envelope. On the second. On the third.

Dr. McGregor.



His name in courier font. Formal. Correct. The kind of correct that has never once felt like recognition.



He sets the envelopes on the desk without opening them.

He already knows what they contain.

More of the same. More funding reviews. More committee language that means nothing has been decided yet and won't be for some time and he should continue as though it has.



He is about to make coffee when he notices the parcel.

It was underneath the letters.



He doesn't remember it being there before he picked them up.

Brown paper. No postage. No return address.

Dr. McGregor. Institute of Advanced Physics.



In handwriting this time.

Small. Careful. The kind of careful that takes effort to look effortless.

He turns it over.



Nothing on the other side.

He should log it. He knows he should log it. Unknown parcels, unverified origin, standard procedure is clear.



He opens it anyway.



Inside the brown paper is a case. Black. Matte. The kind of material that doesn't reflect light so much as decline to engage with it.



Inside the case is the device.

Cold metal in his hands.

Rectangular. Wrong in a way he can't name without weakening it.



He turns it over the way he turned over the parcel. Looking for something. A mark. A manufacturer. A logic he recognises.



Nothing.



The city outside keeps pretending it's whole.

Rain on glass. Light in distance. Movement that thinks it's continuity.



He sets it on the desk next to the unopened letters.

He makes coffee.

He drinks it standing up, watching the device the way you watch something you're trying to catch behaving differently when you're not looking.



It doesn't move.



Of course it doesn't move.

He picks it up again.

His thumb finds the switch.



Click.



The air changes its mind.

Not all at once.

Like it hesitates, then commits.



A point in space locks itself into place.



Exactly one metre away.



Not thrown.

Not placed.

Pinned.



As if something unseen has driven a nail into reality and told it to stay there.



Between his hand and that point — a corridor opens.



Not light.

Not matter.

A condition.



Thin. Absolute. Invisible, except for the way everything near it stops agreeing on what distance means.



The hum arrives.



Sick.



Close.



Like something breathing through a wall that used to be safe.



He is a scientist.

Which means his first instinct is not to run.

His first instinct is to test.



He reaches for the apple on his desk. The one he brought from home three days ago and keeps meaning to eat. He holds it at arm's length.



Positions it at the edge of the corridor and draws it slowly through.

What happens to the apple is not a cut.



A cut would be clean. A cut would give him two halves he could measure and weigh and account for.



What happens instead is quieter than that.



The apple crumples.



Not crushed. Not sliced. It simply stops being held together by anything that agrees with the word apple anymore.



Fast.

Quiet.



Almost disappointing in its brevity.



He stares at what is left of it in his palm. The shape is wrong in the way the device is wrong. Recognisable enough to identify. Wrong enough that identification feels like a mistake.



He notes it. Files it somewhere behind his eyes. Moves on.



Or tries to.



His hand brings it closer before he has consciously decided to do that.



He smells it first.

Nothing. No sweetness. No flesh. Just a faint absence where a smell should be.

He touches it to his lip.



Bitter.



Not sharp. Not chemical. The flavour of something that used to be one thing and has lost the instructions for being it.



He spits.



Wipes his mouth with the back of his hand.



Writes bitter in the margin of the notepad on his desk. Underlines it. Adds a question mark.



And then.



Quietly. Without announcement.



The urge.



Not hunger. Not curiosity. Something below both of those. Something that doesn't consult language before it moves.



He brings it back to his mouth.

He bites.

He swallows.

He stands very still for a moment.



Then he picks the device back up.

He swings it.

At first like he thinks it's meant to be used.



A wide, testing arc through the air.

Half instinct.

Half mistake.



The corridor agrees to the motion.



Not by reacting.

By deciding what was in the way no longer qualifies as stable.



Nothing breaks clean.

Nothing behaves like it should.



It just stops being held together by anything he recognises.



He laughs.

Short. Sharp.



Because it shouldn't work like that.

Because it does.



He swings again.



Slower now.

Listening more than watching.



The hum doesn't change.

It just gets closer when he focuses on it.



Like attention is part of the mechanism.

Like noticing it is permission.

The pin in the air doesn't move.



It sits.



Exactly one metre away.



That's what he keeps returning to.



Not floating.

Not suspended.

Committed.



Like space agreed to hold it and now can't back out.

The corridor between it and his hand feels less like a tool every second.



More like a decision that has already been made and is still happening.



He stops swinging.

Not because he understands it.



Because he wants to see it properly.



He brings it closer.



To his face.



Slowly.



Careful in a way that feels less like caution and more like reverence he doesn't believe in.



The corridor doesn't resist.

It doesn't care about closeness.

It only cares about alignment.



The pin hangs there in the air like an argument that refuses to collapse.



One metre away.



Exactly.



Always exactly.



Now it feels too close for that kind of certainty.

The hum shifts.



Not louder.



Nearer.



Like it has realised where his attention has moved.

Like it prefers it there.

He looks into the space between.

Not through it.



At it.

And the mistake happens quietly.



The more he tries to inspect it, the less it behaves like something being observed.

The more it behaves like something doing the observing back.



Not eyes.

Not intent.

Just structure.



A rule that becomes aware of being examined.

The air between hilt and pin feels thinner now.



Exactly one metre away.



Not less real.

More selective.



Like it is deciding what parts of him qualify to remain consistent.



The hum settles into thought.



Not sound anymore.

Pattern.

He doesn't notice when his grip changes.



Or when his breathing stops matching itself properly.

The room feels slightly misaligned, like everything has shifted a fraction and refused to announce it.



The pin stays perfect.



One metre.



Unmoved.



Unbothered.



There is no step.

No moment he can point at later.

Only the sense that inspection has become participation.

That holding it close enough to understand it has changed what holding means.

The hum doesn't react.

It continues like it always has.



Like it was never separate from this outcome.

The room is quiet now.

Too quiet.

Like it finished explaining itself and no longer needs to be understood.



The device lowers at some point he doesn't remember choosing.



The anchor is still there.

The corridor is still there.

The pin is still there.



Always there.



Exactly.

One.

Metre.

Away.



And he is gone.

It closes.



On the desk, his notepad sits open.



Bitter, it says.

And a question mark.

And nothing after.



Now it sits.



Now it waits.



The letters are still there, unopened.



Dr. McGregor. Institute of Advanced Physics.



The coffee has gone cold.
