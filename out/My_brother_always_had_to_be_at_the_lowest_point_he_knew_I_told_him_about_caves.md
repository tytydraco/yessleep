Have you ever had a rumor made against you? Rumors infect people. Rumors are like snakes, latching onto people, spreading their poison, refusing to let go until their victim subdues. It doesn’t care who’s the victim as long as there’s someone to bite. And it leaves awful scars, serving as a constant reminder of what you’ve done. They can never be erased, no matter how hard you try. I cannot erase what happened to my brother, what I did to him. I miss you, Tommy. I’m sorry.

My brother was a bit odd growing up. For one, he would say things that made no sense, like “the ants remember what we did to them” in the middle of a quiet family dinner and “strawberries scream when we eat them” when our mom asked him why he kept trying to grab his own face in the mirror. He was a bit weird alright. But he was my brother, and I loved him. We used to talk about the strangest things. And although it didn’t always make sense, he always had the most interesting things to say. And I admired that in him. 

But I think everyone knew he wasn’t just another weird kid. Something was seriously off. And it became very apparent to our parents what that was. He always had to remain at the lowest point he knew. I was around four years old when he was born. I don’t remember any of it, but Tommy would scream at the top of his lungs as soon as my parents brought him upstairs.

My mom would describe this odd thing, where Tommy would be normal one second and as soon as she started walking up the stairs, he would cry louder and louder with each step. But as soon as she began going down, he would cry quieter and quieter, until he became silent when she reached the bottom.They realized pretty quickly that they should keep him on the first floor of our house. 

I guess we were lucky enough that our house was located at the lowest point in our town too. But that made other things difficult. We couldn’t bring him anywhere without at least one screaming fit. As he grew older, he became more used to not being at the house all the time, but I could always tell it was difficult for him.

Other than that, everything was normal. We had a normal childhood. Both our parents were a bit conservative though. They knew he wasn’t like me or any other kid. My mom thought Tommy was possessed from time to time. Forced him to see the inside of a church far more than any other kid his age would. When the doctor said he might be on the spectrum, my dad told him that he didn’t believe in such superstitions. Tommy never got the help he needed when he needed it the most. Now I realize he never needed that help against himself.

Everyone knows that kids can be cruel. I had friends, but Tommy always had a hard time finding someone who wouldn’t run away from him. It didn’t help that he hated being anywhere other than our house. I tried to be there for him as much as I could, I really did. But one day, everything changed. When he found out we had a basement.

Our parents tried their best to keep it hidden from him. I think they knew what would happen if he found out about it. My dad put wallpaper over the door, the handle was removable and he always kept it locked. You’d easily miss it if you didn’t know it was there. But I knew, and dad told me that under no circumstance should I ever let my brother know about the basement. He was six years old, I was ten. I remember it clearly. Because I was the one who told him about it.

We were playing hide and seek. I was hiding and he was searching. I always knew when he was close to finding me, because he couldn’t stop laughing. You’d think it was the other way around, but nope, he laughed when he searched. It was the funniest thing. So I knew something was wrong when he suddenly stopped laughing. 

I peeked up from my hiding spot and saw him standing by the door, quietly examining it. I got up and approached him from behind. I grabbed his shoulder and he turned around to face me. 

“Tommy, what’s wrong?” I asked.

“Where does this door lead to?” he said as he pointed to it.

“The basement.”

“Where’s that?”

I realized my mistake very quickly, but there was no going back.. 

“Umm, nowhere.”

“You’re lying”, he said angrily.

“Nuh uh!” 

He grabbed my arm tight and dug his nails into my skin. He was only six but I couldn’t help but feel scared.

“Tell me!” he screamed.

“What the hell are you two doing?!”

Dads words echoed in the narrow hallway, making both me and Tommy jump in our places. Dad came walking over, grabbed both me and Tommy and shoved us into each of our rooms. Later that evening, I could hear my parents arguing in the kitchen. Tommy scratched on my door, carefully opened it and came into my room and crawled into bed with me, and I hugged him tight. 

“Did I do something wrong?” he asked. 

“No, I did”, I said. 

He hugged me tighter and dug his face in my chest. I could feel his eyes begin to water and I hugged him even tighter. I promised to myself that I wouldn’t let anything happen to him. He got up and walked out of my room, but stopped by the basement door. He just stood there, eyeing the door. Dad came over and grabbed him, leading him towards his room.

Something inside of him changed when he found that door. When I told him about the basement. Gone was my quirky, weird brother who always wanted to play with his sister. He became quiet, isolated. Sometimes when I had to go to the bathroom in the middle of the night I would find him there, standing in the dark, hunched up against the door. 

For weeks, day in and day out, he eyed the door leading to the basement. We all feared that he knew the first floor of our house wasn’t the lowest point anymore. And we were correct.m One night I woke up to the sound of scratching against my bedroom door. It was Tommy. He was crying and looked scared. I asked him what was wrong and he whispered back.

“I don’t wanna go down there.”

I grabbed his hand and looked him in the eyes. 

“You don’t have to Tommy. You can stay here with me.”

He looked down to his feet.

“Not anymore.”

He let go of my hand and left my room. He walked slowly past the door and for the first time in weeks, didn’t stop to stare at it. I went to bed and fell asleep, but it wasn’t long before I woke up again. 

A loud bang resonated through the house and made me shoot up from my bed. Again and again, the sounds of something smashing shook the walls of the house. It sounded like someone was breaking into our house and smashing everything in their path. Amongst the violence I gathered the courage to peek through my door to see Tommy throwing himself at the basement door. Our dad came rushing down the stairs and tried to grab him, but he managed to break through the door and went tumbling down the stairs. 

He threw his fragile body so hard against a wooden door he managed to break through. The doctors said he shattered his shoulder, and suffered several bruises and cuts from falling down the stairs. He’s lucky he didn’t break anything in the fall, but he had to stay home for a long time nonetheless to recover. 

Staying in his room wasn’t an option. He would scream so much he broke his vocal chords. Our parents tried their best to keep him from going down to the basement, but they knew it wouldn’t work. Eventually they gave up and he moved in down there. It was cold and gloomy, the cement walls were damp and the air had a strange smell. But Tommy couldn't care, he felt like he was home down there.

After he recovered he was forced to go back to school. But he refused. Dad had to grab Tommy and drag him up the stairs. He screamed and cried for so long. He’d calm down once we got to school, but he became violent. He kicked and punched the other kids. He wanted to be alone and at home, and made no effort in trying to have it any other way.

It wasn’t long before Tommy was homeschooled. He wasn’t himself when he left the basement, and I guess our parents were sick of trying. Mom was a stay at home anyway, but I was forced to help him study after school. We didn’t play as much anymore. Hide and seek was hard in a cramped basement with little to no hiding spots. I tried to be there for him, but I was just a kid too. 

I saw him less and less and we grew distant from each other. I was approaching my angsty teenage years as well, which didn’t help the situation. I only saw him when I had to help him study or when mom and dad forced me to spend time with him. He rarely left the basement and I rarely went down there on my own free will. 

Rumors infect people. Rumors started spreading of my brother. He was always the kid who was alone on the playground, but when people heard he was being homeschooled and living in the basement, rumors spread like an infection. He was called a basement dwelling freak. A monster who lived under our house. I was bullied because I was his sister. Because I thought my brother was being so selfish he couldn’t even eat dinner with his family without making everyone around him feel miserable.

That’s what rumors do. They infect people. And my brother's rumor infected me. I started calling him a basement dwelling freak. I called him a monster. I refused to help him study, even if it meant I was grounded. I didn’t care anymore. I hated him. I hated that I was bullied because of him. I hated that I didn’t have a normal brother.

I knew he always had to be at the lowest point he knew about. But I began to think he was faking it, just doing it for attention. So I wanted to test him. They were having a lecture in history at school, and told us about the many mines and caves surrounding our town. Caves and mines that went under the ground.

I got home that day and told my mom about this new important math chapter we had to learn. She basically shoved me downstairs. I opened the door and slowly made my way down. Tommy was sitting in the middle of the room on the cold stone floor, watching TV. He looked back as he heard me coming down, the stairs creaking and complaining with each step. 

“Mary? What are you doing here?”

“Mom said I need to help you study.”

“Oh, okay. Do you want to play?”

“I’m too old for that.”

“I’m not.”

“I don’t care, just go grab your textbook.”

“Okay”, he said disappointingly. 

He went over to his drawer as I sat down on his bed. He came over and sat down next to me, and asked what we were going to study. 

“History.”

“Cool! What history?”

I looked at him. His eyes lit up and his smile filled the room. He was my brother, just a kid wanting to spend time with his sister like we used to before. But the snake was latched onto me, and the poison was coursing through my veins.

“Caves.”

“What’s that?”

I took a deep breath. 

“They’re like rooms. Under the ground.”

His smile faded from his face as he slowly looked down towards the floor.

“Under the ground?”

“Yeah. There’s places under the ground where you can crawl around and even stand up. Sometimes the rooms are big, sometimes they’re small.”

He didn’t say a word, he just kept looking down. 

“There’s a few around town apparently”, I continued.

He took a shallow breath as his eyes began to water.

“Can you show me?”

“No. I don’t know where they are.”

He looked back up at me with tears running down his cheeks and I regretted coming down here in the first place. 

“Forget I said anything”, I said as I got up and made my way up the stairs. 

Tommy didn’t say anything, he just quietly sobbed for himself. I knew I shouldn’t have told him about the caves, but I couldn’t care any less at the time. I was a stupid teenager worrying more about status than my brother, and I’ll never forgive myself. I just wanted things to change. And they did.

Next morning, dad woke me up as my mom frantically ran around the house. 

“What the hell did you do?!” 

“Dad? Wha- what are you talking about?”

“Tommy’s gone! What did you do?!”

My stomach dropped to the floor. He never left the house anymore. He couldn’t have left, I thought to myself. Then I remembered. The caves. I told dad and mom what I told Tommy the evening before and their faces plunged into a panic. Dad called the cops as my mom became a crying mess. The reality of what I’d done came crashing over me all at once and I begged for my parents forgiveness. 

Multiple search parties were conducted across the span of several weeks. They searched the mines and caves surrounding the town as well, but a lot of them were closed down or too small for any adult to access safely. The ones they did manage to search yielded no result. Tommy was gone.

I became a mess. The first weeks of his disappearance were a blur. Mom and dad didn’t speak with me. They didn’t say it, but I could see it on their faces. It was my fault. And they were right. Countless nights of me lying awake, waiting for Tommy to scratch on my door and come cuddle his big sister, just like we used to do. Countless nights wishing I never told him about the basement, about the caves. I was supposed to protect him. I just wished my brother would come back. One night, he did.

I thought I’d imagined it at first. I heard scratching on my door, but it was coming from the top. I cautiously inched towards it, and slowly turned the knob. And there he was. Tommy. I almost screamed out of excitement but he held his finger up to his mouth. I grabbed him and hugged him tight.

“Tommy! You’re okay! I was so worried.”

“Shhh. We have to be quiet.”

“What do you mean? We have to tell mom and dad you’re home!”

“I found them”, he said.

“What? Found what?”

“The caves. I found them.”

My heart sank. He pulled away from my hug and faced my eyes.

“I live there now.”

“No Tommy. You live here. This is your home.”

“Not anymore.”

He turned around and sprinted out of the house.

“Tommy! Wait!”

I quickly grabbed dads flashlight from a cabinet next to the door and ran after him into the darkness. I wasn’t going to lose him again and I didn’t have any time to wake up our parents. So I followed him. He was faster than me and his small frame grew more and more distant as we ran under the passing streetlights. He veered off to the right, into the dark forest. 

I tried to keep up but I lost sight of him as soon as he disappeared into the forest. But I kept running.

“Tommy! Please come back! Don’t leave me! Tommy!”

The tree's dark figures surrounded me. Their branches became arms, grasping at me, ripping and tearing at my clothes. I ran, until my lungs screamed and my heart pounded out of my chest. I ran until I tripped and twisted my ankle. I quickly got up but fell down again. I couldn’t move. I yelled out for him.

“Tommy! Where are you?!”

Nothing. The forest was as quiet as it always was. Not a single sound was heard except for the leaves rustling in the wind. And a whisper. Soft, and muted. Coming from within the density of the trees.

“I’m here.”

I reached for the flashlight and shined it towards the source of the whisper. I couldn’t see him, but I knew I heard him. I began crawling towards it. It was Tommy, it had to be him. I was gonna bring him home. 

“Keep going”, the whisper said eagerly.

“Tommy! I can’t see you. Where are you?”

“I’m right here. Just keep going.”

I kept crawling through the thick bushes and shrubs until my flashlight met a solid object. I couldn’t make sense of it at first, but as I got closer I could make out a large cliff wall, stretching up towards the night sky. In front of me was a hole in the wall, a dark pit disappearing into the earth. I aimed my flashlight inside, but I couldn’t make out an end. Tommy's whisper echoed from inside.

“Mary. It’s me. Keep going.”

I took a deep breath as I crawled inside. The opening was way smaller than I anticipated and I had to squeeze my way inside. With every push I could feel the rocky surfaces scratch and scrape at my knees and elbows. Once I got my feet in, the entrance started to open and I could use my arms to pull me deeper. My twisted ankle pulsated with pain but I fought the tears and pushed myself inside. 

The entrance opened up even more and I could eventually crawl on my knees. My flashlight began to flicker and I had to shake it every now and then to keep it on. The cave descended just gradually enough to where I could still crawl but had to brace myself so I wouldn’t slide down. My hands strained against the floor of the cave and I could feel the skin on my palms start to peel back.

“Mary. Keeeeeep going.”

Tommy’s words reflected off of the walls. But they sounded so wrong, and intentionally drawn out. It sounded like he was in pain, which only made me crawl faster. I got to a point in the cave that leveled out and opened up slightly, to where I could shift my position and sit up. 

I scanned my surroundings. The opening diverged into two different paths ahead and I felt the panic wash over me. I raised my flashlight in front of me and tried crawling forward to get a better look, but I quickly realized my ankle was still twisted. The pain crawled up my leg and I could barely lift my foot without wanting to scream. I sat back down again and didn’t even try to hold back the tears. 

“Tommy, please. Come back. I miss you”, I whispered into the dark. 

The flashlight flickered again and shut off. I have never experienced the same kind of darkness again as I did inside that cave. The sound of my crying filled the earth. I did this. I made Tommy disappear. I let the snake's poison wither away my soul. 

“Mary.”

“Tommy? Tommy, where are you?!” I yelled as I began hitting my flashlight. 

“I’mmmmm righttttt here”, he said as the flashlight flickered on.  

I aimed it ahead of me towards one of the paths. It curved down and I could see half of Tommy's head peeking up. His pupils were the size of his eyes and his long dark hair looked wet, hanging down on his face. His skin was white as chalk.

“Maryyy. Come with me. I misssss you ttttoo.”

“I can’t. You have to come home with me. It isn’t safe here!”

“Mmmmary. I am home.”

“Please Tommy! I promised to keep you safe.”

“Not to me”, he said with a guttural and hateful voice.

“I miss you so much Tommy.”

“Why did you leave me?”

I couldn’t answer. I left him in that basement to rot. I only have myself to blame. I could have been there for him. I was the one he trusted. He was my brother and I was his world. 

“I didn’t mean to! I’m sorry. I’m so sorry.”

The flashlight flickered off again and the cave went deafeningly silent. I hit it against my hand, when the sounds of cracking bones and joints emerged from where Tommy was made me stop. I held my breath as I tried to make my eyes adjust to the darkness, but I could barely see my own nose. Eventually the flashlight flickered on again and I pointed it towards Tommy. 

His body was emerging from the pit and I could see his entire face. His jaw hung down to his neck and his mouth was wide open, like he was locked in a constant scream. His arms were incredibly long and slender, reaching out in front of him, twisting unnaturally as he grabbed the walls of the cave, pulling himself towards me. His hands were way too big for his thin arms and his nails dug into the rock.

“Mmmmmarrryyy. Don’t leave mmmmme againnn.”

His mouth didn’t move. The joints in his arms cracked and bent with every pull, as he struggled to move in the narrow cave, slowly getting closer and closer to me. 

Instinct took over as I dropped my flashlight and turned around and began climbing upwards the way I came, frantically pulling at the sharp rocks that painted the facade of my purgatory. The ascent tore at my body. I kept slipping and sliding down, scraping my hands and knees and forced myself to brace against the rocky interior even harder.

“Mmmmaryyy. Come with meeeee.”

Tommy’s words grew closer and closer. Strips of flesh peeled off from every part of my body as I panicked and desperately pushed and pulled my body upwards. I couldn’t tell how far I managed to climb before Tommy’s hand grabbed me. His nails dug into my calf as he began pulling me down. I kicked and screamed as I slowly descended. 

Luckily, he grabbed my bad leg with my twisted ankle. I managed to kick his hand with my other leg hard enough for him to let go. I didn’t waste any time and hastily crawled upwards. I reached a part where the cave narrowed and I could see a silhouette of an opening as the  moonlight shined through the trees. The entrance. The narrow push.

I pushed with both my legs, ignoring the unendurable pain. The walls tightened around me and pressed against my body. Sounds of twisting bones and joints emerged from behind.

“Mmmmmaaaaarrryyyyy.”

My hands reached the outside. I grabbed the sides of the entrance and pulled as hard as I could. My vision became blurry as the cold night wind whipped against my face. I braced my arms on each side and pushed. After what felt like hours, I emerged from the cave and landed on the soft forest grass floor. I turned my body around and gazed up at the thick tree branches covering the night sky. 

My breath turned shallow and short as I felt my eyelids heavily weighing down. The stillness of the night surrounded me as I began to pass out. Twisting. Cracking. Bones and joints twisting and aching. Desperate whispers emerging from the cave. Closer and closer and closer and closer.

“I founnnnd them. Mmmmaarrryyy. I foooounnnnd themmmm.”

My eyelids shot up as Tommy’s voice traveled through the air. I looked up towards the entrance and saw Tommy peeking up. His arms stretched in front of him and reached for me. I inched backwards as fast as I could until I hit a tree. I couldn’t move anymore. I couldn’t stand up. My body was giving up. Time slowed down. I closed my eyes as Tommy’s hands stretched further and further, closer and closer.

But nothing happened. He couldn’t reach me. I opened my eyes. His arms groaned and twisted as his hands slammed on the ground, trying to grab my feet, just inches away from his grip. His hands stopped moving and I looked up towards Tommy in the entrance. He didn’t say anything. He just stared at me. 

There was no goodbye. There was no final way of saying he loved me and I loved him. There was no gesture to indicate Tommy was still in there, in the husk of whatever was in that cave. Maybe the fact he didn’t get out to grab me was close enough. But I doubt it. He retracted his arms into the cave. He kept staring at me for what felt like an eternity until he submerged into the earth, gone forever.  

I woke up in the hospital sometime after. I can’t remember how much time had passed. My mom was sitting beside me, crying her eyes out as dad paced back and forth. Everything ached and hurt. Some days after waking up, cops came in to take a statement. I told them everything.

I told them how we used to play hide and seek. How he used to laugh when he was searching even though it should have been the other way around. I told them about the strange things he used to say that made no sense. I told them about his condition.  How I wanted him gone. How I hated that he wasn’t normal. How much I hated myself for betraying my brother. I told them about what I said to him about the caves. How I leapt into the night and searched desperately for my brother. 

But I didn’t tell them about what I saw in there. I never told anyone. The least I could do was keep the memory of my brother as the eccentric and quirky kid he always was when we grew up. Because I promised I would protect him.

But I’m older now. I’ve processed everything that happened to me. Realised I needed saving too, realised I needed protection. My story and Tommy’s story deserved to be told. For every sister and brother out there doubting themselves. Look out for each other.

I never went back to look for him. I always imagined him sinking deeper and deeper into the earth, crawling away from the world above. I suppose he’s where he belongs. Maybe he did find something there. Or maybe something finally found him.
