The house appeared one day, though I can’t say when. It wasn’t like it was built overnight, or like it was hidden in the crook of a forgotten bend of street. It was as if it had always been there. It was old and overgrown with dark vines and leaves that crusted its brick frame. I remember it feeling off from the moment I turned down my street. It didn’t replace anything or end up wedged in a space that already existed. My street was longer than usual, with a more pronounced bend. There were more trees, and the house was nestled among them like a rock in tall grass.

As you can probably guess from the title, it was right up my alley. I love exploring forgotten places and have ever since I was in high school. You see some freaky shit in this hobby, and the adrenaline rush you feel when you’re in the dark cavity of some abandoned building cannot be understated. I’ve seen missile silos from the Cold War, plantation houses in the middle of nowhere, and found a damp room in a hospital littered with the limbs and appendages of dolls from every corner of the uncanny valley.

You experience a fair deal of risk, too. I’ve never been arrested, but some buddies of mine have, and the fines almost put them on the streets. That being said, legal troubles are the safer things to manage when roving around haunts like mine. Homeless squatters have threatened to cut me for encroaching, and I once got chased by a pack of stray dogs for getting too close to some pups. Out of all of them, though, the worst by far are traps.

Teenage shit-heads and sadistic wackos love leaving booby traps in places. Typically, it’s nothing too sophisticated, but the danger and malice that go into them stay with you. The one trap I still remember to this day was a simple tripwire someone placed over a set of stairs leading down into a basement. It was the smallest string of a guitar, stretched ankle high over the top step. I cut it down with my pocket knife and ventured in to see if anyone’s remains were at its base. All I found was a dry, dark splotch on the floor near the bottom step.

What I’m trying to say is, I’ve been through some messed-up scenarios that most people would probably see a shrink for, and that many more would call me insane for enjoying the thrill of. Thing was, I couldn’t help myself. I loved the thrill. I loved the rush of the unknown and the adrenaline pumping through my veins. I hadn’t found a place that was too isolated or too sketchy for me to explore until I found that house. I haven’t been the same since I got out of it, and it’s only with a bottle of liquid courage I can find the strength to type this out. I’ll do my best.

I questioned everyone in my neighborhood, but nobody knew a thing about it. They didn’t even think it was strange. They’d simply tell me they hadn’t noticed it and that it must’ve been unearthed from some recent construction work. I didn’t buy that for a second, but when it came down to brass tax I didn’t really have an idea what I was theorizing. They were being rational. A normal person would see a house like that and come up with an explanation that blamed their own senses. I couldn’t do that. Against every sane thought, I knew that the house hadn’t always been there, nor the woods around it. 

The woods weren’t some mystery, technically. The neighborhood had trees and brush all over it, but this patch was still odd. It was as if someone cut a piece of paper in two, then connected the pieces with a different piece of paper. That’s probably a shit analogy, but the point is, it was like the land was spliced in. The grass in that spot was slightly different in hue, and the bark on the trees was ever so coarser than the others around them. It was like Photoshop but IRL. I tried taking a photo with my phone, but none of the differences I noticed with my eye were visible on camera.

I got creative and started asking the city for blueprints of my area. Sure enough, there it’d be on the maps. I tried satellite imaging as well, and every timestamp I checked showed the same image. It had always been there, and I had no memory of it. 

Once I’d scrounged up everything I could find- which wasn’t much, besides the address and the name of the dude who last lived there (Frank or something)- I grabbed my bug-out bag, some flashlights, food, and boots, then headed for the house. Thankfully, my street had little to no foot traffic or police presence, so getting charged with breaking-and-entering was the last thing on my mind. I made it to the house without being seen and by dusk was standing at the precipice of its decrepit splendor.

The vines looked almost black in the dying light, and the little barbs that ran along their spines stood almost bone white. I’d never seen a plant like that before, but I wasn’t and won't ever be a plant-ologist- or whatever the term is- so I pushed on. A stale breeze kissed my cheeks as I stepped up to the front door and tried the doorknob. I didn’t know why then, but the hairs on my neck stood up. I blamed the excitement of the situation at first, but I know now what I should’ve admitted then. 

I felt like I was being watched. Something, somewhere, had eyes on me that I couldn’t see. I should’ve turned back, run away, and never looked down that street again, but I didn’t. I turned the doorknob and went inside, like an insect willingly flying into a spiderweb. I didn’t even question why a house like that would be unlocked. I just went inside, and I’m still dealing with the consequences.

The house was not at all what I expected. It was dirty and overgrown with those same black vines, but it didn’t look like a standard empty home. There was furniture, for one. An old, dirty couch sat in a living room down the hall from a dining room populated with chairs and a large wooden table. A set of stairs covered in those same black vines led into a second-level cloaked in shadow, and the faint silhouette of a sink down the hall led me to believe a kitchen was in the back, facing the yard. There was a television, covered in grime and dust, and even a coffee table with paperbacks and magazines stacked haphazardly on its surface. 

This immediately struck me as strange. Most abandoned places weren’t furnished like this. When people leave, they take their stuff- especially if it's a TV or couch. The books weren’t anything special, but they were still personal belongings. I went to the kitchen and, sure enough, there were dishes still in the sink. Those same vines covered the backyard window like a web and made the trees look like a wall of dark bark. I turned on my flashlight, but it didn’t help illuminate anything out the window. The back door was locked, so I looped around and continued back to the stairs. I listened to my heart beat the entire way. 

It was like someone had left the place in a hurry. They hadn’t taken anything with them. The table and chairs of the dining room looked nice, and all of the dishes in the room’s china hutch were pristine behind graying glass. I kept my hand near the knife on my belt as I climbed the staircase and into the narrow halls above.

There were only three rooms on the second level. There was a coat closet, complete with some men’s jackets long since gobbled to hell by moths, a bathroom that felt half the size of that closet, and then a master bedroom. There was still a bed with sheets and pillows, a dresser, a mirror, and a window covered in - you guessed it- vines. It was now dark, and my flashlight was my only source of light. I searched through the dressers and found clothes, completely folded and undisturbed. I was starting to get more and more freaked out, and wondered if this house truly was abandoned when my light fell upon a picture frame on the desk.

In the photo was a man, probably in his early thirties, smiling with his arm around a blonde woman. She was leaning into him, placing a tender hand against his chest, showing off a ring. They seemed happy, at first glance.

However, the longer I stared at them, trying to picture them living and going about their lives in that house, the more uneasy I felt. Something was wrong with them, despite the smiles and loving stances they held, and it flickered in my brain like an optical illusion. I told myself it was a trick of the light- a glare or scratch on the frame, but I couldn’t unsee it.

For a split second, I swear it looked like the woman had no face.

I heard a loud and long creek that was probably the house against the wind, but it still sent me rushing down the stairs and back out into the main level. I was starting to panic and was about to rush out the door, but something stopped me. 

Off to the right of the stairs, cloaked by the growing shadows of dusk, was another door. That alone didn’t make me freeze, and my instincts of self-preservation definitely hadn’t disappeared by that point, but another sensation overruled my desires.

This door, a door I hadn’t seen before, that I was sure wasn’t there when I walked in, sat slightly ajar, cracked as if in a wicked smile. It was darker than black, and as I slowly raised my light, I beheld more and more of those black, thorny vines creeping from its maw. I should have left. I should have gone to the front door and run home before whoever or whatever the hell lived there showed up. 

I didn’t, though. I’m too curious for my own good, and I paid for it. The hell I live in now was paid for with the creak of a door hinge. 

I looked into the entryway and discovered another set of stairs that led further down into the house. They weren’t ugly basement stairs either. They were identical to the set I’d come from. Old hardwood that used to have a varnish finish, with a fine wall railing along one side of the wall. 

I know I’m mentioning them a lot, but I cannot stress enough how seriously heavy and thick these vine patches were. Down the steps, it looked like they grew thicker, the leaves bigger, and the thorns…. I wish I had understood how serious those things were. Instead of turning back, I took out a small machete I kept in my bag and proceeded downward into the dark. My hands trembled as I went.

In the basement, or what I assumed was the basement, I found a scene straight out of Jumanji. The entire room was nothing but kudzu, ferns, and those vines. They encrusted everything, leaving only the silhouettes of furniture. An armchair, a table, a counter, etc. However, in the middle of the room was a mass I couldn’t identify. I walked over to it and began to pick at it with my machete. I gave it a kick, and in the process of doing so, got a thorn stuck in my boot. It didn’t stab me, but that thing was fucking huge. I once got stuck by a cactus while scoping out a Cold War government facility in Arizona, and that thing was easily as sharp as a pin. This thorn was the size of a fucking toothpick. It looked more like bone than plant and took a good bit of elbow grease to wrench from my foot. I was lucky I didn’t get stuck, but I wasn’t lucky enough to have common sense. 

I started hacking through the brush and kicking away what clumps I could with my boot. I was half convinced I was unearthing nothing but decrepit vegetation, but then my machete struck something solid. I focused my flashlight, expecting a rock or some other random item, but I wasn’t that lucky.

I screamed and almost stumbled to the ground. I’m glad I remembered to mind the thorns, or my injuries would have been much worse. I stood with trembling legs as my breathing became sporadic. I’d dropped the flashlight, leaving only a slice of white to fill the room around me. I thought about making for the stairs, but I was petrified. I’d never been so scared in my life. The closest thing I could even compare it to was when I’d crawled out of a crash my senior year, the sole survivor of a drunk driver. The shock had rendered me speechless for two hours before the cops finally managed to get me to stutter out a statement. This fear was different. It was a seed that’d lodged itself in my stomach and grown into a desire to never sleep again. 

Through all of my theories about who had or did live in that house, about where they’d gone or when they were coming back, I’d neglected a serious and terrifying possibility. As I stared into the shadows of a face with sunken, grey cheeks and hollow eyes sprouting with black leaves, the answer became clear. 

He never left. 

In some ways, I don’t think I did either.

I need another drink. I’ll update when my hands can stop shaking.
