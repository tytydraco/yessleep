I've spent six years being paid to listen to people.

Not in the way a therapist does, or a bartender, or a patient spouse. I was actually a qualitative researcher at an international development organization. It was a job where they made you interview people as part of research projects. The kind of job where you attain skills that not many possess. You learn to hear the pause before an answer. The word someone reaches for and then abandons. The thing they mean that isn't the thing they said.

I was good at it. In fact, it was the only thing I was ever genuinely good at.

Then the funding got cancelled. It had something to do with the USAID shutting down its operations. All employees received two weeks' notice. The office closed on a Friday. I handed in my access card and walked to my car. I sat there for forty minutes without starting the engine.

I didn’t tell my family. My younger brother's tuition was due at the end of the month. My mother’s next chemotherapy session was scheduled the week after. My quadriplegic father had just gotten a new caregiver.

I am the person who pays for all of it. And I will continue to be that person no matter what.

So I left the house every morning at the same time I always did, laptop bag over my shoulder. I applied for jobs between seven and nine AM at a coffee shop three blocks from the house. The applications went into the void. This kept happening for four months.

That's when I found the app.

If you are a qualitative researcher like I am, you know the one I’m talking about.

It looked legitimate because it was legitimate. It was an audio transcription platform. The rules were simple- clients upload audio files, freelancers like myself transcribe the audio and send it as a formatted document. Payment is made per minute of audio processed. The interface was clean. The payment processing was smooth.

I signed up in under ten minutes and had my first batch of files by the following morning.

The first one was a procurement meeting. The second one was a university lecture on urban water systems. The third one was someone's voice memo to themselves about a grocery list, spoken aloud with the gravity of a man dictating his will.

I was fast. Faster than the platform expected, I think. Six years of listening professionally means your ear is trained to catch words cleanly, to hear through crosstalk and background noise, to fill gaps without fabricating. My accuracy rating after the first week was 98.4%. I remember that number exactly because it was the first number in four months that made me feel like myself.

The pay was modest. Enough to cover the tuition and pay for the caregiver that month. I told myself it was temporary. I told myself I was still looking. I told myself it’s just a thing I was doing for money.

Then, on a Tuesday morning, the app received an update. I now noticed a new section in my account dashboard. It was a separate file queue with a small lock icon that was no longer locked. The queue was labelled ‘*Premium Assignments*’ .

The pay rate was five times higher. Enough to cover my mother’s chemotherapy session and then some.

I accepted the first file without asking any questions.

The early files from the new tier were odd, but not wrong.

The first one was a meeting where the agenda was never stated and nobody introduced themselves.

The second one was a lecture in a strange accent I couldn't identify. I replayed certain passages four, five, six times. I never understood the full meaning. Nevertheless, I transcribed phonetically and submitted. The rating came back perfect.

The third one was an audio track that was just a room. A room where no one was speaking at all. It sounded like someone’s living room- the ambient hum of a refrigerator, a distant vehicle, the particular silence of a space where a person lives. Fourteen minutes of it. No voice. I transcribed the silence as *\[no audible speech\]* and submitted it. I received triple my usual rate within the hour.

I told myself there were research applications I wasn't privy to. Acoustic studies. Environmental monitoring. I had signed NDAs before. I understood that not understanding the purpose was sometimes part of the job.

I was getting paid, and that was all that mattered. It was just a thing I was doing for money.

The next assignment was… strange.

It was nine minutes of a door opening and closing. The same door every time. I could tell by the catch in the hinge at the three-quarter mark, a small resistance and then release. Open. Closed. Open. Closed. Methodical, almost. Like someone counting. The pay for this single file was more than I had made in my best week on the platform.

I transcribed it. *\[Door opening. Door closing. Repeated.\]* Submitted it. I spent the night wondering what it was and didn't sleep well.

The next assignment was a child's voice counting backwards. I couldn't determine the starting number. Every time I tried to type it my fingers produced something adjacent. A near-miss, a number that felt wrong in a way I couldn't articulate, but never the actual number. I left the field blank and wrote *\[numeral unclear\] .* 

I submitted the file. Received my payment, along with another perfect rating.

I was on File 17 when I told myself I should stop.

But the pay was good. Too good. Just a little more, I thought, and I will have enough cash to cover all expenses for the next three months.

It wasn’t a career path. It was just a temporary gig. It was just a thing I was doing for money.

I played the file. I recognized the audio.

It was my own voice.

I knew it immediately, of course. You always know your own voice even when you hate the way it sounds recorded. This was mine. Recorded in my apartment, in the room I sleep in, in the particular acoustic texture of a space I have lived in for two years.

I checked the timestamp.

3:14 AM.

I had been asleep at 3:14 AM. I am certain of this because I have kept careful record of my sleep since the insomnia started, since the office closed, since the depression hit. I was asleep. The recording was made without my knowledge, in my bedroom, while I was asleep.

The file was four minutes and eleven seconds long.

In it, I said my own name. Then my mother's name. Then my brother's name. Then the amount of his tuition. Then the date it was due.

Then silence for the remaining three minutes.

I sat with the file open for a long time.

I remember feeling violated. I remember searching the room for hidden audio devices. I remember staying awake the whole night.

I also remember looking at how much the assignment was worth. I remember thinking about my mother’s cancer, about how it was now spreading to other organs. I remember thinking about how much advanced radiotherapy was going to cost…

I transcribed it. Every word, every name, every figure. I formatted it correctly with timestamps, speaker labels, paragraph breaks. I corrected the one instance where my sleeping voice had slurred a syllable. I submitted it.

The payment cleared in four minutes.

The app updated again. So did my profile.

It now said *Senior Listener*. Accuracy rate 100%.

Which brings me to File 20. The last file of the queue. I have opened it.

I've been sitting here for ten minutes looking at the metadata.

The location is my bedroom.

The timestamp is 30 minutes from now.

I kept reading:

*Duration: UNKNOWN.*

*Number of speakers: 2.*

I don't know what that means. I've stopped trying to figure out what things mean.

What I know is this: I needed the money for the procurement meeting. I needed it for the seminar recordings. I needed it for every file I've cleaned up and timestamped and submitted since the office closed. I needed it then and I need it now.

I know this does not feel right. But I also know that the amount will be good, because the amount is always good when the file is this kind of wrong.

I don’t know what will transpire 30 minutes now. I don’t know who the second speaker is. I just know that I had to write it down somewhere.

If I don't come back to update this, you'll know how it went.

Don’t try to talk me out of it.

It’s just one of those decisions I made for the sake of my family.

Just one of those things that I do for money...

I’m putting the headphones on.
