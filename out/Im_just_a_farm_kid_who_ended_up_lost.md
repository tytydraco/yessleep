TW: Mental health, suicide, emotional breakdown

Just call me Jack, sweet seventeen, hard working, Tennessee born and raised, I'm that stereotypical farm kid, but something happened which changed my otherwise typical life, something that, in the moment felt unreal, and now only exists in short, dream-like fragments.

I remember driving, my 1985 Ford F-350 crew cab, something dependable, trustworthy even, papaws truck is always the best.

I'd headed out quick for the Feed & General Store, a clear blue sky arced above and that familiar heat that made the dust in the truck cab seem alive.

I had been working hard that morning, hence the lack of a shirt beneath my overalls and the straw hat, as I drove down the road between the yellow line and the fields stretching as far as the eye could see, the same telephone poles I'd known for years on this route, window open, listening to one of my old Alan Jackson cassettes.

I had noticed some fog forming, which felt off for the time of year, as I drove, the fog became thicker.

You know when you've been driving in fog and you realize you actually can't see a thing? that's what happened, I could barely see in front of the truck.

I stopped, judging where the side of the road should  be, I felt a gust of wind strong enough to tilt the truck.

The temperature dropped, the cassette warped and static came through the speakers, the last of the road lines vanished before me.

There came a sound I can't quite explain, a growing sound, at first I thought it was the truck, but the instrument cluster seemed normal, no lights, no overheating.

As quickly as it came, it stopped, jarringly.

The fog followed suit, separating in two halves like the curtain on a stage.

Some weird weather event? a random squall on such a nice day? my confusion only increased when the fog cleared.

I was no longer on that road, I found myself on a rather narrow road, my trucks mirror already in the bushes that were on either side.

The sky was overcast, a thick blanket of grey above.

It was cold, I rolled up the window and retrieved a blanket I'd kept for emergencies.

I drove the narrow road as best I could, other cars I hadn't seen before squeezed past, some honking their horns, some drivers displaying some interesting gestures, I had never had this before for merely driving.

The road widened, a sign before me, white with black lettering.

'Welcome to Kent, Please drive carefully'

I resisted the urge to slam the brakes, why was I in England? did I misread something to do with Kentucky? that's when I noticed the cars had yellow licence plates with black lettering, i'd seen those plates before.

In a postcard of the United Kingdom.

I had to stop, how did I end up in England? Now safely pulled over on what I saw as the wrong side of the road, I pulled out my phone, No Service, and when I opened the maps app what I saw further confirmed my fear.
That little blue pin was in southeast England.

I drove until I stopped outside of some establishment, a pub, a saloon, or a restaurant I couldn't really tell.

As I got out some woman with a thick British accent started laughing, I overheard something about being dressed as a baby, or a redneck, I ignored it.

I could feel eyes upon me as I asked for a glass for water, and exactly where I was.

'A bit cold for that outfit mate! you must be from the country music event, this is a pub and BNB in Snodland' The main said, sneering.

'I come from America, TN' I said

'Yea, Tonbridge, nice bit mate' He said

I didn't have the energy nor the courage to even say 'Tennessee'

As I lay in a room upstairs that night, looking through the window at my truck in the parking lot, the truck that everyone, for some reason, was obsessed with, and hated with a fiery passion, I'll spare you the colorful language which I heard about it and me.

I did meet one woman, she seemed to be half-interested, half-concerned, we spoke for hours about what life was like in America, she helped keep me calm, but couldn't prepare me for what was to come.

A day later I was out driving again, trying to formulate a plan to get me and my truck out of here, and how it all happened in the first place.

Blue flashing lights behind me, pulled up by a Battenberg Cruiser.

I rolled the window down for the officer, the first thing I'd noticed was the complete absence of a firearm.

'The reason I have pulled you over is that your number plate is not compliant to MOT standards and even if it was, it's not of the correct format, as it returns invalid in our system, can you please take the show plates off and put your legal plates on and I'll let you go after looking at your licence?'

The officers words filled me with fear

'Show plates?' I said

'I understand this must be a show vehicle, the American number plate is nice but not road legal'

'It's not a show truck' I said

'I don't know why I am here'

Breathalyzer, drug wipe, the whole deal, all come back negative, the cop regarded my drivers licence as being fake.

'I don't know what's going on' he said

'But between the way you are dressed, are acting, the vehicle and the licence, I have no choice but for you to come with us'

I had never broken the law in seventeen years, being arrested came as a shock, the time I spent in custody was long, they searched me, taking away what little I had, I refused to give them the pin code for my phone.

They regarded me as not of sound mind, it was a hard few days, between people who identified themselves as social services, law enforcement officers and various mental health professionals, runaway, fake licences, I'm fairly sure the embassy got involved.

Doctors who interviewed me, interrogated me, I told the truth, nothing but the truth.

They told me I was delusional, throwing words around like fugue state, dissociation.

I begged for a phone, they denied, I was stuck, trapped in a world I didn't know, a world I wasn't supposed to be in.
Cold and lonely and scary, I couldn't even understand half of what they were saying, most terrifyingly it was always about me, at times I didn't know who I was, other times I thought I was dreaming.

Five fingers every time, that hurt me a lot, several times I was checked over by English paramedics that called themselves the NHS, after banging on the door crying out that I was in pain and felt like I was going to die.

Some time after, I can't tell how long after, I ended up in an asylum, written down as nothing more than a delusional person who thinks they were American, I could not stand it.

I grew increasingly homesick over the next few days, thoughts of home, little sleep, barely eating.

In truth, I was not of sound mind, but not for the reason they thought.

I spent most of my time mourning the life I had lost, crying over the truck, my dog, Scout sitting on the porch at home next to an empty rocking chair.

It's not a surprise that I bolted when they left the door open, just like my very own horse, I ran free as can be in a world unfamiliar, I stole my overalls back and ran, the truck keys were still in the pocket, nearly everything was.

I ran and ran, fearing being captured yet again, I saw parked up outside a police station a glimmer of hope.

A massive truck.

My truck.

With shaking hands I unlocked it and peeled off into the South London night.

I don't know for how long I drove, I drove until I was out in the country, away from civilization, away from everything.

My mind flashed back to thoughts of home, and how I was a wanted man in a foreign country, I thought of all the missing person reports, papaw driving up and down those country roads while crying, my mama doing the same, all I could hear was a V8 and an Alan Jackson record, only a little taste of what home was like, as I looked up at the stars, a cloud rolling in obscuring nearly all of what I could see of the heavens above.

I realised I was crying myself as I reached underneath the seat of the truck, it was still there, they hadn't searched it?

Chrome plated, heavy in the hand, a beautiful finish, papaw gave it to me because the world is a dangerous place, dangerous animals, dangerous people.

I looked at my hands, rough from twelve years of work, I wondered if I had died somewhere on that road in Tennessee, and what effect dying again could have.

I went to cock the lever.

I was interrupted by a harsh knocking on the window, I hid the revolver in fear.

Familiar accents shouting 'Drop the weapon!' 

Guns drawn all around me

I held both my hands up in the air and followed their orders, and I had noticed something I hadn't.

I recognized those roads, the yellow line, those telephone poles, those fields and those wooden fences.

I was back home, I was crying before the officers had even stopped shouting.

I had to explain everything, according to the officer, he was patrolling this route when he saw what he thought was an abandoned truck.

They confirmed me as a missing person, my phone blew up, now connecting to the network and receiving notification after notification.

Text Messages, WhatsApp messages

'Where are you?'

'When are you getting back?'

'Please call we're getting worried'

Three sob emojis in a row and 'RIP Jack'

Alongside 54 missed calls.

No one believed my story, they thought I fell asleep at the wheel, or was maybe doing drugs, the tests denied that.

As soon as I was free I drove back home, past the telephone poles with 'MISSING' posters baring my face, that was hard to see, country music still playing on the stereo, the warm night air outside like a comforting blanket.

I barely made it out of my truck before I jumped into the arms of my mama, papa, my papaw, even scout came running towards me like a freight train.

I just wanted to sit, tears streaming down my face.

They questioned me as I sat on the rocking chair, I couldn't quite explain what had happened, they wouldn't believe me, I just said I got lost, spent some time in another state.

As glad as I am to be back, I keep having nightmares of fog lately, I don't like them, I wake up, expecting to be in an English asylum, only to be relieved at the sight of a Tennessee farmhouse bedroom.
