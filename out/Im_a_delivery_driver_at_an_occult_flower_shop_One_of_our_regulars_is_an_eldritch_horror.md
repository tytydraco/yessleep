I suppose it's time I talked about The Mold. I mentioned The Mold briefly in my [first post](https://www.reddit.com/r/nosleep/s/eIjEZNmE89) here, but I didn't elaborate. Whenever a customer requests a return or exchange, the recipient of the bouquet has usually already held onto it long enough that all the flowers are wilted by the time I'm able to pick it up. Occasionally we'll get a well-maintained one with flowers that we can salvage, but for the most part, people just leave them out for us on their porch, letting them cook to a crisp in the desert sun. An ordinary flower shop would throw the wilted flowers away. Our shop sends them to The Mold.



The first time I encountered The Mold was a few weeks into the job. Anna had handed me my route sheet, and when I read it over, two of the orders looked different from the rest. Usually they contained a description of what the customer ordered—rose bouquet, best wishes bouquet, fleshbloom centerpiece, and so on—but the first one didn't describe a bouquet. It was an exchange order, from a woman named Dolores B. The delivery instructions described the protocol:



*Take a picture of the new bouquet before entry, then a picture of the returned bouquet, then a picture of them next to each other.*



Seemed simple enough. The second one was merely an address and a name: The Mold. Under the section where the bouquet description would usually be, Anna had written in a note:



*Wilted flowers from Dolores B.*



I was still relatively new to Southwest Sunsets Floral Arrangements at the time, but even so, I'd come to understand the working environment pretty quickly. I let out a jaded sighed, assuming whatever The Mold was would undoubtedly be some kind of grim vision I couldn't unsee, and punched my route into Google Maps. The truth would prove more complex: I was right about being in for a grim vision I couldn't unsee. I was wrong to assume it was The Mold.



I arrived at the home of Dolores B about 15 minutes later. It was buried in a row of houses at the dead center of a retirement community, situated on a hill with a steep incline that my car complained about the entire way up. When I found a place to pull out, I hopped out of my car, then removed her bouquet from a box in the backseat. As I trudged up the driveway to her house, I examined the bouquet a little closer. It was entirely white roses, 12 blooms, and nothing else. Simple and straightforward, normal enough to briefly lull me into a false sense of security. White flowers seemed to be her favorites, too; her front porch bore five potted peace lilies, placed by the brick stairs and on either side of a wrought iron patio bench. Her doorbell played the Westminster Quarters tune.



"Hello?" Dolores greeted me with sparkling hazel eyes and a coffee-tinted smile. She saw the vase in my hands. "Ah, you must be here for the exchange!"



"Yes," I said, holding up the roses. "For Dolores?"



"That would be me!" she said, a cheery timbre to her voice. She opened the door, fighting a slight Parkinson's tremor in her hands, and gestured for me to come inside.



I remembered the instructions I'd been given, and asked, "What was wrong with the previous arrangement?"



"You should probably see for yourself," she said, with an awkward smile. "It's in here, by the fireplace."



I nodded, then followed her inside, briefly explaining that I'd have to take a picture of the two arrangements next to each other before taking the original arrangement back. This all seemed reasonable to her, and when we reached her living room, she gestured at the base of the fireplace. "It's right over there."



The first arrangement was almost identical to the one in my hands—same roses, same number of blooms—but for one key difference. Half of them had completely wilted, flopped over and drooping like heads dangling from broken necks. Rotted, dry, dead as dead could be. I blinked. "Wow. I see why you wanted an exchange."



Dolores chuckled politely. "Yes, well, I'm just glad I was able to get some new ones."



I snapped a picture of the old bouquet, then squatted down in front of the fireplace, setting the new arrangement down next to it. As I rotated the half-decayed roses to get a better look, some of the wilted flowers dropped their petals, and I noticed the ones towards the center of the vase hadn't wilted as completely as the others. I picked out one of them and ran my fingers over the petals. It was halfway wilted. Literally, as if someone had divided it down the middle, kept one half pristine, and let the other half rapidly decompose. I frowned, snapped a picture of the two bouquets side by side, then called up the shop.



"Southwest Sunsets Floral Arrangements, how can I help you today?" Nautilus, one of the shop owners, answered.



"It's Theo," I said, getting to my feet again. "I'm with Dolores B right now, for the return?"



"Ah yes," Nautilus said, "we sent the first one out yesterday."



"Yeah," I replied. "You guys didn't mix up the white roses with the weeping roses yesterday, did you?"



"Are they weeping?"



"No, just wilted."



"Then no, we didn't mix them up."



"Okay, but they're wilted *weird*," I spluttered, frustrated. "Like someone cut them right down the mi—"



I glanced up at the mantle above her fireplace, and I finally noticed it.



All of the items had gone the same way as her roses. The clock was dead, the wood on one half of it rotting and the hands on that side rusted to a standstill. A glass jar to the left held clean packages of Werther's Originals on one side, and torn packages sticky with melted candies on the other. A photo of her and her husband, the husband's side yellowed and frayed beneath the splintered half of the wooden picture frame.



The only item on the mantle that didn't appear to be halfway-dead was a small wooden figurine of a duck. I picked it up and examined it, turning it over a few times. It looked hand-carved by someone who didn't know anything about woodworking, feathers uneven and scraped into the fibers with clumsy, jagged strokes. The paint was chipped in parts and faded in others, but there was enough there to tell me it was supposed to be a mallard duck, its deep green head contrasting against the brown and white markings along its body. Its beak looked more like a cone than a duckbill, and without the paint colors, I might not have been able to distinguish it as a duck at all. But unlike everything else on the mantelpiece, it was unharmed.



"Theo?"



I jumped, startled by Nautilus's voice breaking the silence. "Yeah I'm here, sorry," I replied. When I came to my senses, I finally realized what all of this meant. I groaned. "I think the damage was done by the customer."



Nautilus's tone was as irritated as mine. "You're sure?"



I glanced at a cup full of pens on the mantle, the plastic on one half of each of them cracked like they'd been lying in the sun for 10 years. "Yeah, I'm pretty damn sure."



"Anna went over the exchange policy with you, right?"



"I know, I know," I said, pinching the bridge of my nose. "I just hate to make an old lady sad."



"I don't like it either," he replied, exhaling wearily, "but we do have a business to run."



"Alright. I'll tell her now." I hung up the phone, put the wooden duck back, and let out a very heavy sigh. "Dolores, I'm sorry to tell you this, but we can't exchange these flowers."



No response.



I frowned. "Dolores?"



She wasn't in the room anymore, but I heard shuffling in the kitchen around the corner, followed by the wet sound of something hitting the floor. I followed the noise.



As I approached the corner, my right leg started to feel unusually frail. You know the feeling of hitting your funny bone? Imagine that, but it's everything from the waist down. The impact was nausea-inducing. A wave of pins and needles rolled up from my feet, putting my entire leg to sleep with a buzzing pain that made stepping on it impossible. I stumbled, clinging to the threshold to stay upright. I'd experienced this before; my progressive neurological disease often did this to me on my bad days.



The shooting pains stopped, and it went numb. I tried to walk, but I couldn't feel the foot I was stepping on. Where I should have felt my weight pressing into the floor, there was just, nothing. My breath became shallow. I already knew my disease was stealing my ability to walk, but the process was supposed to be slow. It wasn't supposed to happen until I was older.



I heard another wet splat in the kitchen, and I glanced around the corner. Dolores was sitting on a bar stool, facing the window to her right and angled so I could only see her left side.



I swallowed uneasily. "Dolores?"



She turned, slowly, to face me. The right side of her body was nearly mummified, skin and wasted flesh clinging to her bones. Half of her shirt had frayed and unraveled, revealing the skin around her chest stretched so taught as to be torn open by protruding ribs. By her stomach, a few threads of fabric remained intact, but the skin did not, ripping open vertically from the base of her gut. A few bits of viscera poked out around the edges, and as she turned, the hole opened wider, allowing her liver to slide out through it. It landed on the floor with a third splat, right next to her two kidneys.



I tried to reel back, but my leg wouldn't work with me. Panicked and frustrated, I reached down to grab my leg and move it with my arm. But my arm felt frail too. When I caught sight of it, I stifled a scream.



My skin was paler, and thin enough to be translucent. My veins wound down my arm like red and blue branches, channels winding beneath an ever-growing forest of wrinkles and liver spots. The hair on my forearm was white, some of it falling off and sticking between the balls of lint on my now moth-eaten sleeve. I saw a crease in the fabric move in a way that it shouldn't, and I looked closer. No, the crease hadn't moved. The *pattern* did. Each of the flowers on the floral print had wilted, casting out threads and lint balls with each falling petal.



In a brief moment of clarity, I recalled something Anna had told me on my second day at the job. If I ever sustained any paranormal injuries, I should contact their on-call witch doctor, and I should keep his number in my contacts just in case.



I reached into my pocket for my phone, but the fabric had frayed, and my phone fell through a hole at the bottom. I tried to pick it up, but my now-arthritic fingers couldn't grasp it. With only one leg to balance on, bending over proved too difficult, and I tumbled to the ground. I groaned and rolled over, grabbing the phone with my still-healthy left hand. I called the number.



He answered after one ring. "This is Max's Curse Removal and Exorcisms."



"I'm hazvghhh…" I hadn't noticed the right side of my jaw was rotting, making it difficult to speak. I winced, tried again. "I work for Shouthwesht Shunshets, I'm hafvimg a shupernatural emergenshy."



He sighed loudly. "Alright, give me the address."



I hardly finished telling it to him, when a glowing circle appeared in the living room next to the fireplace. The lines were thin, like pencil strokes of smoldering embers, carving themselves into the hardwood floors in a spiral of occult symbols. A dark hole appeared in the center, from which a man—Max, I assumed—rose out.



He wore a pair of weathered overalls, covering a long-sleeve flannel shirt and paired with a green baseball cap from Bass Pro Shop. For all intents and purposes, he looked like an ordinary maintenance guy, complete with a utility belt and red toolbox. An untrimmed beard that spread all the way down to his neck and thick-fingered calloused hands completed the look. However, most of the items on his utility belt weren't your typical hand tools. They included a crucifix, a bundle of dried sage, two heads of garlic, and a few bottles of some unknown potions. He did also carry a few normal screwdrivers, but they were covered in dried ectoplasm.



He glanced indifferently at me, then at what remained of Dolores. "Alright," he said gruffly, "what happened here?"



No longer able to speak through the necrosis sinking into my jaw, I pointed at the fireplace. He stared at it for a few seconds, then pulled out a pair of store-brand work gloves from Walmart, with circles of occult symbols embroidered into the palms. He picked up a few of the items on the mantle, turning them around and examining the damage. From the front pocket on his overalls, he retrieved a rusted tuning fork, flicked it until it rang, then held it up and slowly moved it along the mantelpiece.



When it passed by the crudely-carved duck figurine, the pitch spiked, becoming a warbling squeal. Max grunted affirmatively, then silenced the tuning fork and returned it to his pocket. He took down the figurine and grasped it in both hands, one gripping the body and another gripping the head. He twisted roughly, jerking the two sections of the figurine in opposite directions. Once, twice, three times. On his third attempt, the wood splintered at the neck, and the duck broke in half, spraying a few wood chips onto the floor. With a satisfactory nod, Max took one last look at the two pieces, then tossed them into the fireplace, following it up with lighter fluid, a match and a few pinches of rock salt.



As the figure burned, I felt a slight decrease in pain. I looked down at my arm, and watched as the decay slowed to a stop. But it didn't reverse. It was still stuck in that state, as was everything else the cursed duck had affected. Max seemed like he was about to leave, but I made an awkward noise to grab his attention and pointed to the rotting side of my body.



"Oh, yeah," Max grunted, as casually as if he'd just been reminded to turn off his headlights. He kneeled next to me and rummaged around his toolbox, pulling out a paper effigy of a person and some scotch tape. He set them down on my chest, then grabbed my aged wrist, feeling for my pulse. Thankfully it was there, however faint. Once he found it, he plucked a hair off my arm and taped it to the right side of the paper effigy.



It began to smoke and burn. As half of the paper man turned to flecks of black and white ash, the pain on my right side numbed and faded, and the feeling returned to my leg. I glanced at my arm, examining the sleeves of my coat. The wrinkles and arthritis slowly disappeared, and the flowers within the floral print bloomed back to life. When the process was done, Max let go of my wrist, and I rubbed it weakly, exhaling with relief. "Thanks, man," I croaked, finally able to talk again.



"Mm-hm." Max grabbed what remained of the paper man, then tossed it into the fire, following it up with a mixture of pungent herbs and sawdust.



I looked back at the two vases, to find the second bouquet had also halfway rotted. With chagrin, I remembered my route wasn't over yet. I still had to take them to The Mold. "Hey, Max."



He turned around. "Yeah?"



"Are those safe to take in my car?"



He turned back to the flowers, bending down and squinting at them. He nodded, then stood back up, putting his hands on his hips. "Should be. Just wear some gloves when you handle 'em."



"I don't have any gloves."



"You could use some garbage bags instead," he suggested. He pointed at Dolores, now dead as a doornail, slumped over her dining table as her rotted organs oozed out onto the floor. "I doubt she'll mind parting with a few of them."



I hesitated at the idea of stealing from a dead person, especially an old woman. Then I remembered that Dolores had willingly led me to a cursed object that would have rotted half of my body off. I shrugged. "Fair enough."


I got to my feet, happy to find they both worked again. And as he sank back into his teleportation circle, I searched for a few garbage bags out of Dolores's kitchen.



The Mold lived in a large manor where the desert met the base of the mountains, built into the side of the foothills. The steps up to Its residence unfolded like a long tongue, leading into the yawning maw of the pillared entryway. At the top were mahogany double doors, intricately carved with reliefs of animal and human bodies, strung up in vines and gaunt with decay. The wood itself also looked weathered, warped and splintered in some places as if it had been pulled from medieval ruins. To the right of these doors, beneath an extinguished oil lamp covered in cobwebs, was a modern Ring doorbell with an attached camera. I pressed the button.



A voice came through the speaker. It was deep, the kind of baritone that you can feel in your chest. It spoke the way a snail crawls, slowly, slurred and wet. "Who approaches The Mold?"



"I'm from Southwest Sunsets," I replied. I held the garbage bag full of flowers up to the camera. "I have a delivery for you."



"Ahhh, you must be the new driver," The Mold replied, a smile audible through a pronounced transatlantic accent. "I was hoping to receive a delivery today. One minute please, my good sir."



A fuzzy black substance oozed through the crack between the double doors, crawling along the wood and blooming with dark green clumps of spores. The left door creaked open hardly a few inches, and two pale green eyes peeked out through the murk, tilted at an odd angle and set in a greenish-black blob that loosely approximated a head.



"Thank you," The Mold said warmly, three tendrils slithering out of its torso and wrapping around the garbage bag. "Ah, and they've been pre-seasoned!" It exclaimed with delight. "What a lovely surprise."



Before I could ask what It meant by that, It reached into the bag and pulled out one of the wilted white roses. A mouth appeared out of the amorphous blob, wider than the head, with rows of teeth spiraling down into a black gaping hole that should have been a throat. The Mold bit off the bulb of the rose and began grinding it between thousands of molars, making a contented noise. "Delicious," It said, between a mouthful of petals. "Thank you, young man."



I blinked. "Yeah," I said awkwardly. I realized that sounded rude, and I tried again. "I mean, you're welcome, of course."



"I believe I owe you a tip," The Mold said. It took another bite off the stem, then two more tendrils emerged from within It, holding out three gold coins of unknown origin. I almost took them, but something about this reminded me of Spirited Away, and I decided accepting this tip might be a bad idea.



"Uh, thank you," I replied uncomfortably, "but I don't really need it."



"Is this not an acceptable amount of currency?" The Mold asked, with a slight bit of dejection in Its voice.



"No no," I replied, "I just can't really buy anything with gold coins."



"I see," It replied. "Well, if you change your mind, I shall give them to you next time we meet."



"That's very kind of you," I replied, with the fake smile I usually gave to customers. "I'll see you later."



The Mold grinned, baring Its many teeth and sending me off with a friendly wave. "Until we meet again, my good sir."
