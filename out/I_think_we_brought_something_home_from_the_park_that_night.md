My friend and I have a habit of going to this park late at night.

It isn't some small neighbourhood park either. It's huge. There's a long walking and biking trail that wraps around the area, and a massive river running through the middle. During the day it's actually pretty nice. Families walk their dogs, people ride bikes, and kids play near the water.

At night, though, it feels completely different.

The night this happened, it was pitch black.

There weren't lights near where we were sitting. The only light came from our phones and the moon reflecting off the river. The water looked almost white, like someone had poured milk across the surface. Everything else around us was swallowed by darkness.

We were sitting on our usual bench beside the river, smoking, talking about random things, and scrolling through our phones after we ran out of things to do.

After a while I looked around and realized just how dark it really was.

I remember turning to my friend and asking, "Do you think there could be people watching us right now?"

He looked up from his phone.

"What do you mean?"

"I don't know. It's just really dark. What if someone's standing out there somewhere and we can't see them? Or ghosts or something."

He laughed.

"Nah. It's too dark for anyone to see us. And ghosts aren't real."

I immediately told him not to say that.

"Don't jinx us."

That only made him laugh harder.

Beside our bench was a tree with long branches hanging down. The leaves nearly brushed against us whenever the wind picked up.

My friend stood up and started slapping the hanging branches around.

"No ghost can touch me," he said while laughing.

I told him to stop being stupid.

Instead, he kept going.

Eventually I joined him. We were both standing there like idiots, smacking the leaves and joking around.

Looking back, I really wish we hadn't.

About twenty minutes later we decided to call it a night and head home.

Nothing felt strange at first.

We got into his car and started driving out of the park. The exit road is a long stretch surrounded by trees with barely any traffic at night.

That's when we heard it.

BANG.

A violent crash came from the trunk.

Not a little rattle.

Not something shifting around.

It sounded like somebody had punched the inside of the trunk as hard as they could.

I looked over at my friend and laughed nervously.

"Haha. Very funny."

He looked genuinely confused.

"What?"

"The trunk."

His expression changed instantly.

"I swear to God that wasn't me."

The chills that went through my body were immediate.

Neither of us said a word for several seconds.

Then another thought hit both of us at the same time.

What if somebody was actually in there?

We pulled over onto the side of the road.

My friend kept a baseball bat in his car, so he grabbed it and slowly walked toward the trunk. I stayed a few steps behind him.

I could hear my own heartbeat.

He unlocked it and lifted it open.

Nothing.

The trunk was completely empty.

No bags.

No loose objects.

Nothing that could have made that sound.

We just stood there staring into it.

Eventually we convinced ourselves it had to be something mechanical. Maybe something underneath the car. Maybe we'd hit a bump.

Neither of us actually believed that, but it was better than having no explanation.

Eventually he drove me back to his house where my own car was parked.

I got in my car and started driving home.

At first I was just trying not to think about the trunk.

Then I stopped at an intersection a few minutes from my house.

That's when I saw her.

An old woman was standing on the footpath.

She was holding one of those walkers with a built-in seat that elderly people use. She wore a long grey coat that hung almost to her ankles, dark shoes, and a beige headscarf wrapped tightly around her head.

Her back was badly hunched.

She wasn't moving.

Just standing there.

I remember thinking it was strange seeing somebody that old outside alone at nearly eleven o'clock at night.

The traffic light changed and I drove off.

A few minutes later I glanced out my passenger window.

My stomach dropped.

The same woman was standing there.

The exact same woman.

Same coat.

Same headscarf.

Same walker.

Same hunched posture.

I immediately started trying to explain it away.

Maybe it was another old lady.

Maybe I'd mistaken her for someone else.

Maybe she'd crossed through some side streets and gotten ahead of me somehow.

But even as I thought those things, I knew none of them made sense.

There was no possible way she could have gotten ahead of me.

I was driving.

She could barely stand upright.

My hands started shaking on the steering wheel.

I kept checking my mirrors.

I don't know why.

I just felt like something was wrong.

Really wrong.

I sped up.

I just wanted to get home.

About a minute later I approached a roundabout less than a street away from my house.

As my headlights swept across it, I felt every hair on my body stand up.

She was there again.

Standing beside the roundabout.

Waiting.

The exact same woman.

This time she wasn't looking down.

She was looking directly at me.

I still remember her face.

The wrinkles.

The pale skin.

And the smile.

It wasn't a normal smile.

It was too wide.

Too deliberate.

Like she knew something I didn't.

For a second I genuinely couldn't breathe.

My chest felt tight.

My vision started tunnelling.

Every instinct in my body was screaming that I needed to get away from her.

I floored the accelerator.

I don't even remember the drive home after that.

I only remember pulling into my driveway and practically falling out of the car.

I ran inside, locked every door, and sprinted to my room.

I spent the rest of the night staring at my bedroom door.

Every little noise made me jump.

Every shadow looked wrong.

I don't remember falling asleep.

I just remember waking up the next morning exhausted.

I've tried to come up with explanations ever since.

Maybe the noise in the trunk was unrelated.

Maybe the old woman was a coincidence.

Maybe my mind was playing tricks on me.

But here's the part that still bothers me.

The next day I called my friend and told him about the old woman.

I hadn't even finished the story before he interrupted me.

His voice sounded nervous.

He asked me one question.

"Was she wearing a grey coat and a beige headscarf?"

I never told him what she was wearing.

To this day, neither of us will talk about what happened after we slapped those branches.

And neither of us has ever gone back to that park.
