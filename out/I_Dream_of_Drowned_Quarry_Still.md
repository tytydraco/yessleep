When I was child, we lived near a lake. No, calling it a lake would be misleading, it was a big pond, a small dam, water contained by the combined efforts of human ancestors and physics.

Around it was a small forest with uncommonly sparse trees. You have to understand, for most of us ‘the woods’ meant densely packed rainforest, not whatever this was. Thin trees with so much space in between each other as to allow the accumulation of what once was human filth, but which through time had alchemized itself into nature. Rusted down machines, parts of cars, some trash, mostly things that felt like sculptures, remnant memories that, to us, were ancestral, but more realistically couldn’t be that old.

The place was beautiful and forested and mostly deserted. For whatever reason that was never made clear to me, the adults didn’t like going there. Looking back, I don’t think I ever saw a single person much older than 16  years of age near those shores. The children, on the other hand, loved it with a deep and uncomplicated love. For them the water was more symbolic than real, as if in its briar-dark drops was a taste of the autonomy the future promised, as if in their quietness, in their open secret was something beyond sacred. 

The lake, such as it was, had only one true mystery: its origins. Well, being honest, it was probably no real mystery, but seeing as the children refused to talk to the adults about the lake, it classed as one. We lived in a boring neighborhood of a boring large city, so we took our adventures as they came. It wasn’t like any of it was an actual secret, just a quiet and weak-willed taboo, the kind of thing that is commonplace, but you’d never even think to talk about with your parents or teachers.
I don’t remember when it began exactly, but at some point, me and some friends decided to ‘figure out’ what the lake was. Not through research — of course not —, but through story-telling and make-pretend, as if once the perfect story was found, existence would rewrite itself and transform our make-believe into more real than reality.

At first we called it an ocean. I'd seen the sea before — so had some of the others — and it looked nothing like the lake. Its waters were too green and calm, its surroundings too verdant, there was no sand and no cool breeze. Yet none of us had ever seen an ocean, we’d heard plenty about it, seen images of high waves and big infinities of water. Well, for us, this small lake was infinity itself and those waves had to be simple contingencies of the steely skies that always accompanied them in photos. Skies we’d never seen with our own eyes, and which we, therefore, presumed belonged to some distant place, some other, cooler and angrier climate. So it was our ocean, and that lasted awhile.

We’d dive looking for fish — there were some, but they were both few and minuscule —, making up stories about the monsters hiding under the waves or in burrows in the stone. Once, B. found an indent in one of the walls around the lake; we called it a cave, but it’s difficult to trust my memories of its size, it might have been little more than an irregularity in the rocks. 

To us it became proof of something big living in the lake, something that nested there, something that might, having caught our scent, slither into our rooms in the quiet of night — through windows open to keep a flow of cool air, through crevices under doors, through the pipework of apartment complexes — and snatch us away. I remember B. beaming as he told us of this danger, while K. went as pale as her complexion allowed and I felt tears welling up in my eyes, those were quite common for me in those days. There were others too, and though their reactions ran the whole spectrum from B. to me, this story isn’t theirs — it’s not even mine, not really.

Not that all our fantasies were that macabre, but that place did bring out something in us, in me, that is difficult to explain. For all its beauty, for all its quiescence, it felt haunted. Not the haunting of some shade, all obscurity and sadness, but rather something active, a specter of force and reverence, an apparition of being. Like the haunted feeling you get in a clearing in the woods full of trampled footprints and bottles and rags, rather than the one found in a dilapidated building. One isn’t any more pleasant than the other, but they can’t really be compared.

And we played at it, both the somber and the merry; we played hide and seek and marco polo, we ran in the woods and swam in its waters, we explored its shore and what little we could find of its depths, we found artifacts in the form of old things, made from iron and steel and plastic, we reenacted grisly scenes probably cribbed wholesale from movies, we talked and talked and talked, sharing stories, real and imagined.

I wasn’t silent, but at the same time never talked, instead, it fell to me to create, I was seen as some mix between leader and bronze statue, just as much an artifact as the things we hunted in those woods. I don’t know, sometimes I wonder if future taints past, if what I would become colors the way I think about what I was. 

One summer, after a class about a large damming project the government was working on — ‘the largest in the world’, so large it even affected the rotation of the planet — somewhere ‘far away’, some place with a name that slipped through my young mind like so much smoke, K. decided that sounded cool and so that was what our lake had to be. She imagined an entire forest, whole cities, laying somewhere down there, waiting for us to dive deep enough to find them, waiting to, once more, be witnessed by human eyes. 

I remember nightmares: a very avoidable flood, cataclysmic in proportions, water devouring trees and houses and people alike, in my mind that far away place, the place that ‘had no people’ couldn’t have had no people. In my mind, the flood killed and expropriated with the force of culture. In my mind, I saw houses empty of nothing except life. I kept quiet, afraid that saying it out loud would make it so, afraid to admit that that possibility frightened me, afraid to truly express the tragedy of it all.
 
I dreamt too of those choking cities, of diving into them, of swimming through streets with kilometers of water above my head. Of street lamps with nothing to illuminate but blue-black, of fishes making their way through broken doors and busted windows. I dreamt of the light of a flashlight, cutting cleanly through abyssal depths and showing the windows of a skyscraper like the many eyes of a sea monster. Underwater are trees that cannot rot, and houses that will never be bulldozed, streets wrecked but preserved — cracked and spewing black tar —, whole worlds turned into so many snowglobes, entropy stopped in its tracks, prostrated before something far more sinister.

The next summer, we learned about industries, we learned about mining and quarries, about the huge scars they leave behind. We were older, slowly growing disenchanted with the world, so we decided the lake had been just that, a deactivated quarry. 

That time I think we might have been closer to the truth, such as it was, just a large hole. Dug once, filled with water over time. A mosquito breeding ground, kept in check only by scant population of fish dropped by birds from who knows where, by the chemical death dissolved in its waters, whether naturally or by human intervention. I often wonder just how close we were flirting with death, chemical burns and alkali stomach ulcers without even realizing it. At the time, we were far too focused on what that pit contained and far too little on what contained it, whether it be the stones, slowly releasing their chemical bleach into the water, or even in a more abstract sense, our very lives, with all their sense of strange reverberation.

K. called it Drowned Quarry and it just kind of stuck for us. She must have heard it somewhere else, because I’ve since heard other people, ones who didn’t used to go out with us, calling it the same thing. But mostly we just called it the pond, the lake, the drop. It was as unspectacular as the names suggest, beautiful for sure, but not special, we kids used to spend the entire summer around it, swimming and playing and screaming, it was far from paradise, but it was still home.

The surface of the water was sunk into its surroundings unevenly. There were shores, but also large drops, sheer faces of rock, the kind which, in movies, would entice the kids to jump. Real life is always different, it was too high, with too many trees in the way, it was too scary and unsafe, and so, no one ever jumped.

We did goad each other into it, with all the subtlety and finesse available to teens and preteens, challenges and dares abound, but, in my time only one person ever took the bait. 

It was during the summer I turned 9. That year, some girl I never knew, because she was a few years older than me and my friends, tried the jump. I sometimes remember her face, or what I believe was her face, in a smudged kind of way, a pointillist smile, down cast eyes of no particular color, hair that flows with or without any breeze, kept neat in a ponytail. I don't know what convinced her and I'm not sure what went wrong, maybe she missed the water, maybe she hit a branch, maybe the water had been too shallow for the drop. 

Sometimes, I can almost conjure up the entire scene. A blank yet contorted face and a non-descript outfit, the infinity of those few seconds, hair flapping in the wind. Crunch. Splash. Screaming.

I never saw her again, I don't even know if she lived. I just remember the talks afterwards, scared parents making kids swear they wouldn’t go jumping, or even get near Drowned Quarry ever again. For those moments we were solemnity itself, but as soon as they turned around, we ran back to those waters. It’s funny, I never once considered that that girl, the girl whose name I can’t even remember, whose face exists like a flickering ghost in my mind, I never once considered she had not, in fact, missed the jump, not really, and I hadn’t once considered that that might have been why the explicit ban on the children’s activity in the quarry lasted as little as it did. I think it was easier for everyone to do as the parents and obliquely believe in one of the many half-baked rumors about some deadly game or bet running around than to face the reality of the tragedy.

While we rarely went to the quarry at night, there was one time in which we — me, B. and K. — went out looking for something I don’t really remember. We had escaped our houses — in my case by doing the brave deed of walking out the front door — to meet in the woods and in our quixotic search ended up nearing a cliff that dropped into the pond.

It had been only a couple of months since that girl had jumped, but she was already far from our minds. I remember the moon was high and bright, it illuminated the green water, giving it an almost supernatural glow that felt at once fitting and utterly alien. I remember how intimidating the trees themselves had become in the dark, branches like reaching arms, like claws ready to strike. I remember looking at the ground to avoid looking at the drop and, amidst the shrubbery, seeing all manner of trash, from hairbands to what I would later understand were condoms, but which then must have looked like strange little plastic bags. 

That was the first time I saw it. Out there, in the middle of the lake, was a shape, something that I had never seen before, it seemed vaguely human, but through the darkness and distance, making out anything more was impossible. I thought it was some kind of sculpture, placed recently by one of the older and more daring artsy kids.

Years later, long after our little group disintegrated, K. told me that that night she and B. had shared their precocious and awkward first kiss while I sat by that cliff, fear forgotten, legs dangling in the air as I stared into the moon. I only remember walking home alone, the cold light and the shape and the dark, the sounds of the woods at night and the smell of petrichor.
 
I didn’t go to the lake again for almost a week after that. When I did, once more playing with K. and B., there was nothing out of the ordinary, the thing I’d seen in the water, the thing I could remember so vividly, was nowhere to be seen.
I tried to brush it aside, what did it matter after all? Something was there, and now it wasn’t. It had been taken down during the week, I was mistaken in what I’d seen, whatever.

We went from playing to racing, testing each other in how fast we could swim and for how long we could hold our breaths underwater. And I guess I couldn’t keep that thing, that statue, out of my mind as well as I’d thought because next thing I knew, B. lightly pulled on my arm and asked me what I was doing. I froze. Turning around, K. lagged behind us, she wasn’t a great swimmer at the best of times, and now looked worse for wear. 

I had driven us further and further from shore — had I noticed it? — between floating and swimming and drifting. Now, we were almost where that shape would have been a week ago — close, so close — and for whatever reason I remember the water growing thicker, like I was swimming through syrup, but maybe that had more to do with my arms growing tired. Yet, as close as we were, there was nothing, no statues of course, but no shapes underwater and no… What? Signs?

I stared into his brown eyes, momentarily unable to speak, threading water. But K. reaches us, her strokes are erratic. She is scared and quickly tiring out, but says nothing. I know she feels that’s the price of our friendship, our oblique sort of love, this blankness, all silence and emptiness. Beneath, the dark has never felt so dark, so cold, so infinite.

There’s little to do for her, you can’t stop someone from drowning by yourself, they inevitably pull you down. We make our way back to land, slowly, floating most of the way. None of us talk, and for a moment it seems as if the entire lake is empty, quiet, as if all the other children are gone, the adolescents somewhere else and the cars speeding by in the surrounding roads nowhere to be seen, all I can hear are the birds and the insects. It’s less peaceful than it ought to be.

When we make it to land B. doesn’t say a word, he just darts away and I’m filled with shame. K. drags herself of the water, coughing and breathing hard, she lays on the stone, in the mud, quiet, eyes closed. I sit by her and the warm breeze has suddenly turned cold and I shiver and silent tears join the water that runs down my face.

We stay like that as our shadows grow less and less defined. K. shivers and I open my mouth to speak, but what is there to say? What is there to do? 

She gets up and looks at me, really looks. In her eyes there is something that I’ve never seen before and I don’t know what it is, I guess I’ve become a stranger to her and she suddenly feels so much older, so much wiser. My heart beats faster and faster and faster as a kind of existential pain that washes over me.

She walks away as I stare into the setting sun. And I know that I fucked up at least twice, and now there’s nothing to be done, so instead I get up. My legs are covered in mud and little pebbles, they are red and itchy, my eyes well with tears again and I, too, run home.

And the next day, all should have been forgotten, and that might have been the case, had any of this had happened a little earlier, had the circumstances been a little different. But it wasn’t and they weren’t. So the weight of that silence and that dark and that kiss I didn’t even know had occurred hung above our heads, and to our young minds that nothing was like the Sword of Damocles.
One day turns to two, two, into three. Nothing is forgiven, nothing is forgotten and yet we talk, and we laugh, and we walk. K. is quieter, B.'s eyes harden, I walk home alone every day now.

It happens again in one of these walks. The sun was setting and for no reason in particular I decided to go to the Drowned Quarry, it’s not like anyone would miss me back home. So I take a detour, hoping to sit down by the shore, hoping to find something I don’t yet know I lost.

And all I can hear is the lapping of the water, the distant cars on the motorway, distant sounds of animal life, both human and non-human. The wind runs through the coppices of trees and everything smells of damp earth, of oaks, fern and moss. And the moon rises through the forest and spills into the waters.

Far away, silhouetted against that pale light is, once more, a shape. From this distance it looks even more human than before. It sits in the middle of the lake, unmoving? No. It sways like an anemone, swimming against an unseen current, moving against an unfelt breeze. I stare, there’s not much else to do unless… But no.

That night I dream of the ocean. I am alone in its depths, above me — but where is above? It’s all blue and black, just blue and black — I know the moon rises, but below… What is it that rises below?

I swim blindly, but it’s impossible to say if I even move. I open my mouth and it fills with so much salt water I can taste blood alongside it. But I don’t die, instead I drown and drown and drown. And as my mind shuts down, as the fear and pain of drowning becomes too much, as my bones weigh me down to the depths, as the rigidity required of normal pressure environments betrays me, I see a house.

When I wake up, nothing makes sense. I remember all of it, at least what I think is all of it. I remember a wall, a wall I somehow knew was concrete rising up from the infinite blue, I remember the knowledge that it was a house or that it housed something, and I remember drowning.

I don’t skip school the next day, I don’t think I’d have known how to do that, but I don’t talk, and I avoid everyone’s gaze and as soon as I’m allowed, I dart out the front gate.

I raced against the sun, hoping we would get a late twilight. Around me cars whizzed by, but they don’t exist, they are shapes and colors, changes in pressure and wind velocity. A man screams at me as I cross the road, already panting, and I barely even notice, words are meaningless, just a sequence of sounds.

I reach the woods in good time, but it’s useless, the moon rises and I contemplate going home, out of frustration if nothing else. But I can’t, there is this one last thing I must do, so I walk through brushes and between trees and I find myself on that shore again, looking at nothing, staring at an empty lake and at the reflection of the crescent moon.

And time grows untethered. I stay on those shores, hugging my knees against my torso and the night goes by and then weeks and then years. And I move through life, but somehow I’m still there, staring at the waters, watching the moon make its slow way through the sky. And I don’t return to the quarry, and B. moves away and K. moves on. And one day, I turn 16. But I’m still the kid staring out the water, though I haven’t been to the quarry in a long time, I’m still that little girl.

Memory is a curious thing, despite these words, I remember very little of this extended childhood of mine. When I think about that time, all I really remember is that brief image, that shape, the lady of the lake, as it flayed hypnotically. 

I tried talking to K. a few times, both of us more reserved than, perhaps, we should have been. Me trying to explain, she not quite understanding, but still trying to hold me up, she was a good friend, I think.

My teenage years weren’t eventful in the normal sense, I didn’t go to parties, though I was sometimes invited, I didn’t date, because I never wanted to, I kept to myself and the world moved on, mostly without me. 

One day, after a truly tremendous fight at home, I ran out and into the night. There was nothing on my mind, but a need for the darkness, for the absolute anonymity of the forest.

Unconsciously, I made my way through the same paths I used to take in childhood. Specifically, I made my way through bush and wood to the quarry, a place that had been so important to me in such an askew way, and which I hadn’t visited for whatever reason, at the time I just assumed it was a product of a perfectly natural growing disinclination towards swimming.

I reached the quarry without ever really knowing that that was where I was heading. I could hear the echoes of loud music booming elsewhere. The lake itself was empty, and so I stayed there, distantly listening to bad pop remixes and EDM, watching shadows dance from what I vaguely remembered was some kind of party the third-years were throwing.

I was almost asleep when someone touched my shoulder and I practically jumped out of my skin, falling from the awkward sitting position I’d adopted. Behind me a sweet gravely voice laughed, not unkindly. She wasn’t quite soaked, rather she was mildly damp, but still fully and indistinctly dressed, she had a kind face but hard blue eyes that sparkled with an almost childish sense of mischief. Still, there was a sadness to her countenance that is hard to explain, her smile was beautiful though, beautiful, but full of teeth.

I laughed despite my unease. From previous experience, I knew the best way of remaining invisible is to mimic and disengage when the option becomes available. I didn’t know her and that struck me as odd, the school wasn’t huge, it wasn’t even big, I should have had at least some inkling that I’d seen her face before, but I hadn’t.

“Sorry for scaring you”, she smiled again. I must have looked like I’d seen a ghost, because her voice had gone even softer, like she was speaking to a scared cat or a confused child. “So… what are you doing here?” Her voice perks up again and her words are elongated with a tone of striking familiarity, she gently motions with her eyes through the lake and to the party. “I mean the party is aaallll the way over there” she points at the dancing lights, so far away, on the other shore, and smiles cheekily, her words are a little slurred. Drunk, I thought, and relaxed, that put the situation into a frame I could understand.

But she doesn’t wait, or even seems to expect an answer, instead, she sits by my side, now silent. Close, so close. We stay like that for a few moments  and I can feel the coldness emanating from her skin, water still seeping through her clothes.

I know I should get up, make some lame excuse and disappear into the night, but I don’t, I stay there and watch the moon and the lights, dancing like the shape in my dreams. And she gently lays her head on my shoulder, and I feel water seeping into my shirt and condensing down my skin.

Eventually, my heart rate goes down again. I still don’t know this girl, who looks to be about my age, but something stops me from asking, stops me from talking in general. I cannot ask her about herself any more than I can tell her goodbye and move away. We stay like that and I can feel her body warming up little by little, the silence is strangely pleasant.

“Well” she finally says, getting up “nice to meet you”, I tell her my name and she smiles and repeats it to me, I ask if she needs help getting back to the party, but she only smiles before she’s back in the woods and I lose her once more. I never get her name.
Looking back, all of it seems so strange, like I was in a dream, one full of dark inevitability. Like when you run but can’t move, or when you know you are going to do something before you actually do it. At the time, though, I thought nothing  of it and when I actually fell asleep, still sitting on that shore, I dreamt of her eyes and cold blue oceans, the depths of the mesopelagic zone, sinking and sinking.

The next day was a mess, I discreetly tried to track who she was, but had very little to go on. I didn’t know her name and none of my friends seemed to know who I was talking about, description was never my strong suit. So I asked K. to keep me posted on parties, much to her delight. She had tried dragging me to a couple over the years, but had mostly given up on it, telling me I was welcome to isolation and theatrics if I wanted to.

It took a couple of months, but the end of the year was coming on and ad-hoc graduation parties were popping up, those were the biggest and the most likely to attract ‘outsiders’, like I presumed mystery-girl was. I also felt that, for some reason, those wouldn’t be quite so awkward to attend to, so I picked one almost at random.

What came next was the first real party I attended and honestly, it was kind of a nightmare. Human shapes contorted themselves under the strobing lights, the music was so loud that my whole body resonated to the rhythm of the bass, the room was feverishly warm and humid despite the unseasonably cold and dry summer night, some asshole ran around offering people E and had a hard time accepting `no` for an answer, the beer was vile and tasted nothing like beer. All around me was a kind of humanity I didn’t belong to, like they were organs of something wholly alien to me but perfectly sympathetic amongst themselves.
I remember feeling like the cells in my body were about to puke, like each and every one of them was revolting against life itself as they shivered against the hard wall of low amplitude soundwaves.

I knew almost no one there, which was something of a surprise, but I did eventually find K., already extremely drunk and a little belligerent. She was being chaperoned around by a friend I knew by face but not by name and as we talked, I had to concentrate on her lips to understand what she was saying. In the way of some drunks, K. put her arms around me in an awkward and heavy sort of hug, I could smell the alcohol leaving her pores even through the mustiness of the room, through the sweat and stale beer of the party. She looked like she was having fun, so my offer to take her home died in my throat. 

I exchanged a few gestures with her friend, who was also kind of drunk, but at least not will-forget-this-even-happened drunk, just to make sure they’d be at least kind of safe, I frankly did not have the willpower to get them out of there if they didn’t want to leave, so I left them to it and hoped everything would be okay. What a joke.

I left the party soon after, or at least left the premises, looking for somewhere colder and quieter to think a little. The night was dark, a little sliver of new moon made its way through the solid layer of clouds in the night sky, streetlamps buzzed and pulsed, cars sped by beyond an iron gate.

I considered just leaving, walking out into the night or calling a cab. The chill calmed me down some, yet my heart was still going at the speed of light and my body still revolted against the possibility of life, even from this far away I could feel the ground lightly trembling to the bass. I was simply taken by the artifice of it all. 

I walked around a little, trying to get some blood flow going, when I made my way around a corner of the property and there, leaning against a brick wall, was her. Her face was lit only by the smoldering ashes of the cigarette in her mouth and she looked even more stunning than last time, there was something of a cruel sneer to her mouth as she stared off into the cloudy sky.
I remember very little of that conversation, and what little I do remember makes no sense, like we were running at different speeds or having different sides of different conversations, like both of us were talking to different people — I wish I could recall whom I was talking to. 

I vaguely remember the dry taste of her mouth, the deep salt of her lips, covered in ash, the algae of her tongue, the sharpness of her fangs, the dampness of her hands and body, and her eyes reflecting the stars beyond the cloudy sky.
I remember staring at her, awestruck, being taken by the hand. I remember walking through underbrush in the dark, cutting myself on a briar and watching the blood seep and coagulate, feed the forest, and I remember the feeling of the water lapping at my ankles. And my body burning. And then,

nothing.

The rest are only dreams. Her nakedness, her skin turning to glinting bronze underwater, the shape of her turning more and more indistinct, like she was dissolving, her movements like the currents of a stream.

Sometimes I follow her into the lake, and down and down and down. She swims into darkness and I follow her, deeper and deeper. Alone but together we venture into nothingness, feeling only the stark cold of dream-reality. In those dreams, she sometimes is a person, but most often a bronze automaton or an anemone. The reality of her, of her feel, of her sight, gently sways, like a reflection on a lake.

I keep following what I tell myself is her lead, but which in actuality is nothing of the sort. Beneath it all, I can see the glimmering silver of a fishing line which sinks into nothing — or does it rise into the sky? — and disappears. 

For a microsecond, she turns around, as if acknowledging me, but I know better than to believe that. Where there should have been serenity — some kind merciful resignation perhaps, acceptance, even — instead is painted anew by the pain of relief and shame. Looking behind her only confirms what she has always known: it had always already been too late.

Yet, I grasp for her, but she melts away into seafoam. It takes as long as the blink of an eye, but also as long as eternity itself. The process is far from graceful, her naked bronze skin grows translucent and her inner workings are visible as clockwork. Skeins of blood vessels and nerve endings, pumping esophagus and coiling intestines, her ghostly indistinct face forever in a rictus of pain and desperation, an implosion of the body, and all her insides are on the outside as she shoots her life out from in between her legs and out of her mouth like a bursting star. And she’s all blood red, then coagulated black-brown, then dirt-white. 

Foam, at last. 

And I scream, and the water rushes my lungs and my alveoli burst like zits filled with blood. I feel every second of my chest caving into itself, water invading my cells and shattering their walls. And I wonder whether she’s the one I scream for. Whether the feeling in my guts is the pains of regret or of a fishhook settling deeper. Whether that matters in the slightest.
The dreams in which I don’t follow her are the worst ones. I don't watch her gruesome end, the moment life leaves her eyes, the moment it leaves mine, instead I get to feel as the entire lake quakes, and I get to live with the consequences of my life.
That night, there was a small earthquake around the lake, which wouldn’t be unusual except there are no earthquakes in this state in the entirety of our recorded history, that was the first one. And in the dreams, the earthquake isn’t an earthquake, rather it is the single beat of a heart.

In those dreams, I vomit up water only to realize that my skin, too, is bronze against the greenness of the lake. In those dreams, I hear her laugh one last time. And in those dreams, full of awful inevitability, I know there’s something under those waters, under reality, and I know that she’s no cause, but neither is she effect. In those dreams, I have a moment of awful clarity, in which I see myself reflected into the waters, and in that reflection is her, my Galatea.

At the time, I tried to understand it, tried to make sense of the dreams and the girl. Tried making conspiracy boards full of my misgivings and neuroses, full of B. and K.’s kisses and rates of death by suicide. I tried to fool myself into enlightenment, to pretend that I was at the heart of all things.

These days, I take the dreams as they come, and understand that there is nothing to understand. I never saw B. again after he moved out and I never saw K. after we both went to university — I think she got engaged last summer, it might have been to a woman, but I didn’t ask —, and it goes without saying that I never saw that woman, who was not a woman, but a girl, ever again. And though the image of her body is burned into my memory like a photo-negative, her face is already smoke, perhaps it always was. 

I interacted with the quarry only one more time after that night: when I made my jump. I cannot tell you what came over me, but by that time the girl was only a recurring dream, and the previous jumper… well, I didn’t even remember her at all. 

I did not miss, I dove through the dark in what I assume was a beautiful arch and hit the water hard. I remember nothing of the drop, I remember nothing of that day.

I sometimes wish I’d been traumatized by it, some part of me missing the pain that never came and wishing to wake up in the middle of the night in cold sweats, still reeling from the cold air bathing my soft skin as I dive and dive and dive in the blackness of night, into the dangerous waters that had grown as quaint as a tamed tiger. It hurt, it hurt more than I thought it would — it always does — and the shock of the cold made me almost as dizzy as the drop itself. I threw up afterwards, just bile and water.

My parents were both angry and worried at my state when I got to the house, my clothes soaked through, missing a shoe that is probably at the bottom of the quarry to this day. The really funny thing is that I had forgotten all of this, forgotten the drop, forgotten that night, forgotten the admonishments that followed it, until, the other day, I passed by that neck of the woods and saw a stern no swimming sign on the trail to the quarry.

I’m not sure why I’m sharing this now, after all these years. All I know is that we all have to believe in something, and I believe that that night I don’t quite remember, that apparently unprovoked jump and that ersatz fairy-tale — faux love-story and all — they birthed me far more thoroughly than my mother. I came to this world through soaked clothes, stern sermons, a hole in the ground and an aching absence. A first memory I constructed over the years and forgot over time.
