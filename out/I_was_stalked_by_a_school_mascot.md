I’ve worked long and hard to forget about the worst day of my life. Countless hours of therapy had diminished the memories into mere whispers in the farthest corners of my mind. Recent events have woken those sleeping memories. Their fangs have sunk deep into the folds of my brain, and I must once again face their agonizing sting. I could have lived the rest of my life with these memories being relegated to the very worst of my nightmares, never again having a conscious thought about them, but I am not that lucky, and fate is not that kind.

When I was young, really young, so young that I would never remember these events if they hadn’t been so scarring, I suffered from an unexpected and unexplainable fear of people. No traumatic trigger. No life changes. Just a complete inability to be around anyone other than my parents and grandparents. We worked with doctors and therapists with marginal success.

On top of my social difficulties, my family was rocked by an incident at my parent’s work.

My father and mother taught English and Theater at the local high school. Mom often stayed late preparing for production, so my dad would use the school’s weight room to work out. One night, he lost track of time and stayed much later than he had planned. As he was rushing out, a strange noise caught his attention. Each step forward brought him closer to the sound. The closer he got, the clearer he could hear it. A cry?  A whimper? A moan?

Dad followed the noise until he found himself in the last place he wanted to be. The noise was coming from the girl's locker room. He pushed the door open and rushed inside. Whatever he imagined would be inside could not compare to the horrors he would find.

On the floor, he found Mr. Clark, the science teacher, hunched over one of the senior students. When the student saw him, she screamed and covered herself.

While my dad was by no means in trouble, he was heavily involved in the investigation. He was the main witness to the crime, as the student was unwilling to testify. Mr. Clark had convinced her they were soul mates, that he was going to leave his wife and be with her. My heart goes out to that poor girl.

Because the student was eighteen, although barely, and Mr. Clark was only twenty-five, a handful of parents and students questioned his firing and the criminal investigations that followed. There was even discussion about my dad and how he had no right to break into the women's locker room. They were definitely a minority, but they were very vocal. And they were active.

They left notes in our mailbox, threw rocks at our windows, and even spray-painted *perv* and *snitch* on our car. It all really got to my dad. He even considered quitting.

To show their support, the school invited my dad to be a “guest of honor” at the homecoming football game. Dad thought it was important that he attended, but he wanted us to go as a family. Mom was obviously worried about how I’d handle it, but she hoped if she kept me close, I’d be alright.

 As soon as we left the car, I began to shake. Mom attempted to soothe me, but the evening was off to a rough start. By the time the game started, I was clinging desperately to my mother and on the verge of a meltdown. Mom did everything she could to console me. Then the band started up. The sudden loud noise immediately sent me into a screaming episode.

My parents stood to leave, doing their best to keep me as quiet as possible until we were out of the stadium, when I abruptly stopped crying. My parents looked at me and followed my gaze. Running across the field, leading the charge of student athletes, was the school mascot. He was just a teenager in a saggy bulldog costume, but he was something magical to me.

Not only did my episode end, for what could have been the first time in my life, I started giggling. The whole game, I kept my eyes fixed on the Bulldog, laughing at his flips and cartwheels. When halftime rolled around, the mascot walked over to a small courtyard next to the concession stand to take pictures. Mom asked if I wanted to take a picture with him, and I emphatically nodded.

She carried me down. While we waited, I began to get restless. Nervous. Anxious

The mascot must have noticed because when I got to the front of the line, he squatted down to my level and stuck out his fist. I hesitated, but returned the jester, lightly taping the dog's massive fist. He flung his hand back and then shook it while shaking his head. He then jumped to his feet, pointed at me, and then flexed his arm. I smiled widely, barely containing another giggle.

 The Bulldog stretched out his arms, and my mom handed me to him. He sat me on his knee and supported my back with one arm while flexing with his other. I mimed his gesture, flexing my tiny arm, and my mom snapped a picture.

After that game, I slowly started to get better. I was no social butterfly, but I was able to accompany my parents in public without losing my mind. The more games we attended, the more silly dances he did with the cheer team, the more of the little footballs he would throw to me after a touchdown, the better I got. My parents never figured out why I was so infatuated with him, but it didn’t really matter. Each game pulled me farther out of my shell.

The last picture I took with the Bulldog was the first round of playoffs, which meant little to my younger self except that there would be far less seating and far more noise. My parents were never punctual, and that night was no different. We arrived at the stadium with only minutes before kickoff and a line halfway out of the parking lot. It moved rather quickly, but I still felt we waited for eternity. We were still a few spots from the ticket booth when I heard the blast of trumpets and the thumping of drums that always heralded the beginning of the game. I whined to my dad, but he shushed me. The music began to roar, the crowd began to cheer, and the ground shook as a herd of young men roared across the field. Yet we were still standing in line. Still missing the Bulldog. So, I did the most logical thing my little brain could think of. I ran.

I bolted out of the line and through the gate. My dad yelled after me, but I didn’t listen. I weaved through the crowd of people and made it to the field just in time to see the teams line up for kickoff. I missed it. The thing I had been looking forward to all week. Tears began to coat my eyes until I felt a weight on my shoulder.

I turned and found the Bulldog looming over me, his hand gripping me. I squealed with excitement before wrapping myself around his leg. I expected an embrace or some sign of warmth, but nothing came. I looked up into his gaping black eyes. His signature animated enthusiasm was gone, replaced by a cold stiffness. The only movement he made was the rising and falling of his chest with each heavy breath he took. The longer he stared, the more uncomfortable I became. I let go of his leg and backed away. Each step I took, he shuffled the same distance until my back was against the fence. For a moment, it looked like he was about to reach towards me until a harsh voice rang out, calling my name.

My dad stomped towards me, fire in his eyes. He scooped me up and swatted me on the bottom. I would have cried if I hadn’t been so relieved to see him, which just made me want to cry more. He carried me to the stands, lecturing me about safety the whole way. As we walked away, I watched the mascot tilt his head to the side and stare me down. I turned back to my father and clung tightly to him.

The stands were packed with parents and students like sardines in a tin. We were barely able to squeeze into a row beside the farthest end of the stands. As the game continued, the Bulldog only acted stranger. He just stood, scanning the stands until a cheerleader prompted him to either dance or watch the game. Whenever we scored, the bulldog would lazily toss the mini footballs, barely making them to the stands at all. It was like he had completely forgotten the traditions I once held so dear.

  At one point, he wandered across the sidelines to our side of the stands. His empty black eyes scanned the concrete steps until he spotted me. He brought his hand up slowly and waved at me. I turned and tugged on my mom’s sleeve, but she was too wrapped up in the game. When I looked back, he was being pulled away by one of the cheerleaders.

For the rest of the game, I would catch him staring up at me from various places on the sideline. His eyes never seemed to leave me. Just before halftime, my dad asked me if I wanted to get a hot dog. I agreed instantly. He could have asked if I wanted to see the dentist, and I still would have agreed. I’d have done anything to get me away from those hollow eyes. We pushed through the crowds until we reached the concession stand. A long line of people had already gathered. My dad was irritated at the wait, but the crowd of people made me feel more secure. Then one of the cheerleaders spoke. Line up for a picture with Fight-o!  My stomach dropped. I looked over my shoulder and saw the grey, sagging frame of the mascot, his black eyes glaring directly at me. I was frozen in thick, heavy terror. Dad noticed I’d turned and followed my gaze.

“Do you want a picture, bud?”

I wanted to scream at him for even thinking I’d want to be anywhere near that thing, but fear wrapped its fingers around my throat. I couldn’t even manage to shake my head. My dad’s face dropped into a frown.

“Come on, buddy, don’t get shy on me.”

The line was short. Only half a dozen people separated us from him. Each photo took less and less time. He hardly looked at the kids, keeping his eyes locked on me. It was our turn before I knew it.

My dad handed me over to the mascot’s outstretched arms. I tensed up as his dark sockets looked down onto me. He’d never looked so scary to me. My dad snapped a picture and reached for me. The Mascot held tightly onto my hand.

 

In an instant, the bulldog pulled me to him and took off. My face was pressed into the scratchy fabric of his costume, obscuring my vision. Dad was screaming my name, but his voice trailed off as the bulldog took me further away from the crowd.

We were nearly out of the back gate when an officer tackled us. I flew from the mascot’s hands and slid across the grass. They fought for a moment before the Bulldog landed a shot across the officer's jaw and ran into the woods that neighbored the stadium. Just as he disappeared into the trees, I saw him glance back at me.

The police combed the woods for hours but only found the torn remnants of the mascot’s gloves. My parents, the student athletes, faculty, and the mascot’s parents were all questioned, but no one had any clue why the student, a boy named Ryan Osmond, would do such a thing, nor where he would be hiding. The cheerleaders reported he hadn’t even shown up for the pregame warm-up. He only showed up when the game started.

The game was canceled, and after hours of questioning, we were allowed to go home. Mom and Dad sent me straight to bed. I was out the second my head hit the pillow. My sleep was restless and filled with the image of that damned dog.

I woke shortly after in complete blackness. I squinted and glanced around the room, but only saw the vague features of my furniture. I was about to drift off again when I heard the squeal of old hinges. A streak of moonlight crept through my window, illuminating the space in front of my closet. The door slid slowly on its hinges, revealing the complete darkness inside. From that darkness, I could hear deep, muffled breathing.

I sat frozen in fear. I couldn’t scream. I couldn’t breathe.

Slowly, his face crept out of the shadows, revealing his features one by one. His sagging gray skin. his toothy underbite. His hollow black eyes.

The light reflected off of something he held in his bare hand, now free from the suit.

His slow breathing began to hitch. A laugh echoed out of his oversized head. Then, for the first and only time, he spoke to me.

“Hello Tommy.”

I screamed.

The mascot was bathed in light as my dad tore into the room, brandishing a golf club. He swung, sending the metal head into the mascot's back. He let out a guttural cry, but my dad didn’t stop. He swung over and over, the club thudding and cracking as he pummeled the stranger in our home.

The Bulldog cowered, slowly crawling away from my dad. When my dad raised the club over his head, the bulldog managed to shove his knife into my dad’s foot. He grunted and stumbled back into the hallway. The bulldog turned and began charging back at me. I leapt out of bed and ran. He grabbed me by the collar with his free hand and thrust the knife down on me. I squirmed just enough for the knife to slice me in the arm but miss my body. The wound burned as if I’d been struck with fire. He raised it and thrust the blade again, this time embedding it in my forearm. White-hot pain erupted through my entire arm. I howled in pain. Tears clouded my vision as he tore the blade out of me. Through my blurred eyes, I swear the mascot's mouth curled into a giant grin. I waited for another strike, but only a sickening crack followed. The knife clattered to the ground next to me. Through my blurry vision, I could see my dad swinging the golf club again.

The mascot caught the club, and the two began fighting over it. I crawled towards them and grabbed the knife. I reared back and planted it directly in the center of the mascot’s oversized foot. A deep scream erupted from behind the mask. Dad used the opportunity and pushed the mascot backwards. He struggled but couldn’t resist with his injured foot. He stumbled back until he was pressed against my window. My dad took a step back before kicking the mascot square in the chest, sending him tumbling out of our home. His body hit the ground with a horrid thud.

My dad hobbled to me, sobbing just as hard as I was. He held me in his arms. Over his shoulder and through the window, I could see the Bulldog lying flat on his stomach. His back rose in spastic breath.

Slowly, he struggled to his feet and turned back to the window. His hollow eyes locked mine for a moment that felt like hours. He turned and hobbled towards the woods, but only made it a few steps before he collapsed. This time, he stayed down. The police arrived soon after. My mom called them while my father fought the Bulldog. They unmasked him to reveal not the face of a teenager, but the disgraced science teacher who had brought my family so much turmoil.

They rushed him to the hospital. He had 3 broken ribs, a collapsed lung, a concussion, numerous bruises, and a stab wound in his foot, but he lived. Dad and I were both looked at. My arm required several stitches, a cast, and physical therapy. The whole event sent me back into an anxious spiral. I didn’t start school until I was seven. I’ve worked with multiple therapists over the years, and ultimately, I got over it. The events of that night have largely stayed in the shadows of my mind, but recent events have brought them to the surface.

Mr. Clark was charged and convicted of attempted kidnapping, assault of an officer, breaking and entering, attempted murder, and probably some other stuff. In all, he was sentenced to 25 years in prison. He got out two weeks ago.

I wasn’t keeping up with his time because, frankly, I had put that part of my life behind me, until I received an envelope in the mail. It was devoid of any typical markings and only had my name scribbled on the front. Inside was a torn page from my parents’ photo album showing me in the hands of the mascot. I called the police and sent them to my childhood home, where they found my dad. He was barely breathing, a bloody golf club at his side. They don’t know if he’ll make it.

There are police circling my block, searching up and down for any sign of him. I know that they’re doing the best possible job they can, that they’re doing everything they can to keep him away from me, but I can’t stop fear from taking hold of me. Deep in my mind, I hear that fabric-muffled breathing. I keep trying to clear it from my mind, but sometimes it sounds like it’s coming from inside my house.
