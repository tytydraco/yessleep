I broke the only rule he gave me.

He told me not to open the basement door.

I opened it anyway.

I keep telling myself that if I hadn’t, none of this would’ve happened. But I don’t actually know if that’s true. It might’ve happened either way.

I just know something is wrong now.

I found the job in a local housesitting Facebook group. The pay was higher than most listings, but not high enough to seem fake. I checked everything before agreeing. His profile wasn’t new. Tagged photos going back years. A LinkedIn that matched his name and employer. Property records confirmed he owned the house.

It was in a quiet subdivision outside Lansing, at the end of a cul-de-sac. The kind of neighborhood where nothing dramatic ever happens.

That’s what makes this feel worse.

Daniel answered the door himself. Mid-forties. Calm. Clean-cut. The kind of guy you’d forget five minutes after meeting him.

He said his wife had already left because of a family emergency.

I never saw her.

The house felt staged. That’s the only word I can think of. No clutter. No half-open mail. No dishes in the sink. Even the trash can was empty.

It didn’t feel lived in.

It felt arranged.

There was a thin folder on the kitchen counter labeled “House Instructions.”

Inside was one sheet of paper with the Wi-Fi password and alarm code written neatly at the top.

At the bottom, in different handwriting — shakier — someone had written:

“I didn’t open it.”

I almost asked him about that.

I didn’t.

When we got to the basement door, he stopped walking.

“You won’t need anything down there,” he said.

“Okay.”

“There’s a locked room in the basement. Don’t try to open it.”

It wasn’t dramatic. Just flat.

Then he added, almost like it slipped out:

“Even if you hear something.”

That made my stomach drop.

“What would I hear?” I asked.

“You won’t,” he said. “The dog doesn’t go down there.”

That didn’t answer my question, but I nodded anyway.

He left about fifteen minutes later.

The house felt bigger after he drove away.

Too quiet.

The first two nights were normal.

Murphy followed me everywhere. Slept outside the guest room door. He wasn’t nervous. He didn’t bark at nothing.

On the third night, I heard something from below.

Not loud. Just a shift. Like something heavy moving.

Murphy’s head snapped up immediately.

He stared toward the hallway.

I stood there longer than I want to admit, just listening.

The house felt too solid for that kind of sound. Too quiet. Like there shouldn’t be that much space under us for something to move around.

I told myself it was pipes. Or the furnace. Or the house settling.

I went back to bed.

At 2:13 AM, I woke up to three knocks.

Slow.

Evenly spaced.

I checked the time because it felt deliberate.

2:13.

Murphy was already awake. A low growl in his chest. Not barking. Just staring toward the hallway.

The knocks were three seconds apart.

I didn’t count the first night.

I counted the second.

Because it happened again.

The next night.

2:13.

Three knocks.

Three seconds apart.

That’s when I texted Daniel.

“Hey — random question. Does the basement make noise at night?”

His reply came almost immediately.

“Do not go downstairs.”

That was it.

I told him I wasn’t planning to.

He sent one more message.

“It should be finished soon.”

Finished.

I didn’t ask what that meant.

Murphy stopped walking past the basement door after that.

He’d pause, stare at it, then take the longer route through the kitchen.

That night, I wedged a dining chair under the knob. I don’t know what I thought it would do.

At 2:13, the knocks came again.

The doorknob moved.

Slowly.

Back and forth.

Not violently. Just enough to know it wasn’t random.

My phone buzzed.

“Please don’t interfere.”

Another message followed.

“It’s almost done.”

I typed, “Done with what?” but he never answered.

The knob stopped moving.

The house went completely still.

The next morning, the chair was back at the table.

Pushed in neatly.

I remember dragging it across the tile.

That’s when I decided to look.

The basement door was open slightly.

I’m certain I latched it.

The smell hit first. Metallic. Like pennies.

The lights were already on.

I don’t remember turning them on.

The basement itself looked normal. Storage bins. Shelves.

Except for the reinforced door at the far end.

A deadbolt.

Scratches around the inside of the frame.

Inside.

That’s when my phone buzzed again.

“You weren’t supposed to look.”

I turned toward the stairs.

The basement door at the top was closed.

I didn’t close it.

I ran halfway up and grabbed the handle.

It wouldn’t turn.

Then came three knocks.

Behind me.

Three seconds apart.

A voice followed.

“Please.”

It sounded exhausted.

Human.

The handle in front of me rattled.

Not from upstairs.

Right there.

Something was between me and the rest of the house.

My phone buzzed again.

“It wasn’t ready to switch yet.”

Switch.

I don’t know why that word scared me more than anything else.

Footsteps moved across the living room above me.

Then I heard my own voice.

Calling Murphy.

Murphy started barking.

From upstairs.

With it.

Behind me, the locked door shook.

“Don’t let him out,” the voice whispered.

The basement handle turned in my hand.

Unlocked.

I don’t remember unlocking it.

I don’t remember deciding to open it.

But I did.

The stairs were empty.

Murphy sat at the top step.

Calm.

Like nothing had happened.

My phone buzzed again.

A message.

From my own number.

There was a photo attached.

It was me.

Standing in the basement.

Facing the locked door.

Taken from behind.

Timestamped three minutes earlier.

Another message followed.

“Thank you.”

I left before sunrise.

The house is listed for sale now.

No basement photos.

The description ends with:

“Access upon request only.”

When I got home, my apartment felt different.

Cleaner.

Too straight.

There’s a door at the end of my hallway now.

It wasn’t there before.

I know it wasn’t.

It has a deadbolt.

At 2:13 this morning—

I heard three knocks.

Three seconds apart.

From inside.

I haven’t opened it.

I don’t know if that makes a difference anymore.
