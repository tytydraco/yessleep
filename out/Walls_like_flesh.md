I used to work for a private surveying company that mapped old municipal tunnel systems under Northern California. Most of the work was boring. Storm drains, utility access routes, runoff chambers. You’d spend ten hours underground breathing mold and rat shit just to update a city blueprint nobody would ever look at.

Three months ago, my company got contracted to inspect an abandoned tunnel grid beneath a section of Fairfield slated for redevelopment.

The city records called it a “decommissioned flood diversion system.”

That was the first lie.

There were four of us sent down there:

\- me

\- Luis

\- Harper

\- our supervisor, Dennis

The entrance was hidden behind an old pumping station fenced off from the public. The weird thing was the lock.

It looked new.

Not rusty. Not abandoned.

Brand new steel.

Dennis joked that maybe homeless people were squatting down there. None of us laughed very hard because the place already felt wrong before we even entered. The air coming out of the access shaft smelled warm. Not hot. Warm. Like breath.

The tunnel itself didn’t match the city schematics.

According to the maps, there should’ve been one central runoff corridor splitting into three branches.

Instead, there were dozens.

The walls looked older the deeper we went. Concrete turned to brick. Brick turned to something else entirely. Smooth black surfaces that reflected our flashlights strangely, almost wet-looking.

About thirty minutes in, Luis stopped walking.

He just stood there staring down a side passage.

Dennis asked what was wrong.

Luis said, very quietly:

“Do you guys hear that?”

We all stopped.

At first I thought it was water moving through pipes.

Then I realized it sounded like people talking from very far away.

Not echoes.

Conversations.

Harper laughed nervously and said sound travels weird underground. But the deeper we went, the louder it got.

Hundreds of overlapping voices.

Whispering.

Crying.

Some laughing.

At one point I heard someone say my name.

Not shouted.

Whispered directly into my ear.

I spun around so fast I slammed my shoulder into the wall.

Nobody was there.

I grabbed and massaged my sore shoulder. 

Dennis wanted to turn back after that, but our radios stopped working around the same time. Full static. No signal at all.

That’s when we found the markings.

Names carved into the tunnel walls.

Thousands of them.

Not graffiti.

Precise cuts.

Some looked over a hundred years old.

Others were fresh.

Still covered in dust.

Luis started panicking. He kept saying we needed to leave because he recognized one of the names.

His brother.

The fuck?

The problem with that was his brother was still alive.

Or at least he was supposed to be.

Things got bad after we crossed the flooded section.

The voices stopped sounding distant.

They sounded close enough to touch.

I heard crying directly behind me for almost ten straight minutes, but every time I turned around there was nothing there except the tunnel stretching behind us.

Then Harper disappeared.

No scream.

No footsteps.

One second he was behind me.

The next second he wasn’t.

Dennis started yelling his name while Luis kept saying we had to keep moving because “they notice you when you stop.”

I asked him what the hell he meant by that.

He looked at me like I was stupid.

“You can feel them looking at you, right?”

And honestly?

I could.

That was the moment I realized the tunnel wasn’t empty.

Not physically empty.

The walls started changing after that.

I know how insane this sounds, but parts of the surface looked soft. Veins underneath it. Pulsing slowly.

Like the entire place was alive.

We found Harper eventually.

Or most of him.

He was standing mangled chest-deep in black water in a maintenance chamber, facing the wall.

Completely still.

Dennis climbed down to get him and immediately started screaming.

Not from fear.

Pain.

He clawed at his own face hard enough to tear skin off while yelling that he could “see through them.”

I don’t know what that means.

I hear his voice saying it in my sleep. Over and over.

Luis dragged me out before I could help.

Maybe he saved my life.

Maybe he didn’t.

Because ever since that night, things haven’t stopped. Ive been fucking traumatized. 

Sometimes when I close my eyes, I see tunnels.

Not dreams.

Memories.

But like... not mine?

Does that make sense? I know it doesnt...

But I’ve seen people drowning underground. Workers sealed behind cave-ins. Children crying in darkness. Faces pressed against walls that move and FEEL like skin.

Last week I heard my own voice whisper my name while I was trying to fall asleep.

Yesterday I found mud in my bathtub drain.

Black mud.

The same kind from the tunnel.

I live alone.

I found out Luis killed himself two weeks after the survey.

No note.

Just one sentence carved into his apartment wall.

\>"NOT DEAD. STILL DOWN THERE!"

The city halted redevelopment around the pumping station after “structural concerns.”

That was the official statement.

But yesterday I drove past the site.

The fence is gone now.

And the hatch is open.
