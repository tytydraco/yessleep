**Part two: twenty-two years later (every respect to the film of similar title…)**

Thank you to those who took an interest in my last post, which would almost have

been nothing more than a throw-away vent had u/Fund_Me_PLEASE not asked me

about the cause of death - which in turn sparked more questions and intrigue in my

mind.

Back then, the coroner – almost two years later because the wheels of the court

grind slowly – came up with a verdict of hypothermia. But who could believe it?

What, four healthy, presumably sane members of the same family bury themselves

inside snowmen and wait patiently to die of the cold? Without disturbing the snow

around them? Even if others were involved, how did they not disturb the snow?

The verdict was stupid – but they don’t have a verdict that reads, “We haven’t got a

bloody clue”…

With all these questions in mind, I decided that this was the perfect opportunity to

take a trip down memory lane. And maybe even get enough material for a podcast

that might actually get me some ad. revenue. (Yeah – all these years later, I’m still

skint.) So yesterday, I packed up my gear into my 2003 Ford Fiesta with the bumper

duct-taped in place and began the four hour drive to my old home town.

Six hours later, I made it. The roads leading in were gridlocked. Apparently, transport

lorries full of cows heading to the auction house had crashed just before the town

boundary, releasing their bovine passengers on to the main roads – evidence of

which was shown in cowpats everywhere you turned.



As I finally arrived at the boundary I was instantly reunited with my mate. As the only

police officer in town, he was now also acting as the only traffic warden and the only

riot officer, as a mob of furious cattle farmers and distressed lorry drivers were

arguing about liability.

“Wasn’t my fault – as we got near, the bloody cows were kicking hell out of the semi-

trailer! They’ve wrecked the door – who’s going to pay for that?”

“If any of my cows can’t be sold, I’ll have your balls! The doors can’t have been

properly secured!” yelled one of the farmers, squaring up to the driver.

“Enough!” bellowed my mate, stepping between them in a completely foolhardy

manner. “We need to focus on rounding up these cows, or none of us are going

anywhere. Save the threats for later – they’ve all bloody scarpered!”

Amused by the shit-show going on, I pulled my car up on to the side of the road to let

other cars get by and turned the engine off to watch as PC Plod and the farmers ran

around the fields trying to gather up cows – but every time they drove a cow back

close to the border of town where the vehicles were, the cows would immediately

storm off in the other direction.

Knowing what I know now, it occurs to me that not only were the roads and fields just

before town completely snow-free, unlike the town itself, but also that this particular

entrance into town was the start of the property line of what once was the Dacres’

house. Finally, after one of the herders gained a logical braincell and moved the

trucks closer to the cows, the issue was resolved relatively quickly. But as the

auction had been and gone, they all drove off rather furious at the entire waste of

time and money.

My mate, who had already been throwing dirty looks at me as I laughed at his efforts,

walked over to my car.

“Is there a problem, officer?” I asked innocently.

“You’re a real dick!” He grinned at me.

“Lovely to see you too, Louis,” I laughed. “Pub?”

Upon finally entering town for real, I couldn’t help but notice how thick the snow was.

This is supposed to be the start of Spring, but here in the dale there was still calf-

deep snow everywhere. Beyond the town, all was green – even the hill-tops were

green - but within the town, every sound seemed muffled under a thick, white

blanket. And as for the snowmen… they were everywhere – on every lawn,

pavement corner, allotment. It was as if everyone had forgotten those snowmen and

their dead inhabitants. I felt sick.

I pulled up at Louis’s home, where he introduced me to his wife and five-year-old

daughter. I really had been out of touch for too long… Louis changed out of uniform

and we headed for the pub. I was surprised to see that even the pub’s entrance had



a generously sized snowman right next to the main entrance; some one had

adorned it with a flat cap.

Inside, having bought two pints at the bar, we sat down at a small table just within

the warm aura of the log fire. Of course, the first thing I asked him about was the

photo he sent me.

“Oh, don’t get me started!” Louis groaned.

I noticed, as he took a long swig from his pint, that his hand was shaking.

“So, when I sent you that photo – ‘cos I really felt I had to – I contacted the city lot

and sang the song about the mysterious deaths, and the sign about ‘Still the best in

show’ – because surely that hinted at it being the same murderer, yeah? So they

come out, mob-handed, photographers, CSI, forensics and all of that, and

investigate the snowmen. And what do they find? Snow. Just snow. Nowt else.” He

swallowed another large draught. “They took my dignity. And my budget. I’m a

laughing-stock…”

“Is that why you were playing the Lone Ranger this morning and didn’t send for

help?” I asked, sympathetically, for a change.

“They wouldn’t have sent anyone… I might as well live at that North entrance,” he

added gloomily.

“Why?”

“The amount of stuff that happens there: folk getting thrown from their horses, drivers

losing focus and crashing their cars, you name it. One of the weirdest things is the

dogs…”

“Dogs?” I asked, confused.

“Yeah, anytime one of the dogs in town leaves by the north entrance on a walk or

anything, they break free from their leads and pelt for their lives. There’s a regular

dog group led by a bunch of retired folk, but one day they took their dogs for a walk

along the fields past the north entrance, and when they turned back, all twelve dogs

broke free and ran!”

“Twelve dogs ran off all at once?”

“Yeah, and add those twelve to the notice board in the station I had to put up

specifically for missing dogs and it totals forty-eight! And once they’ve gone… you

don’t find them again.”

“Jesus! I didn’t think there were that many in town!”

“I tell you, mate, some seriously weird shit is going on around here, and I’m too much

of a laughing-stock for anyone to take me seriously. I really needed to tell a friend



who might actually listen.” He gazed at me, unusually serious, and I could tell how

much this was taking out of him.

“Well, I’m here now. Consider me deputised,” I said in jest.

“Don’t be daft, we’re not in America,” Louis laughed. “But if you want to poke around

a bit and see if you can find some answers…” His voice was low, and though he tried

to joke, I could hear the undertone of desperation.

We stayed long into the evening talking and drinking, reminiscing over old times and

filling each other in on all we had done. Well, Louis mainly filled me in on what he’d

achieved – God, I really dropped the ball on the whole life thing.

We stumbled out of the pub at closing time, the air crisp in our lungs. The landlady

laughed at the state of us as she shovelled rock salt on to her walkway outside the

pub under the cold, watchful gaze of the flat-capped snowman.

As we turned the corner onto Louis’s street, we were instantly sobered by a piercing,

inhuman shriek, followed by the scream of the landlady.

We ran back towards the pub, skidding and slipping on the ice and snow. As we

raced to the rescue, we arrived to find the landlady smashing the flat-capped

snowman to pieces with her shovel, sheer terror on her face.

It took us some minutes to disarm her of her impromptu weapon and to calm her

hysterical sobbing.

“It screamed! It screamed at me! My salt hit it and it screamed at me!”

Louis and I looked at each other, uneasily. Whatever had happened had turned her

jollity into terror in seconds.

I stared, in horrified fascination, at the half-demolished snowman. I might be

misremembering because I had had a skinful, but I couldn’t help but think that some

of the snow looked discoloured and glassy. And there was a sulphurous smell, as if

of burning.

What the hell have I come home to?
