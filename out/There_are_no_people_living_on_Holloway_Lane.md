My girlfriend and I had moved into a lovely three bed detached house, nestled on a quiet rural street, just out of town. Honestly when the estate agent old us the asking price, our jaws hit the floor.



We questioned whether it was too good to be true, expecting all manner of hidden issues, but we’d lived there for two months with almost no problems, at least none with the house.



Our street bordered two fairly expansive agricultural acreages and with them cutting us off from the main body of our small town, the area had little foot or vehicle traffic. That being said, for some reason, since our move in date, I hadn’t seen another soul.



Yes, there were the unmistakable sounds of people, in their homes, on the street or even the sounds of passing vehicles, but at no time did I ever emerge from the property to see them in person.



Sophie didn’t seem to find that concerning, especially being an introvert at heart, simply says they might not be occupied. Still, apart from the strange feeling of being watch, which I chalked up to being the new family, something else also caught me off guard.



Our dog Ronny was an energetic and frankly loud lab, which is part of the reason we were looking for a property with a large garden, though for some reason, he wouldn’t go outside.



Opening the side door and even clipping him up for a walk, Ronny would fight to stay inside. The monumental flip from a high-octane outdoorsy animal, to a quiet reserved inside dog was just bizarre.



I’d occasionally persuade him out for a short toilet run in the light of day, but as soon as visibility dropped, he was locked to Sophie’s side, almost cowering in fear of something unseen.



It came to a head when I tried to get him out before bed and it took all my strength to wrestle the squirming, flailing animal out to the door. In the process, he nipped me which is something he’d never shown any tendencies of before moving.



Sophie said he might have developed a fear of the dark due to the new environment, but I wasn’t buying that. Still, content to agree I fitted a motion activated light to our backdoor, though that didn’t entice him to out.  



Working from home, as I’m a self-employed carpenter, I'd often find long discarded items as I sought to clean up my new workspace. 



Rummaging around my workspace, just trying to catch up with a series of slowly piling commissions, I found a small picture frame. Expecting it was simply an item left by a previous occupant, I rotated it in my hand. 

  
Though the image had sustained noticeable water damage, I imaged it showed a family. Obscured at the back were the two parents with their daughter stood front an centre, as her long black hair osculated in the breeze of what looked to be a beautiful day. 

  
With a great view of the road from my workshop, I’d occasionally catch the faintest inkling of eyes on me and find myself reacting to any slight sound of movement on our street. 



Rushing out to the gate like a crazed person, the street would be as desolate as ever. Sometimes I felt as if the otherwise ambient sounds of people doing their thing, was intended to lure me out.



The more I focused on that oddity the more I started to notice. Every single property would have a light of some sort on in the evenings.



Whether it was a lounge, kitchen or bedroom light, it was evident people were actively switching them on and off. Weirder still, their cars would move, though I’d never catch them departing or returning. Moving vans, people parked on the street, even a motorbike on the drive.



I stayed up for two consecutive nights, just watching, waiting for someone to emerge, but nothing.



Slightly strange I know, but I started cataloguing everything. Each house number had a tally. Anything from which room had lights on, when the cars were missing and at what time they returned.



My work stalled as the strangeness of our street took a twisted precedent. The worst thing though, were unmistakable sounds of people talking. Each noise mimicked human speech patterns exactly, though the words spoken were jumbled, matted.



In each case they were just loud enough to be heard but not fully understood, with an instance where I swear, I could hear out estate agent mixed in amongst.



In the day, the street was silent with no motion, so long as I observed. However, an echoing cacophony of family chatter filled those vacant rooms as the night drew closer.



Though I’m ashamed to admit it, I peaked.



Watching through those otherwise animate widows, there was nothing life-like. The rooms lay bare, filled with just enough furniture to seem lived in from the outside, but sanitised, stale.



Some part of me wanted to try the handle, just to see if there were really people in, but I stopped myself. Some primal part of my brain screamed for me to back away and I’m thankful I did.



I couldn’t help but bring it up to Sophie again and she gave me that same tired response. To her, this was nothing more than another ramble about an issue I’d crafted, but I was unsettled.



I tried to drop it in to conversation with my supplier, cashiers and even the homeless guy outside of the superstore, but nobody seemed to have a clue, skirting around the question and giving no clear answer.  



Arriving home from a later than expected delivery to a client out of town, I clambered from my car, oblivious to the dead street around me. The far-off bark of a dog or mumbled chatter from next door didn’t do anything to tempt me as it had the days prior.



Glancing up at the glow of a bedroom across the street, a silhouette caught my eye. I’d been so used to the lack of expectation that I didn’t even compute what the figure might have been.



Though, as my tired synapses fired, my attention refocused. Stood in the second story bedroom was a woman. That or a girl, though I couldn’t make out their face fully she had long flowing black hair.



Slightly excited that I’d actually seen a living person in one of those houses, I waved. I don’t know if it was simply a subconscious reaction or my brain seeking conformation that I wasn’t hallucinating.



To my surprise, she waved back. A slower, more unnatural motion, as if she were copying an action she couldn’t fully comprehend, but real movement none the less.



Reiterating that to Sophie, I expected her to drop the sarcastic ‘I told you so’, but her nonchalant response confused me.



“You joking? That couple moved out last week, didn’t you see the van?”



Racking my brain again, I was left with more questions, though an accident drew my focus. Some fairly loud yelling had worked Ronny up, causing him to relieve himself on the kitchen floor.



Panicking as the stream continued, I rushed him to the door, hoping he’d willingly finish up outside as the shouts still echoed back.



Dropping like a rock, he fixed himself to the tiled floor holding firm as I tried fruitlessly to yank him closer. Fortunately, he’d stop by that time, but just to be sure he still needed to go out.



Knowing he wasn’t going to budge without a fight, I opted to step out into the fluorescent glow of the porch light, hoping that would satiate his fears.



Stepping through the open doorway, the short click of the bulb returning to life silenced those scrambled barks into faint murmurs, trailing off as I emerged into the light.



Taking a hand full of steps to the perimeter illuminated porch, I turned to the now petrified dog. His wide yellow eyes shot past me into the shadowed black of our garden.



Not as sensitive as his, my eyes didn’t outline that gang of misshapen silhouettes at first. Staring deeper into the inky black, I quickly grasped what they were.



Anywhere from five to ten humanoid shapes glared back through blank visages. Each one jerked in a spastic twitch as their forms fluctuated just enough to mimic the natural rhythms of a human body, though far too rigid to be flesh.



Feeling each ice-cold sweat bead dribble from my brow, we were locked in place for who knows how long. Each second I stood petrified, the silence stirred. The group muttered, each sound getting louder and louder, though far too scattered to be decipherable.



Mustering the courage to take one step backwards, still locked in on their forms, they advanced at an identical pace. Sadly, that prolonged period of petrification had triggered the resulting click.



Instantaneously my body flung itself back for the door, reaching for the handle as the porchlight returned to life once again.



Slamming that brittle barrier closed behind me, I collapsed against it as they called. Lounder than before, enough to draw Sophie’s attention.



She questioned what I was doing, but I couldn’t mumble out the right string of words to get to understand. My jumbled sputters were not dissimilar to those still human adjacent calls outside.



Pushing me back away from the barrier I’d held in place, she stepped to the frame.



It was almost as if I knew what she was about to do, but as time stood still, I was powerless. In the same way your body alerts you the millisecond before your alarm blares, I knew what she was reaching for.



Paralyzed, I could do nothing but watch that frail wooden door implode the moment she twisted the handle, being swallowed up by the advancing mass.



Turning through tear filled eyes, I bolted for the safest place I know, like a coward. Not turning back, his yelps chasing up the stairs as those calls reverberated back at me.



Slamming the door and heaving my bed up, their croaks quickly reached my makeshift blockade. Though I couldn’t be sure how strong or cognitive they were, it didn’t matter.



Mixed in with the jumbled mess of voices, still regurgitating broken sounds, was her soft tones. Unmistakable in the amalgam symphony, I knew what she wanted.

  
I questioned opening that door, but no matter how much I may have once cared for the person they'd once signified, I knew they were as hollow as this street. 



As soon as I relayed my address, the operator on the other end cut the line, leaving me with no hope of salvation. I doubt I'm the first and I scarcely believe I'll be the last. 



Right now, I have no way out. I can hear a new wave outside the window, though they’re obscured by the descent below.



If I make it until the morning maybe they’ll disperse, avoid being fully viewed, but that a long shot. I doubt they’ll let me leave so easily.



Maybe if you’re here next, moving into a beautiful property that’s just too good to be true, you’ll hear me.



Another puppeteered voice just audible enough to give the illusion of an unassuming neighbourhood.



Leave while you have the chance to.



There are no people living on Holloway Lane.
