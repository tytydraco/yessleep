Ramping up in frequency and intensity. Like the Shadow Man, as I’d come to call him, instinctively knew when I was alone and defenseless.

Many hours of rest were lost to sleep paralysis as the entity stalked me in my room- just standing there silently observing me. The terror that resulted from the near constant nightly visits plagued me, following me around in the waking world. It was ruining my life.

I was severely sleep deprived, forgetful, irritable, exhausted. My hair started to thin and fall out and I lost twenty-five pounds in the span of a month and a half. 

My job performance suffered. I started messing up all the time. Falling asleep at my desk while on calls with clients, yelling at my coworkers for typing too loud, small things like that quickly added up. When I’d get out of a mood, I’d apologize and explain the situation at home. Unfortunately, just like everybody else in my life, nobody believed me. There was fake sympathy, but I could tell by all the tight lipped smiles and judging eyes they all thought I was crazy too.

It got to the point where management and HR got involved. Since I’d been a good and loyal worker for the past fifteen years, my job forced me to take a brief leave of absence to rest and recuperate instead of outright terminating me. Everyone blamed it on the divorce, that it must’ve fractured my psyche- inducing a mental breakdown of sorts. I was informed that when I got back I’d be placed on a PIP- Performance Improvement Plan. If I couldn’t get my act together then, that was it for me and I’d get canned. Given the pink slip. Fired. That simply wasn’t an option, so begrudgingly, I did as instructed. But I wanted to be anywhere but that fucking house. 

Finances were tight so I couldn’t move, hence why I bought the damned thing in the first place. Obviously the realtor hadn’t disclosed the full history of the house- hindsight is now 20/20- but I couldn’t pass up the price and it was close to Spencer’s school. Not like there was anywhere else I could go anyway. There were no friends for me to turn to. My mother and the rest of the family lived on the other side of the country. Bridges had been burned with Rob and his family. But, more importantly, I didn’t want to leave since staying in the house was good when it came to court and keeping majority custody of Spencer. The illusion of home stability is everything to the government and was the sole reason why I didn’t just quit my job.

One early morning on my leave, after another sleepless night, my body was exhausted- my brain even more so. My neurons were on the fritz, firing off like millions of cannons, launching little steel balls every which way, keeping me awake. Needing rest, I figured I'd go out on a run- try to tire myself out enough to the point my brain couldn’t help but shut down. Intense flashes of the nightmares that night still lingered on the back of my eyelids every time I blinked. This only motivated me to run faster. 

“How’s my prediction coming along? Any closer to moving out of that old place?” Maggie joked, yelling from across the street, waving me over on my way back. Birds chirped a sweet tune, the sun about to crest the horizon- vanquishing the dreary light of dawn with it. “You look like shit,” she stated after getting a good look at me.

“Thanks. Feel like it too,” I chuckled, tucking a wiry wisp of hair behind my ear. Gerald waved at me from the seat next to his wife while taking a sip out of his mug. I waved back.

“Want some coffee?”

Despite it seeming like a bad idea to ingest caffeine in my current state, I accepted. With a nod and a smile, Maggie headed in to fix me a fresh cup. Feeling awkward just standing at the bottom of their porch, I made my way up and leaned against the wooden railing, rubbing my hands up and down my upper arms to generate some warmth in the cool morning air. 

Gerald just cleared his throat while downing another sip of coffee and picking up that day's newspaper to read through it, paying me no mind. His cold demeanor seemed to be a complete one-eighty from how our last interaction ended. He probably just woke up and was cranky, I reasoned. He did look pretty tired and worn out. 

Why did I care? And why did he seem so… *familiar?*

The sunrise basked the porch in a soft glowing light, illuminating the man. He wore an aged wife beater and gray basketball shorts underneath a blue and green striped robe. His hair seemed different from what I remembered, almost saltier, like there was dust or something in it. It was faint, but a stale smell wafted off him.

Maggie interrupted my thoughts when she came back out and handed me the much welcomed warm drink. It helped that the coffee tasted good. It reminded me of my favorite brand. Speaking of, I made a mental note to get more on my next grocery run. The new bag I bought seemingly disappeared overnight. As much as I’d love to blame this one on the ghost, admittedly, I had been consuming more of the stimulant than intended lately. 

After another sip or two, I practically chugged the rest of the mug. Unfortunately, I did not get the much needed energy boost I’d been hoping for. Nevertheless, I wiped my mouth off and set the cup down on the railing, giving Maggie and Gerald my thanks.

“Glad you liked it,” she said, sitting back down. “Gerald picked up a new brand for us to try. Bones Coffee, I think it’s called. Have you heard of it?”

By the time they were desperate enough to ask for our help again, it was already too late,” Gerald ended, eerily.

A chill crawled up my spine, an unsettling feeling overcoming me as a pit settled in my stomach. His face was stoic, his tone serious. There was just something about Gerald that compelled me to believe him.

A nervous chuckle left my mouth. Let’s just say my relationship with religion is complicated after an unpleasant experience in my youth.  “I-I’ll have to check my schedule," I told him, excusing myself. “I really should get back though. That was an intense run and I’ve already stunk up your porch enough.”

My mother sighed on the other end of the line. The click of her bedside lamp being turned on barely audible. “I'm sure he’ll be home any minute, hon. You gave me a couple frights back in the day, but you turned out just fine.”

What if he had something to do with this? It had been pretty tame in the house lately, perhaps it was because he had been planning something? I shuddered to think about The Shadow Man possessing my only son.

“Do I need to come out there?” She asked, in a tired and raspy voice. “I’ve been meaning to come and visit. Maybe I can talk a little sense into him.”

“What makes you say that?” She questioned, incredulously. “Are you sure things are alright over there? T-this isn’t about the house again, is it?”

Not only was this the latest he’d ever dared to stay out, but I strongly suspected he’d snuck out multiple other times as well. He started to back talk to me, worse than ever before. Spencer started blasting Death Metal and Screamo music from behind his locked bedroom door into the late hours of the night when he was home. Until you can get your act together, no phone, computer, tv, video games, hanging out with friends, nothing. You come home from school, do your homework, then your chores, and go to bed.”

“That’s final. Now give me your phone and go to your room.”

He grunted angrily, standing up and digging his phone out of his pocket. Instead of complying with my demands, Spencer threw his phone to the ground- his screen cracking audibly. Then you could join the ghosts you’re always talking about and I could live with Dad forever!”

As he pushed past me to his room, right before he slammed and locked his door, I swear to God I saw The Shadow Man standing behind him, his darkness gripping my son’s shoulders tightly, pulling him away from me. His eyes shone, looking at me pleadingly. Then the lights flickered and it was gone. Spencer was gone. All that stared back at me were dark, soulless angry eyes.

A sinister, evil laugh emitted from its throat as the door closed, “No, no I’m not!”

I sat in the resulting silence for a moment before I imploded, slamming my front door behind me so hard I cracked the frame as I stormed off. Once outside, even the breeze couldn’t calm the heated buzz in my blood. Like a woman on a mission, I marched over to Maggie and Gerald’s house, banging on their front door until someone answered.

Knowing my son was possessed by a demon was worse than being molested by our pastor for years, being gaslit into thinking it was all my fault. I needed to save my baby before he was lost forever.

Father Arnold came highly recommended by Maggie and Gerald’s pastor as the best exorcist around. He was tall but lithe, with short and well maintained brown hair. He was very official looking in his clerical collar and vestments when he showed up at my front door. His demeanor stiffened as he stepped through the threshold of the door after I invited him in.

“There is a presence here,” he tensed, setting a leather briefcase down on the coffee table. “I can feel it.”

I nodded my head, finally feeling vindicated after all this time. I wiped a tear from my eye as I recounted the events leading up to the previous night- how that thing threatened to kill me.

“Show me the boy,” he said, getting right down to business. I led him over to Spencer’s room, knocking lightly before opening the door. His room was dark, darker than it should be at eleven am. I tried his light, no luck. The bulb must’ve busted.

“What do you want?” He groaned, turning over in his bed.

“To help you, my child,” Father Arnold answered.

This stirred a reaction out of Spencer, who quickly sat up. “Mom? What is he talking about? What’s going on?”

“Come to the living room, Spence,” I said softly, trying my best to smile sweetly. “We just want to talk.”

I prayed I wouldn’t have to drag him out kicking and screaming, and something out there answered as Spence hesitantly followed us out into the living room. He sat down in the same spot I’d lectured him in last night, wearing one of his dad’s t-shirts and a pair of fuzzy pajama bottoms. 

“Are you a God fearing man, Spencer, was it?” Father Arnold asked, clearing his throat. He opened his briefcase and carefully took out a bible, placing it in the middle of the table. He stared at the book then to Spencer, expectantly.

“M-mom?” He stuttered, looking at me, nervously.

“Don’t worry, sweetie,” I reassured and soothed him, handing him a cup of tea Father Arnold instructed me to make using a vial of herbs he’d brought. It was supposed to calm him, he’d told me.

For the first time in a long while, Spencer did as asked without making a fuss or fighting back. He gulped the drink down in one swallow.

“Well?” The Father prompted.

Spencer slurred his speech as he tried to answer, “Uhhhsh-” the tranquilizer taking effect rather quickly.

“Don’t worry,” Father Arnold laid Spencer down on the couch as his eyeballs rolled to the back of his head, petting his hair gently, “if you weren’t before, you will be.”

After confirming that Spencer was unconscious, Father Arnold and I carefully carried him back into his room, where his arms and legs were bound tightly to the bed frame with fabric. I stifled a sob, looking at my son all tied up like that. The only reason I was able to make it through was by reminding myself that it wasn't Spencer lying on that bed, that this was for his own good. To save him. 

Once all the preparations were finished getting set up, Father Arnold tore the curtains open, flooding the room with bright light. This caused the thing in the bed to stir. It tugged at its restraints. Confused and coming to, it said “I- I can’t move. Mom, help I can’t move! What’s going on, what are you doing to me?” It started to cry, tugging at its restraints harder, jerking the bed frame against the wall, violently. 

“Sp- Spencer?” I asked, my breath catching in my throat. I held out my hand, taking a step forward.

Father Arnold waved me back. “Don’t listen to it. That’s not your son, it’s trying to lure you into a false sense of security.”

“What are you talking about?” He yelled, looking around, shielding his eyes from the bright light. “This is crazy!”

“Quiet demon,” Father Arnold scolded, “I command you, in the name of Jesus Christ, to come out of that boy!”

The thing wearing my son’s face laughed, as if this was all some big joke. “Mom, do you really think I’m possessed? Seriously?”

“I’m- I’m sorry,” I said, biting my lip, turning away.

Father Arnold glared at me. “Do not speak to it,” he demanded.

“It’s so convincing,” I whispered.

“That’s what makes them so dangerous.” The exorcist then dug a bottle of holy water out of his briefcase, flicking the liquid all over Spencer. “What is your name, demon? Who do you serve?” 

“Oh, for the love of- I. AM. NOT. POSSESSED!” It screeched, tugging harder and harder at its binds, trying to escape. The bedframe smacked against the wall so hard, cracks began to form in the plaster. It looked like it was in pain from the holy water searing its skin. I could’ve sworn I saw small whisps of smoke furl into the air.

“Be gone demon! I beseech thee!”

“HELP! HELP! SOMEBODY HELP ME! I’M BEING HELD AGAINST MY WILL!”

“I think it’s best you leave now,” Father Arnold told me, motioning for me to leave the room. “There’s a lot of work ahead of us. It’s not something a mother should see.”

“O-okay,” I stammered, wiping tears from my cheeks. It was shattering me, hear him scream like that. “Just give me back my boy.”

“THEY’RE TORTURING ME!!!!!”

“And he said to them, ‘this kind cannot be driven out by anything but prayer,” Father Arnold said, solemnly, quoting Mark 9:29.

Four days passed in agony as Father Arnold did his work, shouting tirelessly non stop, throwing holy water, and cleansing the air with sage and other holy herbs.

“Begone, Satan!” I’d hear him shout Matthew 4:10. “For it is written, “You shall worship the Lord your God and only him you should serve.”

Spencer's sobs would then fill the entire house.

At first I wasn’t sure, but the exorcism seemed to be working. The demon grew weaker with each day. Its skin was sweaty and pale, its figure gaunt. After the middle of day two, it started to spit the water I fed it back out. The same with food. By day three it stopped eating, drinking, and using the bathroom entirely. It would cry occasionally, but eventually even stopped doing that, ceasing all forms of communication too. Father Arnold reassured me this was good, that the demon was close to coming out.

It all came to a head in the late morning of day four. I sat on the floor outside of Spencer’s room, clutching my rosary beads so tight, they left imprints on the skin of my palms. I couldn’t sleep, I only prayed. Prayer after prayer after prayer filtered through my mind, hoping my son would be okay. 

From inside the room, Father Arnold recited forcefully Mark 16:17, “And these signs will accompany those who believe: in my name they will cast out demons…”

“P-please, stop. I don’t feel good,” It muttered softly, having spoken for the first time in over twenty-four hours. Then it broke out into an intense coughing fit. I ran in with a water bottle, the exorcist moving out of my way as I pressed the rim to his lips. Spencer opened his eyes, they were cloudy as he looked around, confused. I poured some leftover water on a rag and began to dab sweat from his forehead, stroking his hair as I did so. We were so close, I could feel it. The air was charged and electric. This was it, I was going to get my boy back.

“For he was saying to him, ‘Come out of the man, you unclean spirit!”” Father Arnold commanded grandly, pressing his palm to Spencer’s forehead. Upon his touch, Spencer started convulsing as his eyes rolled to the back of his head. “Help me hold him down,” the exorcist instructed, him holding down his shoulders, me his legs. “The demon is leaving, it has been cast back down to Hell!”

Spencer’s spit turned into frothy white foam as it spilled out of his mouth and onto his pillow. Shallow grunts escaped his throat as he seized for what seemed to be no end but was actually another three minutes. Then he started to come out of it, his body going stiff. “M-mama…” He gasped weakly, opening his eyes, looking at me. Then, slowly, he let out a deep breath. 

“Shhhhh, be free my child, be free,” Father Arnold said softly, giving Spencer a kiss on the forehead before closing his eyelids shut- irises a cold, glassy, and unforgiving shade of grey.

“Is- is he okay?” I asked, shakily, covering my mouth. 

“His soul rests in God’s hands, now.”

“Oh… Oh my god. Spencer, he- he’s not dead is he?” But deep down, I already knew the answer to that, but didn’t want it to be real. I didn’t want to face reality. My knees buckled and I fell to the floor, clutching his cold little hand in mine as I cried, “NO, SPENCER, WHY! NO, WHY MY BOY, WHY!”  My body went numb as it felt like my heart broke into a million pieces.

“I’m sorry,” The father offered his condolences, putting a hand on my shoulder.

Then a bang sounded from my living room as the front door was kicked in. “What the fuck is going on in here?” A cop demanded to know, drawing his gun on the priest and I.

Apparently my mother had called the police to do a welfare check on us after I hadn’t called for a few days, worried about how our last conversation ended. To add to that, some neighbors were concerned after they saw a figure run away from the back of the house. Apparently there had been increased reports of a peeping-tom on the prowl in the surrounding area so the cops barged in, thinking that he might’ve done something to us. Instead they found the aftermath of Spencer’s exorcism.

His cause of death was severe dehydration and stress that led to his seizure and subsequent heart attack. The media had swarmed the house within the hour after that news came out.

I feel so terrible. But what makes me feel even worse, is that after taking Father Arnold and I into custody, they turned my house upside down as they conducted a full investigation. Terrifyingly, the police found evidence of someone recently living in my crawlspace and walls. There were pillows and blankets, food wrappers, bottles full of pee, some of Spencer’s clothes and dirty magazines, even my underwear down there. I was informed that when something like that happens, it’s a thing called phrogging and is defined as the act of secretly living in another person's home without their knowledge or permission. So everything: My kitchen being rearranged, the lights, the missing food, my nightmares, The Shadow Man- it was all from the phrog, a real person, fucking with me, driving me insane for their own amusement. 

That broke the internet. If it wasn’t already bad before, now random strangers online were gobbling the facts of my life up like candy.

And the phrog, they turned out to be someone a lot closer to home than I thought. As the police raided my house, some little girl’s dad caught Gerald down the street peeping into her window. When confronted, he acted confused- like he didn’t know what he was doing or why he was there. Still, the man pressed charges and he was arrested. On a hunch, after testing the DNA that had been left down there, forensics confirmed that it had been Gerald in my house all along. He says he had no idea, but according to Maggie in the past couple of years- right around the time the ‘hauntings’ started- he had developed a habit of disappearing for several hours at a time. She’d even wake up in the middle of the night without him by her side. Now she knows what he was up to.

I feel absolutely disgusted and violated, but it all makes so much sense now.

So, here I am writing this memoir of mine from prison. Maybe someone can learn a thing or two from my story and not end up here like I did. 

Unfortunately, Gerald’s fate was not as fulfilling as one would hope. He didn't go to jail on account of his doctors thinking he was suffering from early stage dementia- which caused his out of character and creepy behavior. Instead of being behind bars, Gerald gets to live free as long as his daughter is his primary caretaker and keeps a strict track record of his whereabouts and the medication he takes.  

As for Father Arnold and I, we were both charged with felony assault on a child causing death. You know most people say they don’t look good in orange, but I beg to differ. Though, I don’t think I’ll be sticking around here too much longer. My lawyers think I have a good shot pleading temporary insanity as a result of Gerald’s actions. I know I felt even crazier then than I do now- living with all my guilt. And as for Father Arnold, well, I couldn’t say the same for him. But I don’t really care what happens to him, he killed my son. 

While waiting for my hearing, Robert came to visit me only once in prison. The guards led me out of my cell and sat me at one of those phone booths with the bullet proof glass and chunky outdated phones. He was already sitting down when I got there. I got a good look as I plopped into my seat. He was clearly in mourning. Unruly stubble grew on his chin, thick bags clung to his eyes. He didn’t seem to be sleeping much, if at all. His eyes were bloodshot, dull and lifeless- missing the spark that used to make them feel alive. His white dress shirt was wrinkly, untucked, and full of beer stains. He looked like a mess, the worst state I’d ever seen him in.

I picked up the phone, pressing the receiver up to my ear, waiting for him to say something. Robert waited for a moment, unable to look me in the eyes. When he finally picked up the phone, his breath hitched before he spoke. All he could ask, while sniffling and wiping away a stray tear, was, “Why?”

“I thought Spencer was possessed. Rob, I swear…” I sniffled, trying to keep myself from wailing. “I fully believed with my whole heart that he, that *my house,* was haunted.”
