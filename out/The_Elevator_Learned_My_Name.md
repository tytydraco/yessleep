I played the Elevator Game because I needed proof that something—anything—was wrong with the building.

I’d just moved into Crestview Towers, a concrete high-rise from the 70s that smelled like bleach and old carpet. The rent was cheap for a reason. Doors opened on empty floors that shouldn’t have existed. The elevator sometimes stopped between levels and sighed, like it was tired of holding us up. And once—only once—the display flickered and showed 13 even though the building had twelve floors.

I told myself it was wiring. Old tech. Settling foundations.

Then Mrs. Kline from 4B went missing.

The police said she wandered off. Her son said she never took the elevator because it “knew her name.” He laughed when he said it, but his eyes didn’t. A week later, a note appeared taped inside the elevator: DON’T LET HER IN.

The building manager tore it down. I took a photo.

That night, I googled “elevator knows your name.” That’s how I found the game.

For those who don’t know: the Elevator Game is a ritual. You enter an elevator alone, follow a precise sequence of floors, and if you do it right, the elevator takes you somewhere else. Another world. Another place. There are rules. Break them and you don’t come back the same.

Rule one: be alone.

Rule two: follow the order exactly.

Rule three: when the woman enters, do not speak to her.

Rule four: if she asks you where you’re going, do not answer.

Rule five: when the elevator rises without input, do not look at the floor indicator.

The forums were full of jokes. Fanfic. People claiming it worked, people calling them liars. But one comment stopped me cold:

If your building is missing a floor, it’s easier for the elevator to find the gap.

Crestview Towers was missing thirteen.

I waited until 2:13 a.m. because every story said late hours mattered. I left my phone recording in my pocket. If this was nothing, I’d have proof. If it wasn’t… well.

The elevator was already on my floor.

The doors slid open with a wet sound.

I stepped in and pressed 4.

The doors closed. The lights hummed. We descended one floor. The doors opened.

Empty hallway. Beige walls. Same as always.

Next, 2. Then 6. Then 2 again. Then 10.

My hands shook as I pressed the buttons. The elevator felt warmer now. Like breath trapped in metal.

At 10, the doors opened slowly.

A woman stood there.

She wore a long coat that didn’t move with the air. Her hair was dark and hung in clumps, like it had been slept in for weeks. Her face was… wrong. Not distorted. Just unfinished. Her eyes were there, but they didn’t focus. Like they were waiting for instructions.

She stepped inside without a sound.

The doors closed.

I stared straight ahead.

The elevator began to move on its own.

The indicator flickered. I kept my eyes on the seam where the doors met. The air thickened. My ears popped.

“Where are you going?” she asked.

Her voice came from behind me and inside my head at the same time.

I didn’t answer.

She moved closer. I could feel her cold through my jacket. I could smell something sweet and rotten, like flowers left in a hot car.

“Where are you going?” she asked again, and this time she used my name.

I bit my tongue until I tasted blood.

The elevator stopped.

The doors opened.

I knew, without looking, that this was not any floor in Crestview Towers.

The light outside was wrong—flat and gray, like a photo printed too many times. The hallway stretched impossibly far, doors lining both sides, each one slightly ajar. I could hear something breathing, a slow, wet rasp, echoing from everywhere and nowhere.

The woman stepped out.

She turned and smiled.

I know I shouldn’t have looked at her. I know that now.

Her mouth was full of teeth. Too many. Layered, like a shark’s, all of them human.

“Don’t get lost,” she said.

The doors slammed shut.

The elevator plunged.

I screamed. The indicator flashed symbols I didn’t recognize. My phone vibrated violently in my pocket. The walls bowed inward, then snapped back. The lights went out.

When they came back on, the elevator was still.

The doors opened to my floor.

Everything looked normal.

I stumbled out and ran to my apartment. I locked the door and slid down it, shaking. My phone had stopped recording at the moment she spoke my name. The audio was gone. The video corrupted.

I didn’t sleep.

The next morning, I checked the elevator.

There was a new button on the panel.

13.

The building manager said it had always been there.

I started noticing other things. Doors in the hallway were sometimes open when they shouldn’t be. I’d hear footsteps behind me, matching mine perfectly. Once, in the mirror, I saw her standing in my kitchen, just out of focus, like a reflection that hadn’t loaded yet.

Last night, someone knocked on my door at 2:13 a.m.

I didn’t answer.

They knocked again. Slow. Patient.

“Ben,” a voice called softly. “You forgot me.”

I’m writing this because the elevator just dinged outside my door.

I live on the seventh floor.

The display reads 13.

And the doors are opening…
