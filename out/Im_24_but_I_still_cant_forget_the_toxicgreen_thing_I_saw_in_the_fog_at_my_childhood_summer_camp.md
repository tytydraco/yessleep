I’m 24 now. A grown man who stopped believing in monsters under the bed a long time ago. But there’s one memory from my childhood that’s been stuck in my head like a splinter. My brain has spent years trying to convince me it was just a dream or an overactive imagination, but that feeling of primal terror was too real to be made up.

I was ten years old. My parents had sent me to a summer camp in the woods for about three or four weeks. It was a normal, unremarkable camp.

It was around the fourth day. We had a great day, running around, and after lights out, everyone scattered to their cabins. My bed was right next to the window. That night, I couldn't fall asleep for a long time: the mosquitoes were driving me crazy. I lay there in the stuffy room, swatting them one by one, and out of sheer boredom, I started lining up the dead bugs in a neat row on the windowsill. Only after finishing this weird little ritual did I finally drift off to sleep.

And then I woke up abruptly.

It wasn't your usual waking up, where you slowly open your eyes. I was jolted awake by an unbearable, suffocating sense of dread that sent ice-cold shivers down my entire body. There was dead silence in the room; all the other kids were fast asleep. I turned my head toward the window right next to me.

Outside, everything had changed. The camp courtyard was swallowed by an incredibly thick, dense fog—the kind you rarely see in real life. And inside that fog, something was moving.

At first, I couldn't believe my eyes. Something was running across the grounds. It looked like a hazy, semi-transparent silhouette of a human wrapped in a massive, billowing cloak. But the most terrifying part was the color. The creature glowed with an unnatural, toxic-green, piercing light that cut right through the thick veil of fog.

It moved completely wrong. It was as if gravity simply didn't exist for this thing. It wasn't running in the usual sense—it was more like it was swiftly floating above the ground. And then, with terrifying ease, it vaulted over the high camp fence like it was made of cardboard.

I stared at it for about ten seconds. I was in complete shock; my ten-year-old brain flat-out refused to process what my eyes were seeing. And then some sort of defense mechanism kicked in: I just "shut down." I instantly collapsed back onto my pillow and fell into a deep, heavy sleep.

I woke up the next morning with a heavy weight in my chest. There was a clear, oppressive feeling, as if I had peeked at something no human was ever supposed to see. I don’t remember if I told my friends or the counselors about it. Probably not. Who would believe a ten-year-old kid?

But that green, floating silhouette is still burned into my mind. And now, whenever I see thick fog outside my window, I always draw the curtains.
