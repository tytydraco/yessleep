Have you ever wondered what happens when your days last for 25 hours instead of 24? When you’re born with a broken internal clock? Not metaphorically – your brain actually doesn’t know how long a day is. You still need eight hours of sleep like anybody else… but you get them an hour later than yesterday. The problem is that your brain works on a longer schedule than the Earth’s rotation. One night you manage to fall asleep at 11 p.m. The next night, you lie awake staring at the ceiling until midnight. The next, until one a.m. Then two and three. You keep pushing forward, trying to live in a society that demands you show up to classes at eight in the morning regardless of your internal clock.



My name’s Kevin. I’m 16, and I’ve been blind since birth. And for the last few weeks, my life has been falling apart.



People usually assume being blind means seeing a pitch-black void, but the closest I can describe my reality is if the world was a picture printed on some damp paper and left to dry; everything is blurry, faded, and the edges bleed into each other. I couldn’t read a street sign if my life depended on it. To make out details (and write) I need to hold objects about two inches from my nose. Currently, I survive high school with a cane and thick glasses. It’s exhausting and it drains most of my energy. My free time is mostly retreating to my bedroom and listening to music or audiobooks, or hanging out with my cat Yoruichi. She sleeps on my bed every night – well, actually, it’s her bed now and she lets me sleep on it.



Living with limited vision *did* make my other senses better, mostly my hearing. I can tell who is walking down the hallway by the specific tone of their footsteps, or when someone is coming from the squeak of our front gate. And I also know my neighbours are selfish pricks who leave their two huge German Shepherds chained outside all day and all night. They bark at everything. Cars, other animals, insects, the wind, and their own smell. So, I have this constant barking soundtrack in my life, making an already awful sleeping situation even worse.



The sleep thing, yes. That’s the story I was telling.



Non-24-Hour Sleep-Wake Disorder. I know, the name sounds like something a kid makes up as an excuse for not finishing their homework. It happens mostly to visually impaired people because our eyes can’t process the sunlight and properly reset our circadian rhythms. I rely on melatonin gummies and terrible herbal teas, which can help for a while. But eventually, my cycle gets fucked up again. Working out during the day used to help to get me tired, enough so I would collapse on the bed, but it’s not something I can do every day.



So, this was my life so far. I lived a 25-hour day, meaning my sleep time shifted forward one hour every night. It sucked, but I could predict and somehow plan around it. Until a few weeks ago, when the shift started stretching out.



I would find myself turning in my bed for ten more minutes past my “planned” schedule. Then half an hour. Just last Monday, I hit the 45-minute mark. Right now, my brain demands a full 26-hour cycle before it finally decides to let me sleep. And I still need to be at school by 8. The math is brutal, as you can imagine. When you have to add two hours to your bedtime every single day, you end up living most of the week in complete opposition to the rest of the world. I tried taking afternoon naps after school, but that just ruins the nighttime. The exhaustion never leaves my face. My school life narrowed to managing not to fall asleep during classes and barely retaining anything the teachers say.



This brings me to last night, when my real story begins. The peak of my desync.



The night before, I had fallen asleep at one a.m. So my brain wasn’t scheduled to shut down until at least three a.m. I was on my back, staring at the dark ceiling, trapped in my miserable state of being too tired to get up but not enough to pass out.



Yoruichi curled up in her usual spot, right next to my chest. Her purring vibrated, travelling through my skin and bones like a tiny bit of comfort. It intensified every time I ran my fingers through her fur. And the night was loud as always. The fridge in our kitchen hummed with a low note through the floor. Outside my window, crickets sang their usual songs against the damp dirt of the garden. Somewhere in the distance, a car rumbled down the main road. And, of course, the neighbour’s dogs played their night concert, barking at their own shadows.



I was almost asleep. That state where you’re not gone yet but your thoughts don’t make sense. I was right on the edge of it. Until I became aware, very suddenly, that things had just changed. Like waking up without having fallen asleep. I don’t know how to explain it.



First, the dogs had stopped. I held still for a moment, because sometimes they did that for a minute, before starting again. This felt completely different. The noise had cut off mid-bark. I listened and there was just nothing to listen to. The fridge was no longer humming, but there had been no power cut. It was like it… ceased to exist.



The crickets’ songs were also gone. And I don’t mean just gone quiet. They were absent, like when you mute the audio on your phone. I couldn’t hear Yoruichi’s purring anymore.



For a second there, I thought I had fallen asleep without noticing, because that happens. But my eyes were open, and I could see the blurred shapes of my room in the dark, normal and still, like every night. Even the ambient rush of air moving through the vents disappeared and everything felt stagnant; it almost crushed my eardrums, heavier than the chaos of just a minute earlier. Like a sudden vacuum that should have made my ears ring but didn’t.



I blinked and rolled onto my side, careful not to disturb Yoruichi. I reached for the digital alarm clock on my nightstand. I pulled it right up to my face until the bright red lines stopped being blurred spots and settled into sharp lines. It should have read 3:00. Instead, the first digit was a 3, but the other ones were… broken?



They each looked like a capital backwards ‘E’, mashed together with a ‘7’. As I squinted at the display, the shape flickered and the last digit morphed into an upside-down ‘A’ with missing lines.



I set the clock down on the mattress and sat up. A chill ran down my spine, and I didn’t understand why. Okay, the clock was broken. Fine. So? It happens; clocks can break. Nothing wrong with that, right?



I fumbled around the blankets until my hands found my phone. Bringing the screen an inch from my nose, I checked the time on the lock screen and gasped. It showed the exact same nonsensical symbols. Two unreadable, glitched characters where the minutes should have been. My breathing picked up speed as I lowered the phone. My ears wanted to catch anything – a single sound, a passing car or the wind, the stupid neighbour’s dogs or the crickets, or my cat’s purring.



Nothing.



Where was she? Yoruichi. I reached back to the mattress where she had been sleeping, seeking her familiar warmth. I found her. My finger touched her side. But it was wrong; she wasn’t moving, her chest wasn’t rising or falling. I slid my hand over her back to shake her awake, but I stopped.



Her fur was soft and warm like always, but her body underneath was completely rigid. Not the stiffness of a dead body, no. It felt more like a… living statue. I wrapped my hands around her torso and tried to lift her. Nothing moved. She didn’t budge. I tried again, first gently and then harder, putting all my strength into trying to pick her up.



Nope. Yoruichi remained there, in her curled-up position, like she had been part of the bed herself. Fixed in space with a terrifying density, a completely immovable object. Bolted into the air above the mattress. My cat was glued to the fabric of the universe, but my phone lifted without resistance when I grabbed it off the bed.



I reached for my cane – it lives on its hook against the nightstand, always, the only rule about it. My fingers found it and I stood up. It moved; that was the first thing that reassured me. Then I grabbed the bottle of water from the nightstand and tilted it. The water moved inside it, normally. I looked at Yoruichi’s shape on the bed once more and didn’t touch her again.



I crept out of my bedroom. The dark grey blur of the hallway felt so strangely hostile in the silence. My parents’ room was just next to mine, so I dragged my free hand along the wall to keep myself oriented. When I reached their door, I knocked against the wood. The knock didn’t make the normal thud. Instead, what came out was a noise that stretched out, warping into a sickening vibration that distorted the pitch like a broken guitar string. Slow, as though it was… lagging. I pushed the door open.



“Mom? Dad?” I said.



My voice came out with the same distortion, and this time it made me feel sick. The vacuum that had taken over the house swallowed the noise the moment it left my mouth. I walked over to my mother’s side of the bed and touched her shoulder. Her joints were rigid. I shook her – or at least I tried to – but my fingers just slipped off her nightshirt. Like I wasn’t touching her real shape, but just a picture of her. So weird and surreal, like a dream. Was I actually dreaming or what?



“Mom?” I screamed this time.



No answer. The silence just devoured my voice. They were both immobile, just like Yoruichi.



I scrambled out, almost tripping as I grabbed my phone and literally punched 911 on the screen. I pressed it to my ear, but nothing happened. No one answered. I didn’t even get a dial tone, or an error message. The phone was just a piece of plastic and glass, the time still showed a three followed by unreadable, corrupted symbols.



I put it back in my pocket and stood in my parents’ dark room, trying to think – until I noticed it. Through the blurred curtains in my vision, something outside was moving. Not a lot. A slow variation in the glow of the streetlamp, as though the light was breathing. I stood there for a minute to be sure: it was real. I wasn’t imagining it.



I leaned my forehead against the glass and squinted into the yellow fog that was the streetlamp outside. A grey smudge stood at the edge of the light. I waited to see the usual trailing smear that tells me something is moving in front of me, but it never came. Instead, one moment the shape was near the sidewalk, and the next, without a proper transition, it moved a few feet to the left. Like it just teleported. A static image deleted and then immediately redrawn, skipping every frame in between.



My first thought was to open the window and yell for help. But something – call it a survival instinct – told me it would be a terrible idea. I listened to it and slowly backed away from the window. My legs were shaking with that horrible feeling of insects crawling up my skin, and I had stopped breathing.



Call me an idiot but… I wanted to know. I needed to know if I was dreaming, or if the whole world had broken. With no ambient noise of the fridge or even just the air movement to guide my ears, my spatial awareness was dead. I bumped my knee against the living room couch and stumbled until I found the handle of the front door. I pulled it open and stepped onto the porch.



Our front yard is small, with a cobblestone path, a few potted plants, bushes and two trees along the fence. The street beyond was lit up in the yellow fog from the streetlamp, which for me meant I could see shapes better than inside. My neighbour fence ran along the left side of the yard. I walked towards it.



Their German Shepherds were there; two large brown smudges against the green beyond the fence. They should have been barking at me, like they always did when I walked by. But they were caught mid-lunge. Even as simple blurs, when I leaned closer I could make out their jaws wide open, frozen in time, silent. I waved my cane an inch from their faces. No reaction.



Across the yard, a car sat in the middle of the street, its headlights blasting a wall of white into my ruined vision. I tapped my cane over the cobblestone path until I reached the car, and I pulled the driver’s door handle. The metal creaked and opened. The guy inside was gripping the steering wheel, his face turned towards the windshield. I waved my hand in front of his open eyes, then tried to push him. Just like Mom and Yoruichi, he was fixed to the seat.



I backed away and my elbow brushed against the tree and the bushes near the sidewalk. Their leaves didn’t bend or rustle. Instead, they scraped my skin like they were made of sharp iron. It hurt. I began to understand: all living things were frozen in time, immovable; the inanimate objects were the only things that could still move. But I was a living thing, wasn’t I? So, why could I move?



And then, about twenty yards down the road, the grey blur I had spotted earlier from the window shifted under the light. The same size and shape as a human being, and yet, something about it felt just wrong.



It was moving, but… without moving. It wasn’t walking. It stood completely still for a few seconds, then vanished and instantly reappeared a couple of feet closer. The best way I could describe it was like watching a videogame running on a single frame every few seconds on a terrible PC. Except it was real and standing just in front of me. Frame one: the shape was standing under the streetlight. Frame two: it teleported by the neighbour’s mailbox.



Frame three brought the sound. Whatever that thing was, it let out a noise that vibrated through my teeth and physically hurt. It sounded like your audio driver crashing before a blue screen of death – this harsh digital screech grinding into my ears, but dragging on and on without taking a break.



I took a step back out of panic. The tip of my cane smacked the fence. The impact rang out with the same distortion as when I knocked on my parents’ door; it stretched into this warped and alien frequency that echoed through the whole street. And there, that grey blur snapped its attention towards me.



The noise coming from it raised into a deafening, high-pitched scream. Its shape disappeared and materialized halfway across the street. I dropped my cane. Then, I ran.



Navigating was an impossible task; without even the sound of my shoes slamming on the asphalt, the mental map I’d spent 16 years of my life building in my head, evaporated. I just aimed for the lit up patch on the other side of the road, as my lungs burned and my legs hurt from the vision of a living glitch chasing me.



I turned to glance over my shoulder. That thing was teleporting constantly, getting closer and closer, skipping the frames across the asphalt while the rest of the blurry world was paused.



Something touched my ankle. It whipped against my skin, feeling like freezing wet metal. An electrical shock followed, crawling up my leg and violently contracting my muscles. I lost my balance and slammed into the asphalt, rolling over and gasping, unable to move my leg anymore. The fall tore the skin off my knees and elbows. My phone flew out of my pocket and slid to a stop inches from my nose. The screen glared, and the time still read 3 followed by those symbols.



I turned on my back to see the thing looming over me. Since it was now standing right in front of my face, my eyes could finally make out some details. That was not a person – not even a beast, or a monster. It was a… mess of black cables and exposed wires, sparking with electricity, forming a shape vaguely resembling a human torso and limbs.



Where the head should have been, a dozen thinner wires writhed around like tiny snakes, each ending in a glowing sphere. And all of those spheres twitched, focusing on my face, examining me like many eyes. Whatever this thing was, it wasn’t attacking me. I felt it, deep inside me. It was studying me with horrifying curiosity, as if I were the glitch in the system.



A violent blast of air hit me so hard I almost choked. The world exploded into noise. A car horn first blasted in my ears and then tires shrieked against the asphalt just a few feet from me. Two headlights blinded me right when the neighbour’s dogs resumed their aggressive barking. The crickets’ songs also returned, together with the hum of the streetlamps.



I threw my arms over my face and scrambled backwards, unable to hold a scream. The sound didn’t distort this time. That wire-creature was gone. In front of me, a man stepped out of the car and yelled.



“What the hell are you doing in the middle of the road? Are you insane?”



I lay there on the asphalt, hyperventilating and staring at his blurry shape, moving like a normal human being does. My trembling hand picked up my phone. On the screen, it read 3:01.



The man helped me to my feet and walked me to my front door. My parents woke up to the commotion and rushed out in their sleep clothes. I didn’t need to be able to see their faces to know they were terrified.



I sat on the couch, shaking with my knees bleeding and a bruised shoulder. I told them I must have been sleepwalking. Never in my life had I sleepwalked, but they both hugged me and seemed to believe it. Because it made sense. A safe and rational explanation to justify a blind kid wandering into traffic at night.



That was last night. Now, I’m sitting on my bed. My door is locked, all my windows are shut. The dogs are barking outside and Yoruichi is purring on my bed. I’m staring at the digital clock on my nightstand, holding my breath and trembling as I press my back against the headboard.



I don’t know if that thing was real or not, but I pray that whatever it was, it doesn’t know which house is mine. Because just now, the numbers on the clock turned into unreadable symbols, and the world just went silent.
