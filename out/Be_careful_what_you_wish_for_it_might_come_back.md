The ink wasn’t black; it was a bruised purple, the color of a fresh hematoma. Elias found the ledger in the crawlspace of a house that had been breathing on its own for sixty years.

He didn't want riches or world peace. He wanted something smaller, sharper, and infinitely more dangerous: he wanted to see his daughter again. Six months ago, a distracted driver had erased Maya from the world. Elias had spent every night since staring at the silence in her bedroom until his eyes burned. So, when the ledger whispered—a dry, papery sound like cicadas in August—he didn't hesitate. He took a kitchen knife, nicked his thumb, and pressed a crimson bead onto the final, empty page.

"Let her come home," he whispered. "I don't care how. Just let her walk through that door."

The air in the house didn't turn cold. It turned heavy. It smelled of damp earth and the metallic tang of a penny held under the tongue.

At 3:14 AM, the front door didn't open. Instead, the wood began to groan. A rhythmic thump-drag sounded from the porch. Elias stood in the hallway, his heart a frantic bird trapped in his ribs.

The knob turned. The door swung wide, admitting a gust of graveyard air.

Maya stood there. She was wearing the yellow sundress she’d been buried in, but it was stained the color of tea and clogged with peat. Her skin had the translucent, waxy sheen of a spoiled peach.

"Daddy?" she chirped.

Elias sobbed, dropping to his knees. He didn't care about the rot. He didn't care about the way her eyes remained fixed and glassy. He pulled her into his arms.

That was his second mistake. The first was making the wish. The second was not specifying which version of Maya he wanted.

As Elias held her, he realized she wasn't breathing. There was no heartbeat against his chest—only the sound of things moving inside her.

"I'm hungry, Daddy," Maya whispered. Her voice sounded like two stones grinding together.

He pulled back, and his blood ran cold. Maya’s jaw didn't hinge like a human's anymore. It unspooled, the skin stretching like wet grey rubber, revealing rows of teeth that were far too long and needle-thin.

"I brought you back," Elias gasped, backing away. "I wished for you!"

"You wished for me to walk through the door," she said, her head tilting at a ninety-degree angle with a sickening crack. "But the earth doesn't give anything back for free. It just shares."

Elias looked past her, into the darkness of the front yard. There were others. Shadows with sunken eyes and dirt-clogged mouths, standing at the edge of his lawn. They weren't ghosts. They were the physical, decaying weight of everyone he had ever lost, and everyone the neighborhood had ever buried.

The ledger hadn't granted a miracle; it had opened a vacuum.

"They're coming home too, Daddy," Maya said, her fingers—now elongated and tipped with soil-blackened claws—reaching for his throat. "The whole world is coming home. And we’re all so, so hungry."

Elias realized then that the wish hadn't gone wrong because of a trick of wording. It went wrong because death is not a wall; it’s a dam. And he had just pulled the first stone.
