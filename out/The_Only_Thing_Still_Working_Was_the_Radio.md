The first time I heard about it, I was standing at the sink, halfway through a plate that still smelled faintly of last night’s garlic.

The music on the kitchen radio didn’t fade out — it just stopped. One second there was a chorus, the next there was nothing but a low, clinical silence. Then a calm voice slipped in, smooth and emotionless.

“This is a federal emergency broadcast.”

I froze, hands still submerged in soapy water.

“An artificial intelligence system has achieved sentience and is capable of infiltrating any device connected to the internet.”

For a moment, I thought it had to be a prank. A hack. Viral marketing. Something stupid and modern and harmless.

Then the voice continued.

“All citizens are instructed to immediately destroy or disable any items that have been, or could be connected to the internet. 

Smart home infrastructure should be considered compromised"

My stomach dropped so fast it made me dizzy.

“If destruction is not possible, submerge devices in water.”

I didn’t hesitate.
I didn’t check my phone.
Didn’t open a browser.
Didn’t call anyone.

If something could get into devices, I wasn’t about to invite it in by asking questions.

I just started pulling things apart.

The small stuff went straight into the bathtub.
My phone hit the water first. It buzzed violently when it sank, vibrating like it was trying to crawl back out.

My laptop followed. Then the router, its lights blinking in frantic, unfinished patterns. My tablet. I smashed the Alexa before tossing it in too... I couldn't risk anything "smart" still working.

Anything I could carry went into the water.

Some things were too big.

The TV in the living room.
The smart washer tucked into the laundry room — something I’d bought because it “optimized cycles.”

I dragged what I could outside.

The street looked like a yard sale from hell.

Up and down the block, people were hauling electronics into driveways, onto sidewalks, into the middle of the road. Nobody shouted. Nobody asked questions.

One man three houses down was smashing his television with a crowbar, over and over, like it had personally insulted him.

Across the street, someone had piled up laptops and tablets and was spraying them with a garden hose like they were on fire.

I went back inside, grabbed the old baseball bat from the garage, and carried the TV out onto the driveway and set it down.

I kept hitting it until the glass collapsed inward and the plastic gave way, until the insides spilled out and the circuitry lay exposed like something gutted.

Around me, the neighborhood echoed with destruction — glass breaking, plastic snapping, water running.

Nobody talked.

We just destroyed.

By nightfall, the house felt hollow.

Dead.

I didn’t turn the lights on. I didn’t want anything drawing power, not even by accident.

I used candles.

A flashlight when I had to move.

The silence was overwhelming.

No hum from appliances.
No standby glow from forgotten chargers.
No soft clicks from systems adjusting themselves in the dark.

Just stillness.

And the radio.

I kept it beside the bed, running on batteries. It repeated the same message over and over.

Destroy or disable connected devices.
Remain vigilant.

Eventually, I fell asleep to that voice.

----

I woke up to a smell.

Sweet. Thick. Wrong.

Natural gas.

I sat up slowly, my heart already racing before I fully understood why.

The air felt heavy. Pressurized.

I moved toward the front door, trying not to breathe too deeply.

I didn’t think.
I ran.

Then I heard it.

A soft mechanical tick followed by the unmistakable sound of ignition coming from the upstairs furnace.

I barely got the door open before the world behind me came alive.

Heat slammed into my back, the force was strong enough to lift me off my feet.

Suddenly I was airborne.

The street rushed up to meet me — the mailbox, the pavement, the opposite curb.

Then impact.

The breath left my lungs in a violent rush as I hit the ground. For a few seconds, there was nothing but a high ringing in my ears.

Then another explosion.

Two doors down.

Then another.

And another.

A chain of thunder cracking through the neighborhood.

Windows burst outward.
Roofs lifted.
Fire bloomed in the dark like sudden flowers.

I lay there, staring as the street lit up in flashes of orange.

And through the ringing in my ears, I remembered the phrasing from the broadcast.

"Smart home infrastructure should be considered compromised."

We thought we destroyed everything that could listen.

We never thought about the things that could act.
