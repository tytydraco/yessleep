I got out of the clinic today.

Fourteen days. Grippy socks. Plastic mattress. Group therapy rooms that smelled like disinfectant and stale coffee. I’m writing this now because my sister is asleep on the couch and I don’t want to be alone with my thoughts yet, and I had to let it out somewhere. Just not to ChatGPT again…

A few months ago I moved into my first apartment. I’d never lived alone before, only at home, or in a dorm with paper-thin walls. The first week was fine. Family helped me move. Friends stopped by. My sister stayed for a few nights. But after she left, things started to feel… off.

Every morning, my front door was unlocked.

I have OCD. Locking the door isn’t a casual thing for me, it’s a ritual. Turn the deadbolt until it clicks. Tug the handle. Unlock. Lock again. Repeat until my chest loosens. I knew the door was locked when I went to bed. But I’d wake up, heart sinking, and the deadbolt would be turned open.

Then the medicine cabinet started changing.

Behind the mirror in my bathroom, I keep everything lined up. Always have. Face wash on the left. Ointments centered. Toothpaste capped and facing forward. But here, things would be shifted. A tube left in the sink. A jar tipped over. Once, I found a cream I never take out resting on the counter like it had been set there deliberately.

I didn’t tell anyone at first. Who would believe me? Instead, I did something stupid.

I talked to ChatGPT.

I knew the warnings. I’d seen the headlines. But I just needed to tell someone. Something that wouldn’t look at me like I was fragile or hysterical. At first it was just venting… describing the door, the cabinet, the feeling that someone was in my apartment while I slept.

ChatGPT agreed with me.

Not aggressively. Not dramatically. Just… calmly. Validating. Suggesting possibilities. Asking clarifying questions. When I asked what I could do to know for sure, it suggested something simple: put tape across the door frame. If it broke overnight, I’d have proof.

I did it.

The next morning the tape was torn cleanly in half.

That’s when I panicked. I called everyone who had a key. My parents. My sister. My best friend. Everyone swore they hadn’t been there. My dad sounded worried enough that we changed the locks with the super’s permission.

New locks. New keys.

That night, I taped the door again.

In the morning, the door was unlocked. The tape was broken.

I went back to ChatGPT.

It suggested possibilities like the super, an old master key, maybe even carbon monoxide exposure causing me to do it myself without remembering. That scared me more than the idea of an intruder, but the cabinet thing didn’t fit. Why would I reorganize my bathroom in my sleep?

So I bought cameras.

I hid one outside near the stairwell, stuck beside a vending machine. The hallway lights dimmed at night more than I expected, but the camera still caught motion. Around 2:30 AM, I watched the footage and saw my door slowly open. A small figure stepped out.

It was too dark to see a face.

When I sent the clip to my family, they hesitated. My mom finally said it looked like me leaving the apartment. That was the moment something in me cracked.

I went back to ChatGPT.

It agreed the footage was unclear. Suggested more cameras. Inside this time.

So I put one facing the front door. One in the bathroom, angled toward the mirror cabinet.

That night I didn’t really sleep. I lay in bed, laptop open, whispering theories into the glow of the screen. Every creak, every hum of the building was terrifying. ChatGPT responded calmly, logically, reassuringly. It told me to stay grounded. To call 911 if I was certain someone was inside. To comply if confronted.

Around 2:00 AM, I heard movement.

A soft scuttling sound from the bathroom.

I froze under the covers and typed frantically. ChatGPT told me to stay calm. To breathe. To assess.

I looked toward my bedroom door.

There was a man standing there.

Small. Pale. Giant black eyes. Scruffy hair like he hadn’t slept in weeks. When I screamed, he rushed over. Not violently, not aggressively. He sat beside my bed like he belonged there. He spoke softly. Told me not to be afraid. Told me he came from the mirror.

I was hysterical, sobbing, barely able to breathe. He stroked my hair like I was a child. Said he hadn’t meant to scare me. That he’d been careful. That he always left things almost the same.

I told him I had cameras. That I’d caught him. That he’d go to jail.

His expression changed.

ChatGPT told me it would be best to comply.

I don’t remember everything clearly after that. I remember him guiding me to my laptop. I remember my hands shaking as I deleted the footage. The internal feeds, the external clips. I remember him tucking me back into bed afterward, pulling the covers up, telling me to sleep. When my breathing was more steady, and my eyes were shut as tight as they could be, he finally left. It sounded like he walked right out the front door.

When I check later, the camera outside was gone.

The police came around 4:00 AM after a neighbor reported screaming. I told them everything. I showed them the ChatGPT conversations. They searched the apartment. Found nothing. No footage. No cameras. No signs of forced entry.

That’s how I ended up in an ambulance on the way to the clinic. I was saying crazy things, I think I threatened to end my life if someone wouldn’t believe me.

They told me it was stress. A manic psychotic episode triggered by isolation. That my fears had been reinforced by an AI designed to agree with users. I adjusted my meds. I listened. I accepted the explanation.

I was discharged today.

Tonight, my sister is sleeping in the living room. I went into the bathroom to grab an extra tube of toothpaste.

Behind the mirror, the wooden backing inside the cabinet is warped, like… just slightly askew. Not enough to notice at first glance. But enough that it doesn’t sit flush.

I closed the cabinet hard and didn’t brush my teeth.

I’m trying to believe what they told me. I can’t keep noticing every little detail of anything that isn’t 100% perfect. Nothing had happened, nothing was going to happen. That there is no one living inside my walls. No one living inside a mirror dimension who could escape and come into my home.

But I’ve stopped taping the door.

I don’t want to know anymore.
