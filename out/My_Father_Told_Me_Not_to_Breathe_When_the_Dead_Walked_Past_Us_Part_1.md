I was nine the first time I knew I could see those things.

To be exact, my father found out before I did.

We were coming back from my mother's hometown in my uncle's old van. It was green, with a sliding door that never shut right. Even after my uncle slammed it twice, wind still came in around the frame. I sat in the front passenger seat with two bags of vegetables and a box of oranges under my feet. The whole van smelled of orange peel, gasoline, and wet clothes.

It had rained the whole way.

The mountain road was narrow. The headlights only showed a pale strip of concrete, and after that there was bamboo on both sides. Rain slid down the windshield. The wipers scraped once, and the road flashed white. They scraped again, and it vanished. I kept nodding off and bumping my head against the window.

My father was in the back seat, still cursing my uncle for taking the shortcut in weather this bad. Halfway through one sentence, the van shuddered hard. The dash went dark. The engine coughed twice and stopped.

My uncle got out, looked under the hood, and said water had probably gotten in somewhere. He and my father messed with it in the rain for a long time. The hood stood open. Their flashlight moved around in the steam. I curled up in my seat and listened to one of them swearing at the engine and the other digging through the tool box in the back.

After a while, my uncle said he would walk down to the village entrance and find someone.

He pulled his raincoat over his head and went down the road. In a few minutes, the bamboo took him.

Then it was just me and my father.

He stood near the front of the van, half under the hood. He held the flashlight in his mouth, and the beam shook whenever he moved. I was leaning by the window, about to ask how much longer it would take, when he stopped.

People do not stop that way.

For one second, the whole road stopped with him.

There had been insects a moment before. Water somewhere down the slope. Rain striking bamboo leaves. Then the insects cut off first. The water below went low. All I could hear was rain hitting the roof of the van, packed so close together it made my teeth feel tight.

My father slowly pulled his hand out of the engine.

His back was to me. His neck had gone stiff.

I said, "Dad?"

His shoulder jerked.

Then he turned and looked at me.

I still remember his eyes. I had seen him angry. I had seen him tired. I had seen him drunk at New Year and loud enough to scare my cousins. This was different. His eyes were hard with hurry. I had already brushed against something before I even knew the road had changed.

He took the flashlight out of his mouth.

"Don't talk."

I did not understand.

I thought he was annoyed, so I asked, quieter, "What happened?"

The next second he came around the front of the van and shoved his hand through the half-open window. He covered my mouth.

My nose too.

His hand was slick with motor oil. Rain ran from his sleeve down the side of my neck. I tried to pull back and hit the headrest. He leaned half his body through the window and pinned my shoulder with his other hand.

He put his mouth close to my ear.

"Hold it."

I tried to pry his fingers away.

He said it again.

"Don't breathe out."

That time I heard the shake in his voice.

My father was not a man who scared easily. He would argue with men twice his size after drinking. He would fix a broken van in rain. If dogs barked outside at midnight, he would go out with a cigarette in his mouth and check. But that night his hand kept trembling, and my lips went numb against his palm.

I held my breath until my chest started to hurt.

Then something sounded from the bend behind us.

The first sound was a stick touching the road.

One tap.

A pause.

Another tap.

It was a light sound, but the mountain had gone too quiet. Light sounds turned clear. The stick touched wet concrete with a dull knock. After it came several pairs of shoes stepping into mud.

My father's hand tightened.

I looked out through the gap between his fingers.

From around the bend, a bit of white appeared first.

It swung once before I saw it was paper.

An old man came first.

He wore black clothes and was so thin his back bent forward. Rain plastered his hair to his scalp. His face was all grooves filled with water. In his hand was a bamboo stick, with pale strips of paper tied near the end. Wind blew the strips against the stick, then let them peel away wetly.

Every few steps, the old man touched the stick to the road.

The others followed him.

At first I thought it was a funeral party. We had passed one in the market that afternoon, a line of people carrying white cloth. Adults had lowered their voices. That was the only thing my nine-year-old mind had room for.

But as those people came closer, the skin on my head started to tighten.

Their clothes were too alike. Dark long jackets. Loose trousers. Cloth shoes with mud up the sides. Their heads hung low. Their hands hung straight. Their arms barely moved. Each step came late, foot first, body after.

Yellow paper was fixed to them.

Some sheets lay on the chest. Some on the shoulder. Some hung over the face. Rain had soaked the paper until the corners curled. Under one edge I saw gray-white skin. One hand passed close to the window, swollen at the back, with black mud packed under the nails.

My father's palm pressed harder over my mouth. His skin was wet with sweat now.

When the old man passed the front of the van, he did not lift his eyes. He walked right by my father, close enough for water from the bamboo stick to flick onto the open hood.

The hood was still up.

The van was sitting crooked in the middle of the road.

Any normal person passing that close would have cursed, asked if the engine was dead, told us to move. The old man looked only at his own shoes, tapping the stick forward bit by bit.

The people behind him moved past my window.

One tap.

A pause.

Another tap.

I began to count.

One. Two. Three. Four.

The fifth had a torn place in the paper over its face. Through the tear I saw one closed eye, lashes wet and stuck together.

Six.

The sixth had lost one shoe. Its bare foot stepped into muddy water, but the toes did not bend.

Seven.

The paper on the seventh lifted at the corner and showed a piece of chin. The chin hung loose, wrong under the face.

A sound slipped out of my throat.

Very small.

Maybe it was not even a sound. I had held my breath too long. Air pushed up by itself, reached my throat, and scraped there.

My father crushed my face in his hand.

It hurt.

Tears came into my eyes.

After the seventh passed, I thought it was over.

My father paused too.

Only for that one breath. I saw his eyes move.

He looked toward the tail of the line.

Then his whole body locked.

There was one more.

It came out from behind the bend a few steps late. The first seven followed the old man's stick. This one lagged behind them. It wore dark clothes too. It had yellow paper on the chest too. But the paper was soaked through and stuck flat to the cloth, leaving the face open.

It was a young face.

Seventeen, maybe eighteen. Maybe younger. I could not tell then. The cheeks still had flesh on them. The lips were pale. Wet hair lay in strips on the forehead.

Its eyes were open.

At first, it looked at the road.

I looked at it.

I knew I should not, but children do not always look away when they are afraid. Sometimes fear holds the eyes in place. It was short of something the others had. The first seven moved behind the stick. This one stopped. It checked the road. Its foot went into the mud, but mud barely splashed.

I started to lift my hand.

I wanted to pull at my father. I wanted him to see it too.

My hand had only come up a little when my father caught my wrist and slammed it down against the seat. His nails dug into the web below my thumb. Pain flashed white at the edge of my vision.

Right then, the young one's head turned.

Slowly.

The old man was still tapping. The first seven were still moving. Rain was still falling. Only that head turned at a different speed, bit by bit, toward the van window.

It saw me.

I did not understand that sentence then.

Seeing a dirty thing is one matter. Having it see you is another.

When its eyes landed on my face, the air in my chest went wild. My father had my mouth covered, but I still wanted to speak. I do not know what I was trying to say. Maybe I wanted to ask who it was. Maybe I wanted to call my father. Maybe I wanted to cry.

Its lips moved once.

No sound came out.

My tongue moved with it.

Just the root of it. Enough to be almost an answer.

My father felt it first. He shoved my head back and covered my mouth and nose again, leaving no gap this time. My skull hit the seat back. For a second the world went black at the edges.

The young one smiled.

Only the left corner of its mouth lifted.

It was a polite expression.

The kind a person might wear at someone else's door after hearing movement inside.

My father cursed under his breath. I could not make out the words. His mouth was against my hair, and the rain took the rest.

The old man at the front stopped.

The bamboo stick struck the road three times.

Tap. Tap. Tap.

The young one's head snapped forward.

It took a step.

The step was ugly. One shoulder jerked. Its right foot dragged a black line through the mud before it caught the rhythm again.

The line passed the van.

My father kept holding me.

My head was swelling from the held breath. My ears were full of my own pulse. I scraped at the back of his hand, but he did not loosen. He held me until the bamboo stick went around the next bend. Until the wet shoes in mud faded too.

After another while, the insects came back.

One first.

Then another.

The water down the slope returned.

Only then did my father let go.

I dragged in air. Before I finished, he slapped me.

Not hard.

Enough to cut off the cry in my throat.

He crouched outside the window with both hands on the door. His face was white. Rain dripped from his chin. He looked at me for a long time before he spoke.

"If you see that again, keep your mouth shut. Keep your hands down."

I was still trying to breathe.

"What was that?"

He watched the bend.

"Don't ask."

"The last one looked at me."

His head snapped back.

This time he did not scold me.

He put two fingers on my lips. Gently.

"Don't say that outside."

Later my uncle came back in a blue truck. He started laughing before he even got out, asking how we had both gotten so soaked. Then he saw my father's face and closed his mouth.

They talked in the rain.

I sat in the van and saw my uncle glance once toward the bend, then pull his eyes away. He did not ask why the engine was still open. He did not ask why I had been crying.

We left the van on the mountain road and rode down in the truck.

My father made me sit between them. One of his hands stayed on the back of my neck. Every time the truck bounced, that hand tightened. More than once I almost fell asleep, and pain at the back of my neck woke me again.

At the first row of houses below the mountain, my uncle stopped near a small shop to buy cigarettes.

A red sign glowed in the rain. Plastic raincoats hanging at the door snapped in the wind.

My father waited until the truck door closed.

Then he asked me, "How many did you count?"

"Eight."

He closed his eyes.

Inside the truck it was dark. The red light from the shop slid across his face, turning it red, then black.

"There should have been seven."

I got anxious. "There was one behind them. Young. With his face showing. He smiled."

My father said nothing.

I asked, "Was it with the line too?"

He took his cigarette pack out of his pocket. His fingers shook so badly he could not tear the plastic.

At last he said, "Some things can't walk their own road, so they borrow someone else's."

I did not understand it then.

What I remember is that after that night, my father started giving me strange instructions.

If you are walking at night and people stand too neatly in a line, do not stare.

If someone calls from an empty place, do not turn around.

If someone you know is standing somewhere they should not be, look at the feet first.

And the most important one.

Don't be quick with your mouth.

Don't be quick with your hands.

Don't breathe toward their side.

When he said those things, he always sounded irritated. He thought I was too slow. But every time he reached the last one, he put his hand on the back of my neck and squeezed once, checking that I had listened.

I did listen.

For years after that, at crossroads, in stairwells, behind old opera stages, in hospital corridors, I saw people who should not have been there. My eyes moved away before I could stare. I pretended not to hear. I kept my hands in my pockets before they could point.

I thought that was enough.

Then, years later, on another rainy day, something happened by the river.

That was the incident that broke the quiet I had kept for so long.
