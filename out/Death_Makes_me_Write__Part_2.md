For four straight years, as I transitioned from my college to contributing to the economy, those trysts with the divine abomination became my secret rhythm. I didn’t see any issue with meeting an imaginary being inside the realm of my imaginations.

His visits kept shortening like breaths after a sprint. No longer useless meetings, just quick, sharp summons.

‘How do you imagine yourself [dying](https://www.reddit.com/r/nosleep/comments/1q0g48d/death_makes_me_write)?'

He would rasp, swirl tongue, clack his teeth. I’d spin a tale- gory, funny, cosmic, remorseful. He would just nod or smile or just make a face that cannot be described in words. All his acts felt painful. Pain, I needed to go through to reach serenity that kept me working member of this esteemed society. Then he would disperse. 10 minutes, tops. Enough to stitch me back, leave me still with serenity.

I kept thinking about the real death I witnessed years back, the suicide on train tracks, and how it changed the course of my life. I kept thinking about my imagination of Death as being and how his body had the elements I never witnessed or imagined in my life.

  
The thoughts of my death became just another narrative exercise. I was living for that serenity drug and the high would last for months. I would often have more than I needed. So, I grew stronger. Healthier. Calmer. The black stains on my soul seemed to always exist and only now I was recognizing them this closely. I didn’t feel broken because of my trauma, rather I felt glued together with the universe now. Entirety of vocabulary and language would fail to describe the sense of relief I felt after imagining that God. So much so that I fell in love with the world. Because I was the world. I felt like the world. That is the closest thing I can say to describe that feeling. 

It was overwhelming initially, but that stillness entered violently and filled quietly, steadily, entirely.    


My parliament of voices were silenced, obsolete. Therapy? Forgotten. I got my break in a small book publishing firm, as a horror novel editor. I was happy being alive. Ends justified the means. My version of Death pruned me and finally made space for my family. Apparently, family had space for me too. Home stopped clawing my old wounds. My mom’s self-victimizing episodes turned to episodes of having conversation on mundanity around both our lives. And my dad, angry-flirtatious-smart-adulterous dad, cracked open. He didn’t let go of his smart jibes and flirty innuendos, which was a nice retention. 

Nothing ever becomes normal, but I finally was willing to accept the dysfunctionality of my family, just like how I accepted the black stains of my soul.  We all had flaws and we all are chasing serenity. My mother found it in mundanity of life, my dad in his movie collection and I, in narrating deaths.

One night, over scrambled eggs I cooked (spinach, cheese, a pinch of chaos), he apologized. Not flowery; just gruff words about regrets, the abnormality we'd baked together. I forgave him. Not for him, for the threads Death wove in me. Reconciliation bloomed. We talked. Laughed at old horror movies, his favorites with the grand deaths. I shared sanitized versions of my stories. He chuckled at the gory ones. For the first time, home felt like a harbor, not a hurricane.

He confided in me his desire to visit the location of one of his favorite movies with him. That was the first time I realized the significance movies carried in my dad’s life. I always knew, I was just fifteen years late to this realization.   
  
Die Another Day. Iceland.   
  
He wanted to see Iceland with him. He talked in lengths about how the Ice Palace scene was the first time he saw ice in that kind of glory on screen. He was always a mountain person, now I knew the reason. That day over the wholesome meal, I fell in love with my dad again and we made our plans to visit Iceland next winters.  
  
When we all went to sleep, that was the first time I questioned my methods. I saw the glimpse of familial innocence and became skeptic about the narrative exercise I was implementing. I kept pondering whether I could sustain my life without this. If I can sustain my life without the Death. I tried to sleep on them only to find my mind questioning about my dependence on my next visit of my imaginative Death. Those questions kept bothering me, even though never affected my emotional state on functional level. One fine day, I decided that at some point, I would have to get rid of this method, till then I can reap the benefits of this.

Then the winters came, family trip to Iceland as we both promised. Soon after reaching the destination airport, we piled into the SUV. My mom chattering about recipes, dad cracking dad-jokes, me driving with a grin. The air thinned as we climbed; pines whispered like my old voices. Lodges with roaring fires, hikes through needle-sharp forests. Dad thrived, hiking boots laced, inhaling the cold like youth. We all were excited for this once in a decade family trip. 

  
It happened on day four. We trekked a glassy ridge, icicles dangling from overhangs like frozen daggers. 

‘Thousand needles.’

I'd once imagined for myself. A plunge into a sea of them, body pierced, soul shredded. 

Dad lagged, admiring the view. He just stopped, hearing a cracking sound. Avalanche tease, not full. Enough to calf the overhang. My motor neurons stopped responding catching the glimpse of the white rain. Icicles showered. Thousands. Not blunt, needle-fine, razor-edged from the freeze-thaw. They impaled my dad mid-step.  
  
Face first, eyes popped, cheeks shredded to ribbons. Throat pierced a hundred times, gurgling red froth. Chest a pincushion, ribs splayed like broken comics. He twitched, conscious, shivering in the exquisite agony I'd scripted for myself. Snow turned crimson slush around his mash of meat and bone. My mom exhaled her loudest scream, only to tease the avalanche again. Soon the crimson was covered in holy white again.  
  
I froze, seeing Death again. White. Spiky. Approving my father's death with his tongue swirls.  
  
This one's mine, he seemed to say. 

Dad's eyes locked on mine, not pleading- accusing? grateful? serene? Soon, gone. Face flattened under the final laughter of that ungodly white rain. The white Death laughed too. Not in my head. Out loud, warping the air. The family vacation ended in his grandest engineering yet. Architected by me.  
  
And I? Serenely shattered, craving the next summons with Him.
