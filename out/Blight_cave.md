My name is Issac Stiltner, and I used to be a cave explorer. Keywords being “used to”, as I quit because of the events that unfold in this story. You may not believe it, but please don’t downplay my misfortune. I’ll tell it to you as if it were you, to give you a clearer story as to what had occurred that day.

The jungle in western Honduras had been restless after recent rains. Landslides had ripped through the limestone slopes, leaving boulders and debris scattered along the narrow trail. I pushed through the underbrush, boots sticking to damp soil, leaves brushing against my legs.

A sudden movement caught my eye. Something tiny, brown, quick, darted across a stone at the mouth of the cave. My hand shot down instinctively and squished it against the rock. The wet crunch made my stomach turn. My skin crawled. I felt a shiver race down my back, an unnamable dread curling in my gut, and yet I knew I had to move forward.

The landslide had opened a cave that wasn’t on any map, a narrow crack partially obscured by debris. A cold gust of air seeped out, carrying a damp, earthy scent. My chest tightened, muscles coiling with tension I didn’t entirely understand. I dropped my pack, checking my headlamp and flashlight.

I crouched at the entrance, telling myself I would only go in a little way. Just a few minutes. The limestone walls were slick with moisture, roots crawling across the floor like veins. I switched on my headlamp and began crawling forward, elbows scraping against the rough stone. Darkness swallowed everything beyond the beam.

Then as I moved through the darkness I saw a light- an irregular light. I crawled closer as to get a closer look at it, until I realized it was a reflection of my headlight.

I froze for a fraction of a second, something was catching my headlamp beam ahead. A shimmer, too precise, too patterned to be random. My chest tightened.

Through the dim light, I saw a strange texture—hundreds of tiny points of reflected light, like a thousand miniature stars flickering back at me. They moved subtly as I shifted, forming a mosaic that almost seemed… alive.

I blinked. Leaning closer on trembling elbows, I realized the points of light were arranged in a geometric, honeycomb pattern. Fly-eye lenses. Hundreds of tiny facets, each reflecting the beam of my headlamp in sharp pinpricks.

And then I noticed something more. In those tiny reflections, something else shimmered—shapes, lines… a face. My face. Or at least, my head, distorted, fragmented across the thousands of tiny lenses. Every blink multiplied the image, fractured it, like staring into a thousand mirrors at once.

Instinct took over. I spun and began crawling backward, this time on hands and knees, palms scraping raw against the stone. The creature didn’t advance immediately, but the sound of legs clicking echoed softly, unnervingly, like dry gravel shifting. My arms burned. My knees scraped raw. Every breath came shallow and fast.

And then the floor changed. My headlamp revealed movement everywhere. Hundreds, maybe thousands of smaller cockroaches—on the floor, on the walls, crawling along the ceiling. They were everywhere. Black, brown, shiny. They flowed like a living carpet over every surface, weaving between rocks, disappearing and reappearing as they moved.

One slid across my arm. Another across my chest. Tiny legs tickled and pinched, scraping the hairs on my skin. Their antennae twitched, brushing against my neck. My stomach churned. My hands twitched, but I couldn’t stop crawling.

I could feel the faint, uneven vibrations of the colony through the limestone beneath me, a subtle, constant tickle that made my muscles shudder. Every elbow scrape, every inch of movement brought more of them into contact. A few clung to my shirt, tiny legs pressing into my skin as I dragged myself backward. I tried not to scream.

Eventually, exhaustion claimed me. I collapsed onto the floor, chest pressed against cold stone, sweat dripping down my face. The giant creature loomed ahead. My headlamp rested on it, reflecting in its enormous, faceted eyes.

It didn’t move. Not a twitch. Not a scuttle. And yet, the colony swarmed all around me, climbing over rocks, walls, and ceiling. My chest heaved as cockroaches crawled across me, brushing over my collarbones and slipping under my elbows. My stomach twisted, my skin crawled, every nerve raw with queasy panic.

Then I realized: the giant creature hadn’t moved at all. As long as the light stayed on it, it remained frozen. Its stillness contrasted violently with the writhing mass around me. My fear sharpened into a focused terror—one part fascination, one part primal disgust.

I crawled backward, keeping the beam of my headlamp fixed on the massive creature. Every blink, every twitch of my head caused it to advance. Exhaustion made me falter again. I fell to my elbows. And then, finally, I understood: as long as the light stayed fixed on it, it stayed frozen. Its patience became a kind of cruel standoff.

At last, daylight appeared. Relief surged as I crawled fully out of the cave and fell onto the dirt outside. My arms were raw, my legs trembling, but I was alive.

I grabbed my backpack and pistol. Hands shaking, I fired blindly into the cave. Echoes rattled the limestone walls. I emptied every round. The tapping and clicking vanished.

For a moment, I thought the creature had fled or perhaps I had hit it in my blind flurry of bullets. But the silence was deliberate. It had waited. Patiently. Calculating. Waiting for the noise to end.  

I heard its legs tap against the rock, and its mouth click as it moved. The noise became fainter by the second. I sighed of relief, and fell to the floor, and began to weep.

A week later, I returned—not to explore, but to mark the cave on a map. The landslide had recently opened it, leaving jagged limestone walls and a narrow entrance. I carefully sketched the entrance and recorded my observations.

The creature remained inside, patient and still. Its presence lingered in my mind like a shadow.

I gave it a name in my journal: Blight cave. Short, simple, and ominous.

Even now, thinking of the reflective eyes, the grinding mouthparts, and the quiet tapping of legs makes my skin crawl. But the small, squished roach at the entrance—the one I had destroyed instinctively—left a strange clarity behind. I had survived something that made my body revolt at the mere thought of it, and yet, strangely, I felt… different. Less afraid. Somehow stronger.

Blight waits in the cave, patient, silent, and eternal. And I know I will remember that quiet lesson, the one my body had understood before my mind could name it.

Even after telling the entire story, I still feel like I haven’t done it justice. That may partially be cause I refuse to dig it back up. That moment will forever live in my mind, locked away in the darkest depths, or should I say caves of my mind.
