We assume that our souls (if we believe in the soul at all) are somewhere within us, but we couldn’t be more wrong.

It was about a month ago when Jerry moved in next door. I have lived in a small retirement community for the last ten years, and I always try to make myself a good neighbor. Despite my age, I’m still fairly able bodied, and I like to make myself of use to our less fortunate residents. I was hoping to make a new friend and be of some assistance if necessary.

I knocked on the door, he answered, and I saw a short hunched-over man with thin white hair that dangled in front of his piercing blue eyes. Jerry looked incredibly old, older than anyone I’d ever seen before. I thought to myself that if I was 85 years old, this man must be 115.

I introduced myself and he invited me inside. In spite of his seemingly decrepit body, he got around about as well as I did. We made a little small talk and I asked him if he needed any help settling in.

“No, I’m all settled, thank you. But would you do me a favor? Could you come by every day, about this time, and take down that jar for me?”

He pointed toward a large mason jar on top of his kitchen cabinets. I was tall enough to reach it, but he would need a stepladder, and I’m sure that he didn’t want to risk that.

“If you don’t mind, of course. It would be a big help. It’s very precious to me, and I would never forgive myself if I dropped it trying to get it down.”

I told him that I would give him a hand with it whenever he needed it. He smiled ear to ear when I agreed, and I thought that it looked a tad sinister.

“Would you mind taking it down now for me?”

I took the jar down and set it on the kitchen table. Inside was a layer of some sort of sand, with what looked like millions of tiny colorful orbs throughout and on top of it. Gazing into the jar, I felt almost immediately mesmerized by how the little spheres of light would change from one fantastically vibrant color to the next, and how they shifted and pulsated in the sand, seemingly of their own volition. Astonished, I asked him what it was.

“It’s a collection of mine. There’s nothing like it in the universe. Don’t you think?”

I agreed, and asked him what it was a collection of.

“I suppose you could call them eggs. I used to be an explorer of sorts when I was young. These were found during one of my travels to a very remote place. You would never have heard of it, much less be able to find it on a map. Ha! Few have ever laid eyes on these, and none but me can claim to have ever touched one of them.”

It was a very strange answer, and I wanted to know more, but I decided not to ask for more information if he didn’t want to give it. Jerry pulled another jar out of his kitchen cabinet which was filled with a dark purplish liquid. That jar he sat next to the jar with the orbs on the table, then stuck his finger in the fluid and swirled it around. When his finger was sufficiently wet, he removed it and hovered it about the other jar, letting droplets of the unknown fluid fall into the orbs and the sand. He repeated this process three times, then returned the jar of liquid to the cabinet. I asked him why he had to do that to the eggs.

“It’s a nutrient solution, to keep them healthy. I need to do just one more thing, then I’ll have you return the jar to its place.”

He reached into the jar, pulled out a pinch of the orbs, placed them in his mouth, and swallowed. I gasped audibly, and he chuckled. Shocked, I asked him why he would do that if his collection was as rare and precious as he claimed.

“Don’t worry, there will be more. I need to keep the balance. Return it to its place now, will you?”

I did as he asked of me, then made some excuse for why I had to leave. The scene had been very weird and unsettling to me, and I wished that I hadn’t agreed to do this on a daily basis. He wished me goodbye, and I went home.

Every day for the next week, I came over to his house to take down his strange jar so he could feed his eggs and eat a pinch of them. If I asked a question, he gave me a vague and unsatisfying answer, so I stopped trying. The orbs in the jar would glow, and I would just stand in silence and stare into them until the ritual was over.

The accident happened shortly after the first week. Jerry was hovering his finger above the sandy jar like he always did, but his arm quivered and he accidentally tipped the container over. What looked like thousands of those mysterious orbs tumbled onto the floor. I stood there frozen, unable to breathe, as I saw all of the lights flicker out. A stream of obscenities and words in some language that I didn’t recognize poured out of Jerry’s mouth. The eggs on the floor disappeared before my eyes, leaving no evidence that they had ever been there.

“I need you to keep your eye on me while I’m doing this from now on,” Jerry said, now unnaturally calm. “That wasn’t supposed to happen. Not now.”

I didn’t dare to question what that meant. I nodded my head in agreement, and then we continued where we left off.

Later that night, I turned on the news. There had been an extremely unexpected earthquake in a city in Asia which had caused thousands of deaths. I didn’t know why, but a macabre thought came to my mind: was there somehow a connection between the falling of the orbs and this natural disaster? Chuckling to myself, I dismissed the idea immediately. How could what I saw at Jerry’s have possibly caused a perfectly explainable phenomenon?

I kept keeping the ritual at Jerry’s over the next week, and that odd thought kept coming into my mind. Finally, I decided to do something to test the theory. While Jerry was reaching for his jar of liquid and his back was turned, I quickly reached inside the sandy jar, grabbed a handful of eggs, and crushed them in my hand. A second letter, I opened my fist and saw what I expected: absolutely nothing. Jerry was none the wiser and continued with his routine as usual. I left when he was done and ran home as fast as an old man like me could.

On the news was another calamity. A skyscraper had fallen and led to the destruction of an entire city block. Again, thousands dead. I couldn’t understand how, but those luminous orbs were absolutely connected to these deaths somehow.

The next day, I went over to Jerry’s house as usual. When I saw him this time, however, he looked even older than usual. If he had looked 115 before, he looked 200 now. His skin clung to his bones so tightly that I could see the outline of his skull behind his face. Walking toward me slowly, he pointed a curled and unsteady finger at my heart.

“What have you done? What have you done?” he whispered at me.

I tried to play it off. I told him that I hadn’t done anything except what he told me to.

“Liar. You were in my jar. You destroyed what is mine to destroy. They’re mine, you understand me? You have no right. No right! You’re going to pay for this. Wait and see.”

Jerry turned around and walked into the kitchen, and I slowly followed him in. He reached for his mysterious jar, and his torso twisted and stretched upward until the object was in his hand. Stupefied, I watched as his torso uncoiled partially and brought Jerry’s face level to mine. There was no skin on him at all now, he was just bones. His jaw flapped in a sinisterly mocking fashion, out of sync with his words, as he said to me: “You fool, you are mine to destroy. I obtained this power with a price. You have no right to your own life, much less any others.”

He reached into the jar and held a single infinitesimally small orb of light between the bones which were once his fingers. Jerry, or whatever this being was, answered the question that I was too frozen with fear to ask: “I am the angel of death.”

I don’t remember exactly what happened next. All of the remaining courage within me rose to a boiling point, and in a burst of blind self-preservation instinct I made my move to save my life. Somehow I managed to wrestle the orb from his skeletal grip and escape, but not without receiving some serious cuts and explicable burns across my arms and face. I ran away from the house as fast and as far as I could, which wasn’t very fast and wasn’t very far, but by the time I ran out of breath I realized that there was no one following me. Slowly and carefully, I made my way back to the retirement community. When I got back to Jerry’s house, it was cleaned out. There was no evidence that he had ever been there, and the jar of souls and the jar of soul-nourishing liquid were both gone.

My wounds need medical attention, but I can’t risk going to the hospital. I have to look after the orb and make sure that no harm comes to it. It’s my life. I managed to transport it home without dropping it or crushing it in my hand, and now I have it sitting in a jar with some dirt in it. Every day its light gets a little less bright, its colors a little less vibrant. I am dying. With no way to obtain the nutrient fluid, it won’t be long now. I am dying. We believe that we have control over our own souls, but we don’t. They’ve been stolen from us. I am dying.
