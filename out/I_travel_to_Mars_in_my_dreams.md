Crimson. What a beautiful color, I thought as I gazed upon the terrain. The sand squished between my toes, finer than the sand you’d find on any beach. I dropped to my hands and knees to dig my fingers in it, feeling the loose powder stick to the oil of my appendages, feeling it sneak its way underneath my fingernails. I sat now, attracting even more mineral to my unclothed body. I stretched out my legs, leaning back. I traced my eyes over hills and valleys. Craters and mountains. All of it bathed in that beautiful zesty red. Once satisfied with my visual bounty, I flopped backward. Laying there in that beautiful fine dust, I gazed at the small blue speck in the sky that must be Earth, where my real body is asleep right now. 

This has been my nightly routine for as long as I can remember. And for all that time, I’ve only had one goal in life: to see it in person. 

And so I would. 

It took years of training. Years of hard work, hard tests, and hard days. But at the end of each day, I got to go back to that wonderful red planet. The place I love so much. The place that is the entirety of who I am and what I’ve done. 

I spent months on the ground simulating the journey. Two years in low Earth orbit preparing. 

But it would all be worth it. 

I wake. It is my last day on Earth. I stretch and look around my room, knowing it’s the last time I’ll ever see it. The pink walls, the white curtains. My vanity. The dresser and the closet. A mounted tv. I get dressed in my jumpsuit, a uniform I will wear for the next nine months of flight. I look in the mirror of my vanity while I tie up my long, brown hair. 

This is it. 

My mother met me down at the station. 

“Lydia!” She screamed the second she saw me. She sprinted my way and crushed me in a hug. There were tears in her hazel eyes. “How do you feel? Nervous?”

I breathed in deep, “no,” I said confidently. I wasn’t. “I’m excited.”

She hummed, softly touching my cheek. “I know everything will be fine,” I knew she was worried. She pressed her forehead to mine, “Your dad would be so proud of you,” she whispered, a tear rolling down her cheek. 

Dad passed away a few months back. Cancer. He had been determined to see me off, but he had been too sick. In the end, he looked at me with his grey eyes, grey skin, and grey hair. He took my hand in his own shaky one. Tubes came out of every orifice and created new ones unnecessarily.    

He had been determined to see me off and see my return. 

But he was too sick. In too much pain. 

That last night, he blinked slowly at me as tears fell from his eyes. Trying to communicate how much he loved me. 

I cried at his side and told him he could let go. It broke my heart, but it’s what he needed to hear. I knew he was holding on for me. 

It wasn’t long before his body gave up. 

“I know,” I said, my own eyes watering. 

She held me tighter, “I’m proud of you.”

I wiped the tear from her face, “I know,” my voice was becoming thin and shaky. I should’ve known I’d cry today. 

“I love you,” she squeaked out. 

“I love you,” I wailed, knowing this really was goodbye. 

She held me tight as the coordinator came up and sheepishly told me it was time. I sniffled as I kissed her temple. 

“I’ll see you when you get back,” she smiled, petting my hair. 

She wouldn’t. 

I always throw up during takeoff. You think there’s a lot of turbulence on a plane until you’re on a speeding rocket breaking the barrier of Earth’s atmosphere. 

The next nine months passed slowly. I chatted with my colleagues, I ate the terrible freeze-dried food, I used my mastery of how to go to the bathroom in microgravity. 

“I’m just ready to see some different sights,” Michaels told us over a game of cards. “I’m tired of the walls of this ship. It gets claustrophobic.”

“You said it,” said Zhong. “We can finally stretch.” 

Peters stretched out his bare foot, “these piggies are going to market!” He cackled. 

“Gross,” she pushed him away. 

Peters went spinning the other direction. 

“Guys, I don’t know if we want to land.” I chimed in. “Then we can’t send Peters off like that.”

He cackled some more, still spiraling into a wall. 

I shook my head. This group of idiots is who I’m sharing my last days with. 

The night before we landed, I spent my time on the red dunes traveling. When I found the ship, I dropped to my knees and used my finger to write a message in the dust. 

When the ship’s landing gears set against the ruby terrain, it felt like coming home. It was hard not to be antsy as we ran reports, talked with coms, and checked the ship over. 

We suited up. Michael’s was staying back to monitor. We checked our coms, and they worked great. Michael could see what we saw and talk back and forth to each of us. 

I fought so hard for my place, but after a lot of arguing, reasoning, and pleading, I got the place to be the first human on Mars. 

As the airlock cycled and the door finally opened, I took a deep breath. It felt like it was in slow motion. My foot extending past the threshold and lowering to the scarlet sand. 

Finally, my foot made contact with the red planet. My eyes fell on the red dunes and craters. The valleys and hills. The familiar mineral and all I had to learn. 

I looked up. The stars above felt so much more important. I’m on a celestial body that can be seen from Earth right now. 

I looked down. The ruby ground beneath my feet beamed up at me. It was so vivid and real. I’d walked this ground so many times during my slumber and today is the day I make it a reality. 

The first of any human. I deserved to be here. This was the culmination of so many years of hard work. I knew I could do it. I knew I’d be here one day. 

“Well, go on,” Micheal’s encouraged. 

I realized I was practically panting I was so excited. 

I screamed a “wooo!!” as I made my way onto the planet. 

Space suits are heavy and bulky and not graceful. But all the same, I ran, and spun, and shouted. 

Zhong and Peter’s were behind me, and they too yipped and yowled and ran around. 

Finally, we all landed lying on our backs looking up. 

“Beautiful view,” Zhong commented. 

“Beautiful,” I agreed, tears making their way over the hills of my cheeks as I sobbed. 

Peters, not finding anything sarcastic to say, said nothing. 

Then I remembered something. I got up as fast as I could (falling a couple times), and looked around. 

“What are you looking for?” Zhong asked. 

I didn’t know how to answer that until I found it. 

“Her mind,” Peters smiled. 

I started to panic, I couldn’t find it anywhere. 

What if my dreams weren’t real? What if it had been nothing? What if I chased a ghost all my life?

I skidded to a stop. 

There it was. 

“Guys,” I called. Then when I saw they weren’t moving, I screamed “guys!”

Slowly getting up they made their way to me. 

“Is it water?” Zhong asked excitedly. 

“Is it a little green guy?” Asked Peters. 

“Look!” I bounced. 

“Whoa,” Zhong’s eyes widened. 

Peters was once again speechless. 

“What the fuck?” Micheals mumbled into his microphone. 

Scrawled in the sand and was the very same message I’d written last night. 

“Hi!”

It was all I could think to write. 

“Wha-what does this mean?” Zhong shook. 

“Who coulda done this? We got aliens for sure?” Peters’s hands came up to the sides of his head. 

“This is.. this is crazy,” Micheals breathed. 

“What are we supposed to do?” Zhong grabbed me. 

“I… I don’t know,” I realized. I hadn’t thought that far ahead. 

We set tags around it and took pictures and samples, almost like a crime scene. We even wrote a “hi” back. 

We set up the rest of our equipment and ended our day as the sun gradually faded. 

Getting out of that suit felt amazing. I could scratch my itchy ankles and air out all my sweat. 

Coms down on the ground wanted to know everything about the message. They wanted 24/7 surveillance on that spot. They received all the data we could gather and seemed mildly satisfied for now. 

After washing up, I went to bed. And that’s where it really began. 

I spent all night on my next message. 

In the morning, after coffee and terrible freeze dried food, it was time to suit up. Today was my turn to stay inside and monitor. I was happy to do it, I had spent my time well. 

It wasn’t long before they came upon it. 

Micheals exhaled as he marveled, “holy shit.”

The others joined, ooing and ahhing. 

Micheal’s read aloud, “my name is Lydia Ackerman. I’ve been coming here every night for as long as I can remember. I dream it, but they are not just dreams. It’s traveling. It’s occupying space. It’s being just as at home here as on Earth. I spent so much time and energy and blood and sweat and tears to be where I am today. And I’m proud to be on this crew with you.” 

They were all silent for a while. 

“Ackerman,” Micheals finally said, “did you really do this?” 

I had done it! It had worked. “Yes,” I managed. 

“Holy shit we got part alien Martian demon on our team!” Peters cheered. 

“This is unbelievable,” Zhong whispered. 

“Believe it! This is some real twilight zone shit.”

“Shut up, Peters,” I told him. 

“Never,” he made a heart with his hands. 

When the crew came back in and ground control got the updated information, they urged us to check the cameras. 

The image of the crimson sand greeted us. It was a long time of scrolling through dead footage. When it came time, no figure appeared, no shadow, no ghost, just an invisible finger to write my tale. 

There was a barrage of questions from mission control. They wanted to know everything. 

“How do you do it?” Was the most asked. 

“I sleep,” I answered simply. 

We were getting nowhere. This was completely unprecedented. There was no set way to study this because no one thought they would ever have to study this. They said when I get back I’m doing all sorts of tests. 

I’m not going back. 

That night I laid in my bed, waiting for the others to slide off to the land of normal dreams. I slid out of my bunk quietly, and made my way around the ship. 

The airlock made too much noise for my liking, but I didn’t hear anyone stir, so I continued on my way. My breath hitched as I shut it behind me, and prepared for the cycle. 

This had been my plan all along. This was where I planned to end it. The years I spent on this planet in my sleep were the best times of my life. This was the place I felt at home, the place I felt safe. This was where I belonged, not down there on Earth where every day is a struggle. I’ve wrestled enough to make my way in the world. I had achieved everything I had ever wanted. I’d lived a full life. The only thing left to do was open that airlock. 

I thought of my crew mates, the three people I’ve spent years with through training and our mission. They’ll certainly be confused about my decision. 

I thought of my friends, how they’ll miss me and talk of my triumphant and my embarrassing moments. 

I thought of my mother, how she had held me and told me she was proud. How she must’ve reacted to her baby girl being the first to step foot on the red planet. 

I thought of my father, how hard he’d fought for me. How much pain he was in. How hard it must’ve been to let go. To die. I remember the soft look in his tender eyes. His quivering grey eyebrows that were so thin they were only a whisper. His drooling lips, hardly even in control of them anymore. He shook. And finally, he didn’t. He was brave in the face of death, and he knew it was his time. 

It’s my time. 

This is it. It’s time to return to the planet that gave me everything. This was where I was meant to be.

Where I was meant to die. 

I turned to face the outer door as it cracked open. 

Instantly all the air was sucked out and I began to be sucked toward the door. My ears popped painfully and blood began to trickle down the sides of my neck. It became freezing cold and I felt my tears and saliva turn into a thin coating of ice. Moving my tongue, which now felt like beef jerky, set cracks in the frost. My blood flash boiled for only an agonizing second before freezing too. As it inched even more open. All my mucus was frozen by now. The air robbed from my lungs. I moved my arm and my frozen veins cracked and crunched as they fractured and fell apart. Open more yet, my skin like tough leather. Ice expands and as it did my chest bowed as my organs swelled. Open and open more, the sun reached my eyes, instantly scorching them into darkness. It hurt. God, did it hurt. This was meant to be. Finally the door was open all the way. The dead silence rang in my deaf ears as my body floated, bloated, into air. My feet touched the sand as my toes began dissolving, my fingers too. The waves of flesh and bone expelling from my body and into space. I was certainly dead by now. It crept up my arms and my legs. It felt like spreading out. Like with it I became numb as my nerves stretched and burned. It’s made it to my torso now as my head began to go. Expanding and scattering ever still. At last, my heart was the last to go. 

I had returned to the stars.
