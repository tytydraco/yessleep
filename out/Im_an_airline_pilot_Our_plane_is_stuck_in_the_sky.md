We've been up here for several hours now. Rescue teams are on their way up to start offboarding the plane, but I won't be coming with them.

I've been a commercial airline pilot for a little over three years now. After getting my certification, I got a job for a regional airline, staying mostly within the Mid-Atlantic area. I thought they'd enjoy life out here, away from our provincial lives in the Midwest. I love Iowa, but from personal experience, there's only so much to do growing up there. Aside from declining education rates and rising drug abuse, my wife Sherry and I believed suburbia in a new state would be a fresh start for our next big chapter; Ashley and Samantha. Our two beautiful girls. The move to the coast was hard at first for me and Sherry, but we learned to make it work.

The thought of being a stay at home mother was daunting to Sherry at first. She even went to group therapy for a while just to settle in. She claims that the people there help her, though they can be a bit shrill at times. Thankfully I've never been away from my wife and kids for more than a night. She told me at times she could look up to see me flying right above our known home not far from Krobach, MD. She'd even bring Ashley and Samantha out to see their daddy high in the sky.

Well, today I wasn't sure if I'd get home at all. I was piloting an aircraft out of Washington DC to Pittsburgh. It was the first of only three legs I had scheduled today.

It was Sunday at around 4AM when I left home to start my day at the airport. I met up with my copilot, Roger, who had stayed at one of those Marriott hotels beforehand. Roger served in the airforce, and had a plethora of stories he'd share during our flights together. It's hard to imagine his short and stumpy stature being once an Ace, but I enjoyed his stories nonetheless.

\--

I didn't need to go into work this early. Today was Roger's birthday, though, and I wanted to meet up with him for a halfway decent breakfast of airport eggs and bacon. Roger was away from his family, so as his co-pilot and friend, I thought it'd be nice to keep him company until our departure at 8AM. I even got him a silver flask (empty, of course).

After so long of flying people too and from destinations for so long, a lot of the faces you see start to mesh into one person. Especially this early in the morning, baggy eyes meet mine with tired gazes. It's only a handful of passengers that leave an imprint, and they are never the ones you'd want to sit next to. Thankfully, Rosanne and Grace were two excellent attendants on today's flight, along with four other stewardesses that I hadn't met before. Rosanne and Grace were both pleasant, young women, but I've seen them scrape with some unruly passengers from time to time. If I ever got into a bar fight, they'd be the first ones I'd pick on my team.

This flight had one such passenger. I'll spare him the embarrassment of outing him, so let's call him Dave. Dave walked into the plane furious, carrying a Louis Vuitton bag, large noise cancelling headphones around his neck, and wore an oversized blue suit for an already oversized portly body. I could see sweat dripping down his red face, snarling already from the argument he had outside the gate.

A shy woman wearing a grey fleece sweater followed right behind him. She looked pale and looked to be malnourished, keeping her eyes down to the floor. A massive welt bruised her left eye.

"Unfucking believable," he muttered, explosively. "If I pay for good seats, I *expect* good seats."

Dave turned to me directly, and asked "Hey, Maverick, you run this show right? The bitches at the gate wouldn't give in when I tried asking for an upgrade. I'm tired and row 30 just ain't gonna work for me."

I've never had a passenger ask me directly on something like this before. Since Roger and I were the heads of the plane, anything was technically our call. I didn't want to mess up the system's processing and checking and, frankly, I didn't appreciate referring to any of my coworkers as bitches.

"Sorry sir, all seats are final at this point. If you have any other requests, our attendants will be happy to assist you."

Dave huffed, "I don't take no for an answer." The pale woman behind him kept her head down.

Thankfully, Rosanne stepped in to escort Dave and the woman to their seats. I was worried at first if I'd have to call security, but after a brief discussion with Grace and complimentary whiskey, Dave settled down.

\--

Our plane took off at 8:14 in the morning. The early start to the day let us take off early.

Leading up into the sky, I saw the distant fields, marshes, and rivers that stretch across Maryland. Looking out, I thought of Sherry, hoping she was out of bed to see me fly overhead. The girls too.

It was exactly 8:39 AM when we hit it. The immense force met the plane like a speeding car running head on into a semi-truck. The impact drove Roger and my bodies' directly into the flight controllers, smashing our ribs and over the joysticks like tridents. I recall my forehead whipping so fast that I could feel my spine almost snap from the unknown collision. Keep in mind that planes at that elevation are going at least 150 mph. The pain in my chest and hips were so immense, that I thought I shattered my pelvis.

Roger's portly body did more bad than good. I could hear his shoulder pop loudly out of place as he tried to move. It seemed the weight of his torso flung him with more force, leaving a bloody mess when he removed the switches from the control board that pierced his gut. We were both in a lot of pain, but after several minutes we began to regain our senses. If it weren't for the airbags installed in our seats, who knows if I'd even be alive to share this story with you all.

The entire plane had stopped moving. I don't mean it lost power or the engines failed, I mean the entire craft just *halted* in mid-air. From what I could tell, the plane was simply hovering over the Earth, sitting in the sky like an unwavering storm cloud.

"Wh-what is this?" I stammered out, the wind still knocked out of my chest.

Roger looked at me, wincing in pain from his injuries. "Don't fucking know. God DAMN, what the hell was that?"

My confusion and pain momentarily went away when I heard the moaning and wailing out in the cabin progressively get louder. Unbuckling my seatbelt, I worked my way up to stand, preparing myself for the damage done.

The entire cabin was pandamonium. On both sides of the aisle were rows and rows of injured passengers. Those who weren't clutching their knees to their chests from the pain of the impact were shrieking up to the dangling oxygen masks which had dropped from each overhead compartment. Through the sea of wincing, bloodied eyes and broken faces, I saw the body of Grace laying face down by the back row, twitching in her purple attendant outfit.

I moved quickly to check on her. In first class, an elderly couple had bruises all over their forehead and forearms, black veins starting to form through their broken bodies. Towards the main cabin, a mother was wailing, shaking wearily the body of her two year old daughter, whose spine was visibly protruding internally from their neck. I passed by a man who held his wife in his arms, vomiting on the floor with red blots tracing through. In hindsight, I'd say it was a blessing that there were more survivors than I expected. If I had to guess, the cushions of this larger vessel saved most people's lives from whatever the impact was. All I could think of is what kind of disaster did we find ourselves in?

As I got closer to the back, a hand shot out at me, gripping the collar of my white, now bloodied captain's shirt. Dave's bloated head swung at me like a boulder, seething with agony and rage.

"WHAT THE FUCK DID YOU DO? WHAT KIND OF R------- JOKE ARE YOU PLAYING AT I WILL SUE THE HELL OUT OF YOU DUMB FUCKS SO HELP ME GOD I WILL..."

He went on like that for a while. The pale woman sitting next to him was now leaning head first into the seat in front of her. I could see her teeth piercing her lips, and long broken nose  now loosely filling in with blood. Her breathing was weak, and she probably passed out from the pain, but she was alive at least.

I removed Dave's hand from my collar and moved forward. Pain soared through my sides as I kneeled down to Grace's body. Rosanne met me there.

"Joey...I...what happened? Is Roger alright? What exactly is--"

"I don't know," I blurted, my words pouring out slowly from my gut like vomit. Each vowel was like a tight rope straining my neck. I could barely speak, but needed to help out Rosanne as much as I could.

\--

For the next two hours, the new attendant (named Phil), Rosanne, Roger, and myself were running through the corridor providing first aid and supplies to those who needed aid first: broken faces and limbs, profuse bleeding, anything at all that seemed life threatening. Those lucky enough to survive with only a few wounds received water and food to help mend themselves and recover. Those who lost a loved one and were inconsolable, like the mother from before, were given tarps or blankets to help cover their dead in the back row of the plane. We were eventually able to move enough passengers forward from to prop the dead up in the seats, per company policy. There were 17 blankets in total.

Sitting up the last of the bodies, I could hear Dave's murmur "At least I get to move up now."

At this point, those with severe injuries were resting, and those with only minor injuries were asking questions.

Despite the sudden impact, it didn't seem that the plane itself took any damage. It simply stopped moving. Looking out the right side sat the fast Atlantic open, and the West a bright, sunny view of Maryland's Western shore. It was a sunny day, and nothing obstructed our vision, beside the swelling. As far as anyone could see, the plane wasn't stopped by *anything*.

Roger had been contacting air traffic control for sometime now. In the time it took Rosanne, Phil and me to assist with the injured, Roger was doing the real heavy lifting by figuring out what was going on.

"*Flight 1536, we are handling the situation as best as we can. For the time being, all flights will be halted. We've also alerted the national guard who are actively investigating the area. Hang tight boys, we'll get you down soon. In the meantime, we advise not to touch any of the equipment out of precaution for national security."*

Roger brought his bandaged hand up the speaker *"*10-4. Keep us posted. We have a lot of injured people up here who need help. Who needs moving."

Roger and I sat in silence in the cockpit for a minute. We could still hear the scared murmurs of the passengers behind us and the soft but firm voice of Rosanne answering questions as best as she could, followed by Dave's hacking and harrumphing. The fat bastard kept requesting water bottles, despite those who really needed it.

*National security,* I thought. *Could this really be some kind of attack? Terrorists wouldn't have this kind of tech. Hell, does the army have this kind of tech? And what would that make us, some kind of experiment? Do they think they can sweep this under the rug? I mean these are* people *and they can't just use us like this. Perhaps it was an enemy attack, and they missed the mark on DC. Were there more of us out there? How many--"*

Roger interrupted my thinking with a firm "Hey." My pensive face gave away my concern.

"We can't lose our cool. Trust me, I don't want to be here either, but for the folks out there, we have to see this through. We're in control here. We aren't falling yet, so let's just hang tight until the cavalry arrives."

Sitting and waiting has never been my style, but Roger was right. For now, we'd have to do everything we could until air traffic control told us what to do.

I got up to stretch my legs and use the restroom. I've never been afraid of heights, but the thought of sitting untouched in the sky was unnerving. Especially to Dave, who at this point was in such a state of panic that he removed his suit jacket and collared shirt, sitting in a pool of his own sweat and spittle. Dave snagged a half filled water bottle from the pale woman, now sitting one row ahead of him.

"Sir, please, I understand you're under pressure, we all are, but resources are low."

"FUCK YOU!" bellowed Dave. "If it weren't for you c---s and those fuckers flying the plane, we wouldn't be in this mess! I was just trying to go to Pittsburgh, for christ's sake. We're going to die up here thanks to you!"

The anxious crowd grew more paranoid. Dave was an asshole, but it was clear from a few more faces in the crowd that the feeling was becoming mutual. I explained to Dave and everyone that help is on the way, and that we were doing everything in our power to keep them safe, but for now we needed to stay put.

\--

Five hours passed before we got official word from air traffic control (ATC). Airforce jets flew by and gave us more detail on our situation. Our plane was a little under 10,000 feet off the ground, so while altitude sickness could be a concern, the plane would not lose oxygen anytime soon. There was no external damage to the plane, though the national guard said the outside appeared to be coated in some 'shimmering substance'. After testing with a few water solutions, the coating seemed to vanish. Roger and I braced to potentially move again, but nothing happened.

"*Flight 1536, the national guard is telling us to hang tight still. I'm sorry, but it looks like its going to take longer than we expected."*

Roger took the talkie, "Thank you. Is there any chance of rescue vehicles coming up to take some of our injured. No one else has died thankfully, but I'm concerned for the damage that took them."

*"We'll see what we can do. Once we get the word from the investigators down here, we can launch official rescue operations."*

"Investigators? What else could they possibly be looking for? All the craziness is up here."

"*They're investigating the general area underneath the plane. Once they ensure that the area below the affected plane is safe, we can resume rescue operations."*

*--*

We sat in the cockpit for several more hours. I was missing Sherry. My two girls. I wish I could contact them from up here, but ATC requested we keep our devices off until the guard said it was safe to do so. The cabin was thankfully compliant at this point, the trauma of the past 24 hours exhausting them. Or maybe it was just learned helplessness. Rosanne was sitting idly, the first time in hours. Even Dave was resting comfortably. For the first time, there was finally a still quiet that was comfortable enough to sleep too. Despite the fear of lingering off the ground, it was finally feeling like how a short, boring flight should be.

I got up to use the restroom, running into Rosanne. "How's it looking from the ground up?"

*Not great*, is what I wanted to say. "It's coming together. We just need to keep everyone in line for the time being. How's the scene out there?"

"As good as it can be, I suppose. Phil is taking some time in the restroom to process everything. It's only his first week after all."

"Poor guy."

"Yeah, tell me about it."

Rosanne stepped closer to me. Quietly and calmly, she muttered "We need to do something about the bodies. The cool air is preserving them well enough, but the smell is starting to get pretty bad. I don't think it'll be easy to maintain peace here if it smells like death."

I sighed, "I think there may be some fabreeze in the back. Not great, I know, but lavender smells better than shit."

Rosanne chuckled "That's one way of putting it. Can ATC or the guard do anything to at least grab the dead?"

"We're working on that, though I did ask them to bring 17 body bags when they were ready."

"18."

'Excuse me?"

"You mean 18, right? That's how many bodies there are."

"Did someone else die? Why didn't you alert me?"

"No one else died on our watch, Joey. They were always there. I learned to count when I was four, so don't even try me."

I ran to the back. I started from the back rows. Three. Six. Nine. Twelve. Fifteen. Eighteen?

Rosanne was right. The number of blankets changed. What body did I overlook? I could have sworn it wasn't there before.

From the third row from the back, seat C. Directly to my right hand side, a gurgling noise began to bubble out from the limped corpse. From the inside of the makeshift body bag, I could see blood trailing from what looked like the eyes and mouth of the victim, slowly spilling down and being absorbed by the blanket's cotton fibers. The crimson sheet grew tighter, and showed the expression of the body's visage. Their short forehead, small mouth, and narrow nose. It almost looked like...

The horror shot through me in an instant, like a rifle through the heart. I unbuckled the dead passenger's seat and lifted the tarp from over their head. Sherry's bloody eyes met mine. Only she didn't look dead, as her eyes rolled back and forth, darting around the main cabin, confused like a small.

"SHERRY!?"

*'What are you doing here?*' was all I could think of. *How* could she be up here? My panic must have been apparent, because just as Rosanne came back to check on me, I fell flat on my ass.

Sherry's eyes rotated slowly down below. She opened her mouth, blood pouring out slowly. From further back, I could see the wounds scattered throughout her chest.

"Oh, it's you."

I got up, crawling towards her. "Sh-Sherry?! Baby, what are you...I mean, how are you here? Oh god, baby, please no, nononono... we'll get you help, baby, ok? Just please lay down and I'll get--"

"How did you get here?"

"What? Sher-bear, you're not making sense."

"No, you can't hold them."

I sat there stunned. Rosanne, behind me, held her hand to her mouth, tearing up. Tears of blood streamed down Sherry's face. She looked right at me, grimacing her face in a collage of fear and anger. "Oh, it's you."

Despite my efforts, the scene was drawing attention. Dave, turned around and saw my wife's visage. "Jesus FUCKING Christ, what it that?"

Rosanne darted back at him, pointing at the pale woman, who had also seen Sherry's body "Sir, sit down with your wife we're handling this."

Dave turned back at the woman. "You think I'd marry this broad? I don't even know who this is!"

Rosanne sighned in frustration "Sir, sit the fuck down now!" Dave sat down, unsure of what to make of the situation. The pale woman kept staring.

While this was happening, Sherry continued. "How did you get here? No, you can't hold them."

The pale woman stood up. I turned to her and, to my surprise, she was more haunted by the sight of my wife than I was. Sherry saw this. Her dead eyes shifted towards its new target.

Bellowing angrily, Sherry repeated "OH, ITS YOU. HOW DID YOU GET HERE?"

The pale woman shrieked when Sherry stood up. I almost did the same.

"NO, YOU CAN'T HOLD THEM!"

And just like that, Sherry vanished. Evaporated from within the cabin itself. Those who saw everything were too scared to speak. Myself included.

The pale woman started to sob. More than that, she was wailing, just like the mother up front. "I'm so sorry, I'm *so* sorry. I just...they were so pretty." Guttural sobs continued to belt out from her frail frame. Who was this woman?

\--

Roger signaled me back to the cockpit. I don't think he heard what transpired in the back of the plane, but the look on his face looked sullen.

"Um...Joey...I need you to come back in here. The national guard is back. They are ready for rescue operations."

I went inside the cockpit. The bullet of fear in my heart had festered into a pit of dread that was consuming me. What was Sherry trying to tell me? And how did she know that woman?

Once I calmed down a bit, Roger hailed ATC. "Ok boss, he's here."

The voice on comms was an agent. A leading investigator for this operation, I think his name was Jameson, or something. It didn't really matter.

"Hello Joey. First off, we appreciate your assistance as we continue to resolve this case. You and Roger's leadership is admirable."

I muttered a thank you as my mind was still trailing off into the distance. "Any updates to the investigation? We're getting pretty tired up here. Starting to see things."

"What kind of things?"

I didn't know what to say, so I just told them it wasn't important. "Find anything in your 'investigation'?"

There was a long pause in our communications. I could tell he was still on the other side.

"Joey, we were surveilling the area directly below the airplane, approximately 500 meters in diameter below the cockpit's hull. It took so long because...well...we had to search and examine each and every house in the neighborhood. We located the first house that was directly under you both. It was your house, sir."

My heart sank. "Get to the point." I responded "What do you mean?"

"Well, outside of any geospatial anomalies between your family's home and the plane, we had to interview potential suspects. I'm sorry to share this with you, sir. Your family was found deceased when we arrived."

\--

Roger left the cockpit to give me time to process everything and continue my conversation with the investigator. No one knew for sure who had committed the crime, but one or two witness reports saw a tall woman walk out of the house at around 5:30 AM. Witness reports claim she was slender, and had a noticeable welt on her face. Yesterday morning, right after I left the house. I should have stayed longer. I *could* have stayed longer. Sherry, my two girls, gone now because I wanted to spend a few hours in the morning in an airport.

The investigators said they were still searching for potential suspects. They didn't need to though. I already knew who did it. Sherry told me.

I thanked them for their help and their service. The investigator said that they were prepared to start bringing supplies up and begin the offloading process with helicopters if everyone was prepared and able. I told them yes.

As per company protocol, we will transfer each body last once we get every living passenger off the plane. The investigator asked me one last time to confirm the number of bags needed.

I told them 19.

Two other people aren't making it off this flight. Sherry led me right to one of them. Once the emergency doors open, I'll be taking the pale woman with me, one way or another. It's a straight shot down. A straight shot to my home. To my Sherry. To my girls. To my family. I’m coming soon.
