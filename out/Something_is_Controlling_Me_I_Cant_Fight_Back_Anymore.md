The first one was barely two months ago. It was the strangest thing, a tickle in the back of my head.

Something external to my own brain, an idea, a compulsion, a command that wouldn't go away.

“Drop your toothbrush in the toilet”

Don’t ask me why it started. I don't wash my hands enough? God decided he hates me? I couldn’t tell you.

I didn’t do it obviously, not at first.

I just pushed the though away for a few days, figuring it was some crazy thing that would go away on its own. But it didn’t.

Over the next week it occupied more space in my mind, filling it with pictures, sensations, and just the feeling that the only thing I needed to do, was drop my toothbrush in my toilet.

So I did it. 

One morning I just picked it up and dropped it into the water. The effect was instantaneous; my body flooded with a sensation of perfect calm. I felt amazing, my mind was clear, my skin was glowing, and my vision had snapped into the crystal clear focus. It was as though I was doing what I was meant to, and it was so simple. 

People looked at me differently, people treated me differently. I felt like my life had become, just all around better. And then after just two weeks, it was gone, replaced by a new command. 

“Spill your coffee at work”

It felt like I had been hit by a truck. My body was heavy and sore. When I looked in the mirror it was as though a stranger was looking back at me, with heavy, hollow eyes, set against flat grey skin.

So I did that one too.

I was in the office kitchenette with one of my coworkers, Bill. He had been staring at me all morning, and finally brought it up. “Wow man, you look really rough, are you feeling okay?”

I tried to play it off as best I could, “Oh yeah, I just didn’t get a ton of sleep last night, thats what the coffee’s for isn’t it?”

He fiddled with his goatee, seeming satisfied. “Totally man, but hey, if you still feel bad later you should head out early.”

I gave him a thumbs up as he walked out of the room. Then I spilled my coffee, turning it 180 degrees, coating the tiled floor in a layer of weak black coffee. At the same time, my body was wracked with that same sensation of joy. It was like, despite the mess I knew I had made the right decision. 

The effect was just as awesome as it was before, I felt great, my life was great. It lasted maybe nine days before another command came, stealing the light from my life as it did.

“Tell Beth you hate her.”

Strangely, it didn’t feel so impossible. Any other time in my life it would have felt difficult, or maybe ‘wrong,’ but I just went with it. I mean, I had to, my body was in terrible shape. Every movement was made with concerted effort against the soreness that permeated every one of my muscles.

I couldn’t do it in person, even though I knew I should. I whipped out my phone and dialled her number from memory.

She picked up on the second ring. “Hey… is everything—”

I cut her off, “I hate you.”

From the other side of the phone came a combination of a gasp and a half formed sentence, “What the —“

I cut her off again,  “I’m so sorry, I didn’t want to—“

And then it hit, comfort and bliss washed through my body, filling every corner, replacing those awful feelings of pain. I hung up, there was no reason to deal with situations like that at a time when I was feeling so great.

I decided to call out of work too, and just sat on my couch soaking in the sensations that tingled through my body every time I moved.

I went back to work the next day, and the day after, but this time the feeling was gone by day three. There was no new command, but I could still feel myself growing worse and worse over the fourth day.

Bill noticed again, coming by my cubicle to speak to me, “Hey dude, I don’t want to be mean, but you look really really bad.”

I couldn’t really hide it this time. My body was soaked with sweat, and I had barely gotten any work done today. I tried to play it off, but I knew he wouldn’t buy it, “I’m just looking forward to going home, you know the last hour of work really gets to you!”

He definitely didn’t buy it, his faced etched with worry. “Yeah… well… hopefully you feel better tomorrow.”

I waved him off, trying to imply I had something I needed to get done. And then it came.

“Follow Bill home.”

I wanted to resist. I tried to resist even as I stood in the parking lot beside my car. I watched Bill pull out of the lot in his new range rover, telling myself that I wouldn’t follow. But I did.

I scrambled into my front seat and started my own car. Before Bill was fully out of sight I peeled out of the parking lot and followed him at a distance. Through my blurry eyes I kept an eye out for his orange paint job, something which had become less obvious through the grey haze over my vision.

Every turn he made, I shakily followed. Left turn onto Maple st., another left onto Fredrick’s, a third onto Margaret Ave., and a fourth… back onto Maple. His car stopped.

I tried to quickly pull to a stop, but could only manage to slowly roll to a stop with the strength I had.

He got out of the car and walked up to my window. He looked angry. “WHAT ARE YOU DOING? ARE YOU FOLLOWING ME?”

From where I had sunken into my seat I looked up at him. There wasn’t much I could say.

His face was full of disgust. “Look I know you’re on drugs or whatever, but this is seriously messed up. What is wrong with you?”

I tried to smile, but my skin felt stiff. “I’m not, I’m not on any drugs, why don’t we just go to your house and we can talk it out?”

He took a step back from the window. “You’re not going near my family like this.”

I tried to get out of the car, tumbling onto the ground in the process.

Bill’s face morphed into an expression of fear and disgust as I crawled to my feet in front of him. In barely a whisper, I plead with him, “Just take me to your house, thats all I need.”

He turned and started walking away without a word, before turning back for a second. His voice sounded like we were underwater, “Don’t come near me ever again, I will call the —“

With the last remnants of strength I didn’t know I still had, I struck him over the head with my tire iron, sending him crumpling onto the floor.

I didn’t know if he survived, but I dragged him to his car and shoved him into the passenger seat.

I don’t remember the rest of the ride. I don’t know how we didn’t crash, I don’t even know how we made it to his house. The only thing I remember from the rest of that night is me, standing in front of Bill’s front door still holding that tire iron, his body slumped beside me on the stoop. There was no pleasure, I barely even felt functional, but at least there was no pain. I just ran, away from his wife, and kids that I knew were inside, and away from my life as a whole.

I know they’re looking for me. before I ditched my phone I was getting calls from everyone I know, my parents, my work … Beth. That was 4 commands ago, each one worse than the last. I never feel good anymore, and I barely recognize myself when I get a chance to look in a mirror. I'm not sure I even pass for human walking down the street anymore, not that I have time to care.

The worst part is that theres no gap between them, they just enter my brain one after another, forcing themselves through the walls of my brain. I tried to resist for as long as I could, but now I just spend all my time planning these 'tasks' before I stop being able to function.

The only reason I’m even writing this right now is because I was told to, not even my confession is my own. But theres something else too, a scratch at the back of my skull, telling me what I need to do next. Its bad.

I need someone to talk me out of it, please, I just want to stop.

But I know I won’t.
