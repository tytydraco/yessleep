I couldn’t sleep, so I did what everyone does at 4AM when their brain won’t shut up.

I started digging.

Not casually browsing. I mean digging.

Old forum archives. Dead video hosting sites. Spanish message boards that hadn’t been updated in fifteen years. I was deep in the kind of places where broken links outnumber working ones and half the usernames look abandoned.

I was specifically looking for old footage of Mexico City from the 80s or early 90s.

That era is weirdly hard to find. Not highlights. Not documentaries. Just raw, normal footage of neighborhoods. Streets. Corners. Everyday life.

My grandma’s house is in an older part of the city, and lately I’ve been thinking about what it must’ve looked like when my dad and his sister were teenagers. Before the pollution darkened the buildings. Before everything aged.

So I kept searching.

At 4:07AM — I remember because I glanced at the clock in the corner of my screen — I found something buried in a forgotten thread.

The title was simple.

“Mexico City Night Broadcast - 1984.”

No description. No comments. The thumbnail looked like someone had recorded a television screen with a phone years ago.

I clicked it.

It wasn’t a short clip.

It was an entire broadcast recording.

A reporter in an oversized blazer and wide tie was walking through different parts of the city at night. The image had that old magnetic softness. Reds bled into shadows. Streetlights flared slightly. There was a low hum under the audio like tape friction.

He walked slowly. No cuts. No edits.

It felt authentic.

I let it play.

Minutes passed.

Then more.

He moved from one area to another. Busy streets. Quiet residential blocks. Eventually he approached the historic center near my grandma’s house.

Fireworks were going off in the distance.

The plaza came into view.

Families walking. Vendors. Kids running.

It didn’t feel staged. It felt lived in.

Then he turned down a side street.

My heart started pounding.

That was my grandma’s street.

The camera continued forward.

And there it was.

My building.

Except it didn’t look like it does now.

The white cement exterior was bright. Clean. No pollution stains. The edges looked sharp. The terrace railing didn’t have the slight rust it has today.

And above it, the upstairs window.

My aunt’s old room.

Thick curtains moved gently inside.

Warm light glowed behind them.

That room in real life feels almost hollow now. The floor slopes slightly. There’s termite dust along the baseboards. An old wardrobe still stands, but everything feels abandoned.

Seeing it alive like that made my chest tighten.

The camera slowed.

The reporter’s voice became faint.

Then it disappeared entirely.

The camera kept moving.

And something shifted.

The movement stopped feeling like it belonged to the cameraman.

It felt like it belonged to me.

You know how when you’re on Google Street View trying to find your own house, it feels familiar but off? Or when you’re driving through a version of your neighborhood in GTA and everything is almost right but not quite?

That’s what this felt like.

Except I could feel my body turning when the camera turned.

I could feel myself walking when it moved forward.

I didn’t panic.

I didn’t even question how.

I thought, I’m back.

Somehow.

And instead of fear, I felt calm.

Comfortable.

Like I had been allowed somewhere I wasn’t supposed to go, but gently.

Almost like I was being guided.

I walked down the street.

I overshot the house at first and ended up near the back of the plaza. Fireworks exploded overhead, filling the sky with red and green flashes. The air felt warm. The entire city had that soft 80s glow over it, like reality itself was slightly filtered.

I turned around.

Found the right gate.

It was open.

I stepped inside.

The living room looked mostly the same structurally, but it felt alive. Lights on. Air warm. No heaviness.

I didn’t check my dad’s old room.

I went upstairs.

Her door was open.

The room looked exactly like it did from the street.

Clean. Bed made. Desk organized. Wardrobe upright and intact.

It felt lived in.

I walked toward the bathroom.

In real life it’s small.

Here it wasn’t.

It was larger. Clean tile. A bathtub. A sink with her toothbrush, hairbrush, and a small portable radio sitting casually.

There was a small box on the counter.

I opened it.

Inside were old watches. Small gifts. Personal items.

For a second, I wanted to take one.

It felt like proof I was really there.

But something inside me said not to touch anything.

So I closed it.

That’s when I saw her.

My aunt was in the tub.

Unconscious.

Her face red and bruised.

Another girl was kneeling beside her.

I didn’t recognize her.

She looked at me immediately.

“Did you do this?”

“No.”

She stood quickly and covered my mouth.

“Do you have earplugs in?”

The question confused me.

“No.”

“Then whose room is this?”

I knew the answer.

I know her name.

But what came out of my mouth wasn’t it.

I said the wrong name.

The girl’s expression changed instantly.

Like something had confirmed her suspicion.

I tried to correct myself.

I couldn’t remember my aunt’s name.

It was blank.

Behind her, through the open bathroom door, something moved.

From behind the bed.

At first I thought it was just shadow.

Then it unfolded.

It wasn’t just “big.”

It was wrong.

Too many legs. Long, jointed limbs that bent at angles that didn’t look natural. Its body was low and wide, like it distributed its weight across the floor. Its movement wasn’t chaotic. It was precise.

It didn’t hesitate.

It sprinted.

The sound of its limbs hitting the floor wasn’t loud, but it was fast. Rapid. Controlled.

I tried to yell.

Her hand was still over my mouth.

The sound came out muffled.

I panicked and dropped to my knees, slamming my palms against the tile floor as hard as I could.

The impact hurt.

I kept hitting the ground, trying to create noise.

Trying to signal her.

I didn’t know she already knew.

I thought she hadn’t seen it yet.

I needed her to turn around.

I needed her to understand something was coming.

The creature was ten feet away.

Eight.

Six.

She turned her head calmly.

Lifted her hands.

Everything snapped.

I was back at the gate.

Reset.

It took me a few seconds to understand.

I went upstairs again.

Bathroom.

Same question.

Same wrong name.

Same creature.

Reset.

On the third loop I understood the timing.

About ninety seconds from entry to impact.

And something else clicked.

The reason I said the wrong name wasn’t random.

It felt like there were two versions of me.

The one standing in that house.

And the one who knew exactly what was happening.

The real me knew her name.

But the version inside that timeline wasn’t fully synced.

It was like signal interference.

When she asked the question, the part of me with full memory wasn’t reaching the part of me speaking.

The loop wasn’t just survival.

It was me trying to sync the two selves.

On the fourth loop, I said her name immediately.

Before she asked.

She froze.

The creature still came.

I hit the floor again, palms stinging, trying to warn her.

Muffled yelling.

Desperation.

She turned before I finished.

Reset.

On the fifth loop, she didn’t cover my mouth.

She looked at me differently.

“You remember,” she said quietly.

“Yes.”

The creature hadn’t appeared yet.

She grabbed my wrist.

The room blurred instead of snapping back.

We were still in the bedroom, but the walls looked thinner. Less solid.

“How did you find it?” she asked.

“The broadcast.”

Her face changed.

“That’s how I got here too.”

She told me she had found the same video years ago.

Same late-night search.

Same deep dive for rare footage from the 80s.

She kept watching until the perspective locked.

Except she never left.

Every time someone finds it and watches long enough, they get pulled in.

Most don’t remember between resets.

They panic. They fail the question. The creature reaches them.

She watches them disappear.

She thought I was another one.

The reason she asked about earplugs was because sometimes people inside the loop act like they’re not fully present.

Like something is blocking their awareness.

The creature appears when the system detects someone who doesn’t belong.

The name question exposes you.

If you hesitate, you’re flagged.

If you answer wrong, you’re flagged faster.

She’s learned how to push against parts of it.

That’s how she teleports us before impact.

But she can’t leave.

“You’re not anchored here yet,” she told me.

“You’ll go back.”

“And you?”

She didn’t answer.

Something shifted in the hallway.

Not rushing.

Waiting.

“If you ever see it again,” she said, “don’t let it think you want something from here.”

The room flickered.

The fireworks grew louder.

Everything went dark.

I was back at my desk.

Monitor still glowing.

The video open.

Except it wasn’t.

Just static.

I refreshed the page.

Dead link.

Gone.

I haven’t tried to search for it again.

But I still remember the warmth of that upstairs window.

And I still wonder how many other people are out there at 4:07AM, digging through the darkest corners of the internet, trying to find something rare.

And accidentally finding it.
