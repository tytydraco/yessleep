“The Three Hills Mall, located near downtown, closed a month ago. The last tenant to move out was a nail salon. The owners of the salon insisted they could stay afloat in an empty mall due to their loyal client base. But the mall’s many problems, dirty water leaks, heat and A/C failures, insect infestations, finally convinced them it was time to go.”

I flipped my phone camera around to record the entrance of the mall. The new moon darkness felt positively alive in contrast to the visible finality of the black beyond those glass doors.

I glanced around for security. The stagnant shadows in the parking lot showed no lurking vehicles other than my own hatchback, parked in a far corner.

“Time to go inside,” I said to my phone. I approached the entrance, a little unnerved by the viscous silence of the place.

I grasped the door handle and pulled, the unoiled hinges screeching at me. I winced and whipped my head around, again on the alert for security.

Turning back to the mall, I marveled at how thick the air felt. I smelled sweet rot, the kind you get when you’re near something that recently died, like the possum that had met its end in my garage.

Finally, I breached the amorphous barrier between inside and outside, and I raised my phone up to record. Of course, it wouldn’t see much in this darkness, but when I felt safe, I’d turn on the flashlight.

Damp carpet under my feet, and ahead of me, the old mall directory cube. I focused my phone on it, risking a little bit of flashlight, hoping to capture the last time the guide had been updated. It still showed a few stores active – a GNC, the nail salon, a photography studio, and the JC Penney, still labeled.

That rot smell really pierced through now, and I wondered how many critters had met their final moments as the mall did the same. I grabbed the Vicks out of my pocket and rubbed some under my nose – this wasn’t my first rodeo in an abandoned mall, after all. I also donned a surgical mask in case of toxic mold. That helped.

I stopped and listened for footsteps, walkie-talkies, anything that might indicate the presence of security. Dripping, creaking, settling sounds, but nothing threatening.

Walking along the corridor, my sneakers sinking into the carpet with each step, I pointed my phone flashlight at the empty storefronts. Each held a reaching darkness, and every now and then I saw fingers, hands, beckoning me forward, the trickery of the shadow and light play from my flashlight.

And now I noticed the walls, just how much muck and mold and residue ran down the surfaces of each one. Every instinct told me to stay the hell away from whatever that was – sewage, mold, dirty water, rivers of disease and poison.

I smelled something new. Beyond the rot. Something fresher but no less indicative of death. A coppery tang that cut through the Vicks and the surgical mask, coming from the rivulets of muck that painted the walls. Curiosity got the better of me. I donned surgical gloves and approached one wall. Sliding my finger along the muck, it felt warm, thick, and when I looked at my glove, a glutinous red coated two of the fingers. Rubbing them together, the fluid smeared like syrup.

And that was when I really looked at the wall, saw the cracks, the ragged wounds in it, and I stumbled backwards, the rot and copper stench overwhelming me. I tore off the surgical mask and retched, soaking the wet floor in bile.

“What…the fuck…” I managed to gasp out when I’d recovered.

I stood back, my mask again firmly attached to my face, and, with shaking fingers, pointed my flashlight back at the wall.

My mind screamed at what I saw. The wound. The wall had split along a seam, the edges black and glistening with fluid, and inside that hole I saw not wood beams or insulation, but meat. The gray, sagging surface of undefined entrails, slick with blood, and beyond them, the unmistakable glint of bone, a spine, a ribcage.

I retched again, barely getting my mask off in time, and stood there with hands on knees, shaking.

And then I saw the floor. Really looked at it. What I’d thought was wet carpet, I now saw for what it was. A mass of limp skin, bruised, fragments of carpet stuck to it like hair. It extended the length of the corridor. As I stepped, some of the flesh tore, and black fluid poured forth from new wounds, sharp and acrid.

I screamed and stumbled backwards, running toward the entrance of the mall, which I now saw as a distended jaw, the flesh and drywall dripping off it, slick bone beneath.

And then I made it out. Out of the dead mall. And I retched again. I recovered, steadied my mind and my breathing as much as I could.

I studied the outside walls, how normal they looked, the stucco nearly unblemished, but as I looked closer, I saw the cracks, and in those cracks I saw red.

I returned my gaze to the glass doors, and now I couldn’t help but see those teeth, that jaw, that skeleton, lurking there in the darkness. I shivered.

Did the mall die when the last tenant left? Or had it died a long time before?

Was I the only one to see its corpse?

I looked at the footage on my phone, my gorge rising once again.

I couldn’t show this to anyone. Nobody needed to see this. Nobody needed to come here.

I erased the footage and stumbled, shaky, sobbing a little, back to my car.
