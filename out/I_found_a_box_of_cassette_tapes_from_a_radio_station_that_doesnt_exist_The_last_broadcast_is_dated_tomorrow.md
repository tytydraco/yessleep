I collect dead media. It’s a hobby that started cheap and got expensive, but the thrill of the hunt keeps me going. Estate sales, flea markets, the back rooms of dusty antique shops if it’s recorded on magnetic tape and smells like mildew, I probably want it.

Most of what I find is junk. Home recordings of birthday parties where no one speaks, mixtapes from bad high school breakups, hours of silence interrupted by sudden, deafening static. But sometimes, you find a ghost.

Last weekend, I hit an estate sale in a coastal town about three hours north of here. The previous owner had been a ham radio operator; his shack was a goldmine of vintage equipment, most of it stripped for parts. I dug through a cardboard box under a workbench, pushed aside tangled cables and rusted connectors, and found a stack of Maxell XLII 90s. High-quality chrome tape. The kind you used when you wanted something to last.

They were labeled in neat, architectural handwriting.

*WYAL-FM 103.3 - Aircheck 10/12/99* *WYAL-FM 103.3 - Aircheck 10/19/99* *WYAL-FM 103.3 - The Hollow Hour 10/26/99*

And at the bottom of the stack, one tape simply labeled: *The Last Broadcast - 10/31/99*

I didn’t recognize the call letters. "The Whale" sounds familiar enough for a coastal station, but 103.3 in that area is dead air, or at least it has been as long as I’ve been scanning frequencies. I bought the whole box for five dollars.

I finally got around to listening to them last night. I started with the October 12th tape, expecting the usual: classic rock blocks, local car dealership ads, maybe a weather report.

What I heard was… wrong.

The audio quality is crystal clear. Too clear for a twenty-five-year-old cassette. There’s no hiss, no wobble. It sounds like it was recorded yesterday directly from the soundboard.

The DJ he introduces himself as Vince Hollow has this smooth, deep voice. The kind that’s meant to keep you calm while you’re driving alone on a dark highway. But he sounds exhausted. There’s a strain in his voice, a tightness that doesn’t fit the "late-night jazz and chat" vibe of the show.

It starts normal. He takes a call from a woman named Sarah. She sounds terrified. She talks about seeing a man in a lighthouse who has no face, just smooth skin where his eyes should be. She says he was "conducting" the ocean like an orchestra.

Vince tries to calm her down. He tells her he’s the only one in the lighthouse. He says he locks the tower himself.

Then the line goes dead. And Vince… he forgets he’s on the air. Or maybe he doesn’t care.

"I’m alone here," he whispers. You can hear the chair creak. "I’m always alone."

Then, faintly, just under the noise floor of the recording, there’s a sound. Wet, rhythmic breathing. It’s not coming from Vince. It sounds like it’s coming from *behind* the microphone.

I ejected the tape. I told myself it was just a weird art project, some college radio drama that never aired. I googled "WYAL-FM". Nothing. I googled "Vince Hollow radio host". Nothing. No Wikipedia entry, no fan archives, no FCC license history for a station with those call letters in that frequency range for that year.

But the tapes are sitting right here on my desk.

I put the second tape in. October 19th. The vibe is darker. Vince sounds like he hasn’t slept in days. He takes a call from an older guy named Tom, a fisherman. Tom talks about a woman walking on the water in the fog. He says she smiled too wide, showed too many teeth.

"It’s almost time," Tom says she told him.

"Almost time for what?" Vince asks. He sounds genuinely afraid.

"That’s your problem now, Vince," Tom says. "You’re the one in the lighthouse."

I listened to three more tapes. Each one is worse. The callers aren’t just telling scary stories; they’re confessing. They’re reporting things that shouldn’t be possible. A diver finding a city underwater where the people in the photos look back at him. A priest talking about hundreds of thousands of handprints on the ocean floor, all pointing toward the lighthouse.

Vince is deteriorating with every recording. He starts losing time. He forgets his own name. He talks to people who aren't there.

But it was the last tape that made me leave my house.

I put in the one labeled *The Last Broadcast - 10/31/99*.

It starts with static. Heavy, rhythmic static that sounds like waves crashing. Then Vince’s voice cuts through. He sounds… peaceful. Resigned.

"We think we’re so alone," he says. "Sitting in the dark, listening to voices on the radio, trying to connect. But we’re not alone. We never were. Something has always been listening. Always recording."

He says he’s going upstairs to the transmitter room. To see what’s been using his voice. He says goodbye. "I’ll see you on the other side."

There’s the sound of footsteps on metal stairs. Then a door opens.

The sound that follows isn’t something I can describe easily. It’s a mix of mechanical humming and… squelching. Like walking through mud. There are voices, hundreds of them, whispering over each other. I could pick out fragments. "Broadcast." "Forever." "You were always here."

Then a door slams. Vince screams. He begs to be let out. He screams until his voice gives out.

Then silence.

And then… the broadcast starts again.

"You’re listening to WYAL-FM, 103.3. The Hollow Hour. I’m Vince Hollow."

But it’s not him. The voice is too smooth. Too perfect. There are no breaths between the words.

"Thank you for listening. We’ll be here. We’re always here."

I stopped the tape. My hands were shaking. I looked at the label again. *10/31/99*. Halloween.

But then I saw it. written in pencil, faintly, right under the date.

*Re-broadcast schedule: \[Today's Date+1\]*

I froze. I live three hours away from where I found these tapes. But I have a radio.

I turned it on. I scrolled the dial to 103.3.

Static. Just static.

I left it on while I tried to research the location of this supposed lighthouse. I found an old maritime map from the 50s that shows a structure on a jagged point of rock called "Hollow’s Point," but it’s marked as condemned/abandoned.

Then, about ten minutes ago, the static on my radio changed.

It’s not white noise anymore. It’s rhythmic. Like waves. Or breathing.

And a moment ago, just before I started typing this, a voice cut through.

"Eleanor, you’re listening very carefully, aren’t you? You should go check your back door."

My name isn’t Eleanor. But my neighbor’s name is. She’s seventy years old and lives alone.

I’m looking out my window at her house right now. Her back porch light just turned on.

Her back door is open.

And I can hear music coming from my radio. A faint, distorted jingle.

*You’re listening to The Whale…*

I don’t know what I brought home. I don’t know what’s on these tapes. But I think the broadcast isn’t done. I think it’s just catching up.

If you’re near the coast… if you see 103.3 on your dial… don’t listen.

Please, don’t listen.
