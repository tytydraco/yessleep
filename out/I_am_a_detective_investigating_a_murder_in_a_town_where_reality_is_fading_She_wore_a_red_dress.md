The world is monochrome. It always has been.

Every morning I open my eyes, and all I see is ash. The sky is dull, the asphalt is wet, and the faces of the people in this town look like they were sketched with a blunt gray pencil. I hated it. I hated the routine. I hated the absolute boredom of my own existence.

Until three days ago.

I found her in the deep, gray woods just outside of town. The mist hung low between the pine trees, but she pierced right through it. She was lying on the damp moss, dead. But she wasn't gray. She wore a silk, blood-red dress. A violent splash of color in a world of dust. A perfect femme fatale.

I knelt beside her. I didn’t care who killed her. I only cared about how beautiful she looked against the dead grass.

I took her green slime toy from the dashboard of my car. I don't know why, but I placed it on her chest. As the old radio in my car played dull jazz through the open doors, something impossible happened. The green plastic began to contract and expand. In and out. In and out. In perfect sync with a breath she no longer had.

Since that moment, the case stopped mattering. The obsession began.

I started seeing triangles everywhere. In the cracks of the pavement, in the way three crows sat on a wire, in the number of stairs leading to her apartment. Thirteen steps. Thirteen hours on my watch that never seemed to move forward.

Yesterday, I lost my mind completely. I went to a cheap, dimly lit motel. The room smelled of old smoke and rain. I paid a local prostitute to put on a replica of that red dress. I made her stand by the window, under the flickering neon light. I needed to see it again. I needed to feel it.

When I put my hands around her neck, I wasn't trying to kill her. I was trying to recreate the crime. I was trying to freeze the frame. To capture the exact physics of how that red silk folded when life left her body. The girl choked, coughing, but I didn't see her. I saw the image. The illusion.

His voice started to fade out. It sank low, turning into an unrecognizable, underwater hum. I didn't hear him anymore. I didn't want his reality to ruin my illusion.

A single tear rolled down my cheek, leaving a wet, shining track in the blue light. My face remained completely frozen. I stared directly ahead, past him, past the trees, straight into the empty space.

With a dead, hollow voice, I interrupted him:

"Describe her details at that moment. What color was the thread sticking out of her left sleeve?"
