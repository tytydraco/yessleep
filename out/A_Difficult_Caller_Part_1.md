You know that feeling when it's dark outside and you’re looking out the window into the pitch black night, and you suddenly realise that anyone could be looking right at you and you’d have no idea? I’ve been feeling like that a lot lately.



Four nights a week, I work the graveyard shift at the Samaritans call centre talking to people in crisis or who just need to talk to another human being. I haven’t really been able to sleep at night since my son Joey died, so I thought I’d put my time to use and volunteer at the call centre. I’m going to be awake anyway, so I might as well be helping someone if I can – I’ve always been told I’m a good listener. I’ve been doing this job for years and nothing has phased me.



But this time it’s different. I just don’t know what to do.



You never know what type of call you’re going to get – whether you’re talking someone off the ledge or just being someone who will listen. I try not to bring my work home with me as it won’t do me any good fretting about what happens to these people after they hang up the phone. It normally works, until now. She just won’t stop calling.



She rang for the first time a few weeks ago, just after 3am. I didn’t think anything of the call, it was pretty standard really. She said she had been struggling to sleep recently, and she just wanted to hear someone else’s voice. I can relate to that – I know how lonely it can get at night. All the volunteers follow a handbook: tell the caller your real name, keep the connection real, engage with them, don’t share personal information about yourself, put all your focus on helping them. I suggested some activities she could do to form connections with people in her community (we have lists of different organisations for each area to provide to callers). She said she was interested in the sewing classes and ladies acrobatics group. I initially thought it was funny she picked those ones from the list - they’re the exact same hobbies I do - but now I'm not so sure.



All through the call, I could hear this low-level hissing sound in the background. It was sort of electronic, like a tape recorder rewinding. It’s hard to describe, but it was really unsettling, although the sound would stop every time she spoke. I brushed it off and we ended the call after about half an hour – she said she felt much better after being able to talk to someone. That call concluded my shift, so I logged off and headed home just as the sun was rising.



She called again at the start of my next shift. I’ve been working there for years and I’ve never been assigned the same caller twice. The call was pretty similar to the last one, she just needed to someone to talk to. She sounded a little different this time – like her accent had changed a bit? It was eerily similar to my own. I only noticed because she pronounced the name of a local shop the exact same way I do – Joey always used to tease me about how I was mispronouncing it - I’ve never heard anyone else say it the same way as me. That hissing sound was in the background the whole time again.



The next call was when it started to scare me. Her calls being connected to me twice was surely just a coincidence, but three times? What are the chances of that?



As the call went on, the hissing sound continued. We spoke, and I reassured her that it was perfectly normal to feel lonely sometimes. What she said next is the reason why I’m writing this.



‘You must be lonely without Joey.’



This stopped me dead in my tracks. How the *hell* did she know about Joey? I spluttered out an excuse that I had to go and hung up the call.



I *know* I didn’t mention his name; I wouldn’t have. I couldn’t have. I never say his name out loud, it's too hard say his name, to acknowledge that he’ll never be able to respond.



I can’t stop thinking about it. I’m not sure if this post even makes any sense or if I’m just obsessing over nothing but I can't help but feel that something just doesn’t add up. *How* did she know his name?



I haven’t been back to the call centre since; I don’t know what I’d do if she called back.
