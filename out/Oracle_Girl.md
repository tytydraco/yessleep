I’m thirty years old, getting married in six weeks, and last night Mary my fiancee asked me if I had secrets.

You know, normal couple stuff. Lying in bed. Her head on my chest.

She knew how to get to me. Said I made her feel important, said I was always there for her, all that stuff.

I kissed her forehead.

Then she asked about the secrets again.

She said that was how people got close. How you really knew someone.

I told her I didn’t have any.

She laughed. “Everyone has secrets.”

So yeah.

Here’s mine.

Once I tell you, you’ll understand why I can’t tell Mary.

Part of me is still back there, you know? Sixteen years old, stupid, being with Leena like she was the only real thing in the whole town.

First love isn’t something you just walk away from, you know.

Leena was eighteen, almost exactly two years older.                                                                            

Back then she had black hair, huge brown eyes, and this way of looking at you that calmed you down.

Her crappy dad took off when she was little. Left her and her mom, Nora, with nothing. Took the money, savings, even the damn car.

What got to me was how everyone always acted like something was wrong with her. Maybe because she always looked sad and tired.

Then the dreams began and it got worse, people started calling her Oracle Girl. They said she wasn’t right in the head. Me, I knew she was just a scared girl who wanted everyone to leave her alone.

It started when she had this dream about Mr. Jacobs, her shit boss at the supermarket. She said it felt like he was going to die or something.

I still remember how she told me that in the evening, when we were drinking gas-station beers by the water at Skorrs Pond. She said it didn’t feel like a normal dream. It felt real-real. Like she’d been there. Like she’d seen it happen.

She said it woke her up sweating and terrified.

I didn’t pay much attention to it at the time. I just liked how warm and good it felt when she put her head in my lap and told me about it. That was one of the first times I ever felt like that with a girl.

And then it happened. Really happened.

They said he died in his sleep.

Then the birds came. Right before dawn, a whole mess of blackbirds showed up on the roof of the Jacobs family house. Just sat there, people said. Quiet. Like they were waiting or something.

Rumor was, they stayed until his wife found him and screamed.

Then came the dreams about Mr. Kran, the science teacher, Ed Barnes, Dina from the pharmacy, Jenny, the server from Spark’s downtown, and then Old Gordon.

All of them died the next day, blackbirds on the roof and all.

She hated being feared, but you could tell it made her feel powerful too. But when Old Gordon died something in the town shifted. I could feel it, so I went to Leena’s house.

“Can you stay tonight?” Nora said to me. “In the spare room. Door open, whatever you want. I just need someone we trust in the house.”

Leena stood barefoot in the hallway, arms wrapped around herself. She didn’t ask me to stay.

So I said yes.

Nora put me in the little room by the stairs. It smelled like dust and lavender candles.

I lay there listening to the house settle, pipes ticking, Nora checking locks, Leena sighing down the hall. I wanted to go to her. I didn’t.

I probably fell asleep.

Then Leena was above me, soaked in sweat, hair stuck to her face.

“You were in it,” she hissed.

I sat up too fast. “What?”

“My dream.”

Nora stood behind her, pale and shaking.

Leena’s mouth trembled. “You were dying. You were on your bedroom floor. And you looked so scared.”

I didn’t know what to say. My throat just closed up on me.

Then we heard it.

A soft thump above us.

Then another.

Then the roof filled with fluttering.

Leena covered her mouth.

Nora stared at the ceiling and whispered, “Is… is it the blackbirds?”

Leena grabbed my wrist hard enough to hurt.

“Run.”

We went out the back, barefoot through Nora’s dead tomato plants, over the fence, into the road. The roof behind us sounded alive.

We didn’t stop until Skorrs Pond.

She bent over, gasping, losing it, still holding my sleeve like I might vanish.

“I won’t let it happen,” she said. “I saw you die.”

“I know.”

“You don’t know.”

Two blackbirds came out of nowhere and landed on a branch right beside us.

Leena saw them and went white.

“No,” she said. “No, no, no.”

She grabbed my hand and tried to pull me farther down the bank, but I didn’t move.

I guess I couldn’t take it anymore, so I just reached into my pocket.

I took out the little bag of special grains and shook some onto the sand.

Both birds dropped immediately.

Then more came.

Leena stared at them.

Then at me.

“What is that?”

I wanted to say something smart. Something that would make it sound less horrible.

But I was sixteen, and stupid, and in love.

So I just smiled at her.

Not proud.

Her face stayed confused for maybe two seconds.

Then it changed.

“You?” she whispered.

I nodded.

“You don’t know what they said about you, Leena. I just wanted them to see you.”

She looked at me like I’d ruined something. Then she ran off into the dark. That was it.

Never saw her again.

So yeah. That’s my little secret.

That’s just how I am, I guess. Love takes work.

Mary’s promotion wasn’t luck, let’s put it that way.

But I learned from Leena. I don’t make it so messy anymore.

Shit, I forgot. We’re almost out of grains again.
