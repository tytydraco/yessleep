I’m sitting in a diner as I write this, about two hours out from Black Rock—a rural mountain buried deep in the Wilderness. Somewhere along the last decade, I guess I became a city boy. After what happened to me as a kid, beeping cars and wailing sirens are comforting in a way untouched forests never will be. Things lurk where man doesn’t build his skyscrapers.

I was raised by my father in a small cabin about half way up Black Rock. He was a writer, and when my mother died, he went a bit mad and decided we would live “off the grid.” When I was young, he hand-built a cottage deep in the Idlewood Wilderness. To reach it, we had to creep miles into the woods by jeep along routes my father insisted remain clandestine. 

The cabin sat in the shadow of a massive limestone formation that looked black when it swallowed the sun. We rarely went into towns—let alone cities. Occasionally, my father traveled to a metropolis for a publisher meeting or a book signing. But there was one time each year when we always left the cabin on purpose. 

Every year, he prepared at least a week in advance. He packed an overnight bag and told me, “We’re going on a vacation.” After a few years, I realized that eating junk food in a roadside motel wasn’t exactly a luxury getaway, so he rebranded it as our *tradition*.

Then one year, everything changed.

I was twelve. A blizzard came out of nowhere—no forecast, no warning. There was no chance of getting down the mountain. I’d never seen my father so disheveled.

“Grab that plywood,” he yelled. “We need to board the windows.”

We fortified the cabin until no light could penetrate our little home. Once our Fort Knox was finished, we waited.

The grandfather clock struck 4:30. Outside, a violent wind rose.

“Put out the fire,” my father said. “We don’t want to risk any light escaping.”

We sat on the floor, wrapped in blankets, the cabin shuddering around us. Every time I asked what was happening, he squeezed me tighter and repeated the same words:

“I’ll explain when you’re older.”

Hours passed. Then I heard it—the drums. Deep percussion. Each strike vibrated the floor beneath us. In the dimness, I saw my father go pale.

Then came the voices. Hundreds of them, chanting in unison. At first the parade was faint, but it grew louder with every minute until it was upon us. I heard their footsteps crunching through the snow, each step in perfect military cadence. The voices were deep and resonant, as though their diaphragms had been carved by the earth itself.

My father pulled me closer than he ever had before. There was nothing to do but wait.

Suddenly, everything stopped. The parade fell silent. The world went dead.

We listened.

Then a single voice spoke from the peak of the mountain—deep, commanding, spoken in a language unlike anything I had ever heard. As it spoke, a vibration rolled down from the summit. Something large. Otherworldly. A creature of Goliath proportion.

A dialogue followed between the leader and the crowd below. It reminded me of a litany you’d hear during a Catholic mass. All the while, the vibration shook the ground around us. 

Then, the drums returned. The chanting swelled. Instruments joined the cacophony—sounds unlike anything I had ever known. I remember thinking they might have been woodwinds or brass, but fashioned from the limestone buried beneath the mountain itself.

And then, cutting through it all, I heard something familiar.

A human scream.

It was a young woman, screaming from the mountaintop. Pure terror—like she was witnessing something no human mind was meant to comprehend. The chanting and instruments accelerated as her shrieks rose in pitch and desperation.

Louder. Faster. More intense.

Then behemoth bellowed and the earth seemed to twist. The vibration swallowed her cries, as though her very essence were being torn away.

Silence returned. This silence lasted for far too long, until the silence was cut with a cackle. 

The creatures outside shrieked and laughed. The instruments resumed, and some kind of diabolical celebration erupted all around the mountain side. They danced to the grotesque music. They shrieked. They laughed and danced through the night. 

My father and I clung to each other, praying that whatever was outside would not tear through the walls of our hand-built home.

After what felt like an eternity, the drums slowed. The parade resumed its perfect cadence. The sound faded with agonizing slowness until only a faint echo of chanting remained.

Finally—silence. Heavy and complete.

We left the cabin several days later, once the snow cleared.

By spring, I was enrolled in a boarding school in Pennsylvania. I barely spoke to my father after that. I spent my vacations with my wealthy grandparents and swore I would never return to that mountain.

Now it’s been twenty years.

My father is dead. The cabin is mine. And though part of me wants to burn that place to the ground, curiosity has finally outweighed fear.

The waitress just dropped off my check. I think I’ll ask for one more refill of my cup of coffee before heading into the mountains. My father’s “tradition” date is only a few days away. If you're reading this, I am not sure what will have happened to me. 

Whatever that parade was—the one that has haunted me since I was twelve—I need to see it again.
