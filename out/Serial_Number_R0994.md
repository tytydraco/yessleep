I’ve never posted on a forum like this before. Usually, men in my profession—"contractors," if you want to be polite, "mercenaries" if you don’t—keep our mouths shut.

 But it’s 3:00 AM in a rain-slicked flat in Bucharest, and if I don't tell someone what happened in the Carpathians, I think my mind will finally fracture.
My name is Stefan. I spent twelve years in the Romanian Land Forces before the private sector started calling. The money is better, but the soul-debt is heavier.

Last October, I was hired by a private energy conglomerate to "secure" an abandoned mining facility near the Transylvanian border. The official story? Eco-terrorists were sabotaging the equipment. The real story? The company had found something in the deep shafts that they didn't want the government to see.

We were a team of four. There was Lukas, a massive Swede who could carry a light machine gun like it was a toy; Mateo, a silent Spaniard with eyes like flint; and Elena, our tech specialist from Serbia.

The facility, Mina de Fier, was a jagged scar on the side of a fog-choked mountain. From the moment we hiked in, the air felt... wrong. It wasn't just cold; it was heavy, like the atmosphere was trying to push the breath back down your throat.

"I don't like the silence," Lukas grunted on the third night. He was right. There were no birds, no crickets. Just the low hum of the wind through the pines.

On the fifth night, we heard it. A sound like wet leather slapping against stone. It came from the main elevator shaft—the one that went down three kilometers into the tectonic shelf.
We moved in formation. Mateo had the lead with the thermal scope. He stopped dead.

"Stefan," he whispered. "Look at the heat signatures."

I looked through my own NVGs. There were dozens of them. But they weren't human. They were spindly, elongated shapes clinging to the ceiling of the shaft. Their body temperatures were barely a degree above the ambient rock. They looked like starved children, but with limbs that were far too long and eyes that reflected our tactical lights like polished silver.

Then they screamed. It wasn't a vocal cord sound; it was a psychic tear, a screech that felt like a needle behind the eyeballs.

I’m not proud of what happened next. We didn't fight like soldiers. We fought like cornered animals.

Lukas went down first. One of those things dropped from the dark and tore through his tactical vest like it was wet paper. They didn't just bite; they latched. They were parasites, draining the life out of him so fast his skin turned gray before he even hit the floor.

Elena and Mateo didn't make it to the extraction point. I did. I used a pressurized thermobaric charge to seal the tunnel entrance behind me. I told the company they were lost in a cave-in. I took my blood money and I ran.

I've spent the last six months looking over my shoulder. I see shadows in the corners of my eyes. I hear that wet leather sound in my dreams. I thought I was the survivor. I thought I was the lucky one who got away from the vampires under the mountain.

But here is why I’m writing this. Here is the part that is making my hands shake so hard I can barely type.

The company sent a medical team to "debrief" me after the incident. They gave me a full physical, took blood samples, and told me I was clean. Just a bit of PTSD, they said.

Tonight, I was looking at the photos Elena had uploaded to our private cloud server just minutes before the attack. She had managed to snap a high-resolution photo of one of those creatures up close. 

I zoomed in on its face—the pale, translucent skin, the needle-teeth, the silver eyes.
And then I saw the mark on its shoulder.
It wasn't a natural growth. It was a faded, blue-ink tattoo. 

A serial number. R-0994.

I stood up and went to my bathroom mirror. I stripped off my shirt and turned around, holding a hand mirror to see my own back. I looked at the small, surgical scar I’ve had since my military discharge physical three years ago.

I took a scalpel from my first-aid kit. I didn't feel any pain as I sliced into the scar tissue—I haven't felt much of anything lately. I reached in with tweezers and pulled out a small, encrypted microchip.

I realized then: the "eco-terrorists" weren't the target. We weren't the guards.

The things in that mine weren't ancient monsters. They were us. The company wasn't mining ore; they were "recycling" soldiers of fortune, using a mutagenic strain to create something that could survive the coming wars without food, water, or sunlight.

I’m not "looking over my shoulder" because they are hunting me. I’m looking over my shoulder because my skin is starting to itch. My reflection in the mirror is getting paler every day. And tonight, for the first time... I noticed that my tea tasted like dust, but the cut on my finger looked absolutely delicious.

I'm not a survivor. I'm just the next version.
