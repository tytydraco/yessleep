My name is Tom Reyes. I've been fishing these waters for thirty years, longer than some of you reading this have been alive. I'm posting this because I need to know I'm not insane, and because if something happens to me, someone needs to know what I saw.

This is going to sound impossible. I know that. But I need you to understand something first: I am not the kind of man who sees things. I don't drink. I don't do drugs. I've never had a hallucination in my life. I'm the guy other fishermen call when their equipment breaks down, because I can fix anything with an engine. I'm practical. Rational. Boring, according to my daughter.

Or at least, I was.

October 23rd, 2024, The Day It Happened

It was a Tuesday. I remember because Tuesday is when I run my maintenance routes checking lobster pots, inspecting the long-lines, the kind of routine work I could do in my sleep. I've been running the *Saint Catherine* out of Mercy Harbor since 1994. Thirty-foot commercial fishing vessel, diesel engine I rebuilt myself in '09, every gauge and switch exactly where it should be.

The forecast called for clear skies. Light wind. Ideal conditions.

I left the dock at 5:47 AM. I know the exact time because I always log my departures Coast Guard requirement, and I'm meticulous about paperwork. My wife Marie jokes that I'm the only fisherman she knows who color-codes his maintenance schedules.

The water was glass-calm. I could see the lighthouse from six miles out, its white paint bright against the morning sky. WYAL-FM broadcasts from there the old lighthouse station. I had it on low in the cabin, just for company. Some late-night DJ wrapping up his shift, playing old Johnny Cash.

At 7:23 AM, I was three miles offshore, checking my first string of pots. That's when the fog came.

I've seen fog before. I've navigated through soup so thick I couldn't see the bow of my own boat. But this was different. It didn't roll in it *appeared*. One moment I could see clear to the horizon, the next I was wrapped in gray so dense my spotlight couldn't penetrate more than a few feet.

And then my engine died.

No warning. No cough or sputter. The Saint Catherine's diesel engine has 47,000 hours on it and has never quit without reason. I know that engine like I know my own heartbeat. Every rattle, every vibration means something.

This was just... silence.

I went through my checklist. Fuel: full. Battery: charged. Oil pressure: normal. Starter: nothing. Not even a click when I turned the key. The electrical was working my instruments were still on but the engine was dead as a stone.

That's when I heard the singing.

A woman's voice. Crystal clear through the fog. Beautiful in a way that made my chest hurt. She was singing in a language I didn't recognize not Spanish, not Portuguese, not the French Canadian some of the old-timers speak. Something older. The melody was simple, almost like a lullaby, but it had this quality that made me think of deep water. Of shipwrecks. Of things that should stay buried.

I'm not a superstitious man, but every instinct I had screamed at me to stay quiet.

I called out anyway. "Hello? Is someone out there? Do you need help?"

The singing stopped.

The silence that followed was worse. In thirty years on the water, I've learned to read silence. There's the good silence of a calm morning. The tense silence before a storm. The peaceful silence of drifting with the current.

This was none of those. This was the silence of something listening.

Then I heard footsteps.

Footsteps. On water.

Not splashing. Not swimming. Walking. Like someone crossing a wooden deck, steady and measured. Coming toward me through the fog.

I grabbed my spotlight, swept it across the water in the direction of the sound.

And I saw her.

She walked out of the fog like she was stepping through a doorway. Walking on the surface of the ocean like it was solid ground. Each step created a small ripple, but she didn't sink. Didn't bob with the waves. Just moved forward with this terrible, deliberate purpose.

She was barefoot. Wearing a long dress that looked like it had been underwater for decades torn, stained, covered in something dark that might have been seaweed or might have been something worse. Her skin was pale, almost gray. Her hair hung in wet ropes around her face.

But her eyes.

Jesus Christ, her eyes.

Completely black. Solid black from corner to corner. No whites. No iris. No pupil. Just darkness, like someone had cut holes in her face and there was nothing behind them but void.

She was maybe twenty feet from my boat when she smiled.

Her mouth opened too wide. The corners stretched past where they should have stopped. I could see too many teeth more than a person should have. And they were wrong. Not quite the right shape. Not quite the right size.

I couldn't move. I'm not ashamed to admit it I was paralyzed. Thirty years of experience on the water, and I froze like a rookie in his first storm.

She walked closer. Ten feet. Five feet. Right up to the side of my boat.

She leaned down, slowly, deliberately. Her movements were wrong too too smooth, too fluid, like she was moving underwater even though she was in air. Her face came level with mine. I could smell her. Salt water and rot and something chemical, like formaldehyde.

She reached out one hand and placed it on the hull of the *Saint Catherine*.

I felt the boat list hard to starboard. Felt the whole vessel tilt from the weight of her hand. She was solid. Real. Heavy enough to shift three tons of boat and gear.

Her face was inches from mine. I could see water dripping from her hair, could count the wrong number of teeth in her mouth. Those black eyes stared at me without blinking.

She whispered two words: "It's almost time."

Her voice was clear. No distortion, no water in her lungs. She spoke English with no accent I could place. Matter-of-fact. Like she was telling me the tide schedule.

Then she pushed off my boat I felt the vessel rock from the force and stepped back.

The engine roared to life.

Not started *roared*. Like it had been running the whole time and I just hadn't noticed. Every system came online at once. And the fog didn't lift or dissipate. It just... vanished. Snapped away like someone flipping a light switch.

Bright sunshine. Clear sky. Calm water.

She was gone.

I sat there with my hands shaking on the wheel. The radio was still on the same song was playing, Johnny Cash, "Hurt." According to my instruments, no time had passed. According to my instruments, nothing had happened at all.

I hauled my pots on autopilot. Every single one was empty. Not just no lobster *empty*. No bait left. No seaweed. No barnacles. Just clean metal cages like they'd been sandblasted.

I made it back to harbor at 11:34 AM. I tied up the Saint Catherine and sat in the cabin for another hour, trying to convince myself I'd hallucinated. Carbon monoxide, maybe. Stress. Something rational.

But I could still feel where the boat had tilted. Could still smell that smell.

The Days After

I didn't tell Marie. How could I? She'd think I was having a breakdown. Maybe I was.

I called the Coast Guard three separate times. Hung up each time before anyone answered. What was I going to say? "I saw a woman walking on water"? They'd pull my license. Require a psychological evaluation.

I stopped sleeping. Maybe an hour or two before I'd wake up hearing that singing in my head. I tried going back to work, took the boat out Wednesday, turned around after twenty minutes. Couldn't do it. The ocean felt different now. Like it was watching.

Thursday I told Marie I was sick. Stayed home. Couldn't concentrate on anything. Kept checking marine weather, Coast Guard reports, anything that might mention unusual sightings. Nothing.

Friday I started searching online. "Woman walking on water New England." "Black-eyed woman ocean." "Singing fog Maine." Nothing useful. Just ghost stories and cryptid forums.

Saturday night, I was in my shed. I've got a workshop out therewhere I repair equipment, build lobster pots, the kind of work that usually helps me think. I had the radio on. WYAL-FM, that old lighthouse station. The DJ was doing his late-night call-in show. The Hollow Hour.

I usually don't listen to talk radio, but I was too distracted to put on music. The DJ, Vince Hollow, I think his name is, was taking calls from listeners. People sharing strange stories.

I was half-listening, rewiring a bilge pump, when a caller came on.

Young guy. Nervous voice. Said his name was Derek.

"I'm a diver," he said. "And three nights ago, I was doing a deep dive about three miles offshore..."

My hands stopped moving.

"I was coming back to the surface when I saw her. Standing on the water. Just... standing there. Barefoot. Wearing this torn dress. And her eyes..."

I dropped my wire cutters. They clattered on the concrete floor.

"Her eyes were completely black," Derek said. "And she smiled at me. But it was wrong. Her mouth opened too wide. And then she said something."

The DJ asked what she said.

"'It's almost time,'" Derek whispered.

I stood up so fast my stool fell over.

"Same thing," the DJ said quietly. "Every caller tonight has reported the same thing. The same woman. The same words."

There was a pause. Then Derek said something that made my blood freeze.

"How many callers?" he asked.

"Seven," the DJ said. "You're the seventh person tonight who's seen her."

Seven. Seven people. In one night.

What I Found

I spent all of Sunday searching. Facebook groups for local fishermen. Reddit forums. Marine incident reports. News archives.

And I found them. The stories.

September 12th: Commercial fisherman out of Portland reports seeing a "woman on the water" during heavy fog. Coast Guard finds his boat drifting, engine dead, but working perfectly when they arrive. Fisherman refuses to go into detail. Quits his job two days later.

September 28th: Recreational boater near Boothbay Harbor reports similar encounter. Same description. Black eyes. Torn dress. "It's almost time."

October 3rd: Lobsterman from Rockland. Same story. Posts about it on Facebook, deletes the post six hours later. I found a screenshot on a local news forum.

October 9th: Charter fishing captain. Four witnesses. All describe the same woman. The captain sold his boat the next week.

October 15th: Two separate encounters on the same day, forty miles apart.

And that's just what I could find with basic searching. How many people saw her and never reported it? How many are like me, too afraid of being called crazy?

I started making a map. Plotting each sighting. They're all concentrated along this stretch of coast. Roughly a thirty-mile radius. All within the last six weeks.

And they're accelerating. One sighting in September. Three in early October. Twelve in the last week alone.

All the same woman. All the same message.

"It's almost time."

Tonight

It's 11:47 PM on Sunday night. I'm sitting in my shed typing this on my old laptop. Marie's asleep in the house. I told her I was working on tax paperwork.

The Hollow Hour is on the radio. I've been listening every night since Saturday. More callers. More sightings. A priest called in earlier tonight, said forty-three people in his congregation have been having the same dream. Thousands of the drowned, rising from the ocean floor. Walking toward shore.

Toward the lighthouse.

I can hear the ocean from here. My shed is fifty yards from the water. I've listened to that sound for thirty years. The rhythm of the waves. The cry of gulls. The horn from the lighthouse.

But tonight there's something else.

Singing.

Faint. Distant. But I can hear it under the sound of the waves.

That same melody. Getting closer.

I need to post this now. I need people to know this is happening. If you live on the New England coast and you hear singing in the fog, *do not answer*. Do not look. Start your engine and go.

But I don't think it matters anymore.

Because I just looked out the window of my shed.

The tide is out. The beach should be empty.

But there are figures standing on the wet sand. Dozens of them. Maybe hundreds. All facing my shed. All standing perfectly still.

All wearing torn dresses.

All with black eyes.

And they're all smiling.

The singing is louder now.

I can hear Marie screaming in the house.

Marie won't stop screaming. I ran inside, she was standing at our bedroom window, pointing at the beach. She sees them too.

She's asking me what they are. I don't know what to tell her.

The lighthouse beacon just turned red.

I don't know how to explain this. The figures are gone. The beach is empty. Marie is in shock, I gave her the Xanax her doctor prescribed for flying. She's sleeping now.

But our back door was standing open. The one that faces the ocean. I know I locked it. I *know* I did.

There are wet footprints on our kitchen floor. Dozens of them. Leading from the door to our bedroom. To the side of our bed where Marie was sleeping.

They stop right there. Right next to her pillow.

Like something stood there. Watching her.

I'm taking Marie to her sister's house in Vermont tomorrow. Inland. As far from the ocean as I can get.

But I keep hearing that last thing the caller said to the DJ on Saturday night.

"That's your problem now. You're the one in the lighthouse."

What did he mean? What's at the lighthouse?

I think I need to go there. I think I need to understand what's happening.

But I'm terrified of what I'll find.
