It's been a few weeks now, but I remember coming home late from work that night. It was pouring down with rain. I’d gone shopping on the way home and was carrying a few soggy bags in both hands. I stumbled through my front door, tired, immediately seeking the comfort of the warm, dry interior. Though my body protested, I put the shopping away and went straight to bed. When I woke up the next morning, as I left for work, I found my front door was already unlocked. I must have been so desperate to lay my head down, I completely forgot to lock it. I won’t make that mistake again.

A couple of days later the same thing happened, although I distinctly remember locking it this time. I tried the mechanism a few times and gave the handle a wiggle. The door stayed firmly in place. I must be losing it.

When it happened a third time, I was starting to get worried. Anyone could walk in off the street. I contacted my landlord. Had they needed to stop by at some ludicrous hour? Did the previous tenant still have their key? He paused then insisted he hadn’t been over, and he gave some vague answer about the previous tenant, not fully remembering if they had handed back all their keys. I pushed for a new lock on the door. He only agreed if I paid half.

The new lock was finally installed, and I slept a lot more soundly that night. Especially when I saw there were only two keys: one for me, and one for the landlord. I’d even celebrated a little, ordering my favourite takeaway. This only made it much worse when I discovered the door was unlocked again. Why was he doing this to me? I confronted my landlord this time, openly accusing him of coming into my home whilst I slept! Of course he denied it, so I called the police, but they didn't help. No signs of forced entry, and nothing had been taken. No crime committed, as far as they were concerned. It’s safe to say he and I aren’t on the best of terms anymore. I decided I wasn’t going to let myself suffer this invasion of privacy any longer, and I handed in my notice. He eagerly accepted, even waiving the usual 4 weeks’ notice period. I put my things in storage, and I couch surfed at some friends’ houses for a little while.

Even there I wasn't safe and their front doors would be unlocked by the morning too. That’s when I started to feel like I was going truly mad. Even worse, I was now being accused of leaving doors unlocked, to the point where most stopped offering me their sofa to sleep on. By that point I had a new place secured could almost move in, so I only needed to stay in a hotel for a few nights. I managed to get a decent, single room, with keycard access, cameras in the hallway, and security by the main entrance. I felt safer than I had for a long time.

And I was safe for a little while. I checked the door every night. It was always secure. I was sure of it. But of course, on the morning I checked out, my door was free to open. The magnetic, keycard-controlled locks, nothing but useless lumps of metal. I asked the front desk why my lock wasn’t working, but they gave me their best customer facing smile and offered me platitudes to get me out of the door without making an official complaint. I was about to ask to see the camera footage from the previous night, but I noticed security was eyeing me up, readying to intervene, so I left. I had to move my stuff into my new place anyway, and the hotel cameras gave me the idea to set one up of my own. I would train it on the door of my new place and catch whoever is playing this cruel trick on me.

For the first day, I moved in the immediate essentials. It was getting late, and I was getting tired, so, as planned, I set up my phone to record the front door. I started recording just before heading to bed, making sure to get me locking and testing the door. I fell into an uneasy slumber that night, anxiously waiting to see what my phone captured. I woke up groggy, around 5am, but I got out of bed and went to check the door. It’s unlocked. I’ve got them this time! Holding my phone in a shaky hand, I pluck up the courage to stop the recording and play it back. There was a whole load of nothing. Me, locking and testing the door, then nothing. Just an unassuming, locked door. A few motes of dust would occasionally drift past, reflecting the light back into the lens. I cranked the speed up to x2, then x3, when I noticed something move into frame and back out again. I hammered the pause button. I could feel my heartbeat in my chest and throat. A sudden realisation hits me. This was inside. It caught something inside my house. Feeling very exposed, I retreated to the safety of my bathroom. Locking the door, I checked I was alone before slinking down onto the cold tile floor. I played it back again waiting to see the figure then hit pause. Squinting, like that was going to sharpen the pixels on my screen, I tried my best to see who had invaded my home. This person matched me in height and build. Even their gait was similar, yet more sluggish. They oddly wore no shoes, and very lightweight clothing, given the current weather. The few final frames manage to capture their face, and my mind swam with incoherent thoughts. It was me on the camera. My eyes tight shut in slumber yet walking towards the door, unlocking it, and retreating out of frame. This didn’t feel right. I didn’t feel right. I don’t sleepwalk.

I repeated my experiment a few more times. It was always the same. Even the time was consistent. Every night at 2:48 A.M., I would appear, unlock the door, and return to bed. This is when I decided to see what would happen if I stayed awake. I returned home after an unfocused day at work, armed with caffeine and determination. I set up my phone again, lighting the space well. I had coffee, movies, games, alarms, anything to entertain and distract. It was soon 2:30am. My eyes were struggling, but I could hold out. I’d just regret it later. The readout on my watch blinked 2:45 A.M. and I’d dropped any pretence of trying to distract myself. I was sat on my sofa, staring… 2:46… 2:47… 2:57… That wasn’t possible. I was laid in bed now. Pulling back the covers I ran to check my phone’s recording. There I was, again. The bright lights made things easier to see. I was more animated this time. I ran to the door. I searched frantically for the keys on their usual hook, fumbled them, then urgently jammed them into the keyhole. After unlocking the door, I quickly stepped back and braced myself, as though waiting for something to happen. But nothing did. My head cocks to one side as though listening and, after a beat, I turn around, eyes still shut tight and shuffle out of frame.

After this point, things started to become more erratic. My daytime was unaffected, but I can never stay awake until 2:48 A.M. I kept setting up my phone at night. I no longer walk calmly to the door but run. I unlock the door with an increased sense of urgency, step back, brace, wait, listen, before finally turning around, and go back to bed.

This brings me to why I’m writing to you all. Last night I followed my same set up and recorded my minutes of nightly mayhem, but things didn’t quite go as before. I woke in the morning to find my phone on the floor, knocked out of its tripod. When I watched the footage back, I was no longer standing but hunched over, crouched even. I approached the door like you might a wounded, wild animal, with steady movements, offering an outstretched, reassuring hand, yet flinching at any sign of movement. Only the door didn’t move. After finally unlocking it, I sat on my haunches and waited. Occasionally, my head would snap to a point in the room out of frame, before returning to the door. What I did next, just imagining it, still sends a creeping chill through my bones. My nightly version of me, still sat on my haunches, went completely stiff and still. With strained, jittery movements, my head started to turn towards the phone, I was smiling, it felt unnatural, and, for the first time my eyes were wide open, staring directly down the camera. An arm shot out towards the phone, sending the images tumbling. I was so caught off guard I almost missed it. It was only a couple frames before the phone was knocked down. The door handle had started turning.

I’m currently sat in a coffee shop with my laptop, typing this out to you all, trying to stave off sleep. I don’t know what is happening to me, or what is on the other side of my door. I only get the sense that whatever happens tonight, I might not be around to tell you all in the morning. I shouldn’t have tried to stay awake that night. Maybe one of you can learn from my mistake. Until the morning, stay safe and good night.
