Until Teddy’s death, I didn’t realize just how tied up my identity was in the circumstances that surrounded me. It took the floor dropping out from under me to find that my selfhood was the first to fall away.

In high school, my personal identity was intimately tied to the things that I *did*. The extracurriculars I participated in, the clubs and volunteer groups that I joined just because my academic advisor told me they’d look good on a college application. I was a soccer player, a member of National Honor Society, the 17th ranked student by GPA in a senior class of 450. Those things proved my worth, benchmarks of achievement that told me I was *this* good, *this* valuable, *this* loveable.

In college, the perspective shifted from the things I did to the things I was *going to be*. As an adult, you sell your accomplishments. As a student, you sell your potential. And there seemed no limit to my potential. I was *going to be* a computer engineer. I was *going to be* working for a Fortune 500 company. And with those things would come all the rest of it. I was *going to* have a wife, kids, the dog, the starter home in a safe clean neighborhood. 

So as hard as my classes got, as isolated as I felt away at school, those things didn’t actually define who I was. That person was somewhere far in the future, already enjoying the life he’d made for himself.

After Teddy’s death, some inherent drive for achievement had been sucked out of me, like somebody grabbed a fistful of my entrails and then *pulled*. My professors tried to meet me halfway, set up some sort of online coursework so I’d at least have a chance to complete the semester. At some point, I just stopped answering their emails, as well as the ones sent from the registrar. I only went back to campus once to load the family SUV with the tiny pile of belongings still left in my dorm room. I never even saw my roommate.

When I pulled into the driveway of my parents’ house, it finally dawned on me that the identity I’d so carefully curated had been stripped clean away, down to the bone. I was no longer working toward some goal that would make all this struggle worth it.   
I was just… *here*. Existing. Waiting around for… something. 

My parents didn’t discuss my studies with me, or question my decision to move back home. They only made one thing clear to me: I had to get a job. I knew of only one place that would hire me on such short notice, and the thought of it made me sick to my stomach, but I didn’t have many options in a place as small as my hometown, with the summer season having already wound down. Eventually, with nothing else to do, I swallowed my pride and went begging for a job at Carson’s Diner.

I’ll never forget the image of Gary Carson peering at me over the top of my job application in his too-small office that was about twice the size of the broom closet next door. Even with its location near the back door, far from the kitchen, it still stank of fry grease, a stench that permanently soaked into every atom of hair and fabric that it came into contact with. Carson inspected the paper with glee, especially the Employment History section, which only contained a single line for my two years of back-of-house work from high school at the very diner in which we sat.

Despite how large and portly of a man Carson was, he seemed much smaller to me then, someone who resented his father and grandfather for leaving him a disgusting grease-pit to manage and his two siblings for making him shoulder almost the entirety of the responsibility by himself. His life afforded him very few opportunities for merriment, but the sight of one of the punk kids he used to employ, who went away to college with lofty goals for his life, crawling back for a job was a shot of adrenaline for his Little Caesar Complex. But given that I had even a modicum of executive function, he wasn’t really in the position to turn me down. 

My interview lasted ten minutes, after which Carson tossed me a hideous uniform in my size and said, “Get on back there. Everything’s the same as you left it.”

If I hated working at Carson’s in high school, it was tantamount to torture after dropping out of college. I hated the endless hours on my feet, my knees aching and plantar fasciitis slowly spreading throughout my soles. I hated standing over 350-degree fry oil, little droplets hitting my forearms like a thousand pinpricks whenever I’d drop a basket of fries or an order of onion rings. I hated that my acne had returned for the first time since I was seventeen. And I hated my coworkers, the lineup of which hadn’t much changed since I left, and who were just as satisfied as Gary Carson that I had been brought back down to their level.

Most of them probably assumed that I just couldn’t hack it at college, and came slinking back home a failure. They were the last people I’d open up to about the circumstances of my brother’s death, so I let them get their little jabs in, bearing it in silence. I wouldn’t rise to the bait when Robbie, my forty-five-year-old coworker with sleeve tattoos and a goatee, who always reeked of weed after coming back from taking the trash out to the dumpsters, would clap me on the back and call me *Dropout*.

My father continued teaching high school, but he’d come home every day looking like a pallid, deflated balloon, his back literally bending forward under the weight of his day. The teenagers he taught English Lit to had kept him on the knife's edge of quitting his job for the past decade, but he always steeled himself, picked up his flag, and charged back up the hill every August. At the very least, he’d *complain* about the work. Now, he’d just shrug at any questions about his day, heating up leftovers in the microwave and then taking them down to the basement, where he’d stay until after I went to bed. He’d turn up the television loud, but it didn’t mask the sound of his crying as well as he probably suspected. 

Mom just stopped going into work altogether, opting to spend all day in bed and all night walking around town, an activity that became more and more concerning as the early winter chill crept into the air while the weeks pressed on. She’d started the habit the first night after Teddy’s funeral, and hadn’t bothered to stop. One morning, I came into the hallway, finding that the door across from mine, Teddy’s door, was slightly ajar. I cracked the door a little more to peer inside, finding mom asleep in a bed half her size, her knees curled to her chest, her jacket and shoes still on. The items in the room were still perfectly preserved, exactly where Teddy had placed them on the morning of his death. A thin film of dust had gathered over every surface.  

If I had a different relationship with my parents, one where we’d have hard conversations and say hard things, I’d have asked her where she went every night, but I could never summon the courage in the rare instances when we’d pass through the same room. In those weeks following Teddy’s death, it felt more like the three of us - mom, dad, and me - were more disinterested roommates than anything resembling family, sustaining ourselves on little morsels of small talk as we floated aimlessly through the same space. 

So my curiosity remained unsated, growing steadily as November turned over into December. And every night, as I was about to fall asleep, I’d hear the telltale whine of my parents bedroom door opening and closing, the distinct patter of mom’s bare feet on the hardwood floor, the characteristic squeak of the fifth step of the stairs. A brief pause while she laced her shoes, and the slightest of clicks as the front door closed behind her. Then she’d disappear into the night, already home by the time I’d wake.

Though it was unspoken, I think my dad and I both assumed that we’d just have to let it run its course. Mom was going through something, and so were we. Who were we to say that she was doing it wrong? In hindsight, I wish I would have said something sooner, instead of being a passive observer. Maybe I could have stopped it… stopped *her*.

Eventually, the material realities of mom’s absence from the clinic began to manifest. For the past fifteen years, she’d run a very successful private family medicine practice, having split off from one of the larger clinics in town around the time I was five. She had only a few employees, and her administrative assistant, a cheerful thirty-year-old named Katherine, had shouldered the burden of rescheduling several weeks worth of physicals, immunizations, and well-child visits while mom recovered from the death of her son. 

At first, her patients were understanding. Many of them, my mom had treated for a significant part of their adulthood, or possibly the entirety of their lives. *Of course* they could reschedule. Lydia lost her son after all. This ache can wait a few weeks. But week delays turned into month delays, and by the end of November, patients had started calling Katherine not to reschedule, but to notify her that they’d be moving to a new PCP across town, one who would actually agree to see them. Mom didn’t even seem to bat an eye, even when Katherine put in her two weeks notice, having seen the writing on the wall.

The walks continued. And my curiosity grew into an obsession. But one particularly frigid night in early December, I finally got some answers.

Cleanup at Carson’s had taken an extra hour than normal that night, since we were down a man on the Wednesday evening shift. A seventeen year old kid named Ricky had mouthed off to Gary and gotten himself fired, storming out of the kitchen during the dinner rush. It was the kind of shift where Murphy’s Law was on full display, a cosmically aligned shitstorm. Hours later, I pulled my car into my parents’ driveway, turning the key to kill the engine and music. I just sat there in silence, my head back, my eyes closed, breathing in through my nostrils and out through my mouth. Reflecting on the fact that this was my life now.

The characteristic click and slam of the house’s front door made me open my eyes, looking over to find my mother on the porch, bundled in her heavy jacket and scarf, gloved hands rubbing the sides of her arms while her breath frosted in the air. And draped across her body was a leather bag that sat on her hip. My father’s toolbag? I hadn’t seen it in use since my parents had started hiring out for their home improvement work, but there it was all the same, slung over my mom’s shoulder rather than collecting dust in the garage. 

She must not have seen me, because she stole a careful glance up at the bedroom windows above her head, then made a beeline through the front yard, taking a sharp right to head down the street at a surprising pace.

I climbed out of the car before my feet and hands even knew what they were doing, pulling on a winter coat saturated with the stench of fry grease. I didn’t even think to grab my hat and gloves, a fact I lamented as I hurried after my mother at a far enough distance that I wouldn’t be detected. I doubt it made much difference anyways, since I don’t remember her ever turning around to see if she was being followed. Why would she? 

It was a clear night, my mother’s form illuminated by the moonlight overhead as she traveled out of our cul-de-sac, out of our neighborhood, out to the country road that led uphill past the farmhouses dotting the landscape. By the time we got outside of town, I felt a pang of guilt for having never guessed it from the jump. I suddenly felt like a sick voyeur intruding on my mother as she walked the two miles to visit my brother’s grave, but I couldn’t stop my feet from pushing me forward, like I was in a trance, my desire for closeness to my mother drowning out any other alarm bells going off in my head.

The roads were well-plowed, but half an hour trudging through December slush had caused the tips of my toes to go numb by the time I reached the cemetery. The graveyard parking lot was empty of cars, a blanket of unbroken snow over the ground, save for the beeline of mom’s footsteps and the rows of headstones that sprouted out of the ground, each with its own little cap of white. The weeping willow presided over the entire affair, its leaves long having dropped away, leaving naked twisted bark in their place. The branches, without their plumage, were gaunt and spindly, drooping over the frozen pond below like the hair of a witch.

I was much more exposed on the road, so I hung back far enough that mom had nearly become a dot on the horizon while I followed her for the last leg of the journey. By the time I made it to the fence at the front of the graveyard, she was already kneeling in front of Teddy’s grave. Weeping. While the willow watched, an impartial observer. 

My lucidity to the moment, where I was, what I was doing, came all at once, and I felt a queasiness bubble up from the bottom of my stomach. I had done something wrong. And I was watching something I wasn’t supposed to. I’d violated my mother’s privacy. Profaned it. I had to get out of there, my heart pounding in my ears. I was about to turn and leave, when mom suddenly stood up. I froze in place, sure that she’d turn around, sure that she’d see me. But her silhouette didn’t turn back toward the road. Instead, she pivoted toward the willow tree across the pond.

The trance was back, my past sins forgotten, as I craned my neck to see what mom was up to. Slowly, deliberately, she circled the water, sidling up next to the tree. She placed a palm against the bark of the trunk, and despite the whipping of the wind on the hill, I could almost make out that she was… talking to it? Her head dropped back as she inspected the branches above, still saying… *something*, then pressed her forehead against the trunk. I crept a little closer, trying to keep the crunch of the snow as quiet as possible as I snaked through the lines of headstones. 

Mom fell silent, then her body went rigid, like she’d decided something important. And in a quick motion, she dug into the toolbag at her side, producing my dad’s cordless drill in one hand and a small glass jar in the other. Then she pressed the tip of the drill against the weeping willow and pulled the trigger, the whine of the tool offset by the rumble of the wooden flesh that it ripped away. While I watched, images rushed back to me of a school trip I took in the sixth grade for science class, where we watched maple trees get tapped for sap. 

Mom pulled the drill back, and a viscous, yellow-brown liquid immediately began to rush from the hole. She placed the jar against the bark, filling it up about halfway before putting a lid over the top and placing everything back in the toolbag. It was all so fast, so efficient, that my mind was still trying to catch up when my mom spun on her heels to walk back down to the front of the cemetery… only to find me watching her. 

I had never had a very warm relationship with my parents, but I can’t say I’d ever been *scared* of them growing up. That switch flipped in the graveyard that night, because I was *certainly* frightened of mom at that moment, not just for the consequences of my violation, but also because of the downright strange behavior I’d just witnessed. 

My mother walked slowly down the hill toward me, unblinking, one hand gripping the strap that crossed her chest. She stopped in front of me with a blank expression, her eyes searching my face, while my body stayed frozen in place and my mouth remained closed. She didn’t look sad, or even angry. There was something colder behind her eyes, something darker. She only said one sentence before brushing past me to walk back down the hill toward town, not even checking behind to see that I was following. One sentence that made goosebumps rise on my neck and arms, and a chill to run up from my frozen toes to the nerves in my scalp.

“Don’t follow me again, son.” 

Then she was gone.

I wanted to go back home, climb into my warm bed, forget that this night had ever happened. But I couldn’t leave yet, not without inspecting the tree that mom had been so entranced by. I couldn’t go back, not with her still so close, so I went forward, approaching the tree slowly, warily, like it was going to jump out and attack me if I moved too suddenly. *It’s just a tree*, I reminded myself. *It’s just a tree*. And yet, the closer I got, the worse I felt, like I was pressing further and further toward the epicenter of a nuclear disaster, the radiation silently eating away at my insides, tearing my cells apart.

I stopped in front of the tree, dried sap sticking to the bark below the hole, like blood from a gunshot wound. It smelled foul, and I had to breathe through my mouth to keep my eyes from watering. The benefit of hindsight and research has afforded me the knowledge that I didn’t have at that moment. That while uncommon, willow trees *can* produce sap. It’s usually a sign of infection, rot, damage. It’s a signal that a tree is *sick*, like pus oozing from a wound. And here my mother was, bottling it up and taking it home.

But there was something else I noticed about the hole in the wood behind its offensive odor. There was a darkness to it, a kind of black that felt absolute, perfect, untarnished. A blackness so flat and all-consuming that the light from the moon and nearby streetlamps behaved strangely around it, like they were swallowed whole in its orbit. I have no idea why I placed my palms against the trunk and brought my eye down to be level with the drillhole, only that I did.

For a few breaths, there was nothing, just an endless black void where a few inches of damaged wood was supposed to be. Then I saw something that turned my stomach, that I still think about on cold lonely nights all these years later. It was there one moment and gone the next, but it was there all the same. A human eye. Wet, green, sickly, with red vessels spiderwebbing through its white flesh. Then it blinked at me.

I yelped, falling backward into the snow, the cold stinging my palms and fingers and the wet from the ground seeping into my pants. Once I caught my breath, I scrambled to my feet, throwing one more glance at the willow, then practically running downhill toward the cemetery gate, toward the country road, toward my neighborhood, my home, my bed. 

As I hurried back toward town, nervous glances thrown from time to time over my shoulder, I kept thinking about the eye, the image of it seared into my memory. My pace quickened with every step, first a determined walk, then a fast walk, then a full-on jog as the terror rose in my chest.

And every few minutes on that empty, moon-soaked road, below the wind, below my own breath, below the splashing slush and snow thrown with every footfall, I thought I could hear something. A whisper so faint that it was gone as soon as I tried to listen, a hushed gasp that fled as soon as I was attuned to it, carried away by the chilly air that whipped across the nearby fields.

But by the time I made it to my front porch, my throat burning and my feet numb, I had caught enough fragments to piece together what I’d heard out there. I don’t know if it was a warning or a plea.

“*Don’t go*,” the wind whispered. “*Don’t go.*”
