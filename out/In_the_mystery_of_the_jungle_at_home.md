I was driving back from my village last month. It was my job. I collect old dues for a finance company. The kind of job that takes you through villages nobody has heard of. The road was empty. No bikes, no trucks, nothing. Even the birds were silent. It felt like the whole forest was holding its breath, waiting for something.



It was 7 PM. The sky was still light, but dark clouds rolled in fast. Within ten minutes, it was black. Then the lightning started. The thunder was so loud I could feel it in my chest. The rain hit my windshield like someone was throwing buckets of water. The wind was insane. I saw trees bending at angles they shouldn't.



Then it happened. A huge peepal tree, right in front of me, just snapped. It crashed onto the road with a sound like a bomb. My car skidded and stopped inches from it. I got out to check. The road was completely blocked. There was no other way. The forest was too thick to drive around.



I got back in the car to turn around. I turned the key. Nothing. The engine was dead. No click, no light on the dashboard. Just dead. My phone had no signal. Zero bars. I was stuck.



That's when I saw it. A light. Deep in the forest, maybe 200 meters away. A single, steady yellow light. In a place like that, any light means a house. A house means people. People mean help. Or at least a roof for the night.



I grabbed my phone for a torch and started walking towards it. The rain was still heavy. The ground was mud. I slipped twice. As I got closer, I saw it was a small, old-style cottage. The walls were white, the roof was tiled. Lights were on inside. But there was no sound. No TV, no talking, nothing.



As soon as I reached the gate, it creaked open by itself. I froze. I called out, "Koi hai?" My voice sounded tiny in the storm.



From inside, a woman's voice replied. It was soft. "Haan. Main hoon."



I asked, "Can I stay here for the night? My car's broken down."



She said, "Yes. Come in."



I pushed the door and stepped inside. The air was cold. It smelled like wet earth and something sweet, like jasmine. A woman walked out from a dark hallway. She was beautiful. Young, maybe 25. She wore a simple white saree. Her hair was long, black, and wet from the rain. Or so I thought.



As she walked towards me, her face changed. Her skin turned grey. Her eyes went completely black, no white at all. Her lips pulled back over her teeth in a smile that was too wide. Her fingers became long and claw-like. The door behind me slammed shut with a bang. I tried to run but I couldn't move.



Her hair started growing. It wasn't wet. It was moving on its own, like snakes. It shot across the room and wrapped around my neck, my arms, my legs. It was cold and it burned at the same time. I couldn't breathe. I couldn't scream. The last thing I remember is her face an inch from mine, whispering something I couldn't understand.



I woke up the next morning on the side of the road. The sun was up. The rain was gone. My car was parked perfectly. The engine started on the first try. I looked around, confused. The giant peepal tree was gone. There was no sign it had ever fallen. No broken branches, no marks on the road.



I looked into the forest where the house was. There was nothing. Just trees and bushes. No path, no clearing, no cottage.



I thought it was a nightmare until I looked in my rearview mirror.



Draped over my left shoulder was a long strand of wet, black hair.



It wasn't mine.



And I'm still wondering what she did to me while I was unconscious.
