"It's awful, I'm so behind and if I miss one more deadline then I'm pretty sure that's it."

"Hmmm." Ted said. "What're you doing here then?"

"You don't get it, it doesn't matter how long I sit in front of the computer screen and try and focus, I can't make the words appear. My family were so sceptical when I started to seriously look into writing but after the success of the first book... That can't have been a one off. I can't be a failure. I..."

Ted gestured over at the bartender to get us a couple more drinks.

"Right, maybe I don't get it. But you're overthinking this."

I opened my mouth to protest but he cut me off.

"Seriously! Didn't you write pretty much all of the last book right after your break up with Sue? And no offence but you were a mess when that happened. So maybe the problem is that you're too much in your own head right now."

Ted handed me one of the drinks.

"Anyway," he said, "don't they say you should write drunk and edit sober?"

"What? No, that's-"

"Well, someone's said that. And you've tried your way so do you really have anything to lose?"

Conventional wisdom would suggest that if you were going to try writing whilst drunk you wouldn't want to be *drunk* drunk, you'd just want a drink or two in you. Maybe three. But whilst I can't remember exactly how many drinks I had with Ted it was definitely a number bigger than three. I can't even remember how the night ended but next morning I woke up in my own bed with my mouth somehow both dry and tasting of acid and the computer switched on in the corner of the room. Despite feeling like shit I couldn't fight the curiousity of what words would be on the screen.

I managed to get over to the computer without vomiting and my first impression was just how much I'd managed to type last night. There were pages of stuff and whilst I was too hungover to say for certain it seemed *good.* It was a little rough around the edges but for a first draft it wasn't half bad. If I could churn out a similar word count for the next week then I'd be back on track.

Except I couldn't. The words that drunk-me had summoned so easily completely eluded me in my sober state. The day stretched on and it was 7pm before I reached the obvious solution. I mean, if the drunk version of myself didn't have any writer's block then there's one surefire way to get back to him. I poured myself a drink and downed it quickly, not wanting my brain or body to have any time to object. I usually like to enjoy my drinks but this was for function, not fun. I poured another and grimaced to myself before that one too was drunk.

Getting drunk at home had the same effect on my writing that getting drunk at the bar had had. I felt worse when I woke up but than I had the night after I'd been drinking with Ted but that was a sacrifice I'd have to make. I didn't even pretend that I was going to get some writing done that day and spent my time feeling thoroughly ill and sorry for myself. The next three days followed the roughly the same pattern – getting drunk in the evening and feeling like shit the next day.

The hangovers were getting steadily worse though. Predictable symptoms like nausea and headaches were joined by the rarer symptoms of nosebleeds, dizziness and mild difficulties breathing. I hadn't had hangovers like this for a long time but I told myself that it wasn't forever. I just needed to write a little more and then I'd never drink this much again. I went to start up last night's drinking and then realised that I stupidly hadn't bought anymore lifestyle despite racing through the bottles I'd had in the house. Oh well, I felt so awful that I'd probably get drunk more easily than I could when I was healthy anyway. I drank everything I had in the house and let things fade away.

I should've drunk more. Instead I woke up in the middle of the night and heard the gentle, rhythmic tapping of keys. In the white light of the computer's screen I could see somebody typing away frantically but his shape was all wrong. I sat up sharply only to fall straight back down again. There were several things wrong with my body. My vision seemed wrong in a way I couldn't place and my pulse seemed unusually fast as my circulatory system raced to compensate for my changes.

At the noise of my movement the thing in the corner turned away from the computer screen and I realised two things at the same time. The first was that the creature who had been typing was missing several parts. He had hands, a head and two thick arms but he had no legs and barely had a torso. His face only featured one eye – shining, central and familiar. The second thing I realised was made apparent once I put my arm out in front of me. My arms no longer had hands at the end of them. I threw the duvet off my body and saw that my torso looked emaciated and my legs were outright missing. Every part of me that I had lost, the creature in the corner had gained.

The thing moved from the near the computer. It rolled off the chair and began to move towards me. It didn't want me to observe it. I knew this instinctually and began to flee the creature assembled from my parts. Is this how I really wrote my first book? I'd been drunk a lot of the time back then. Once I dragged myself along the floor into the bathroom I closed the door behind me and listened to fingernails that no longer belonged to me scrape against the door's wood.

If I'd thought that my heart had been racing before then it was at a crescendo now, burdened by fear and effort. Which parts of my insides had the creature even taken from me? The strain that being incomplete was having on my body was physically painful and I realised that the only reason that the creature was approaching me was to recombine. He wouldn't type with me conscious and so he was trying to make me whole again.

I couldn't lock the door from my position on the floor and eventually my other half would be able to reach me. I knew what I had to do.

I opened the cabinet under the sink and forced a bottle of mouthwash onto the floor with me. I clenched its lid between my teeth and held the bottle with the ends of my arms, twisting it until it opened. I drank the mouthwash in fast, desparate gulps, thankful that I hadn't opted for an alcohol free version. Dizziness overwhelmed me and the creature managed to successfully open the door just before I passed out.

The next morning I woke up in pain on my bathroom floor and pulled myself up to look in the mirror. I looked ill but all of me was there at least. Now that was settled I crawled through to my bedroom and climbed carefully onto the swivel chair in front of the desk to check last night's word count. He'd written enough, thank god.

Just three more nights of this and I can move onto editting.
