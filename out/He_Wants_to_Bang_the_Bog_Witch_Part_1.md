*Not more than a week ago I was called out to identify a property that was mysteriously destroyed. Just to be clear, I’m not a detective. I’m far more effective, I’m an insurance fraud investigator. This place was way out in the swamps, some little shit stick house that was renovated by a local.*



*I can’t imagine they truly cared much for it, but they had put the shack up as a haunted swamp experience and put in enough money to warrant insurance. From what they told me; Two tourists had booked the place, and they and the house disappeared the very same night. The owner’s staff, those who were meant to provide the scares and props for the experience, report being chased off by gunfire, they never docked on the Island. The company wouldn’t accept that as evidence of malicious intent by the two guests, so I was sent to the site to find some evidence.*



*When I took a boat out there to check it out, I struggled to find the site. It was supposed to be a little island ringed with trees, but the GPS led to me nothing. It took a while, but I eventually found some remnants of the building sticking out of the water. The island and the house had sunk into the swamp. While odd, it wasn’t unheard off, many of these swamp isles are just floating clumps of dirt held together by roots and the will of the currents. What was more concerning was the few fragments of building that I could see, were charred.*



*I called It in and wrote up a suspicion of arson, which we would have to compensate. Of course, HQ didn’t want that, so they asked me to find another cause. The fact that the island and house had sunk meant there was another element in play. I looked around a little longer and found a camera nestled in the roots of a nearby tree. The thing looked like it had been thrown or otherwise launched there. It was pretty banged up and the lens and screen were cracked. I seized it as evidence and got out of there fast. Something about that place was unsettling, even in broad daylight it gave me the creeps. Though it could’ve been me, I hated swamps, all those nasty smells and that Spanish moss that hung like wigs on a rack.*



*When I got back to the security of civilization, I was able to save the SD card on the camera, which was thankfully full of footage. I took it to HQ, and we all sat down to watch. I won’t go into detail, but it was… disturbing. It was made by two paranormal investigator wannabes, an Australian and his Eastern European cameraman. Either they were good at making authentic horror films, or something genuinely supernatural happened out there.*



*The insurance company decided that the footage was enough evidence to prove that the property and island sank before it burned. They wrote the insurance claim off as natural causes, leaving nothing for the owner. Not my problem, but when the case was closed the footage was seized and destroyed.*



*I wished I had kept it, or hidden it, or something to save the evidence of those events. For they haunted me. Every night since, each detail has been etched into my dreams. I wake up with it at the forefront of my thoughts, reliving what I watched. It progressed over the week to the point that it was me in the footage, that I was there and I embodied one of those tourists, the cameraman. These memories remain burned into my psyche, and if I don’t get them out, I fear they will haunt me forever.*



*That is why I am posting this here; this is the narrative retelling of the footage on that camera. I wish beyond reason I still had a copy, or I hadn’t turned it in at all. But I hope to have done my utmost to convey every detail, I took few creative liberties, only inferring details from research and these memories that won’t let me rest.*

Darren wanted to bang the Bog Witch. Or that’s what he’d said when Dimitri had shown him a picture of the creature they were searching for. Deep in the Okefenokee Swamp, right on the border between Georgia and Florida.



Their fishing boat jumped and skidded across the murky water. The bottom of the boat and the rudder scraping the soft mud on the shallow swamp floor. Dimitri clutched at the heavy camera case that sat across his lap. His hands cradling the precious device from the unclean embrace of the bog. He tightened his legs around a cooler filled with Darren’s liquid necessities. It was overflowing with beer bottles and ice, so much so that the lid didn’t even touch the container. Of course, the man himself carried only his own things in a sling bag across his chest. He sat towards the prow of the boat, splayed out and relaxed, easy in every situation.



“Listen mate, one night. One night only, then you come tomorrow yeah?” Darren explained for the fourth time. “We only need a bit of footage for my documentary.”



The Local, tired of the Australian tourist’s bullshit, just nodded and grunted. “I’ll be there.”



“That’s what I’m talking about lad. You said this cabin is a hotspot for witch stuff and creepy shit, right? That’s the kinda thing we need to get on camera.” Darren reached over between Dimitri and fished out a bottle of slightly warm beer for himself. He then pulled out a pack of cigarettes and paused, remembering something. “Anyone else want a smoke?”



He got half points for courtesy, Dimitri and the boat pilot both took one. The offer perked their guide up a little. Enough for him to talk over the sound of the wind whipping by and the water slapping against the hull.



“You’re hunting to get a video of the Bog Witch, that it?” The man nodded to the camera. He spoke with that distinctive Georgian drawl. “I say good luck to y’all. Theres plenty of Gators, bugs and other nasties, to look at. But no witch, no spooks. The swamp is calm, even peaceful at the best of times, smelly at its worst.”



They laughed along with the boat pilot’s weak joke, but only Dimitri had to fake it.



The boat pilot took a drag, smoke streaking away as the boat sped along the waters, the tip of the cigarette glowing red as the wind sucked it down fast. He hesitated, opening his mouth to speak then stopping, clucking his tongue and frown this way and that.



“I will say, never be disrespectful to the Okefenokee, this place… it has its own ways.” The guide said, finally choosing his words and sounding far more serious than one should expect.



“Of course, mate, I’m made of respectfulness. And hey, I can handle gators mate. I’ve literally eaten bigger crocodiles then any gator you’ve seen.” Darren made sure to let everyone know, for maybe the seventh time. Dimitri had heard many of the various detailed retellings as they travelled up Florida.



The Local only gave the Aussie a flat look, then went back to speaking.



“Like I said, I can’t say too much about what you’ll see out there. It’s a nice little cabin you two picked out. First saw it out there as I took people on tours and such. Some people came and did some work to it, even brought in some natives for ritual work. Maybe it’s blessed ground or something. I stayed there once with my wife, a little getaway. It’s peaceful. Good fishing and such.”



“You never saw anything odd?” Dimitri said, shifting his camera on his lap, it had been recording for a while, and would remain recording the whole night. Darren sometimes did things on a whim, so Dimitri had learned to capture every scrap of footage he could. Though he was expecting another waste of time, where they would see absolutely nothing supernatural and stretch thin their already frayed budget.



“I’ve never seen anything odd. But my wife swears on her soul owed to God, that she heard women singing. There’s been a few sightings of strange women out there, but people like to argue over if’n it was the Daughters of the Sun or their guardian…”



“The what?” Darren, who had stopped listening, immediately perked up. “Who are they?”



“The Guardian or the Daughters of the Sun?”



“Second one.”



The local paused to take a breath as he put together his thoughts. “Alright, the tale goes; When conquistadores arrived in these parts in the late 16th century, they heard stories about a tribe of beautiful, dark-eyed, maidens known collectively as the 'Daughters of the Sun.' These women were said to speak in accents of music and looked like angels. It was said that they lived in the Okefenokee on an island that was protected by swirling mists, uncrossable rivers and alligator-filled lakes.”



Darren grinned wide. “Fuck me dead mate. Why aren’t we looking for these lovelies?”



Dimitri just shrugged, who knew what would catch his friend’s focus from one moment to the next.



The guide went on, only stopping to signal for another smoke, which was freely given. Darren had stockpiled on cheap cigarettes with plans to smuggle as many back to his country as possible, they were beyond expensive there. Most of his luggage in their cheap hotel room was a suitcase filled with cartons of cigarettes. How he planned to get them through TSA was not something Dimitri’s cared to be a part of.



“That is not all. There is another that protects this illusive island and the mystical tribe of women there. They say the Bog Witch lurks in the shallow waters, slipping between the roots of trees. Her presence floating in the churned-up froth and slime around the isle. Forever circling her sisters who cast her out, envy twists her to do cruel, and hateful things to the beautiful.”



“Hah, Dimitri here won’t have to worry then.” Darren said, giving him a slap on the chest.



“Right.” The guide continued. “Well, there are different versions to the tale of course. Some say she was cast out from the tribe of maidens; some say she manifested from the churned-up slosh of the swamp, some say she is a woman of our world who drowned trying to find the island and now roams the swamp looking for it. There are a lot of folk with many stories over the years. All with some truth to them. Plenty of people have gotten lost in the Okefenokee and seen all sorts of odd things. Many more never left and had their spirits mixed with the black mud. Any one of them could be true.”



Darren gave a low whistle. “Struth mate. Were there any tales like that in Mother Russia Dimitri?”



Dimitri sighed as the boat pilot froze upon hearing those words. He had yet to meet any American, that lived through the cold war, not react upon hearing what part of the world he came from.



“No, I’m not from Russia, I’m from Georgia.”



The boat pilot nodded, “Right, you’re from here, but your parents where Ruski’s.”



“No, I am from Georgia, the country.”



“The county?”



“Country, in the Caucuses. Between Turkey and Russia.”



The local just shook his head, attention focused on the trees dotting the swamp. “Sir, I have no idea what you’re talking about.”



Darren waved his hand and shooed several flies away, he finished his smoke, flicked the butt into the water and went straight for another. “Who cares where he is from, all I want to know is if his people had any creepy legends. Every culture has one or two, and I’m trying to find them all, one of them has to be real. Dimitri tell us about yours.”



“Yes, there were some. Many tales of ghosts, many old battle sites, where soldiers were slaughtered by the thousands. We have tales of people freezing in deep snow, their body reanimating into some frozen ghoul that would come scare children.” Dimitri said, not used to being the centre of attention, which rarely happened around Darren. “All tales and grandmotherly folk nonsense, I’m sure. But it led for a superstitious childhood.”



Darren laughed, “And that is why my cameraman makes an excellent paranormal investigator, he gets scared easily. The audience loves it.”



Dimitri gave Darren a dirty look, and the Aussie just shrugged and grinned out into the swamp, a cigarette held between his teeth.



The local nodded. “We have something similar. I remember my old Mee-maw, telling me about Bog Bodies. Mummified corpses, deep under the mud that dig their way out and come to snatch the ears and noses of naughty children. They trap the unwary and incautious, so that they can come replace their missing parts. Until they get it, they hide their rotten faces in canvas bags. They say they can be appeased with gifts, but the dead are never satisfied.” He shook his head, chuckling softly. “When I was little, I used to leave old Halloween candy and pennies outside my window, so the Bog Bodies didn’t take my face. I swear my cousin used to snatch them just to scare me.”



“Bloody hell, that’s one way to give kids nightmares.” Darren said, he handed out slightly warm beers to everyone, this time forgetting to share the cigarettes.



“Hah, it did. But there’s some truth to it. Anyone who puts a foot wrong out here and sinks down to their waist in mud knows it. The bog is dangerous. But kids always need something scary to keep their curiosity in check.” Boat pilot took a swig of his drink. Darren had already finished his.



“Have you ever seen one?” Dimitri asked, not expecting anything.



“Oh yeah sure. They wash up from time to time. Just a preserved body, not some monster. Sometimes they make a news headline, sometimes some yokel just hangs it up as a decoration. Though no good folk would do that, most would respectfully cast them back into the swamp.”



“Hey maybe we will get lucky and see one, that has to be good footage.” Darren said eagerly.



“The chances of that are happening are practically zero.” Dimitri said, and the local nodded along.



Still the Australian continued to spout his unfiltered stream of consciousness, detailing every possibility, every chance they could take to make it big. So self-assured in his belief that there would be paranormal sightings here in the Okefenokee, that Dimitri couldn’t help but expect the same. It was an odd feeling; to be dragged into someone’s else’s worldview then realise you had been taken for a ride during the fact. It was something Dimitri had grown used too, and it was part of the reason he has stuck around so long. How could any man, especially a man like Darren, be so certain about the unknown.



It was another half-hour of the Aussie blowing wind out of his mouth, before they finally reached the cabin. It was surrounded by dense roots from ancient trees, so old and thick they almost formed a wall around the island. Thick foam and slime pooled around the roots and clung to the muddy banks of the isle. Spanish moss hung down like hair from branches, pale and showing flecks of green. It gave the place a mouldy look, which wasn’t helped by the humid hot air and the thousands of insects hovering over the swamps murky water.



The Guide pulled up to a rickety pier and helped get the two tourists and their things off. A few words were passed and some smokes. Then he was off without a backwards glance. Dimitri wondered if the man would really come back tomorrow morning.



“Ah, how’s that lovely swamp air?” Darren took a deep exaggerated breath, then spluttered, choking as a he sucked down a mosquito. Coughing it up violently he gave Dimitri a withering look. “Fuck mate, a little help?”



“What am is supposed to do?” Dimitri shouldered the camera bag, the one with both their clothes on his back sagged, and he struggled with the cooler filled with ice and beer. “My hands are full.”



Spitting and coughing off the side of the dock, Darren wiped his mouth and grabbed the cooler and lifted it easily from Dimitr’s hands.  “I don’t know… moral support. A pat on the back.”



“Well alright then. I hope that’s not an omen.” Dimitri said.



“Omens don’t exist dumbass.”



Then why were they out here looking for the paranormal? Dimitri left the question unsaid as they walked the short distance to the cabin. Braving the rest of the pier that was so waterlogged it would crumble at any second, they quickly reached the island itself.



The ground was squishy and glued to their shoes, only where firm grass roots held the mud together did it even resemble dry land. This was a place of nature not man, and you could taste that fact in the air. The place stunk sharply of rotting vegetation, but whenever the breeze picked up, the sweet smell of water lily and other swamp flowers washed all the sticky hot air away. Dimitri could only imagine that feeling is what those air freshener commercials try to recreate visually.



They reached the cabin, though shack was a better term for it. The thing was built on stilts, and had obvious new extensions made from cheap pine wood that did not match the original building at all. Though there was working heating, lights, and running water. All recent additions. There was even a generator in a shed out the back too, so they needn’t have brought their own power sources. A critical choice by Dimitri who would have hated to lug around spare batteries for his gear.



“Nice place. Too nice. Pick a better swamp shithole next time yeah? Better footage.” Darren said. He sniffed the air and scrunched up his nose. “Ugh, this place reeks lad.”



He pulled out a lighter and held it out in front of him and said, “Methane check.” Before Dimitri could so much as utter a croak, the idiot flicked a spark. The flame burst to life and held steady.



“Christ’s sake, is that how you check for gas leaks? You could’ve gotten us killed?” Darren tried to grab the lighter, but the Aussie shoved him away.



“Relax mate, nothing happened anyway. Go get the gear ready, I reckon we start looking for creepy shit outside.” His focus now entirely on stocking the fridge with beer and liquor in whatever configuration made them the coldest.



Dimitri sighed but did as instruct. That man would be the death of him.



“I’m not so sure we are even going to see anything man.” Dimitri said, fiddling with the camera’s settings and frowning at a smudge on the lens.



He found the wall plug and made sure to give the camera a full charge before he and it were subjected to Darrens recording whims. Which usually took place about four to five beers deep, that’s when things started catching the man’s attention.



Dimitri was glad he’d picked this place; it had far fewer reviews on the list of haunted swamp houses. Too recent of an addition maybe, or too obviously well made. Though there was definitely potential for the two men to spin up something spooky.



Above every window and door hung a little effigy, some simply evenly shaped sticks tied together by twine, to make out shapes, or other symbols that made no sense to Dimitri. Though they seemed the type of thing his grandmother would hang up to ward away evil spirits. Most likely case: they were local superstitions to add charm to the site. They’d make good supernatural material.



Some of them were macabre, above the main doorway hung a little stick figure with what seemed to be a strip of sackcloth wrapped around its head. Gruesomely, the effigy had a small animal’s ribcage as the frame of its body. Straw had been stuffed into its chest cavity, to give it a bit more mass.



It obviously represented the Bog bodies, though relying on a tale used to scare children for paranormal footage never went anywhere. People wanted ghosts, moving chairs, cabinets opening and other things easily faked by some creative ingenuity and some fishing line. Darren understood that but he had a fetish for authenticity.



Above the door to the bathroom perched a child’s toy, stained white dress wrapped around a doll, stuffing long hollowed out. The hair had been replaced with some of the Spanish moss that draped near every tree. Oddly the thing was wet, moisture coalesced at the ends of its ‘hair’ and dripped onto the floor with a steady tap-tap. Must be a depiction of the bog witch, they were quite well made, and sent a unsettling shiver up Dimitri’s spine.



He took a close-up picture of it, then of the bog body effigy, they might just be part of the atmospheric decor, but he was loath to mess with them. A deeply religious childhood had taught him better than to mess with symbols.



“Fuck yeah lad.” Darren said from the tiny kitchen built into a small offshoot from the cabin. “Mate, come check this out.”



Dimitri crossed the cabin and leaned into the room. Darren squatted by the small fridge and held out his hands to the carefully laid out display of beer and assorted liquors.



“Cool man, is that what you wanted me to show me?”



“Mate, I want you to appreciate the majesty of well stocked beer fridge. There are few finer things on this earth. Except maybe this.” He opened the freezer revealing two boxes of off brand pizza rolls. “God fucking damn, ain’t that a sight. We are living it up in this swamp house.”



Dimitri just shook his head, regretting this whole venture even further. “So do you want to get some recording done now before it gets dark?”



“We haven’t even wet our lips yet man, c’mon.” Darren took a beer, and twisting the lid off he downed the bottle in one go. Even spinning it so it swirled as it poured into the bottomless pit that was his gullet. With a refreshed ‘ah’ he grabbed three more, handing one to Dimitri. “Now I’m ready. Let’s have a poke around.”



Dimitri took the camera’s sling and hung it from his neck, positioning it carefully so it was braced against his chest. Darren rustled around his bag, tossing items across the floor. He found what he was looking for and pulled them out, a pair of top shelf sneakers that probably cost as much as the camera.



“Got to have the kicks on for this one. I got a good feeling mate, the vibes right, real fucking creepy around here.”



“You’re wearing your designer shoes in a swamp?”



“If we get a sighting of this Bog Witch baddie and I’m not in my best, I’m going to be pissed. Besides, if we get footage of anything weird out here, this documentary is going to be make us filthy fucking rich lad. Hey, show me a picture of that witch again.”



Dimitri opened his phone and scrolled to an article where an artist had made a creative interpretation of what the bog witch looked like. A pale woman, white dress clinging to her slight frame, hunched over, greenish dark hair covering most of her body. Apart from what Darren described as thick thighs.



Dimitri could see she had a wet slimy sheen to her skin, and one detail peeked through her hair, revealing a deep haunted socket, with dry cracked mud filling the indents where her eyes should be. The picture did not seem like a genuine depiction, but it must hold some truth to the local myth.



“Damn, I want to bang the Bog Witch. She kinda cute though, hey?” Darren said. “Would you if you got the chance?”



“I don’t think she would look exactly like this picture. I’m sure there are other depictions. What if we see something and it’s a weathered old crone, stinking of swamp water?”



“Smash.”



“That’s foul man.”



“What you choose to believe is your reality. I choose to believe she looks like the first picture.” Darren said, then stepped up to the door, one of his cheap and tacky baseball caps on. It said crocodile hunter in bold gold letters. “Hey what’s this?”



Darren had noticed the bog body effigy, and before Dimitri could say anything, the Australian reached up and grabbed it, pulling it off the nail it had hung from.



“Fuck, don’t touch that man.” Dimitri said, feeling a sudden panic dredge itself up, expecting the worst.



However, As Darren looked the thing over in his hand, flipping it this way and that, no offended local came running, no monster emerged from the depths. Nothing happened. It was just a decorative piece.



It had to be the same for the many different arrays of sticks and twine symbols hung over each window. Likely they represented a local spook or myth, like the bog body and the swamp witch. Glancing at the walls, none were recognizable, but he was no expert on Okefenokee swamp lore.



“Why not? I reckon this creepy little thing will make great material; we should set it up in some of our takes.” He stepped out onto the patio and sat it on the railing, so it was looking out across the small island.



It faced a stand of living oak, wreathed in Spanish moss. The bank of the swamp lapped against the trunks of the grey monoliths; little knobs of wood called the tree’s knees sticking out of the shallow water in tightly packed collections.



Dimitri dropped the issue, what was the problem anyway. Nothing odd was going to happen, nothing ever did. He and Darren had been on plenty of ghost tours and done many camp outs across the paranormal hotspots across the world. Little to nothing more than the usual ever came of it. But Dimitri had a natural unease with all things that go bump in the night.



Though he supposed it’s why he did so well on camera, or at least behind it. Like Darren had said, Dimitri carried enough authentic fear for the emotion to transfer through the screen into the viewer. One needs to believe in your own acting. Darren was not at all the same, filled with bravado and bad ideas, he spat in the eye of ghosts and demanded they haunt him. His overwhelming personality did well on camera, and in the end that’s what matters most.



“Alright, check it.” Darren did some warmup moves, shadow boxing into empty air. His back to the cabin, the grim sack head effigy behind him, slumped over on the railing. “We Rolling?”



“One, two… Go.”



“Oi Oi what have we here mate? It looks like your lad Chad, the Impaler…” Darren immediately followed his embarrassing title with even more cringeworthy hip thrusts, exaggerated with a scrunched-up face. “…Is out in the sticks, stinking of shit. Well, why are we out here in bum fuck nowhere? You might be asking, well I’ll tell yah.”



Dimitri rolled his eyes as Darren leaned in close to the camera lens, head turned at an angle, his rat tail hair hanging down behind him.



“I want to bang the Bog Witch.” He half shouted into the camera. He jumped back with his arms outstretched and let out some approximation of a war cry and then jumped back into scene. “You better believe it mate, we aren’t dealing with any old creepy crawly or prison ghost. Nah, we have ourselves a guardian witch, said to protect an island full of lovely maidens, the daughters of the Sun. They say their music is like sweet birdsong, and they are a damn sight prettier than my unfortunate cameraman…”



Darren grinned wide, making sure to show his slightly misshapen teeth.



Dimitri looked up into the sky, blowing out a breath of tired air. Just glad the clouds where high and the sun was going down, it had been a hot day. Maybe all the flies would all decide to melt away if he was really lucky.



“That’s just it though. I’m not here for no maidens. I’m here for their tag along, their plus one, the sloppy seconds. I want that Bog Witch. Here’s a picture of her.” He paused for only a second, Dimitri would put a picture of said witch in post. “Fuck me, ain’t that a beaut. Real quiet and gloomy, but I bet she is an absolute freak under a couple of layers. So lads, your boy Chad, the Impaler, is going to find and impale this witch. Right after this quick break to talk about today’s sponsor…”



Darren dropped out of character, if only slightly, and grinned. His focus now on Dimitri.



“Do we even have a sponsor?” Dimitri said, stopping the recording. Darren only ever did one take, made recording go fast, if not perfectly.



“Wishful thinking mate. If we get one, it’ll flow easy into the video, if we don’t we can just flash big bold letters saying; No sponsor, we are broke.” Darren laughed to himself and cracked open his beer. “Alright what’s next.”



“Should we have a look at the surroundings, maybe get a few shots of the house. Its almost dusk but I think now is better than in the pitch-black night for some atmosphere.” Dimitri said.



He’d never understood the witching hour stuff, sure it’s the dead of night and all is silent, but dusk? Shadows grow long, the air heavy with the residual heat of the day, bugs chatter then go silent. One by one. The calls of nature drop with the sun, life becomes silent. The hours of change, a shift from one state to the next. Transience made visible to the naked eye, bringing uncertainty.



“Yeah, sounds good mate.” Darren wiped his hands on his pants, his beer already empty, it was full not ten seconds ago. “Uh you do that, I’m going to go grab another drink, and chuck the oven on. I’m hungry, and those pizza rolls are singing a siren song to me. Ah wait you can kind of hear it.”



He held up his hand to his ear, going still and quiet. Dimitri frowned but listened in. A light breeze had picked up again, a taste of fragrant water lilies wafted in, pushing aside the fetid smell of rotting vegetation. Ever so faintly on the air, distant singing could be heard, melodic tongues spinning foreign words. Even distorted by distance, they pulled and tugged on the soul, brought to forefront of one’s mind, great thoughts, ideas, dreams.



Dimitri stood looking out into the swamp, searching for its source, and seeing its beauty firsthand. Dragonflies danced across the gently lapping water, little flitting dances of chitin red, and carapace blue. Wings reflecting the sinking sun, here, there, all across the canvas of ancient trees and peaceful swamp.



“Hey Darren, this is beautiful, we should… Darren?” The man had vanished.



Dimitri snapped his head left and right. He stood alone on the small stretch of island. The spell broke. The wind dropped out, the air went sickly still. The fetid smell of rot on the banks returned. The soft slap of water on dock now tedium, the only sound. No insect chattered, no bird cried its mournful song.



His eyes scanned the surroundings. Nothing really seemed to change, but the dark between trees grew deeper, blackspots in the fading light. Spanish moss became shapeless heads of hair hanging down, deathly still. The distinct tap-tap of water dripping, among the few noises of the night.



Pandemonium from inside overwhelmed the quiet, light poured forth from open windows and doors. Darren clattering trays and slamming doors, swearing to himself all the while, at least there was a constant. On the porch sat empty chairs, rickety and worn. The railing sat empty, waiting to be leant on.



“Darren?” Dimitri called out to him, his words dissipating in the heavy air.



No response, just more vague complaints. Straining his ears, Dimitri listened, trying to pick out word from noise, when he heard it. A distant chatter, the familiar sound of combustion, of mankind. A boat engine was sputtering its song across the waters of the Okefenokee. Some of the tension inside him ebbed away, but not all.



It drew closer still, the sound of the engine more defined, and with it the energy of music. Country music, maybe a bit of banjo, played from a speaker, as it was not an authentic twang. A twist formed in Dimitri’s gut as they rounded a corner in the distance, the dark shape of the vessel, and its spotlights cutting across the swamp shallows. This is what he had been worried about.



“Darren, we have visitors!”
