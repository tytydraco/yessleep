Living in eastern Europe, there were a lot of folk tales designed to keep the youth of our isolated town safe. Though as kids we feared the boogiemen in those legends, for the most part, we never really believe them wholeheartedly.

Stories about creatures stealing from your bag if you didn’t zip it all the way up, or animals sniffing round the house if you left anything on your plate, were thinly vailed excuses for good behaviour.

That being said, many tales had a different message, one centred on sticking within the bounds of our town. Each one emphasised to never walk the streets of our town alone.  

\-

Walking home from school with my younger sister in early December, the breeze sang a shrill song as it cut through our oversized jackets. With the dense clouds overhead blocking out a single corner of that otherwise pearly blue sky, we could tell a storm was brewing.

Not unnatural for this time of year, we pressed on up the path between our neighbouring streets. Finally pushing free from that narrowing gap, we emerged onto the final concrete path of our town.  

To the left, small soviet style houses were lit by the dim fires their inhabitants clung to, while out right was an endless expanse of birch trees, the first two layers only stumps. Bridging the gap were short squares of farmland, though I’d never seen any animals in there.

Remembering those stories and heeding our grandparents warning, we held our gaze on the freezing pavement, quickening our pace.

Glancing over my shoulder to see if Lena was keeping up, I was surprised to see her motionless a couple of meters back, staring to our right and into the treeline.

Tracing her gaze across the field ahead and into the grove, I was unable to figure out what had entranced her so much.

Shaking it out of my mind, I grabbed her by the hood and hurried her along, though that laser like focus remained. In my haste, my footing gave way under the lubricated frost layer as I toppled to the floor.

That simple act seemed to pull her back to her senses as she turned to laugh before rushing the rest of the way home, as I joking chased on behind.

Just as we breached the gate to our home, some deep routed sense of unease welled up as I felt compelled to glace back towards the tree line. Though resistant at first, that pit in my stomach only grew, threatening to engulf me if I didn’t take on final look. So, I did.

Stopping on each individual trunk, my eyes darted across the first layer, expecting to see something, but I was only met with the outstretched visage of those branching silver silhouettes.

Hours later, after eating my dinner and retiring to my bedroom, I reached aimlessly into my schoolbag to find I was missing two books. Retracing my steps, the only thing I could think of was the fall, cursing my decision to zip up only halfway.

Hurriedly grabbing my coat, I opened the door to the sight of rapid snowfall. Though it could only have started a short while ago, I knew my search would likely be fruitless.

Ignoring my mother’s concerned questions, I bolted out, losing my footing again on the now hidden ice, beneath the freshly laid white blanket.

Kicking my feet through the snow, that nipping cold crept up my finger, as I buried them deeper into my packets. I knew my eyes wouldn’t be able to cut it, especially with the lowering visibility and one tone sheet of snow.

Focused so intently on my feet, that feeling crept up on me once more, however this time I didn’t hesitate. Swilling round as I tried in vain to locate the presence I felt, my eyes again fell on the tree line.

As the wind swept the branches of that bare canopy, they seemed to sway to the howling rhythm it screeched. Content, I was prepared to turn back to my task, but the lack of movement from one tree-like shape threw me off.

There, assimilated into the grove of swaying silver birches, was one stationary tree. It was around the size of a person, splaying out its thin winery branches from two long offshoots. For a second they appeared to be hand spanning out like fan and in that moment, I swear they waved at me.

Rubbing my eye and focusing deeper, it wasn’t dancing with the rest, no part of it showing any effect of the storm’s early stages.

Trying to force my mind back to the books before the blizzard picked up any more pace, I couldn’t stop myself from periodically glancing back. Before long I was stationary, facing fully towards that uncanny silhouette.

Blinking, the cold bit harder at my extremities as my vision was so obscured so much by then, I could hardly see the treeline ahead.

No wanting to get frostbite, I sacrificed my books to the elements and turned homewards. By then the storm had picked up and no matter how hard I tried, walking into the full force of the gale had slowed me.

Dragging my feet through the ankle high barrage of powdered snow, I used the wall and railings of the adjacent homes, to guid me back, though that feeling didn’t dissipate.

I’d tried to push it out, rationalise it, but now there was no way of detailing anything further than a couple of meters in any direction.

All of a sudden, a voice called out, breaking through the howling wind. Something between familiarity and a warming comfort, that lulled me to turn to my right, leaving the crutch I’d clung to.

Guided by that sound I took three steps, before its silhouette came into view. No closer than a rooms length from me was a thin outstretched shape. If I’d squinted anymore, it could have been my parents or even my sister, but as my eyes acclimated, its branching limbs came into full view.

Only this time, it wasn’t stationary. Each lurch on unseen legs had its splayed ends reaching closer and closer, though my mind was paralyzed by the calming voice it wore.  

Twisting and fleeing frozen feet, my body moved on its own accord. Though I could hear that voice in pursuit, I dare not turn back to give a more vivid form to that splintering, creaking shape.

Each step on numb digest had me teetering on the edge of collapse, just barely holding my footing, though I knew one slip would be enough for its wooden net of branches to snap shut.

And there just up ahead, a golden light guiding me as voices shouted out my name into the blistering torrent. Unlike my pursuer, I could make out their faint images as they stood under the porchlight.

Each encouraging shout driving me that bit further to the salvation I hoped my home would bring. In retaliation the thing behind screamed out, shredding the mask of safety in a horrendous death rattle.

Leaping the gate and stumbling up to the door, my parents gripped my frozen jacket and threw me inside.

Sputtering there on the chipped floorboards, my eyes rose to meet their terrified gazes. Though they ask no questions and shushed my tired explanation, the warmth they radiated thawed my panicked mind, as I collapsed in the ensuing adrenaline crash.

\-

I got the next day off school, which Lana wasn’t happy about, however she welcomed the walk down with our mother. Sitting at the dinner table with my father glancing out the door, I again expected to be chewed out, or at least get one of those disapproving looks.

Though my father didn’t speak until my grandparents shuffled through the door, I could see the worry on his face as he step out to meet a large group of other men. Most I recognised for the town, each clenching a hatchet as they strode across the street.

My grandparents sat with me, played boardgames and made me a hot chocolate, never bringing up the previous evening’s events, even when I tried.

It stayed that way, no matter how much I wanted to question the newest line of stumps pushing back the treeline, I knew they only encroach on us again.

I learnt to heed those stories, reiterating them word for word to my own children when the time came.

I still catch myself glancing out the window on stormier afternoons, counting the seconds till they make their way up that narrowing path.

I can only hope their gaze doesn’t wander into the ridged hills and winding groves that surround our little town. Who know what lies beyond those silver stumps.
