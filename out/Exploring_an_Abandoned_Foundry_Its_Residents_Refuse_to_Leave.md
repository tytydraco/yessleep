We arrived in the town of Farfield this morning where the foundry resides. Farfield looked to be just as abandoned as the foundry itself ocming in. To our surprise, Farfield was still home to many residents.

Much of the town's history, from its founding to financial collapse, was tied directly to the foundry. The corporate city was designed primarily to hold its workers in captivity disguised as an American small town. Most families left after the foundry ceased its operations in the 1960s. Though official reports say it shut down due to air pollution problems, the mystery of the foundry lives rent-free in the minds of Farfield's residents. Entering the town this morning, very few people could be seen, and those who we could approach didn't have much to say.

Thankfully, we met with the owner of Farfield's self-described greatest (and only) diner. Jed, the owner of the establishment, and his family lived in the town for several generations, often feeding the workers of the foundry from dawn until dusk. The food of the establishment was good enough, but the stories Jed's family collected over the years were invaluable.

Eliza and I recorded our conversation with the old man for a few hours. There's too much to transcribe, most of it nonsense, but I can share enough of our conversation to get the point across:

We were grateful to be offered a place to stay at the Abermar Inn, which was simply Abermar's old family manor before his death. The caretakers were excited to hear of our interest in the town and comped the few nights we'll be staying.

To get there, we'd have to pass by the foundry's compound, so Eliza and I decided to explore a bit to get some B-roll before we check-in. Might as well be efficient with our time.

We started through the residential neighborhoods leading up to the foundry. Despite the town's dilapidation, there was a strange symmetry between them. The town's zoning is built on a massive gridlike system. We passed rows upon rows of small houses, with each street and alleyway leading to the main road going directly to the foundry.

None of the houses were impressive. You could tell they were built for efficiency, small spaces. It felt less like a town and more like a workers camp, cramped, grimy, and uninviting. Most looked unlived in, unsurprisingly. I could almost imagine the workers getting up like clockwork to head towards their grueling shift, the weight of burden dragging them down with each step towards their place of employment. The sheer number of these doll-house abodes made me wonder what Farfield must have been like in its heyday. While most here suffered accidents on the job or were slugging through 12 hours of unregulated hell, for the most part it was probably just like any other small town. I can't imagine what growing up here was like. Was there even a school? The only place we saw for socialization was Jed's diner.

Upon arrival, it wasn't hard to find the scale of the Abermar Foundry truly impressive. The sheer size of the property seemed to go on for at least three miles. Much like the town, the property had an eerie symmetry looking directly head on. Front and center was the industrial complex, filled with dozens of silos, smokestacks, with a massive blast furnace the size of a small tower. And I mean *massive,* like one of those old school NASA rockets. The titular furnace itself stood prominently in the center of the facility, with ladders, catwalks, and steel pipes coiling around its frame. From our notes, this is where the pig iron was smelted, refined and processed before being stored in the silos up front., with some spilling out old residue from its bygone purpose.

Grey towers stood idly by, blackened by the residual pig iron stored inside them. For some reason, they had never been emptied. Metric tons of the raw iron just sat there for decades. Probably worth a fortune, I wonder why they didn't sell it beforehand? Let me know your thoughts in the comments.

Fun fact, it was uncommon back then for a foundry to have its own blast furnace. This is because foundries normally only mold iron and metal, rather than process the ore itself. Abermar was revolutionary for streamlining the pig iron ore processing directly to its foundry facility. Though the foundry didn't make complicated pieces of iron products, it had molds for major products for industry at the time, mostly steel beams for buildings and railway parts. This new model and lack of diversity in its products was probably the real reason for the foundry's financial distress.

We continued our trek towards the foundry building, where the iron casting was actually performed. The foundry warehouse was smaller than one would guess. It was less imposing than the preceding furnaces, but its decrepit form splayed out across the compound. Large iron doors were accessible from each side of the warehouse, leading into racks of casting equipment, massive iron drums, and smaller furnaces for high-alloy carbonization. Heavy steel beams canvased over the old workers lines, matched with swaying chains for extracting each oversized finished product. We filmed some B-roll on top of the elevated platform where the manager would have supervised the workers pouring molten steel day in and day out. I recorded our brief exploration, directing Eliza as we highlighted where the steel beams were created and a furnace of where a man was supposedly thrown in against his will by a jealous husband.

Black mold and steel caked the walls inside the foundry warehouse. With our ventilation masks, we were protected from any residue or infections in the air. Nothing was on the walls of the foundry, besides the miscoloration from decades of chemical residue and scars from foundry machinery. Holes from aged rust left open wounds scattered across the warehouse's exterior like a plague victim. It felt as though we were rats troughing through a corpse. No wonder neighboring towns consider this place haunted.

Heading further south laid the workers' administrative offices, colloquially called the 'barracks' due to workers often staying days on end to maintain the facility's business and operations. Tall, dark, and narrow, the white brimmed shacks stretched as far back as one could see, forming a grid-like pattern much like the town. Symmetry was a massive component to Abermar's early designs, it's interesting to see it stretch into urban planning.

Between each barrack sat small courtyards, some with little plaques showcasing a former worker's valiant efforts in administrative support, others with small steel tables for the occasional lunch break. They looked hardly touched, likely from the no breaks policies that were self-imposed by the anxiety ridden clerks and staffers running round the clock. The furnaces and the foundry were impressive and hosted the majority of its abused workers, it only made up roughly forty percent of the compound combined.

The barracks stretched much further and served as the administrative, management, and legal wing, along with everything a township may require. The parallel hallways were key in its design, with each of the four barrack rows stretching across the compound's rocky ground. Each barrack row was named "Quincy", "Adams", "Washington", and "Harrison", from west to east respectively, and incorporated exactly nine buildings included in each facility. Its design was reminiscent of old box car trains I'd see sitting in those abandoned stations.

I hoped out of our car to take more B-roll through the hallway. I was tired at this point and didn't want to explore more than I had to until the evening. Eliza took the car down past the barracks to record herself and set up some cameras facing north from the barracks' south entrances--a noted hotspot for paranormal activity.

I entered the North side of the Western barrack line, "Quincy". Walking through the doors of each long hall felt like entering a different apartment in an inner city complex. Each space felt the same, with similar furniture sitting along the walls, but all had their own flair. I took the quincy path.

The narrow halls weaved through one another, with each wooden door frame between the subsequent halls. I walked by old oak desks and dusty glass. Perhaps an old bank teller or a financier. Every corridor of each barrack seemed to serve a separate purpose; from financial administration, mess halls, cafeterias, showering, prayer, shopping (with company store tokens, of course), clinical, fitness, pleasure, entertainment, legal, and more. There wasn't a mayor of Farfield until Abermar's death, so many of the barracks were used for legistical purposes for both the workers and the townsfolk.

No talks about how disorienting symmetry truly can be, especially as each new building looms closer in on you. Walking a straight line, the constant question of what lurks behind the next corridor rakes behind every door. I'm not one to believe in ghosts, but with a location like this anything seemed possible. Looking forward could be a new world of hell, with my only options of escape being to move left and right to a parallel corridor until I reach the end.

I woke up an hour later. The welt on the back of my head still pulsated, a stinging sensation racking my mind with each throb. Once my eyes adjusted to my surroundings, I was met with a decadent (albeit tight) display of the Abermar Manor's parlor.

Somehow, I was dragged into the inside of the building and gently placed inside to rest. The old Victorian style room was familiar. I've seen similar rooms in past expeditions and at old tourist spots in England. The only difference with this one is that it looked neat, *too* neat, as though it were freshly built yesterday.

I recollected from Jed that Abermar was well known for his fanciful tastes. The ornate decorations were proof of this, with satin walls brimming with old paintings and portraits of unknown battles and histories. The furniture was an odd mix of old victorian style recliners and turn of the 1910s, mission style chairs, like two centuries bleeding into one another. The layout was like any other home you'd expect, with a low table in the center of the room surrounded by an assortment of random seats. I, somehow, was placed delicately in the warm palm of the chaise placed diagonally in the corner of the room, next to the bookshelf.

A knock came from the door closest to me.

The same tall figure lunged in. Though my panic leaped once more, it was soon stiffled once I saw the cheery smile of the hotel manager, Roddy.

Roddy was the caretaker of the Amermar Inn and a former butler to the Abermar family back in the day. He's never left Farfield, though he insists that he wouldn't want to leave even if he could. Too many memories in the manor, he shared.

Roddy was an elderly black man, about 70 if I had to guess. He claims didn't fit in much growing up apparently because of his freakish height (7' 3"), and scared folks just by walking around town. He earned a job at the manor mostly because he could reach places no one else could at the time, and soon became a fulltime employee for Abermar's personal staff.

He felt terrible startling me as he did back at the barracks, and assisted Eliza to get me into the room I'm in now. Eliza checked us in, and turns out we're the only residents staying. Go figure.
