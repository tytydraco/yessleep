I think… God, I know this sounds ridiculous, but… I think my neighbor is a serial killer.

Yeah. I know. It sounds completely absurd.

But I have my reasons.

Just… hear me out. And then tell me if I should do what I’m planning to do.

The apartment complex where I live has always been a perfectly balanced place. Not quiet—that would be an imprecise simplification—but stable.

The neighbors followed predictable routines. The flow of cars outside moved in consistent patterns. Even the nighttime noises had an identifiable frequency. Everything fit within acceptable margins.

Add to that a reasonable rent—unusually reasonable by current standards—and you get an optimal environment.

Perfect for someone like me.

A digital security systems developer. Detecting anomalies isn’t just part of my job… it’s who I am.

But all that calm, all that order, started to change when he moved into the apartment across from mine.

I remember that day was cloudy. It’s not a relevant detail… but I remember it clearly.

When I found out there would be a new tenant—and more specifically, that he would be taking 405—I knew I had to meet him.

Introduce myself, observe, establish a baseline first impression.

That kind of thing.

I found him next to the moving truck, smiling and chatting with the workers who were unloading his things.

An average-built man. Neither fat nor thin. Slightly below average height. Nothing remarkable.

His face… well. I don’t want to sound rude, but it was the kind of face your mind wouldn’t be able to place in a crowd.

Normal wrinkles. Crow’s feet. A slightly receding hairline, the early signs of his thirties.

He introduced himself with a handshake.

The leather of his gloves brushed against my skin with a faint squeak.

"Matt Revers. Financial advisor for small businesses."

No accent. No variation in cadence. No irregularities.

A… flat voice.

I helped him carry a few things until other neighbors joined in. Thanks to that, we finished the move quickly.

During all that time, I couldn’t help but notice something strange—but not strange in the usual sense. Or rather, it shouldn’t have been strange for others, but for me, it was.

There was nothing.

Matt didn’t have a single distinctive trait.

Not one.

Which is strange. I don’t know if you’ve noticed, but everyone—absolutely everyone—has some kind of defining quirk.

It doesn’t matter how simple you think you are, how normal—you still have your own little peculiarities.

A way of dragging out words.

A pattern in the way you walk.

A repetitive gesture.

Even the way you dress says something.

Always.

But him… no.

I don’t know if it’s just my obsession with details or something else, but it made me pay a lot of attention to Matt.

For several days, every time we crossed paths, I observed him.

His clothes were very normal, nothing that could say anything about his personality or supposed job.

I don’t want to sound rude if someone here actually works in what Matt said he does, but I’ve met quite a few of them, and they usually dress in a way that reflects their profession. Some in elegant clothing, others in suits.

Not him. He wore dark blue jeans and a long-sleeved plaid shirt with a red pattern, fading into a tone between red and black, ending in black.

That doesn’t mean he looked weird, though. Not like those odd people in movies or shows where you can tell from a mile away that someone’s a psychopath—because they’re antisocial, quiet, and act strange.

No. Quite the opposite. He was very sociable, always greeting the neighbors politely. He helped others. He went jogging. He even helped me once when I had an electrical issue in my apartment.

Matt was pleasant, but he never went beyond basic conversation.

Do you get it? It was strange how not strange he was.

Like I said, that’s what made me notice him at first. But it was more like a small splinter I couldn’t pull out of my foot.

Weeks passed like that, until two months had gone by since his move.

All that time, Matt remained normal.

He greeted people.

He helped.

He went jogging.

Nothing out of the ordinary changed in the building. Once the novelty of the new tenant wore off, we all returned to our daily routines.

It wasn’t until that night, two weeks ago…

Friday, July 26th, at 23:00, a thunderstorm started in the area, so sleeping was a hard no for me. So I decided to do the most productive thing possible in that situation.

Work.

I started coding for a program my team was in charge of. If I made enough progress, I could surprise my boss. Besides, the next morning I’d have to fix a few lines of my coworkers’ code anyway.

So from 23:00 to 3:00, I was programming, testing, debugging, and hunting for bugs.

Until my body decided it was time for a break—to take care of business, grab something to eat, and sleep, since the storm had finally calmed down.

These apartments are oddly designed. It’s always given me anxiety not being able to organize things properly.

The kitchen is near the door, with very little space. The counters are built in a way that doesn’t allow you to play around with object placement, and the outlets are even worse.

Because of that, when I moved in, I couldn’t place the refrigerator where it should naturally go—near the stove. Instead, I had to put it close to an outlet, leaving it near the hallway, almost next to the door.

When I opened the fridge to grab some lettuce and cheese for a sandwich, I heard a faint noise.

A series of dry sounds, almost inaudible if it weren’t for the silence that always follows a storm.

It reminded me of the sound my meat knife made when my mom accidentally dropped it onto the carpeted living room floor—after deciding it was a good idea to use it as a wire cutter, completely ignoring its natural purpose.

Then the wood creaked slightly, like when someone shifts their weight onto one leg and crouches.

Curiosity got the better of me.

I wanted to see who was wandering the hallways at that hour—partly to satisfy my curiosity, partly to make sure it wasn’t an intruder. Even if the area is safe, you never know when someone might try something.

I looked through the peephole. At first, all I saw was darkness.

It took a moment for my eyes to adjust, but when they did…

I saw Matt—my normal neighbor—carefully picking something up from the floor, trying not to make any noise.

I honestly couldn’t tell what it was.

He seemed to be cursing his luck, though I couldn’t hear clearly because of the darkness.

I stepped away from the door slowly.

I don’t know if it was instinct or what, but something told me I shouldn’t let him know I was awake—or that I had seen him.

Back in my room, I lay down and tried to analyze what I had seen.

It was Matt—I was sure of it. His build was unmistakable among the neighbors. In the middle of the night, trying not to be heard. Picking up something metallic from the floor.

What could he have been doing?

Those questions kept me awake until it was time to go to work.

When I left, with the image of Matt still fresh in my mind, I paid closer attention to the hallway.

There, on the reddish carpet with black edges and orange line patterns, was something that shouldn’t have been there.

I knelt down. On top of one of the orange lines, there was a cut.

A very subtle one, almost imperceptible, revealing faint glimpses of the wood underneath.

That hadn’t been there the night before. I would’ve noticed.

Looking closer, I realized it was exactly where Matt had been. The mark was probably where that metallic object had fallen.

I don’t know why I did it. Maybe I wanted to confirm something, or maybe it was just my obsession with knowing every detail of this place—but I lifted the edges of the cut.

Underneath… there were dark red stains. Almost black.

And on the wood, I saw a small chipped mark.

I spent some time working in my father’s carpentry shop, so I can recognize the mark of a sharp object hitting wood—even if I were blind.

And that… was clearly the mark of a blade.

Then I remembered the noise from earlier that night.

I stood up immediately and left for work.

It couldn’t be that Matt had dropped a knife. It had to be something else.

…But what?

At the end of the day, I came back to my apartment.

On the way, I noticed something else.

The carpet no longer had the small cut I had found that morning.

Looking closer, I saw that the spot had been repaired—stitched with threads just slightly off in color.

Someone had fixed it.

Was it Matt? Or maybe old Mrs. Rosewell? She loved sewing and repairing things.

… I couldn’t tell.

Just as I was taking one last look, I heard the wood creak behind me.

Matt’s voice, with that same neutral tone, asked me what I was doing.

When I turned around, I felt a small chill.

I couldn’t say why.

I felt like I had to lie. Something quick. Something believable. Something that wouldn’t make him ask too many questions.

I told him I had lost my keys.

He replied with a simple, "Need help?"

I immediately looked back at the floor, touched the carpet a bit, then stood up saying I had found them.

We both laughed—but I could tell it was uncomfortable on both sides. I think he realized I didn’t want to talk.

I said goodbye and went into my apartment.

As soon as I closed the door, I looked through the peephole again. Matt was gone. Probably back in his apartment.

My heart was racing.

Could it be that the man I had labeled as ordinary had more beneath the surface?

That same night, I decided to do something pretty disturbing—but I needed to know what was going on. I was going to spy on him through the peephole.

At 23:00, I grabbed a chair and waited, just as planned. About an hour later, his door opened.

Silently. Not a single sound.

In the darkness of the hallway, Matt appeared. I couldn’t clearly see his clothes, but I assumed they were dark. What stood out was that part of his face was covered by a ski mask.

From what little I could see, he was carrying what looked like a large bag… or maybe a sack. It was hard to tell in the dark.

With the same silence, he walked down the hallway, barely making the wood under the carpet creak.

Come on—you can’t tell me that isn’t strange. Who goes out like that at that hour?

I mean, going out at night quietly? Fine. You don’t want to bother the neighbors.

But that—combined with carrying a large bag with who knows what inside, heading somewhere unknown, dressed in dark clothes to blend into the night? That screams suspicious.

I considered that maybe Matt was involved in something shady. Robberies? Drug dealing?

There was only one way to find out. Wait.

For the next few hours, I stayed glued to the door. Occasionally I’d take breaks, but I kept my ears sharp for his return.

Eventually, he came back.

Like before, he walked softly down the hallway.

When he passed the peephole, I noticed something. His figure was clearer now—not just because my eyes had adjusted, but because he wasn’t wearing the same clothes.

Now he had on a gray hoodie. His face was visible. And the bag—definitely a large bag—looked lighter. That would explain his quieter steps.

Before he closed the door, I saw his lips moving.

He was talking to someone.

And… there was a patch on his cheek. A small reddish spot.

Then the door closed.

I quickly checked the time: 4:15. He had been out for almost four hours.

I couldn’t sleep after that. I stayed awake until it was time to go to work.

But I called in sick.

Something inside me was pushing me to keep digging. I think, in my mind, I had already started believing Matt might be dangerous.

Without wasting time, I searched online.

I went to the police website, to the missing persons section.

In the last two months, there had been over fifty reports.

I’m not stupid—I didn’t jump to conclusions. Disappearances are common in cities. Many aren’t even real cases—just stupid teenagers and worried parents.

So I spent the rest of the day noting dates and locations.

And that’s when I found something that made my blood run cold.

At first, the data was as expected—various types of disappearances, spread across different parts of the city.

But as I filtered the data, a pattern started to emerge.

Out of the fifty, about twenty were within a 400-meter radius of my building.

Still could’ve been coincidence, right? The dates didn’t match. Some were from six months ago, others from a year… and a few from two months ago.

Two months…

I kept comparing dates and locations until…

In the last two months, there had been six disappearances. And the radius had shrunk—to about 100 meters from my building.

The most recent was a fifteen-year-old girl, Maritza—a student who had gone out to a local bar. Reported missing Saturday, July 27th, at 10:00.

If that’s true… wouldn’t that have been the day after I saw Matt drop that object?

… Maybe I’m losing my mind.

That’s what I found.

Now I’ve spent hours thinking, analyzing, trying to remember anything else unusual about Matt.

But nothing. Nothing! I can’t think of anything else.

It’s starting to frustrate me.

But… I think I have a solution.

Even though this building doesn’t have internal cameras, it does have a recording system at the entrance, facing the street.

I think I might be able to access it. I’m not sure, but I’d bet the system is old—I could try to get in and check the recordings, compare dates.

I might even catch Matt on one of his “night trips.”

But… if I get caught, I’ll be kicked out. I’m sure of it. The landlord isn’t someone you mess with.

That’s why I need your advice. Should I do it? 

Maybe this is just paranoia.

Maybe I’m seeing patterns where there aren’t any.

But… what if I’m right? Wouldn’t it be my duty as a citizen to find out and report it?

I think I’m going to do it… unless I come up with something better."
