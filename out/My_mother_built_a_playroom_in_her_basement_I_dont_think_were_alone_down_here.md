My six-year-old son and I moved back in with my mom a week ago.

My mom lives in the upper peninsula of Michigan, so she turned the basement into a playroom to help deal with the horrible winters. But nothing could prepare me for how… *elaborate* it was.

As soon as Aiden saw it, he squealed—but I froze.

It was... a *lot.* A huge wooden fort, raised off the ground. A green plastic slide leading from it to a massive ball pit. A yellow brick road painted on the floor, that melted into the Wizard of Oz mural on the west wall. Each wall was different—the others were Lion King, Snow White, and Rudolph the Red-Nosed Reindeer. And there were not one, but *two,* plastic playhouses across from the fort.

Honestly? It was almost a little creepy, how elaborate it was.

My mom and I don’t have the closest relationship. We used to be close, but she never approved of my (soon to be ex) husband Robbie. And she was never crafty or artsy. Did she hire people to do all this? She was as miserly as they came.

And while the basement was souped up to the max, the lighting was still depressing. Three bare bulbs, fighting to light the space. The bright colors of the ball pit in dim shadow made the place feel more like Five Nights at Freddy’s than Chuck E. Cheese’s. Shadows pooled behind the slide and the fort, and I wondered if my mom had ever eradicated her mouse problem.

I watched as Aiden slid down the green slide, into the ball pit, screeching with laughter. “How much did this cost, Mom?” I asked, turning to her.

She waved her hand nervously. “Don’t worry about it.”

“No, seriously. How much did you spend?”

She shrugged. “I did it myself.”

“You did not,” I said, breaking into a smile. “Come on, you can’t draw a Simba to save your life.” I gestured to the Simba walking next to Nala, as they entered the Elephant Graveyard. It wasn’t perfect—there wasn’t much shading and the faces were a little screwy—but it was way better than my mom could do.

Hm. Elephant Graveyard—interesting choice. Actually… all four murals were a little morbid. I stared at Snow White, leaning out of her cottage, reaching for the shiny red apple the witch was holding up. Definitely not the fairest one of all anymore, with one eye an inch higher than the other and her arm twisted at an impossible angle.

“Okay, I had a little help,” she admitted. “But seriously. Don’t worry about it.”

Honestly, I was grateful. With no friends here and a bitterly cold winter ahead of us, this would be absolutely amazing for Aiden. I smiled and gave her a hug. “You didn’t have to do it. But thanks, Mom.”

As I hugged her, I stared at the corner shelf stacked with dolls, including a Dorothy with a miniature Toto, and Belinda, my favorite doll from when I was Aiden’s age. They stared back at me with glassy eyes.

\*\*\*

The first few days were phenomenal.

I felt so free. Like this was the first day of the rest of our lives. The divorce was almost final, I was hundreds of miles away, I was with my mom, and Aiden was having the time of his life in the playroom. Even though we were already under six inches of snow, things were looking up.

Until Tuesday.

After school, I let Aiden down in the playroom while I got some food ready for him. He wasn’t alone more than ten minutes, but when I went down… he wasn’t there.

“Aiden?” I called.

No answer.

“Aiden?”

I spun around.

He wasn’t in plain sight. Probably in the raised fort or one of the plastic playhouses, ignoring me as per usual. “Aiden, please come out, *now,”* I said. “I have your food.”

Silence.

*Something is wrong.*

“If you don’t come out now, no screens for the rest of the day!” I called, panic creeping into my voice.

Nothing.

I hurried over to the fort, hoisting myself up on the ladder. A Snow White book and a Hot Wheels car, but no Aiden. I came back down and wrenched open the plastic door to each playhouse. *Pop, pop.*

Empty.

“Aiden?” I called.

*Tap, tap, tap.*

I whirled around—just in time to see a few balls in the ball pit move. Just the tiniest bit, but enough for me to catch it.

*Oh, great. We’re playing hide and seek now, are we?* I scrambled over, crouched at the inflatable plastic edge, and began digging where I’d seen the balls moving. I didn’t think the pit was even deep enough to fully hide Aiden.

*Where the fuck are you?*

I blanched as my hands hit the bottom of the pit. Dirty white plastic stared back up at me.

No Aiden.

*“Where are you?”* I yelled.

*He’s not here. That must have just been a mouse, or just the balls settling from me running around here.* But something told me to keep digging. He had to be down here—I hadn’t heard him come up the stairs. And the windows were far too high for him to reach.

I kept digging.

And then I saw it.

His shoe.

*Oh God.*

He was lying on the bottom of the ball pit.

*No, no, no.*

I dug wildly, uncovering him. A leg. A torso. Then his face. His eyes closed.

*Oh God he’s dead.*

*Nonono—*

*“Aiden!”* I screamed.

His eyes fluttered open. “Mommy?” he asked, groggy.

I pulled him up and hugged him tight, tears streaming down my face. “What happened?” I choked as I finally pulled back. “What *happened?”*

“I don’t know.”

“Did you fall?”

He thought about it, then shook his head.

“Did you hurt yourself?”

“No. I think I was just… sleeping.”

Sleeping. He fell asleep. At the bottom of the ball pit? With only a thin plastic liner between him and the hard, ice cold, concrete floor?

*He must have hit his head.*

*He must have passed out.*

But then how did all the balls get on top of him?

“Does anything hurt?” I asked.

He shook his head. “I went to sleep.”

My heart began to slow.

Maybe he really did fall asleep?

Maybe he lay down and covered himself with the balls. It was dark and quiet, and he was tired from school probably, and he had low blood sugar probably…

*Maybe?*

I’d heard of kids going missing, the police being called. Only to be found sleeping in some weird place no one checked, like a closet, or in a somewhat dangerous place, like under a bed.

I guess Aiden had occasionally dozed off in front of the TV, or in other slightly strange places… especially when he was sick… but always on a couch or something soft.

Everything about this felt wrong.

“I’m okay,” he insisted, sounding annoyed now. He pushed himself up and looked around. Then he went over to the table, where several of his Hot Wheels cars were lined up.

I was still gasping for breath, still shaking.

*He’s okay,* I told myself. *He’s fine. See? He just fell asleep.*

But everything felt wrong.

\*\*\*

“Did you get the basement tested for mold?”

Mom looked up at me, frowning. “I mean, a few years ago, I did.”

“What about radon?”

“Yeah, we did that too, when we moved in.”

“Carbon monoxide?”

“There’s a detector down there.” She crossed her arms. “You know I wouldn’t put Aiden in any place that wasn’t safe.”

“I know,” I said, hesitantly.

“Is this still about him falling asleep?” she asked, turning her back on me to stir the pot on the stove. “Don’t worry about that. Kids fall asleep in weird places all the time. One time you fell asleep on the floor. Sprawled out, like this...” She flung her arms out. “I freaked out and thought you were dead! But you were fine.”

“I guess.”

I still felt anxious about it. I knew my kid, and my motherly-sixth-sense was screaming. *Something is wrong. Something is wrong. Something is—*

“Your nerves are fried,” Mom said. “You’ve been in survival mode for seven years now. And who wouldn’t be, with that bastard. But it’s over now. You can relax. Not everything is a threat. Not everything is the end of the world. You’re safe now.”

“Robbie wasn’t that bad,” I sniped, even though I hated Robbie with every fiber of my being. Dammit, I hated it when she was right.

Mom ignored me. “The doctor said he was okay. Right? So he’s fine.”

“Yeah.” I’d taken him to the urgent care place, out of an abundance of caution. But the doctor said he seemed completely fine. Chalked it up to the move, fatigue, stress.

Mom moved the pot off the stove. “Soup’s ready. Are you sure Aiden won’t eat it?”

“Yeah. He hates soup.”

“All kinds of soup?”

“Yup.”

“Okay,” she said with a laugh. “Guess he’s more like you than I thought.”

Yeah. Even though Aiden had Robbie’s long, wavy, golden-brown hair and bright blue eyes, he had none of his aggression, none of his anger, and all of my gentleness.

I would do my best to make sure it stayed that way.

\*\*\*

I couldn’t sleep that night.

At about 1 AM, I got out of bed and went downstairs. The house was peacefully silent, and I sat at the table with some weird lemon yogurt my mom had bought, trying to relax. But I couldn’t.

*What if Aiden really did hit his head, and the doctor didn’t notice?*

*He could die in his sleep. Subdural hematoma.*

*Or what if there was a mouse in the ball pit, and it bit him?*

*What if it had hantavirus? Rabies?*

*What if he’d touched or eaten something toxic, and then passed out?* All this stuff was from Facebook Marketplace, according to Mom. For all I knew, the ball pit could have bacteria, mold, lead paint from an old house. The play fort could be covered in asbestos. *Or drugs!*

*Did she even clean this stuff? Vet the sellers at all?*

*The doctor didn’t run any blood tests or anything! We wouldn’t even know!*

I forced myself to take a deep breath. *Don’t spiral. Don’t. If Aiden huffed cocaine off the floor of the playfort, you’d know. YOU’D KNOW.*

Before I knew it, though, I was walking back down the basement stairs. *I just want to see. Then I’ll go to sleep.*

The stairs creaked under my weight. When I got to the bottom, I shuddered; other than the dim golden light spilling out from the staircase, the basement was pitch black. I could only see the wet glint of the dolls’ eyes on the corner shelf. I walked forward, cursing as I stepped on a Hot Wheels. I reached my hand up and flailed for the string.

*Click.*

The light came on, jittering to life. Now, in the quiet of the night, the basement looked ten times worse. Like those photos you see of abandoned play places and theme parks. There was something so disturbing about brightly-colored slides, smiling stuffed animal faces, juxtaposed with silence, shadows, decay. The play fort rose above me, the flying monkey stuffie staring down at me with mean little eyes. The balls in the pit glinted under the harsh light, bright reds, greens, yellows.

I turned on my phone flashlight and walked around the perimeter of the basement first, looking for mouse droppings. I didn’t see any, and the tension in my chest eased up. I walked over to the first playhouse and pushed the door open with my foot. The plastic squeaked. I shone the light inside; gossamer cobwebs gathered in the upper corner, but other than that, empty.

The second playhouse wasn’t empty.

In the middle of the concrete floor sat a shoe. Red and white, with dirty little laces. Slightly smaller than Aiden’s size. I reached in and picked it up. *How did this get here?* It wasn’t ours.

Maybe my mom had bought Aiden a pair of shoes before we arrived?

*But these look used.*

I swallowed and tucked the shoe into a corner. I’d ask her about it tomorrow. I continued my search, climbing up the ladder to the wooden fort and shined all around. Nothing looked amiss there, either. Sighing, I made my way back down (which was much harder than it looked.) Finally back on solid ground, I stared at the ball pit.

I stepped inside.

The plastic balls bumped against my calves. The pit felt deeper than it had earlier—definitely deep enough to hide a child.

I waded through them to the middle of the pit, roughly where Aiden had been lying. The middle seemed even deeper, the balls nearly up to my knees. *It must’ve cost a fortune to fill this.*

I crouched down and dug into them, until I could see the white plastic liner underneath. I half-expected to see a smear of blood, proving that Aiden fell, but I didn’t. I slipped my hand under the balls and moved it in a wide circle around me, feeling for a crease or a slippery spot that might have tripped him.

The balls rolled against my arm, all the way up to my elbow, as I widened the circumference of my circle and kept feeling.

My hand collided with something.

Something that *jerked back* at my touch.

*No no no.* I yanked my hand back and froze. My heart pounded in my ears.

*Something’s in here. Oh God. Something—*

*Plap.*

A red ball in front of me moved.

It bounced down the others. *Plap-plap-plap.* Just that one ball. Not as a mouse scurrying underneath, making them all move. No, it was like something underneath had *intentionally* poked just that one ball.

*What the hell?*

I stood there, frozen, the cold plastic balls pooling around my calves. I couldn’t move. Couldn’t breathe.

*Plap.*

A green one. Spinning and rolling towards me.

*Oh God there’s something in here—*

I scrambled towards the edge. *Plap-plap-plap.* Behind me, getting closer. Something caught my foot. Alive or not, I couldn’t tell. The ball pit flew up to meet me as my arms pinwheeled. *Oh God, no—*

Brightly-colored balls filled my vision.

Then darkness.

\*\*\*

My eyes blinked open.

Bright sunlight streamed in through the tiny windows eight feet up. Cement walls crudely painted. Sharp pain throbbed through my head. *Where the hell—*

*Oh.*

The events of last night came flooding back. I was still in the basement. Terrified, I thrashed out of the ball pit. Wincing in pain, I pulled myself up.

I ran upstairs and checked on Aiden. He was fine—still sleeping, since it was only six AM. “Mom?” I choked out, pounding on her door.

I told her everything through sobs. When Aiden woke up, I went back to the doctor. I was certain he hit his head and blacked out. Just like I had. But he was fine. I got checked out myself, too.

I had a concussion.

When we got home, I went down to the basement and emptied the entire ball pit. It took an hour. But all I found was the other red shoe, near where I’d tripped and fallen. No explanation for the balls moving.

“It was probably a mouse,” my mom said.

“It wasn’t.”

I cleaned the rest of the basement, too. Looked in every nook and cranny. I didn’t find any evidence of anything. Still, I couldn’t let Aiden play down there. Every time I thought about it, my stomach twisted.

“I don’t want Aiden playing down there,” I told my mom.

“You’re freaking out about nothing,” she replied. “He fell asleep after school because he was tired. You got yourself so worked up, you tripped and fell and could’ve gotten a concussion or something!”

“I don’t care. It’s my kid, and I don’t want him going down there.”

\*\*\*

For a few days, everything was fine. Then Aiden came down with a fever. At 2 AM, I woke to him screaming. “Don’t—feel—good,” he managed between cries.

I pressed a hand to his forehead. He felt a bit hot. But with that came relief. *Maybe he really did fall asleep in the ball pit a few days ago. When he’s sick, he’ll fall asleep anywhere.*

I ran downstairs and rummaged for the thermometer. But we hadn’t unpacked all the way yet, and I had no idea where my mom kept hers. *Fuck, fuck, fuck.* Well, at least he didn’t feel too hot. But still...

I ran back upstairs—but when I pushed the door open, I froze in my tracks.

Aiden wasn’t crying anymore. He was just... sitting there, in the middle of his racecar bed, staring at the wall.

“... Aiden?” I asked.

He didn’t turn towards me. Just kept staring. I scrambled over to him, got in his face. “Aiden!”

His eyes finally tore from the wall and looked at me.

“Are you okay?”

“Don’t feel good,” he repeated. And then he started crying again.

After holding him for several minutes, I ran over to my mom’s room and banged on the door.

She didn’t know where her thermometer was, but she got some Advil for him. I gave it to him, and he settled back to sleep.

For forty-five minutes. Even though I’d given him Advil, he kept waking up every hour until dawn. At which point, the fever seemed to have broken, and he decided he felt well enough to watch TV.

*Fuck this.*

I set him up in front of the TV, but I was so tired I could barely move. When my mom woke up, I ran over and begged her to watch him.

“Of course,” she said, going to the fridge and getting out her orange juice. She smiled sweetly at Aiden. “We’ll have fun together! Won’t we?”

Eternally grateful, I hobbled back up the stairs.

\*\*\*

I woke up and rolled over, looking at my phone.

2:13 PM.

*Holy shit.* I’d slept that long?!

I got out of bed, dressed, and went downstairs. “Sorry, Mom, I’m up!” I called, as I zipped up my hoodie.

The house was quiet.

A coloring book lay out on the kitchen table, some crayons scattered around. Aiden’s Hot Wheels were scattered on the floor. There was a plate of half-eaten chicken nuggets on the counter.

“Mom? Aiden?”

*She must’ve taken him somewhere.*

Unease settled in my gut. I leaned to look out the window over the sink—to see both her and my car in the driveway.

*Oh God. What if my mom fainted or something? What if Aiden ran out the door?* I scrambled around— “*Aiden? Mom?!*”

“We’re down here!” a faint voice called.

I stopped.

*Of course.*

The basement.

I thundered down the stairs. Mom was sitting on the floor, reading a book, while Aiden sat in one of the playhouses, talking to himself.

“I told you not to bring him down here,” I said.

“I know. But he was so bored, and he was on YouTube for like two hours,” she replied.

“I told you it wasn’t safe!”

“Will you stop? There’s nothing wrong. Besides, you checked the whole place anyway. Everything is fine.”

I wanted to yell and scream. But. I *had* checked the whole basement, and she’d been stuck with him for hours while I slept. Now that Aiden was sick, it made more sense that he really had fallen asleep for a nap down here, and I really had freaked myself out and fallen and hit my head. Even though I knew I’d seen the balls moving. I’d checked the entire place. No one, nothing, was here.

“Okay, well, thanks. I’ll take over now.”

“Why don’t you get a bite to eat?” she asked.

“I said, *I’ll take over,”* I replied.

“Okay.” She closed her book and headed towards the stairs. I waited for the door to close, then turned to Aiden. I could see a bit of his long, wavy, golden-brown hair poking out of the playhouse window. Bobbing up and down as he played.

“You have fun with Grammy?” I asked.

He didn’t reply.

I scooted closer to the playhouse. The plastic little flowerboxes looked especially fake up close, pointy seams cutting through the lavender and peach-colored plastic. “Aiden, are you hungry? Want to go upstairs and finish your chicken nuggets?”

Still nothing. Which wasn’t unusual when he was really in the zone. I watched the back of his head, through the playhouse window, slowly bobbing.

But then I realized.

There was something... *off...* about the way his head was moving. It wasn’t bobbing up and down, as if he were concentrating on something. It was more... swinging... slowly back and forth.

“...Aiden?” I asked, my throat going tight.

I grabbed the plastic door and wrenched it open.

All the blood drained out of my face.

His hair. His hair taped to a baseball, tied to the ceiling of the playhouse.

Slowly swinging back and forth.

*Oh God what the fuck what the--*

“Gotcha!”

I spun around to see Aiden behind me, giggling madly. “Fooled you!” he said, pumping his fists.

“What the...”

“Grammy gave me a haircut! See?” he asked, pointing to his new, short hair.

I just sat there. Mouth gaping open. “This... this isn’t funny,” I finally croaked out.

“It is funny! Hahaha!” he taunted. “I pranked you!”

I reached in, yanked the ball down, and stormed up the stairs.

“Mom!” I shouted.

She was sitting in the kitchen, eating a sandwich, scrolling her phone. “What?” she asked, but then she turned and saw I was holding the ball.

“Did you tell him to do this?!”

She hedged, seeing how mad I was. “We... did it together. It was his idea,” she added defensively.

“And what about the haircut? Was *that* his idea?!”

“He looks much better with it short,” she said, averting her eyes.

“I don’t care! Did you make him cut his hair?!”

“I *suggested* it. He seemed fine with it.”

I couldn’t believe this. She’d cut his hair. Told him to tape it on this ball. Or maybe did it herself. To do this totally unhinged, sick prank?!

“This is *crazy*,” I said, holding the ball up. “Fucking *crazy.”*

“Okay, calm down, okay?” she said, holding her hands up. “I watched him for like, seven hours. I gave him a haircut. He was bored. I told him we could prank you when you got up.”

“You said it was *his* idea!” I shouted.

“It was. I just told him we could prank you—”

“I can’t believe you would do that to your own—”

I was cut off by Aiden’s yelp. Coming from downstairs.

For a split second, I froze. Time seemed to stop as Mom and I stared at each other.

Then I sprinted for the basement stairs.

I was screaming. But when I found Aiden, he was standing in the middle of the basement, not upset but *laughing.* “Look! Look, Mommy!” he shouted.

I paled.

He was wearing the red shoes.

I thought they were too small for him. But now they seemed to fit perfectly, as he jumped around and grinned. “I got cool shoes!” he said, over and over.

“Take those off,” I said. “*Take them off! Now!”*

“Why?”

“They—they could be dirty.” I dodged for him and grabbed the shoes, tearing them off him. This was too weird. He couldn’t wear some other kid’s shoes.

We went back upstairs and I threw them out. My mom watched me, arms crossed, a concerned look on her face.

\*\*\*

For the next several days, Aiden seemed off. Meltdowns, barely talking to me. *He must be sick,* I kept telling myself. But his fever was gone, and he wasn’t coughing, wasn’t showing any symptoms.

*Something is wrong.*

There are long term illnesses that can happen after an infection. Long Covid. PANDAS/PANS after strep or other infections. Neurological symptoms of Lyme. His behavior was so different and erratic, I wondered if the fever had caused some sort of neurological response.

I took him back to the doctor to get his bloods taken. A full exam. Everything. The most random things were thrown around, from gluten sensitivity to stress from the move. Change the diet. Get him more exercise. Do a mold test. I felt like I was being pulled in a thousand different directions.

But nothing prepared me for tonight.

I woke up with a start around 3 AM. I rolled over, trying to get comfortable. But as I was falling back asleep, I heard a soft sound.

The sound of something rolling across the floor.

I jerked up. Just in time to see a red ball rolling out from under my bed. I watched in horror as it bounced against the closet door and came to a stop.

Hands shaking, I grasped the edge of the bed. I leaned over, peering down. Couldn’t see anything. Just darkness.

Then there was the sound of something shifting underneath me.

Two small hands shot out, fingers curling around the underside of my bed. Pale and gray in the darkness. *“Aiden?”* I whispered.

The sound of cloth sliding against wood.

A tuft of hair poked out from under the bed. Then a pale forehead. Then—Aiden’s eyes, staring back up at me.

Unseeing.

Blank.

“Aiden!” I screamed.

He pushed out further from under the bed. His lips moved frantically, forming whispered words I could barely hear—

*“I don’t want to go I don’t want to go I don’t want to go…”*

I jumped out of bed and grabbed Aiden. Shook him awake. For a minute he remained in the trance, the sleepwalking state, until he woke with a start. “Mom?” he murmured.

I put him back to sleep, sobbing. When he was finally soundly asleep, I ran to my mom’s room. Pushed the door open.

The bed was empty.

“Mom?” I called out, my entire body weak.

“I’m down here,” she called.

I ran down to the kitchen. My mom sat facing away from me, her reflection in the glass door staring blankly back. “Have a seat,” she said, her voice shaking. “I have something to tell you.”

My heart plummeted. I sat across from her, my legs weak. “What’s… what’s going on?”

Her hands shook as she reached for her cup. Took a long sip. “I didn’t get the stuff downstairs from Facebook Marketplace. I got everything… from one person,” she said.

I stared at her, frozen.

“One of my friends knew someone, who knew this woman. Her son had just died. Cancer. She was looking to give everything away. A ball pit, the play fort, everything.” She finally looked up at me. “Now that I think about it, it’s weird. Why would she give it all to me? Why wouldn’t she split it up, or give it to a friend, or something?”

She took another drink.

“When I went over there, we chatted first, and she wanted to see a photo of Aiden. And I showed her. I did.” She blinked back tears. “I’m so sorry. It didn’t seem weird then. She showed me a whole bunch of photos of her kid. And he looked a lot like Aiden. Just a little younger. And he…”

She began to sob.

“He had short hair. And wore those red shoes, every photo.”

“Mom… what are you saying?”

She sniffed. Then her eyes met mine.

“Earlier tonight, I made soup,” she whispered. “He ate it. He ate it all.”

My heart dropped.

“I don’t think that’s Aiden anymore.”
