When I folded the last token and tucked it into the pouch, I still believed the ritual was precise for a reason, that the purity dress, the transient hay, and the bouquet would make the Wall accept her.

My hands trembled with excitement. Last night I couldn’t sleep. Every time I did, I saw the grey tile Wall, open, watching me. This was my first ritual not as an apprentice, but as a preacher, an actual preacher. Since that morning, I had been preparing, crumpling up the tokens, laying the hay beside the church door, hanging a copy of the white purity dress above it. It almost looked like the real thing, but the folds were off, and the whiteness wasn’t as bright. Nothing could beat the original. As I admired it, I heard steps behind me. Thomas, the attendant, stood next to one of the pillars.  
​  
“The dress should be hung two inches further to the left,” he said.  
​  
“Oh, yes,” I replied.  
​  
“You should do it now, preacher. The ritual needs to be precise.”  
​  
“Yes, yes, I will,” I said and climbed back up the ladder.  
​  
“Have you prepared the white purity dress, bouquet, and transient hay?”  
​  
“Yes.”  
​  
I took a deep breath.  
​  
“So it begins.”  
​  
“Yes,” he said. Not excited. Certain.  
​  
I nodded and opened the door, walking to the sanctuary.  
​  
The familiar smell of old polished wood and carpet filled my nose. It made all my muscles relax every time I entered. Looking around, the place was spotless. The windows were clear, the carpet was vacuumed, and the pews and pulpit were freshly dusted. Just the way it should be. To the left of the door, Thomas stood, his face expressionless, cold as always, but I could barely contain my smile. The large wooden door creaked, and I saw the first faces come in. The townsfolk were eager and excited, smiling and talking among themselves.  
​  
The sanctuary slowly filled up with barely any empty pews left. The only ones left were David and Samantha. A beautiful young couple whom I had the pleasure of marrying three years ago. David worked a clerical job at the mayor’s office. It was enough for Samantha to stay home, but the work was demanding and time-consuming. They’ve been coming to me for the past four months to work on and repair their marriage. At first, they would argue a lot. Barely look at one another, but David decided to take on less at work, and recently they had been coming in holding hands, looking at each other the same way as they did the day I wed them.  
​  
“Preacher, has the Wall really opened?” a woman in the front row asked.  
​  
“Yes, it has.”  
​  
“You could be the one,” she said, and turned to her teenage daughter.  
​  
“You know that the one who chooses the lucky token gets to enter the hole and become one with the Wall, merge with the tiles?” I asked.  
​  
The girl smiled and looked at the ground.  
​  
“I might give you the lucky token,” I said and winked. Her still-small eyes lit up, and she grinned at me, excitedly gripping her mom's hand.  
​  
The door creaked again, and in walked David and Samantha, hugging each other around the waist, laughing. David looked around the sanctuary, but his face remained the same, unbothered by the fact that they were the last ones. They slowly walked to their pew, still talking and laughing.  
​  
I walked back to the pulpit and gripped the wooden desk tightly.  
​  
“Fellow neighbours, friends, for those who have not yet heard, the Wall has opened today. As it always does. On time.”  
​  
A few surprised faces showed in the crowd. The people began to talk loudly, their eyes lighting up. Only David and Samantha didn’t change, still whispering to one another, holding their hands.  
​  
“I commend the Choice to start. Please, every woman between the ages of 15 and 55, come to the stage.”  
​  
The women stood up and walked to the stage. I pulled out the pouch, with crumpled-up papers rustling around. The string around the pouch was tied tightly. It took me a few seconds to get it undone, and by that time, a line had formed going down the stairs.  
​  
I wished each of the girls good luck as they drew their tokens.  
​  
The line was getting shorter and shorter, but I still saw Samantha sitting down with David.  
​  
“Samantha, please join the girls.” Her eyes opened wide, she whispered something to David, let go of his hand, and ran up the stairs.  
​  
“Sorry, preacher,” she whispered.  
​  
“It’s okay. Good luck to you.”  
​  
“Thanks.”  
​  
She took her paper and stood at the end of the line, smiling, looking at David.  
​  
“Unwrap.”  
​  
The muffled sound of paper echoed through the sanctuary as the women, one by one, uncrumpled their tokens. I could already see disappointment in their eyes. The teenage girl was looking at her token, her eyebrows furrowed. It was her first time.  
​  
“No luck?”  
​  
She opened her palm - the paper had a large X on it.  
​  
“Maybe next time.”  
​  
She frowned and looked at her mom.  
​  
All the women stood together, holding the open tokens in their hands, but there was still no call of excitement. Then my eyes darted to Samantha. She stood at the end of the line, looking at the ground, her hands trembling, her face pale.  
​  
Was she okay? The Choice was happening, but I had to be sure. I walked over, ready to ask what was wrong, but then my eyes fell on her right hand.  
​  
I understood now.  
​  
She held the open token. A large black square was painted on it.  
​  
“Congratulations, Samantha!”  
​  
The people began clapping.  
​  
“Everything okay…”  
​  
But I couldn’t finish. Through the claps, David’s voice sounded through the sanctuary.  
​  
“No. No! Not Samantha.”  
​  
I narrowed my eyes and stared at David.  
​  
Why was he flipping out? Samantha won!  
​  
“David, she won.” “It’s an honor,” the crowd repeated.  
​  
“No!” he kept screaming.  
​  
He walked out of the pew and began making his way to the stage.  
​  
I looked back. Thomas had already taken Samantha’s hand and led her out the door. She was weeping softly.  
​  
“Get your hands off her!” David was already halfway up the steps when two firemen grabbed him from behind and threw him back on the ground. The bang echoed through the sanctuary.  
​  
I looked around. All the townspeople were looking at each other, silent, their eyes narrow, their lips tight.  
​  
“People, please, leave the Sanctuary, we will meet at the Wall when Samantha’s ready,” I yelled.  
​  
But they didn’t listen; they were talking among each other, looking at David.  
​  
“The Wall had opened today. This is not the time. Please leave immediately.”  
​  
They looked at me and slowly started getting up, still murmuring to each other. I quickly ran after Thomas.  
​  
My steps echoed through the empty hall. Thomas glanced back slightly, then fully turned his head.  
​  
“Preacher, you needn’t come.”  
​  
“I want to be sure Samantha’s okay.”  
​  
“She’s okay.”  
​  
“Thomas, please.”  
​  
He stopped and looked into my eyes.  
​  
“You may not interfere.”  
​  
I nodded and followed after.  
​  
The smell of hay was strong in the preparation room. It looked much different from what I remembered. The walls were painted white with a few shelves spread sparsely around, a large bag of the transient hay, and a dentist's chair in the middle of the room with straps on the armrests. The white purity dress lay neatly on one of the shelves; next to it, the gifted flowers lay.  
​  
Thomas sat Samantha in the chair and began stuffing hay into a pouch he pulled from his back pocket. Samantha stared at me.  
​  
“What’s wrong?”  
​  
“Does it need to happen?” she whispered.  
​  
“Samantha, you should be honored. People dream of being chosen.”  
​  
She closed her eyes hard and grabbed the armrests. Thomas came over with a pouch of hay in his left hand and the dress in his right.  
​  
“Put this on.” He laid the dress over her. She kept her eyes on it, not moving an inch.  
​  
“Put this on,” Thomas repeated, louder, but Samantha still didn’t move.  
​  
“Please cooperate, Samantha.”  
​  
Nothing.  
​  
“We can give you privacy.” I turned around and motioned for Thomas to turn around, too. He stared at me for a second, but then turned around. The chair creaked, and an iron belt hit the floor.  
​  
I slowly turned around, nodding at Thomas. Samantha sat back in her chair, dressed in the white purity dress, gripping the armrests.  
​  
“Open your mouth.”  
​  
Her eyes widened again, and she shook her head vigorously.  
​  
Thomas narrowed his eyes and walked over to one of the shelves. Metal objects rattled as he stood over it, searching for something.  
​  
The rattling soon stopped. He turned around, holding a mouth prop.  
​  
“Thomas, there’s no need.”  
​  
“The hay is part of the ritual.”  
​  
“Yes, but this?” I pointed at the mouth prop.  
​  
“Her mouth needs to open.”  
​  
I turned back to Samantha. “Samantha, please. You don’t want to do it this way.”  
​  
But she didn’t answer, her eyes darting from Thomas to me. Thomas walked closer to her, grabbed her head with one hand, holding the mouth prop, and rubbed his finger on his shirt. Samantha let out a soft wail and finally opened her mouth. Thomas immediately put the mouth prop in. Samantha let out another wail.  
​  
I could feel my hands starting to tremble. Thomas picked up a handful and started stuffing it into Samantha’s mouth. Her wails echoed through the room, and tears rolled down her face. My feet felt frozen on the concrete. The hay. The transient hay. It was necessary, but was Samantha’s struggle worth it? I opened my mouth to speak, but  Thomas already picked up another handful, stuffing more in, filling her mouth. He then pressed the bouquet into her hands.  
​  
“You may stand up now.”  
​  
Samantha’s pupils shook, but she stayed still.  
​  
“Stand up,” Thomas said louder.  
​  
Samantha slowly got off the chair, almost falling, but Thomas quickly caught her and stood her back up.  
​  
I opened the door, staring at the ground, trying to avoid Samantha’s eyes. She walked out, and I followed after her.  
​  
She was still shaking as we walked to the corridor. The folks had already gathered, standing silent in a semi-circle before the open grey Wall, holding their black hoods. They turned their heads as our steps sounded on the concrete floor. Some folks still smiled, but there was something behind their eyes, a glimmer of unhappiness, brought on by David’s scene. He was the person at the center of the semi-circle being held by two large firefighters, gagged, his eyes staring right at Samantha. Her eyes were fixed on the ground, looking at the floor with each step.  
​  
I clenched my jaw and walked on, forcing a smile as we passed the people. Samantha’s shaking grew with each step. The bouquet rattled, and a few leaves fell off it. I walked faster to the hole, leading her into it. Thomas already stood before the hole, getting here faster than usual. He was staring at Samantha. He could also understand this had to be done fast. Thomas took her other hand as I let go. My hand practically slipped out of Samantha’s the moment I stopped holding on. He let her in, stood her facing the crowd, and quickly walked out, nodding my way.  
​  
“We gather here today to thank Samantha for being the gift to the Wall.”  
​  
“Thank you, Samantha,” the town said in unison and wrapped their hands into a prayer.  
​  
“Please, Wall, accept Samantha as the divine gift and bring us safety and prosperity as you have always done.”  
​  
The people whispered after me, their eyes closed.  
​  
“Let us put on our hoods.”  
​  
The folks took their hoods and put them over their faces. The two firemen threw David’s hood over his head and then pulled theirs over.  
​  
David began moving his head, throwing it up and down, the hood moving with it. He threw his head back again, and his hood came over his face a little so that one of his eyes became visible. I opened my mouth to yell at the fireman, but then I heard it. A sound. A faint pat. I didn’t realize what it was at first, but then it hit me.  
​  
I quickly turned my head. A wave of coldness washed over me. The flowers were on the floor. For a moment, I didn't believe what I was seeing, but it was real. Above them, bent over, stood Samantha, shaking violently, her eyes wide with terror. I looked back at David, his eye was still out.  
​  
“David’s hood came off!” I yelled.  
​  
The two firemen quickly searched David’s face with their hands and pulled the hood back down. I quickly pulled my over, my eyes moving nervously in the dark.  
​  
She dropped the bouquet. She dropped it! The Wall. Our dear Wall. I counted to five as usual, but was there a point? Would the Wall close? Would it protect us?  
​  
My hands searched my neck for the hood.  
​  
I slowly took it off.  
​  
The Wall had sealed. It sealed with no problem. The strand of hay stuck out of it, as always.  
​  
Samantha dropped her flowers. But the Wall had sealed.  
​  
“Everything okay, preacher?” sounded from the crowd.  
​  
“Yes…yes. You may take off your hoods.”  
​  
The folks took off their hoods, letting out sighs of relief and happiness. The two firemen let David go. He fell to the ground, his hood still on, barely moving. The folks then, one by one, walked out, a few of them thanking me for my service. After folks left, I still stood there, staring at the Wall, at the strand, trying to collect my thoughts.  
​  
Was this what the Wall really wanted?  
​  
I don’t know how much time had passed before I decided to leave. When I looked at the floor, David still lay there with the hood over his head, unmoving.  
​  
I walked back to my office and slouched down in my chair.  
​  
The Wall is still closed. It closed!  
​  
I put my head into my hands and shook it around, trying to think, THINK. But then the crack of the door pulled me out of my thoughts.  
​  
“Please, I need a moment of privacy,” I said, thinking it was one of the townsfolk coming in for advice, but there was no answer.  
​  
I turned around in my chair, but before I could really see who it was, what was going on, the figure grabbed me by the collar and threw me to the ground.  
​  
I looked up. David’s eyes stared into mine.  
​  
“David, what are you doing?”  
​  
But he didn’t answer. His hands twisted around my neck, and he began to squeeze.  
​  
I grabbed onto the hands to get them off, but the grip was too strong.  
​  
My hands moved over his face, his chin, pushing it away, but David wouldn’t let go.  
​  
“You saw it, preacher. She dropped the flowers, but the Wall didn’t care, ” he said as my consciousness began to slip away.  
​  
Then a loud bang.  
​  
Liquid splashed over my face.  
​  
David’s grip released.  
​  
My mind slowly returned.  
​  
I gripped the carpet and opened my eyes.  
​  
David’s body lay next to mine, unmoving, his eyes rolled back into his skull.  
​  
Above him stood Thomas with a large hammer in his hand, his face expressionless, cold, as always. He took the pouch out of his pocket and wiped the hammer on it. Not hurried, not shaken. As if this were another routine. As if this was part of the ritual.
