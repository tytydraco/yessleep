My requests for a transfer were widely ignored, probably because I was only a bit over a year out of my residency. This wasn’t even considered that good of a hospital. Though, by virtue of being in the heart of the city, it was usually pursued by newcomers so they could have a nearby strip of bars and restaurants and have the same sort of social life they had in college (to a point). I didn’t like going out, so that wasn’t a priority. I liked my job. I liked the patients. I liked the small contributions I could make in getting them better. But I hated the city.

More and more, I imagined myself alone. Able to afford an apartment without a roommate. Not falling asleep and waking up to the sound of cars and construction and chatter. I didn’t always romanticize solitude. I liked the idea of having a cabin, maybe. Somewhere I could be on my own, if I wanted, but didn’t have to be. But now, it was all I wanted. I didn’t tell anybody I felt this way. I knew my parents would be concerned. My friends, too, if I still kept in touch with them. Maybe it was school, maybe it was the job, or maybe it truly was just being in the city, regardless, I felt myself calcifying.

I found out I was chosen for the nocturnist opening at Plainville Community Hospital at the end of September. It was a critical access hospital, kept alive with government subsidies and having a total staff of about a dozen. It served Plainville and its 456 person population, but honestly was more of a holding center, ready to transfer its patients to a different, better equipped location.

I moved out in four hours. I barely owned anything, and my bed frame and futon I just left at the side of the road. I texted my roommate that I was leaving, and that was it. I feel bad in hindsight. They probably had to scramble to replace me. Honestly probably could’ve taken me to court or something. They didn’t, though, thank God. In my 2009 Taurus, I drove three hours across the state. I got to Plainville a bit after 7pm. I found a cheap rental above a bookstore in the town. Though, “town” is probably a strong way to describe it. Plainville was a single main street with a short row of buildings on either side for about a block. A new and used bookstore was in the town’s center, and a studio apartment sat above it. There were two parking spots behind the building, one for the owner and one for the tenant. To get to the apartment I had to enter the bookstore and climb the stairwell in the back. They had already closed when I arrived, but luckily the owner stayed late, waiting for me. We exchanged pleasantries and I went to my room.

The weekend was my time to settle in, Monday my job started. If I was smart, I would’ve started slowly adjusting my sleep schedule. But I didn’t. A nocturnist is a physician on staff overnight. Graveyard shift. For a critical access hospital like Plainville Community, the nocturnist was the only physician on duty overnight. In charge of admitting, providing care, and keeping an eye on every patient, keeping careful note of every occurrence. Based on how I’d been feeling lately, a dream come true.

I laid low and then Monday came. My parents tried calling a few times. Texted in between. I didn’t answer. I told myself it was because I was nervous about starting work, but that was a lie. I knew it was a lie the second I thought it. My shift began at 7pm, and it was only a five-minute drive away. I sat on the couch, eating leftover pizza I ordered Friday night (my trick is to order two pizzas at once so it lasts a few days), then left fifteen minutes before, giving myself ten minutes to ease in. There were maybe six cars in the parking lot, but three of them were in the way back – locals probably used it for carpooling. I was directed to park at the lot’s eastward side, where all the employees had to park. The three other cars were there. My heart was racing. I wasn’t sure why. It was maybe the last year or so that I started getting these episodes. Even for the more innocuous aspects of my life, I’d be anxious. I had mentioned it to a colleague and they recommended I take something, but I wasn’t even thirty yet. It seemed like a big jump to already be on some sort of medication, even anti-anxiety when that had only been a problem as of late. I took a deep breath and exited my car. Headed for the hospital entrance. The automatic doors slid open and I stepped in and on a rug that read in large, printed letters, “Welcome!”

The first co-worker I encountered was Lisa. She was probably in her sixties and was the Patient Access Representative. A fancy way to describe receptionist. She was bubbly and greeted me with a smile.

“You must be Adam,” she said.

I nodded. “That’s me.”

With a strained sigh she stood and held out her hand to shake. “We’re happy to have you. Welcome.”

I shook her hand. “Thanks.”

She nodded back towards the threshold between the lobby and patient care, asking, “Want to get started?”

I glanced towards the vending machine in the lobby’s corner, I had hoped to grab a soda and sit for a few minutes, but now it felt weird to break away. “Uh,” I hesitated, “sure.”

“Great.” Lisa turned and shuffled towards the locked metallic doors, reading her card to scan. She walked slowly and weakly, her feet never truly leaving the floor even as she stepped. It was sweet in a way. She reminded me of my grandma.

At the door, she held her card to the small black box beside the right-side door. The light on its top remained red and it beeped with a “denied” tone. She frowned and tried again. Still nothing. She laughed to herself and tried again. The light turned green with a friendlier “beep” and the doors both opened on their own.

She looked at me and smiled. “Think it’s time I get a new card.”

I didn’t react. She led me into the unit, which I guess wouldn’t be the most accurate way to describe it. The existence of a unit implies other units, like the hospital I came from. Plainville Community only had a single unit. So, I guess, a better word for it would be “ward,” or just the general term we’d use in the city when describing where we work, “the floor.” In this section were all patient rooms and all types of care. This included their MICU, or, Medical Intensive Care Unit, which had a few designated rooms further down the floor, consolidated in the hospital’s back corner.

“We only have one patient at the moment,” Lisa told me, “Derek up here on the left. Gets TIA.”

TIA, the acronym for Transient Ischemic Attack, is essentially a micro-stroke. Derek would have episodes often and became a fixture here. Lisa explained that he slept here more than his own home. His wife had passed a few years earlier, and Lisa supposed that he was just lonely and now overly cautious since his wife had died from a stroke.

Lisa glanced at me just before we reached his room and whispered, “He can talk.” She then stepped into the open doorway and waved. “Hey Derek, I wanted to introduce you to our new physician, Doctor…”

She looked at me expectantly. “Oh.” I cleared my throat. “Doctor Meyer.”

Derek sat up in his bed. He had a shaggy bear and mop head of hair, both colored a mix of black and gray. Seemed to be around 65. He smiled at me and shouted, “Meyer-man, good to meet ya’, how you doin’?”

 “Well, thanks.”

Lisa patted my shoulder, telling Derek, “Doctor Meyer is our nocturnist, so if you ever need anything during the night, he’s your guy.”

Derek giggled and exclaimed, “Alrighty, Doctor Meyer, I’ll warn ya’ now, I’m a bit of a night owl, hope you’re ready to see a lot of me.”

My stomach sank. I already knew what that meant, I’ve met these types of patients before. They want to keep your ear, talking about whatever they want while bringing up various medial concerns in between their random topics so you can’t just walk away from the conversation. Derek seemed nice enough, but I could already imagine him following me around, hassling me with stories from his past and political opinions. I swallowed the concern and forced a smile. “I’m looking forward to it.”

With a brief farewell (for now) to Derek, Lisa continued the tour. The floor as a whole was pretty unremarkable. Fluorescent lighting shined down on scraped up vinyl flooring, and the yellowing white paint on the walls in addition created an overall nauseating, not-sterile-enough feeling. The rooms were occupied by decades-old beds and outdated equipment. Par for the course for these smalltown hospitals.        

Lisa was on my shift, so I’d be seeing the most of her. She liked arriving a bit early to relieve the first shift Patient Access Representative. Not for the overtime pay, but to get in the “work mindset.” We had that in common. I was happy that of all potential types of people I’d have to work alongside, it was her.

The rest of the week went by quickly. Acclimating myself was a bit more of a hassle than I expected. Plainville was much more disorganized than where I had come from, and the other physicians who had been there for years had become lazy in their notetaking and cataloguing. I hardly felt like a doctor, more like an assistant. Or an IT guy.

It was Wednesday when I had the nightmare, one I hadn’t had for a year or so. When I had just begun the city job, a man was brought in at the beginning of my shift. He was homeless, and the other doctors seemed to recognize him. Must’ve been in before. Maybe a few times. He was found unresponsive on the sidewalk not far from the hospital. I was tasked with looking him over. He smelled bad. Had lice. He wasn’t waking up, and then suddenly, he did. His eyes went wide and he began convulsing. Blood dripped out of his nose and from the corners of his mouth, and I just watched. I was scared, and I just watched. Didn’t make a sound. Almost a minute later he went still and flatlined. We couldn’t bring him back. Maybe that’s when I started hating the city.

His name was Michael.

In Plainville I was assembling a routine. During the day I’d sleep, watch TV for an hour or two, and then go to work. My landlord said I could borrow books from the store if I’d like, which I took him up for on Thursday.

On Friday, I should’ve been killed.



It was only my fifth shift, yet I had already fell into a pattern. I arrived for my shift fifteen minutes early and greeted Lisa as I scrubbed the bottom of my shoes on the “Welcome!” mat. I now made it a point to immediately go for the vending machine, purchasing myself a diet cola, and sitting in the lobby, staring out its windows and watching dusk turn into night. Lisa had already gotten into the habit of showing me videos she thought were funny once I arrived. Just before my shift began she’d sit beside me and scroll through what were usually cute cat videos and babies acting like adults. It made her laugh, and she had one of those laughs that made me laugh. Despite us being two people who wouldn’t normally be friends, we were quickly becoming so.

I’d then go into my “rounds,” but at least in this first week, I only had one: checking on Derek, making sure he was comfortable, and taking note of any changes in his condition. There never were. I was going to go to my designated desk for entering the notes into the system, but Lisa called at me through the lobby doors.

“We got one in MICU this morning.”

“What?” I asked.

Lisa took another step in, repeating, “MICU. A young woman was found this morning. Severe burns all over her body. Probably won’t make it to transfer.”

I stood and walked into the hallway, facing Lisa at its end. “Do they know what happened to her?”

Lisa explained that in the morning, a farmer saw a smoke plume rising from the edge of his field, at the tree line. He figured it was some sort of brush fire, but found the young woman burning. He covered her in his jacket to extinguish the flames. He called the cops and an ambulance brought her here. She was to be transferred soon to the city, to a proper burn unit.

“Do they know what happened?” I asked.

“No,” Lisa said, “All they know is that she’s too disfigured to identify, and no one has reported anyone missing in the area. Or, at least anyone missing that matches the description of her.”

“How old?”

“I heard them guess around early twenties. Hard to say, though.”

I nodded, then grabbed my clipboard. “Thanks, I’ll check her out.”

Lisa nodded too and went back towards her desk in the lobby. The doors shut behind her. I spun around and headed deeper into the floor. Rechecking the notes I was left, there was only a mention of someone being brought in, but no other details. Not that they were in critical condition, not that they were to be transferred, nothing. It must’ve been a shit show, no time to jot anything down. The physicians here (not that I knew them well), I could surmise from the overall care they took in their notes and cleanliness that they weren’t necessarily the top in their field. Not that I was anything special. But, I suppose that gave me the authority to recognize shortcomings. For me to have noticed, they must’ve really been incompetent.

I reached the hallway’s end and turned right, the beeping of a heart monitor leading me to the last room on the left. The lights were low and the blinds were closed. Lying on top of the bed was a body wrapped head to toe in gauze. A ventilator stuck in their mouth and a tube hang from their nostril, fed in between two layers of gauze. A yellow puss had already started to seep through despite the bandaging clearly being new. The smell of burnt flesh and hair hung in the air, as did the smell of death. Dead tissue, I guess. They wouldn’t last long. Maybe through the night if they were lucky. Or, lucky wasn’t right. Burns were agonizing. A quick death would be lucky. Now, dying while unconscious would be the best case for this poor woman.

The smell was pungent enough where I stayed near the door, taking my notes from there. Outside of the monitor and the ventilator, the room was quiet. The breaths of the patient were strained and quick, like they were nearly hyperventilating. Through where the bandages met, I could just barely see the shadows of her eyes. I didn’t think she could see or hear me, but still I felt it important to say, “I’m here to help you. I’ll make sure you’re in good hands.”

I watched her for a few moments more, until a voice sharply said over my shoulder, “Quite the circus when she arrived.”

“Oh, fuck!” My shoulders jerked and I turned to face the voice. It was Derek, out of his room, just over my shoulder.

He laughed. “Sorry, didn’t realize you were so jumpy.”

I took a deep breath and lowered my clipboard. “I’m not.”

Derek held his hands up in surrender. “Okay, okay, whatever you say.” He nodded towards the closed blinds. “She started seizing after they put her in here. Figured out it was light sensitivity, probably from all the damage to her eyes. Closed the blinds and she stopped.”

I stared at him. “Were you watching?”

Derek shrugged.

“You shouldn’t be doing that.”

“They didn’t see me,” Derek giggled.

I shook my head and stepped away, heading back down the hallway. Derek caught on, following behind me, headed back for his room. We parted ways there, not exchanging any words. I was headed for the lobby to talk to Lisa, hoping to get a time when the patient transfer was supposed to take place. I scanned my ID badge and stepped into the lobby. Lisa was on her phone. That was fine, it’s not like she had anything else to do.

“What time is the transfer?” I asked.

“Should be soon.” Lisa didn’t look up from her phone.

“Do you have a specific time?”

“Uh…” Lisa slowly lowered her phone to her desk, not taking her eyes off it. “Yes.”

I waited for her to continue. She didn’t. “Lisa.”

“Sorry, sorry.” She rolled her chair closer to the desk and squinted at the computer screen. She scrolled through the day’s schedule. “Around eight P-M.”

“Oh.” I glanced at my phone. 7:29pm. “Great. Thanks.” I walked back to the threshold to the floor and scanned my badge. “Let me know when they arrive.”

Lisa saluted me, loudly barking, “Aye aye, captain.”

I laughed and left the lobby. An analog clock hung in the hallway in view of my desk, and I watched it closely, ready to spring into action. A transfer is always a little nerve-wracking, let alone one for a burn victim (and one as severe as this). Focus was the name of the game. Not that I wasn’t normally focused, but this was my first real test. Screw this up and I may not have a job on Monday.

Minutes passed slowly, as they always do when you closely track the time. I could hear Derek giggling in his room at whatever he was watching on TV, the kind of giggle that was meant for other people, trying to draw someone in to ask, “What’s so funny?”

I wasn’t going to give in.

My fingers rolled on my desk and seconds wore on, the clock finally reaching the turn of the hour. I stood and headed for the lobby. Scanned my badge and left the floor. Lisa was staring at the hospital entrance.

“They almost here?” I asked.

She shrugged. “I thought they arrived a few minutes ago, but they haven’t pulled up.”

My gaze turned towards the entrance and surrounding windows, and faintly on the parking lot trees I could see the turning of a red ambulance light illuminating them. “Huh,” I mumbled. I walked for the doors and stepped onto the “Welcome” mat. The automatic doors slid open and I looked outside. At the east entrance to the lot I spotted the ambulance. The doors were open and it wasn’t moving.

“Do you see them?” Lisa asked.

“Yeah,” I said, “They’re just sitting there.”

Lisa’s chair rolled and I could her feet dragging towards me. She walked up beside me and looked the same direction I was. We both just watched, standing in silence. The breeze was cool and smelled nice, I decided I should get air more often going forward.

Lisa shook her head and asked, “What are they doing?”

“No idea,” I replied.

Again, we stared in silence. The ambulance appeared to still have the engine on, and the headlights were pointed directly at the hospital entrance. The driver and passenger doors being open would tell me that the EMTs possibly had to prep the back or maybe move something out of the way from their path.

Lisa looked at me. “You should check it out.”

I looked back at her. “You don’t want to?”

She smiled and I did too. I took a deep breath and glanced back at the doors to the floor. Derek would be fine for a few minutes left to his own devices, I figured. I sighed and looked back at the ambulance. “Alright, I’ll be right back.”

I left the entry and stepped outside. Lisa didn’t move, keeping her eye on me. The vibe of it all shifted the moment I left the hospital. There was a charge in the air. A sourness. And a quietness. Like every person and animal and insect had left. I couldn’t even hear the leaves shake in the breeze. The only sound was the engine of the ambulance, gradually growing louder with every step closer I took.

My pace slowed the nearer I got. Then my stomach knotted. That same anxiety I had become so familiar with. I dug my fingers into my palms and breathed rhythmically. “Not now,” I repeated to myself. “Not now, not now, not now.”

I scanned the area, expecting to see some hint of where the EMTs went, but if they were still in the area, they must’ve been either messing with me or hiding from something. The moment I thought they might be hiding, the knot in my stomach only got worse. I pushed it out of my head and called out, “Hello?”

I kept perfectly still. Didn’t breathe. Just listened. Silence. After a few seconds more of waiting I approached the ambulance. My eyes were fixed on the open door, rattling alongside the engine. On the edge of the door was when I first saw it. A smearing of red. A red I recognized: blood. I took another step, got a clearer look inside the cab. Blood was pooled near the pedals and over the seat. The passenger side too. Dark and fresh. A coldness ran over my neck and down my back. Someone watching me, it said. I looked over my shoulder and held my breath. Listened again. Or, more accurately, waited. Waited for someone or something to come for me.
