I posted Part 1 on a Friday.

On Saturday morning at 6:47 AM, I received a message from someone else who had seen the channel.

Just four sentences:

*I know what Static is. I’ve been watching it for eight months. I know things about it that you don’t and that you need to. If you’re real, meet me at Pullen Park by the carousel at 2 PM today.*

The username was a string of characters. There was no post history or profile picture, and the account was created three minutes before the message was sent.

I almost deleted it. I stared at the message for a long time. Then, I opened the app and checked the channel. There were 34 episodes. There had only been 29 when I’d written the post, and that was only three days ago. Five new episodes in three days.

I didn’t watch any of them. I looked at the titles and the thumbnails and felt the old familiar pull, the gravity at the edge of the cliff whispering *what if you jumped*, and then I scrolled down to the newest addition. Episode 34.

The title was *The Park.*

The thumbnail was small and compressed, the way they all were, but I recognized the image immediately. It was a wide shot of a carousel surrounded by oak trees in full summer green. Behind the carousel, barely visible through the tree canopy, I could see the curve of a lake.

It was Pullen Park.

I felt something cold settle into the bottom of my stomach. It was the park I’d just been told to visit. The exact location. And there, in the thumbnail, was the carousel.

I didn’t click on the episode. Casey found me still sitting on the couch, laptop open, *Static* channel page glowing on the screen. She stood in the kitchen doorway in her bathrobe, arms crossed, watching me the way you watch someone doing something you’ve asked them not to do.

“You’re doing the thing again,” she said.

“What thing?”

“The thing where you don’t sleep and you stare at your computer and you look like someone who’s been told they have six months to live.”

“That’s very specific.”

“Jane.” Her voice was careful now. “Whatever this is, it’s not good for you. I don’t know if it’s a project or an obsession or a really committed bit, but you look terrible. When was the last time you ate something?”

I closed the laptop. “Someone messaged me. About the thing I posted. They say they have the channel, too. They want to meet.”

Casey’s expression did something complicated. “Meet? In person? A stranger from the internet who read your horror story and wants to meet you in a park?”

“It’s not a horror story.”

“Jane.”

“I know how it sounds.”

“Do you? Because from where I’m sitting, it sounds like a true crime podcast waiting to happen.” She pulled her bathrobe tighter. “I’m going with you.”

Saturday was one of those late February days that doesn’t know what season it wants to be. The sky was overcast but the air was mild. Pullen Park was half-empty, the way parks are in the ambiguous weeks between winter and spring.

Casey and I arrived at 11:45 and sat on a bench near the carousel. It was closed for the season. Casey had her phone out, pretending to scroll but actually, I knew, keeping one eye on every person who walked past.

At 1:58 PM, a man sat down on the bench across from us.

It was hard to determine his age.  He could have been anywhere from his late twenties to mid-forties.  He looked like he had recently lost weight in a way that wasn’t intentional, the kind of weight loss that comes from forgetting to eat, from the body consuming itself when the mind is occupied elsewhere. His hair was dark and needed cutting, and there were shadows under his eyes. But it was his hands I noticed first. They were never still. His fingers moved against his thighs in a constant, unconscious rhythm. He was tracing shapes. Small, repetitive shapes, over and over.

He was holding a phone but not looking at it. He was looking at me. And then, very briefly, his eyes flicked to the dark screen of his phone, checking his own reflection. It was fast, a glance that lasted less than a second, as if checking to make sure nothing was behind him.

“You’re the one who posted,” he said. It wasn’t a question.

“That depends on who’s asking.”

He almost smiled. It didn’t reach his eyes. His mouth performed the motion, but his eyes stayed exactly as they were, flat and watchful, as if smiling and seeing had become two systems that no longer communicated with each other.

“I’m Silas. And I drove three hours to be here, so I’m hoping you’re real, because if this is a prank, I’m going to be really upset, and I don’t have the energy for upset anymore.”

Something about the way he said it, the flatness and exhaustion that went deeper than physical tiredness, made me believe him instantly. It was the sound of someone who had been carrying something too heavy for too long and had stopped expecting anyone to help.

“I’m Jane,” I said. “This is Casey. She’s here because she thinks you might murder me.”

Casey gave a small wave. “Nothing personal.”

“Smart,” Silas said. He looked at Casey, then back at me. “She can’t see it, can she.”

It wasn’t a question either.

“No,” I said.

“Nobody can. That’s the first thing you learn.” He leaned forward, elbows on his knees, and studied the ground for a moment. “How many episodes have you watched?”

“Eight.”

He closed his eyes briefly.

“How many have you watched?” I asked.

“141.”

The number landed in the space between us like something heavy being set down. 141. I’d watched eight and felt like I was coming apart at the seams. 141 was incomprehensible.

“141,” I repeated.

“The count on the page is higher. There are over 200 episodes on mine now. But I’ve watched a 141.” He paused. “Well, 140 ½. There was one I couldn’t finish.”

He said this casually, but when he said it, his fingers stopped tracing shapes on his thigh. It was the first time his hands had been still since he sat down.

Casey looked between us. “Okay. I’m going to sit here and listen, and I want to be supportive, but I also need one of you to explain to me what the hell is going on, because from my perspective, Jane has been losing sleep over a channel I literally cannot see on her screen, and now there’s a strange man who drove three hours to talk about it in a park.”

“That’s a fair summary,” Silas said.

“It’s not an explanation.”

“No,” he agreed. “It’s not.” He looked at me. “Your post was accurate. Everything you described—the episodes, the figure, the way the number grows. The pictures that come out wrong. The fact that nobody else can see it. All of it’s real. The forum you found that disappeared. It all happened to me, too. Different forum, same story. People who had the channel, people who were talking about it, and then they stopped talking, and the thread was gone.”

“You found others?” I asked.

“Three. Across different sites, different platforms. The same pattern each time. Someone posts about the channel, a few people respond, and then one by one they go quiet and the thread vanishes. I’ve been looking for almost eight months. You’re the first person I’ve been able to actually talk to.”

He said it matter-of-factly, but underneath the even tone, I could hear the weight of it. Eight months of watching something impossible alone, searching for proof that he wasn’t losing his mind, finding traces of others only to have them evaporate.

“The message you sent me,” I said. “You mentioned Pullen Park specifically. How did you know where I live?”

“No. But your profile did.” He said it simply, without apology. “After I read your post, I went through your account. Post history, comments, anything that might tell me if you were real or just another person performing belief. You’d commented on a thread about local restaurants in Raleigh a few months back.”

Silas opened an app on his phone the same one, the same interface, the same platform. He navigated to a page I recognized instantly. It was his *Static* channel page.

“This appeared last night, an hour after you posted. Episode 207,” he said, and turned the phone toward me.

The thumbnail showed a park with oak trees, a lake, and a carousel. The date stamp in the corner was too small to read on the phone screen, but the image was unmistakable. It was the park we were sitting in.

“I usually watch them in order, but I’m behind. There are always more episodes than I’ve watched because it grows faster than I can keep up. But sometimes a new episode appears and its thumbnail is different from the rest. It pulls at you. This one…” He tapped the screen. “I saw the thumbnail and something in me recognized it. I skipped ahead. I watched it because the channel wanted me to watch it, and I’ve learned that when the channel pushes an episode at you, the pressure doesn’t ease until you comply.”

He looked at me to see if I understood. I did.

“The footage in my episode was shot here, in this park, on what looks like a fall afternoon, maybe ten or fifteen years ago. Someone was filming their kid riding the carousel. Normal, wholesome footage. The figure was there, standing by the tree line near the lake, watching. And at the end of the episode, the camera panned across the park and I saw the sign at the entrance. Pullen Park, Established 1887. I searched for the park online and saw it was in Raleigh.”

“That could be a coincidence,” Casey said.

Silas looked at her with an expression that had once, probably, been capable of amusement. “Three hours after I read a post from someone describing the exact same channel I’ve been watching for eight months, an episode appears on that channel filmed at a park in the same city? It’s not a coincidence.”

We sat with that for a moment. A jogger passed on the path in front of us, earbuds in, oblivious. A squirrel sprinted up an oak tree. The world continued being normal around us.

“You said you have to watch,” I said. “What happens if you don’t?”

Silas’s jaw tightened. It was a small thing, barely visible, but I saw it. A reflexive bracing. He didn’t answer right away. He looked out toward the carousel, or maybe past it, toward something I couldn’t see. His fingers resumed their tracing.

“In August,” he said, “about two months after the channel first appeared, I decided to stop. I was at maybe 35 episodes. I was exhausted. I wasn’t sleeping, I wasn’t eating, I couldn’t concentrate at work. I deleted the app, and this time I wiped my laptop and phone. Factory reset. I bought new devices. I canceled my subscription to the streaming service entirely. I did everything short of throwing my electronics into the ocean, and I told myself it was over. I was done.”

He paused.

“For the first two days, nothing happened. I felt better. Lighter. Like I’d put down a bag I’d been carrying for miles and could finally feel my shoulders again. I started sleeping through the night. I ate a real dinner. I called my mother. Normal things. I thought I’d beaten it.”

Another pause. Longer this time. A jogger passed on the path in front of us, and Silas tracked him with his eyes the way prey animals track movement.

“On the third day, I was brushing my teeth in the morning. The bathroom was quiet. And I heard it, just barely at the threshold of perception, like a sound arriving from very far away. Static. That hissing, roaring white noise, the sound of a dead signal, coming from nowhere and everywhere.”

“I turned off the faucet. The sound was still there. I went into the living room. Still there. I went outside, and it was fainter, but it was there, underneath the traffic and the wind and the birds, a constant low hiss.”

Casey shifted on the bench. I could feel her skepticism fighting with something else. Not belief exactly, but the unsettling recognition that this man was not performing.

“That was day three,” Silas continued. “On day four, the static was louder. Not dramatically. Just enough that I noticed the increase, the way you notice a room getting warmer before you realize someone’s turned the heat up. And I started seeing things. Not hallucinations I wasn’t seeing things that weren’t there. I was seeing things that *were* there but shouldn’t have been. A shape at the end of my hallway that was gone when I looked directly at it. A reflection in my bathroom mirror that lagged behind my movement by half a second. I’d turn my head and the reflection would follow a beat late, as though it was being operated by something that had to watch me first in order to copy the motion. A shadow on my bedroom wall at night that didn’t correspond to anything in the room. Always in the periphery. Always almost-but-not-quite there.”

“The figure,” I said.

“The figure. But not behind a screen anymore. It was in my apartment. In my mirrors. In the dark spaces I couldn’t quite see into. And every day it got a little clearer. A little closer to the center of my vision. Like it was learning how to exist in my world the way it existed in the tapes, by degrees.”

He looked at his hands. They’d stopped tracing again.

“On day five, I woke up at 3 AM because the static was loud enough to wake me. Not ambient anymore. It sounded like it was coming from the wall behind my bed. I lay there in the dark and listened, and I could hear something *inside* the static. Not words. More like a rhythm. Something that rose and fell like like a pattern that was almost biological but came from something that had never been alive. When I looked at the bedroom door and the hallway beyond, it was dark. But the dark had a shape. It had edges and the suggestion of shoulders and it was standing in my doorway and I could not move.”

“How long?” My voice came out thin and strange, and I realized my hands were gripping the edge of the bench hard enough that my knuckles had gone white.

“I don’t know. Minutes. Hours. I lay there and looked at it, and it stood in the doorway and looked at me. The static was everywhere, filling the room. I understood that it was not coming from the wall. It was coming from *it*. Static is what it sounds like when it exists near you without a screen between you.”

He pressed his palms flat against his knees, steadying himself.

“On day six, I saw it in the daylight. I was standing in my kitchen filling a glass of water, and it was in the corner by the refrigerator. I could see it clearly and it was…” He stopped. His jaw clenched. “It was wrong. I had seen it in the tapes dozens of times at that point, but seeing it on a screen and seeing it in your kitchen are different things. On a screen, your brain can negotiate. It can tell you it’s a glitch, an artifact, a trick of light. In person, there is no negotiation. You see it and your brain just… screams. Every part of you understands at once that you are looking at something that should not exist.”

He was quiet for a moment, and when he spoke again, his voice had dropped.

“The shape is almost right. Almost human. The proportions are close enough that your brain wants to accept it, the way your brain wants to read a word even when the letters are scrambled. But there’s something about the joints. The angles where the limbs meet the torso. They’re *off*. Not bent the wrong way, that would be easier. That would be a broken thing. This was something that had never been assembled correctly in the first place. And the head. The head was tilted, the way it tilts in the tapes, but in person you could see that it wasn’t a tilt. The neck was too long. It was just the way the head sat on the neck, like it had one too many vertebrae. And the face…” He stopped again. “I won’t describe the face. I can’t. Not because I don’t remember it. Because I remember it too well, and if I describe it, I’ll see it again, and I have to sleep tonight.”

“What did you do?” I asked.

“I dropped the glass. It shattered on the floor. And I ran. I didn’t think about it. There was no decision. My body just moved straight through the kitchen, through the broken glass on the floor, through the living room, out the front door. I didn’t grab my keys or wallet or anything. I just ran. Barefoot, down three flights of stairs and into my building’s parking lot at 7 AM, and I stood there shaking so hard my vision was blurring, and I could still hear the static."

He paused. His fingers, which had resumed their tracing, went still again.

“My apartment is on the third floor. I looked up, and it was in the window.”

He said this quietly.

“Not behind the window. It was *in* the window. Pressed against it. Filling the frame. The window is maybe three feet by five, and it was occupying the entire space. I understood for the first time that the thing I’d been watching on the tapes, the thing I’d been calling a figure was bigger than I thought. Much bigger. What I’d been seeing in the tapes was something folded down, something compressed into a shape that could fit inside the geometry of a room. In the window, pressed against the glass, it looked like something that was trying to fit itself into a space several sizes too small.”

Casey made a sound. I don’t think she meant to.

“Its hands were on the glass. I could see the palms. They were flat against the window and they were wron*g*. Too many points of contact, too many places where something like a finger met the surface. And it was moving. Shifting. Pressing. Testing. The way something tests a barrier it doesn’t understand yet, like it could feel the boundary between where it was and where it wanted to be and was trying to find the seam. The glass should have been cracking. I remember thinking that. And its face…” He stopped. Swallowed. “I said I wouldn’t describe the face. I’m not going to. But I will tell you this. It was looking down at me, and its head was tilted. It was looking at me like I was something it had been waiting for, something it knew it was eventually going to have.”

He was quiet for a long time after that. A full minute, maybe longer.

“A woman was walking her dog on the sidewalk. She didn’t look up. The dog didn’t bark. Nobody in the parking lot saw it. But the woman stopped when she saw me.” He glanced down at his feet. “I’d run through the broken glass. Across the kitchen floor, through the shards. I hadn’t felt it. But I was standing in the parking lot leaving red footprints on the asphalt and I didn’t know it. The woman saw the blood before I did. She was staring at my feet and then at my face. She had her phone half-raised like she was deciding whether to call 911, and she said, ‘Sir? Sir, are you okay? You’re bleeding.’ I looked down and realized I had glass in the soles of both feet, blood pooling on the pavement, and I hadn’t felt a thing. That’s how scared I was.”

“And the figure?” I asked.

“Still in the window. Still pressing against the glass. I could see it over the woman’s shoulder while she was talking to me, and she couldn’t see it at all. I must have looked insane to her. Barefoot, bleeding, staring up at a window with nothing in it, shaking like I’d been pulled from cold water.”

He looked at his hands again. The tracing had resumed, faster now.

“Somehow, I knew the only thing that would make the static stop was watching an episode. I had my phone. It had been in my pocket when I walked into the kitchen, and I knew the channel was on it. I’d found that out weeks earlier.”

He must have seen the question on my face, because he continued without my asking.

“A little over a week into watching, back before I tried to quit, I’d done the same thing you described in your post. Pointed my phone at my laptop screen and tried to take pictures of the figure and record video. The pictures and recordings came out wrong, and I gave up. The next day, I opened the streaming app on my phone to watch something normal, a show I’d been halfway through, and there it was. The channel page, sitting at the top of my watchlist. Same scratched white letters. Same row of episodes. It had spread to my phone, and the only thing that had changed between the phone being clean and the phone being infected was that I’d aimed its camera at the channel. I’d pointed it at the thing on my laptop, and the thing had looked back through the glass, and that was enough.”

A cold feeling spread through my chest. I thought about the screenshots I’d taken, the video recordings. I’d pointed my phone at the channel a dozen times.

“So I sat down on the curb,” Silas continued. “Right there in the parking lot with blood all over my feet and the woman backed away from me, because she’d decided I was either on drugs or having a breakdown. I opened the channel on my phone, and I watched an episode on a four-inch screen in a parking lot with my feet bleeding onto the asphalt and I didn’t look up. About halfway through, the static started to fade. By the end, it was gone. The pressure lifted. The world felt normal again.”

“And the window?” I asked.

“I sat there for a long time after the episode ended. Maybe 45 minutes. I picked the glass out of my feet.  Some of the shards were deep.  I still have a scar on my left heel. Every few minutes I’d look up at the window. The first time, I wasn’t sure. The light was hitting it differently and there was a glare, so I couldn’t quite tell if there was still something behind the glass or if it was just the reflection of the sky. The second time, ten minutes later, the window was clearer, and I thought I could see a shape, but it was fainter. Less solid. Like it was losing resolution. The third time I looked, there was nothing.”

He exhaled slowly.

“When I finally went back inside, the kitchen was empty. The glass was still shattered on the floor. The water was still pooled around the shards. The corner by the refrigerator was just a corner. I cleaned up the glass, bandaged my feet, and sat down on the couch.” He looked at me. “Everything was normal. Everything except the channel, which had grown by eleven episodes in the six days I’d been away. And the figure in the new tapes was closer than it had ever been. It was standing directly behind people. Sometimes, its face was right beside theirs.”

I understood then.

“You can’t stop watching because watching is what keeps it in the tapes?” I asked.

“Yes.”

“It’s like our attention is what keeps it inside the channel?”

“That’s how I think of it,” Silas agreed. “The tapes exist because someone is watching them. The figure stays on the other side of the screen because your eyes are on it. You look away, and the lid comes off and it starts to get out.” He exhaled. “You watch because the alternative is having it here, in your life, in your home, getting clearer every day until…”

He stopped.

“Until what?”

“I don’t know. I didn’t let it get that far. But the people on the forums, the ones who went quiet? I think they stopped watching. And I think whatever happens at the end of the six days, or the seven days, or however long it takes, I think that’s what happened to them.”

“How often do you have to watch?” I asked, because the practical question felt safer than the existential one.

“I’ve tested it. If I go more than about two days without watching an episode, I start to feel it. Nothing dramatic at first. Just an unease, a pressure. By the end of the third day, I’ll hear the static. After that, it escalates fast. So, I watch every day or every other day. Sometimes two a night if the channel’s been growing quickly. It’s like a job. The worst job I’ve ever had, and I once spent a summer cleaning septic tanks.” He almost smiled again, but it still didn’t reach his eyes.

“The phone thing. You said the channel got onto your phone because you pointed the camera at it. Is that the only way it spreads?”

“As far as I can tell. You point a screen at it, and it looks back through the glass. The channel doesn’t need to be captured. It just needs to be seen by the device. One look through the lens is enough.” He turned the phone over in his hands.

My stomach dropped as I thought about my phone and all the times I’d held it up to my laptop screen aiming at the channel over and over. Every time, I’d been opening a door I didn’t know existed.

“My phone,” I said. My voice sounded far away to me. “I took pictures. Dozens of them. I recorded video of every episode where I saw the figure.”

Silas looked at me. His expression wasn’t surprised.

“Then it’s on your phone,” he said. “It’s been on your phone since the first time you pointed the camera at your screen.”

I did not take my phone out of my pocket

“What about the people who posted in the forums? Do you think they’re dead?”

“I think they’re gone. Whether that means dead, I don’t know.” He was quiet for a moment, and I watched his eyes do the quick flick to the dark surface of his phone, checking the reflection, then away. “But I think some of them are in the tapes.”

The words hit me strangely.

“What do you mean?”

“The newer episodes. The ones from 2015 onward. The footage gets clearer, the timestamps move closer to the present, and the figure gets closer to the people in the videos. But there’s something else. In some of the newer tapes, there are other shapes. Not just the figure. They’re in the backgrounds of the footage the way the figure used to be, at the edges, in doorways, half-hidden. But they don’t look like the figure. They look like people who don’t belong in the scene.”

He hesitated.

“Their faces are wrong in a different way than the figure’s face is wrong. The figure’s face won’t resolve. It’s like a photograph that’s permanently out of focus. But these people, they have features and expressions. Every single one of them has the same expression. They look like someone who has just understood something terrible and will spend the rest of eternity understanding it.”

“How many?” I asked.

“I’ve counted five so far. One is a man who shows up in the background of a barbecue from 2017. He’s standing on the patio behind the sliding glass door, hands at his sides, face pressed almost against the glass from the inside. Another is smaller, harder to see, always at the very edge of the frame in two episodes from 2019, and I can’t tell if it’s a man or a woman because the body is compressed somehow. Folded in on itself.”

He paused again.

“There’s a woman in three of the more recent episodes. She shows up in footage from 2020 and 2021, and she’s the clearest of any of them. The first time I saw her, she was in the background of someone’s living room, standing behind an armchair. Each time she appears, she’s a little closer to the camera. In the first one, she was across the room. In the second, halfway. In the most recent one, she filled the right side of the frame, close enough that I could see the texture of her skin, and her mouth was…”

He stopped. Not for effect. Because the memory was doing something to him. I could see it in the way his shoulders drew inward, a protective reflex.

“Her mouth was open. Not wide or screaming. Just wrong. Her jaw was dropped about an inch, the way a mouth hangs open during sleep, except her eyes were wide and focused and looking directly at the camera. There was a kind of slackness to it, like the muscles that controlled the jaw had stopped working, or like her mouth had been in that position for so long that the muscles had forgotten how to close it. And inside her mouth was dark. Dark the way the corridor in the church tape was dark. Like whatever had happened to her, it was happening from the inside out.”

I didn’t say anything. I couldn’t.

“I don’t know if she’s trying to do something or if that’s just what happens to them. But every time I close my eyes, I see that face, and I know that if I stop watching, that’s where I’ll end up. In a tape, with my mouth open and the dark coming from inside.”

Casey had been silent for a long time. She spoke now, and her voice was different than it had been earlier.  Less skeptical, more.

“Silas. I need to ask you something, and I need you to be honest with me.”

He looked at her. His fingers paused their tracing.

“Is this real? Are you actually experiencing this? Because I’m sitting here listening to two people describe something I can’t see, and either you’re both having the same delusion, or you’re both very good at improv, or something is happening that I genuinely cannot explain.”

Silas studied her for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice had a quality I hadn’t heard before, the gentleness of someone who remembers what it felt like to live in the world Casey still lived in, where things like this didn’t happen.

“When I was a kid, we had a dog that used to stare at the corner of the living room. Just sit there and stare at a spot on the wall where there was nothing. My mom said he could see things we couldn’t. She was joking. But here’s the thing. The dog could see the corner. We could all see the corner. The difference was that the dog was seeing something in the corner that we couldn’t. Not because it wasn’t there. Because we didn’t have the right kind of eyes.”

He looked at Casey for a long moment.

“You’re not missing anything because there’s nothing to see. You’re missing it because you haven’t been chosen to see it. And I mean this as the most sincere thing I will ever say to you, be glad. Be so, so glad. Because once you see it, you can’t unsee it. And once you can’t unsee it, it knows.”

“What does it know?” Casey asked.

“That you’re watching. And once it knows you’re watching…” Another pause. “It starts watching back.”

We talked for two hours. Silas told me about the progression. The early episodes were exactly like mine. Footage from the ‘80s and early ‘90s, the figure distant and peripheral, the tapes warm and ordinary. Then the footage moved forward in time, and the figure moved inward in space. By the 2000s, it was in the room. By the 2010s, it was standing behind people, sitting beside them, occupying their spaces with the quiet entitlement of something that had always been there and was only now letting itself be seen. In one episode from 2018, Silas said, the figure was sitting at a kitchen table while a family of four ate dinner around it. The mother passed a bowl of mashed potatoes through the space where its hands should have been. A child’s elbow rested against what would have been its arm.

“They can’t feel it,” Silas said. “No one in the footage ever reacts to it. It’s there but it’s not there. It exists on a layer that only we can see.”

“What about the episode with no date stamp?” I asked. “One of the forum users mentioned it. He said he couldn’t watch it again.”

Silas went quiet.

“You’ve seen it,” I said.

“That’s the one I couldn’t finish.”

I noticed that his fingers had stopped tracing entirely and were wrapped around edge of the bench, and I understood that he was grounding himself.

“The undated episodes are different. There are three of them on my channel now. The footage isn’t from the past. The quality is modern and high definition. They’re shot in a way that makes you realize the old footage had a kind of mercy to it, the grain and the blur and the warmth were a buffer between you and what you were seeing. The undated episodes have no buffer. They’re clean and sharp and cold, and the footage is *your* life. Your apartment. Your street. Your bedroom at 2 AM when you think you’re alone. Filmed from angles that shouldn’t exist, from cameras that aren’t there. You’ll see your own kitchen from a vantage point near the ceiling. Your bedroom filmed from inside the closet. The view from your window, but shot from the outside, looking in, from something that would have to be standing on a wall that has no ledge.”

“I saw that,” I said. “The last thumbnail before I posted. It looked like my apartment.”

“It was your apartment. And in the undated episodes, the figure is close. It’s standing next to your bed while you sleep. It’s sitting on your couch while you watch TV. It’s in the mirror behind you while you brush your teeth. In the footage you can see its face reflected next to yours, and your sleeping face or your brushing-your-teeth face has no idea. You look peaceful. You look normal. And it is *right there*.” His voice had gone flat. “The undated episodes show you what’s already happening. The figure is already there, in your life, in your space. The channel is just letting you see it.”

A jogger passed behind us. I flinched.

“The one you couldn’t finish,” I said carefully. “What was in it?”

He shook his head. Refusing to remember, but the memory came anyway.

“It was me. Sitting at my desk. Watching the channel on my laptop. Wearing the shirt I was wearing at that moment, in the room I was sitting in at that moment, and on my laptop screen in the footage I could see the *Static* channel page, and on the *Static* channel page in the footage was the episode I was currently watching, which meant the footage was showing me watching the footage that was showing me watching the footage, and for one horrible second the recursion opened up beneath me like a trapdoor and I could feel it. The figure was behind me in the footage, standing right behind my chair, and its face was…” He exhaled sharply. “It was right behind me. I was watching myself watch it, and in the footage I looked exactly the way I looked in that moment, wearing the same clothes, sitting in the same position, and I realized the episode was being filmed right then, in real time, and the figure in the footage was in the room with me right then, standing behind my chair, and if I turned around…”

“Did you?” Casey asked. Her voice was barely above a whisper.

“No. I closed the app. I sat very still for a very long time. I didn't turn around. But I could feel it. The way you feel someone standing close to you in a crowd. For what felt like hours, I sat in that chair and did not turn around until I could feel that it was gone.”

The sun was getting lower. Somewhere across the lake, a heron stood motionless in the shallows. The world was beautiful and indifferent, the way it always is when you’re in the middle of something terrible.

“What do we do?” I asked.

For a moment, the exhaustion in Silas’s face gave way to something. Not hope exactly but something adjacent to it.

“I don’t know,” he said. “I’ve been trying to figure that out for eight months. I’ve tried everything I can think of. I’ve talked to priests and therapists and a guy who claims to cleanse digital hauntings for $500 an hour. None of it worked. The channel is just there. It grows. I watch it because the alternative is worse. And every day the tapes get a little more recent and the figure gets a little closer.”

“But now there are two of us,” I said.

“Now there are two of us,” he agreed. “Which is either better or worse, and I genuinely don’t know which.”

We exchanged phone numbers and agreed to stay in contact to compare episodes, document patterns, and try to build something like a map of the channel’s behavior from two different vantage points.  As we were leaving, Silas said something that I haven’t been able to stop thinking about.

“The episode of the park. Episode 207 on my channel."

“Yeah?”

“I watched it before I came here. The footage was old. Maybe 2009, 2010. A family at the carousel. The figure was by the lake, watching. Normal for the channel. But at the end of the episode, the footage cut to something different. More recent. Same park, but the light was different. Winter light. Overcast.”

It felt like there was a stone in my stomach again.

“There were three people sitting on a bench near the carousel,” he said. “Two women and a man. The footage was shot from across the lake, zoomed in. And the quality was modern. Not tape or a digital camcorder.”

“It’s us,” I said.

“It’s today. The episode showed us meeting, filmed from a distance, before the conversation happened.”

I opened my mouth and closed it again.

“We’re in the tapes now, Jane. We’re part of the footage. And the figure…”

He glanced toward the lake. I followed his gaze. The heron was gone. The shallows were empty except for the fading rings of something that had just moved beneath the surface. And at the far edge of the water, where the tree line met the bank, there was a stillness that seemed to have weight.

“…was in the shot,” he finished.

I looked at the trees. I didn’t see anything, but the stillness felt unnatural.

“I’ll call you tomorrow,” I said.

“Watch the episode tonight,” Silas said. “Your episode 34. Watch it and tell me what you see.”

I nodded and walked to the car where Casey was waiting. She looked at my face and didn’t ask any questions, which was the kindest thing she could have done.

That night, I sat on my bed with every light in the apartment on and my laptop open to the *Static* channel page.

37 episodes now. Three more since the morning. The channel was growing faster.

I scrolled to episode 34. *The Park.*

The thumbnail glowed on my screen. The carousel. The oaks. The late-afternoon light of a day that had already been absorbed into the past.

I pressed play.

The footage opened on a wide shot of Pullen Park, filmed from the northern bank of the lake. The image had that particular quality I’d come to associate with the mid-2000s, cleaner than tape but not quite the sharpness of HD. A date stamp in the lower corner read **09/14/2008** in small white digits.

For eight minutes, it was a summer day in the park. Families on blankets, kids feeding ducks, and a man pushing a stroller along the path while talking on a flip phone. The camera was stationary, filming the park as though someone had set it down and walked away.

The figure appeared at the four-minute mark, standing on the far side of the carousel. No one at the park reacted.

At nine minutes, the footage changed.

The afternoon light of 2008 was replaced, mid-frame, by something colder and grayer. It was winter, and the date stamp was gone.

The camera was in the same position, but everything had changed. The park was nearly empty, and the lake was still. Sitting on a bench near the inert carousel, filmed from across the water with a clarity that no 2008 camcorder could produce, were three people.

I recognized the jacket I’d been wearing. Casey’s red scarf. Silas leaning forward with his elbows on his knees. The footage held on us for 45 seconds. At the edge of the frame, near the tree line by the lake, was the figure.

In the clean, modern image, I could almost make out the shape of it, the proportions that were almost right but fundamentally wrong, the outline of something that had learned to approximate a human.

The footage cut to black.

I picked up my phone and texted Silas.

*I watched it. I saw us. I saw it in the trees near the lake.*

The reply came in seconds, as if he’d been waiting.

*It was closer in yours, wasn’t it.*

I stared at the message.

*How did you know?*

*Because it’s always closer in the version it wants you to see.*

I set the phone down and closed my laptop. The apartment was quiet.

I have 37 episodes on my channel. I’m going to keep watching, because I have to, because the alternative is a sound in every silence and a shape in every shadow and a face in every reflection.

I caught myself doing it just now, as I typed that last sentence. I glanced at the window to my left. It’s dark outside, and the window is a mirror. I looked at it the way Silas looks at his phone screen, fast and furtive. I didn’t see anything. But the checking is the point, isn’t it? The checking means I’ve accepted, on some level, that there is something to check for.

If you’re reading this, check your watchlist.

And if you see the thin, scratched letters and the row of episodes with no descriptions, don’t watch them.

And if you happen to glance at a dark window as you finish reading this, if you happen to catch your own reflection and feel the urge to look at the space behind you, don’t.

It’s probably nothing.

It’s almost always nothing.
