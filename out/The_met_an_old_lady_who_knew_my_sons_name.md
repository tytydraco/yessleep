I awoke in a near-stupor, the remnants of some now forgotten nightmare clinging to my consciousness. I struggled to drag myself into a sitting position, my eyes painfully dry against the glare of morning light cutting through the window blinds. 

It was Lucas's calling that roused me, gradually increasing in volume until I finally mustered the energy to shout back,

"It's alright Luc! Mummy's coming!" 

I staggered out of bed, the cold air further encouraging me to become fully awake, even while the leftover feeling of dread from my dreams clung to me like a stain. 

Lucas swayed in his bedroom doorway, the stair gate blocking his path. 

He grinned when he saw me, and shoved a chubby too-large-to-fit-fist into his mouth as I gathered him up and kissed the top of his head, breathing in the scent of warm baby. 

"My sweet boy, up early again are we?"

At least Lucas had slept soundly. Yet again, I had lain awake well into the early hours, a storm of anxiety winning the war against resting. Sleep deprivation and I were old friends by now - I knew I needed help, but that would require having the energy to reach for it. No, I could survive this, as long as I had Lucas. 

The atmosphere of the supermarket blinded and deafened me in equal measure. The fluorescent lights seemed to glare directly into my eyes, causing black spots to swim across my vision, whilst the noise of busy shoppers disorientated my senses and left me struggling to focus. 'What was it we needed again? Milk? Milk and.. bubble bath? No, I bought that on Tuesday..it wasn't that, it was -'

"Oh Lucas, you're getting so big now aren't you, handsome boy!"

An elderly lady bent over Lucas like a crow, her wrinkled fingers reaching for his face, glassy eyes focused on his chubby cheeks. The leathered texture of her scalp was clearly visible through her remaining tufts of downy white hair, and I struggled to breathe as I inhaled the scent of bad breath and musty clothes. I was sure I had never met this woman before.

Her fingers made contact with his flesh and I cringed, unable to summon up the strength to protest, paralysed by indecision and agitation. I told myself that whilst I minded, Lucas didn't seem to. In fact, he smiled at her, giving her a glimpse of his one front tooth. A pang of guilt coursed through me, causing my stomach to twist uneasily. I tried to calm myself and gave the woman a flat smile, moving past her with my trolley. She didn't return my smile, instead choosing to glare through milky-white eyes and mutter something about 'mothers these days'. 

My nerves had been fried further yet by a mix-up at the checkout - then Lucas screaming with impatience and drawing all eyes to us as I heaved overstuffed shopping bags into the boot of our rustbucket of a car, the biting cold of the wind whipping my unwashed hair around my face, stray strands sticking to my lips. 

I drove home with shaking hands, my knuckles whitened as I gripped the steering wheel. I was desperate to be away from prying eyes in the one place I could gather some kind of mental fortitude. 

I rocked Lucas steadily in his seat, while he fought sleep all the way. He alternated between sucking in air to wail at the injustice of it all and staring into the ceiling corners of the dimly lit living room. Occasionally, I would follow his gaze and catch movement out of the corner of my eye - a darting shadow, a cloud of darkness, always just out of direct view. God, I needed to sleep. 

As soon as Lucas had begun to nap, I went to make myself a cup of tea. The last of the sun's rays striped across the small kitchen floor. Our small lawn outside was falling into gloom and dandelion clocks swayed as they cast shadows across the grass. Somewhere, a cat began to yowl. 

I stood by the window and sipped my drink, noticing a hunched-over figure shuffling along the pavement. As they drew level with where I stood, they stopped and turned their head to face me. I recognised it as the old woman from the supermarket, despite her face being half-masked by the sun. She held my gaze for what felt like hours but could only have been seconds. My skin crawled, yet as much as I wanted to I could not look away.

 A slow grin spread across her face, the stretching of the skin causing her lips to split, beads of blood oozing in the cracks. She hacked a cough and spat a mouthful of something dark onto the concrete, then turned away, resuming her slow journey along the pavement. A lone pigeon flew down to inspect the mess. I turned away, disgusted. My heart was in my throat, and I couldn't help but feel deeply unsettled. I poured my tea down the sink, completely put off. 

As soon as Luc had began to nap, his little chest rising and falling in a steady rhythm, I felt the emptiness creeping in like a rising tide.

What did I have to offer Lucas, really? A life of struggle, that's what. 

I glanced over at the envelopes strewn across our small dining table. Bills, bills and more bills. I'd given up replying to them, they seemed to arrive in a greater frequency since then, always after another crumb from our empty plates. This house was a tomb. The phone had stopped ringing well before I had stopped paying for our landline. We were just scraping by, Luc and I. I wondered for the umpteenth time if my love was enough. 

"You need a bath, you stinky boy!" 

I chuckled, holding Lucas aloft in the air. He roared with laughter - I was always glad to be his personal comedian. He kicked his legs and wriggled, a glob of drool dangling from his grinning mouth. I took him to the bathroom and strapped him into his bathseat, holding my nose and catching his eye as he giggled. The last of the day's sunlight cast orange stripes across the walls. I fumbled with the tap. Realising I'd forgotten to bring the bubble bath upstairs, I went to leave the room. 

"Just a second, my sweet boy." 

I blew Luc a kiss as I darted round the door, dashing along the landing and taking the stairs two at a time. As soon as I reached the kitchen, I tripped on an errant shoe that had been left on its side in the doorway. It was as if I watched my own body from afar, I lost all coordination, folding to the floor helplessly as if my strings had been cut, my reflexes non-existent. My skull hit the doorframe with a crack. 

Black spots swam across my vision. I lay on my side, my head pulsing violently, stomach rolling as I tried to lift myself up. The house was quiet, dark. I tried to focus my eyes, causing the pain in my head to intensify.

 What had I been doing? And how long had I been out for?

With a sharp inhale, I lurched to my feet. 

"Lucas! Lucas!"

I called for him, frantic. I struggled to stay upright, vertigo threatening to drag me to the floor. I felt a drip of warm liquid slide to the tip of my nose and then splat on the hard wood.  I dragged myself to the stairs, hauling myself up using the bannister. Every step took more energy than I had left, shadows creeping in from my periphery, seeming to race me up the stairs, taunting me with every heave. 

Suddenly, I heard a thump. A muffled laugh.

"Luc? Lucas!?" 

I rounded the landing on clumsy steps, my heart beating an erratic rhythm. Liquid pooled out beneath the door and all I knew was a shrill, deafening ringing as I entered the bathroom and saw the old woman, standing by the small window, holding my little boy. She cradled him in her arms and cooed his name, liver-spotted fingers stroking his cheeks, her yellowed fingernails tracing circles on his chin. I hung from the doorframe, barely able to stand.

"What are you doing with my baby?" 

I tried to shout, yet it came out sounding much weaker than I planned. She smirked and waved one hand around the room. 

"Doing what you cannot..taking care of him." 

Her attention went back to Lucas, and I staggered forwards, falling to my knees in the spilt bathwater. Terror overwhelmed me, my body refusing to oblige my desperate need to get to him, to keep my baby safe.

"Please, please, I don't know who you are, but...please, give him to me. He's my baby."

She seemed to contemplate my words, silent for a few seconds. Lucas didn't once look at me, mesmerised by the crone's face, his mouth open as if in awe at what he found there. She twisted her neck to look at me with sharp, focused eyes, all trace of cataracts erased.

"No," she croaked.

And then as if they had never existed, she and Lucas were gone and I was all that was left. I heard the shrieks of a wild animal pierce the quiet of the night, violent in its grief and realised, it was me.
