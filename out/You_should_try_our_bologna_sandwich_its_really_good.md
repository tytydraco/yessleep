I live in a small town. We don’t really have much going on. The town is not quite historic, no important people are from here, but we do have one thing: our sandwich shop.

That may not sound like much, but this sandwich shop is the heart of our community. Other delis have tried to open, some larger chains even, but none of them could compete. This little small-town shop starved them all out of business.

Last summer, I was fresh out of high school, and I wanted to save up money for college. My friend, let’s call him Ryan, and I were both looking for a summer job. One day, Ryan told me that the famous sandwich shop in our town was short-staffed. We both applied, and long story short, we got in!

They called us the “new blood”.

Things started great; the owner was extremely friendly, if a little eccentric.

The coworkers were great too; they were a tight-knit group, like family. They had all been there for a long time, and it would take a while for my friend and me to be “accepted”. Then again, all good things take time, at least, I thought.  
When I say the owner was a little eccentric, I meant they had some strange… rituals.

Every day before our shift started, they would give us a ‘pep-talk’ of sorts.

There was this strange point system; everyone got points for how many sandwiches they sold. There wasn’t really much at stake, I thought; it seemed like all you got for winning was the honors, and all you got for losing was ‘disgrace’. However, winning seemed to be incredibly important to everyone. Well, it was more like everyone was trying not to lose…

After two weeks, we were invited to a small party to celebrate us ‘new blood’. Well, they called it a ‘ceremony’, which was strange.

When we got to the party, we were told we would have to try every item on the menu. They said it was to offer better customer service and recommendations that came from the heart.

It seemed innocent enough, at least, until Ryan said something: “This meat tastes a little off.”

Everyone got quiet. Not like they stopped talking, like they were scared to breathe.

That’s when the owner looked at Ryan with a very stern look. Suddenly, he smiled widely and said, “No worries, champ, I guess you’re just not a bologna guy.”

His smile seemed… off. He grinned just a bit too wide, but those eyes…

Those eyes seemed friendly at first, but if you stared long enough, it was as if there was no soul behind them. As if the friendly expression was just plastered on.

Over the next few weeks, Ryan began to change. He completely stopped talking about college. He even started quoting the employee handbook in casual conversation. He became like an emotionless drone, but every time a customer walked in, he would plaster on a friendly smile. That same smile our boss was wearing at that party…

He even became employee of the month, but he didn’t feel like my friend anymore.

It was as if the friend I had known all through high school was gone, and something else was wearing his skin. Something similar to him, but not quite the same.

I guess one day something snapped. Ryan came up to me after work. He looked genuinely terrified, like something or someone was chasing him.

He told me, “This place is not what it seems. They’re doing something to us, man; we need to leave, RIGHT NOW!”

Truth is, I knew Ryan was right. I knew something was up. Something about this place was truly wrong.

I wanted to run away with Ryan. To get as far away from this deli as possible, but I couldn’t.

It was as if something inside me stopped me from quitting. In that moment, that part of me won. I told Ryan: “In the event you see a coworker in distress, you should report it to your shift manager.” I don’t even know why I said that. It was straight out of the employee handbook. Ryan told me not to tell anyone, begged me even, but in that moment, there was nothing I could do to stop myself. My brain was screaming to listen to Ryan, but my body was walking to the office. I told my shift manager exactly what Ryan said. He just looked at me and thanked me for my honesty, his face wearing that same unnatural grin.

The next day, Ryan was gone. My boss went up to me and personally thanked me for reporting what Ryan told me. He said Ryan was having a mental breakdown, and he wouldn’t be showing up for work for the foreseeable future. He told me not to worry, because I would see Ryan soon enough…

College was starting in a few weeks, but I wasn’t thinking about that anymore. All I could think about was making more hours at the deli. I was working there day-in, day out for weeks. When college started, I didn’t show up. I just kept working at that deli.

I dropped out after a week of not showing up. Ryan’s words lingered in the back of my head: “They’re doing something to us, man”, but I told myself dropping out was my own decision. I wasn’t ready for college anyway. Besides, working here for an entire year would mean I would have more than enough money to go to college next year.

That month, I became employee of the month. It wasn’t even close; no one came close to selling as many sandwiches as I had.

My boss congratulated me and invited me to a welcome-back party for Ryan.

He said Ryan was “all better now…”

That Saturday, I went to the party at the deli. “Strange place for a party”, I thought.

When I got inside, my boss escorted me to the back, the processing room.

There was no one else. My boss told me this party was just for Ryan and me.

The processing room was cold, as if warmth itself refused to enter.

It was clean too; sterile, like an operating room. That’s when I noticed the long, sturdy wooden workbench in the middle of the room. The same type of table that a butcher uses to process meat.

At that moment, Ryan walked in. He didn’t seem happy. He didn’t seem sad either; his face was completely neutral. There was no expression to be found. My boss walked in after Ryan, but he was holding something… a meat cleaver.

I was scared to death; every bone in my body was telling me to run for the door, but something stopped me. It said: “Workers must stay at their designated workstation.”

The room was quiet until Ryan shuffled forward. He climbed onto the workbench and lay down on his stomach, lying perfectly still.

My boss walked up to the workbench and suddenly turned his head to me and said: “Champ, do you know what it takes for a business to survive?”

“No, sir”, I stammered softly.

“A business can only survive by removing the waste”, he said, as he turned his gaze to Ryan.

“Ryan here has broken my trust, and all of our hearts”, he followed.

He continued: “He went behind our backs and made up lies about our beautiful company… Horrible lies, but that doesn’t mean he can’t be useful to us anymore.”

He raised the cleaver with one hand, but I didn’t look.  
All I could see was Ryan, just lying there. He was looking in my direction, but not at me; through me.

My boss lowered the cleaver straight into Ryan’s neck, but it didn’t go through. I heard the crunch of the cleaver hitting bone.

He raised the cleaver again and slammed it down on Ryan’s neck once more. It went through; the cleaver making a slam as it wedged itself into the workbench. Ryan’s head hit the floor with a thud, his eyes still facing in my direction. Only this time, I could recognize a look in his eyes: sadness.

The next week, my boss said he would teach me how to make Bologna.

First, he took me to the cold room. There I saw large cuts of meat. It was Ryan; it must have been. The meat didn’t look like the carcass of a cow or a pig; it was human.

Next, my boss took me to the grinding room. In the middle of the room was a large meat grinder. My boss handed me the cuts of meat that were once Ryan. With the press of a button, the meat grinder came to life. It whirred loudly, and I inserted the cuts of meat into the hopper on top.

I heard the squelching of the meat, and out of the machine came ground… Ryan.

The ground meat looked just like any other; there was nothing off about it.

Nothing screamed: “This was a person!”

I must admit that it made the rest of the process easier.

The next step was emulsification. “This is the most important step for bologna”, my boss said.

The ground meat was mixed with salt, spices, and ice, and blended until it became a thick, smooth paste called a "batter."

It no longer looked like meat; it looked like pink frosting.

After that came the stuffing: The batter was pumped into long, tube-like casings, the "logs".

Ryan wasn’t exactly a big guy, so I was surprised how many logs of Bologna were made just from him.

Next came the thermal processing: The logs were hung on racks and cooked. This is where the meat firmed up into the solid texture of bologna.

The smoking room was filled with a rich, savory aroma that combined the savory scent of cured pork with the deep, aromatic notes of wood smoke.

It smelled… delicious.

Once cooled, the logs were peeled and ready to be sliced thin for sandwiches.

And that’s what I did. The bologna sandwich was by far our most popular item, and the Ryan sandwiches sold like hot cakes…

I knew my… processing… of Ryan was a point of no return, but it stirred up something inside me. After that, with each sandwich I sold, my eyes slowly opened to the reality of what this place was, and what had become of me.

Every time I sold another one of those sandwiches, the realization of what I’d done slowly came to me. Every customer who sat in those chairs. Every time I heard them bite down on those sandwiches, I heard the meat grinder squelching. As they chewed, as their lips smacked, all I heard was Ryan pleading to me, begging me not to go to the management, and to just run away with him.

Over the next few weeks, I started quoting the employee handbook less and less frequently.

I started feeling like myself again for the first time in months.

Whenever I talked to customers, I would stumble over my words. I wasn’t selling as many sandwiches anymore, and I was no longer employee of the month, not even close.

I suppose it was only natural for my boss to notice.

He gave me a talking to yesterday.

He said, “I noticed you’ve been less efficient recently. Remember, stick to the employee handbook, and stick to the script.”

I could feel the programming flooding back into my brain.

It was like a virus spreading through me again.

I decided to write this post today because I can feel my mind slipping again. I don’t know how much time I have, but I need to warn you.

If you’re ever in a small town where all the people praise their local deli, you should not….

You should…

You should try our bologna sandwich.

It’s really good.
