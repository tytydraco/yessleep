The thing about Bong was that he was never really anywhere. He was always _about_ to be somewhere, always setting up the shot, always framing the moment just before it happened so that when it did he could say, _guys, here we are,_ as if arriving was the same as being present.

He had 4,200 subscribers. He told people 5,000, rounding up, which he felt was reasonable.

"Guys, here we are at the Ninoy Aquino International Airport, Terminal 3, and we are going on a budget trip to Tacloban City in Leyte! Bonggang bonggang gala with Kuya Bong Gala on a budget!"

He stopped recording. Watched it back. His hair was doing something on the left side.

"Guys, here we are at NAIA Terminal 3 and we are heading to Tacloban City, the city of—"

A family with a large luggage cart cut behind him and he caught their reflection in his screen and stopped again.

He did it six times before he got one he could work with. In five of the six takes he was smiling the same smile, the one he had practiced in bathroom mirrors since he was nineteen, the one that said _I am likeable and going places._ In the sixth take, the one he kept, the smile was almost real because he had stopped thinking about it.

He was twenty-six. He worked data entry four days a week and spent the other three making content that was, he believed, on the edge of breaking through.

The flight was delayed by two hours. This, he decided, was also content.

"Guys, so the flight is delayed, classic CebPac behavior, but you know what, this is the budget travel experience, this is what it's really like, and Kuya Bong is here for all of it! Guys, if you are new here, welcome to the channel, hit that subscribe button, and guys, I promise you, this is going to be the most honest budget travel content you will ever watch."

He filmed himself eating a sad airport hotdog. He filmed the departure board. He filmed a sleeping security guard and then deleted it because he wasn't sure if that was legal. He filmed the empty rows of seats at the gate, whispering to camera that this was basically a private jet experience, guys, and you could get this on a budget airline for less than a thousand pesos if you booked three weeks ahead, which he was going to link in the description.

He did not mention that he had booked it two days ago for three times that price because a travel vlogger he followed had posted about Tacloban and he had felt the specific panic of someone about to be late to a trend.

He approached a flight attendant near the gate and asked if he could film a quick bit, a skit he had written in his Notes app in the taxi over, where she would pretend to bump into him and he would catch her. She looked at him for a moment with the patience of someone who had developed a great deal of it professionally, and said she wasn't allowed.

"Guys, worth a shot, right? Guys, you saw it here, Kuya Bong tried."

He said this to no one in particular. The gate area was nearly empty. The delay had thinned out even the few passengers there were, most of them drifting to the small cafe or finding walls to lean against. Bong counted nine other passengers for the flight. He filmed this too, panning slowly across the empty rows.

"Guys, nine passengers. Nine. This is basically a private charter. Guys, this is what budget travel looks like and I am here for every single second of it."

He was not, privately, here for every single second of it. He was tired and the hotdog had not been enough and the departure gate had no working aircon, but this was not content, so he did not film it.

The plane was small and loud and the nine other passengers were mostly older, businesspeople and a few who looked like they were going home. Nobody filmed anything. Nobody said _guys._ Bong got a window seat and filmed the tarmac and the takeoff and the darkness below, which was just darkness, nothing to see, but he kept the camera on it anyway.

He fell asleep somewhere over the Visayas and dreamed nothing he could remember.

---

They landed at Daniel Z. Romualdez Airport at twenty minutes past midnight.

Bong knew the name of the airport because he had Googled it on the plane and practiced saying it to camera. When they touched down he was still groggy and forgot to film the landing and then the runway lights were already behind them and they were taxiing toward a terminal that was very small and very dark.

"Guys," he said softly, phone already raised. "Guys, we have landed. Tacloban City, guys."

The terminal at that hour was the quietest public place he had ever been in. A few fluorescent lights. A baggage carousel that shuddered to life and delivered their nine bags one by one with a kind of grim efficiency. The other passengers collected their things and disappeared quickly, met by relatives, by a single van, by a man in a polo shirt holding a sign. Within ten minutes Bong was outside and essentially alone.

The night was warm and heavier than Manila nights. It smelled like salt.

There were two taxis. Both drivers looked at him. He looked at the taxis and then at his budget spreadsheet in his Notes app and then at the road beyond the small parking area, where a jeepney sat with its engine idling and its interior light on, that warm yellow that jeepneys always had, like the inside of something alive.

He knew what a jeepney cost.

Before he boarded he turned his phone on himself and panned slowly down the road stretching out from the airport, dark in both directions, the flat black suggestion of water just visible beyond the tree line to his right, the ocean closer to the road than he had expected.

"Guys, look at this. Middle of the night, guys, straight off the plane, and look at this road. This is Tacloban. This is what your budget gets you."

He watched it back. Dark. Grainy. Nothing much to see. He almost deleted it but kept it anyway, the way you keep things you think you might be able to use.

The driver was a man somewhere between fifty and sixty with a face that had been in the weather for a long time. He was eating from a small container and looked at Bong the way people look at things they are not surprised by.

"Kuya, alam po ninyo kung saan ang Real Street? Yung malapit sa Astro Dome?" Bong asked. (Do you know where Real Street is? Near Astro Dome?)

The driver nodded slowly. Ate another bite.

Bong climbed in, bumping his head on the entry, which he filmed.

"Guys! The budget travel experience, guys. This is a jeepney. If you don't know what a jeepney is, I will put a link in the description, but basically it is the most Filipino form of transportation there is and Kuya Bong is doing this the right way, guys. No taxi, no Grab, none of that. This is the real thing."

He liked the inside of the jeepney because it was the kind of thing that was good content without needing to be made into anything. The painted metal siding, the broken LED strip along the ceiling that flickered once and gave up. The small hanging ornaments near the driver. The Santo Nino mounted above the windshield, small and solemn, a rosary looped around the rearview mirror and swaying gently with the idle of the engine. The long benches running along both sides of the interior. The open back, and the darkness of the road behind them framed like a photograph of nothing.

He took the seat directly behind the driver on the left side, where a small sliding window gave him a view of the driver's face in profile. Good for the talking-to-a-local segment he had planned.

The jeepney did not move.

Bong understood this. He had read about it. Jeepneys waited until they had enough passengers to make the route worth running. He settled in and filmed several takes of himself explaining this to his audience, in the manner of someone who has just discovered something charming that everyone else already knew.

"Guys, so the jeepney will wait until it's full, or at least until the driver decides it's worth going. Guys, this is local life. You don't get this on a tour package."

After about ten minutes, headlights swept the road outside. A single motorcycle passing. Then a figure came walking from the direction of the terminal's side gate, a young man, early twenties maybe, wearing a mismatched basketball jersey and shorts from what looked like a local college, a small backpack over one shoulder, scrolling at his phone with the focused energy of someone in the middle of something that mattered on a screen. He barely glanced at the jeepney before climbing in with the automatic ease of someone who had done this a thousand times.

He sat in the middle of the bench opposite Bong, facing him, and immediately returned to his phone.

The driver finished his food, sealed his container, and started the engine properly.

They moved.

The road was straight and dark. On one side Bong could see, when the headlights caught it right, the flat black of open water. The sea was very close to the road. He had not expected that.

He filmed the water. He filmed the road retreating through the open back of the jeepney, the darkness swallowing it. He filmed his own face, making the expression he used when he wanted to convey atmosphere, eyebrows slightly raised, a small appreciative nod, the look of a man having an experience worth having.

The young man across from him did not look up from his phone.

Bong pointed his camera at him and zoomed in slowly, the kind of B-roll he liked, a real local doing a real local thing, and the young man shifted slightly in his seat without looking up, and Bong lowered the camera, not quite guilty, almost.

"Guys," he said quietly, more to himself than the camera now, "this is the life, guys."

They were perhaps two kilometers down the road when the jeepney slowed, though there was no stop that Bong could see, no landmark, no light, just the road and the dark pressing in on either side. He looked out the open back. Nothing. He looked at the driver, who was looking straight ahead.

Someone was standing at the edge of the road.

He saw her in the fraction of a second that the headlights passed over her, and then she was inside, and he understood only afterward that he had not actually seen her board, not really. He had seen her on the road and then she was simply there, at the far end of his bench, at the edge seat near the open back where the handrails were. The seat nearest the exit.

She was young. Younger than the local man across from him, younger than Bong, somewhere in that space between a child and not, the age where a good day still feels like it will last forever. Her hair was dark and stuck flat to the sides of her face the way hair does when it is very wet. Her clothes were light, the kind you wear to the beach on a day you have been looking forward to, a good day, the kind of day you remember. She was wet the way people are wet when they have just come from the water and have not had enough time to dry.

Bong looked at her.

She was looking at the road behind them through the open back, watching it the way you watch a place you are leaving, or the way you watch a place you have already left and are only now realizing it.

He thought, beach. He thought, odd hour for it. He thought, good detail for the vlog, actually.

He raised his phone. Framed her in the shot. Held it there for two seconds. Then lowered it, for reasons he could not have named if asked.

Across from him, the young man's hand extended without his eyes leaving his screen, automatic and unhesitating, the way a hand moves when the body has done something so many times it no longer needs to ask.

"_Alayon_, bayad."

Bong took the coins and the folded bill without thinking, because this too was automatic, because every Filipino who had ever been on a jeepney had been this exact link in this exact chain, the money passing from hand to hand down the bench to the driver, and thinking about it was like thinking about how to swallow.

He looked at it in his hand.

The money was wet.

Not damp from a pocket or a sweaty palm. Wet. The bill had gone soft with it, pliable in a way paper gets when it has been truly soaked. The coins were dark and cold in a way that had nothing to do with the warm night air.

He almost said _guys_ and then did not.

He looked up. The young man across from him had not moved. Same posture, same screen. But something in the set of his shoulders had changed, a tightness that had not been there before, and he had shifted on the bench, not much, just enough, away from the far end of Bong's side, away from the seat near the exit.

"_Ano 'to,_" Bong said, almost to himself. Then he raised his phone, because that was what he did when something confused him, and said to the camera in Tagalog, "_Guys, bakit basa 'tong bayad?_" and laughed, the laugh of someone who does not yet know if something is funny or not.

He leaned toward the small window at the driver's shoulder.

"_Manong,_ bayad po."

He held out the money.

The driver's eyes went to the rearview mirror. Not to Bong. To the back of the jeepney, to the far end of the bench, to the seat near the exit. They stayed there for a moment in a way that was very still and very deliberate.

Then they went back to the road.

"_Ibalik mo 'yan._" (Return it.)

Bong blinked. "_Manong, may dalawa po kaming pasahero, galing sa—_"

"_Ibalik mo._" (Return it.)

Not loud. The voice of someone repeating a thing they have already decided, the way you repeat something to a person who is standing at the edge of a mistake you cannot pull them back from, only name.

Bong looked at the young man across from him. "_Kuya,_" he said, "_ano 'to, bakit ayaw niya tanggapin?_" (Why won't the driver accept this?)

The young man looked up from his phone for the first time since he had boarded. His eyes went to the wet money in Bong's hand. Then to the far end of Bong's bench, quickly, just for a moment, and then away, to a fixed point on the floor somewhere between them. His jaw was set.

His lips were moving. Bong thought he was about to speak and leaned forward slightly. But the young man was not talking to him. His thumb was moving on his phone screen and when Bong looked he saw it was a prayer app, or an image of a saint, something small and luminous that the young man was now holding the way you hold the only thing you have.

Bong looked at the seat at the end of his bench.

The girl was not looking at the road anymore.

She was looking at him.

Her face was young and her eyes were dark and her hair was still plastered to the sides of her face and she was so wet there was a small pool collecting on the ribbed metal floor beneath her seat, and it was moving, that water, slowly, without urgency, finding the low points of the floor, following the faint tilt of the moving vehicle, easing toward him the way water always finds where it is going eventually.

His phone was still in his hand. The camera was still open.

He looked at the screen.

The seat at the end of the bench was empty. The cushion was dark with water. The floor beneath it was wet. The small pool moved on his screen exactly as it moved when he looked with his own eyes. But the seat held nothing. No girl. No wet hair. No one looking back at him.

He looked up from the screen.

She was still there.

He looked at the screen.

She was not.

The water reached the side of his shoe.

It was cold in a way that moved up through the sole and into the bones of his foot and did not stop there, not the cold of seawater, not the cold of any temperature he could name, but something older than temperature, cold like the memory of cold, cold that had been patient.

The driver said nothing. The jeepney moved. The road outside was dark and flat and the sea was very close and Bong did not know, had no way of knowing, that this road had once been under water, that the sea had come across it on a morning in November in a single unstoppable movement and taken everything on it, the houses and the trees and the people who were only going somewhere, and that when the water went back it did not take everything it had touched back with it.

He did not know she had been having the best day of her life.

He held the wet money in his hand and the cold moved through him and he opened his mouth and closed it and the jeepney went on through the dark.

---

He did not remember deciding to put the money down.

He remembered the guesthouse. The woman at the front desk looking at him. He remembered saying _yes, guys, all good_ out of habit, and her expression after.

He did not post anything that night.

He booked the next flight home and did not see the city at all.

He did not post for a week.

When he finally did, he uploaded the jeepney footage raw, no music, no intro, no tagline, untitled, and went to sleep.

He woke up to 300,000 views and a comments section pointing at a clip he barely remembered. The one he shot on the airport road when he first landed, before any of it. The slow pan he did of the dark empty road just to set the scene, the one he had almost deleted because there was nothing in it worth keeping.

At the edge of the frame, standing very still, was a girl with wet hair.

She was already there when he landed.

Bong closed the app. He sat for a long time in his apartment in Taguig, far from any sea, and looked at nothing.

He has stopped checking why, some mornings, there are small wet footprints on the floor leading from the door to his chair. Or why the room sometimes smells, faintly, of salt air and open water, in a city that is nowhere near the sea.

He no longer says _guys._
