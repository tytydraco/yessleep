It was 4 AM, that strange, gray hour where everything feels slightly off. 

Not night, not morning. 

Just a hollow silence that doesn’t feel natural. 

The air was thick with the scent of wet asphalt and that biting pre-dawn chill that settles deep in your bones.

I saw my dad, my mom, and my brother outside by the trunk. The yellow porch light cast long, shaky shadows across the driveway, making the car look like a dark, crouched beast. 

My dad was stacking bags with that focused, heavy silence he always had before a long drive. 

He didn't look up, just moved with a mechanical precision. 

My mom stood by the passenger door, her arms crossed tightly against the cold, reminding him in a hushed whisper not to forget the smaller bags we left on the steps. 

My brother leaned against the rear door, half asleep, his face illuminated by the pale blue glow of his phone screen.

Normal. 

Completely normal.

I helped them with the bags, I could feel the rough fabric of the suitcases under my palms, the cold, wet metal of the trunk's rim, and the sharp bite of the wind on my neck. 

Everything felt real. Solid. 

Grounded. 

I realized I forgot my charger and ran back inside, the door creaking behind me.

The house was pitch black, a jarring contrast to the porch light. 

But the TV was on in the living room, casting a rhythmic, flickering blue light into the hallway. 

It stretched the shadows of the furniture into distorted shapes that seemed to twitch in the corner of my eye.

My family was sitting there, My dad in his favorite recliner, My brother sprawled on the couch, and My mom in her usual seat. 

Everything looked normal. 

But they didn’t look at me. 

They didn't turn their heads or acknowledge the sound of my footsteps. 

I didn’t stop. 

I was in a hurry, pushing through the heavy air of the hallway, heading straight for the stairs.

Something felt off, though. 

Not wrong in a way I could name… just late. 

It was like the room noticed me a second after I was already gone. 

Like the air behind me shifted only when I reached the first step. 

I was halfway into my room, my hand reaching for the charger on the nightstand, when the realization hit me. 

A cold, paralyzing wave of terror.

I just saw them outside.

I stopped. 

I stood there in the dark, my heart hammering against my ribs like a trapped bird. 

There was no way they had come in that fast. 

No way I had missed three people walking past me in a narrow hallway. 

My hand tightened on the door handle. 

My phone vibrated in my pocket. 

A sharp, aggressive buzz that felt like an electric shock.

A message from my dad: “Where are you? Why are you late?”

I stared at the screen, my breath hitching. 

No signal. No Wi-Fi. 

The "No Service" icon was clear at the top. 

But the message was there. 

It had arrived through the dead air. 

I slowly turned toward the open door, looking into the dark hallway. 

The blue TV light was still flickering downstairs, dancing on the ceiling.

Then I heard it. 

“Son?”

My dad’s voice. Perfect. Exactly like him. 

The same weary, impatient tone. 

“Bring me some water.”

I didn’t move. 

I couldn’t. 

Because something finally clicked in my head, 

a detail I had pushed aside in my hurry. 

When I walked past them in the living room… none of them had moved. 

And none of them had blinked. 

Their skin looked like wax under the flickering blue light, pale and unnervingly smooth. 

They were staring at a screen of static with wide, dead eyes that didn't reflect the light.

I stepped back into my room and shut the door as quietly as I could. 

I turned the lock. 

The click sounded like a gunshot in the silence. 

My hands were shaking so hard I almost dropped my phone.

Silence. 

For a few agonizing seconds… nothing.

Then.. a slow creak. 

It came from the bottom of the stairs. 

It wasn't the sound of footsteps. 

It was weight. 

Like something immensely heavy was pressing down on the wood, learning how to move for the first time. 

Creak. Pause. Creak. 

Closer now. Too close.

“I’m thirsty.”

The voice was right outside my door. 

I stared at the thin strip of light underneath it, expecting to see shoes. 

There were no feet. 

Just something wide and dark… blocking the light. Then..

three knocks. 

Not on the door. 

On the wall. 

Right next to my bed. 

Right where my head would be if I were sleeping.

“The water, son.”

I stopped breathing. 

I tried to become invisible. 

Then my phone vibrated again. 

Another message from the dad outside: “Forget the charger. We’re leaving. Get out here NOW or you're staying behind.”

And then..

before I could even react, the thing outside my room spoke again. 

Same voice. 

Same impatient tone. 

“Forget the charger. We’re leaving. Get out here NOW or you're staying behind.”

It said the words a second before my phone buzzed with the text. 

Word for word. 

It wasn't just imitating my dad. 

It was imitating the future. 

I froze. 

Outside, I heard the muffled roar of the car engine starting up in the driveway. 

They were leaving. 

My real family was leaving me.

And whatever was outside my room… wasn’t trying to get in anymore. 

It didn't need to. 

It already knew how to get me out.

“We’ve been waiting for you,” 

it whispered, and I realized the voice was no longer coming from the hallway. 

It was coming from under my bed.
