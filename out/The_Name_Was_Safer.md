I worked night dispatch in north Alabama long enough to hear every kind of panic a human being can fit through a phone line.

 

Domestic fights. Drunks in ditches. Men screaming about deer through their windshields. Old women reporting prowlers who turned out to be possums with criminal confidence. If you have never heard a grown man describe a raccoon in his attic like it has diplomatic immunity, you have not lived a full life.

 

The weird calls had categories. That was the first thing dispatch taught me, and it taught me unofficially. Officially, everything had codes and incident types and response priorities. The paperwork was clean.

 

The world was not.

 

The older dispatchers kept their own taxonomy under the official one. Lights over the mountain, you wrote aerial disturbance and kept your opinions between you and Jesus. A woman screaming in the woods, you wrote possible animal distress, unless she used the words help me. And if somebody saw something white where nothing white had any business being, you did not ask what kind.

 

You wrote White Thang.

 

That was the rule.

 

I learned it in 1997 from a dispatcher named Marlene, who smoked Virginia Slims in her car with the windows cracked like the cigarette was holding her hostage.

 

My third week, a man called from a stretch of county road between Walnut Grove and a name I will not say out loud anymore. He said something big and white had crossed in front of his truck.

 

I asked, "Was it a deer?"

 

Marlene rolled her chair over so fast I thought the wheels would catch fire. She muted my headset and said, "Don't feed it choices."

 

I thought she was messing with me. Night people get strange. Your brain starts chewing the walls around three in the morning. So, I unmuted and asked the caller to describe the animal.

 

That was mistake number one.

 

He said it was shaped like a dog. Then no, maybe a lion, but walking wrong. Long white hair. A tail with a bush on the end. Bigger than anything in Alabama had a right to be. He kept changing it as he talked, trying answers on like a man pulling shirts off a rack, none of them fitting.

 

Then his voice went flat. He said, "It heard me."

 

The line filled with a sound I still cannot name. Not a scream. Not static. More like a wet hand sliding slow across a window.

 

He whispered, "It's got the tail now."

 

The one he'd named. The last thing he gave it.

 

Then he hung up.

 

I entered the call as suspicious animal, because I was young and held a degree from the University of Knowing Better Than Old Women. Marlene reached over and changed the entry to White Thang before the deputy was even dispatched.

 

"Why?" I asked.

 

"Because that name is safer."

 

They found his truck at daylight. Door open. Keys in the ignition. Coffee still warm enough to fog the lid. No blood. No tracks but his own boots, walking from the driver's door into a drainage ditch and stopping there like God had used an eraser.

 

After that, I listened.

 

Not perfectly. I'm still a man, and men are gifted at turning one survived warning into a future disaster. But I learned the shape of the thing without ever once asking it to hold still.

 

A woman near Jones Valley said there was a naked pale person standing in the ditch with no eyes and no ears. I typed White Thang and told her to keep driving and not slow down.

 

A kid in Blount County said a white-haired thing stood by the power pole watching his house. His mother got on the line, mad as fire, saying somebody needed to do something. I typed White Thang and told her to close the blinds without looking back outside.

 

She did. The kid grew up. I check, sometimes.

 

That was the part that kept me sane. Not everybody disappeared. Some got home. Some called back the next morning embarrassed, trying to turn it into an owl, a bear, a white dog, fog, headlights, anything with bones that made sense. I let them.

 

People think the truth is always safer than a vague word. It is not. Sometimes a vague word is a fence. Sometimes being accurate is an invitation.

 

The ones who lived said, "I saw something." The ones who got hurt tried to describe it.

 

That was the pattern, and I was the one who held it. Thirty years I sat in that room and typed the same three words over every white thing the county coughed up. I typed those words more times than I said most prayers. I thought I was protecting the callers.

 

I never once stopped to ask who I was feeding.

 

I retired last winter. Bought a little place outside the city where the porch light pulls every moth in the county and my knees file complaints like unpaid subcontractors. I sleep bad. Dispatch does that. Your body spends thirty years learning that a ringing phone means somebody's worst night, and then retirement asks you to enjoy the quiet like a normal person.

 

Three weeks ago, my old supervisor called. Not Marlene. She died in 2019. This was Daniel, who runs nights now.

 

He asked if I remembered the White Thang protocol. I told him I did.

 

He laughed once, no humor in it. "We had one tonight."

 

Put it in the notes and don't make them describe it, I told him. That's the protocol.

 

"That's the problem," Daniel said. "The caller asked for you."

 

The yard past my porch light was full dark. The pines stood still.

 

I'm retired, I said.

 

"I know."

 

Who was the caller, I asked.

 

He didn't answer for a second. Then he said my full name.

 

My stomach did the old dispatch drop. Not fear yet. The thing that comes before fear, the half second when the call connects and some part of you already knows.

 

"The caller sounded like you," Daniel said. Not similar. Not close.

 

Me.

 

Send me the incident number, I told him. He said he already had. The email hit my phone while he was still talking.

 

I opened it, because being smart is not the same as being wise, and any quality man will tell you the two come from different departments.

 

The notes were short.

 

Caller reports white subject at rear tree line. Caller refuses to describe. Caller states name is safer. Caller states retired dispatcher will understand. Caller advises do not let him look out the kitchen window.

 

I was standing in my kitchen.

 

Of course I was. That's where old men stand when the phone scares them. By the sink, one hand on the counter, a coffee mug close by, pretending tile and appliances are a defensive position.

 

I did not look out the window. I need you to hear that. I did not look.

 

The blinds were already down over the sink, cheap vinyl, bent on one side from a fight I had with them after I hung a little shelf and lost, because home improvement is mostly humiliation with screws.

 

Behind the blinds, something tapped the glass. Once. Then twice.

 

Then it said my name.

 

Not a monster voice. Not a demon. It said it the way I say it when I answer an unknown number and want them to know I'm not buying the warranty. My own flat, tired, get-to-the-point voice.

 

I backed into the fridge hard enough to knock a magnet loose. Daniel was still talking. I couldn't hear him over my own pulse.

 

The thing outside said, "Dispatcher."

 

That was worse. Nobody in this life calls me that. My kids say Dad. My neighbors say sir. Dispatcher belonged to the room I'd left, the room where I had said the name to it, over and over, for thirty years, until it learned the mouth that said it.

 

The bottom slat bent inward. Not enough to see through. Enough to show me it had fingers.

 

I got the phone up to my face. Put it in as White Thang, I whispered.

 

"Already did," Daniel said.

 

The slat bent again, and this time there was skin in the gap. White. Smooth. Hairless. Pressed close to the glass.

 

"Do not describe it," Daniel said.

 

I knew the rule. I wrote the rule into a hundred other men's worst nights. I should have set the phone down, walked to the bathroom, locked the door, climbed in the tub like a tornado was coming, and recited county codes until the sun came up.

 

Instead, I saw one detail through the bent blind. One stupid detail. A brown spot on the left wrist. Same size, same place, as the one on mine.

 

And my brain did what a terrified, arrogant brain does. It tried to make the evidence hold still. It tried to be accurate.

 

"It looks like me," I said.

 

Out loud. Into the line.

 

The same mistake the man on the county road made in 1997, the night he named the tail. I gave it the choice. I told it which one of us to be.

 

The tapping stopped. Daniel started cussing.

 

Outside the window, in my own flat voice, the White Thang laughed.

 

That was three weeks ago.

 

Since then, my driver's license photo has gone soft and pale, the face washing out of the plastic. My old work ID turned white from the face out, like somebody dripped bleach inside the laminate. My bathroom mirror still gives me back to me if I don't stare too long, but cameras can't hold me. Last week the grocery store self-checkout flagged me as item not found.

 

That would have been funny if I hadn't almost cried beside a bag of onions.

 

Every night something walks my tree line. Some nights it has no face. Some nights it has mine, and it's getting better at it.

 

I will not call it a man. I will not call it an animal, a ghost, a cryptid, a spirit, a crawler, a pale person, or anything else your curious internet brain wants to try on it. I fed it for thirty years with the safest word I had, and the safest word is the only thing keeping the last of me on this side of the glass.

 

I call it the White Thang. That name is safer.

 

And if you ever see something white in an Alabama ditch, do yourself a favor.

 

Don't describe it. Don't help it choose.
