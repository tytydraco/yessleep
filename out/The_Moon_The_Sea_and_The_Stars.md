I was still a child when I first boarded that strange vessel. I look back at that fateful moment now with regret. I’m not quite sure what had even possessed me to step foot onto the boat. I remember a soft and beautiful whisper. I can still hear it sometimes, I think. But after all that’s happened, I’m not sure if it’s real or just some delusion of mine.

I was a child when I found myself near the docks, that I’m sure of. I was with friends, of course, but their names elude me. My mother scolded me quite often about the dangers of the docks; infested with con-men, drunks, and all around “ill-folk” as she put it. Despite my mother’s constant and painful reminders, my hard head did not soften. In fact, I seemed to grow bolder the more my mother disciplined me. No doubt my own little acts of defiance gave me some agency when I felt smothered.

I believe it was a game of hide and seek we played. Whether I was a hider or seeker matters little to me now. But I remember that soft, creeping whisper. As if a devilish little spider had tip-toed into my ear. I couldn’t quite make out the words, but they were gentle, yet irritating. I followed the source, determined to see what could be behind such a lovely nuisance.

I pushed through crowds of people and stumbled over loose boards and boxes until I came across a small fishing boat, smaller than anything else on the dock. The voice had gotten louder, like a raspy shout. The wind picked up, pushing me ever closer to the small vessel. That evil boat almost seemed to reach out and grab me. I resisted, at first, but to no avail. I pushed my second thoughts deep within the pits of my being and stepped aboard.

The moment I had both feet onboard, that wretched but sweet sound stopped. After a few seconds of still silence, I began to pace around the boat, gathering my surroundings. There was a strong stench of mildew hanging in the air, almost clogging my nostrils. Yet within that laid a hint of something sweet, as if someone had sprayed a strong perfume to mask the smell. Sea water leaked through the deck, while elsewhere, showed signs of cracks and shoddy repair jobs.

My curiosity waned into disappointment as I made my way off the boat. Before I could take the last step, the boat began to rock and sway violently. There was nothing for me to hold onto, and I fell down onto the deck, hitting my head. It was there that I lost consciousness. I have no memory of the inbetweens, but I’m sure I could not have slept for long. It was dark when I woke up. It was a miracle I didn’t end up face down, drowning in the shallow water.

I sat up, contemplating my poor situation for a moment. My mother would be absolutely livid. I almost considered never going back. I could just stay on the ship forever and avoid her wrath. Thinking back on it, I'd rather her scold me everyday, than the fate I endured. Eventually, I stood up, and I sat back down almost immediately. For a moment I thought I’d lost my mind. At this point my mind is lost now, but then I was sure I had my wits about me. I stood again, looked around with bewildered and wild eyes, then shot back down to the ground, still in disbelief. I squeezed my eyes shut tightly and clenched my fists. I thought that maybe I was in a dream. Maybe I hadn’t really woken up yet.

I scooped up some water and splashed it into my face aggressively. I shook my head a few times and gathered my courage as I stood once more. My fears were confirmed by my horrid eyesight. It seemed that this poor, wretched vessel had somehow found its way to the Moon. It still sounds quite insane to me and I still question that night. Yet, often in my feeble memory, that fact seems clear. I might have panicked or cried, or maybe even let out a little laugh. But after that initial wave of silent terror washed away, I remember one thing clearly. That strong feeling of reckless wonder.

The expanse surrounding me was vast and, seemingly, endless. The surface was riddled with holes and craters, as if some large dog had taken to digging this area up. The air felt empty and cold as I wagered a step off the boat. Immediately, my body became lighter. I floated to the ground as if my body had been replaced with that of a feather. I remember giggling a bit as I took more steps, slow and methodical.

My amusement only grew. Before long, I had graduated from steps and stomps to hops and leaps. My laughter echoed out into that empty field and back at me joining me in my joy. I’d pick up rock and throw them, marveling as they fell to the ground as a sheet of paper might. Thoughts of cartwheels and handstands invaded my young mind as I let loose, unbounded by gravity. I felt untouchable and without limits. Even while thinking back at that moment as the beginning of my doom, I remember fondly how truly free I felt.

I spent a good amount of time frolicking and throwing rocks, but eventually, I was overcome with an overwhelming sense of boredom. My laughter slowly ground to a halt as thoughts of home rushed into my mind. My mother and my friends. I’m sure they were worried about me. I still had to go to school in the morning. Would I be late? Would I miss it? I know I cried at that moment, but I’ve cried so much since this it almost doesn’t matter.

I sluggishly made my way back to the boat and plopped down onto the soaked deck. I leaned my back against a creaky board in anticipation. I didn’t know when the boat would move again, but I knew it eventually had to. I couldn’t be stranded. Mother wouldn’t allow it, was my first thought. I don’t remember when I fell asleep but I remember when I woke up. It was to the sound of shouts and laughter. Under the heat of a high Sun. I sat up, quickly realizing that I was home. Although, something was off.

The dock felt a little different, but I couldn’t quite place it. When I stood, the ground seemed further away. I remember yawning and being startled at how different my voice seemed. At the time, that seemed to be the least of my worries. I quickly came to my senses and hopped off that old fishing boat, cursing it as I sprinted back towards home. My mind raced and prodded for excuses that I could offer. I knew she would never believe my accidental excursion. I hardly believed it at the time. I figured I would just say a gang of boys chased me off and I had to hide. I was sure she’d be too worried to be angry at me.

When I finally arrived home, I hesitated. The grass had grown quite a bit. My home seemed a bit more worn. I thought it strange, but my mother was the pressing matter, so I moved on. I knocked three - no - four times, and waited. A few seconds went by and knocked four more times for good measure. A few more seconds and before I could knock again, an older woman, closely resembling my mother, opened the door. But it couldn’t be her, no, my mother was much younger.

She was also much shorter. I could, just barely, see over the top of her head. The woman stared at me silently and intently for a few seconds. I tried explaining that I must be at the wrong house and asked if she knew my mother. Her eyes widened and she grabbed my face, inspecting it, running her hands across my cheek and brow. I backed away, startled by the suddenness. What was wrong with her? Then she cried and spoke my name.

Maybe she knew my mother? She slowly stepped towards me, sobbing softly. She grabbed my face again, much gentler than before, and looked deep into my eyes. She spoke my name once more. My mother spoke my name once more. How had she shrunk so much? Grown so old? I was only gone for a night, just a night, that much I knew, I KNEW that. I knew.

And then I was hit with a sudden realization. If my mother had grown old, what of me? I pushed past her and ran into the house. Her hurried footsteps and shouts trailed behind me as I raced straight into the washroom. I looked into the mirror. It was not my face I saw, but the face of a man. I don’t remember much of that first moment.

I remember my mother holding me, crying as stared blankly into another man’s eyes. I remember laying in bed for many nights after. Conversations with my mother were short and I never did explain what happened that night. I don’t think she wanted an explanation, for fear of what she might learn. She seemed content enough to have me back, even if the whole of me was shattered. I was not me. Not completely. I hardly ate, I hardly spoke. I moved about as if I were an empty husk. Some days I would walk into town, without a destination, pondering what I had lost.

I’m not sure how many days and nights went on like that; pretending as if I hadn’t lost my childhood. Pretending my mother hadn’t lost her son. My friends had grown and some started families. I grew infuriated at how much I had missed. But my fury was hollow. I needed somewhere to place it. The boat. That cursed boat. I was asleep when it kidnapped me. Maybe, if I knew what happened, maybe I could take it back. I could fix it and have my life go back to normal. Folly.

I waited till nightfall and made my way to the docks, now determined. As soon as I arrived, I heard that devilish spider once more. That soft, irritating whisper acted as a hook on my ear and dragged me back to that cursed vessel. I glared furiously at the cause of my misfortune for a moment, and then leapt aboard. I resolved myself not to sit, but to stand so as to keep my mind awake. I held tightly to the nearest surface I could find, in case the boat rocked and shook as it did before. This time would be different.

The boat began to rock, gently, while the water below started to bubble and churn. The rocking of the boat started to pick up speed as the churning of that water grew more violent. My grip weakened as I spit out salt water and attempted to shield my eyes. Then something unexpected happened. Even now I still doubt myself, but I’m sure - I know - the ship began to plummet.

It was so fast and intense that I could not even muster a scream. I merely grit my teeth as my stomach dropped and I was lifted into the air. The only thing keeping me in the boat was my fearful grip. It was not long before everything grew to a deep dark pitch black. With my vision obscured, my fear only heightened. I began to think that this would be the end of my short, wasted life.

But then, the boat began to slow its descent. I lost my grip and fell to the deck. In that quiet darkness, my panic was replaced with bewilderment as I realized that I could still breathe. I was still surrounded by air, but I was certain the ship had gone down. It definitely went down. I sat in that darkness for minutes, slowly going mad, refusing to move out of fear. The silence had turned to a constant drumming hum, driving me mad with anticipation.

At a sudden instance, a light appeared from outside of the boat. A small, soft blue glow. More little lights began to appear all around the boat, like little specters. I could see a bit of my surroundings. See that the boat seemed to be in a bubble of sorts. The boat had more surprises for me. From the distance and quickly approaching, I heard groans and chirps of all different types. Before I could take a breath, creatures of unfathomable shapes, sizes, and colors began to illuminate that deep dark sea and swirl around the vessel.

The beings I witnessed could not have been from our world. They all glowed soft and cool blues, reds, oranges, purples. The shapes their bodies took were like no fish I’d ever seen, nor any land animal I had ever put eyes on. For some, it was as if I could see through them. See their bones, their hearts. They danced around, as if they had been waiting for a visitor, for someone to witness their beauty. Behemoths rushed past and shook the poor boat, knocking me off balance, but I was too in awe to care. Even more than my cursed journey to the Moon, this moment engraved itself in my being.

Eventually awe passed over me and I realized my mission. I came back for a reason. I’d find out what this boat truly was and make it give me my life back. I frantically scoured every single inch of my horrid prison. I flipped open every barrel, tossed over every sheet or blanket. I even tried peeling away some of the loose and decaying boards. Above and below deck, there was nothing even slightly out of the ordinary. Despite all odds, it was just a normal fishing boat.

As my search dwindled, those horribly beautiful creatures slowly drifted away and back out into the darkness. It was as if the boat knew what I wanted, and once it realized I was done, I was thrown back into darkness. I plopped down onto the soggy deck, hugging my knees. The silence seemed to sit beside me, taunting me. I let out a small chuckle, laughing at the absurdity of my situation. That laugh soon turned into a small sob, and that sob into a choking cry. A minute or so might have passed before I gained my composure and was greeted with silence once more.

I remember laying down on my side, the salt of my tears mixing with the salt of seawater, almost indistinguishable. I closed my eyes and I must have drifted off to sleep. I remember waking in the same condition I had before. Soaking wet, with the sun beating down on me. I laid there, defeated, for quite some time. I ignored the bustle and sounds of the docks. It was maybe an hour before I decided to move. I sat up slowly and scratched my face, feeling some hair there. This was the only confirmation I needed. My journey was once again, unsuccessful.

More years of my life, gone. How old was I? I had already aged from a boy to a man. At this point, I must be approaching my autumn years. I slowly departed from the boat, cursing it again. My walk back home was slow, filled with detours as I walked around town to see what had aged in my absence. There were more unfamiliar faces than before. Or maybe they were just familiar faces grown unrecognizable by time?

When I reached my home, I hesitated. It seemed just about the same as before, but a little more worn. What would my mother think? To lose her son a second time? Would she even look at me? I sighed and knocked. Once. Twice. Thrice. I waited. And I did not knock again. I turned to leave and at that moment the door swung open. It was a man, much older than I. The only thing on his face I can remember is the sad and tired eyes. Yet in those same eyes I could see the faintest smile.

I hesitated slightly, but then I spoke up, asking if he had seen my mother. At that moment, the smile left his eyes and something more akin to anger revealed itself. I backed away as he stepped outside and shut the door quietly, as if he didn’t want to alert anyone. He started into my eyes, my soul, piercing me with what seemed to be a deep hatred. And he spoke.

He told me of the troubles my mother faced. She lost her husband, my father, not long after I was born. When I was a child, she lost me too. She was devastated. Heartbroken beyond all repair. But then I came back. She began to hope again. Even in those terrible moments where I refused to speak. When I refused to eat. Even as the lifeless shell of my body lazily waltzed around her home, that was enough for her. Just me being there was enough. But then I disappeared again. No. I left. I left her. He made sure to emphasize that word. Left.

He threatened me. It had taken a miracle for my mother to even smile again. According to him, me coming back would only make things worse again. She had finally moved on. She had found solace in my absence. Her hope once dwindled sparked into a new joy that maybe, wherever I was, I was happy. And she could live with that. How could I come back into her world and reveal the woe and anguish I have endured? How selfish of me.

I do not know whether I made the right decision. At that point in time, it seemed too late for right or wrong decisions. When the old man finished, I could only sigh and nod as I turned away. I walked back into town, aimless once more. I found a dark, damp alleyway and lay leaning across a wall. Amongst the dirt, the garbage, and the little rodents, I felt I most belonged. How could I call myself a person when I had been deprived of personal experience? I had become a mere animal scavenging for some sense of self. Seemingly halfway through my life I had done nothing. I had nothing. And as the days went by and the fury and sadness intermingled and birthed some new enraging feeling I had a new thought.

The boat. That cursed boat. It had to pay for what it did to me. I could destroy it, punish it for taking my life from me. In the cover of night, I swiftly broke into a shop and stole oil and candles by which to enact my plan. Repercussions were the least of my worries. I sprinted towards that wretched vessel, fury and wind carrying me to my destination. That devilish spider did not accompany me now. As if the boat feared my coming and refused to beckon me.

I did not hesitate. When I arrived I jumped onto the boat. I poured the oil across every surface I could reach, making sure to douse it properly with what little I had. But I was sure even a small fire would do the job. When that was done, I stepped off the boat and threw the lit candle towards the sail, setting it ablaze.

Before me rose a great and terrible flame. The heat seemed to reach out and grab me as it roared ever louder. I stepped back, refusing to shield my eyes, despite the pain, so I could witness the death of my captor. As this great fire lit up the dock, a smile crept up my face. Maybe, I would not get my life back, but I could avenge those lost years. Surely!

But as the flames slowly died down my smile turned into cries of anguish. That wretched vessel. Untouched in the face of those uproarious flames. It was as if nothing had happened. The rocking and creaking of the boat sounded almost like laughter, as if it was mocking me. In my rage, I screamed and leapt onto the boat. I felt determined to destroy it any other way I could think of, but I would not get the chance. As soon as my feet hit the deck, the vessel shot upwards into the air.

It shot up with enough force to push me down onto the deck, as if I were being held down by a giant hand. I clenched my teeth and my fists. What had I done? In my anger, I had once again made that same mistake. Where would this foul creature take me next? I tried my best not to swallow the stale seawater that pooled on the deck, constantly sputtering as I struggled to move. Before long, I could feel the ship slow and control of my body was returned to me.

I stood up quickly and shook my sleeves in a feeble attempt to dry myself. As I scanned my surroundings, I could find only anger in place of the awe my child self might feel. I walked to the front of the small boat and gripped the railing, digging my nails into the soaked and brittle wood. I could feel the splinters dig into my hands as I fought off the urge to rip up the railing with my bare hands. A cold and heavy wind pushed against my face, almost suffocating me, as this evil ship raced through the stars.

I was surrounded by millions upon millions of specks of light. The same stars I marvelled at as a child, seemed no bigger out in this vast space. In fact, they seemed smaller, as if I could only grasp their true distance while in their domain. I never understood why some called space a void. How could you call it such a thing when it was infested with stars and planets? I realized then, that the void was the loneliness within my soul. How careless and stupid of me to think that I could somehow avenge my lost years. This poor fishing boat had made a fool of me. It taunted me with wondrous sights knowing that it would take my life in exchange.

I loosened my grip on the railing, pulling back my bloodied hands and wiping them on my seawater soaked shirt, hardly noticing the sting of the salt mingling with my wounds. I fell down defeatedly, refusing to cry or scream. I merely stared into the shallow pool of water, past my sad reflection and into nothingness. I closed my eyes, pushing all dreadful thoughts to the side as I waited for sleep to take me.

I awoke the same way I had before, damp and groggy under a beating Sun. I did not stand immediately. I couldn’t. As I sat up and shifted my weight, I could feel bones cracking and muscles aching. The pain in my back was sharp and worked its way throughout my body. I looked down at my wrinkled and spotted hands as I leaned over and attempted to stand, failing and flopping onto the ground. I groaned in pain, startled at my deep and raspy voice.

Before I could give up, I heard the sounds of little footsteps and shocked voices. Children? Had they seen me fall? Were they here to help? A realization quickly washed over me as I realized. I could not let those children step foot on the boat. They could not share my fate. I screamed and thrashed yelling in my hoarse voice. Every obscenity I could muster, I lashed at them. They squealed and ran away at the sight of a crazed old man rolling around and cursing.

Soon, a few older gentlemen pushed past the children and made their way towards me. I screamed at them as well, warning them not to step aboard. I cursed and threw things at them, splashing around in the shallow water. They ignored the ramblings of an old man and grabbed me. They were gentle, as they lifted me up and dragged me away. I kicked and screamed. I thought if I fought against them hard enough, they’d leave me. The men were too determined and kind. As we left the boat, one of them led me into a nearby bar.

The man argued quietly with the bartender before I was taken to a room in the back. I was offered a change of clothes. Left in the room alone, I changed out of my wet and filthy clothes into the fresh set I had been given. They had even left a nice pair of sandals for me. Every inch of my body groaned as I paced towards the mirror in the corner of the room. I sneered at the sight of my withered body, disgusted at the way my skin sagged on my bones, wrinkled and spotted. I wanted to punch the mirror, but I knew I would only hurt myself instead.

When I walked out of the room I was greeted by two men. One was the bartender, and the other the man who had led me inside. I could tell they had just finished arguing about something. Most likely me. The kind man gently took me by the arm and guided me towards the bar. He sat me down and the bartender was behind him a few moments later, with a plate of food and water. They asked me question upon question, but I only stared lifelessly down at my plate as I slowly ate the food. I would occasionally offer a grunt or a nod, no more.

When I finished the plate, I grabbed the glass of water and chugged it loudly. I looked up at the both of them, the concern on their faces almost sending me into a rage. I took one large sigh, thanked the two strangers, and stood to walk out. The kind man tried to stop me, but the bartender put a hand on his shoulder and pulled him back. I nodded and walked out.

As I walked the streets of my town, I had that constant feeling of a word you know but can’t quite remember. It was my town, I knew that, but I recognized none of it. Just like I was a shell of my former self, so was my humble home. I did not acknowledge the existence of a single soul as I made my way towards my destination. I opted for the one place I knew my mother would be.

I knew exactly where to find my mother’s grave marker. I had visited my father's grave many times as a boy, and I knew she’d put hers next to his. I slowly sat down and crossed my legs, grunting and wincing at the pain of doing so. I could not cry, at first. I simply picked at the grass and told my mother a lie. I made up a story of how I ran off to the city and got a job in a nice office. How I met a few girls until I finally found the one I would marry. How I bought a house and had three children and a dog. How my children grew up and how they visited me with their children. How my wife passed, and our family attended her funeral. How I was just stopping by to say hi before I would go back home to babysit my grandchildren as my eldest son went on vacation with his wife. As I neared the end of my tall tale, I began to sob. Oh, how much I will miss my dear mother.
