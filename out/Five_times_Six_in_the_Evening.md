You know, boredom can make you do a lot of things. Curiosity makes it worse. Mix the two together and you have yourself a fine cocktail that mostly leads to absolutely nothing. Emphasis on mostly. Every now and then, though, you get lucky. You find something unusual. Something that stays with you long after you leave.

This was not one of those times.

The light around me was slowly fading from orange to a tired pinkish red, and that too was being swallowed by a sheet of grey clouds. The wind was persistent. It picked up dust and bits of trash and carried them aimlessly across the ground. The chatter of tourists was thinning out. People were leaving. I would have to leave soon as well.

I had already spent nearly two hours here and had not found anything like what I had found last week.

Still, I was certain there was something here.

I just had to look deeper.

Last week, I had gone for a walk near a small lake not far from my house. I picked up a couple of cigarettes from a roadside vendor and walked around looking for a place where I could sit alone and smoke without interruption. The usual crowd was there. Students killing time. Couples pretending they were not being watched. Men with nothing better to do. I walked past all of them and headed toward a denser stretch of foliage surrounded by scattered rocks.

It looked quiet enough.

A large banyan tree stood in the way. Its roots hung down like ropes, some brushing against my shoulders as I passed. The tree looked old. Unnaturally old. I expected some disturbance from within it, crows or bats perhaps, but nothing moved.

The shade grew heavier as I stepped further in.

While scanning the rocks for a place to sit, I noticed a narrow passage tucked between them. It would have gone unnoticed if not for the carving at its entrance.

The carving was crude but deliberate. A stick-like figure wearing what seemed to be a hat. That part was ordinary. The limbs were not.

It had four arms and one leg.

There was something about it that suggested age. Not decades. Possibly centuries.

Naturally, I walked into the path.

The pathway was laid entirely with stone, worn down and uneven in places. It was just wide enough for one person to pass comfortably. As I continued, the rocks thinned out and trees returned on either side. The ground sloped downward gradually.

It was not a peaceful walk. Mosquitoes swarmed relentlessly. I nearly startled myself into a panic when a chameleon shifted across a branch and for a second I thought it was a snake. A rat, unusually large, crossed the path in front of me and disappeared into the undergrowth.

I kept walking.

Eventually, the path split into five.

One path led directly ahead into what looked like an opening in the ground. The entrance was framed with the same stone as the walkway, but beyond a few feet it was completely dark. The other four curved into dense vegetation. Each of them had faint streaks of reddish yellow discoloration along the stones.

All of them were clearly constructed. Designed.

I stood there longer than I should have.

I did not have the time to explore five paths. And I did not have the resolve to walk into the one that led underground.

So I turned back.

As I retraced my steps, I lit a cigarette. The path was easier to follow on the way out. By the time I reached the edge of the trail near my residential area, the light had dimmed considerably. I threw the cigarette down and crushed it under my shoe.

That was when I noticed the wall.

It was a fragment of an old stone boundary. Broken on both sides, only a short stretch left standing. I had seen similar walls across the city before. They were remnants of the boundary of the kingdom that once ruled this region.

The same kingdom that built the Golkonda Fort.

The thought occurred to me that perhaps those paths were connected to something older than they appeared.

Later that night, I looked into it. The fort was not far from where I lived. It was open to the public, though many sections were restricted. Certain areas had been closed off decades ago due to structural concerns, and the list had grown since then.

While searching through images of the fort, I saw something familiar.

The carving.

The same four-armed, one-legged figure appeared in scattered sections of the structure. There was no clear explanation for it. No detailed record of what it represented.

That was enough.

I decided to visit.

Now I stood within the fort grounds, facing a sign instructing visitors not to proceed further. Behind it was a pile of garbage. Discarded bottles, wrappers, random debris. Beyond the trash, dense foliage covered one side. On the other was a short stone wall with an opening roughly the size of a doorway.

And there, partially obscured among the debris, was the carving.

Unmistakable.

The stonework around it resembled the fort walls, though it felt slightly different in texture and tone. Similar, but not identical.

I looked around to make sure no one was paying attention. No one was.

I stepped past the sign.

The opening led downward into darkness.

I had brought a torch with me, along with my phone as backup. Before proceeding further, I shared my live location with a friend. It seemed like a reasonable precaution at the time.

The first few steps inside were unsettling.

My footsteps echoed too clearly. The silence felt thick. I could hear my breathing as though it were amplified. There was a cold breeze moving through the tunnel. That detail unsettled me more than the darkness.

The entrance disappeared from view sooner than I expected. I did not remember turning or bending around a corner.

The walls were damp. Water trickled down in thin streams. The stone floor was slick. Occasionally, I heard what sounded like water striking stone somewhere beyond the walls.

That did not make sense.

The fort was built on elevated ground. I had not descended deeply enough to justify the sound of running water.

At some point, I noticed the path had begun to slope upward.

That detail stayed with me.

My watch vibrated.

Six in the evening.

I had set an alarm as a reminder to leave wherever I was by that time.

I decided to turn back.

As I did, I noticed something that halted me mid-step.

I was climbing upward.

That implied I must have walked downward earlier.

But I was certain I had not.

My watch vibrated again.

Six in the evening.

Again.

I looked at it carefully.

The slope shifted beneath me. The path now angled downward.

I took out my phone to call my friend.

No signal.

That part at least was logical. Underground structures rarely allow reception.

I began moving quickly in what I believed to be the downward direction. The tunnel had been linear. If I continued straight, I would eventually reach the entrance.

I stumbled and fell hard against the stone floor, luckily having braced most of the impact with my palms. I crawled on my four for a brief second before I lifted myself back up. When I lifted myself back up and shone the torch ahead, I felt something close to dread.

Five paths.

Arranged almost exactly as they had been near the lake.

I stood there, trying to steady my breathing.

My watch vibrated again.

Six in the evening.

The sound that followed was not subtle. It was a high, strained screech that did not resemble any animal I was familiar with. It did not sound entirely human either.

At that point, rational thought was no longer helpful.

I closed my eyes and ran.

I collided with the walls more than once. My palms scraped against stone. I lost my grip on the torch at some point but did not stop.

Eventually, the vibration on my wrist pulled me back.

I opened my eyes.

There was light ahead.

Natural light.

My watch read 5:47 PM.

The time I had first entered.

I did not pause to question it.

I ran toward the opening, exited the tunnel, crossed back past the sign, and descended the steps of the fort until I saw people again.

I sat down heavily on the stairs, trying to steady myself.

After a while, I walked to a small shop nearby and asked for a can of Red Bull and an energy bar. I reached for my phone to pay and found my right pocket empty.

A brief wave of panic.

Then I felt it in my left pocket.

I pulled it out.

That was when I noticed my wrist.

My watch was gone.

My left wrist was bare.

A moment later, I felt a vibration.

From my right arm.

I looked down.

The watch sat there.

Six in the evening.
