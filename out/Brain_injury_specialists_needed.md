I joined the hiking club when I was a freshman in college, 21, overwhelmed by mountains of assignments, my only skill being surviving on instant noodles and three cups of Lipton iced tea a day. I joined in October because England was muddy then, the air thick with the mixed smells of wet wool and burning wood. What a wonderful time of year.

That's where I met Luke. He was handsome, at least not the kind of guy you'd doubt where did the gene for properly grooming hair go? He wore North Face gear that looked like it had actually been used, not just bought for looks. Behind him followed a dog named Snow, loosely leashed on a red leash. Snow was a Goldendoodle with caramel-colored curly fur and eyes like two shiny buttons, seemingly filled with some kind of obsession. I'd never owned a dog before. My mother is allergic to dogs, and my father is allergic to me having living creatures to cling to; neither of these conditions is suitable for a pet.

"He likes you," Luke said.

I looked up. Luke was smiling. A bright, radiant smile.

"He's staring at my sandwich," I said. I was sitting on a damp log, holding a ham and cheese sandwich that was practically inedible.

"No," Luke crouched down and untied the leash. "Go say hello, buddy."

The dog didn't snatch the sandwich. He trotted over, sat down, and placed a thick, curled paw on my knee. He stared intently into my eyes.

"See?" Luke smiled. "He recognizes people. I'm Luke."

"Ross."

"Yeah, Ross. My dog approves of you. Frankly, he has higher standards than I do."

And that's how it all began. Everything was so easy. So easy, like falling asleep on a rainy day. We'd been dating for three months before the accident.

During those three months, I learned that Luke was an elementary school teacher, that he liked rare steak, and that he liked to turn the movie volume up really loud. He'd joke that Snow was the "third wheel" in our relationship, and I felt like Snow was half our child.

"He's too clingy," Luke would say, pushing the dog off the sofa. "Spoiled."

But for me, Snow was a surprise. When I was anxious about my thesis, he'd rub his wet nose against my hands. When I was washing dishes, he'd rest his chin on my feet.

"I think he likes me more than you," I joked one evening.

Luke was chopping vegetables. The knife tapped rhythmically on the cutting board, a smooth, rhythmic sound. "Don't flatter yourself, Ross. He's just a dog; he likes whoever has treats."

"You're so cynical."

"I'm a realist. Dogs are simple: in, out, food, and loyalty."

Then, the weekend arrived.

I couldn't go. I was mentally deranged from reading education teen literature. So Luke and his friend Sam went, Sam being a loud, boisterous man who thought buying drinks was a personality trait. They brought Snow.

Sunday night, I got a call.

"Ross?" It was Sam. He sounded lucid, the first sign of trouble. "Listen, something... something's happened, something's gone wrong."

My stomach churned. "What happened?"

"We were near the ridge, and the path was slippery. Luke... he fell. It was bad, he fell into the creek."

"Is he alright?"

"He broke his leg. Tibia, fibula, all broken. But Ross..." Sam's voice trailed off. "He was submerged."

"What do you mean, submerged?"

"Head down. Stuck between two rocks. I... it took me about five minutes to get down there and pull him out. He hadn't been breathing when I pulled him up. I did CPR. He's awake now, but they're keeping him at the hospital for observation. Maybe it's oxygen deprivation or something."

"Oh, my God," I whispered. "What about Snow?"

"The dog's fine. Just terrified. Barking incessantly. You see, I can't keep the dog, it's a student apartment. Could you look after him until Luke comes out?"

"Sure," I said. "Bring him over."

For two whole weeks, it was just Snow and me in my apartment. My apartment was a complete mess, books everywhere, half-used teacups, and clothes hanging out to dry. 

Snow, however, blended in perfectly. Those two weeks... were peaceful. Snow was a true gentleman. He slept at the foot of my bed, heavy but reassuring. He walked briskly, and when I studied, he would curl up under the table, his breathing rhythmic, like a metronome. He would beg for food, of course, but politely, gently placing his paws on my thigh and whimpering softly. A tacit understanding had formed between us.

I visited Luke in the hospital every other day. He was pale, his leg in a thick cast, slinged in a harness. He was quiet, unlike before.

"How are you feeling?" I asked, holding his hand. His skin was cold and dry, like paper.

"It feels like I fell down a mountain," he said hoarsely

.

"Sam said you were submerged for a long time."

Luke stared at the ceiling. "Black," he murmured. "So dark and so cold."

He gripped my hand tightly. So tightly that I was startled, but he didn't let go immediately.

"How's Sam?" he asked.

"He's fine, worried about you."

He didn't ask about Snow. Not even once. "Do you want to see Snow?" I finally pressed. "He misses you."

Luke slowly turned to look at me. His eyes were the same color as before, a cloudy, pale brown.

"Who?" he asked.

"Snow. Your dog."

"Yes," he said, the word like ashes in his mouth. "The dog."

"I take him back to my apartment. He's very good."

"Really?" Luke smiled. "Good."

Finally two weeks later Sam drove Luke home from the hospital. He moved stiffly, his cane tapping on the sidewalk. A few hours later, I was about to take the dog there, secured in the back seat with a seatbelt. When I got there, Snow jumped out, his tail wagging tentatively. He trotted over to Luke.

Then he stopped.

The fur on Snow's back stood on end. Like a dinosaur's spine,he let out a bark I'd never heard before. It wasn't a bark, but a primal warning.

"Snow?" I reached for his collar. "Hey, it's Luke, your dad."

Luke stood there, leaning on his cane. He didn't reach for the dog. He just looked down at it. But it lunged at Luke's good leg, barking incessantly. It was a frantic, terrified bark. Go away, go away, go away!

"Snow!" I grabbed the leash and pulled it back. The dog was trembling, his eyes wide.

"He doesn't recognize me," Luke said flatly. ". The smell of the hospital."

"Yeah," I said breathlessly, struggling to control the huge dog. "Yeah, that must be it. He's lost."

But as I dragged Snow into the apartment, he slid across the hardwood floors, turning back to look at Luke with terror, as if he were a stranger. Or a predator.

I stayed at Luke's house for the next few days, helping him settle in. The atmosphere in the apartment was tense. Snow refused to be in the same room as Luke. If Luke was in the living room, Snow would hide in the bedroom. If Luke went to the bedroom, Snow would rush to the kitchen and snuggle up next to me. But the nights were the hardest.

That night, we went to bed. Luke's cast was on the pillow. I turned off the light.

"Come here and let me hold you," Luke whispered in the dark.

I shifted closer to him, but suddenly, something warm and furry squeezed in between us. It was Snow.

"Snow, get down," I whispered.

The dog didn't move. He sat firmly in the middle of the bed, like a furry barrier. He was trembling. He rested his head on my chest and let out a long, trembling sigh.

"He won't move," I said.

"Kick him away," Luke said.

"He's scared, are you crazy?"

"He's not scared," Luke said from the darkness on the other side of the dog. "He's jealous."

We fell asleep like that, the dog like a wall. I woke up several times during the night. Once, I thought I saw Luke sitting in the dark, just staring at the dog. Just watching him.

Three days later, it finally came to the last straw,we wanted to be affectionate. Intimate, or rather, to try. I kissed Luke, trying to ignore the lingering chemical smell on him since the accident.

Snow started barking. He jumped onto the bed, biting Luke's shirt, whimpering, scratching at me with his paws, trying to pull me away.

"My God!" Luke yelled.

He grabbed the dog by the scruff of his neck. For a man with a broken leg, his movements were astonishingly fast. He dragged the barking dog off the bed and pulled him toward the hallway.

"Luke, don't hurt him!"

"I'm going to put him in the bathroom," Luke said. "I need some peace and quiet."

He tossed the dog into the ensuite bathroom and slammed the door shut. I heard the lock click shut. For the next hour, Snow kept scratching at the door. A soft scratching. Then a sharp whimper. Then silence.

When  I opened the bathroom door half an hour later, a stench hit me. Snow was huddled in the corner, shivering. He'd soaked the bathroom mat. His water and food bowls were in the bathroom.

I stared at them. 

I went back to the bedroom. "Luke?"

"Hmm?" He was looking down at his phone.

"Did you move the dog's bowl to the bathroom?"

"Yeah."

"Why?"

"So he'll have everything he needs in there," Luke said without looking up. "If he wants to act like an animal, then keep him in a cage."

"He peed on the floor. He never does that. He might be sick... I'm going to make an appointment with the vet now."

Luke finally looked up at me. "It's been two weeks, Ross. He's developed a deep bond with you. He sees you as his mother, and he's very possessive. He doesn't want me to take his mother away. It's classic separation anxiety plus resource possessiveness. You are his resource."

"That sounds... complicated."

"Dogs are like wolves, Ross. They want a leader. Right now, he sees you as the leader, and I as his rival."

"Dogs don't have that kind of awareness, and I don't think wolves do either. They only have parental awareness."

"So now I'm the bad dad? And you're a good mom? You're just spoiling him."

When I looked into the bathroom, Snow didn't see me as a resource. He stared at the door as if a monster might walk out at any moment.

And so we remained at an impasse until Valentine's Day.

Luke insisted on cooking. "Roast chicken," he said. "I can roast one leg."

The apartment smelled of rosemary and thyme, a homey scent, yet tinged with a hint of artificiality. I was in the kitchen, opening a bottle of Pinot Gris. Snow was hiding under the table. He no longer barked at Luke. Now, he just stared at him silently, unblinkingly.

Luke took the baking tray out of the oven. The chicken was golden brown and glistening with oil.

"Look," Luke said, "perfect."

He turned and placed the chicken on the counter.

I didn't know what had happened. I saw a golden shadow flash by. Snow darted out from under the table. He didn't bite Luke. He used his weight to press down on Luke's uninjured leg.

Luke stumbled. The baking tray tipped over. The chicken crashed to the floor with a thud. Hot oil splattered onto the tiled floor. The ceramic baking tray shattered.

"You damn dog!" Luke screamed.

The sound was deafening. It wasn't anger; it was rage. Cold, searing rage. Luke reached for his waist; a leather belt draped over the back of the kitchen chair, the one he'd taken off earlier. He grabbed it.

"Luke, stop! It was an accident!" I shouted.

He lashed it down. A sharp crack. Snow staggered backward, his claws darting wildly across the grease.

Luke swung again. He wasn't aiming. He just wanted to hit something.

I grabbed his arm. "Luke!"

The belt lashed out, striking my forearm. It stung like a wasp sting. I cried out, clutching my arm.

Silence fell over the room. Luke stood there, panting, the belt dangling from his hand. He looked at me, then at the raised red welt on my skin.

"Ross," he said, his anger vanishing like water draining down a drain. "Ross, baby, I'm sorry. I didn't mean to... I just wanted to hit the dog."

"You shouldn't have hit the dog!" I trembled.

He threw down the leash. "I know, I know. My leg hurts. I'm under a lot of stress. I'm sorry." He bent down to pick up a broken plate.

Snow lunged at him, biting Luke's hand as he reached for the plate. The bite was hard and loud.

Luke yelled and jerked his hand back. Blood welled up in his thumb. "He bit me!" Luke stared at the blood in surprise.

"He was protecting me," I said, my voice trembling. "You hit me."

Luke looked at the dog, then at me. He gave a dry laugh. "Yeah. Yeah, I think he was protecting his mother. A fucking good boy." He sucked on his bleeding thumb, his eyes fixed on the dog.

I didn't stay at his place that night. I went back to my apartment. I needed some space.

The next day I called. No one answered.

I called again at noon. Finally, at four in the afternoon, Luke answered.

"Where have you been?" I asked.

"Busy," he said, "cleaning."

"How's Snow?"

"It's about this," Luke said, "He ran away."

The world seemed to stand still. "What?"

"I opened the door to get the mail. He suddenly ran. Just ran out. I couldn't catch him, Ross, my leg is hurt."

"You... you just let him go?"

"I called him! He didn't come back."

I hung up. I got in my car and drove away. I didn't go to Luke's apartment. I went to the places I'd taken Snow for walks those two weeks. The park. The path along the canal. And the old cemetery behind the church on the hillside, where Snow loved the squirrels. I walked through rows of Victorian tombstones, calling his name.

"Snow! Snow!"

I heard a sob. The sound came from a patch of overgrown sage near the back wall.

I ran over. "Snow?"

My heart broke. He lay curled up in the dirt. He was shivering, his teeth chattering. But what truly disgusted me was his appearance; his beautiful caramel-colored curls were gone. Someone had shaved him, and terribly at that. His pink, reddish skin was exposed to the cold, damp air. He looked like a large, skinned rat, pitiful.

"Oh, baby," I cried, kneeling down. I took off my coat and wrapped it around him. He didn't lick me. He just buried his face in my shirt.

I had a huge argument with Luke in the car with my phone. "What did you do to him?" I screamed. I was holding the shivering, shaved dog in my arms.

Luke didn't say at first. "Ross, he pooped on me. I was checking his paws, and he had diarrhea everywhere, all over his fur, it was all matted."

"So you shaved him all off?"

"I had no choice! He was too dirty, and it had rained before. I was worried he'd get... a skin disease, you know Goldendoodles  are prone to skin diseases."

"He has tiny cuts all over his body!"

"He's been moving around a lot." Luke stood up, leaning on his cane. "You see, he's been behaving strangely these past few days, vomiting, bringing dead things. This morning he brought a dead rat to the bed. I think he's sick."

"He's afraid of you.”

"He's a dog, Ross! Don't anthropomorphize him. He's sick."

I turned off. "Then I need to take him to the vet."

The ER vet was a kind man, but his eyes looked tired. He placed Snow on a metal table for examination. Snow stood there with his head down, his tail tucked tightly between his legs, almost touching his belly.

"He's healthy," the vet said, stroking Snow's shaved skin. "A little dehydrated. But these wounds..." He looked at me. "Why did you shave him like that?"

I opened my mouth, wanting to tell the truth. But I looked at Snow. If I said that, they might call the police. 

"My boyfriend," I said calmly. "This is his first dog. He... he doesn't know anything. The dog had diarrhea, he panicked, and wanted to shave it. He's such an idiot."

The vet sighed. "Okay. Keep him warm; his protective layer has all come off. Also, keep the wounds clean."

I carried Snow to the car and bought him a whole set of toys. "I'm sorry," I whispered to him, "I'm so sorry."

At eight o'clock that evening, I drove to the only store still open. It was a large toy store with a pet section.

"Look, Snow," I said, carrying him down the aisle, "toys." I held up a squeaky duck toy.

He stared at it blankly, then turned his head away.

I went to the snack section. I bought some expensive liver treats. He would usually get excited and nuzzle me for them.

I gave him one in the car. He tossed it from his mouth onto the floor mat. He didn't eat it, didn't play with it, just stared at the dashboard.

We went back to my apartment. I put him on a blanket on my bed. He curled up, shivering. I lay down beside him, my arms wrapped around his naked, pink body; he seemed so vulnerable. I drifted off to sleep, dreaming of black water, streams, and things that plunged into them only to come back empty-handed.

I slept  until the phone on the bedside table buzzed. I reached for it, blinked, and shook off the sleepiness. It was Luke.

I can't keep a dog that bites. I have to get him. I have to take him to the shelter. It's the best option.

I read the message twice.

The shelter.

I looked at Snow. He was awake, looking at me. He knew. I swear he knew.

I'm coming to get him.

I was  scared, and when I was scared, my mind started racing. I was afraid the dog knew what was floating up in the water, maybe it wasn't Luke at all. Luke had drowned in that creek. What floated up was something else. It didn't understand love, only possession. It saw the dog as a biological machine to be controlled or discarded, not a companion.

Snow sobbed, pressing his head against my chest. I didn't reply to the text message because I didn't know what to do.
