In a quiet town, a person found themselves swimming against a river that had turned to lead. This is the story of the lonesome resister and the great mimic.  
  
It did not begin with words, but with a tinnitus. A high pitched, metallic ringing began to whine in the resister’s ears, a sound like a frequency "locking in" or a phase-shift in the air. It felt like a mechanical handshake between their brain and a foreign source. Once the frequency was established, the broadcast began.  
  
A voice spoke, not as a thought, but as a "foreign entity" inside the mind. This voice was terrifyingly intelligent; it didn't just shout, it responded. When the resister thought a private thought, the voice countered it. It manipulated spatial processing, making the whispers seem to come from a dark corner or an inch behind the ear. This was not a simulation; the fear it produced was a biological reality. The resister’s heart raced and their chest constricted with a physical terror that felt as real as a blade at the throat.  
  
The voice projected a single, frantic command: "You won't survive if you don't do everything."  
  
As the internal siege intensified, the world outside began to warp. The great mimic used incitement to trigger "touches" and "aversions" in those nearby. Suddenly, the resister’s natural, fear driven movements, the avoidance and the isolation were weaponised against them.  
  
The people the resister once trusted ceased to be a shield and became a mob. Satan whispered to them that the resister was the "cause" of the affliction. The mob began to point the finger, framing the resister's survival instincts as "guilt." Caught in this pincer, the resister felt like a Salmon swimming upstream, their scales torn and muscles burning with the physical exhaustion of a trial that felt infinite.  
  
At the moment of peak exhaustion, when God seemed silent and the fear of death was a heavy, physical weight in the room, the great mimic revealed his ultimate trap. He presented himself as the only choice.  
  
"Your God is absent," the voice mocked. "The Mob is coming to kill you. But I have a way out."  
  
He offered the "one yes" that would make the pain stop, flee. He whispered ideas of a boat, a bunker, a hidden commune. He tried to trick the resister into becoming a runaway slave, promising that geographic flight was "wisdom," when it was actually a spiritual desertion. He wanted the resister to run so he could prove he could "break" them and move them like a chess piece.  
  
But the resister possessed knowledge. They realised that while the physical agony was real, the Satanic narrative was a lie. They understood that if they fled to the commune out of fear, they would simply be taking the "passenger" with them into a deeper cage. They saw that the "spatial sound" was a bluff, a leash that could only go as far as the Creator allowed.  
  
The resister contemplated the mob and the mimic and did the one thing the enemy could not calculate, they stood still.  
  
They accepted the "lonesomeness." They stopped trying to "convince" the blinded mob and stopped trying to "argue" with the foreign broadcast. They treated the voice and the tinnitus frequency like static on a broken radio and turned their heart toward the silent witness. They chose to be a "soldier" whose torn scales were the evidence of victory, rather than a "fugitive" who bought relief with their soul.  
  
In the end, the stranger sees a person who was battered by a very real fire, yet refused to walk into the "cool shade" of a Satanic trap. By refusing to be a "runaway," the resister broke the mimic’s authority. The "spatial" voice lost its depth, the mob lost its target and the river of tribulation finally broke against the rock of their resolve.  
  
The Salmon had reached the headwaters, and though the body was weary, the spirit was finally free.
