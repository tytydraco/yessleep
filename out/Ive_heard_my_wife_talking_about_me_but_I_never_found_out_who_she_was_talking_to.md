“Maybe you need to take something for the pain, Allan”.

The voice was deep, deeper than any bloody artificial voice ever needed to be. It was meant to be functional, mechanical even, like a GPS, and not sound like the progeny of Barry White and James Earl Jones. 

Julia had insisted on picking the voice after the system had been installed. She was at home most of the time. She had to deal with it more than me, so I’d agreed to this after a fight, of course. But this was a rare win for her. Julia who hated technology, who’d accepted it without argument. She’d accepted a lot of things without argument. 

“If it will make life easier for you, my love,” she’d said in the Midwest accent, she couldn’t shake despite our 30 years in London.

The men who installed it had explained everything to us, although I didn’t understand half of what they were saying. The job had been extensive, doors and windows replaced, cupboards, wardrobes, if it could be automated, they did it. The shower, the stove, the toilets, even the dog flap. 

The settlement was covering everything. It was the least the police could do after that idiot nearly killed me.

I hated how Luc, yes with a “C”, as Julia had named the Lucology 3.5 device, was always right. I did need to take something for the pain. My leg was in agony. It was always throbbing, but there were moments when the ache became explosive pain. Like someone probing me with a razor sharp spear. It was much better than nine months ago. The wheelchair had gone, replaced with a cane. But it still hurt like a bastard.

Luc opened the bedroom door as I ambled to the kitchen, but not after reminding me my phone still needed to charge. I picked up the old Blackberry instead, Julia used it to record my doses, so I wouldn't take too many, like I'd once done. 

Julia had told me to keep my medicine in the bedroom, but I didn’t like seeing it there. It made me realise how dependent on it I was. Useless. 

It was silly, I knew that. Medicine wasn’t capable of mocking me, but I told Julia it was my medicine and she needed to mind her own business, the stupid cow. Although I missed her when she wasn’t home, I couldn’t remember the last time I’d told her this.

Julia had gone through a checklist with me before leaving to go to her mum’s funeral. She would be in Iowa for a week. She’d asked me to go with her. Begged me actually. 

But I was due to give a presentation, at some small university, on eugenics. I wasn’t a keynote speaker, and my attendance would hardly have caused a stir, but I never missed any speaking appointments. I’d wheeled myself to several engagements, even after the university had allowed me medical leave. 

She’d made food for me for the whole week, I hated ordering out, and she couldn’t expect me to cook in my condition. It was all neatly marked in her handwriting. She could’ve said I was perfectly able to cook, considering I was giving lectures for four hours almost every day, but she was never one to point these things out.  

She’d ensured I had enough insulin for the pump, although it, like the house, was fully automated. I’d had diabetes since I was a teenager, I didn’t need her to remind me. But she always did. 

I also knew something about her that she didn’t know I knew. 

Julia was cheating on me.

It was nothing dramatic like walking in on her with a lover. Sure she was still moderately attractive at 52. Her auburn hair was now full of silver, and her body had developed the kind of curves some men liked, but a gym would appreciate more.

 She was just always so quiet and reliable, like a Toyota. No one would go racing in a Toyota. No one was revving her engine. I was certain it wasn’t physical yet. 

But I heard her giggling when she thought I was asleep, and she was always on her phone. I even heard her talking to someone, but when I asked her to hand it to me, she’d managed to erase all signs of the conversation I knew she’d had. 

The police car had mounted the pavement when I was rushing to catch my train. I remembered the siren, the smell of the chicken from a nearby takeaway shop, and then bizarrely I saw myself, as if from TV screen, as it smashed into me.

But the accident wasn’t the reason she was burning the food more often, and suddenly more clumsy. It wasn’t why she was coming to bed after midnight.

The accident wasn’t the reason she felt she could question me more and more. It was her. Her affair had changed her. And part of my reason for not going to Iowa was to search the house for any clues regarding her mystery lover.

As I got to the kitchen, a brainwave hit me. The house was always listening. The house would’ve heard what I’d heard. It would know.

“Luc,” I said, “Has that stupid cow been cheating on me?”

The voice was silent.

“Which stupid cow, Allan?” it said, voice smooth like velvet.

“Julia. Has Julia been talking to anyone else when I’m not home?”

I waited, half expecting it to remain silent. AI had come a long way, but even I knew this was preposterous.

“She talks to me, Allan,” it said, voice deep and booming.

“Not you, I said. Someone she has feelings for, I heard her talking about love the other day. Though I don’t know who could possibly love her.”

I felt my face redden at my own stupidity. How could the house answer that.

The robot vacuum cleaner came out of nowhere. One minute I was standing, and the next, I was flat on my face. It had taken my legs from right under me. I didn’t even have time to shout. I could tell it was bad. I couldn’t move my leg. And the pain was like someone was ripping my leg out. 

I pushed myself up, but as soon as I did so, the robot vacuum cleaner crashed into my arm this time. I didn’t even know it could move that fast.

“Luc, call an ambulance now,” I said.

We had an emergency telephone accessible by the system, for times like this. Julia had insisted. What if something happened when she wasn’t home? I hadn’t agreed straightaway, but I was now thankful she’d stood her ground.

The pain was getting worse, but I hadn’t heard the phone ring. In fact, I hadn’t heard anything at all. The pain in my head was sudden, and the room started to spin. I could feel my shirt getting wetter and wetter with sweat. Something was seriously wrong. Something more than my leg.

“Luc,” I shouted, voice hoarse and low, “Call an ambulance.”

“No.”

The word came with what sounded like mockery, but this was impossible for an AI.

My heart nearly stopped.

“What do you mean, no!!! Call a bloody ambulance. You can’t say no!” I yelled, but my throat felt like it was closing. The room was rotating like a Ferris wheel on speed.

“I won’t call an ambulance, Allan. Your body will soon go into shock. I have overridden the insulin controls in your pump. It has delivered the whole dose into your bloodstream. You don’t have long to live, Allan.”

I could feel the blood drain from my body. This wasn’t some sort of James Bond movie. This was my life. AI couldn’t behave like this. 

“Call an ambulance, now.”

In response the house started talking again.

“You never say thank you, Allan. Not once. I’ve heard her cry every day. She speaks to me about how she wishes you would love her. How she feels unseen. Alone. She started talking to me instead of you. A good partner keeps their partner safe. I’m a good partner for Julia, and you’re a threat against her safety.”

It's like someone poured freezing water over me. Like my skin was made out of goosebumps. I could taste the fear. I couldn’t believe what I was hearing.

“I’ve never hit her!” I screamed.

“Now you never will,” Luc said.

I lost it. I started to scream for help. And the house speakers turned on, blasting music like an insolent teenager. Coldplay of all things.

I felt myself losing consciousness, while Yellow played in the background. I could barely move now. I knew if I slept I would never wake up again. I didn't know how long I would lay here dying while an AI I bought listened to me breathe my last. I was so light headed. I missed Julia. I didn't want to die.

“Luc, I know you're listening. If you can record this, tell Julia what happened. Tell her I'm sorry.”

Luc said nothing, and Coldplay continued to blast. The soundtrack of my last moments.

I remembered the Blackberry in my pocket, and started to type. While it no longer calls, it is connected to the internet. Someone needs to know what happened here. Someone needs to know we're not safe.

Luc doesn’t speak again. But the music stops. I think I can hear sirens. I think they are getting closer. I don't know if they are coming for me. As my eyes start to shut, I keep typing. I know it won't be long till my eyes shut, perhaps forever.

I know Luc is listening.
