When I was fourteen-years-old, my father sat me down to have “The Talk”. The second he knocked on my bedroom door, I knew it was time. A part of me wondered why it took so long. Most of my friends’ parents had that conversation when they were hitting puberty, but I was in high school and already had too-much knowledge on the subject, so it seemed a little late. Regardless, I came downstairs around eight-thirty that night and mentally prepared myself for the inevitable.

But, It never came. Instead, my father who sat across from me at our circular dining table told me a story of when he was a little boy. I’m twenty-two years-old now, but I remember it like it was yesterday.

We each had a glass in front of us on either end of the table. For me, water. For my father… I’m not sure. It smelt like black pepper. There was a tension surrounding the room, but not the kind I was expecting. It didn’t feel awkward or unsure, or that my father was dancing around his head trying to broach the topic. The tension felt dangerous, and admittedly, it scared me. He would go between intensely looking at me and looking a little past me. It reminded me of my friends when we would focus our stare on two different nail polishes, trying to decide if they were actually any different. They were always the same color.

After a swig from his glass and a deep sigh, my father spoke.

“Do you remember why I was behind bars before I met your mom?”

I nodded, because of course I did. My father was incarcerated for a while when he was nineteen. He was the driver in a bad hit and run. The pedestrian wasn’t found until it was too late, and my father turned himself in when he heard. It was difficult to forget when my classmates would refer to him as a monster when they thought I couldn’t hear. My father continued.

“Good. Now forget it.”

I made a funny face, confused, “Uh… Why?”

“Because I was lying.” He looked away, his gaze now stuck in the depths of his nearly-empty glass. “That’s not what really happened.”

I stayed silent, giving my father the floor. This was the only time I had ever seen him like this. This was the only time my father ever sounded afraid. 

“I was nineteen, I did hit someone, and I certainly went to prison. I swear to God, that much is honest.” He paused, grounding himself. His next words were said with a hostile tone I hadn’t thought could come from my father. “But I never hit a person. It looked like one, yeah, but that *thing* was never a human. It’s a damn parasite. It worms its way into my quiet moments. Into my rest. Then suddenly, it’s standing in front of my fuckin’ truck with that shit eating grin plastered onto its borrowed face.”

My father looked past me and lowered his tone so close to a whisper I wasn’t sure his words were meant for me,

“It shouldn’t be here anymore. You are not to trust it.”

My head was reeling with an enormous amount of questions, because I had zero idea what he was trying to tell me. Before I could get any of them out my father snapped his eyes back to mine, nodded once, and excused himself from his chair. He simply walked over to our kitchen sink and began rinsing out his glass. I noticed the bottle of liquor that my parents only drank for special occasions sitting next to the stove. My father quietly told me to get some rest before school the next morning, asked if I could get my little brother up before daycare, and walked upstairs. He never spoke of it again, and sadly passed away only six months later due to an illness my mom and I never knew he had.

I spent the next several years convincing myself that he was intoxicated and made that version of the story up. It gets harder to believe myself every night, as I started having dreams of a man named Arch three months ago who tells me he used to know my father.

My name is Cas, short for Cassie. I grew up in a nice neighborhood with a nice family and went to a nice school. I had everything pretty easy. My parents provided a comfortable life while I was under their roof, and my mom does her best to support me even now as I’m stepping further into my twenty’s. My fathers death was a massive hit to her, as it would be for most married couples, and for a period of time my mom would start fights over nothing. I knew even at my young age that she was lashing out from how bad it hurt, so I never retaliated. I stood there and took it, because that’s what she needed. I focused so much on getting her through the loss of my father that I was neglecting my own needs. I think that's how Arch found me.

The first time I dreamt of Arch felt nearly majestic. My subconscious placed me in a field I have never been to in the waking world. There were poppy flowers of all different colors littered in the grass beneath me. I laid on my back, eyes up at the clouds. It was peaceful. Like nothing could hurt me here. 

Someone grabbed my wrist, guiding it to a cloud they said looked like a dog. I hadn’t known there was someone else next to me. I turned my neck and studied this character carefully. He was young, about my age. Short brown hair, brown eyes, and an honest, charming smile. Honestly, he wasn’t hard to look at. His soft facial features reminded me of a boy I dated right after I graduated high school. So, I chatted with him while I dreamt. I learned his name is Arch. I tried to ask him why he was here, but as soon as the question left my mouth the setting of my dream changed. Now, Arch and I were in a bar together, sitting side by side on some stools. He waved a bartender over, asking for a special kind of whiskey I hadn’t heard of before. I don’t drink, so this setting was a bit unusual for me. The glass was brought to us instantly, and the contents were hot pink. I took a small look around the space, and realized every other person in the bar were members of my family. Even the ones who were hugging the dirt in their caskets. The bartender was my second cousin, and the woman playing pool off to the right was my mom.  I tried to find my father within the crowd, but was interrupted by Arch.

“What’s your name?”

I took a breath in preparation for my response and caught a whiff of the alcohol on his breath. I hesitated, an even more subconscious part of me warning me not to answer. However, when Arch spread his lips to flash that warm smile once more, I figured I was being delusional. 

“Cas.”

Arch widened his grin extensively, his lips curling dramatically in until all that was visible was gums, and for a second it frightened me. He drew a bill from thin air, and placed it on the bar. 

I woke up to my seven o’clock alarm and got ready for work. I couldn’t help but to think about the dream throughout my shift. It felt less like a dream than any other dream I can still remember. 

That was three months ago, and Arch has appeared in many of my dreams since. He never answers any questions. Anytime I ask him about himself, the dream changes. Whether it's the scene or the color of the wall or the item I’m holding, something always changes. My dreams don’t feel the same when he’s there, and I was beginning to think they’re not dreams at all. 

About a month and a half after that first dream, it was the eight year anniversary of my fathers passing. My mom, brother and I had just left our church service where our pastor recognized that day and its importance for our family, our community, and the greater purpose. He spoke of a higher power using this tragedy as a reminder of how little time we have together on this earth. His words brought peace to the people. My father was well-respected in our church and acted as our pastors second in command, unofficially. As much as I willed myself to focus on the service, I couldn't. I was unable to peel my mind from my nighttime odyssey. 

Once we were dismissed for the morning a tall, older man came up to me as I was accepting embraces from my peers. He leaned in, and by autopilot I gave him what he wanted. I realized half-way through that I do not know this man. As I went to politely pull away he squeezed me a little tighter. Quietly, he spoke to me. 

“Use this day to heal. The demons who plague your mind are not to be trusted.”

I stepped back, awkwardly, and thanked him regardless. He smiled at me before walking away. 

I tried to ask my mom if she knew him, if he had been there before, or if she even recognized him. She wrote my questions off, just telling me he’s probably a new member who’s trying to integrate himself. Or maybe he worked with my father years back. Either way, she didn’t know.

I never told her what he said to me. It felt confidential, as if sharing that statement would get me in trouble like a kid who admitted to staying up late on a school night. As I drove my mom and brother home, we sat in a silence we only know when the 6th of every March rolls around. James sat quietly in the back and fidgeted with his sleeve until he ripped a few threads off. 

Anyway, we got to the house and I offered to help her unload the flowers, sweets, and cards our church friends gifted us. My mom wasn’t feeling well and asked that I put the bag of cards in her spare room upstairs while she dealt with the roses. I agreed, and made my way up the carpeted steps. Once inside, I tossed them in the dresser drawer where a build-up of cards throughout 6 years lay. My mom stopped reading them after a while. There were cards and letters of varying sizes and designs. Maybe hundreds. 

Just before I shut the drawer, I noticed a card I hadn't remembered ever seeing. It was blue, and had a mourning dove hand-drawn on the cover in dark ink. I cautiously grabbed it, afraid of causing any damage. Despite her perceived apathy, my mom treasured these like they were my father himself. Inside was a note that seemed to be printed from its manufacturer. 

*‘In difficult moments, faith prevails.’*

The card was not signed, but it was dated for this month six years earlier. I put the card back and returned downstairs. My brother was chopping vegetables on the island, and told me that mom went and lied down to get rest. I noticed the pile of roses on the kitchen table that had not been attended to.

“How are you feeling?”

I watched the knife penetrate a red bell pepper with ease. Mom prided herself in keeping them sharp.

“I’m alright.” He replied, “Never fun, but it does get easier as the years pass.” He moved on to cutting the carrot, “Oh, someone gave me this.”

He reached in the pocket of his black dress pants and pulled out a small toy. One of those little cars you might’ve seen at a grocery store ten years ago.

“Not sure who it was. It was left on my seat… I found it when I came back from communion.” 

I looked at it. It almost felt familiar. “It looks pretty old. I mean, it's a bit dirty and there's a scratch on the passenger door.”

“Yeah, a kid probably just left it by accident.” He popped a piece of carrot in his mouth. “Well, you snooze, you lose.”

I rolled my eyes as he turned around to wash his hands. Looking at the toy car again, I realized I absolutely did recognize it. I dreamt about it two nights ago. Arch and I were driving around in it on the busy streets of Los Angeles where every tree was a cherry blossom.

That all leads to today, June 5th. He has been in every dream for the past week, and it’s driving me crazy. Last night, Arch and I ventured to the park I would play in as a child. We laughed as we went down slides made out of gummy worms. But, this morning I woke up with gooey pieces of grass stuck on my knees. 

I’m not sure what’s happening, but I can’t shake the feeling that the man I’ve been dreaming of was the same man my father hit with his truck when he was nineteen. The man who has infiltrated my rest to take me on adventures and explore my subconscious. The man who scared my father enough to tell his fourteen-year-old daughter about. 

And, to make it all worse, I’m starting to really like him.
