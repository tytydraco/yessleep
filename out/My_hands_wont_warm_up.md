It started today after class.

The walk back to my dorm only takes five minutes, but it was brutally cold out. The kind of cold that doesn’t just sit on your skin; it sinks into you. I hadn’t brought gloves. Halfway across the courtyard, my fingers were already burning, that sharp, electric sting like they might snap off if I bent them wrong.

By the time I reached my building, I could barely feel my keys between my fingers.

I remember fumbling them.

I remember the wind.

And I remember thinking I should’ve checked the weather before leaving that morning.

When I finally made it inside, I rushed to my room and cranked up the heat. The radiator groaned to life. I flexed my fingers in front of it, waiting for that painful thaw to begin.

It never did.

I sat at my desk and opened my laptop to start homework, but typing was difficult. My fingers felt stiff, disconnected. Like they didn’t quite belong to me. When I looked down at them, they were pale, not just cold pale, but flat. Drained.

I told myself it was normal. Circulation takes time.

Fifteen minutes passed.

Then thirty.

The heat was on. My room was warm. I could feel it on my face, on my neck.

But my hands were getting worse.

The color shifted slowly from pale to a faint purple, creeping from my fingertips toward my knuckles.

That’s when I started to get nervous.

I pulled on a sweater. Then another. I turned on my space heater and aimed it directly at my hands. I wrapped myself in every blanket I owned until I looked ridiculous, like some kind of human burrito hunched over a glowing plastic heater.

Nothing changed.

If anything, the cold was spreading.

My forearms began to pale next. The skin looked thinner somehow. Tight.

I checked the thermostat on the wall.

65°F.

That didn’t make sense. It felt warmer than that. I turned the dial higher anyway and went to the sink, running my hands under hot water.

I could feel the heat.

But underneath it, inside my hands, it felt like there was a snowstorm raging beneath my skin. Like something deeper was freezing faster than the surface could warm.

I stared at my reflection in the dark kitchen window.

My face looked flushed.

Normal.

My hands did not.

By then, it had been nearly an hour since I’d gotten home.

My roommate should’ve been back already. I grabbed my phone and sent them a text:

“Hey man, the heat isn't working, I'm freezing over here!”

The message showed one checkmark.

Then nothing.

I tried again.

Still nothing.

That’s when I noticed how quiet everything was.

Dorms are never completely silent. There’s always a door slamming, music playing faintly through walls, someone laughing down the hall.

There was nothing.

I tried calling my doctor, just to ask if extreme cold sensitivity was a thing. The phone rang.

And rang.

And rang.

It never went to voicemail.

It just kept ringing until, suddenly, it stopped.

Not a click. Not a disconnect tone.

Just silence.

When I exhaled, I saw my breath.

At first I thought it was stress.

But no.

There it was again, a thin plume of fog curling in front of my face.

Inside my own room.

My skin had started to change.

Fine cracks formed along my knuckles, like old paint drying and splitting. My fingers didn’t hurt anymore. I couldn’t feel them at all. The cold had reached my shoulders now, seeping downward into my chest.

My legs were the color of old porcelain.

It was 5 p.m.

My roommate should have been home an hour ago.

Panic finally broke through whatever shock I was in. I ran into the hallway.

Empty.

Every door closed. Every light on. No sound.

I took the stairs down to the lobby.

Empty.

I stepped outside.

The campus courtyard stretched in front of me, silent and still. No students. No cars. No movement.

Snow edged the walkways.

The wind didn’t move.

That’s when I noticed something strange in the glass of the main entrance.

My reflection.

It didn’t look purple.

It didn’t look cracked.

For one fragile second, I thought maybe this had all been in my head.

I rushed back upstairs to my room and went straight to the bathroom mirror.

I don’t know what I expected.

But it wasn’t that.

My eyes were sunken deep into my skull. My skin wasn’t just pale, it was grey, waxy, stretched too tightly over bone. My lips had a faint blue tint.

I looked…

Wrong.

Not sick.

Not freezing.

Dead.

I stood there, staring. Waiting for my reflection to lag behind. To twitch. To prove this wasn’t real.

But it moved exactly as I did.

And then something shifted in my memory.

Not an image.

A feeling.

The wind.

I remembered how sharp it felt in my lungs when I left class. How my fingers had already started to sting halfway across the courtyard.

I remembered fumbling my keys.

But not at my door.

In the snow.

I never reached the building.

I remembered dropping my phone.

I remembered my knees hitting the ground.

And I remembered how quiet everything became.

Not like an empty hallway.

Like snow.

Like the kind of silence that swallows sound whole.

I never opened my dorm room.

I never turned on the heat.

I never sent that text.

That’s why it never delivered.

That’s why no one answered.

That’s why the campus was empty.

I pressed my hand against the bathroom mirror.

It didn’t feel cold anymore.

It didn’t feel like anything.

Slowly, I walked back to my desk. My computer screen was on. The cursor blinked patiently on a blank document.

Waiting.

I don’t know how long I have.

My calls didn’t go through.

My texts didn’t go through.

I don’t know if this will either.

But maybe this is different.

Maybe you can see this.

If you’re reading this right now…

Are your hands cold?

And do you remember how you got home today?
