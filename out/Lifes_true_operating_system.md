

# TITLE OF WORK: Life’s true operating system

—---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

On April 1st, I was arrested and brought to confinement in a tiny jail cell in Los Angeles. From there, I was being transported to the hospital.

Very quickly, I realized something was wrong. What's worse, nobody on the outside knows or would believe it is happening.

I was told by one of the police officers, as he was placing me into the back of the ambulance, that "I was going to the hospital, to get the needle," and through the phrasing and horror in his face, I understood something was off.

There were rumors that something was happening and that the hospital was where they were starting their program.

He then asked me "how I felt," and I replied, "How do you think I feel?". I was nauseous. I had just spent hours in a cell. Nothing about this felt normal. 

When I arrived at the hospital, they immediately placed me into a bed in a hall of patients and inserted a needle into my arm. That's when I noticed things that I didn't register earlier. There were very still bodies being placed in stretchers, covered with parchment. There were people, slowly breathing, unresponsive in their beds. 

I could not believe it, but I still doubted myself.

I could not speak because of fear.

Almost nobody else seemed to recognize any danger.

There was one man who protested loudly, but was ignored. He then yelled something like "They're taking us up!!" and pointed to the ceiling.

At which point, two women in hospital attire contained him and removed him from the floor.

Another woman, who I recognized as being my classmate in elementary school, flagged down hospital staff from her bed. I overheard some of their conversation:

"Why can't I leave?"

to which the worker replied

"Do you really want to leave now? The chemical is already inside your body"

At this point, while I was gaze locked out of sheer terror, I peered over to her, and her eyes were full of tears.

"I don't want to die," she said.

To which the staff replied,

"There are no other options. Within an hour, your transition will be complete.”

She mumbled something back with great pain which I could not make out.

There was another man that said, “The Russians and Chinese are doing this shit to theirs too.”

Staff were making their way around the floor, constantly moving beds bound with still bodies, and disinfecting new beds for the floor. They were avoiding eye contact with us.

At one point, two workers were conducting what looked like a routine check. One looked at my stretcher then down at her clipboard: “Why is he here? He shouldn’t be here.”

The other said, “Third time drunk driving.”

I continued to look straight ahead, where I saw a man sapped of life. He was gasping for air while confined to his bed. The needle that was in his arm was oozing blood. Our eyes met.

I looked away and began to cry. I heard the muffled cries of others.

Bodies were being removed with new ones placed in their stead. I saw one of the bodies, completely still, in the stretcher as it passed me by. The staff laughed: “He saw it!”

The desire to escape was quashed by the certainty of my death regardless. The staff seemed to be unarmed, but I was strapped tight to my seat. We were trapped. They had, in one swoop, removed all resistance.

I began to feel a heavy drifting in and out.

—---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Society is a machine that operates to survive. In its design lies the feature of mass murder. This terrible node functions as a force for when society experiences instability, with the goal of counter self-regulation. It occurs when societies experience revolutions, collapse, civil war, famine, etc. It is a force of function, need, and undeniable inevitability.

At its center are leaders. Command nodes for executing the logistics, policing, and supplies. They remove the burden on the individual. In the turbulent times of today, let us not forget this reality.
