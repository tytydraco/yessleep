It was in 1942, in the midst of the Great Patriotic War. I was with my battalion in a small village whose name I no longer remember. We were supposed to meet a German battalion stationed in a neighboring village. These villages were separated by a dense forest that would have been pointless to go around, as it stretched for several kilometers.

We needed to know what the Germans were doing and what they were preparing for. We sent two scouts into that forest. We waited for them for a day, then two. They didn’t return. Strangely, the Germans were also nowhere to be seen. We sent two more scouts — the same situation. Then the battalion commander gathered the soldiers and their commanding officers.

“Here’s the situation,” the commander said, bringing a candle to the map of the area lying on the table. We were in the local school (the village was abandoned); the room still contained textbooks from former students, reminding us of peaceful days gone by. “We’ve lost four men in five days. No Germans, no sound of them, no sign of them. Our men haven’t returned either, which is very strange. We need to take up positions in that village in two weeks! So, Lorov, come here!”

A man stepped out from the crowd of soldiers. He was the captain of the platoon I was in. None of the soldiers knew how old he was, but he looked about forty. He had a gaunt face, but what I remembered most was the scar on his left cheek. Rumors said that when the captain was captured by the Germans, they tortured him severely, but he only cursed them out, so they slashed his mouth open, leaving a scar on his cheek. The captain silently approached the commander.

“Lorov, you and your platoon will go into that forest. Find our soldiers, or at least what’s left of them,” the commander ordered. The soldiers in my platoon exchanged glances. I understood that some kind of fear had gripped us. We were afraid of a forest.

“Ah, men, what’s there to be afraid of? There are twelve of us!” one of our soldiers tried to ease the tension.

An hour later, we were heading toward the forest, carrying the necessary equipment, including PPSh-41 submachine guns. Evening was approaching, and it began to get dark around eight o’clock since it was August. A few minutes later, we reached the edge of the forest but stopped. No one dared to enter; we all peered into the thicket, hoping the scouts would return so we wouldn’t have to go in. Lorov was the first to step into the forest, stepping forward and saying:

“Well, comrades, we must move forward!”

We moved deeper into the forest. The farther we went, the quieter and more cautiously we tried to move. After about an hour of such slow movement, our platoon decided to take a five-minute break — some drank water, others attended to personal needs, all while carefully looking around, since the Germans were no fools either.

I stood drinking water from a stream when suddenly a soldier named Avdiy ran past me. We were good acquaintances but not friends. Avdiy ran up to Lorov.

“Comrade Captain, permission to report!” he said quickly.

“Permission granted,” Lorov replied calmly.

“Not far from here — German uniforms with human bones!”

I couldn’t believe my ears. Avdiy seemed to be raving. I’d never seen him like this.

“What nonsense are you talking?” another soldier interrupted.

“It’s true, I swear on my life! Come with me!”

Avdiy ran back the way he came. I and the other soldiers, along with Lorov, followed him to see what he had imagined. We probably thought we were wasting time — until we reached the place. It was a small clearing. Just a clearing, if not for the scattered German weapons, uniforms, and bones... Human bones, gnawed, lying all over the clearing. From the German uniforms, we realized these were the bones of five Germans. I felt nauseous and stepped aside, along with several other soldiers. Lorov stood in shock, while the other soldiers, pale-faced, tried to process what they had just seen.

“Alright, comrades, let’s continue the search!” Lorov said, quickly leaving the clearing and leading the soldiers away before anyone passed out.

We continued our slow march. It was beginning to get dark. The forest grew quiet, only the rustling of small animals in the bushes. We probably covered about a kilometer like that. We were about to take another break when suddenly, from our side, machine-gun fire rang out. Bullets hit one of our soldiers; he fell dead. It was the Germans. We immediately took positions: some hid behind trees, some in bushes, some in a ravine. I, Avdiy, Lorov, and another soldier jumped into a ravine, from where we continued the firefight that had already begun while we were taking cover.

The Germans didn’t spare ammunition, and neither did we. There were about thirteen Germans. But in a moment, there were ten. We also took losses. Suddenly, a grenade flew into our ravine. We jumped out immediately. The grenade exploded, showering me with dirt and slightly deafening me.

Then my gaze was drawn to a wolf about a hundred meters away, deep in the forest. It stood strangely, its head twitching — unnaturally. Suddenly, the wolf’s snout began to stretch, and foam came from its mouth. At the same time, its back began to grow — no, its entire body: torso, legs, neck! The wolf turned into a huge monster, probably three meters tall! The most terrifying thing was that this creature stood up on two legs or paws, and I saw its long claws! I was seized by a fear I had never experienced in my life. Suddenly, someone touched my hand. I flinched. It was Lorov.

“Get up!” the captain shouted. My hearing returned, and I again heard all the explosions, gunfire, and bullets flying past me. I turned my head to where the wolf had been, but there was nothing there. “Imagined it?” crossed my mind. “Get up, or you’ll die!”

Lorov pulled me to my feet, and we took new positions. There were seven of us left, and seven Germans. I fired my submachine gun and managed to wound one German. The wounded German crawled behind a half-rotted tree trunk. Suddenly, from the bushes, the monster I had seen in the forest leaped onto that German! I’ll call it a werewolf. The werewolf grabbed the German with its teeth and claws. Amid the gunfire, I heard the crunch of the poor man’s bones... The nearby Germans fell back in fear; those who stayed on their feet opened fire. Our soldiers, seeing the creature, stood frozen at first, and only then did a few open fire — I was not among them. Bullets hit the gray fur and skin of the werewolf. In pain, the monster roared — if that roar could be called anything like any animal sound I knew.

It was dark. Suddenly, from the bushes beside me, another werewolf ran out and lunged at the soldier who had tried to encourage us at the beginning. I decided to pull myself together and opened fire on the monster. Meanwhile, Lorov was trying to fend off a third werewolf from another soldier. The werewolf dragged the poor man into the forest; I didn’t dare follow.

I looked around and realized there were already five werewolves. Suddenly, a monster lunged at me. It grabbed my back with its claws, scratching my skin. Then the werewolf howled. I saw a German captain drive a large knife into its head. The monster fell dead. The German helped me up. I was in shock.

“We must fight together to defeat these beasts!” the German captain said to Lorov with an accent.

“You’re right!” Lorov said.

The German and our captain shook hands. I was in shock — even though we were enemies, the German was right.

“Everyone in a circle!” Lorov ordered.

“Im Kreis!” the German commanded.

The Germans and our men — those still alive — formed a circle, which made it easier to fend off the monsters. It felt strange and uncomfortable to stand shoulder to shoulder with an enemy who had now become an ally. We fired at the werewolves. More and more kept coming! It was night; we didn’t even know what time it was.

Our circle didn’t hold for long. One of the creatures leaped at us, and the circle broke apart. In the panic, two men fell into the monsters’ jaws. We realized we couldn’t win. Even Lorov, a brave fighter, was shooting with his last strength — monsters had already attacked him, but he managed to escape. I had very few bullets left.

Suddenly, a huge paw struck my back. The weapon flew out of my hands, and I was thrown against a tree. Turning onto my back, I saw a werewolf running at me, but it didn’t reach me because Avdiy started attacking it. The monster switched its attention to Avdiy. Half-conscious, I decided to count how many of us were left. Four... Me, Avdiy (who was running from a werewolf), Lorov, and the German.

I stood up, grabbed the nearest submachine gun lying on the ground, and rushed back into the fight. Suddenly, I heard a scream. It was Avdiy. A monster had bitten his leg. I rushed toward him, but another werewolf blocked my path. I opened fire into its face. Many bullets flew into its fierce muzzle. The monster charged at me.

Suddenly, Lorov grabbed me and dragged me into the forest, away from the clearing. The German ran beside us.

“Run, or we’ll die!” he shouted.

I saw a pack of werewolves attack Avdiy, and I saw them chase after us. We ran with all our might, though we had little strength left. Suddenly, the German fell. We tried to pull him up as we ran, but the werewolves yanked him from our hands.

We kept running. Adrenaline was high. We dropped all our gear — it was extra weight. Suddenly, we saw a gap in the trees. I was overjoyed — it was our village! Exactly! Strength returned! We ran toward our people, bursting out of the forest. But in the darkness, we saw not our village, but another! And near the houses, with torches and weapons in hand, stood a German battalion...

The Germans shouted something at us, but we couldn’t hear them over the furious roar of hundreds of werewolves pouring out of the forest. Lorov pushed me down into the grass. We hit the ground. The werewolves, forgetting about us, attacked the German battalion. I heard countless gunshots.

“Run back into the forest!” Lorov said.

We stood up... We wanted to run, but that decision was a mistake. Several bullets hit me in the back. I fell. Out of the corner of my eye, I saw Lorov fall beside me. “Is this the end?” — my last thought that night. I lost consciousness...

I opened my eyes. The sun brightly lit a large room with white walls. The room was filled with beds holding wounded soldiers. Nurses were running around. I was in a hospital. My whole body ached. I was covered in bandages. I looked for Lorov with my eyes, but he was nowhere. Suddenly, two men in official suits entered the room. They asked a nurse something; she pointed at me, and the men walked over.

“Now, listen to me carefully,” one of the men said as they approached. “If you want to live, you were attacked by Germans — more of them than you in that forest. And there are no wolves in that forest at all. If you start telling everyone nonsense about werewolves, we will come for you. You’ll have two options: say goodbye to your life, or go to a mental hospital under the pretext that the Germans used poison gas and you imagined everything. Do you understand me?”

“Yes...” I barely managed to say.

“Good. We’ll see you again.”

The men left. I stayed in the hospital for almost a month, and then I was sent home. I never found out what happened to Lorov. Now it’s already the 21st century, and I think those people have long forgotten about me.
