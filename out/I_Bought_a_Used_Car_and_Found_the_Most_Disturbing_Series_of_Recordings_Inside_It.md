*“You are of your father the devil, and your will is to do your father’s desires. He was a murderer from the beginning, and does not stand in the truth, because there is no truth in him. When he lies, he speaks out of his own character, for he is a liar and the father of lies.”*

*— From the Gospel of John, Chapter 8, Verse 44*

It all started when my previous car broke down on the side of the road. It had been making strange noises for a while and it was clear it didn’t have many years left in it — after all, it was one of the few cars still on the road from the nineties. But the moment I got stranded with it on the shoulder, I understood there was no point in taking it to a mechanic, and maybe it was time to start looking for a different car — at a price I could actually afford, of course.

My name is Dan, and at that time — 2015 — I was 26 years old. I live in a remote settlement in the north of the country that you’ve probably only heard of when you made a wrong turn on your way to a cabin in the Golan Heights. In any case, I was single, and one of my dreams was to become a father. I didn’t grow up in a supportive environment and I never really had a normal father figure — he wasn’t really in the picture, and even when he was, I preferred not to get close to him. I work at an accounting firm that a friend got me a job at before I moved to that settlement. The offices are located near the town of Ariel in Samaria, so the commute is especially long, and as you probably know — public transportation is not the ideal solution, especially on roads like those. So that’s me: a young salaried worker living paycheck to paycheck in the reality of a brutal cost of living, dreaming of having a good wife and, more importantly, of being a father. I’m sure I’d be good at it — just being there for my son or daughter. But at that time, my dreams had to wait, and I had to focus on finding a car I could afford with the few meager thousands sitting in my bank account.

The day before, on the one and only time I took a bus to work — which took over five hours — I found a business card for a car dealership located in my settlement. There was no mention of it online when I searched, but when I arrived at the dealership, I understood why. It wasn’t exactly the most legitimate operation you’d ever see. They sold cars and parts there that were off the books, and I assumed the shady owner wanted to keep the business under the radar. That said, the prices were relatively decent. I tried not to fall for the junk heaps that would die on me after two days — and believe me, there were plenty of those — until I found it.

Among the old cars in the dusty lot, sat a black Toyota Auris, model year 2010. To be honest, it wasn’t in great shape at all — it had a few dents and scratches — and the driver’s door had been replaced with one that wasn’t original, a color that was trying to match the car’s black but clearly wasn’t quite right. It looked like it had been in an accident. But when I took it out for a test drive, it seemed to run well enough given its condition, and its price matched exactly my modest budget. When I asked the dealership owner how such a decent car could be offered at such a low price, he mumbled something about it being relatively new and a real find. I knew he was bluffing and that this car had been sitting with him for a while — but what surprised me was his answer to my second question.

“It has no seller,” he answered, while chewing on a tuna sandwich that was stinking up the small office he sat in.

“What do you mean?” I asked.

“A friend of a friend brought it to me, without any documents. Said he was doing me a favor and that it was a great car.”

So that’s why the price was so low — he didn’t have the previous owner’s paperwork. I’d figured him out.

He wrinkled his nose and gave a small smile. “So do you want it or not?”

“We have a deal.” We shook hands, and that same evening, the black Toyota was parked downstairs from my building.

Honestly, I think I got a good deal — a car like that I would have found for four or five times the price out there on the market. But it seemed like fate was smiling at me for once. The next morning I got ready to drive to work in my new, not-so-shiny-but-functional car. I got up as usual at five-thirty in the morning to make it to the office by eight. It was an eight-to-eight shift, morning to evening, so as you can imagine, I barely had any time to myself.

When I left my apartment and walked toward the car, the sky was overcast. I think I started to feel a drizzle, which surprised me since it was the middle of June. But what surprised me even more was the moment I approached my new car and discovered that the driver’s door — the replaced door — wouldn’t open. I tried again and again but it seemed stuck. I was afraid to be too aggressive with it; I had just bought it the day before and the last thing I needed was a trip to the mechanic. The problem was that the car had no remote key fob, so you could only get in through the physical opening of the driver’s door. It seemed like the bent metal had jammed the locking mechanism. Eventually I managed to get it open. Believe it or not, it delayed me by a full hour — half an hour trying to open it and another half hour because I couldn’t beat the usual traffic. So I didn’t exactly get to enjoy all the car’s features, and I was pretty busy trying to drive fast to work while my boss screamed at me over the phone.

The work day dragged on slowly, and at nine in the evening I stepped out of the office. The morning’s delay had left me dealing with extra paperwork. I was exhausted when I got into the car, and I had a two-hour drive ahead of me. And if that wasn’t enough, a thunderstorm broke out. Heavy rain began to fall, and I was grateful that at least I had a car and wasn’t running soaking wet between buses and hitchhikes. I started the engine and got ready for this grueling drive. I was tired. I started flipping through the car’s radio stations — maybe I’d catch some upbeat, energizing channel that would keep me alert. But the reception in the area wasn’t great, and adding the weather on top of that, I realized the radio wasn’t going to save me.

I noticed that above the radio system sat an old disc player — and come to think of it, that was already an upgrade over my previous car, which had nothing like that. I pressed the open/eject button and hoped the previous owner had left a disc as a gift, maybe with a few good songs. But no disc was waiting for me. I began searching the car — maybe the previous owners had left some discs somewhere. After all, who uses discs anywhere other than in a car? And then, in the glove compartment, I found it. Beside some old rags and a few folded papers — sat a single disc. I pulled the disc out of the compartment and noticed that through the hole in its center, a colorful beaded bracelet had been threaded and knotted. An interesting item. But what excited me in that moment was the disc itself. It was old, a little scratched, but it was my solution to the tiring and challenging drive ahead.

I pulled the colorful beaded bracelet off and set it aside, and then I noticed something. On the other side of the disc, written in marker that had faded over time, was something in Hebrew: “The Gospel of John, Chapter 8, Verse 44.” Interesting, I thought to myself. Maybe the owner was religious and liked to listen to scripture verses while driving. But wait — this book wasn’t part of the Hebrew Bible. It was from the New Testament. I was worried that what I thought was a disc of songs might turn out to be a recording of some preaching priest. But the moment I inserted the disc into the player, my concerns vanished.

The disc began to spin in the reader and started playing, and the first song came on. It took me a few seconds to recognize it — the quality wasn’t great and there were quite a few audio interruptions — but it was “The End of the World” by Skeeter Davis. I was surprised. I hadn’t expected a song from the sixties, but I let it play and started driving. I’d heard that song a few times in my life. The singer tells the story of her beloved, saying that the day he stopped loving her, it was the end of the world for her. Pretty ironic that in the middle of a miserable storm, this was what I was listening to — but there was something calming about it.

Suddenly, as she sang the line cause you don’t love me anymore, the disc abruptly began to skip. I didn’t understand what was happening. It seemed the disc was stuck. I tried pressing the skip button but it didn’t respond. The song kept stuttering and looping back to that same line from the singer. The rain was intensifying and I was afraid to mess with the stereo when visibility on the road was so limited. I gave the system a light tap. It didn’t help. So I raised my hand and gave it a harder knock — and the song stopped.

That was strange, I thought to myself. But before I had time to mourn the songless drive now waiting for me, with the disc apparently stuck — I heard something. The disc was still spinning, and it was playing what sounded like a recording. A recording from a microphone. Static noises were audible, and then a chair scraped in the background of whatever room the recording had been made in. Suddenly, a deep male voice cleared its throat and moved closer to the microphone.

“Is this working?.. Good.. Sunday, September 14th, 2011. Recording number one of: The Beginner Dad’s Guide.”

The Beginner Dad’s Guide? I asked myself. That’s very… random. And in some way, it also felt a little aimed at me. The male voice continued.

“We brought her home from the hospital today. Emma. She’s just… she’s stunning. Liat is feeling a little tired — after all, it was a twenty-hour labor and tons of painkillers that had been preceded by nine months of hell — but when I look at Emma’s face, Liat and I both understand that it all makes it worth it.”

Okay, that’s genuinely random. The previous owner of this car used to listen to some kind of parenting guide that some guy used to record. Or maybe… the dad was the previous owner? That was already more intriguing. I decided to abandon the desire to listen to music and instead surrender myself to this man’s words. Maybe I could learn a thing or two from him, as someone who dreams of being exactly that.

“I don’t know what it means to be a dad. I never have been one. So I’m starting to record this journey from today — from the day of birth of my first daughter, Emma — so that Emma, or you, my son, or really anyone who’s listening to this, can join me on the most exciting and meaningful journey in a man’s life: becoming a father. So as I said, today is my first day as a dad. I’m already dying to find out what kind of journey awaits me together with the love of my life, Liat, and together with my new love of my life — Emma. So right now, all I have to say is that I have the most beautiful daughter in the world, and even though we’ve only known each other a few hours, she’s the person I love most in the world — just like my Liat. So that’s it for recording number one. I’m going to get some rest after this exciting day. I hope to keep this travel diary going as much as I ca—”

A woman’s voice: “Amos, can you come here for a second?”

“Oh — my darling Liat needs me. That’s all for now.”

The static noise stopped and the recording ended. Something in Amos’s voice made me feel at ease. And despite my exhaustion, something in his story made me want to keep listening. I pressed the button to skip to the next audio file. This time it worked, because the microphone static returned.

“Tuesday, October 1st, 2011. Recording number two of The Beginner Dad’s Guide. So last time I was probably pretty excited and emotional after Emma’s birth. Three weeks have passed since then and I can’t put into words how happy I am. My wife Liat is feeling much better. She and I both got an extension on our parental leave from work, and we’re using the time to rest at home with our Emma.”

Extended parental leave. Nice. My own boss would never have approved something like that. On the contrary — if I’d become a father, he would have done everything he could to get me back in the office as fast as possible. I envied Amos and his life with Liat and Emma, without even knowing him. Just his loving, happy voice made me want to be in his shoes.

“So I already have one lesson I’ve learned as a new father, and that’s to prepare yourself for as few hours of sleep as possible — because I’m telling you, Emma is charming, but I have never functioned on so few hours of sleep in my life. Emma wakes up roughly every half hour at night — sometimes to nurse, sometimes to eat, and sometimes just because she wants her mom and dad next to her. I’m telling you, it’s something special. No matter how many times I get up, the moment I see her calm little face when she’s in my arms, all the tiredness and irritability that might have been left in me just disappears. My temper issues are holding steady as best they can. I’m keeping them in check better than before. And that’s mainly because I’m in love with this little girl and also with her mother. My Liat understands this too and agrees with me. So that’s it for the second recording. I just wanted to share this here. I’m going to try to go to sleep now. As much as I can.”

The second recording ended and the disc automatically moved to the third.

“Saturday, June 5th, 2012. Recording number three of The Beginner Dad’s Guide.”

The third recording was nine months after the second. I was surprised. Amos, the enthusiastic new father, had forgotten about his travel diary for such a long stretch of time — which had to involve some kind of justification, or at least a few good lessons. It was funny, but I felt invested in the process. As if I was really going through this journey alongside him, and the anticipation rose in me at how much he’d missed.

“I know a lot of time has passed, but I’m just learning so much. Emma is already walking on two feet. It was moving. We took her to the park by the sea near our house, right on the Poleg beach, and she walked on her little clean feet from Liat’s hands — a few small steps — until I wrapped her in my arms. It was incredibly moving. There’s no way I can describe the feeling of pride — that a life you created is now a little girl walking toward you on her small feet in the sand, her short blonde hair, her eyes bluer than the sea she’s walking beside.”

“Anyway, another happy event happened: Liat and I bought a new car. We both went back to work and put our savings toward a new car for us and little Emma. A beautiful black Toyota — and pretty new at that — a 2010 model.”

I paused for a moment, trying to figure out if I had imagined it. I rewound the recording a little just to make sure — but I hadn’t imagined it. A beautiful black Toyota. Well, not quite beautiful and not quite new anymore — but I was driving the car of this wonderful family. Or at least a car that had belonged to them.

“We’re pretty happy with it. It’s new, and the black color really suits it. There isn’t even a single scratch on it, and I’m hoping Liat keeps it that way — but don’t tell her I said that, ha. Anyway, one of the fantasies Liat and I had even back when we were together in high school was to be the kind of family that goes on trips as often as possible — that we’d be able to accumulate as many experiences together with Emma as we can. In general, Liat and I have quite a few dreams together. We met at sixteen in high school. Even back then I knew I was going to marry this woman.”

“After university, Liat told me she was pregnant. We had both just stepped out into the big wide world and chose to continue together and build a family. So a week after the news, I took her to the cliff in Netanya at sunset and got down on one knee. You should have seen the joy in her eyes — and heard the happy shriek she let out when she screamed ‘Yes.’”

“My Liat is something else, I’m telling you. She wasn’t like the usual girls who loved boy bands or the most popular pop singer. She was an outsider. On our first date we went to see a horror movie. We both had this thing for the dark side of the world. For my seventeenth birthday she bought me a Satanic Bible at some old bookshop — not that I intended to use it. And honestly, I was a weirdo too.”

“I used to struggle with serious anger and violence issues, and Liat convinced me to go to therapy, which pretty much took care of the problem. Anyway, our first slow dance song was her absolute favorite — ‘The End of the World’ by Skeeter Davis. A lot of people see it as a depressing song, but Liat and I find a different interpretation in it — about love. A love for which we’d go to the end of the world.”

“The point I want to make is… I found the love of my life. Beautiful, and interested in the same things I’m interested in. We were worried that when Emma came, her presence would diminish our excitement for each other — but honestly, I love my Liat even more than before. She’s the woman of my life. My secret partner. And for her and for Emma, I’m willing to do anything. Even go to the end of the world. So lesson number three, I think, is this: know that if the love is real, your son or daughter will only lift that love between you and your wife or husband even higher. It’s like an infinite circle of love. I love them so much. And I hope you’ll experience a love like this too. That’s all for now.”

Wow. This thing — these recordings — that I found on a random disc in the middle of a storm in my car. I felt like they were a kind of compass for me. So much better than any podcast or show I’d ever listened to. Especially because I felt like I had so much to learn from Amos. Maybe this would prepare me better for the next stage of my life — finding true love and building a family.

I kept listening to the recordings one after another. Amos talked about the stage when Emma started going to kindergarten. In another recording he described her first friend she met there. Other recordings were about his and Liat’s relationship, their wedding, and even about a time they flew together with Emma to a haunted house in America just to test whether it was real. What a pair of crazies.

I really felt like I was connecting to them — to this beautiful couple and to Amos’s journey of becoming a father, and to all the good things that came with it.

I was already halfway home when I pressed play on the next recording.

The next recording began with the static of silence. And then, out of nowhere —

Shouts of a man. The sound of a chair breaking.

Instead of hearing Amos’s happy voice, the sounds of what seemed like furniture being deliberately and aggressively smashed flooded the speakers. I listened, disturbed by what I was hearing. Those screams were Amos’s. After some time of breaking things, his footsteps approached the table where the microphone sat.

“Liat… my Liat!!!”

He couldn’t finish the sentence. Confused and worried, as if I were sitting with him in that same room, I waited for him to calm down. But he didn’t. For several long and disturbing minutes — during which he didn’t stop recording — Amos simply screamed toward the microphone. I was frightened and skipped to the next recording.

Static sounds again. Heavy, slow breaths approached the microphone.

“Sunday, December 1st, 2013. It’s been a month now since Liat is no longer with me.”

Oh no. No no no. I said to myself.

“That day, I had been planning to record a guide for you and tell you that Emma had brought home her first drawing from kindergarten. She’s a true talent, that one. I picked her up from kindergarten and she showed me a colorful drawing she’d made in poster paint — of me, of her, and of… Liat. I called Liat to ask when she was coming home from work so I could tell her there was a surprise waiting for her from their daughter. The line just kept ringing and ringing. And then after about three hours, she did call back — but it wasn’t her. It was some doctor. And he told me to come urgently to Beilinson Hospital. And… that didn’t feel right to me… the way she was lying there… and then they covered her… my Liat… Oh God… I miss her so much.”

I stared at the road, wiping the tears running down my face. Why. How. A moment ago he was telling us how much he loved her.

“She was on her way back from work. That day she had stayed late. A bad storm was raging outside. She was driving our relatively new Toyota, and for some reason, some problem with the battery caused her to stop on the shoulder. All her lights went out and she got out of the car on the side of a fast, rainy, dark highway to get her yellow safety vest from the trunk. She opened the driver’s door and the moment she did — a truck driver had just been looking at his phone. He noticed too late. He swerved fast but not fast enough. He ran over Liat, who was standing right in front of the open front door, crushed her to death at fifty kilometers per hour between his bumper and the door, and ripped the door off with her. I could barely recognize her body. I miss her so much. What a needless tragedy.”

The rain drummed on the windshield of the car, growing stronger. Drops falling on the Toyota I was driving — the Toyota in which Liat had died. I looked at the driver’s door against which I had been leaning my knee for the entire drive. I immediately pulled my knee away and broke all contact with that replaced, non-original door. What an awful disaster.

The disc moved immediately to the next recording.

“Wednesday, May 7th, 2014. Nothing has felt right since she’s been gone. The car’s insurance said they could reimburse me for its value. But I chose to keep the Toyota and repair it. I replaced the front door and restored it — thinking that maybe it would restore you too, my Liat. But you’re not here. I don’t know how to manage, Liat. I don’t know what to do. I stopped going to work. And I know you wouldn’t like that. But the temptation was too easy. The gin and the vodka are the things that make me forget this awful reality.”

“I don’t know if you’re hearing this. You know that I overcame those violent impulses of mine. But I’m just losing it. I promise you I’m trying to drink as little as possible. But sometimes her crying — I just can’t control my hands. I promise you I’ll stop hitting Emma. Please come back. Emma misses her mother and I miss my wife. I’ll do anything. You know, sometimes at night I play our song — ‘The End of the World’ — hoping that maybe you’ll come and dance with me. Remember, my love? I’ll go to the end of the world just to have you back.”

The recording stopped. I sat in the car, my face shaken. What happened to him. How did he turn like this. What a broken man. I felt sorry for him. And I felt even more sorry for little Emma, who deserved none of this — a father who had lost his mind and was hurting her more and more. I hoped she was okay. It was difficult, but I had to know what had become of Amos. I played the next recording. And the situation had not improved.

“Here… here!! Wait a second…”

He spoke in a manic, unhinged voice, at a rapid pace. He moved away from the recording device, then addressed someone else: “Leave me alone!! Stop it!” Then he turned back to his listeners: “Sorry. It’s him again. He’s trying to distract me from the truth. Anyway — you won’t believe it, but I figured it out. Liat’s death was planned. It appeared on page 45 in the Satanic Bible. He didn’t want me to be a dad. He didn’t want us to become a family, my Liat. He doesn’t want Emma. He doesn’t want me. But don’t worry. I know how to keep the devil away from me.”

And then, without warning, in the middle of a lonely road — the engine of my Toyota stopped working.

Oh no. I pulled over to the side of the road. I wiped the window beside me and noticed it. I had been so swept up in the story that I’d ignored the Waze and ended up on some side road. No lights anywhere. No nearby gas station. The rain only intensified. Thunder struck outside. I needed to get out of the car and open the hood, check what the problem was, so I could get out of there as fast as possible.

But the fear. Knowing that Liat had broken down in exactly this way and ended her life here. I couldn’t open the door. I was too afraid that some inattentive driver would meet me with the same fate as Liat. I was in a dead zone. All I could do was turn on my hazard lights and hope someone would pass by and stop to help. I stayed inside the car. I turned on the hazard lights. The battery kept working, and so did the disc player. And the recording.

The disc jumped to the next recording. Which was also the last one.

“Saturday, July 7th, 2015. The Beginner Dad’s Guide — the final chapter.”

Amos’s voice sounded calmer this time. A few seconds of static silence passed.

“So I’ve been through quite a crazy journey here. I learned how important it is to have family by your side. And how cruel this life can be. But I found a solution. The devil won’t reach our family anymore.”

“My Liat… I finally understand what it means to be a father. Being a father means doing everything for your family. Even going to the end of the world, like you and I always loved to sing. Even though you’re not here… Emma and I miss you so much. Especially Emma. Well. So… I sent her to you.”

Silence.

What. What. No. NO.

“I couldn’t watch her suffer here in this world without a mother and with an unstable father who only hurt her more and more. So Emma is now together with you, with her mother. It didn’t hurt her. Her little head couldn’t resist your pillow. She didn’t feel it. And she felt even less when I shattered her skull. But that’s what was written in the book, my Liat. Only this way would the devil not reach our minds. I know life didn’t smile at us, but I still want to leave this world a keepsake of our family. So to whoever is listening to this — you’re surely listening to this disc in a car. So the disc is the keepsake from me. A guide on how to be — well — how to try to be a successful father. The car is the keepsake that will always remain from my Liat. And don’t think I didn’t take care of a keepsake from the most beautiful thing in this world. From Emma. Emma loved bracelets. I didn’t want to leave you just any bracelet. I wanted to leave the world a bracelet that truly came from Emma. Literally from inside Emma herself. It’ll be waiting for you in the glove compartment beside the disc.”

“And now, my Liat… The devil is lurking just around the corner. It’s time for dad to join you both.”

“And to you, dear listener — this was the final chapter of The Beginner Dad’s Guide. I hope you learned how important family is, and I wish you a little more luck than my family had. And whatever you do — love your family. And do everything for them. Even go… to the end of the world.”

The song “The End of the World” began playing through the recording. I listened to it in shock. And then, at the end — a gunshot. Amos had shot himself in the head.

I… I don’t know what in the hell… No. What. Why. Why did he do that?! What was going through his mind. Why did he do that to himself, and why on earth did he do that to Emma like that.

And then I remembered what he had said. Emma’s keepsake. The keepsake from Emma, more precisely.

I opened the glove compartment, where the small beaded bracelet was resting. I picked it up and then I noticed it. The bracelet wasn’t made of beads. It was made of… fragments of Emma’s skull.

I threw the bracelet and vomited inside the car. I had to get out of there. This was too much for me. I overcame my fear and pushed the driver’s door open. I fell out as a large vehicle drove past — moving fast. I was sure it was over for me.

But then it stopped. A young man got out and asked if I was okay, if I needed a hand fixing the car. I told him I never wanted to go near that car again and asked him to take me to the nearest gas station. I was convincing, because I shouted these words at him. I was an emotional wreck. We got into his car and he started driving. I looked in the rearview mirror — that car with its horrifying history sat there in the rain, disappearing over the horizon as we drove farther and farther away.

So that was my disturbing story. As I said at the beginning, this happened several years ago. And the reason I’m only telling it now is that a lot has happened since then. My situation improved dramatically after that night. I found a new job, moved to a different city, and found an amazing partner. And my biggest dream came true. Just a week ago, a beautiful daughter was born to us. Her name is Hannah. And she is the most beautiful thing in this entire world. I can’t describe to you how much I love her.

And well — the reason I’m telling all of you this is that I’m starting to understand that Amos. Don’t get me wrong. But when I look into Hannah’s little blue eyes, I remember why all these years I wanted to build a family. And why I wanted to be a dad. I love my new family. And I’ll do everything for them. I’ll do everything. For the love of my family.

I’ll go to the end of the world.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
