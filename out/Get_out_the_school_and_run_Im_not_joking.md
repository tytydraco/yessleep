Two years ago, my friend Ethan convinced me to go on this weekend getaway trip that was being run by two guys from our semester. I didn't personally know the two guys, but Ethan did; their names were Owen and Seb.

According to Ethan, Seb was massively into ’80s and ’90s rock music. That was a reason for me not to go, and I almost considered declining the invite, but someone going on the trip mentioned to me about an abandoned school in the area that was apparently massive and easy to get lost in.

This sounded perfect to me: a deserted school in the middle of nowhere.

I’d always been adventurous as a kid. Growing up, I would sneak off at night with my older brother, Damian, and make YouTube videos of us exploring abandoned places. Most of our videos flopped and got less than 1,000 views, but that didn’t mean anything to me; it was the thrill of being somewhere that feels cut off from the rest of the world and nobody knowing you’re there.

I hadn't been urban exploring for almost three years, so this felt like a lottery ticket for someone like me.

The nearest town to where we’d be setting up camp was roughly three and a half miles away. To get there, it took me about 80 minutes. I distinctly remember the feeling I got when I stepped out of the car; it’s just one of those specific moments you don’t forget.

It felt like my life was just beginning, even though I was only going on a two-night camping trip.

After spending what felt like hours setting up a small two-man tent with the help of Ethan—who was practically useless—I went and joined a group of five guys kicking a football to each other.

One of the guys I knew from my business seminar, though I’d never really spoken properly to him before.

Eventually, I spotted Ethan talking to Seb and went and joined them. Seb was wearing a Metallica hoodie and sunglasses that felt extremely out of place for a camping trip halfway through November.

The three of us stood there talking about what music we listened to as early evening quickly transitioned into night.

I soon found myself standing next to a small campfire, eating a flimsy hotdog with watery ketchup running onto my hands.

Ethan came up to me, followed by a group of four guys using their phones as torches. I recognised Omar, the guy who’d told me about the school originally; he was wearing shorts and a t-shirt as if it was the middle of summer.

Seb was just about to start a solo as we filed our way out of the group; Ethan joked to me that seeing a ghost would probably be less scary than hearing Seb sing.

I couldn’t help laughing to myself as we made our way through the pitch-black campground with Seb’s voice trailing off into the distance.

Then I felt a shiver of excitement as everything became silent, as we made our way to the edge of the campground towards a broken-down fence.

We hopped over it effortlessly and found ourselves in a massive field of dead corn.

No one really said much as we wandered continually through large, muddy fields. We were all just soaking up the thrill.

I shone my torch up into the night sky; I’d never seen so many stars before, as I’d spent pretty much every minute of my life in the city.

We finally reached a quiet country lane and walked across it. On the other side was a footpath that led through more endless fields.

This is where I remember getting a weird, uneasy feeling, like something telling me I’d gone far enough already.

The footpath felt like it was never going to end, then a huge patch of woods appeared in the near distance.

Omar said we were getting closer to the school and that it was on the other side of the woods.

I could feel that familiar rush of anticipation and excitement as we made our way through the trees.

I didn't expect the woods to stretch on so much. The tall trees towered above us, giving me the feeling that we’d come too far to turn back.

Finally, the trees started to scatter out, and we were walking towards a large, decaying building.

We shone our torches at it, revealing decades of thick moss and vine branches that covered every square inch of the exterior. Every window was boarded up, and any brickwork that was visible was covered over with graffiti.

I realised how massive the school was when we split up to try and find a way inside.

One of the other guys called us over to where he’d found a broken window that hadn’t been boarded up.

Climbing in proved to be a nightmare, and I ended up tearing a hole in my coat on a shard of broken glass.

Stepping inside the school felt like entering the frozen aisle at a supermarket.

I found myself in some kind of long, narrow corridor that smelt like dust and mildew.

We split up into three groups of two. Me and Ethan made our way down the corridor towards a wooden staircase that reminded me of the nightmarish stairs down to my grandparents’ basement, which terrified me as a kid.

The stairs creaked loudly with every step we took.

Something about reaching the top of the stairs and seeing a corridor full of old cardboard boxes triggered a primal fear within me that I’d never experienced before.

I tried hard not to imagine someone hiding behind a pile of boxes, just out of view. The boxes were all just empty and covered in dust.

Ethan carried on down the corridor and disappeared into a room at the opposite end from me.

I stood there watching a massive house spider trying to escape the light of my torch, deciding whether or not to try and scare someone with it.

I picked it up and continued down the corridor towards the room I’d seen Ethan entering a second ago.

The room turned out to be another corridor, this one even longer than the one I’d just left. Most of the doors were closed, some of them even boarded up, which I found slightly unsettling.

As I came to the end of the corridor, I could see a light, which I assumed was Ethan’s, coming from the far end of what looked to be a massive library.

I turned my torch off, hoping to land a good scare on Ethan, and began making my way through the rows of empty bookshelves, moving slowly so Ethan wouldn't hear me.

Then I stopped dead in my tracks.

There wasn't just one light; there were two.

I knew for a fact that Ethan had gone off alone, and no one else had gone on ahead of me except for him.

As I stood there, frozen in the realisation that we might not be the only people in this school, I must’ve been making noise without noticing.

One of the lights from the far end of the library was aimed in my direction.

I managed to duck down behind a bookshelf with about one millisecond to spare.

This was the moment I knew for certain I was in the room with two complete strangers, as a woman’s voice cut through the silence.

I couldn't work out what she said, as it was in a foreign language; it sounded like Spanish.

But judging by her tone, it sounded as if she was expecting to see someone she knew standing where I was.

That meant there were more than two of them, and none of my group were aware.

My heart was racing like crazy as one of the lights began to move forward in my direction.

I caught a glimpse of a really tall guy—who had to be at least 6'3"—making his way over to where I was hiding.

He was wearing a grey ski mask and a tracksuit that didn't fit him properly.

I literally had to force myself not to start running, knowing it would only make my situation worse and escalate the potential danger I was in.

I felt helpless as I crouched down, trying to control my breathing from getting too loud, which was frustratingly difficult.

All I could do was wish I was still at the campground listening to Seb’s terrible singing.

Then, just as the man was turning round to go back to where he was before, my phone vibrated in my coat pocket.

I cursed under my breath as the light from the man’s torch swung round in my direction.

For a split second, I considered the possibility they were just another group of urban explorers who happened to be in the same place as us.

But something didn’t feel right about the way they were behaving, not to mention the guy had a ski mask on.

I’m not sure whether he’d seen me or not, but the thing that made me take off sprinting was when I saw the other lady moving quickly over to where the tall guy was.

Maybe I was imagining it, but I swear she had some sort of knife in her hand. I never got a proper look at what it was, and I’m honestly glad I didn't.

I was on my feet, running full speed back through the library, trying to remember the way I came, even though it was only five minutes ago.

I turned my torch on, even though it made me 100 times more visible; but looking back, I don’t think I would have been able to navigate my way back to the exit without it.

My stomach twisted as I remembered how we’d entered in through a broken window.

My chest felt like it was being weighed down with fear as I reached the final corridor.

I just couldn't get myself to take the risk of climbing through, so I switched my torch off and darted into the nearest room I could find.

I could hear my phone vibrate in my coat pocket, like it did before.

Then footsteps filled the silent corridor, and I prayed that my phone wouldn't give me away.

To my utmost relief, whoever it was who’d gone after me decided I wasn't worth their time and disappeared back the way they’d come.

But my relief quickly turned back to fear as I thought about Ethan, who’d gone ahead of me and probably had no idea the danger he could potentially be in.

On top of that, there were four other guys who were most likely just messing around in groups of two.

Then I remembered how the lady had thought I was someone she knew; this meant there were three, possibly more, complete strangers roaming around in this abandoned school we were exploring.

I took my phone out of my coat pocket and checked my notifications (and made sure to turn my phone to silent).

I had two unread messages on the group chat Seb had set up for the camping trip.

They were both from Ethan:

Get out the school and run. I’m not joking.

I still remember the feeling of anxiety I got from reading those messages. It felt like a confirmation that my situation was real.

I sent a message telling everyone to get out immediately, pocketed my phone, and made my way out of the room I was in.

Just like climbing in, climbing out proved to be a nightmare.

This time my shoulder caught on the glass, digging its way through my coat.

I then jogged towards the woods, keeping my torch switched off to make myself invisible in the darkness.

Once I was a safe distance from the school, I crouched behind a bush and pulled out my phone.

The group chat was filling up with spam messages from people back at the campground. Most of them just assumed it was a joke.

I messaged Ethan privately, asking him where he was.

I waited a few minutes, and then he responded saying he had literally just found a way out.

Five minutes later, Ethan appeared and we hugged each other for like the first time ever.

Ethan joined me behind the bush, and we sat there waiting for everyone else to appear.

Ethan added the other guys to a group chat, and we used it to ensure all of them knew where we were.

Ten minutes later, all six of us were present, and we began jogging back the way we’d come, pointing our torches at the ground.

Every so often, I would look behind me to check we weren't being followed.

Once we reached the empty road we’d crossed earlier, we stopped and walked the rest of the way back to camp.

This time round, the atmosphere was inevitably different. No one was soaking up the thrill. Tension hung in the air, and even the stars felt like they were lying in wait.

At some point, one of the guys asked me and Ethan what had even happened.

I explained my story in detail, then listened as Ethan gave a genuinely unnerving account.

After walking into the corridor where I had last seen him go, he continued straight through the first door on the left.

This explained to me why he hadn't noticed the light coming from the library.

He found himself in a classroom that had a door leading out into another corridor.

This corridor led all the way down to the other side of the school, and eventually to a flight of stairs that led down to the ground floor.

But as Ethan was walking them, he started hearing faint voices from under him.

Like the woman who I thought I saw holding a knife, Ethan was certain he could hear people having a conversation in Spanish.

This is the moment he sent his first message, telling us all to leave.

But just as he was about to turn back, he heard someone coming down the stairs.

If he hadn't turned his torch off to send a message, it would’ve given him away completely.

Before whoever it was could round the corner, he hurried to the bottom of the stairs and began pacing through corridors, not wanting to find out who it was.

He took a quick opportunity to confirm that his previous message wasn't a joke.

As he moved deeper, he started to feel more and more like whoever else was in the building was looking for him.

He wanted nothing more than to find a way out of that school.

Eventually, he knew he had to stop wandering through corridors endlessly and try to find a broken window or something.

He shone his torch aggressively into every classroom he passed, hoping to come across a way out.

But the next thing he saw is what freaks me out even more than my own encounter, and I can’t explain why.

As he shone his torch through a doorway, it revealed a broken window that Ethan could’ve fitted through.

But he didn't.

He still doesn't know why he didn't.

The room was extremely long, and the window was only just in view from the doorway.

Something inside Ethan had just stopped him from entering the room.

Call me what you want, but that part of his story still keeps me up at night, even two years later.

Thankfully for Ethan, he soon managed to find a door that was completely off its hinges.

Back at camp, literally everyone had gone to bed.

This was the first time I registered how late it actually was.

The time on my watch was exactly 2:00 a.m. when I looked.

I’m pretty sure over three-quarters of that time was spent walking there and back.

We made the decision to call it a night instead of trying to convince everyone to go home at two in the morning.

I don’t think I got more than ten minutes of sleep, though, and I don’t think Ethan did either.

He looked rough the next morning, and so did I.

No one mentioned the night before. I just went along with people when they assumed it was a joke.

By the time evening came, the four other guys who’d gone with me and Ethan had gone home.

Omar thanked me and Ethan for our warnings before he left.

Me and Ethan left at the same time before sunset, as we too weren't staying around for nightfall.

Since this experience, I haven't gone urban exploring again, and I probably won’t for some time.

Surprisingly, the six of us actually still keep in contact through the group chat Ethan made on the night, but the school is rarely brought up.

I’ll probably never be able to forget the terror of being in that school library with two complete strangers in the dead of night.

Also, like I said earlier, I can’t explain why the end of Ethan’s story haunts me more than anything else that happened to me on that night.

Something about the way he explained not being able to enter through the doorway and into that room, towards the broken window, scares me in a way I can’t explain.
