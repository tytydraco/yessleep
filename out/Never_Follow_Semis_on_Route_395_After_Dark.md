This story occurs back in the late nineties. I had just graduated college and was feeling the freedom I thought would happen when I turned 18. I was born and raised in west LA. I was really close to all of the developing surfing and skateboarding culture, but instead I was climbing all the time. While I mainly did bouldering in the nearby gym, I also did a bit of lead climbing when I was thirteen. Since there weren’t ohms yet (little thingies you clip on the first clip while lead climbing so the lighter belayer won’t get yanked halfway up the wall and likely hit the climber), I got hurt quite a bit. I still loved it, so I was climbing every day I could, whether it be ropes or boulders. I got pretty good, eventually climbing V9s by 17. Since all the money I had went to the gym membership, a coach offered me free technique lessons in exchange for helping with some of the summer camps.

He drove me to my first comp and it went pretty well. It was all indoor bouldering and I got all (age specific) sends in the first couple tries and even flashed (first try) a dyno V8 (jumping). That's where I met Nick. He was the same age but from out in the desert, similarly doing his first comp. Instead of V9s, he was regularly sending outdoor V10s which was crazy. He won the comp and we began hanging out. Since comps were occasionally offering prizes and were mostly in LA, he drove out on weekends from 29 Palms, hung out, and we competed against each other. Mostly, he sent harder boulders and I sent easier ones.

At one point, we both drove out to Bishop where there were thirty foot boulders. I got there and waited for hours before he arrived, visibly shaken. His eyes darted to the highway where a large truck was passing. I asked about what was up, but he wouldn’t talk though, all he wanted to do was climb. I obliged. We drove out from the gas station we agreed to meet at to Buttermilks. We warmed up on some of the easier climbs, eventually progressing to a 20 ft top out boulder. It was rated a V8, but was obviously giant on shitty pads, so it was often advised to climb a grade or two down.

I fell off around 10 feet up as my arms were super pumped. It felt like the longest routes in a gym times two. He climbed after me and got to the last couple moves. I could see him straining more than he ever normally did and he went for a move, then fell. Luckily, he fell on the pad, even if at the side of the pad. He fell bad, but was able to walk it off. The thing he wasn’t able to walk off was his finger. His pulley in his middle finger was excruciatingly painful.

On the walk back to the car, he was blowing on his fingers and muttering swear words. I put the pads in the trunk and we went for dinner. It was tense and it kept feeling like he was on the verge of telling me something, but stopped himself just short of it. He said he couldn’t climb on it and just wanted to go to bed. We both slept in our cars. When I woke up, he was moving his fingers one at a time and would wince every time his right middle finger went.

“I gotta see a doctor about this. I think I’m going to head home,” Nick said, “I don’t want to drive, is that ok?”

I asked what he was going to do with the car. He pondered it for a second, then, “You know how to avoid that big road?”

I suggested going left on the highway after Lone Pine and finding his way with that and he shrugged. He got into his car and drove back. He called me a couple days later and said that the finger injury was really bad and he probably couldn’t climb for months. That obviously sucked as he was thinking about going professional.

Over time, I climbed less and less, but remained good. College came and went. I was out of a job and my parents had moved to the east coast for a ‘fresh start’. Then Nick called me.

I had pretty much forgotten about him. He hadn’t climbed with me in over four years and hadn’t talked on the phone in around three. I picked up and he told me he had moved to LA a week ago for a job and was bored and didn’t know anyone. We talked for an hour, then I suggested getting some burritos. He sent me his address and I picked up some mexican food and brought it there. We talked for a while and of course the topic eventually landed on climbing. Nick said that his finger had healed, but hadn’t climbed for a long time. I was in a similar location. Besides the meh college climbing club I was in, I hadn’t really done anything over a sandbagged V6 in years.

We hung out more and more and started climbing at the local gym a couple times a week. I was able to send a soft V8 and him a solid 9. After a couple months, I suggested doing outdoor. He agreed and suggested Joshua tree. He had grown up at the military base in 29 Palms with nothing to do, so spent most of his time outside of school bouldering and trad climbing. I wanted to go up near northwest Tahoe for a week and suggested going to Bishop and Mammoth on the way. He froze for a second, then breathed a little nervously. He agreed and said he could take a week off in a month. I put it on my calendar. When the time came, I packed up my gear, a bucket of chalk, a harness and some quickdraws, plus the pads. We decided to drive together.

Since he had gotten off his last day of work around six, we left around seven. The way to bishop is pretty relaxed. Go through the hellhole known as Palmdale, then ride the U-395 past Lone Pine and eventually to Bishop. It was dark as we went past Palmdale and the night was moonless. Nick was seated in the passenger seat and was fiddly. I eventually broke the silence and asked, “What’s up? It seems like whenever I mention Bishop you get really quiet. We can skip it if you want. I know you got hurt there and everything.”

Nick nodded for a second, then had a half smile that didn’t reach his eye. He paused another moment, then said, “It’s not Bishop. I love the desert and I was here, well really Lone Pine but who cares, but that was a couple years ago to hike Whitney. It’s just… this road. You know?”

I stared ahead for a bit, then asked, “You hiked Whitney? That’s awesome. I’ve been wanting to do that for years.”

Nick chuckled then said, “Yeah, yeah. Was with a friend. They got deployed a couple months ago and I just decided to move from 29 Palms to LA after that.”

“Yeah,” I said, “What about the road?”

Nick breathed out then shook his head, “I just saw something when we went to meet at Buttermilk and it kind of freaked me out, you know?”

I thought back to seeing an awful car accident on the 405 a couple years back and never really wanting to go on that road again and nodded, “Yeah, similar happened to me except different place.”

I continued driving for a while in silence, then turned on the radio. About half an hour later, I needed to take a leak, so I waited for an area of the road with a side I could pull off on. I turned on my hazards and got off the road. There was a slight decline off the road and I scrambled down. Nick got out and followed down the slope before turning the opposite direction of me. After I was done, I spotted something metal on the ground a little bit away. I kicked it and it rang out a clang. “Nick, you see that?”

I went up and opened the trunk of my car where the pads almost rocketed out before I stopped them. I grabbed a headlamp and turned it on, aiming where the metal piece was. It was a long capital ‘i’ shaped piece. Nick looked at it for a second, then said, “looks like an undercarriage for a semi.”

He stared at it for a second, then I said, “Huh, weird. Let’s get going.”

I got back in the driver's seat and he took a second to get into the passenger seat. I reentered traffic and began driving again, this time a couple hours.

After the time in the car, we were both famished. We waited until we got to a bit outside Lone Pine. There was a half diner, half gas station. The tank had less than an eighth of the gas remaining, so I pulled up and filled up the tank before parking the car. A couple truckers idled next to us, one taking turns smoking and whistling. Nick got out of the car and stretched, before opening the doors, the bell above ringing. A hostess with a valley twang told us to sit in the back.

The lights thrummed as I sat down. She brought two menus and handed them to us. “Coffee? We only have black.”

Nick and I both nodded and looked at the menus. I ordered an omelet and Nick got french toast. She picked up our menus, then started to walk away, but turned back quickly, “Are you meeting someone here?”

Nick shook his head and she sighed, “Sorry, you looked about his age. He’s been playing with his cup of coffee for a couple hours and looks a bit lost. You’ve been on this road before?”

“Yeah,” I said, glancing at a kid in the corner of the diner, but not focusing, “Why?”

The waitress smirked, not in an unkind way, but more of a way of trying to say something difficult, “He… I think he had his first run in. Why don’t you go talk to him?”

She gestured to the kid. As I looked at him, I noticed he was eighteen at the most, dirty blond hair, and clearly terrified. He was fiddling with a sugar cube, his eyes unblinking. He then noticed our eyes and stopped, dropping the cube where it split on the rim of the mug. Nick locked eyes with the kid and I turned to face him as the waiter walked off. Nick didn’t look back at me though. I turned again to the kid who was shaking as he stared at Nick. I chuckled uncomfortably and looked at Nick again, then the kid. The silence felt like it held a tension. I looked at the kid again, then noticed a perfect way to break it. “Oh hey, you have Merells! You hiking whitney?”

That seemed to snap them both out of it. The kid snapped to staring at me, then took a breath in like he hadn’t been breathing for minutes. “W-what?” he said, startled.

“You’re… wearing hiking shoes. You’re a hiker?” I repeated.

The kid ignored me, then turned back to Nick, “You know what I saw?”

Nick took a second, “Yeah. A couple years back.”

I looked around like this was some sort of joke I wasn’t in on, even though I was starting to feel like something was definitely wrong. “How long ago?” Nick asked.

“The sun just went down…” the kid said, jittery, “like eight?”

Nick nodded. “You were driving?”

The kid nodded. “I had a tailgater so I couldn't slow down.”

“Shit,” Nick breathed, “where are you headed?”

“I have a hotel tonight in San Bernardino,” the kid said, then switched to sarcasm, “and that’s totally going to happen.”

“Yeah,” Nick said, “You think you’re going to stay here?”

“Closes at eleven,” the kid said, “Sure as hell not sleeping in the car though.”

Nick nodded, then said, “We’re going up to Bishop. If you are fine with that, there’s a seat in the car empty.”

The kid shook his head, then said, “That’s the opposite direction, sorry.”

Nick thought for a second and said, “You could drive, stay behind a normal car the whole time.”

There was silence for a couple seconds, then the kid outstretched his hand into the air, staring at it. The fingers were shaking like he had just taken drugs, “I don’t know, man.”

The waiter approached, in her hands two plates. She sat them down on the table, then placed two empty mugs next and scurried to the back where she emerged again with a coffee pot, pouring it into our cups. “Thanks,” I said.

She gave a quick smile, then returned to the other side of the diner. “You know anyone near here?” Nick asked the kid.

The kid shook his head softly, “Was staying with some friends in Mammoth, but I can’t backtrack.”

“Ok,” Nick said.

I began to eat my omelet. It was bright yellow and contained bell peppers and jalapeno slices. In fewer words, it was really good. “If you don’t want to drive on the 395, you can catch the 136 to 190 halfway to death valley then go to Ridgecrest from there. I’m pretty sure there are plenty of options to get to LA from Ridgecrest.”

“How far down the 395 is that?” the kid asked.

“I think only about a mile or two,” I replied, “When Nick got freaked out, he took that and was fine.”

Nick nodded. “Shit,” the kid said, thinking, “That sounds good.”

I nudged Nick and whispered, “Eat, I don’t want to get into Bishop too late.”

Almost like he remembered he had to eat, he started to dig into his french toast. The kid took a couple sips from his mug, then nervously asked, “Is there any chance, once you're done of course, can you go with me to check the cars.”

I raised my eyebrow, but Nick agreed. We continued to eat. Eventually, I finished off the meal and sighed. Nick finished soon after. I stood up and headed to the front to pay. The kid got up and followed me. Nick went up last. My headlamp was still in my pocket from when I pissed on the side of the road. I opened the door and took the headlamp out, “so what are we looking for?”

The air was strange. It was dry and felt warm like daytime, but also carried a chill. “If we see it, you’ll know, but if not, it’s all good,” Nick said, extending his hand for the lamp.

I tossed it to him and he caught it, turning on the light. It flickered for a second, so he hit it with the palm of his hand and it went fully on. We walked over to the parking spots on the side of the diner. Nick shone the light at the hitch of the SUVs, walking parallel to them. Eventually we got to the two semitrucks.

The first semi was normal, the light illuminating the somewhat new steel bottom. We continued to the chainsmoker’s semi, and as Nick shone the light to the back of it, he froze. The chainsmoker saw us looking at the back and exclaimed, “Every time! Every single time on this goddamn road!”

Across the back bumper and metal plates were large deep scratches. Four extremely apparent ones were across the reflective back as they stood out as the only parts not reflecting the headlamp.

The kid started to hyperventilate, staring at the scratches in pure fear. “There’s nothing there still, it’s gone,” Nick stated, trying to calm the kid down, but the fear crept through his voice.

Nick turned around and physically turned the kid around to the row of normal cars, “Which one of these is yours. I’ll check the entire thing if you want.”

The kid choked out, “Red one.”

Nick walked over and the kid followed a couple seconds after. I inspected the back of the semi, dragging my finger over the deep cuts. As a stupid test, I tried to scratch it with my fingernail, obviously failing. I ducked down to further inspect the bottom, but Nick called me over. They were in the red car, looking everywhere for anything. Nothing was there, so we parted ways with the kid. He thanked us and we went back to our car. I noticed Nick checking the bottom, seeing nothing out of the ordinary.

I put the key in the ignition and started the car. Nick stood outside the passenger seat looking at the red car pulling out of the parking lot before he opened the door. I backed up and merged onto the 395 heading towards Bishop. I drummed my fingers against the steering wheel, “Where did you see it?”

Nick inhaled sharply before replying, “a bit under 10 miles south of Independence.”

“And we’re going right through there in a couple minutes,” I said.

Nick chuckled, then said, “Get behind a normal car.”

I slowed down, falling behind the semitruck in front of us, letting a blue SUV pass us before regaining speed. We got to Bishop a couple hours later. We found an empty parking lot and put our seats back. Nick repurposed one of the mats as a mattress, despite me warning of all the diseases on it. I just leaned back in the driver's seat.

The rest of the trip went well. We sent some V6s and V8 highballs near Bishop, then went through Mammoth to Tahoe. We camped and did some sport climbs and large hikes in desolation wilderness. It was really great. We headed home through Sacramento and took the 101 back down to LA.

That was over 20 years ago. Just last week from when I am writing this, I headed out to Lone Pine to hike Whitney. I left super early with a friend (not Nick, his name is Joshua) and got back pretty late at night. We didn’t get much of a good dinner, so I said we should drive to Bishop where there’s this burger place that has the best milkshakes I’ve ever had. The plan was to drive out and sleep in the car and get it for lunch and hang out in Bishop for the day before driving back home. I had pretty much forgotten about the rules about driving at night from Nick, and much less tell Joshua. He drove as I was in no state to drive after the long hike. We trailed a couple hundred feet behind a semi for about five miles. My friend was the first to notice something hanging on to the bottom.

“What’s that?” he asked.

“What?” I replied tiredly.

“Below the semi,” he responded.

Suddenly, the memory of my experience with Nick flashed into my head and I bolted up. “I don’t see it,” I said, “Fall back a bit.”

I aimed my tired eyes at the bottom of the truck, not seeing anything. Joshua turned on the brights for a second and that’s when I saw it. There were claws visible covering some of the bumper, holding on. He didn’t slow down. “Do it!” I yelled, panicked.

He broke suddenly, enough to jolt motion sickness into my head. Not completely, but losing around 10 mph in a second. A car behind us let out the horn and swerved to avoid hitting us, going into the oncoming traffic lane. Luckily, there weren’t any cars, so he passed us without a collision. The guy gave us the finger.

I relaxed a little bit. “Let’s continue to slow down a little,” I said, staring unfocused at the bright blue Audi driving in front of us. Eventually, our low speed had the two cars disappear into the night. As we drove, Joshua asked, “What the hell was that about?”

I sighed and didn’t answer, slapping my hands on the car seat. Time passed as the night got darker and darker. The low rumble of the street almost lulled me to sleep multiple times, but the primal fear of the experience shot my eyes open again. “Can you tell me what happened?” Joshua asked.

I groaned, stretching my legs as far as I could in the passenger seat, then asked, “You know Nick.”

“Uh huh.”

“Well, he had an experience with what was hanging on to the semi,” I said, and that was enough.

I think Joshua saw more than just the claws as he was normally very confrontational to topics around religion and supernatural occurrences. About twenty minutes later, with no cars in front or behind us, I spotted something bright off the road. As the headlights slowly revealed the shape of the object, it began to form the shape of a car. A bright vibrant blue Audi, windshield shattered, no driver in sight.
