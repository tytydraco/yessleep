I awoke to an overwhelming bump in my seat as some minor turbulence had dragged me from a dream I could not remember. I looked outside my window and I could now see the airport which rested almost entirely isolated with only the faintest glimmers of the runway lights peeking through the fog that surrounded it. At 20,000 feet there should have been lights, towns, highways, something, anything at all-but there was barely anything to be seen from up there.

There were few houses to be seen nearby and those that I found rested at least a mile apart from their closest neighbor. I hadn’t exactly done a lot of research on this place before I left, which in hindsight was probably a foolish thing to do, but this had not exactly been what I imagined when Adrian had previously painted his home town to me. He had made it sound like a quaint little town where everyone knew everyone. But from up there it felt like a world where no one knew anyone. The kind of place where faces are lost to the distance of the road and forgotten entirely.

It was then that the captain’s voice rang throughout the cabin letting us know that we were about to embark on our final descent. He kindly requested we raise our tray tables and fasten our seatbelts just as the plane began to fall. Once again I felt that awful lurch in the pit of my stomach as we made our way down towards the runway. 

To take my mind off my own nerves I pulled out my phone to check the time. It was almost a quarter to 10 pm. I checked my service and found that I still had 0 bars. I shut off my screen then watched out my window as we approached the runway.

When we did finally touch down onto the tarmac we did so with the expected thud that threw everyone forward in our seats as the plane broke down to a halt. Within minutes we found ourselves taxiing the runway until we could finally park at the proper terminal. When we were finally approved to exit the plane everyone stood almost in unison as they collected their belongings and made their way off the plane. Seeing that I sat almost at the very back of the plane I was one of the last to exit the aircraft and just as I did so one of the stewardesses smiled at me and said “Welcome to Blackwater”.

As I exited the jet bridge I found myself inside Blackwater International which, to my surprise, was the smallest airport that I had ever seen in my entire life. There were only two terminals in the entire airport which rested at the far end of the building right across from one another. Each terminal housed nearly 2 dozen empty seats, each groaning with years of neglect. Beside one set of seats stood an old janitor mopping between the rows as he pushed his mop bucket alongside him. The old man turned towards me and for the briefest moment we made eye contact. Then he just nodded and carried on his way.

I moved my way through Blackwater International passing two separate retired kiosks that I can only assume once housed small restaurants or snack stores, and just as I turned the corner to head down the stairs towards the baggage claim I heard the crack of thunder behind me. I turned and looked towards the wall of floor to ceiling glass windows and watched as the sky opened to the burst of lightning which filled the night sky with flashes of light. Heavy rain started falling against the windows which echoed throughout the empty terminals. It was the kind of rain that sounded like bullets against rooftops, only periodically silenced by the trumpets of thunder roaring overhead.

As I made my way down the stairs and towards the baggage claim I found entire rooms completely devoid of any light. Occasionally I would find a worker fumbling around the facility, but whatever responsibility they were paid to perform was now being circumvented by the usage of their phones or iPads. 

Finally, I made my way to the baggage claim and was relieved to see other passengers who I had just spent the last 3 and a half hours with on our flight. Not to say that I knew anyone, but in some strange way it made me feel less alone. Like I was not the only visitor to this old rundown airport. I stood between an elderly asian woman and a teen boy as I waited for the carousel to run its course and bring my luggage to me. I was a little more than nervous at this point if I am being honest. Maybe it was the environment and decor or perhaps it was the fact that I had just traveled halfway across the United States to meet a man I had only started talking to weeks ago. Regardless, the teenage boy paid me no mind as he just slipped his ear buds back in the moment I started talking and tuned me out entirely. As for the elderly woman I quickly found that, although more than happy to carry a conversation, she did not speak a word of English.

To pass the time as least awkward as possible I pulled out my phone to check my messages. As it turns out I had 2 unread messages. The first was from Kim who had texted me around the time we were descending to ask if I had arrived safely. I took this moment to text her back letting her know I was picking up my bags. The other message came from a number I did not recognize. It read “Paul has been confirmed as your designated driver. He will be at the doors waiting for you at 10:15 PM”. I looked at the top of my screen for the time. It reflected back 10:06 PM. I didn’t like this at all. So, I navigated through my messages until I found Adrian’s name and opened our messages. His last message “See you soon” still stood out across the screen. I quickly tapped the text field and typed out a short message.

“Just landed and getting my bags. I guess some guy named Paul is picking me up? Friend of yours? What address should I be giving him?” I contemplated softening my message. I didn’t want my unease to read through so easily, but truthfully I was not at ease. Something here felt wrong. I could not decide whether or not it was related to Adrian, but regardless I did not like it. In the end I left the message as it was and pressed send.

Within moments of pressing send I heard a thump of the conveyor belt as more luggage came forth from the back rooms of the airport and there, riding the line, was my old large suitcase. It took the bends and made its way around the curve of the belt until it passed right before me. I grabbed it, extended the luggage handle, then tipped the bag back onto its back wheels and turned towards the exit. Near the exit was an old car rental kiosk which left no question of its vacancy. It did not require a master detective to deduce that it had been years since anyone had rented from there. Just then my phone buzzed in my pocket. I pulled it free and saw a response from Adrian.

“Glad to hear you made it safe!” followed immediately by a second text which read “Paul knows where to take you. If you're hungry I can prepare something for you to eat.” I could not tell whether Adrian’s text made my nerves better or worse.

The storm continued to rage outside the walls of the airport and the fury of rain and thunder fell like the footsteps of giants. On a few occasions when the thunder cracked hard enough and close enough the power in the entire building flickered, went out for a second or two, then came back on. I checked my phone which read 10:11 PM. Most of my fellow passengers were gone by then. Some had walked out into the darkness of the night, vanishing entirely to the rain and lack of street lamps. Others seemed to disappear out of thin air right inside the “safety” of the building. I swear on my life when I say I never saw any sign of them leave. They simply were there and then, without notice, they were just gone. One thing was for sure: There was no way I would be taking a single step outside this building until I saw a car waiting for me. Then, right on queue for 10:15 PM there was an explosion of lightning which illuminated the entire parking lot right outside the airport main doors and that is when I first saw it. Driving down the twisting aisles I saw a car rolling towards the main gate. Seeing that I was officially the last person in the building, from what I could tell, I told myself that this had to be my ride.

An old black Cadillac pulled down the lane and turned onto the main stretch of road that stood just outside the airport doors and stopped right outside the main gate. The rain fell heavy against the windshield which echoed so loud that I could hear it from inside the building. Then the windshield wipers kicked to life tossing away the buckets of water that had accumulated there in the mere moments since the arms had last moved. 

The car stood still for a few moments when finally the passenger window, which faced closest to the building, rolled down. It was then that I could hear the faint sound of a voice calling out, but I could not make sense of the words. I looked around the area I was standing and reestablished for myself that I was truly all alone. After another moment I stepped forward as the automated sliding door parted, but I did not dare step far enough for them to close behind me. Some part of myself was terrified that if they closed behind me they would never open again. Then I did the last thing I could do.

“Hello?” I asked.

“You are Claire, correct?”

“Umm y-yes I am. I’m assuming you’re… Paul?” I asked as I pointed to my phone.

“I am” He said and just as he spoke the words he threw open his car door and stepped out as he opened an umbrella above himself. 

He made his way carefully around the side of the car and opened the back passenger door then made his way over towards me. As he approached me I was terrified that I had come all this way to meet a cruel and unusual end, but instead, I was met with, not kindness, but professionalism as he handed the umbrella to me. He was tall and about as old as my father was. His hairline had seemingly receded long ago, but he still grew what little he kept. He placed the umbrella in my hand then spoke softly to me.

“I can get your bag for you. There is a warm beverage in the back seat.”

“Oh. T-thank you.” I said with a concerned smile. He nodded then proceeded to collect my bags, only stopping to raise an arm towards the vehicle as if to welcome or escort me into it. At that point I felt no other option within me but to comply.

I made my way towards the car and collapsed the umbrella down as I slipped inside the modest sedan. I could hear Paul close the trunk as a thump told me that he had successfully gotten my bag inside it. I turned to close my car door, but a hand I had never known before gripped the door handle and slammed it closed for me. Then Paul moved back around the car towards the driver seat. As he passed in front of the car the headlights threw his entire figure into the spotlight. Then he vanished back into the shadows and the rain until the driver door opened once again and he dropped himself into the seat punctuating it with the slamming of the door.

He took a moment to pull out his phone as he punched the address into his maps app. Then set his phone on the car mount which hung over the dashboard of the car. He pulled his seatbelt over his extended stomach then shifted the car into drive. Within moments we were out of the airport parking lot. I looked back for a moment as the airport shrunk away into the darkness. I cannot know for certain, but I swear that before it was even gone from my view, all the lights went out.

Paul mostly kept to himself during our drive which both made me feel better and worse about the situation as a whole, but I was curious how long our ride would take. I shifted forward in my seat just enough to get a good glimpse of his phone which indicated that we would be arriving in 20 minutes. 

I shifted in my seat closer to the window and looked out at what little view there was to behold through the rain and night. To be fair, even if the weather had been perfect and it was broad daylight there still would not have been much to see. We rolled past little houses and stores that were modest compared to the cities I had known back home. A few small diners, a hardware store, a church, and homes that were completely blacked out. Every couple miles I even saw old houses whose windows and doors had been boarded up. For those houses there was never anything indicating any living thing had been there in recent years other than fresh graffiti which stretched across the boards and windows. The only thing in the entire town of Blackwater that was open at this hour was a McDonalds. Its golden arches illuminated the small area that it was housed in with a faint yellowish glow. I looked in through the windows, as we passed by, and saw no customers. Only two employees mopping the floors and checking their phones. 

Every few moments the glass of the car window would fog over and I would raise my hand to wipe it away just enough to see a brand new world that did not impress me. To the contrary it unnerved me. It unnerved me in the same way that seeing any place so different from your normalcy would. To imagine that there were people that lived their lives here felt wrong to me. As if living here was a far cry to living at all. I started to wonder what a life here actually felt like. What did the day-to-day life of the locals look like? 

Soon the houses and stores thinned out more and more until they were gone entirely. Our car carried on well past a sign that read ‘You are now leaving Blackwater. Come back real soon!’. We moved towards a thick forest of trees that stretched so high I could no longer see the stars above. There were no street lamps there. No billboards with flashy signs. Not even a single other car to oppose our own and illuminate the area with any more light than we were afforded by our own headlights. It was then that I felt compelled to break the silence.

“P-Paul?”

“Hmmm?” he replied.

“That sign said ‘now leaving Blackwater’. Is that right?”

“Mmhmmm” he barely paid me any mind at all.

“I was under the impression that my destination was in Blackwater” I stated trying to hide any level of unease in the tone of my voice. 

“Address I got is just a few miles outside Blackwater. Probably just told you it was in Blackwater for simplicity”

“Oh... Do you mind if I ask how long you have known Adrian?” I asked. I didn’t actually think he knew Adrian personally, but I felt compelled to keep the conversation moving as the thought of sitting in silence any longer was maddening as my nerves got the best of me.

“I assume that's the name of the feller that booked the ride for you? Can’t say I know him at all.”

“So… you don’t normally drive for him? Or… people he brings out here?” I fidgeted with the strap of my bag.

“Nope. Not often really”

“But you have escorted someone to his house or escorted him somewhere?” There was a slight unease in my voice now. I had hoped to ask him this question with more self assurance or confidence, but nerves betrayed me.

“I brought someone to him once. Few years back.” He croaked.

“Like you drove his family or a girl?”

“Couldn’t tell ya. Barely remember” and with that I could tell that Paul’s patience for questions was running thin.

I sat back in my seat as I looked back out the windows. Shadows of the forest swayed and danced around us as the headlights flew past the trees that engulfed us. On occasion I saw a small animal dart from the tree lines. The forest there felt impossibly dense. I remember thinking that someone could walk into those woods and just disappear never to be found again. A morbid thought, there was no denying that, but in that moment that was where my mind had taken me. Then, after a few more minutes and turns, the forest broke giving way to a view of a cliff side that overlooked the crystal clear water of the neighboring lake which seemed to challenge Lake Michigan in size. The moonlight reflected off of the calm waters casting a midnight glow to the shoreline around it. The road then stretched and curved ahead of us up the cliff side which started to feel more like a mountain than a cliff.

The old Cadillac followed the road with little trouble as we rounded the cliff side. The road there was hardly well kept. Pot holes and vines speckled the asphalt creating obstacles that only an attentive driver could maneuver. To make matters worse the road in some areas became so narrow that only one car heading one way could possibly hope to traverse forward. Even then, there were times where it felt like the tires were at risk of being dragged off the road entirely. I tried to push that thought from my mind. I had always hated heights ever since I was a little girl and the fact that all the way up the cliff side there were no guardrails aided in the exaggeration of my nerves. If I were to open my car door right there, stick my head out, and look down I would see nothing below me but a free fall into the freezing waters below. I sat back in my seat and waited for the last few minutes of our drive to fade away.

As we turned the final curve of the cliff side I finally saw it. I wasn’t exactly sure what I had been expecting. Maybe a small suburb or town that rested at the peak of the cliff. Instead my jaw dropped as I whispered “Oh my god” almost inaudibly to myself. 

At the top of the cliffside sat, not a mansion, but a castle. Which rested just a stone thrown from the edge of the cliffside which overlooked the entirety of Blackwater Lake. It looked like something pulled straight from a fairy tale with large cast-iron gates which wrapped around the front of the massive structure. At each corner of the building rested a spire that rose and narrowed to a singular sharp point which poked into the clouds above. Cobblestone paths and walkways seemed to stretch all around the structure. Even the road, just past the gate, transformed into one long unkempt stone path. It was barely 10 feet wide. There were arches and tunnels scattered across the monolithic home with rotted trees and dying foliage filling in the gaps where structure was either thin or had fallen away due to time. The moonlight cast shadows that accentuated the faded grey stone.

Paul drove right up to the front cast-iron gates which held, in a metal circle, the letter G. The gates had been chained shut and appeared to have been that way for many years. We pulled right up to them as the car slowed to a crawl then stopped in its tracks. Slowly, Paul swung his arm back and placed his hand on the passenger seat head rest as he turned towards me. This allowed for half his face to come into view. 

“This is your stop” he murmured. 

“Here?” I asked. That couldn’t be right. The gates before us were locked, but just beyond them were cobblestone steps that stretched from there to the building just up ahead. There was at least 200 yards that separated us from the front doors of the castle. I could not make sense of this no matter how long I rolled it over in my mind, and the longer I thought about it the more it felt wrong to me.

“Yep”

“Are you sure? Is someone supposed to open the gate or…?”

“Nope.” Paul said as he raised his phone and swiped back to the ride share app. “Says here ‘Drop passenger at the front gate’. That's what they asked and that's what I did.”

“I’m sorry this just seems… off. Maybe I should give him a call.” I said as I swiped towards the phone app. Before I could place the call Paul broke the silence.

“Call him if you like, but I don’t got all night to wait. I’m getting your bags. I got other stops to make” He grunted. Then he threw open the driver door which filled the car with the sounds of thunder and the whistling of the cold night wind. Even here, high above the sea level, rain still fell like bullets on a battlefield.

He made his way around the car and threw open the trunk with a thump as he pulled my bags free. I went to call Adrian, but just as I was about to hit the call button my car door was thrown open and Paul stood there staring at me with as little patience as he could muster. He put out a hand, which I took, and helped pull me out of the car and into the cold rain. Then he slammed the door behind me as a burst of lightning filled the night sky.

“H-how am I supposed to get in?” I asked. He pointed to what must have once been a guard post built into the wall itself. 

“Note he left said the door there was left unlocked.” Then he turned without another word and made his way back towards the driver seat. Unsure how to navigate the situation I tossed him a nervous thank you which he replied with a grunt and a half hearted wave. Then the car slowly backed up, turned, and made its way slowly back down the cliffside leaving me entirely alone in the dark.

I stood there for a few moments dumbfounded by what had just transpired, but the chill of the night air against my now soaking wet clothes made it almost unbearable to stand there any longer. 

I shuffled my bags in hand as I fumbled with my phone trying to call Adrian as I made my way towards the guard booth, but I could not get a call through. Each attempted dial delivered to me nothing more than silence. I checked and sure enough I had absolutely no signal here. 

“Fuck me” I sighed. 

I stuffed my phone into my bag and made my way over to the large castiron gate that had moments ago been drowned in Paul’s headlights. Even then it had looked menacing, but now in the near perfect dark it felt like something ripped from an old horror movie. It stood there, wrapped in chains, creaking ever so slightly as the wind swayed it into soft brisk motion.

A few yards to the left of the main gate sat the old guard booth that Paul had mentioned. It was a little booth built into the gate itself with windows that stretched all around its small structure. As I approached it I tried to peer inside to get a good look of who, or what, could have once sat within those walls. What I saw was primitive to say the least. There were no computers or consoles to be found. Just old filing cabinets and notepads that were left behind with forgotten scribbles and slashes. 

I reached my hand out to the small doorknob, gripped it, then attempted to turn it. Just as Paul had said the door was unlocked and in the moment I wish it hadn’t been. This had not at all been what I imagined tonight would be after touching down in a small town that only a month ago I had never known existed.

I pushed the door inward as the old hinges creaked and moaned. Then I threw myself inside as I dragged my bags behind me. I am not certain if it was just the rusted hinges or the violent winds of, what was now, a raging storm, but I had to fight with nearly all my strength to shut the door behind me. And, just as the latch of the door clicked shut, the whole room was filled with light from the burst of lightning just above the castle. Shortly after, the roar of thunder boomed overhead and echoed within this small metal cage that was now sheltering me from the elements. The thunder there was so loud I heard it echoing deep within my skull. I cupped my hands over my ears and waited for the throbbing pain that now plagued me to pass.

After the initial shockwave of the crashing thunder in my skull subsided I lowered my hands then slowly took a look around the room. The room itself was relatively small. No bigger than a typical one car garage, but scattered along the desks and floors were years of filth and neglect. Old food containers, empty beer bottles, as well as musty blankets that had long been torn to shreds by whatever rodents now inhabited the small building.

I stepped around the shifted desks and found the back door that led to the stone walkway which stretched the length of two football fields from there to the castle. The rain showed no signs of stopping anytime soon and the longer I chose to wait there the longer the cold would have to set in. There was no turning back now. I just had to move forward. Besides, I had seen what little this room had to offer. I decided that I would sooner leave than spend another minute there. I made my way to the back door of the guard building dragging my bags close behind me. I sighed, shaking as I placed my hand on the door knob. I turned the knob slowly until the audible click rang out then I threw open the door which welcomed me with a violent gust of wind to the face that threatened to knock me backwards off my feet. I stood my ground then proceeded to step outside the door and make my way towards the castle doors.

The cobblestone steps of the walkway before me were uneven to put it nicely. Each step I took felt like a war between the pavement, my feet, and the luggage that I dragged behind me. Uprooted stone periodically hooked the wheels of my suitcase, and on occasion, tripped me at the ankle as the fury of rain fell heavy against my back. At that moment the world felt impossibly dark. There had been no streetlamps stationed along this road to Adrian and what little light my phone’s flashlight had to offer did little to cut through the fog and rain that circled me. 

All that there was in that moment was the lightning and moonlight which barely illuminated the looming castle before me. In a strange way the castle, in those moments, felt less like a castle and more like a lighthouse calling out to the travelers of the sea. Or worse, calling out to me. Every few steps I felt my foot sink into a pothole overflowing with icy water which soaked my socks and left my foot frozen to the bone.

On several occasions I contemplated going back, making my way back to the gates, and calling myself a taxi to take me far away from there. Anywhere else, but there. However, with every step of the way I convinced myself that I had come this far. Why not just go a little further? Then the thoughts of Adrian slipped in and I started to wonder how little I truly knew about this man. I certainly had no idea he lived in a place such as this. And on that matter, what kind of host expects their guest to walk such a distance in the freezing cold rain without any assistance whatsoever? 

It was those moments where the red flags should have set in, and yet I found myself continuing forward towards the castle doors. Whether I wanted to meet him face to face, or just uncover what other secrets he was hiding, I couldn’t even tell anymore. All I knew was that I kept moving forward. Each footstep dragging me closer towards that castle. Dragging me closer towards him.

After what felt like nearly an hour I hobbled my way to the end of the walk way on two frozen feet. I felt the rain freezing my hoodie to my spine as I dragged my bags up the old stone stairs which lead to a pair of double wooden doors. Tyler’s silver ‘t’ felt frozen to my chest now only occasionally tearing away with the heaving of my body. 

Finally, I reached the top of the stairs and looked towards the large wooden double doors which had to have been at least 10 feet tall each. Both of which sported old worn knockers that hung from the center of each door. Each of which were shaped like crescent moons. 

I looked around for a doorbell, but there was none to be found. After all of that what had I been expecting anyway? I reached out a frozen hand forward and looped my fingers through the knocker and held it for only a moment before I banged it into the wood five or six times. Then I let it fall back to the door with a thud. I turned around and looked out into the darkness. In the distance I saw the small town of Blackwater in the pale moon light. There were few lights still on and in those fleeting moments as I stared out to the town I saw a few more household lights go out. Something about all of this felt wrong. So wrong my stomach started to twist into knots right there at the doorstep. I felt the urge to run, but my frozen legs would not answer the call of my brain. The functionality of them had all, but abandoned me now due to the cold.

Then I heard it. A series of locks and bolts being alleviated of their duty as the door before me moved. It swung inward slowly on its hinges as the cold night air rushed into the building partially repaid with only the faintest feeling of heat escaping. I remember thinking to myself that this was it. There was no going back now. Then I heard that familiar voice that had, only hours ago, spoken such sweet words to me through the speakers of my phone. I recognized it almost immediately: Adrian’s voice.
