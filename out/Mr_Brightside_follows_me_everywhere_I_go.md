The first time I saw him was after my dad’s birthday party. Dad’s friends were all there, and I was wearing my new yellow dress. At only five years old, I was the belle of the ball, and I don’t think there was a single person there who didn’t stop to fawn over me. My mom busied herself making sure everyone was comfortable and with a full glass. All the while she batted away my dad each time he tried to help. Dad was usually a quiet man, but the two things that made him light up the most were his family and music - particularly his favourite band, the Killers. He even had their whole collection on vinyl. He’d always sweep me up in his arms and grip me in a big hug while we sang to the records together.  
  
Hot Fuss was the soundtrack for the evening, and the first song started playing just as we were sitting down to eat. Everything turned from joy to horror when Dad suddenly clutched his heart and dropped dead as a stone into his starter, freshly dished tomato soup. I was seated right next to him, directly in the range of the splash when he dropped into the thick liquid. Mr Brightside had just come on, and the record player got bumped in the chaos. I just remember staring at his lifeless body, drenched in thick fluid, shock, and sadness, and then the song starting to skip.  
  
 “How did it end up like this?\\How did it end up like this?\\How did it end up like this?”  
  
A question repeatedly asked which had one very simple and depressing answer - a massive and sudden heart attack.  
  
Later on I was in my room, sitting on the bed with my once bright and sunny dress forever stained a stodgy mix of red and orange. Something moved in the corner of my eye. I could see it in the dresser mirror. I was momentarily distracted from my grief as I focused on whatever it was. A dark shadow stood in the mirror. It was barely more than a silhouette, almost entirely devoid of features. Against the backdrop of the dim room, it seemed like a human-shaped void, where the light went to die. The only things that stood out were a cartoonish smile of unnaturally large pearly white teeth, suspended under two beady and shining black eyes.  
  
 My heart jumped to my throat, and I spun around quickly. There was nothing there. I slowly turned, looked back to the mirror and found the shadow right next to me on the bed, towering over my tiny frame and looking directly into my eyes. I screamed and then I ran.  
  
 Mom spent the rest of the night consoling a hysterical young girl. She heard incoherent cries about a black monster with funny teeth, but she didn’t take them seriously. Can I blame her? She was going through the loss of her husband and, as I would later find out, had her own little surprise that made the night’s events even more devastating than they already were. She had no thoughts to spare for boogeymen.  
  
 The events of my dad dying, and what happened after, became a terrible but hazy memory. The dark figure from my mirror had become a kind of mythological figure in my mind. Too strange to be real, but at the same time much too real. That night and The Killers had become permanently linked for me. Over time, I even started to refer to the figure by the name of the song, Mr Brightside. This was just to myself, of course; I would never dare mention him to anyone else.  
  
 The next time I saw him was when I was 13. My little brother, Jimmy, was seven years old at that point, and I, being a typical older sister, couldn’t stand him. Every question and daily hour of hyperactivity and the way he never stopped talking - everything bothered me. He even insisted on calling me by the nickname I hated. No matter how many times I told him that it’s Regina, not “Reg”. Looking back, these all seemed like such small and insignificant details. At the time, it was all too much for my blossoming teenage mind to handle.  
  
 We were on the bus on the way home. It was a nice day, and Jimmy was irritating me less than usual.  
  
 “Reg, let me sit by the window, okay?” he asked while jumping over the aisle seat.  
  
 “Whatever,” I replied, just rolling my eyes and sitting down next to him.  
  
“Henny is going to an amusement park tomorrow with his other friend, but they didn’t invite me. I don’t really care, though, because it’s boring there. He said it’s scary, but I don’t think it is, unless there’s a clown. There’s one of them outside the rollercoaster that checks your tickets. Clowns aren’t funny. Hey, Reg?”  
  
“Sure,” I answered, already putting my earphones in.  
  
 It was a typical run-on Jimmy story. A journey without any destination in mind. I knew the window would quickly grab his attention and he would be quiet the rest of the way home, leaving me to scroll on my phone in my prepubescent angst.  
  
 “Reg!”  
  
 My face tensed up and I squinted harder at my phone, hoping he would just leave me to my peace if I acted like I couldn’t hear him.  
  
 “REG!”  
  
 Exasperated, I looked up to tell him to shut it, and my eyes went wide at what I saw. On the far side of the road, there was an accident; at least an 8-car pile-up. There was smoke, fire, people screaming - the kind of thing you only see on the evening news. My mouth hung open, and Jimmy started to sniffle. I pulled him in close without thinking and turned him away from the horrid scene. We could do nothing else except sit in stunned silence the rest of the drive home.  
  
 When we eventually got back, I brought Jimmy up to my room as he was still shaking from the events of the day. I sat him on my bed, and that was when the floodgates opened. He wept and wept. The whole thing was too much for his young mind to handle. I just silently cuddled him until the crying stopped and I was sure he was asleep. When I heard him start to snore, I got up off the bed and went into the bathroom. I looked in the mirror and realised I had also been crying. I had only just started to use make-up, and it was now running all down my face. I looked like a clown, fake smiling through a grotesque mask.  
  
 A sudden and dark feeling descended onto me. The sound of Mr Brightside skipping started to play in my head, over and over. I felt like I was going insane, and I had no idea why. Just then, I noticed that the light from my bedroom was no longer visible behind me. It was being blocked out by a large and menacing darkness. It was an unmistakable figure of blackness, one I recognised immediately and one I could never forget.  
  
I went cold and froze. I could do little more than watch in the mirror as he stooped lower, his head just poking out from the top of the doorframe. One beady eye locked on to me, and I felt like a lamb ahead of the slaughter. He dropped lower still, hunching under the door frame and moving stiffly towards me. I just watched the mirror the entire time, quaking in place, sure that my life was over.  
  
 “Reg?” said a soft voice from behind me.  
  
 I blinked. It was a moment that seemed to last an age. When I opened my eyes, the spell was broken. Behind me was Jimmy, looking at me with wild, red eyes. Suddenly my body could move again. I whirled around and rushed to him, grabbing his arms in concerned panic.  
  
“Jim, are you okay?” I asked, almost forgetting about Mr Brightside.  
  
 “Reg,” he said with his voice wavering, “why was there a man in your bathroom?”  
  
I saw Mr Brightside many more times after that. Not only I, but Jimmy, too. He was there when we lost our grandmother, and he was there when the family dog passed away. Hell, he was even there when my first boyfriend broke up with me. He had never been bound to the mirror, as I thought. I’d find him in the strangest of places - in the street, on the roof, or sitting across from me on the bus. He would just look at me - watching. I tried to meet his gaze, to let him know I wasn’t afraid of him. He was getting stronger and, each time, a little braver.  
  
 More and more, every time I felt upset, he was there. It was torture. He made me afraid of fear itself - at the very idea of being vulnerable. My strategy was to become like a robot, not only to avoid negative feelings but positive ones too. What goes up must come down, I thought. In order to never go down, I had to stay level. I wish I could say it worked better than it did.  
  
To make matters worse, Jimmy started seeing him, too. In a weird way, it helped us get close and stay close as we grew up. We never told anyone else about him, but we kept tabs. We even had a kind of digital diary we could both access to record where we saw him and when, how close he had got, and anything else. It almost sounds like an adventure as I’m writing about it now, but it really didn’t feel like it at the time. It was survival.  
  
Things basically stayed that way until I was 18. I was at a house party after a particularly stressful week. I hadn’t seen Mr Brightside in a while, so this was just normal human stress for a change. Then, the DJ started playing the Killers’ song. I was actually having fun before that, but, as soon as I heard that opening line, my mood soured. I knew he was coming and that my night was ruined. It was so unfair. I just wanted to be normal. I couldn’t even relax after a shitty week without my ghostly stalker showing up.  
  
 I started hyperventilating and looking around wildly. Sure enough, there he was, standing just behind the DJ, who was none the wiser. It was like the loud music gave him power. He started moving towards me, through the crowd, his horrible teeth standing a foot above the tallest person there, easily. He moved straight through the crowd like they were water, all the time his eyes locked on to me.  
  
 I ran. I didn’t care about looking strange or getting stared at. I had to get out of there immediately. I just ran as fast as my legs could take me, out into the street, where a car, not expecting me just as I was not expecting it, crashed into me.  
  
I spent the next two weeks in hospital. The car had crushed my leg. I’d walk again, but it would take time and a good amount of rehabilitation. Jimmy and Mom were there with me all night on the first night. Eventually they had to leave, though I really didn’t want them to. Jimmy tried to argue but Mom quickly shut it down. He had school in the morning, and it was already 2 AM by the time they were leaving. Jimmy gave me a knowing look before they left.  
  
“Stay safe, Reg,” he said before Mom ushered him out.  
  
 Almost as soon as they were gone, he was there. Mr Brightside was in the corner of the room, just next to the door. The nurses came in and out and didn’t so much as look at him. He just stood there, watching as he usually did. He wasn’t coming any closer now, but I just couldn’t stand it. All of it, any of it. I was done and I didn’t care what happened to me anymore. So, I let it all out.  
  
I screamed at him. I screamed and screamed. It was the first time I had said anything to him, that I had even directly acknowledged him. He remained perfectly still, and when I was finally done with my tirade, he slunk away. The nurses rushed in and gave me tranquillisers to calm down. When I woke up in the morning, there was no sight of him.  
  
 I still saw hints of Mr Brightside after that, when I was feeling down, always out of the corner of my eye. But he never came fully into view after that. Eventually, he seemed to have retreated entirely. In time, even Jimmy said he didn’t see him anymore. I thought I had won, that I had beaten the monster under my bed. I couldn’t have known how wrong I was.  
  
A couple more years went by and I saw no sign of my demon. And, although I no longer looked over my shoulder every time I was sad, I still got a sinking feeling whenever someone mentioned that song.  
  
 I was 25 when I got a call from my mom one seemingly ordinary morning. I could barely make out a word between her heaving sobs. Finally, after a lot of questions and pleas to calm down, she did manage one word that I wished I hadn’t heard: "Jimmy".  
  
He was walking to the convenience store around 11 PM when someone jumped a red light. Hit and run. No one else was on the road at the time. He was dead before anyone could even call the ambulance.  
  
 That night I was alone in my apartment, weeping into my pillow. I was a broken woman. I loved Jimmy so much. I felt so guilty, like it was a sick joke that we’d both had car accidents, but his killed him. It wasn’t even his fault. How did I escape with a limp and him without his life?  
  
 Out of the corner of my eye, I saw something. It was something I could never forget. There, in the dresser mirror, I saw the clear visage of a shadow. Mr Brightside was back.  
  
 My crying stopped immediately. I wanted to run, just as I had before. I wanted to get run over again and this time have the vehicle deliver me from this nightmare for good, to deliver me to Jimmy. I wanted to, but I didn’t. Instead, I turned to face it.  
  
 He stood there in the corner of the room, like some kind of sheepish guest, too nervous to announce itself. I clenched my fist and gritted my teeth. I was an inch away from attacking this creature. He looked small and pathetic, I thought, and I would not be intimidated any longer.  
  
 Just then I paused. Small. He really did look small. All times before, Mr Brightside had loomed over the entire room like a haunting grandfather clock. But today, for the first time, he looked smaller than I.  
  
 There was something different about him that I could not quite place. My anger faded, and I just looked into his eyes, neither of us making a move. The more I looked, the more I could see that it wasn’t only his height that was different but his face too. His eyes were bigger and his grin not quite as wide. This was not the same face that had haunted me for so long.  
  
 Just then he started opening his mouth. It was a move that appeared painful and foreign, like forcing one’s knees behind one’s back. He gargled out a sound that was like nails on a blackboard. He was trying to speak to me. It was just repeating this sound in short and garbled bursts. Eventually, it clicked. It was one word, over and over.  
  
 “R-r-ee-egg-g”  
  
 My whole body turned to ice, and my heart felt like it was about to fall through my chest. I stuttered out my own word in response, which was as difficult for me to get out as it was for him.  
  
 “… Jimmy?”  
  
He nodded. It was him, there could be no doubt. The brother I thought I lost was here, albeit in a very different form.  
  
 “Come here, Jimmy,” I said. The words escaped my lips at barely more than a whisper.  
  
 He inched closer, moving slowly. His movements were stiff and quivering, like he was not used to walking with these legs. I grabbed his arms to help him. With shaky determination, he latched onto me to steady himself. His mask remained fixed, although I could see behind his skewed smile. I could see the sadness that lay underneath.  
  
 I led him to the bed, helping him through his rickety shuffling, and we just sat together. After that we spoke for hours, or at least I did. Try as he might, he could not manage any more vocalisations that night, or ever after. The best he could do were pitiful croaks which seemed to hurt him greatly. I let him know it was okay. He didn’t need to talk. I just appreciated he was there with me and that he could listen.  
  
 He left after a few hours, just as suddenly as he had arrived. Perhaps he ran out of time or energy; I can’t say which. I blinked and he was gone. I felt the emptiness from earlier start to bubble up, but I suppressed it. I knew there was no more use in sadness that night. I just crawled up in bed, without even changing my clothes, and went to sleep.  
  
 The next few days passed with melancholy. It wasn’t the total despair I had felt when I first received that call, but it was impossible to be happy. My mind was reeling from the implications of seeing Jimmy again. Seeing him in such a state made me both extremely happy and very sad.  
  
 About a week later, I was laying in bed, struggling to fall asleep around the 3 AM mark. Mr Brightside. He had been on my mind constantly. If Jimmy could appear like that, then I knew what - or who - Mr Brightside really was. I had to see him again, to know what I was really dealing with. I got out of bed with a renewed feeling of determination. I had to know.  
  
I grabbed my phone and pulled up my music app. Then I began to pace. I was going to hit play and then everything would change. Or maybe it wouldn’t. Maybe he wouldn’t even show up. Maybe I had scared him away forever. Maybe even these were the paranoid delusions of a woman so desperate in her longing for her family that she made up silly characters with funny faces. Perhaps. But I knew none of those were true, not after all that I had seen. Finally, determinedly, I found the song, and I hit play.  
  
 As soon as the opening melody started up, a wave moved over me. It was a shiver that started at the top of my spine and ran all the way down to my toes. I looked around. Nothing. I ran to my dresser mirror and gazed into it intently. Even with my eyes scanning the room desperately, I could not see the shadow I had run from for so long but now wanted more than anything to see. Before I even knew what I was doing, a word fell from my lips.  
  
“Dad?” I asked the nothingness.  
  
 And then there he was, standing so close to me I could reach out and touch him. I felt his daunting presence tower over me, but for the first time I was not scared. I just turned up the song and pulled him in for a hug.  
  
 Nowadays, I feel much better. I’m not afraid of fear anymore. In fact, in some ways I look forward to it. Because that’s when I know they will both be there.
