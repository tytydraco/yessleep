It was on the occasion of my nineteenth birthday that I received a letter from my beloved, informing me that she would soon marry my brother.

 

There had been no promise between us, she explained. She had simply grown tired of waiting while I marched through Mississippi with the Union Army, eating hard crackers and dodging Confederate bullets. If I still possessed any likeness of her, she suggested I burn it.

 

So I did.

 

That evening, seated beside a campfire with my comrades, I fed her daguerreotype to the flames while they attempted to console me.

 

“There are other, brighter fish in the sea.”

 

“You’ll laugh about this one day.”

 

“At least she waited until you were away before marrying your brother.”

 

The next morning, we advanced against the Confederate positions.  Men fell beside me. Ohers disappeared in the midst of smoke. The air stank of powder and blood.

 

By late afternoon I had wandered farther than good sense allowed, driven less by courage then misery.

 

I found myself separated from my regiment and face-to-face with a Confederate soldier no older than myself. We crossed bayonets, and for a brief moment I wondered if he too had received a letter that began with affection and ended with betrayal. Then I killed him.

 

The struggle left me exhausted. I collapsed beside the boy and knew no more.

 

When I awakened, dusk had settled over the battlefield. The meadow was littered with bodies. Some lay where they had fallen. Others were stacked in rows. Smoke drifted low across the ground like fog.

 

In the distance a wagon approached, Confederate soldiers walking beside it, gathering their dead.

 

I had heard enough stories about Southern prison camps to fear capture more than death itself. So, I crawled to the boy I had killed. Working quickly, I stripped off my blue coat and his bloodstained gray jacket. Then I lay still.

 

The ruse succeeded.

 

The body collectors never questioned me.

 

Strong hands seized my wrists and ankles. “One, two, three,” I was tossed onto the wagon atop a mound of dead Confederates, where the weight of additional bodies soon buried me from sight.

 

Later, we were transferred to a train. Packed among the dead, I endured a nightmarish journey through the darkness, surrounded by men I had spent the day trying to kill.

 

At dawn we arrived in a small Southern town whose name I never learned. The dead were arranged along the main street while families came to identify their loved ones.

 

I knew of course that no one would come to claim me, and again decided to wait until an opportunity presented itself to make good my escape.

 

Finally, all the dead had been spoken for and carted away, and I alone was left lying in the street.

 

I was preparing to make my escape when a heard a woman’s scream. The sound cut through the morning like a knife.

 

I dared to open one eye. A young woman dressed entirely in black was running toward me. She threw herself beside my body and began sobbing so violently I feared she might faint.

 

I almost rose then and there. But before I could move, she turned to the remaining soldiers.

 

“Please,” she begged. “Please help me bring him home.”

 

Eager to aid the lady, the men agreed to transport me to her plantation home.

 

Once inside, they assisted in stripping me of my rather raggedy attire, and then, laid upon a long table, proceeded washing me. Freshly bathed, I was fitted into a gentleman’s suit and dapper waistcoat, unlike any I had ever worn before.

 

Throughout, I willed myself to lie perfectly still, knowing full well that I was doomed, that once the blood had been washed away, she would surely realize her mistake.

 

But it was not to be. My clean, fresh face only added to her grief.  The soldiers then lifted me into a casket left open for the many mourners who now arrived to pay their last respects.

 

I stared back unblinkingly, incredulous - the center of attention at a lavish wake! A beautiful young lady sobbing over me. So very much in love! So heartbroken!

 

It was all I could do not to sob myself.  When my time came, surely no one would attend my funeral, I thought to myself. And certainly, there would be no grieving young widow.

 

There were scented candles, which I was unable to see due to my playing dead, but their faint glow flickered behind my eyelids, their smoke gently teasing my nostrils, causing them to lightly twitch.

 

There came the faraway drone of an organ. The mourner’s voices lifted in sorrowful song, “The Vacant Chair,” one I knew from childhood, having been sung at my father’s funeral.

 

Once the mourners had gone, I was left alone with the lady, who placed some wood in the fireplace, stoking the embers until the flames grew fierce and hot.

 

Afterward, she sat beside my casket, confessing her undying love, and placed a heavy, silver framed photograph of herself in my seemingly lifeless hands.

 

Just we two, I could now make my move. I could bolt up, surely scare her half to death, and flee. Yet somehow, I was unable to. Revealing myself as an impostor, I would destroy the love she held for me. Her kind and loving words like a salve to my letter-wounded heart, it was impossible that I would interrupt her.  

 

But now, something else unexpected. As the fire blazed, the room grew hot, causing droplets of perspiration to dot my brow. And then, something worse. The droplets converged, forming a pool of sweat, and began to flow down the ridge of my nose, collecting in a fat drop at the tip, teetering there, still not dropping, but causing me a tickling sensation that would soon cause me to sneeze, ending the game, which was no kind of game to me.

 

The lady now rose, and turning to a great curving staircase, left me for the night.

 

Myself greatly relieved, the beaded sweat finally dropped. The lady had gone and I was free to sneakily take my leave.

 

But then, a voice from behind in the flickering dark.

 

“Dead men don’t sweat,” the widow said, her voice close by, as if to whisper in my ear.

 

And in the moment, I bolted up, about to meet the lady, to explain myself, when suddenly she slammed the coffin shut against my head, using such force that it cracked my skull.

 

I awakened sometime later in claustrophobic darkness. Lighting a match found in the Reb’s pocket, I suddenly remembered where I was, the dim image of the lady’s photograph staring back at me. I remained in the cursed casket. But surely the lady had meant me no harm, and must have been startled by my sudden movement. But why, oh why, was I still entombed?

 

Puzzling out a loud clump, followed by another, and another, I quickly came to a grim realization. The sounds were shovels of earth spilling onto the casket. I was being buried alive!

 

But there was more. Two voices, those of the negro gravediggers, speaking between themselves. Old men, they spoke loudly in order to hear one another.

 

“That woman be crazy, crazy,” said one.

 

“Shore enough crazy,” said the other.

 

“Must be the fourth husband she done buried this month,” said the first.

 

To my mounting horror, I now understood the lady to be obsessed, given to playing the role of grief-stricken widow, as I had play acted the role of the deceased, she laying claim to the unclaimed at the train station, rewarding the mourners, and paying for consignment to the grave.

 

Straining to listen as the diggers continued to shovel, I overheard that she had murdered her true husband in a jealous rage. But why? For once having been unfaithful.

 

Gutted, I cried out and, arms constricted at my side, hammered as best as I was able at the casket lid. But my heart shattered as I squinted out the tiny, clod covered glass window. There above I saw my love, my beautiful widow, placing flowers beside my slowly filling grave.      

 

Now, struggling to adjust my position in the tightly cramped space, I made her out, the veiled Angel of Death placing flowers at a second nearby grave, then a third, and a fourth, perhaps a dozen or more altogether, each marble tombstone bearing a last name that was not my own, and a carved inscription - BRAVE SOLDIER - FAITHFUL HUSBAND.

 

All these thoughts and more roared through my mind as my shrieks were swallowed in utter blackness.

 

I jerked awake, gasping, my blanket soaked with sweat.  Around me, men were already stirring in the darkness. Someone shouted that the Confederates were again approaching. The great battle we had been expecting for three days had finally come. 

 

Before first light, I borrowed paper from Corporal Evans and wrote everything down.

 

I prayed it was only a dream, but if any of you happen to know what it means to witness your death before it happens, I would very much like to hear from you.
