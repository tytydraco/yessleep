I was at an afterwork mixer not that long ago. Nothing big, just a gathering with some out-of-towners. We had a couple of drinks and got talking about the most unusual parts of our job. Nowadays I work in bookkeeping, but I’ve been doing evaluation and audits back in the day. At four drinks in, the conversation drifted to where it always goes; your *weirdest* job story.

Now, I told them this stupid anecdote about an old man who wanted to evaluate his vast collection of erotic art. It’s a funny story that never fails to get a laugh. But that’s not my weirdest job story. That story doesn’t do quite as well at parties.

But just this once, I’d like to tell it. I’d like to tell you about Flamingo.

 

Some time ago, I did evaluations for a firm specializing in liquidation of company assets. Basically, when something went bankrupt, we were called in to evaluate the worth of leftover inventory, assets, and whatever else was legally owned by the company in question. This is not exciting work; more often than not we got it all on paper on day one. We just had to make sure it was really there. Glorified fact-checkers.

It was a scorching hot summer. My allergies were acting up, and the news was flooded about this missing 10-year-old girl. She’d been gone for days, and the video of her family pleading to the public was on repeat. Not a lot happened in that one area of the world, so this would be the biggest story of the year by far.

I was practically melting through the floor when my boss dropped by my desk, fanning himself with a folder.

“You got the e-mail?” he asked.

“What? No.”

“Okay, good.”

He sat down next to me, dropping the folder on my desk.

“I was planning on sending you and Don on this, but Don has gotta take some time off.”

“I got it, it’s fine.”

“No, see, this is bigger than usual. I ain’t sure what to make of it.”

“What do you mean?”

“There’s a lot of questions here, and we don’t have a lot of answers. It’s not just gonna be checking some files. It’s a lot. That’s why I wanted to send Don too, but…”

He shrugged, giving me an apologetic smile. He tapped me on the shoulder and turned as he walked away.

“Just take your time with this one,” he said. “Do it right.”

 

Shortly after, I got an e-mail about a private company that was getting liquidated. It was a small privately owned tech company based out of South Dakota. The sole elderly owner had recently passed in a home invasion gone wrong, and the board had decided to liquidate the company in its entirety. We’d gotten a couple of years’ worth of documentation, but this company had been up and running since the 70’s. I was going to be standing literally neck deep in paperwork. No wonder my boss wanted Don to help out.

We got most of the paperwork sent to the office later that day. I had to sign off on box after box being dropped off at the door. I almost tore my back out carrying them all in, and the heat wasn’t helping. The boxes were immaculately labeled, but we were talking *decades* of work. Financing, projections, board meetings, budgeting, personnel files… it was all there.

By the time I had it all lined up, I had accidentally built myself a cubicle. I hadn’t played in a box fort for years, but this was a decent enough substitute. I didn’t know where to begin. Usually we got something to start with, but this was just dropped off and that was that. I had to start with the most recent reports and work my way back.

 

There wasn’t that much to be excited about. Those first few days I listed assets like a company car and a regional office. There was also a rented storage space. I found some receipts for office equipment and a couple of computers, but most of them were from the early 2000’s.

I think it was somewhere in the delivery receipts that I first noticed the word ‘Flamingo’. It snapped me back to attention for a moment. It’s not the kind of thing you expect to read in a budgeting report, but it was right there. Flamingo. It wasn’t a big cost, but big enough to be included in the yearly budget. No further explanation, no details – just ‘Flamingo’.

I put it aside for a while, but I noticed the word coming up every now and then as I looked through the files. Sometimes it’d show up in delivery receipts, other times in R&D or even storage. I could be reading about Microsoft Office subscriptions and then wham – *Flamingo*. It catches your attention. Especially when you have no idea what it means.

I was about one full week into mapping the company assets when my boss got a call. Apparently, while we were still working on processing inventory, someone had broken into the regional office. The police were asking for someone to come down and do inventory, asking if there was any way to know what’d been stolen. Someone had to go to South Dakota, and it wasn’t gonna be Don. He still had some time off.

 

I drove out on a Tuesday. Windows down, blasting cold air, having an iced tea in my cupholder. The radio was still going on and on about this missing girl, but the tone had turned somber. It was no longer about finding her alive; it was more about recovery and finding out what’d really happened to her. They were speculating, drawing parallels to other missing children, asking uncomfortable questions, and pointing fingers at those in power who hadn’t done enough. It was a nice distraction, even if the subject matter was morbid.

It was a long drive, but the one-story office building was easy to find. They’d taken down the signs, and it was the only parking space that was completely empty – save for a squad car. Two officers were ready to meet me up front. I had printed out a list of items that I suspected would be at the office according to the most recent receipts. Just the big stuff; I figured burglars wouldn’t be looking for staplers and reams of paper. If they just got away with some copiers and computers, we could probably get an insurance payout.

There were two officers out front. They waved me over and escorted me inside, keeping the small talk to a minimum. Just a couple of lines and a ‘how about this weather’. I couldn’t blame them. One of them was on the chubbier side, and he was melting through his uniform.

 

There was no doubt that the place had been robbed. The front door was smashed to pieces. I had to step over the glass as I made my way inside.

“You know what’s strange?” the chubby one said. “The door opens inward, but it’s been smashed the other way.”

“Why’s that strange?” I asked, glass crackling under my feet.

“I mean, if they could get in, they could get out. Why break the door? Hell, the lock’s still there, see?”

He used his flashlight for emphasis. I nodded at him, taking a closer look. He was right; the entire door had been smashed to pieces from inside the office. Torn straight from the hinges. And the lock was, seemingly, untouched.

 

The officers left me to do their job, asking me to call once I was finished so they could get a proper report about missing or damaged items. I was left on my own for a while as I went around taking pictures, double-checking with my list of confirmed assets.

Now, the place was admittedly a mess, but not as bad as one might imagine. The lights were working just fine, and most of the furniture was okay. A couple of things had been tipped over, and the drawers in one of the desks had been thrown to the floor, but all the electronics were still there. In fact, I couldn’t see that anything had been stolen. Not anything big, at least.

One thing stood out though. At the far end of the room there was a supply closet. The door was broken in two, and all the boxes inside were scattered on the floor. One of the boxes had been torn to shreds. An old, dust-caked box with a yellow note taped to the side.

‘Flamingo’.

 

After talking to the police, I decided to take some time to look closer at what exactly ‘Flamingo’ was supposed to be. There were a couple of notes here and there, but it was always separated by a degree of vagueness. For example, I noticed there were gas receipts about once per month when one of the employees went on a longer drive. That always coincided with a pattern of other receipts for things such as glycerin, antifreeze, and something described as “compound five, mix B”. Same pattern, over and over.

Now, I didn’t have a clear idea of what Flamingo was supposed to be, but there were some patterns emerging. It was something that had been ongoing since the 70’s, up to the point of the company’s liquidation. Some kind of ongoing thing that was only shut down once the company lost its funding. ‘Flamingo’ wasn’t that expensive, but it was definitely recurring.

There had to be something else to it. Some kind of documentation that showed what the hell we were dealing with. At the very least I needed some kind of description, so I could do my job. I can’t evaluate something if I don’t know what it is.

 

While in town, I decided I should check out the storage space. I had a pretty good idea of the kind of things they kept there, but I figured I might as well double-check. I’d had the keys delivered earlier that week, and it wasn’t a long drive. Might as well.

It was a private storage space as part of a larger warehouse location. Basically, a walled-off section lined with chain-linked fences. It was already late in the afternoon by the time I got there, but just getting out of the sun for a while was enough to energize me. I was drowsy from my antihistamines; something I kept chewing like candy to deal with my dust allergy. Did you know those things are basically sleeping pills?

Yawning, I got to the storage space. I unlocked the door and stepped inside. I was the only one there as I closed and locked the door behind me, turning on the lights.

 

The moment the light turned on, something *moved*.

I heard things getting knocked over and clattering to the floor as something crashed into the light overhead; breaking the glass and turning the room dark. My pulse exploded as I pressed myself back against the door, fumbling for the lock.

“I’m calling the police!” I yelled out. “You’re not supposed to be here! I’m calling the-“

I got the door open. As light flooded back into the room, I realized how quiet it was. I pulled up my phone, ready to call for help. As a couple of seconds stretched into a full minute, nothing happened. I started to doubt myself, thinking maybe something had just toppled over, or a raccoon was hiding inside. It would be silly to call for help over a wild animal, so I decided to take a closer look.

 

There was nothing in there apart from a strange black mark on the floor. There was this smell of ozone in the air, so I kept the door open to flush it out. The glass from the broken light littered the floor, along with a couple of open boxes. It didn’t surprise me to see that these were labeled ‘Flamingo’. A lot of other labeled boxes in that room were untouched.

Looking through the boxes, these seemed mostly untouched. There were tapes, camera equipment, folders, and a box of what looked like turkey basters made of stainless steel. All the boxes had been ripped open and looked through, but nothing had been removed. They were stuffed to the brim with a variety of items.

I picked one of the boxes at random, marked “Flamingo 1981”. It wasn’t a large, or even heavy box, but it was taped shut. I opened it, just to have a look.

First thing I saw was a children’s drawing, in crayon. It was kind of hard to tell what it was supposed to be, but it looked like a portrait. A child, two parents, and two doctors. On the bottom, a text.

‘tanks for snow gloab!!’

 

I loaded up my car with a couple more boxes and took some pictures of the more expensive items. I was surprised to see medical equipment; this was supposed to be a software company.

I returned to that crayon drawing a couple of times, taking a closer look. I couldn’t tell if the child was a boy or girl, they didn’t have any hair. They also had the outlines of their body drawn in a gray crayon, not a pink one like the parents or the doctors. The reference to a snow globe could be seen in the picture, as one of the doctor characters was holding one out like a present.

I decided to call it a night. I checked into a motel and closed the curtains. I’d have to wait to check the video tapes until I got back to the office; I didn’t want to plug anything into a motel TV. Instead I browsed whatever was left. Photos, reports, test results.

 

Once you got past the ‘Flamingo’ description, there were a couple of names that kept coming back. One of them was Christine, or Chrissie. It didn’t take a lot of digging to connect her to the crayon drawing. A lot of the reports were transcripts from her discussions with one of the doctors, as well as observations made by staff. Most of them were fairly short descriptions, like a kind of progress report. Something that was meant to be added to an ongoing file.

Other names were those of doctors and software developers. It seems they were using some kind of program to figure out a solution to a problem that affected Chrissie. Exactly what wasn’t clear, but they perfected something they called “compound five, mix B”. What exactly that entailed wasn’t clear. Something about seeds, an Echo Drive, and synthetic nutrition. There was a whole section written about the physical effects of consuming antifreeze related to this.

But what really stood out to me were the observations about Chrissie. She’d been part of ‘Flamingo’ since the 70’s, just after the founding of the company. Some of the observations about her were absurd.

“Day 465 – Still unable to enter REM-sleep”.

“Day 476 – Confirmed independent eye-movement and dual cognition, cross-report 8.”

“Day 877 – Procedural breach, found in parking lot(?)”

“Day 1022 – Tongue atrophy.”

 

The reports hinted at this girl in need of medical attention, but it wasn’t clear as to why. They were working on some kind of solution, and had been for a long time. Whatever it was, it resulted in some kind of compound that they had been producing until recently. Something that, even when written out, didn’t make sense to me. What the hell is a helianthus cerulea anyway?

It was almost 1 am when I turned off my lights and closed all the boxes. I crawled into bed, barely keeping my eyes open. I’ve never liked sleeping in unfamiliar places; there’s always noise that you’re not used to. Luckily, I was out like a light the moment my face hit the pillow.

In the morning, as I put on my jeans, I turned my head. My alarm didn’t go off. I rubbed the sleep out of my eyes, only to see that the boxes I’d checked were wide open.

I’m sure I closed them.

 

The boxes had a lot of old media, like cassettes and VHS tapes, but there were a couple of CDs that seemed a bit more recent. My car stereo was pretty old school, so I tried to put something on to listen to on the way back. It was gonna be a long drive, and I figured I might as well get acquainted with the material. Most of the CDs didn’t work, or had text files rather than audio, but a couple of them included what sounded like recorded conversations.

They all started the same. Reciting the date and the name of present staff members. Same names, over and over. They didn’t mention who they were talking to, but I had a sneaking suspicion. One of the recordings went something like this;

“Are you still experiencing issues with your vocal cords?”

There was a murmur in the background, then a clicking noise.

“I see. No effect from the inhaled sulfur?”

Two clicks.

“Sorry to hear that. Any other recent issues you’ve experienced in your new environment?”

There was one click. The person in the recording can be heard shuffling around with some equipment, only to spell out letters. A couple of things were brought to attention, such as ‘food’, ‘tired’ and finally – ‘broke’. That seemed to get the interviewer’s attention.

“Broke? What broke?” he asks.

They spell out the word ‘globe’.

“We’ll get you an extra one after your next session, how’s that?”

One click.

 

‘Flamingo’ seemed to be the collected name for all things related to Chrissie. Even later entries confirmed she was still around, alive, and active. Born in 1976, she’d come a long way from the crayon drawing she’d made in 1981. They still spoke about her like she was a child, promising toys and gadgets in exchange for cooperation and tests.

They never spelled anything out completely, all I learned about Chrissie came from context. The word ‘Flamingo’, for example. It seemed to come from some kind of digestion issue, keeping her from absorbing the kind of nutrients and vitamins a person needs to live. From the day she was born, she was given this “compound five, mix B” as a substitute. Whatever it was, it kept her alive. She’d be dead without it.

They would ask her really strange questions, like how well she could see with her eyes closed, or if she could open doors without using her hands. Or, in this one instance, if she was still experiencing growing pains in her arms and legs. She gave one click for yes.

They seemed to have a good relationship. They’d keep a good tone and even joke around. They sometimes talked about dosage and treatment, but it always had this tune of something pleasant. Almost like looking forward to a meal, or a spa day; it was something to be excited about.

And at the end of every session, they’d give her a snow globe – seemingly for no reason. I think she just liked them. She wanted one from each state.

 

I came back to the office late in the afternoon. Most of the others had already gone home. I’d got an e-mail about Don coming back to work in the morning, and we needed to get him up to date on our progress. I had to do some overtime to write up a summary, or I’d have him tailing me like a lost puppy all week.

I kept the lights on and barricaded myself in my makeshift box fort. Apart from the fluorescent lights, it was eerily quiet. I put the radio on just to have some noise in the air. I turned on my desk light and organized my papers, firing up my work computer as I settled in for a long night. I didn’t mind – it was paid overtime.

I checked out a couple of CDs that I couldn’t get to work in the car. More notes, audio snippets, records, and measurements. They never exclusively spelled out what they were measuring, but some of the numbers stood out.

380.2 pounds. 7 feet, 9 inches. 78.8 degrees Fahrenheit. Too dense to swim.

 

It was about 8 pm when I got up to have a coffee. I had my headphones on, listening to an audio recording as the machine sputtered. They were listing things they’d found in Chrissie’s room that hadn’t been there during their last checkup. For example, she’d gotten a hold of live piranhas which she kept in an aquarium. They were discussing how the hell she got them to begin with.

As I took a sip of coffee, I thought I heard something. Removing my headphones, I listened. I could hear the radio in the other room, but that was it. I called out.

“Hello?”

There was no response.

 

Thinking I was getting paranoid, I silently crept back to my workstation. There was nothing there, but I could’ve sworn I’d heard something. I lowered the volume on the radio and turned off my headphones, trying to pinpoint the noise I’d heard. It was strange enough just being there after hours – the place was usually littered with people talking over one another or tapping away at their keyboards. Now it was just me. Just to make sure, I double-checked the lock on the front door.

Getting back to my desk, I figured I ought to move the boxes a little. Right now I was literally boxed off, and I couldn’t see anything. Something could be standing right outside the door without me knowing. But as I moved the boxes a little, I got this chill down my neck. I kept thinking about that morning in the motel. I was sure I’d closed those boxes the night before.

Something wasn’t right.

I got back behind my desk and sat down. The moment I touched my keyboard, I heard a noise.

The front door, that I’d locked, was slowly opening.

 

I was about to look who it was, but something didn’t feel right. It wasn’t so much the door opening, but the way it’d opened. There was always that familiar click when you slot the key into it – I hadn’t heard that. I suppressed my instinct to call out and instead pulled my feet up off the floor, staying behind the boxes as still as I possibly could. Whoever came in hadn’t seen me yet.

The steps weren’t even. I could hear naked skin slap against the stone floor as someone limped inside. I heard some items getting knocked off desks as whatever was on the other side stumbled through the room.

It made this awkward gulping noise, like someone cold starting an engine. Finally, it coughed – and the lights went out. There was this cool wave of air rushing past me, sending the hairs on my neck straight up.

 

I watched as the box at the top of my would-be fort was effortlessly picked up. The box weighed at least 45 pounds, but it was taken down like it was nothing.

I was so quiet I forgot to breathe. I could see outlines in the dark. There was this shadow on the floor showing something tall and gangly. Every now and then it would make that uncomfortable gulping noise, then proceeded to opening another box.

It felt like hours, but it was probably just a couple of minutes. It was fast, as if looking for something very specific. It didn’t stop to read labels or reports.

Then I got a text message.

I think it was just Don checking if I had anything to send him to prep for the meeting the next morning, but I’d forgotten to put my phone on mute. This one loud ding echoed through the room, and I heard whatever was on the other side react accordingly.

 

It was such a sudden noise, and it was surprised. It stepped back, getting its foot snagged in one of the power cables. It pulled back too hard and dragged along my computer screen and keyboard, knocking over the box fort as it went. About a dozen boxes collapsed to the floor.

I was quick on my feet. The moment it all came down, I hid under my desk. I pressed my face against the ground. There was a small space between the desk and the tiled floor, giving me almost a full inch to view what was on the other side.

I didn’t see much. It was dark, and the only light I got were from the light posts outside. But I did get a clear view of this tall silhouette; almost like a stick figure. A far too small head for a far too long neck. No hair. Long arms with a strange number of fingers.

I couldn’t see it breathing. It was completely still.

 

It raised an arm, and I could see it shaking. It poked at one of the empty boxes, then looked closer at one that’d tipped over. It picked up something small and held it up, turning it over just inches from its face.

Then, something happened. Something flipped like a switch.

The radio burst into static, like the frequency shuffled on its own. The kitchen light came on and instantly popped. A flash of light revealed something gray with leather-like skin. I could hear the microwave running as I saw the shoulders on this thing rise and fall over and over.

It dropped whatever it held and tore the boxes apart. Years of documentation and reports thrown to the wind as it flailed its arms around. It stomped down so hard that one of the boxes popped like a balloon, spilling out folders and employee onboarding handbooks.

It put one of its legs on top of a desk and pressed down like a vice; breaking the cheap wooden furniture like a snapping twig. And as it raged and threw its weight around, sending boxes and desk lamps and screens flying across the room, it accidentally tipped my desk over; revealing me.

And it stopped.

 

Now, I can’t tell what I was looking at. Not exactly. I don’t recall seeing any eyes, or a mouth. But there was something strange about the space where a face ought to have been. I couldn’t see ears, or a chin, or a nose. But there was movement – a hundred little movements. Little things twisting and turning in the dark. A strange circular motion. A hole.

I don’t know why I figured it would attack me. Some animals stop moving when faced with a predator, as moving might trigger an instinct to chase. That’s what it felt like to me. I was curled up on the floor, looking up at something I didn’t understand.

So when it reached for me, I was ready. I ducked down, feeling two of its fingers brush against the top of my head, as I headed for the break room at full sprint.

 

The radio was going haywire. It had gone from playing a shock jockey, to police scanners, to static. There was a blip coming from the fridge as the electronics kept failing. I could see a light outside blinking through one of the windows, and I could hear a car alarm going off in the parking lot.

The emergency exit sign flickered as I ran towards it. Just as I was about to reach the handle, I took a hard right towards the bathroom. As I did, something slammed into the door behind me. I heard the hinges snap as the door flung open, triggering a deafening alarm.

I rushed into a bathroom stall, closed the door, and locked it. I covered my ears and curled up on the toilet, keeping my feet off the floor.

Seconds later, I see the latch on the door move up and to the right – and the bathroom door swung open effortlessly.

 

It’s just standing there, shielded by the dark. The crescendo of failing electronics reached higher and higher. It tilts its head slightly to the right, and suddenly - it’s all quiet. No alarms. Nothing.

It looks at me. It doesn’t move a muscle. It’s staring me down, as if looking for something in me. I’m plastered against the back of the stall, trying to make myself as small as possible; my head growing faint from lack of oxygen. I can’t breathe. Can’t think.

Without a sound, an arm reaches for me. Not menacingly, but determined.

I say the first thing that comes to mind. I don’t know why.

“Chrissie,” I say. “Chrissie, don’t.”

The arm stops.

“I don’t have anything,” I continue. “I don’t have what… you’re looking for.”

The arm falls down to its side, long enough to scrape the floor.

It makes a clicking noise, once, searching my face for the hint of a lie. I take rapid shallow breaths. It turns its head to the left, relaxing its shoulders.

Then it turns around, and leaves.

I watch as it walks out the back door. The moment it passes the threshold, the whole building comes alive. Alarms, radios, microwave, fridge, and every light came back in a wave of impressions that punched my senses dead. I blink as my eyes tear up, and I look outside.

But there’s nothing there. It’s just gone, with a faint smell of ozone.

 

I called the police, and my boss. The security cameras failed to capture anything. Most of the boxes that had been knocked over were fine, and even the reports seemed to be salvageable. There were only a couple of items that’d been broken. A couple of empty test tubes with age-yellowed labels, and what looked like a snow globe. Nothing fancy, just a little souvenir thing with a ‘Don’t mess with Texas’ label.

Just bad luck, I suppose.

It took us a few days before we got back to the office, leaving me some time to go through the last of the reports. It seemed the last of the compound five mixes had been delivered months ago, and that there were no more scheduled deliveries. If this was something Chrissie needed, she was running out – fast. I tried checking a couple of VHS tapes, but they’d been demagnetized.

But more so, I found something I’d been looking for. *A shipping address*.

 

I didn’t go alone. Not only did I bring Don, but we brought the police. It was company property, after all. The place was registered as part of an industrial warehouse, but it was sectioned off with layers of fences and barb wire. All the locks were long since rusted shut; we had to cut them open.

We followed two flights of stairs downward, leading to what looked like an abandoned bomb shelter. It took a lot of effort, but we got the door open. No one had touched it for ages.

The entire room was cylinder-shaped with a drain in the floor. There were no lights. We checked the walls with flashlights, sweeping over empty shelves and open drawers. Someone had been there recently, and they’d cleared the place out. I could tell the place had been packed.

There were hand-written labels taped to the walls. Some of them were simple enough. Labels for pictures, clothes, and tools. Then, a label for snow globes. Every single state labeled, but the shelves were empty. Someone had taken everything and just… left. Don took a closer look, sweeping over the labels with his flashlight.

“It’s missing Texas,” Don remarked. “How about that?”

The place was covered in dust, but a couple of spots were left clear. There’d been collections there until recently. On the far end of the wall, there were test tubes and turkey basters. Labels of C5-MB meticulously dated, going back at least a decade. It had stopped a couple of months prior.

 

The officers left as soon as the coast was clear. While Don did some long-overdue paperwork, I sat in my car and blasted the AC. I thought about that name, ‘Flamingo’. The Flamingo gets its pink color from the shrimp it eats; it’s a result of its diet and environment. Chrissie had never managed to digest the things we take for granted. She grew up on a completely different set of vitamins and minerals. Something they’d made up in a lab, back in the 70’s. Then again, if they hadn’t, she probably wouldn’t have survived infancy.

But what features of ours are inherently human? What is the human equivalent of the flamingo’s pink feathers? What had Chrissie sidestepped, and what had she gained in turn?

I couldn’t make sense of it. Instead, I turned on the radio. Maybe the shock jockeys could drown out my thoughts for a bit.

 

There was no music; all the local channels were reporting the same thing. They’d found the missing 10-year-old girl. They dubbed the rescue “an act of vigilante justice”. A deceased suspect was found on the premises, apparently killed in a brutal fashion. The girl had been released from a locked basement and reunited with her family. She had initially been taken on her way to show and tell, where she was going to show off a souvenir from a recent trip down south.

An unknown vigilante had broken into the house where she was kept. In the one sound bite they got from the girl, she spoke in a Texas accent. When asked to describe her rescuer, she only had this to say;

“That thing stole my snow globe.”

 

We finished our liquidation of the company and closed another case. Most boxes were filed away or destroyed, throwing decades of documentation down the shredders. I never got a clear view of Chrissie, and there were no answers as to what became of her. There was no recipe for the compound. Maybe she figured it out. I guess she had to, or else she’d have starved to death.

When people ask me about my craziest work story, this is the one I want to tell. I don’t know what to make of it, and the people with all the real answers are long since gone. It’s so much easier to tell stories about creepy old men than trying to explain the Flamingo, but I can honestly say I think about it every day.

I can’t help but to wonder. If we are stripped of the building blocks that make us what we are meant to be – what remains?
