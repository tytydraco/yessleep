The empty lot next to my apartment building had been wooded for as long as I knew it, so it surprised me to see a space being cleared through my kitchen window while I washed up. With buildings on two sides, the lot backed onto government land, an expanse of recreational forest used for hiking and mountain biking. I couldn’t see much in the lot, subtle movements, and the absence of larger trees I had befriended over the past year. The lot was a sizeable one, forested with second or third-growth trees, mostly birch, with ash and pine scattered throughout, that were probably ten metres at their highest. 

 

Clearing the land would ruin my view, and likely be followed by construction. I had lived near construction sites before, and it meant early morning of circular saws, impact drivers, hammers and hubbub. 

 

Knowing what was coming, in the coming weeks I kept my eye out the window, anticipating the eventual carnage—the scorched earth before the ten-thousand-square-foot house made exclusively for two individuals. Yet nothing happened. The initial harvest of trees remained as the only sign of change. 

 

When I passed by the lot on foot, I was surprised to see no deforestation along the road. There was a small foot path visible, and that was it. After twenty feet or so, the path turned a corner into darkness. I wanted to know what was happening to the property; the path invited me in, but I didn’t want to be caught trespassing.

I asked my neighbour, Chistian, across the hall if they had noticed anyone working on the property. “Nah,” he said, “is there something going on?”

 

“Do you know who owns it?”

 

The neighbour’s eyes narrowed. “Is there a way to find out?”

 

I researched what I could—internet searches that turned up nothing. Searching for more, I wound up at our local library, scanning microfiche. I found nothing. Since I was at the library, I decided to peruse the fiction for something to take home. 

 

“What you reading these days?” The words came from behind me. I turned to find a shrunken gentleman with gigantic, black-rimmed glasses, ragged jeans, and a patterned cardigan over top of a mud-coloured button-up. The man’s head bore fine wispy hairs; the corners of his mouth had two white dots of crusted saliva, and a thick book lay cradled under his arm. 

 

“Last book I read was a Richard Stark novel. Do you have any suggestions?” I said, choosing to engage.  

 

“I’m reading a book on the history of auto-making in America.” A man of his advanced age with a fascination of history had a high probability of knowing the who owned the lot. I decided to ask him, “Do you know anything about local real estate?” 

 

The man’s face lit up like a firefly on a warm summer night. “Oh, of course, I write about local history.” 

 

The man led me over to a section titled, ‘Local Authors,’ and showed me two books he had written. His name, Leonard Fawkes, was emblazoned on the covers. One book detailed the town’s gold rush era, and the other, ‘The Carlson,’ described a local hotel and its history. I admired them both before asking another question. 

 

“Do you know anything about the empty lot on Henry Street?” 

 

Leonard stepped back and his demeanour changed. “There is nothing but hurt that happened there.” He lowered his voice. “There is a story, but I think it’s best told while one walks.” He leaned in conspiratorially. “It is a grisly tale, and this is a family friendly space.”

 

Leonard’s short stature was evident as we exited the library. The stairs were difficult for him to traverse without a rail. Once on solid ground though, he maintained a pace that was difficult to match.

 

“The family that owned that plot, well, they were disliked folk; massive landowners and landlords. Their relatives still exist here, as does their name. Probably the reason why people don’t talk much about what happened there. That land was owned by Hudson Baxter, who owned a number of lumber mills around town. He was found using them for nefarious purposes after hours, milling men instead of logs.”

 

Electric arcs flew down my spine. 

 

“Seriously?”

 

“Unfortunately. The last person he murdered was a young man named Christopher Moore, the son of the mayor at the time, Thomas Moore. A mob hunted Hudson down and found him at his property on Henry Street. They killed him, then burned his house down with him in it.” 

 

“Do you know who owns the property?”

 

Leonard stopped and gazed deeply into my eyes. 

 

“Son, there is a history that most don’t talk about here, especially given the Moore’s bloody past. His family established themselves here by killing most of the Indigenous peoples who originally lived here and driving the rest away. 

 

“I believe the psychology of those murders was passed down through generations, and in resulted in the violent nature of Hudson Baxter. I believe, and don’t you go telling anyone, that barbarity exists in his progeny.”

 

“Leonard, thank you so much for the story.” 

 

He grinned through square dentures. “Glad to help. I hope I’ll see you at the library soon.” Then he strode away. 

 

That night, I walked over to the property. The plot didn’t seem the same now knowing what had transpired here. Death. Murder. Torture. 

 

The path into the property enticed me onto it, and I found myself swallowed by the thin trees, suddenly amid a mild wilderness. This trail must have been used by locals to pass through the lot onto the larger trail system on the other side. I wasn’t trespassing if everyone did it. 

 

The impulse that drove me forward was seeing the location of the murders. Why? I don’t know. Was it to piss on the remains of depraved and dance on their ashes, or just to be adjacent to an evil location? Either way, I wouldn’t be the first to do it. 

 

My stomach bubbled, agitated, and I blundered through the lot. My mind conjured images of skin being peeled from muscle, and I shivered, repulsed, but it drove me onward. 

 

Soon, the light became dappled as path narrowed and became overgrown. The past seemed to have disappeared from this place, its memory overrun by the present. The longer I walked, the tighter the trees crowded, with the trail becoming dense to where I was searching for gaps to walk through; this was more a deer trail now than a human one, and it was getting too narrow even for that. The further I walked, the less certain of where I was. 

 

After roughly half a kilometre, I stopped. The lot hadn’t seemed that sizeable from the street. It only ran the length of my apartment building. Had I managed to work my way onto public land without seeing the fences surrounding the property? There were no signs of fences, no sign of a trail, or of anything having walked through this land recently. 

 

The sun disappeared behind a cloud, and I slowed, ensuring that I walked straight for easier backtracking. The burned house was close. A few more steps and I would uncover it. 

 

I stumbled and fell. The ground beneath me was dappled with dark and light grey, and as my hand pushed into it, it flaked like ash. This was it, I thought, this was the remains of Baxter’s home—the place where it happened, and as I pushed to lift myself up, the ground crumbled beneath me. 

 

My outstretched arm snapped as it connected to the earth, sending pain lancing through it, my shoulder and torso. I recovered my bearings as I blinked through tears. 

 

The darkness around was solid—there was the smallest point of light from up the fissure I had made that allowed for the faintest of outlines. Lifting my uninjured hand, I could see its murky grey silhouette. I made out a gently sloping wall nearby. 

 

From the diffusion of sound, I could tell that this was a massive hollow space. A noise echoed within it—what I can only describe as the sound of a cow chewing cud. I remembered it from my uncle’s farm as a kid. It was a calming sound, one that conjured images of a Jersey calmly working its way through a wad of hay. I tried to figure out where it was coming from, but the acoustics of the room echoed all around me, trying to find their way into me. 

 

By feel, the surface beneath me was the same ash-like substance that I had tumbled through. I dragged myself up with a groan, my injured arm hanging lifelessly. This space felt wrong—as though it had swallowed me purposefully and a wave of stomach acid might flood in to drink my flesh and eat my bones. 

 

That thought surprised me. It wasn’t a normal idea, but one that invaded my thoughts because this was a cursed place; the thought was contaminated by the feral psychopathy of this cave. 

 

Struggling to remain calm, I hunted for my phone and found that it had survived the fall. The phone’s flashlight was negligible, but it helped with my growing anxiety. Walking forward, I found the edge of the room; the wall curved upwards shaping into a cone. Within the wall I discovered hexagonal alcoves covered with a translucent membrane. 

 

I stepped backwards, away from them. It reminded me of a wasp’s nest, but at a scale beyond my comprehension. How did this place relate to Hudson Baxter? The correlation made little sense, but I couldn’t shake my unconscious intuitions. I didn’t want to stay here, but there wasn’t an obvious exit, and climbing with a broken arm was impossible. I approached the wall to inspect the hexagonal cavities. There were shadows moving within them, shapes writhing, something active. I considered tearing one open but stopped, frightened. My mind conjured up larvae in pupal stage—with razored teeth—something hungry. 

 

Were these hexagons filled with the souls of Hudon’s victims? Was Christopher Moore writhing just behind the thin skin? The thought pushed me away from the wall, and into the center of the darkened void. 

 

The noise, the chewing, advanced closer, but the scarce light, paired with my ineffectual flashlight disclosed nothing. My breath came in spurts, and my arm buzzed with agony. Something was in this enclosed cavity with me. The chewing sound was close, possibly a hatched grub was devouring something that had fallen into this hell, sucking the meat from a rabbit’s ribcage or scouring a deer’s skull clean of flesh.

 

There had to be a way out of here. 

 

In the darkness ahead, I noticed movement. I inched closer and shone my phone in the shape’s direction. Two feet away, facing a section of wall, something stood with its spine bent. The partial light lit it so I could see a triangular head with large mandibles clicking together. At the top of its face, on either side, shimmered two oversized insect eyes. 

 

“Hello?” 

 

It bent inward and moved toward me, its insect eyes searching for me.

 

This place was a natural obscenity. I backed away, turned off the flashlight, and descended into the grey half-light. Eventually, I hit the wall again and lit the flashlight. The wall’s hexagonals flourished with shadowed obscurities. 

 

I counted the chambers as I walked. They were packed fifty high and surrounded the entire cone. I estimated a thousand cavities fit around the base of the hive. 

 

In the darkness, I heard it walking, the insect man. It was chittering, smashing its mandibles together, searching for me. 

 

Soon, I discovered empty hollows with phosphorescent liquid that smelled like paint thinner leaking from them. The, whatever was in there, had broken free and was in the hive with me. I made out vague iridescent shapes crawling through the darkness. 

 

More hexagons burst nearby. From here I could see them, white worms with the faces of a child, with dozens of segmented limbs with small hands at the end of each. There barely opened eyes searched the space. 

 

I pushed my back against the sidewall and held my breath. The chittering of the man-sized insect caused the worm children to be drawn towards it. If I stared into the darkness, I saw dozens of them mount the creature’s body and drag it down under their mass. 

 

Behind me, I felt a ledge, a small one. Looking back, I saw a path, slick with the wet glowing liquid from creatures who had climbed it before me. I mounted the ledge and trusted its crumbling ash to hold my weight. I wound upward and observed the ground begin writhing with creatures. They crawled on their stomachs with newborn appendages attached to their sides—my mind spun to watch them. At first there were dozens, and then soon there were hundreds. They crawled over and among one another in turmoil. The thick chemical scent grew as I climbed, burning my nostrils, and I became dizzy. My feet wobbled; I nearly slipped into the mass below, but pulled back, clinging to the wall behind me, pushing my feet into the soft ash beneath me. 

 

The summit arrived in a confused haze, and I climbed through an opening chewed by one of the creatures, birthing myself onto the ashen surface outside. Above me were white oak, green leaves, and blue sky; the absurdity remained ricochetting through my skull. 

 

In the clearing that surrounded me was a scorched earth where a house had burned, the ashen remains of a historical evil. Evil had made a nest below me and had birthed something that would swallow us all. 

 

I searched for the exit—wanting to block it—but it had disappeared. 

 

Was I mad? Had this place tried to swallow me, only to spit me out insane?

 

The forest loomed large, and I knew this place would devour me given the chance. This was an evil that couldn’t be raised with a canister of gas and matches. This land needed to be purified. I gathered myself together and pushed into the green trees that reeked of rotting pine. As I reached the edge of the clearing, a scent stopped me, the scent of paint thinner hung faintly in the air. I followed a thin trail of slime out of the forest, to the streets.

 

Walking down the street towards me was Christian, my neighbour. I nearly waved at him, but my unconscious stopped me. There was something off. The look on his face was vapid, his mouth hung open, slack, his tongue nearly lolling from his skull. I paused to watch him stumble through the street. This had to be the result of the escapee, the worm, and perhaps underneath this ground there were more of them—writhing, hungry, and filled with the evil of what had been done here. I wondered if similar hives were hatching at this very moment, around the country, heck, the world, spreading whatever had made Christian into this mindless thing. 

 

It was then I saw myself in the window of a car, and noticed my open mouth, and my arms hanging slack at my sides. I glanced away, fear streaking through my backbone, saw the trees in the lot were tumbling, and the ground writhed with the movement of worms, arbiters of the land’s hidden hatred.
