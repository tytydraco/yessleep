I need to put this down before I convince myself none of it happened. I have the screenshots. I have the photo. And I have questions that no one can answer.

# The Book

I buy used books from a bookstore in Portland almost every week. It's a habit. I like the margins, the dog-eared pages, the evidence that someone else lived inside the same words before me. Two Thursdays ago I picked up a battered copy of *The Stranger* by Camus. Four bucks from the discount section.

That night, I couldn't sleep. I'd been in one of those stretches where 3 AM feels like a permanent address. I opened the book and started reading, and on page 47, in the margin, someone had written a URL in small, careful handwriting. Blue ink. Block letters.

It was a .onion address,  the kind that only resolves in a Tor browser, a long string of random characters that shouldn't mean anything to anyone who didn't already know it was there.

Below it, in the same hand: *Please. Someone find this.*

I stared at it for a while. The handwriting looked recent. The ink hadn't faded.

I have the Tor browser installed. Don't ask why. I typed the address at 3:12 AM.

The page loaded slowly. Black background, no branding, no text. Just a video feed that took almost a minute to buffer. When it connected, I was looking at a basement.

Concrete walls. Exposed pipes across a low ceiling. A single bare bulb casting yellowish light that didn't reach the corners. In the center of the frame, a wooden chair. Someone was sitting in it, facing away from the camera.

They weren't moving.

I watched for two full minutes. The person wore a dark hoodie, hood up, head slightly bowed. The video quality was grainy, washed out, like an old security camera. There was no sound.

I almost closed the tab. Then a chat box I hadn't noticed appeared on the right side of the screen, and text materialized:

>

My skin went cold. I typed back:

>

The figure on screen tilted their head slightly. Like they were reading.

>

A long pause. The figure on screen pressed their hands against the sides of their head.

>

I should have closed the laptop. Instead:

>

The stream cut to black.

# Night Two

I spent the next day in a fog. Couldn't focus on work. Kept pulling up the address on my phone, but it showed nothing during the day. Just a connection timeout error.

At 3 AM, I opened it again. The feed connected. Same basement. Same chair. Same figure.

>

We talked for three hours. I learned fragments. Alex loved 90s alternative rock. Had strong opinions about Radiohead, overrated after *OK Computer*, full stop. Could quote entire passages from books but couldn't remember where they'd read them. When I asked personal questions, addresses, family, what they did for work, the answers came slow and broken.

>

So I made a cup at 4 AM, sat at my desk, and tried. The bitterness first, then the warmth spreading through my chest, the way the smell fills a kitchen.

>

Then:

>

That sentence sat in my stomach like a stone.

# Night Three

I woke up early and watched the sunrise from my apartment window. I described everything to Alex that night in painstaking detail. The pink bleeding into orange. The way the clouds caught fire at the edges. How the light hit the river and turned it into something molten.

Alex asked me to describe rain on windows. What it felt like to be hugged by someone you loved. The smell of a bookstore. The weight of a cat sleeping on your chest.

Each request felt like someone reaching through a wall to touch something they could no longer hold.

>

A pause.

>

A long silence. The figure on screen stood up for the first time in three nights. They walked to the edge of the frame and stood facing the wall, one hand pressed flat against the concrete.

>

# The Address

I drove to the building in Southeast Portland the next morning. My hands were shaking. It was an older apartment complex, painted the kind of green that looked cheerful twenty years ago and tired now.

I stood outside the door marked 2B and knocked.

No answer.

I found the building manager. An older woman with reading glasses pushed up on her forehead. I told her I was a friend of the tenant in 2B, that I'd been trying to reach them for weeks, that I'd borrowed something and needed to return it. The lie came out smooth. I'm not proud of that.

Her face changed. Not suspicion. Something sadder.

"The person in 2B passed away," she said quietly. "Alex Reeves. They found them in the apartment. Police said it looked like they'd been gone for a while before anyone noticed." She looked at me carefully. "How did you know Alex?"

"We worked together. Same field." And then, because something made me ask: "Did Alex like books?"

"Oh, yes. A real reader. Always coming home with bags from the bookstore." She paused. "It's a shame. Young person. Thirty-one. Kept to themselves."

"How long had they" I couldn't finish the sentence.

"The coroner said approximately twenty to twenty-two days."

I did the math in my head. I'd found the book sixteen days ago. Alex had been dead for at least four days before I ever opened that stream.

Every conversation. Every sunrise I described. Every sensation I tried to put into words for someone who couldn't remember what coffee tasted like.

I'd been talking to someone who was already gone.

I walked back to my car and sat there for forty minutes with the engine off, staring at the building, attempting to force the world to make sense again.

When I got home, I searched the obituaries. Alex Reeves. 31. Found deceased in home. No suspicious circumstances. A graphic designer. Freelance.

Same age as me. Same job as me. Same neighborhood.

There was a photo. The standard kind, a headshot someone had uploaded from what looked like a professional portfolio. I stared at it for a long time. I kept expecting to recognize the face somehow, the way you recognize someone you've dreamed about. I didn't. But I kept staring. Something about the way they held themselves in the photo. The slope of the shoulders.

I closed the laptop. I didn't know what I was looking for.

# The Photo

That night at 3 AM, I typed in the address. The feed connected, but the Waiting Room was different. The light was dimmer. The chair was empty.

Alex was gone.

I sat there watching an empty basement for over an hour, typing messages into the chat that no one answered. Around 4:30 AM, the feed began to degrade. The image stuttered, froze, stuttered again. Static crept in from the edges of the frame.

In the static, so faint I almost missed it, I heard something. A man's voice, warm and distant, reading a weather forecast. High of 58, partly cloudy, chance of evening rain. And then a frequency ident: *"You're listening to 103.3, Portland's Classic Hits."*

The same signal Alex had described hearing in the silence.

Then the stream died. The browser showed a connection error. I've tried the address every night since. Nothing loads.

But before the feed cut out, the final frame froze on screen for exactly three seconds. I screenshotted it on instinct.

I didn't look at the screenshot until the next morning.

The Waiting Room. The empty chair. The concrete walls and bare bulb and exposed pipes. Everything I'd been staring at for three weeks.

But in the far corner of the room, half-hidden in shadow, there was a second figure. Standing against the wall. Arms at their sides. Watching the empty chair.

They were wearing a dark hoodie with a basketball team logo on it.

The same hoodie I'd been wearing the night I found the book.

I zoomed in. The resolution was garbage, the face nothing more than a blur of pixels. But the posture was mine. The height was mine. The hoodie was mine.

I sat with that image for a long time. I didn't look away. I didn't try to explain it.

I just looked.

Because here is the thing I cannot stop thinking about: I wore that hoodie the night I found the book, sixteen days ago. Not the night I took the screenshot. The night I *started*. Whatever is standing in that corner, it is not a version of me from the last moment of the stream. It is a version of me from the first.

Something in that room knew I was coming before I arrived.

# The Bookstore

A week later, I went back to the store and found the section where *The Stranger* is usually shelved. I needed to see the book again. I needed to hold the proof.

There was a copy on the shelf. Battered. Same faded cover. Same four-dollar sticker.

I opened it to page 47.

The margins were empty. No URL. No blue ink. No plea for someone to find anything. Just the printed text of the novel, ending halfway down the page with the line: *I opened myself to the gentle indifference of the world.*

I checked every page. I checked the inside cover, the back matter, the spaces between chapters.

The book was clean.

I stood in the aisle for a long time. I thought about 103.3 FM, so I looked it up on my phone. The station is real. It's been broadcasting continuously for thirty years, no interruptions on record, no gap in programming that would explain why Alex could hear it from a room with no windows and no door.

And then I found the archived weather forecast for the night the stream died. High of 58. Partly cloudy. Chance of evening rain.

It matched exactly.

I'm not sure what that means. A real signal reaching somewhere that shouldn't receive anything. Maybe it means the Waiting Room was adjacent to this world somehow, close enough to catch a stray broadcast. Or maybe it means something worse, that the room was *here*, tucked inside the same city where Alex lived and died, just folded into a frequency no one can find.

I don't know what the Waiting Room was. I don't know if Alex found peace or just stopped existing. I don't know why a version of me was standing in the shadows of a room I never visited, at a time I had not yet arrived, watching an empty chair like something that had always been there.

But I know that for three weeks, someone who died alone got to feel less alone. And that the last thing I ever said to them was a description of the sunrise.

The margins of that book are blank now.

I keep wondering if they'll stay that way.

*If you found a URL somewhere you shouldn't have and you're reading this trying to decide whether to open it: I'm not going to tell you not to. I think Alex needed someone to find them. I think the Waiting Room was built from that need, from the specific weight of dying alone in a city of a million people, and that it pulled toward whoever might stay long enough to listen.*

*But I've been thinking about that figure in the corner. The version of me that was already there.*

*And I've been thinking about what Alex said on the first night:*

*Usually they leave after a few seconds. Just me. For so long.*

*Which means I'm not the first person who found the book.*

*It just means I was the first person who stayed.*
