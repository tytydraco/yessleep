The soft play area was, by design, a magical place. In my youth, McDonald's and the other fast food chains seemed a lot more saturated and joyous. This was until it became glaringly obvious that it wasn't just children who were ignorant to nutrition, and they collectively received a sterile makeover. In the 1950s, snack kiosques were faced with a terrible conundrum. People were too afraid to go back for seconds, as it made them feel piggish. So, cinemas across the contiguous United States adopted the practice of offering larger portions. This way, more money could be milked from the hungry consumer, who in turn feels less self conscious about their gluttony. Much to Behemah's delight, this practice soon spread far and wide. Today, you can supersize your already corpulent meals for pennies to the dollar. I first heard of this when I was a small child, and begged my mother to bring me to the nearest off-brand McDonald's. A wage-weary single parent, she needed rest as much as I needed sugar and fat, and so she finally relented to my car-seat kicking. 

She pulled the ‘95 Camry into the drab parking lot outside our town's one splash of color. I leapt from my booster seat, and my mother struggled to keep up with me as I ran to the front door of the fast food joint. Inside, I was greeted by everything a six year old boy could ever want, as well as the smell of grease and sweat. Every surface was a rainbow vomit of color, and sticky to the touch. Plastic nightmares of commercial mascots littered the place, making it seem more crowded than it actually was. Half of the building was occupied by padded seats and messy tables, with the other half having succumbed to a massive tangle of slides and climbing beams. It was the sacred play area. I had heard talk of it since kindergarten, but never thought I'd see it first hand. My hunger quickly faded as the new apple of my eye became the play place. Still, I followed my mother to the counter and ordered a large kids meal. It arrived at our table, according to my childlike perception of time, in seconds. My mother sipped tentatively at her coffee as I wolfed my creep feed. Soon, every scrap of processed potato and beef was gone, and my mother reluctantly let me loose in the play place. 

It was a jungle of oddly smelling padded beams and rope mesh, and I felt similarly to an early explorer venturing into the Amazon. 
I felt a buzz as I kicked off my shoes and slid them into the nearby box rack. Soon after, I fell to my hands and knees and hurried through the first tunnel. After passing the threshold, my memories become a blur. The exact layout of the play place is a mystery to me still, as it was back then. As a child, it gives you a strange sense of independence. Your entire life, all you've known is the watchful eye of an adult. Be it your guardian at home, or your teachers at school. This was, like for many children, my first experience of evading either. It felt like I'd slipped into another dimension, one where children populate a technicolor labyrinth and parents are a myth. 

I crawled through the plastic tunnels, high on the thrill of autonomy. Half a dozen other children hurried around, above and below me. I saw their shapes through gaps in the padded beams, and behind the mesh and ropes. I made a mental note of where I came from, fearful of getting lost never to be found. It dawned on me that no adult would fit through these tightest squeezes, and I didn't trust my fellow hatchlings to carry out their duties. I descended through square foam rungs, each missing an alternating quarter. Everywhere had the funk of socks and encrusted puke in difficult to clean areas. I carried on, however, right to the bowels of the beast. That's what the play place felt like at times. It was as if I'd swapped realities with the burger and fries I'd just devoured, and was now being digested. After climbing down another tunnel, I reached the stomach. Suddenly, an ocean of brightly colored plastic spheres appeared before me. Like impatient Death waiting on an indecisive suicidal, it begged me to jump. And I did. I leapt from the mouth of the tube and submerged myself in the ball pit. 

“Hey, this is our spot!” A nasally voice whined. 

Once I resurfaced, I looked around for its source. Two kids huddled in the corner of the pit, stacking balls into their upturned shirts, intent on bringing some home. Their black, greasy hair matched, as did the rest of them. They were twins. 

“Can't we share?” I replied. 

The two horrid kids thought in silence, before giving their final judgement.

“No!”

But they couldn't make me leave. Even when they started throwing balls at me, I relished in the volley. After submerging myself in the pit, and reappearing in the opposing corner, I took up arms and started throwing balls back. A war soon erupted, but quickly faded as two more explorers of the play place arrived via slide. Pigtails and skirt, I realized in shock. It was an affront, an injustice. If I didn't let girls in my tree house, I certainly wouldn't have them anywhere near my ball pit. Yet I remained too terrified to speak my truths, and became a package of meekness. The other two had the same such reaction, but were more vocal. 

“Ew, get out!” they said, almost in unison, “this is our spot!”

“Shut up Randy!” Pigtails screamed.

Acquaintances?

“That's a bad word, yuck-face!” Randy, or possibly his brother, retorted. 

This tête-à-tête continued for some time. I noticed Skirt, the younger of the two girls, cowering behind Pigtails. Her eyes were wide, wider than I'd ever seen, and I decided to wade across to her. 

“Want to be friends?” I asked, sucking up my antagonising attitude. 

She shook her head rapidly. Well, that was that. I turned to wade back to my spot, only to find someone new occupying it. Another boy, a little older and a little weaker, who'd arrived from the same direction I had. The ball pit was getting crowded, and the new kid realized. For that reason, he sat silently back on the rim of the tunnel and watched the scene play out in front of him. The conflict between the twins and Pigtails was getting heated, and Skirt had overcome her fear and jumped in to defend her friend. A glanced over my shoulder at the new kid, who looked at me then looked away. We both went back to watching the fight. It reminded me of the stuff my mom would watch on TV during her wine hour. Waves of plastic balls crashed with every movement, as did the ones that'd be repurposed into ammo. It was fun to watch for a while, but my gaze soon began to drift. Rarely did a ball-pit ever come stocked with a black variant, which is why the sudden flash of the color was so striking. I stared long and hard at where I thought I'd seen the darkness, until it became visible again. I locked onto it, but it took me a while to realise what it was. When I did, I screamed, silencing the conflict around me. It was a person, or something just less. I could only see the top half of their face, that peered at me half emerged from the ball-pit, occupying the one childless corner. At the sound of my scream, it dipped back under the surface. 

“What's wrong?” Pigtails asked, genuinely concerned. 

I mouthed a response, but no real words followed. Had I really seen what I thought I saw? The answer, quite quickly, was proven to be ‘yes’. After one short minute of trying to explain myself, the thing emerged fully from beneath the ocean-deep mass of colored balls. 

It had the shape of a man, or something adjacent. Its body was moist and pitch black, like how a tar road sweats on a hot day. Encrusted into its flesh were various lost items, from friendship bracelets to food scraps to socks. All were coated in a dark black-green slime, and were slowly being dissolved. Its nose was long and pointed, and its grin was doubly wide. The thing's sunken eyes were its most human feature. Milk white without color, they still managed to exude emotion. As I stared, a strange comfort washed over me. Judging from the reactions, or the lack thereof, from the people around me, I could tell they were under the same affect. The new guest kept rising from the pit of balls until the papier-mâchè crown on his head dinked against the foam ceiling. He towered over us and bent forward, blotting out the cracks of artificial light like a humanoid eclipse. Once he extended fully, he smiled and spoke. 

“Well what do we have here?” The thing said, extending his left arm. 

He ran his hand through one of the twins’ hair, leaving a sticky mess behind. We looked at him in awe, mostly. I was so fixated that I didn't notice the older boy behind me crawl off back down the tunnel in fright. The thing spoke again, after a while of silence while his eyes flitted between us. 

“I am the Ball Pit King. What are your names?” He asked, his smile growing wider for a micro instant before returning back to its former, still unnatural, width. 

His movements were janky, like every few frames were missing. Still, we collectively ignored the obvious threat to life and limb and went around the horn, introducing ourselves. 

“Randy,” said one of the twins. 

“Andy,” said the other. Naturally. 

“Clare,” said Pigtails. 

“Lillian,” said Skirt, her voice breaking slightly as she did.

“Josh,” I said, lying. I wasn't sure if the others had too. 

“Well, it is lovely to meetcha!” The Ball Pit King said. 

His voice was whiny and breathless, and he panted heavily between bursts of speech. He reminded me of this kid at school who had to wear a necklace with an inhaler dangling on the end of it. For a moment, it seemed he was just here to watch us play. Then he noticed Randy, who still had half a dozen plastic balls bunched up in his folded t-shirt. The Ball Pit King locked his eyes onto the child, and began to tut. 

“You should know you're not allowed to take those home with you. You do know that, don't you?” 

“Yes,” Randy stuttered out after a few failed attempts. 

“Drop them,” the King said, his voice now coated in the authority of an adult. 

Randy did just that, letting them tumble back into the pit. The King smiled, and spoke with his previous whimsical tone. 

“Good boy,” he said, his hand of rot distending to cup Randy's cheek.

“It'd be selfish of me to hoard all these beautiful balls while all you kiddies went without, wouldn't it?” The Ball Pit King asked. 

We were all too afraid to answer verbally, but Clare managed a reluctant nod.

“Well then, let's see what we can do about that,” the man of sludge said. 

Suddenly, his chest convulsed. It looked like an inflamed Adam's apple had dislocated, and started rising up his throat. The swell moved slowly upwards with a slick squeak, until it entered the King's mouth with a wet pop. He pursed his lips, then opened wide. A blue ball appeared between his teeth. He reached in and plucked it out, a long string of slime trailing behind it. He handed the black stained ball to Clare. She dropped it immediately, forcing the Ball Pit King to give her this look. It reminded me of the look my mom's special friend had given me when I came home from school a few months ago. A look that was stuck in my mind from how much it frightened me. Hate. Clare seemed to physically get smaller when she met those sunken eyes, and shrank down to pick up the ball. One by one, the King choked up another plastic ball, then another again, until we'd all received his gift. Mine was moist, and warm to the touch. It was also green, my favourite color. 

Then the Ball Pit King turned to Randy. He was the only one of us yet to receive a gift, although he didn't seem too bothered by that fact. 

“Now how could I forget little Randy,” the fetid man said, sliding his finger down the boy's button nose and leaving a snail trail of grime. 

His gullet began to splutter once more. It sounded more pained this time, like biological engine trouble. The ball tore up the King's throat, leaving sagging ‘skin’ where the bulge had been. What before was a wet pop now sounded more like calcified pus tearing out of a fistula. Slowly, the Ball Pit King's smile grew wider, almost bisecting his head. I shook with fear as he opened his mouth wide, revealing the plastic ball perched on his tongue, hemmed in by a picket fence of seventy human teeth. Some of them, I noticed, were milk teeth. It looked like a brightly saturated red pearl found within a dead clam. 

“Take… it” The Ball Pit King said with great difficulty. 

Snot ran down Randy's nose, accompanied by tears from his eyes. Down south, piss dribbled from within the hem of his khaki shorts. The Ball Pit King's mouth was nearing a ninety degree angle, to the point where he was staring at the ceiling without needing to move his neck. Although he couldn't see Randy, the terrified boy acted as if the King's eyes were trepanning his skull. Slowly, his fragile arm reached forward to grab the plastic ball, intent on ending this childhood-scarring altercation as quickly as possible. Black, viscous saliva dripped from the roof of the Ball Pit King's mouth, and from the rows of sharp, hadal-like teeth that had been concealed behind their human counterparts. Randy's hand caressed the plastic orb, and attempted to pluck it from the fleshy pedestal. Throat meat clung to the ball, keeping it in place. Randy tugged harder, and finally popped it from the socket it had grown out of. That's when the King struck. 

His mouth closed like a bear trap on a rabbit. Randy's shoulder splintered as his arm detached from the rest of him. Now severed, the boy fell backwards, becoming half submerged in plastic balls. His brother screamed, as did the two girls, who all rushed past me and into the tunnel. The Ball Pit King's throat distended again as he swallowed. Crying, I watched as Randy was dragged down, disappearing beneath the multicolored surface, likely by one of the King's subjects. I clambered into the yellow tunnel then, looking back to see the Ball Pit King begin to sink alongside the child. The top half of his head was the only part now visible, with sunken white eyes that fixated on me. 

The dreamworld of the playplace became a nightmare hellscape of tunnels, foam and rope, as the three of us made our way out. Every turn, I expected to see the King again, but was greeted only with the legs of the kid crawling frantically in front of me. The four of us emerged, finding the older child had stopped attempting to drag a crowd of adults to the play area. We ran crying to our parents, and my mother hurried me to the car before any mention of the Ball Pit King could be uttered through our tears. The police eventually did come to my door as Randy was declared a missing child. To the officer's confusion, I put forward the same explanation as the other four juvenile witnesses. Randy was never seen again, but a shoe belonging to him was found in a service station play area the next state over. That was 1999, and no new evidence has ever come to light. 

Today, the off-brand fast food place remains where it was. It managed to stay open for another month and a half after Randy's disappearance, before shutting its doors for good. No local developers wanted to touch that property, and so it grows more derelict with every passing year. Its bright facade, a relic of 90's fast food culture, has faded into muted tones. Now grey, it reflects joints the country over. The only vibrance comes from the foliage growing throughout the building, and the graffiti that paints its walls. It was a popular hang-out stop for teens until the disappearances in 2021. The abandonment spread like a tumor, with that restaurant as its center. Now, most of the buildings around it lay in a similar state of ruin, as the town's local economy collapsed from Covid. I remain, however. The action I'm about to commit is borne from a decision I've been contemplating for over twenty years. With my gun and my camera, and my snatched bait, I will finally prove to the world my ultimate truth. Something that has left me branded as a liar for my entire life. Reddit, wish me luck.
