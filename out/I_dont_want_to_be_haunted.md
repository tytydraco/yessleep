…like my mother was. She’d tell me true ghost stories all the time growing up. There were the stories she’d share with other folks. Like the time she saw the ghost of her dead cousin who drowned, or the ghost of our dead cat that time it darted under her bed just hours after it’d died.

Then there were the stories she saved just for me, it seemed, when we were alone and my knees were tucked into my chest under the covers. Like the time something grabbed her ankle in the middle of the night and dragged her to the foot of the bed. Then she’d grab my ankle, laughing, and send me off to sleep. I was supposed to pray for everyone’s safety before sleeping, but all I ever did was pray to never seeing ghosts like she did. Never feeling something pulling on my hair like she did. I never wanted to be haunted and see the things she did.

As I got older, it was easier to dismiss my fears as part of my mom’s morbid imagination. She was the woman who told me that when you saw a shoe in the street, it was because someone got pulled into a car. But then she told the stories around my aunt and uncle, about the hair pulling and the hand grabbing her ankle, and they nodded. They remembered what happened. They all heard her scream and saw the heavy bruises on her ankle. They were in the room when it happened. They saw her get lifted up by the hair.

My prayers came back too. “Please God, I don’t want to be haunted and see the things she did.”

But she never stopped telling me about them when we were alone, about the difference between ghosts and spirits… about the things that were once people who were now wandering around, lost. And the things that were never human, that were waiting for her to die. Maybe she was warning me. Maybe I was like her and she was trying to protect me.

Then she got sick, and I sat there and watched her eyes dart to the dark corners of her hospice room, whimpering at what she saw. I didn’t look, I didn’t want to see them, but they were there like she said. Waiting for her. I could feel them in the room, hear a soft, eager clicking like dry mouths moving and making no sound. I could only hold her hand as she clutched mine, too weak to hurt me and holding on for dear life, like my hand was the only thing stopping her from slipping away.

But she slipped away, and I felt… relieved. I felt like a monster because I hadn’t inherited her ghosts and I felt relief at that, but I told people I was relieved because at least her pain was over.

It’s been ten years since she’s been gone, but she still visits me, sitting on the edge of my bed late at night, staring across the room, her dry lips moving silently as she tells me ghost stories I can’t hear.

At least I’m not haunted by the things she sees.
