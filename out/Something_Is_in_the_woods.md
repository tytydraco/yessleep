This is a story I don’t like to talk about too often. I don’t think I ever have gotten over it. But since we are getting close to the 5 year anniversary of this so ill talk. This happened when I was 20. Early summer in Wisconsin. There were four of us. Me.

My girlfriend at the time, Emily—we’d been dating maybe three months, still in that careful, hopeful stage.My best friend Ryan, who I’d known since we were kids, Eagle Scouts together, grew up camping every other weekend. And his girlfriend Hannah, who he met at college down south. She was visiting Wisconsin for the first time and we wanted our girlfriends to meet becuase we’d actually never had girlfriends at the same time, and we wanted them to mingle.

We weren’t reckless or stupid, week a little stupid but still Ryan and I weren’t those guys. We planned routes, checked maps, packed right. We’d done harder trips before. The goal was simple: start near the middle of the Ice Age Trail and walk as far as we could in a week. No destination. Just distance and catching up. I brought my dad’s Beretta. Not because I expected trouble—just because my dad always said “The woods don’t care how prepared you think you are.” He’d passed a few years earlier, and carrying it made me feel… steadier. Like a habit I hadn’t let go of yet.

Day One

We left and started walking at about 7:30 am The trail was perfect at first. Green everywhere. Bugs just starting to wake up. That early-summer smell of wet dirt and leaves. Hannah was excited—too excited. Loud. Laughing at everything. Ryan and I kept exchanging those looks that say she’ll calm down by nightfall. The first instance of this entity in the woods was about 6 hours in we had made it a good amount though. When we stopped I was sitting for a second when I looked up I thought a I saw a tall thing in the woods about 100 feet out looking but I looked again and it was gone.

By mid-afternoon, Emily tugged on my sleeve and whispered, “Do you feel like we’re being watched?”

I laughed it off. I wish I hadn’t. I told her it was just being in the woods too long, your brain filling silence with noise. Now with this on my mine I knew something wasn’t right 

But about an hour later, Ryan stopped walking.

He was staring off the trail, into the trees.

“You hear that?” he asked.

We all went quiet.

There was nothing obvious. No footsteps. No animal sounds. Just… a sense of pressure. Like when someone stands too close behind you.

Ryan shook it off. We kept going.

That night, we camped a little off trail. Nothing happened. But I slept like crap. I remember dreaming that someone was circling the tent, breathing too slowly to be human too human to be a animal.

Day Two

That’s when it started following us.

Not in a dramatic way. No snapped branches. No running.

Just wrongness.

Every time we stopped, it felt like something stopped with us. When we walked, it felt like we were being paced—never close enough to hear, never far enough to forget.

Emily barely talked anymore. Hannah stopped joking completely.

Ryan finally said it out loud around dusk.

“I think something’s tracking us.”

We stood there, Me and Ryan who’d grown up on scary stories, around campfires. None of us wanting to be the one to say skinwalker or more locally the The Beast of Bray Road or anything like that.

That night, we heard footsteps.

Heavy. Slow. Too deliberate.

Not circling us—just standing somewhere beyond the firelight.

I unzipped my tent enough to slip the Beretta out and rested it beside me. I didn’t sleep. None of us really did.

Day Three

By the third day, we were exhausted. We were thinking of getting a night or two more then heading back home. At one point, I could’ve sworn I saw something tall moving between trees—too thin, limbs too long—but when I looked again, there was nothing.We set camp early that night. Nobody argued.Ryan and Hannah’s tent was set about twenty yards from ours. Emily fell asleep almost immediately. I didn't. I couldn't. I knew it was out there stalking us. Around 2 a.m., I heard it.

Someone whispered my name.

My full name.

In my dad’s voice.

I froze. My heart started pounding so hard I thought Emily would wake up. It felt like it was going out of my chest 

“Hey bud,” the voice said, right outside my tent. Calm. Familiar. “You can come out now.”

My Father had been dead for four years. He died from  throat cancer I haven’t heard him talk in about a decade 

I don’t remember deciding to grab the gun. It was just suddenly in my hands.

The voice got closer. I could see the tent fabric dent inward, like something was leaning against it.

“Don’t be scared,” it said. “I’m right here.”

I screamed. I fired.

I didn’t unzip the tent. I didn’t aim. I just shot through the fabric.

The sound that came back wasn’t human.

It wasn’t even an animal.

It was like something trying to scream through a throat that wasn’t built for it—half man, half something else, stretched and furious.

I kept firing until the mag emptied.

I heard it crash through the woods, breaking branches, moving impossibly fast.

Ryan was yelling. Emily was crying. Hannah was screaming hysterically.

We all stayed up lighting our lanterns and with my gun with my girlfriend basically attached to my side. When the sun rose we looked around before leaving. We saw some dark blood. Too dark. Thick.

It led straight off the trail into deep woods where no one sane would follow. We didn’t. We packed what we could carry and left everything else. Tents. Food. Gear. We walked until our legs gave out and didn’t stop until we hit a town. We called our parents to pick us up and we left. After We never talked about it. Not really.

Emily and I broke up a few weeks later. There wasn’t a fight. Just… distance. Like something had hollowed us out. After college Ryan permanently moved down south. Married Hannah. Two kids now.

We fell out of contact. Last time I saw him was at his wedding. I’m 25 now. Sometimes, when I’m half asleep, I swear I hear dad’s voice outside my apartment door. I never answer. I don’t camp anymore. And I will never go back to that area

Because whatever followed us those three days?

I don’t think it ever needed the woods. The scariest part is that whatever it was it knew what my dad sounded like. But the less I think about it the better.
