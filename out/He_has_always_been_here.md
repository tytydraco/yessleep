My family and I, meaning myself, my wife Dana, and our two children, Mara and Joel, moved into my grandparents' old house almost two months ago. My grandfather passed away in his sleep last spring, and my grandmother has been in assisted living ever since she had a fall and was struggling to get around. My parents are responsible for the property; moving in just made sense. The house was paid off, the neighborhood is quiet and safe, and we needed to get out of our apartment. Together, we packed up and moved in during the third week of September.

The house smelled of my grandparents. Old wood and lavender with something underneath that I just couldn't name, but it was something that settled in the back of my throat. My children thought it was cool, a real house with a yard, a basement, and an attic. My wife, Dana, said she could already feel like it was home. I wanted to feel the same. I almost did.

The pictures started changing on our fifth night there.

My grandparents were photographers. Not professionally, but they would fill the walls of the house with framed photographs over the 50 years of their living here. Family get-togethers, holiday dinners, vacations to places I could only half remember. When we moved in, we kept a majority of them up. It felt wrong to stop the walls, as if it was erasing something.

The first one I noticed was a photo in the upstairs hallway, one I was sure had been of my grandmother and her sister at a carnival. They had cotton candy, and a blurred Ferris wheel was in the background. I’d looked at it the day we moved in, and I remembered it clearly.

On the night they started changing, I had gotten up for a glass of water and passed the photo in the dark, and something made me stop and turn on the hallway light.

It was a man I didn't recognize

It was the same frame the picture was in before, and it has the same yellowing at the edges of the photo. But it wasn't my grandmother and her sister anymore. It was a man, standing alone, facing the camera with no expression. He looked like he may have been in his 40s or 50s, but it was hard to tell. The man wore a dark suit, his hair neatly parted, and his hands were placed at his sides. He was just standing there. looking past the camera at whoever was taking the picture.

I stood there staring for what seemed like forever.

I tried to tell myself that maybe I just misremembered, that maybe I was thinking of a completely different photo. There were so many in the house that I just mixed them up; it's not like I remembered all of them, and I wasn't even certain. I went back to bed. I didn't sleep well.

In the morning, I asked my wife about the photo.

“One of him?” she said , gesturing at the photo with her coffee cup.

“Who is he?” I asked, hoping she'd know

She looked at me with a small, puzzled smile, like I'd asked something I should obviously know. “I don't know his name. He's always been there. Your grandparents probably knew him.”

“Dana. That photo was of Grandma Ruth and her sister at a carnival. I saw it when we moved in.”

She looked at me, confused, and shook her head slowly, “babe. It's always been him.”

I let it go, I thought, or maybe I was hoping I was mixing it up with another photo somewhere in the house, or maybe a picture in an album I saw when I was a kid. The mind fills in details it thinks it remembers, which is something I knew. But I started paying attention after that.

About a week later, it was the big family photo in the living room, the one that was from my parents' wedding in the 80s, and everyone posed for the camera on the church steps. My mother, in her long white dress, and my father, in his suit and tie. I had grown up with this photo, and I could have described every face from memory. But now there he is.

standing in the back row.

Same man, same dark suit, same flat expression as if he has no emotion at all. He was between my uncle and a family friend of my father's, the place where my aunt had been standing. I immediately pulled out my phone and called my mother.

“Mom, your and Dad's wedding photo in the living room, who is the man in the back row? dark suit between my uncle and.”

“Oh, him,” she cuts me off and says it so casually. “I actually don't remember who that is, a friend of your father's maybe? He has always been in that photo.”

“Mom, he was not in that photo. I’ve been looking at that picture my entire life.”

after a pause. “Honey, I don't think you are remembering right. He has always been there, you should know who he is don't you remember him?”

“Dad, who is he?”

Silence falls over the phone for a moment. “He’s always been around. You grew up seeing him at their house, you really don't remember him?”

There it was again. *You really don't remember him.*

“No,” I said. “I don’t.”

“Hmm,” he sounded sorry for me, as if I should obviously know. “Well, i'm sure it'll come to you.”
