**It started with an argument that felt like a thousand others before it, my parents on one side of the kitchen table, me on the other. Mom and Dad were talking about "investing in my future," how college would open doors I couldn't even imagine yet. But all I could imagine was debt piling up while I studied subjects I didn't care about.**

**I tried to explain it to them, that jobs are disappearing faster than they're being created, that AI is already eating everything that pays even remotely well. Even the "bad" jobs require degrees now, and by the time you get one... poof, there it goes. But my parents just shook their heads like I was some dramatic teenager. You know how parents are.**

**"You need a plan," Dad said, his voice low and serious. "You can't just drift." I wanted to yell that having no plan was better than planning for something that wouldn't exist by the time I finished. Instead, I bit down hard on my frustration and nodded along until they were done lecturing me.**

**Then came their offer, since they seemed to realize they weren’t getting through to me: they would buy me a car if I found honest work. I guess they thought it would prove something, show me responsibility or whatever. But really, all it showed was how little they understood what "honest work" even meant anymore. The richest people in the world were taking bribes or rug-pulling “investors” in crypto scams, so maybe that’s what “honest work” was nowadays.**

**So there I was, finally driving the rust bucket they bought me to my minimum wage job at the hardware store. The radio was broken so I was stuck with the hum of the engine and my own thoughts. It could barely even be considered a real car, more like an old tank that's been through three world wars and won none of them. Duct tape held together the bumper, the hood, probably the body panels underneath too if I ever bothered to look.**

**I should’ve been grateful, right? They bought me something instead of just kicking me out to fend for myself. But as I pulled into the parking lot and saw all those shiny new cars belonging to people who actually had a future... it felt like pity more than anything else.**

**The job itself wasn’t terrible: stocking shelves, helping old ladies find paint cans they lost three aisles ago. It just felt so pointless sometimes. I moved product from one place to another for almost nothing per hour while the owners made bank and didn’t even have to do anything. And when automation finally comes (which it will), who do you think is going to get cut first?**

**I used to dream about being something. An engineer, maybe, or even a teacher if that paid enough. But after this? I just wanted the day to be over so I could go home and pretend none of it was real. Of course, the duct tape car waited for me in the parking lot, rusting away like everything else around me.  A reminder that no matter how hard you try to hold things together... eventually they all fall apart anyway. High school was over, and it was all downhill from there.**

**I slouched in the passenger seat, watching the cracked landscape blur through the windshield as I drove home from work. The radio crackled static instead of music and the AC struggled to heat the cabin, another thing that had long since fallen apart on this piece-of-shit car my parents called a "gift."**

**I’m pretty sure I ended up in another argument with my parents that night. I'd tried to explain it wasn't about the money, but of course they couldn't understand. College degrees were becoming like old tube TVs, obsolete before you even graduated. AI could do most entry-level jobs better than humans now, and all the remaining jobs would just get outsourced to some other country to make shareholders more money. I'd be in debt up to my eyeballs for a degree that would just make me more replaceable. But no, they insisted it was an "investment." They didn’t get that I was born too late to get any of the good jobs, but too early for the robot ones.**

**I started driving towards Ace Hardware again later that week, where I'd been pushing boxes onto shelves since graduation day. Minimum wage barely covered gas in such a money pit of a car. The other guys called it "the Duct Tape Express" behind my back… and to my face, as if I didn't already know it was a piece of crap.**

**I looked at the clock: 6:50 AM. It was going to be another shift of lifting heavy junk and periodically going to the cash register, smiling like I really cared about someone's shopping experience. Meanwhile, crypto bros were becoming millionaires overnight with nothing but a laptop and some lucky clicks. Or those busty Instagram girls who just had to flash cleavage and suddenly they're living in mansions and making 10x as much as me from OnlyFans and sugar daddies.**

**I slammed my hands against the steering wheel, not that it made any difference, but it did honk the horn and startle me awake from my pity party. "Get over yourself," I muttered, cold from the pre-dawn chill.  It was time to go be a good little worker bee. But something inside me was simmering, and not in a good way. This wasn't living; it was just existing until someone else decided they were done with me.**

**Traffic suddenly slowed, and I realized I was stuck behind a line of cars that stretched ahead, right up to the parking lot of Ace Hardware. Construction barriers blocked off both lanes to my left, forcing everyone to merge into one lane.**

**The sun peeked over the skyline, casting long shadows across the asphalt. I squinted at the source of the bottleneck and didn’t quite know what I was looking at. A massive metal column or thick pipe jutted out from where the road had split open like a wound, rising up as high as the streetlights.**

**"What the hell is that thing?" I muttered, craning my neck out the window for a better look around the car in front of me. It looked pretty hi-tech, not like any construction equipment I'd ever seen. The surface was smooth and reflected the emerging sunlight; it had symbols etched along its length that weren’t quite legible at this distance.**

**I glanced at the clock: 6:57 AM already. If traffic didn't clear soon, I'd be late again... and there were only so many warnings my manager would give me before he decided to hand me some sort of improvement plan and I’d lose my highly coveted position.**

**Sirens wailed in the distance as more police cars arrived on scene, not just local cops but state troopers too. They formed a human chain across what remained of the open lane, waving glowing batons at drivers like they were herding cattle through a narrow chute.**

**I drummed my fingers on the steering wheel, jaw clenched tight enough to grind my teeth. This whole situation felt like a metaphor for everything else in my life: trapped between forces beyond my control, watching opportunities slip away while I waited for someone else's permission to move forward again.**

**I fiddled vociferously with the radio until it finally caught a signal, static giving way to excited voices from some morning show host who sounded almost gleeful at the chaos that was preventing me from getting to work:**

**"...unidentified object emerging from beneath Interstate 7... police have no comment on its origin or purpose but are urging all drivers to find alternate routes immediately..."**

**I snorted. Yeah, sure, like I was about to find another route when I was already stuck in this mess. My phone buzzed with an incoming text:** 

**Mom: You okay? Saw it on the news.**

**Me: Stuck in traffic. Can't see shit but some giant metal dick sticking out of the ground.**

**I winced at my own crassness, then shrugged. She’d understand.**

**Mom: Language! Just be careful and call me when you can, okay? Love you.**

**"Love you too," I mumbled back, not texting it, but saying it aloud to myself. I pocketed the phone quickly as a police officer approached my window with an official-looking clipboard.**

**"You work around here?" he asked gruffly, pen poised above a checklist of some kind. His eyes glanced over to the duct tape holding together my bumper, judgment written plainly across his face before he even opened his mouth again: "Because if not..."**

**"I do," I said quickly, hating how defensive it sounded coming out. "Ace Hardware right over in that parking lot." I nodded down at my vest and name badge in order to back up my claim with evidence.**

**He nodded curtly and made some marks on his checklist, presumably writing my license plate number. "Alright then. You're through, keep moving when you can." The officer stepped aside with a wave of his baton.**

**I inched forward another foot, tires crunching over broken asphalt that had been tossed up by whatever force had brought this thing to the surface overnight. A sick feeling churned in my gut as I stared at the strange symbols up close. Some of them looked like foreign characters, reminding me of some mangas I had looked at as a kid, but there were some visual depictions as well. I felt like they were trying to tell me something if I could only understand.**

﻿**I scrolled through the local Discord channel at work, where everyone was obviously talking about the column. The theories people put out were absolutely outlandish, and it was hard to tell if they were serious or just trolling.**

**Xyonix: "This is clearly a burial chamber for some ancient tribal chief. The symbols are definitely pre-Columbian."**

**Verdant Blue: "Aliens man! They've been keeping them underground all along and this is one of their prisons!"**

**ShyGuy69: "Guys, it's a sewer access point. I work for the city, we have these old chambers everywhere."**

**I paused at that last comment, trying to decide who sounded most credible. The guy claiming to be an employee seemed reasonable enough outside of his Discord tag... but it didn’t seem likely to me that a sewer access point would have those symbols on it, unless it was some kind of sophisticated graffiti.**

**I zoomed in on my phone screen, studying the pictures people had snapped as they inched by: intricate spirals and geometric patterns I'd never seen before. They looked deliberate, like writing of some kind, though nothing remotely familiar to me. There was a face at the bottom framed with cupped hands and what looked like a sun with three orbiting bodies.**

**My break was almost over; I sighed and pocketed the device. Whatever that thing actually was, I’m sure I wasn’t going to find the answer from other random drivers. There would probably be some official explanation on the news.**

**Being back behind the register usually meant another three hours of scanning barcodes and small talk about the weather, but now it was all about "that weird column thing." Everyone else seemed obsessed as I was, and it was right past the parking lot, after all.**

**"You see what happened over on 7?" Mrs. Scanlon asked as she unloaded her basket onto my conveyor belt. Her eyes were sparkling with gossip hunger.**

**I nodded noncommittally while ringing up her items: "Yeah, it’s within view of the store ya know."**

**"It's just so strange," she continued without waiting. "What do you think it could be?"**

**"Probably some old sewer thing or something," I shrugged, repeating the only non-crazy comment I had read on break.**

**Mrs. Scanlon pursed her lips skeptically: "Hmmm... well, my grandson says online people are saying all sorts of crazy things: aliens, ancient tombs, government experiments gone wrong..."**

**I forced a polite smile even as my mind raced through those same possibilities again. The woman seemed determined to wring every scrap of speculation from me before leaving with her items.**

**"I'm sure they'll figure it out soon enough," I said finally when she showed no signs of moving along on her own accord yet. "Probably just some old construction thing that got uncovered by accident."**

**Mrs. Scanlon hummed thoughtfully but didn't look fully convinced; I could see the gears turning behind those sharp blue eyes as she tried to decide whether my response was honest or evasive.**

**"Well, anyway," she said after a little bit too long for comfort, "just be careful out there yourself, you never know what else might come popping up from underground these days."**

**With that cryptic warning delivered like some kind of prophetic declaration, Mrs. Scanlon finally gathered her bags and shuffled off toward the exit.**

**I exhaled slowly once she was gone, rubbing at my temples where a headache had started to throb steadily since first light this morning, before any giant metal columns or nosy customers complicated things further than they already were.**

**The story eventually started to gain national attention, with experts from various fields flying in to examine the object, which naturally generated a gang of reporters as well. Sometimes I could see them from work, but they were too far away to glean any information, so I watched the news like everyone else.**

**One of the experts that came was Dr. Sema Al-Zoubi, a renowned linguist and archaeologist specializing in ancient scripts. Definitely more impressive than my title of sales associate.**

**Dr. Al-Zoubi made a show of examining the symbols etched into the metal column's surface, her eyes narrowing as she studied each intricate design. I imagined how many takes they did before they settled on this one. Apparently, the face symbol intrigued her most: two hands cupping cheeks beneath a round sun with three distinct lines orbiting it. She traced the lines with gloved fingers before it cut to a standing interview.**

**"A hand on either cheek... could represent balance? Or perhaps a specific cultural practice." Her brow furrowed, looking studious as if she was considering different interpretations.**

**The interviewer spoke off screen. "Any progress on the other symbols, Dr. Al-Zoubi?"**

**She sighed heavily. "Progress is difficult without context or comparative examples. The sun symbol with three lines suggests some sort of celestial significance, possibly tracking time, seasons, or even astronomical events."**

**"Like a calendar?" the interviewer asked hopefully.**

**Dr. Al-Zoubi shook her head. "Perhaps, but it's far too simple for that. More likely an abstract representation of something else entirely." She took on a serious expression. "What concerns me more is this column itself: modern metalwork, perfectly smooth exterior, and no visible seams or entry points."**

**The interviewer spoke again from behind the camera. "They've had teams examining it from every angle. I guess no one can figure out how to open it."**

**"Exactly," Dr. Al-Zoubi said. "If these symbols hold a key, literally or metaphorically, we need to understand their meaning before attempting anything else." She glanced at some papers in her hand, then back up at the towering structure. "I'm beginning to wonder if opening it is even possible... or perhaps intended."**

**The interviewer kept asking questions, not wasting her opportunity with the expert. "What do you mean?"**

**"Consider this," Al-Zoubi says thoughtfully. "If this symbol represent balance, hands on either cheek, what happens when that balance is disrupted? What happens when something is removed, altered, or forced open against its natural state?"**

**She let the question hang in the air, probably pausing at something the interviewer was doing.**

**"Maybe," she continues more softly, "the real mystery isn't how to get inside. Maybe it's why we found this here at all, and whether disturbing it was ever meant to be an option."**

**I found the questions compelling, or at least more compelling than your average nightly news, but the following city council members immediately started talking about plans to open it or dismantle it to make it easier to move. I wasn’t able to finish the coverage since I felt exceptionally tired and fell asleep with the TV still on.**

**Later that week, I watched the scene unfold on O’Mulligan’s ancient tube television, my heart pounding in sync with the festive atmosphere of people drinking and gossipping. The crowd around me was buzzing with excitement, some nervous, others giddy at the prospect of easy money. People had been taking bets as soon as a date was set for the capsule to be opened, and my own family was no exception.**

**My uncle Mike leaned over, his eyes wide and manic. "This is it, man! We're about to be rich!" Unfortunately, I wasn’t quite old enough to drink, so I was stuck at O’Mulligans as Mike’s designated driver. I forced a smile, trying not to show my unease. Something felt off about this whole thing, and even though I was curious about what was inside, I didn’t have any money on it.**

**The camera zoomed in on the column as workers in hardhats approached cautiously. They had already drilled holes along its surface, creating weak points where the metal finally yielded under hydraulic pressure. With a weak groan of protest, one side began to shift, revealing...**

**Nothing.**

**Well, not literally nothing, grey dust billowed out like smoke from an extinguished fire, coating everything within reach in a fine, ashen layer. The crowd at O’Mulligans collectively stopped talking before erupting into chaos.**

**"Dust! It's just fuckin' dust!" someone shouted gleefully from the back corner. Apparently, he'd bet every cent on "nothing."**

**Mike turned to me with a look of pure disbelief, his face draining of color. "$1,000 down the drain," he mumbled numbly, “I could’ve paid off my tab.”**

**I gave him a firm pat on the shoulder as people around us celebrated or cursed depending on their wagers. The bar owner grinned wickedly behind the counter; he probably took bets from both sides and cleaned up either way.**

**The next morning, I dragged myself into work, my stomach churning from the flat soda and stale pretzels at O’Mulligans. The column still loomed beyond the parking lot, battered and violated, an ominous sentinel watching silently from its disturbed resting place.**

**The initial excitement faded faster than mist in sunlight. People were already moving on to new gossip and old grudges, the alien mystery reduced to nothing more than disappointment. There was still some talk on the local Discord about how the dust was the remains of an ancient mummy and how people were cheated out of their winnings, but it all just sounded silly to me.**

**I forced myself through the motions of my shift, stacking shelves with mechanical precision, and eventually I stopped looking at the column outside. The city would send a wrecker to scrap it soon, and everyone would move on with their lives. Except for me, I’d still be here at Ace Hardware.**

**By lunch break, nausea and heartburn had crept up my throat like bile, hot and acidic. I barely made it to the bathroom before retching violently into the cracked porcelain sink. My hands shook as I rinsed away the evidence, splashing cold water on my face until the room stopped spinning.**

**What was wrong with me? I stared at myself in the mirror, dark circles under bloodshot eyes, skin pale and clammy. When did I become this hollow shell of a man?**

**But as I drove past the ruined monolith on my way home, something shifted in the air around me... like a breath held too long finally released, or maybe just the wind changing direction. Either way, by the time I reached my apartment door, all thoughts of nausea and ominous columns had fled as quickly as they came. I mushed myself face-first into my bed and turned on the television.**
