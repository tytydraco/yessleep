STORY 

If you’re ever in Sweden, you might come across those quiet little tourist attractions where people ride metal rail bikes along old, abandoned train tracks. They’re usually tucked deep into forests, far from cities, surrounded by nothing but trees and silence.

I’m not going to say exactly where this happened. Some places are better left unnamed.

A few years ago, my twin brother and I worked at one of those places during the summer. We had done it for a couple of seasons already, so by then we knew everything about the track—every turn, every clearing, every strange little detail you stop noticing after a while.

There were three stations along the track: one at each end, and one in the middle where we worked. Our job was simple. We would greet visitors, explain the rules before they went out on the rail bikes, and make sure everything ran smoothly. But another big part of the job was maintenance.

The track ran through dense forest for several kilometers, and nature constantly tried to reclaim it. Branches would fall, bushes would grow over the rails, and sometimes even trees would collapse across the track. So before opening, we would often ride out and clear anything that could be dangerous.

Our section stretched about five kilometers north to a small wooden cabin near a damaged part of the rail. On a normal day, it would take about an hour to get there and back, depending on how much work needed to be done.

The day this happened started like any other.

We arrived at the station around 9 a.m. The attraction didn’t open until 10, and that morning there were no bookings. That wasn’t completely unusual, but it meant we could take our time. We put up signs saying we were out on the track doing maintenance, just in case someone showed up.

Everything felt normal—at first.

We got on the rail bike and started heading north. My brother was pedaling, and I was sitting behind him, just looking around like I usually did.

It didn’t take long before something started to feel… off.

At first, I couldn’t figure out what it was. It wasn’t something you could point at or explain. Just a feeling.

Then I realized what was wrong.

There were no sounds.

No birds.

No insects.

No wind in the trees.

Nothing.

The forest was completely silent, except for the faint metallic clicking of the rail bike moving along the tracks.

I remember thinking how strange it was, but I didn’t say anything right away. I didn’t want to sound paranoid over nothing. But the deeper we went, the heavier that feeling became.

About two kilometers in, I couldn’t ignore it anymore.

“Hey,” I said, trying to sound casual, “are you getting tired? I can take over if you want.”

“Yeah,” my brother said, slowing down. “This uphill is worse than I remember.”

We switched places. I started pedaling, and for a while, things felt a little more normal again. We started talking—about games, about what we were going to eat later, about random stuff. Just trying to fill the silence, I guess.

But even with us talking, the forest itself stayed quiet.

No response. No life.

After a while, we got close to the end of our section. We knew the area well. There was a clearing ahead where the dense forest opened up into fields on both sides of the track. It was one of the few places where you could actually see far ahead.

We slowed down just before the clearing to switch one last time.

That’s when I saw it.

“Jim… what the hell is that?”

I pointed straight ahead.

At first, my brain tried to make sense of it. It looked like an animal standing in the tall grass near the edge of the clearing.

But something was wrong.

It was too big.

Much bigger than a moose. Bigger than any deer I had ever seen.

It stood on all fours, but its back was arched in a strange, unnatural way—like it was hunched too tightly, almost like its spine didn’t bend the way it should.

Its color was hard to make out. Gray… maybe pale white. It didn’t look like it belonged in the forest at all.

We both froze.

We didn’t move.

We didn’t speak.

We just stared at it.

For what felt like minutes—maybe longer—it didn’t react. Then it slowly started moving its head along the ground, like it was sniffing or tracking something.

That’s when I got a clearer look at its face.

I wish I hadn’t.

The best way I can describe it is this: imagine a deer skull and a wolf’s skull somehow fused together, but not cleanly—wrong, twisted, incomplete.

It didn’t have eyes.

Or at least, not any I could see.

Just dark, empty sockets.

The surface of its head looked rotten, like decaying flesh or matted fur stuck to bone. It didn’t look alive in a normal way.

Then it made a sound.

A loud, cracking noise.

Like bones snapping.

Like thick branches being broken all at once.

It echoed through the silent forest, and I felt it in my chest more than I heard it.

Slowly, it turned its head toward us.

It knew we were there.

It lowered its body, its back arching even more, like it was preparing to run.

That’s when I forced myself to speak.

“Jim… slowly turn the bike around. I’ll keep watching it.”

“But what if—”

“Just do it,” I said, more firmly.

He didn’t argue again.

Very slowly, carefully, he started turning the rail bike around on the track. Every second felt stretched, like time was moving slower than it should.

The creature opened its jaw slightly.

It looked like it was smiling at us.

Then, without warning, it moved.

One second it was there—

The next, it dropped low into the grass and vanished.

I didn’t wait.

I turned and jumped onto the bike.

“Go! As fast as you can!”

Jim started pedaling immediately.

We sped down the track, heading downhill. The rail bikes could go fast—much faster than people expect. We were probably going 30, maybe even 40 kilometers per hour.

At first, I thought we might have gotten away.

I looked back.

Nothing.

Just empty track and silent trees.

Then we heard it.

To our right.

In the forest.

Branches snapping.

Trees shaking.

Something moving fast—keeping pace with us.

It was following us.

For what felt like forever—probably around 20 minutes—it stayed with us. We couldn’t see it, but we could hear it, always just off to the side, always matching our speed.

Then suddenly…

It stopped.

The sounds disappeared completely.

Jim slowed down, completely exhausted, breathing hard.

“I think it’s gone,” I said, though I wasn’t sure.

We stopped for a moment, trying to listen.

And that’s when we heard it.

A scream.

But not a normal scream.

It sounded like a man and a woman screaming at the same time—mixed with something animal. Like a dog crying. It was deep and high-pitched at once, like laughter and pain combined into one sound.

It didn’t sound human.

It didn’t sound like anything that should exist.

It echoed through the forest, and I felt my entire body go cold.

We didn’t wait after that.

We got back on the bike and kept going as fast as we could.

About ten minutes later, something changed.

The forest came back to life.

Birds started chirping.

The wind moved through the trees.

Everything sounded normal again, like nothing had ever happened.

When we got back to the station, it was empty.

We didn’t do any more work that day.

We didn’t tell our boss.

We didn’t tell anyone at the time.

Eventually, we told a few family members—but even then, it felt impossible to explain.

After that summer, we both quit.

Officially, it was because the attraction shut down—low visitor numbers, safety concerns.

But even if it hadn’t closed…

I would never go back.

And if you ever find yourself out there—in a place where the forest suddenly goes silent, where something feels wrong but you can’t explain why—

Don’t ignore it.

Turn around.

And whatever you do…

Don’t wait to see what’s watching you.
