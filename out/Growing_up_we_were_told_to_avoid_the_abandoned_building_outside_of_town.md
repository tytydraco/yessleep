Like many of us who grew up in the Midwest, I'm from a small town that lived in the ruin of industry.  At one time it had a thriving culture and economy, but once the mines dried up the fat cats took their stacks of cash and went elsewhere.  As a teenager, the only real pastime I had was loitering in abandoned buildings with my friends.  There was one building, however, everyone in the town avoided.

It was a square building hidden in the forest just outside of town.  Time had had its way with its boxy brick frame.  Trees and vines wrapped around the outside making it almost invisible if you weren’t looking for it.  Somehow curtains the color of smoke stained walls still hung on the inside, blocking the interior from view.  Peeking out from a crack between the curtains on one of the ground floor windows was an eye.  An unblinking dark brown eye.

You could call it a local legend but that would be inaccurate to the reality of the situation. No one who lived in the town denied its existence.  Even our parents told us to stay away from the building.  Many different theories surrounded it.  The most prevalent, and maybe the most obvious one, was that it was the ghost of a Miner.  Some said it was a demonic entity summoned by the locals years ago.

It was a hazing ritual for freshmen on the football team to have to approach the house late at night.  I was subjected to this torture along with three of my fellow classmates.  The older kids stood back, laughing and jeering as we approached the building on a Friday after practice.  I walked confidently to the side of the house while the other three kids cowered behind me.  I’ve always been too confident for my own good.

There was a window on the side of the building where the curtains had disintegrated just enough to where you could peer through.  I stood transfixed, looking at a site that filled me with a mix of deep fear and indescribable awe.  A disembodied face floated motionless behind the front window.  It was a hollow mask of flesh suspended in midair.  Standing on the opposite side of the room was a tall, almost skeletal body.  In place of its absent face was a dark pit that seemed to shimmer and swirl like water reflecting moonlight.  The form turned and pointed directly at us.  My teammates ran and I was quick to follow.

For the next four years that night lived ever present in my mind.  I dreamed of it constantly.  I felt as if something was calling me back there.  For the longest time I dared not to listen to that interior call.  Eventually though, I caved in.  Senior year after graduation I went back into the forest alone.  I held a flashlight in my right hand and a hunting knife in my left.  I didn’t know what to expect.  I didn’t even know why I was doing what I was doing.  It was one of those cool summer nights that felt deeply nostalgic and mysterious, as if I’d lived that night a thousand times before.

I approached the building from the side once again.  The faceless body turned and pointed, but this time I did not run.  It took me a moment to realize that it was not pointing at me.  It was pointing to the ground just outside the window.  I stepped back to see a wooden hatch covered by vines and bramble.  Against my better judgment I cleaned it off and opened it up.  The doors creaked loudly as a sudden breeze moved through the forest.  Inside an unnaturally steep staircase led into the Earth.

“There is nothing for you here.”  

It was as if the deep raspy voice moved with the wind itself.  Goosebumps covered my skin as my hair stood on end.  I pointed the flashlight down the staircase.  It took me a moment to understand what I was seeing.  A naked woman covered in dirt sat at the bottom of the stairs, her emaciated form nearly blending in with the darkness.  Her smile was deep and wide, her eyes genuine in their empathy.  She held a baby to her breast.  It fed voraciously.

“Who… who are you?”  The words were pitiful and quiet as they slipped out my lips.  The flashlight shook in my hand.

“We are all things forgotten.  Left to rot, in the dark alone.”

The Voice wasn’t coming from the woman but from the baby.  The infant turned away from its mother‘s breast and looked at me.  It wore the face of an old man, its eyes more ancient than the world itself.  I tried to form a thought, to form even a single word.  All that came out was a sad squeak.

“You will leave here now.  You will forget, as all things do.  And one day, far after the ash of your bones have disappeared into the soil, the things that have been forgotten will be remembered once again.”

The ancient infant turned back to the woman’s breast.  I hurriedly shut the doors and walked away.  I left for college later that summer.  Despite my parents' constant invitations, I haven’t been back to my home town since.  It’s impossible to explain to them why.  I’ve learned that curiosity is not a virtue, that there are ancient secrets no human should know.  I hope to live a boring life far away from such secrets, comfortable in my chosen ignorance.
