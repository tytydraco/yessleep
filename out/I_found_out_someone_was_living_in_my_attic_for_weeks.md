I need to write this out because I still don’t fully believe it happened.

For about three weeks, someone was living in my attic.

I live alone. Small one-bedroom house. You can see almost the entire place from the living room. There aren’t a lot of blind spots.

That’s why it took me so long to understand what was happening.

At first it was food.

Cereal running out faster than it should. Bread disappearing. A case of bottled water I swear I had just opened suddenly down to two bottles.

I blamed myself.

I’ve stress-eaten before. I figured I was forgetting.

Then I started hearing movement at night.

Not scratching. Not scurrying. Not pipes.

Weight.

Like someone shifting their knees on wooden beams.

Directly above my bedroom.

The first time it happened, I froze and stared at the ceiling. I told myself it was the house settling.

The second time, it sounded like a slow drag across insulation.

I almost got up to check.

I didn’t.

The cough is what changed everything.

It was 2:38 a.m.

One dry, contained cough.

Above me.

Close enough that it felt like it came through the ceiling.

I sat upright and listened for ten straight minutes.

Nothing.

No footsteps. No movement. Just silence.

I did not go up there.

I wish I could explain why.

Two nights later, I woke up because my bedroom door was slightly open.

I close it every night. I lock it. That’s a habit.

It wasn’t wide open.

Just enough that I could see the dark hallway through the crack.

I checked the front door. Locked. Deadbolt still engaged.

Windows. Locked.

Back door. Locked.

The attic hatch in the hallway looked… wrong.

Not open. Just not perfectly flush.

Like it had been lifted and set back down slightly off-center.

The next morning there was a light dusting of insulation on the hallway floor.

Just enough that I noticed it when I stepped on it barefoot.

That’s when I called pest control.

I told them I thought I had raccoons.

Two guys came out that afternoon.

One of them pulled down the attic ladder and climbed halfway up with a flashlight.

He went quiet.

Completely quiet.

Then he slowly climbed back down and asked me to step outside.

When I came back in, everything was laid out on my kitchen counter.

A blanket.

A pillow.

Empty water bottles.

Protein bar wrappers.

A small camping lantern.

A screwdriver.

A plastic bucket.

Someone had made a place to sleep.

I remember laughing. Not because it was funny. It was just my brain short-circuiting.

I asked how long it looked like someone had been up there.

One of them said, “At least a few weeks.”

There were flattened areas in the insulation where someone had been lying down regularly.

There were food wrappers from brands I don’t buy.

There were cigarette butts in a plastic bag.

I don’t smoke.

I asked if they thought the person was still there.

They said they didn’t see anyone.

But the insulation near the attic opening had fresh impressions.

Like someone had recently shifted their weight.

I told them I never go up there.

One of them looked at the attic ladder and said, “It’s been used recently.”

The fingerprints were on the inside of the hatch.

Not from someone climbing up.

From someone climbing down.

Police came. They walked through the house. Checked the yard. Nothing.

No forced entry.

They think whoever it was either accessed the attic through an exterior vent or had been there before I moved in.

They didn’t find anyone.

Before I left to stay at a friend’s place, I checked my phone.

I don’t know why.

There were three photos in my camera roll that I did not take.

Three separate nights.

All timestamped around 3:12 a.m.

They’re dark.

Grainy.

But clear enough.

They’re of my bedroom.

Taken from near the ceiling.

In all three, I’m asleep.

In the last one, I’m facing the camera.

The attic has been cleared out. The hatch is now screwed shut.

I installed cameras in every room.

The police never called with an update.

They never found anyone.

I don’t know how many nights he stood over me.

I don’t know if he came down while I was awake.

I don’t know if he watched me every single night.

I only know that for weeks, someone was close enough to hear me breathing.

I still live here.

I don’t really sleep anymore.

Every sound above me feels deliberate.

And every time I wake up at 3 a.m., I check my phone before I check the ceiling.

I’m always afraid there’s going to be a fourth photo.
