Looks like I woke up again. A bit weaker today, everything is just a little blurry. There's an amusing look on the nurse's face as they see me typing away. They probably think I’m writing a letter for my relatives. I’ll let them believe that; it’s a sweet idea. Ok, let's see how far I get today. A lot has been coming back quickly. All the horrors I’ve forgotten circle around my bed as if to taunt me.

When I was a child, I went to a daycare. It was one of those big daycares that also offered preschool and kindergarten classes. I was there for three years. I don’t remember the day I noticed it; all I know is I was horrified by this little area next to the building. It was technically inside the fence of the playground, but no kids ever went there. The fence narrowed, and there was only 5 feet or so in between it and the building. In that area was a large tree. It wasn’t the tree that scared me. It was the dark, the shadow. The sun was completely drowned out by the trees' leaves. I would always avoid looking at it, not because I thought something was there, I knew there wasn’t. But because my brain always wondered, what if? What if I stare at that shady area behind the tree long enough that something moves? Something unknown twisting in the shadows. If that happened, I wouldn’t be able to tell myself I was scared for no reason. It would be real, and the fear would be justified. I hadn’t thought about it in a long time.

It was just me, Dr. Bershide, and a few other members of the team staying behind to monitor Ashley. The dark halls of the clinic were still. Beeps and humming of medical machines filled the air like children whispering secrets to one another. We all watched through a viewing window. A single faded blue bed where Ashley lay motionless.

“She’s stable. Not sure why she isn’t awake yet.” Dr. Bershide reported.

“Anybody want a coffee?”

 A member of the team asked quietly. No one responded. We were all focused on something, anything to take our minds off the letterboard which Bershide had hung on the far wall. It’s like we were trying to subtly convince one another that everything was normal, and in doing so, convince ourselves.

“It’s fascinating.” Bershide was looking over the notebook. “She must have been suffering from some sort of hallucination, an effect of anesthesia on a conscious mind. Could be something special.”

“Sounds like we discovered another street drug.” 

A failed attempt at levity. Bershide ignored the comment.

“I wonder what she saw, if she remembers it. Now that would truly be something.”

My skin felt cold and prickly at those words. As we observed her, I couldn’t help but notice how small she looked. The bedsheets cocooned her frail body. The pillow swallowed up her shaved head. The only thing truly pronounced on her pale and near featureless face was the deep blue bags under her eyes. She shifted in her bed.

“I think you’re up.” Bershide nodded at me.

I grabbed a clipboard and exhaled. I could feel the other researchers watching me. I didn’t show any hesitation. The fluorescent light pierced every corner of the room except for the dull shade of what would have been shadows. There wasn’t much in the room: a table, a chair, me, and Ashley in her bed.

“Ashley?”

I approached her slowly.

“You remember me, Ashley?”

Her eyes flickered and danced around the room before finding mine. She nodded slowly, still only half conscious. I sat down and looked over my clipboard.

“I want to start by saying your surgery was successful.”

Ashley shook her head.

“No,” she mumbled.

The veins in her hands pressed against her gray skin. She gripped the blanket tight to her side. She looked like an oil painting left in the rain.

“No,” she croaked out the words, her eyes looking beyond me at the far wall.

“No, what?” I asked.

She shook her head. “I’m not mad at you, you didn’t know what you were doing, but I wish I hadn’t woken up.”

I looked at my clipboard, then went back to Ashley. I turned and peered through the viewing window, unsure of what to do next.

“If I could just ask you some questions, Ashley, do you know what day it is?”

She nodded.

“It’s Thursday.”

“What month?”

“I’m still cognitive. My mind is fine, it’s May 12th.” She shot me a stern look.

“Do you remember waking up while you were under anesthesia? It should have felt like you were conscious but unable to move.”

Ashley shook her head. Tears were beginning to form and slide their way down her cheeks.

“That’s not what it was… oh my god.” Her voice cracked slightly. “My boys, please no.”

I felt the hair on my spine stand up.

“Your boys?”

Ashley had a look of pain spread across her face. She was bright red as her lip curled down and her eyes squeezed shut.

“What’s wrong, Ashley? Please talk to me.”

“That’s not what it was.” She yelped, her eyes still shut. “It didn’t feel like that; it was hopeless, and I saw it. I didn’t just see it; I… Understood it.”

 Her words were choppy now in between the sobs. I rubbed the back of my neck and looked away, a heavy and gross feeling filling my gut like I had eaten a bag of expired meat.

“Ashley, it’s ok, we believe you may have hallucinated while you were under.”

Ashley shook her head from side to side.

“No,” she whimpered.

My head dropped. This was unbearable, her despair, her sadness. Watching a woman who just a couple of weeks ago was so lively and bright be brought to such a state. I looked away, trying to compose myself. Behind the viewing window, Bershide was scribbling gleefully in his research notes.

“You don’t believe me,” she said, turning her head away from mine. She gazed into the wall, as if to hide her face completely.

I exhaled, “Why don’t we try this later…” I began to stand.

“No!”

Ashley screamed with an intensity that made me jump.

“No, please! No! You can’t leave me alone in here, you can’t leave me with it.”

“It’s ok…” I stammered, putting my hands up as if to defend myself from her pleading shouts.

“No!” Ashley shook her head again. “It’s in here, it wants to come closer, it wants to, but it doesn’t want to get close to the other one. It doesn’t want to get close to your one. They stay away from each other. Please don’t leave me.”

I could feel my heart sink. My hands and feet went cold.

The way she said, ‘your one’, the way her eyes flicked behind me when she said it. I looked around the room. Nothing had changed. It was still the empty white room swimming in fluorescent light.

“Ashley, there’s nothing here,” I tried to speak calmly, but my hands and voice were shaking.

Her eyes didn’t leave the corner.

“I couldn’t see them till I opened my eyes. I opened my eyes, and now they are there. I think they have always been there, just waiting.”

She slowed her breathing, and I felt a cold chill overtake the room. Her eyes shot up at me. Eyes that felt foreign, eyes that felt wrong. As if they were plucked out of another animal and put into her skull.

“It was there under that playground tree.”

“What?”

My voice shot out sharply. I knew what she meant. Her eyes stared through mine, pulling that memory to the front of my skull.

“It was there. You knew it as a kid, you were more aware then, but as you got older, you ignored it.”

She twisted slightly against her bedsheets. Her eyes squeezed shut as tears overwhelmed her.

“Every creak, every cold spot, dark corner that made you uneasy. You ignored it, but it stayed with you, even now.”

I felt my breath be taken from me. It felt like I was a bug trapped inside a vacuum.

“Where is it?” I whispered, as if I could hide my voice from whatever Ashley was describing.

“It’s in your shadow.”

I felt small. Goosebumps coated my skin. I could feel the sensation of something behind me, but I didn’t turn around. It was like I was a kid again, scared of a simple patch of shade, terrified of what my mind conjured up in the soft darkness. A darkness which held all possibilities somewhere between its dull obscurity and the vibrancy of human imagination. I didn’t turn around, not because I thought something was there, I knew there wasn’t. But what if?

Ashley groaned and sat up in bed, her body wincing at her quick movements.

“No, not me, not yet!” she stammered.

Her voice grew more panicked. Her back shot straight up with almost unnatural speed.

She scrambled off her bed, her body slapping the floor like wet laundry. Her limbs sprawled out and clawing away from the corner her eyes had been fixed on. She shrank herself into the wall like a frightened child.

“My kids! I need more time!”

She hunched over herself, face tucked between her knees, and began vomiting. I turned to the viewing window.

“Do something!” I shouted, angry at the idea of Bershide being content just to watch.

I suppose I was just as unhelpful as him at that moment. I was frozen in a state beyond fear. Like a man watching a skyscraper tip over toward him. Horrified and perplexed by the gravity and size of the moment.

Ashley’s face shot up from her knees, looking up at something unseen looming over her. I will never forget her face. Pale and hallowed. She stared at it, and for a second, I felt something. A dread so deep it felt ancient. Like something in my chest was being ground down with dull teeth. Not fear, not pain. Hopelessness, like a chained animal staring down a butcher’s blade. The light was directly above her; there shouldn’t have been any shadows where she was, and yet, the area around her was just a bit darker. Through that veil of shade, I saw Ashley’s face wither.  It was like watching all life leave her, the same way juice runs from a crushed orange.

Then her body fell over, folding in on itself in an uncomfortable contortion. She was limp, motionless. A small stream of blood trickled from her mouth and pooled around her face. The nurses ran in and grabbed her. I was frozen in shock. As they put her on the stretcher, I saw her face. A white canvas streaked in blood, and under that crimson red was a twisted look of terror, a look of heartbreak. She was dead.

I haven’t remembered that day in so many years. I felt it then, as I do now. That hungry presence. Ashley and I were not alone in the room when she died, and I’m not alone in this room now.

I can’t fight sleep much longer. I need to wake up one last time. I need to finish this story.
