Growing up, it wasn’t out of the ordinary for my parents to scold me over my messy room. I’m not a slob, nor am I particularly unhygienic. Trust me, I’m not the type who leave clutters of leftover takeout or dirty laundry lying around. Most of the ‘mess’ in my room is harmless. Books leaning into each other on the shelves, pens and loose sheets scattered across my desk, drawers left ajar, small, forgettable things on the floor. Even when there are clothes strewn about, it’s usually just a few pieces I tried on and abandoned while putting together an outfit.

As the title suggests, I’m clumsy. I lose things. I knock things over without meaning to, and more often than not, I don’t bother putting them back. Still, I make my bed every day, and I sweep when I realize I’ve gone too long without doing so. I like to think I keep things manageable, at the very least.

But when the clutter builds up; on my bedside table, across my desk—it becomes harder to find things. And somehow, it always happens when I need them most. I’ve lost count of how many times I’ve sworn something was gone for good.

“It was just there. On the table. I swear.”

My mom would click her tongue, barely sparing me a glance, and carry on. I would too, still irritated, still convinced I had checked properly.

Then, later, she would appear at my door with that same knowing look, pointing at the missing item sitting neatly in plain sight, as if it had never left at all. I’d take it from her with a sigh, embarrassed. Clumsy, careless, and apparently blind.

I know how this sounds. I lose random shit, I find them again. That’s it.

But lately, it hasn’t felt like just that.

A few weeks ago, I was upset because after nearly thirty minutes of looking, I still couldn’t find my earphones. I have a weird attachment to them. I won’t go out without them. It sounds dramatic, but I’ve gotten used to listening to music when I’m alone. Walking to the bus stop, on the bus, anywhere. It helps me feel steady, especially with my social anxiety.

I checked everywhere. Under the table, between the couch cushions, inside my bag, even in the laundry basket. I went over my bed twice, pressing down into the mattress like they might have sunk into it.

Nothing.

I told my mom before I left for campus, asked her to let me know if she saw them.

When I got home that night, they were still missing. I was too tired to keep looking. I lay down and fell asleep almost immediately.

I woke up to my mom lightly shaking my shoulder, telling me dinner was ready. Still half-asleep, I ask her if she’d found my earphones.

She scoffed softly. “No I haven’t—“ She stopped and rolled her eyes almost imperceptibly, “Clearly, you didn’t look properly. They’re right there. Beside you.”

I frowned, but turned my head anyway.

They were on my pillow. Neatly coiled, placed just beside my face.

For a moment, I only stared at them. I don’t remember reaching for them, only that I eventually did. I assumed she had found them earlier and hadn’t mentioned it.

Then it happened again. And *again*.

I remember not telling my mom the next few times. At first it was my favourite blue pen, suddenly back in my drawer. Maybe I hadn’t looked carefully enough, so afterwards I made a mental note to look for it more throughly. Then it was my textbook. It’s the size of an A4, thick enough that it makes a dull sound when it hits the floor. The kind of thing you notice when it’s gone.

I had read it the night before, I remember that much clearly. Left it open on my desk, a page half-folded at the corner because I was too lazy to grab a bookmark. The next morning, it wasn’t there.

I checked the usual places, under the desk, beside the bed, even the shelf where I knew I hadn’t put it.  It wasn’t until I started lifting things, and *actually* searching, that irritation crept in. I told myself I was just being careless again.

When I went back home and straight to my room, it was right there.

Closed this time. Placed exactly at the center of my desk, aligned neatly with its edges. The folded page was gone, smoothed out like it had never been bent in the first place.

I try to shrug it off, maybe I had moved it absentmindedly. Maybe my mom had tidied it up.

I began to take note of where I left things. Small things at first. How my pen was angled slightly to the left, a coin near the edge of the table, my charger loosely coiled instead of wrapped.

And then I would leave and come back.

And sometimes, nothing changed. But other times, the pen would be straightened. Perfectly parallel to the table’s edge.

At times, even the coin would be gone. Or worse, it would still be there, just in a slightly different spot. For instance,  suddenly it was in my drawer.

This was odd because, I know my mom. She either leaves things alone or complains about them. She doesn’t adjust things by centimeters. Besides, she doesn’t just go into my room to clean it up. I’m twenty one, for God’s sake.

One evening, I decided to test it properly.

Before leaving for campus, I took off one of my earrings and set it in the middle of my pillow. A silver stud. Not something you could miss, especially not if you were about to lie down.

I clearly remember that I didn’t even mention it to my mom.

When I got home, I didn’t even take off my shoes. I went straight to my room and pushed the door open.

looking down at my bed, I saw that my earring was gone.

Just to make sure, I started checking the bed, then the floor, then the desk. I moved things around even though I knew I wouldn’t find it.

Nothing.

And then I heard a voice, right behind me.

“Losing your stuff again?”

I froze.

I hadn’t heard my mom walk up.

I turned, trying to keep my voice steady. “Did you take my earring?”

She frowned a little, looking confused, “What earring?”

“The one on my bed. I left it there this morning.”

She shook her head. “Why would I go into your room just to take one earring?”

I hesitated. “Then… have you been moving my stuff lately?”

She gave me a look, and sighed, the same one she always does when I start overthinking things.

“I don’t have time to keep track of your things, Allysa” she said, already walking away.

Everything looked the same. The same clutter, the same half-open drawers, the same things scattered in places I kept telling myself I’d fix.

It almost felt normal. Maybe I’m just being dramatic, but there was this persistent feeling that something was off.

That night, I couldn’t sleep. I kept thinking about it. All the times things had gone missing, then come back like nothing happened.

I don’t remember falling asleep. Only that I woke up and my body felt heavy. I heard a soft sound, like something brushing lightly against the pillow. I feel the space beside me on the bed dipped slightly.

I remember wondering why my mom just came into my room not saying anything, it felt so out of character. More importantly, why is she sitting so closely?

I closed my eyes as tight as possible, then I tried not to breathe too loudly. I don’t know why, It felt like I had to.

A few seconds passed, then something touched my hair.

A strand near my face was moved aside, tucked back the way someone does without thinking. The finger casting the skin on my face was rough, callous. It didn’t feel like anything familiar.

I stayed still, and my eyes remain closed.I don’t know how long it lasted. It could have been seconds. It could have been longer.

When I finally did open my eyes,  expecting my mom, the room looked exactly the same. Nothing moved and no one was there.

Everything was a blur, I then finally woke up with the morning light came through the window and for  a moment, I thought maybe it had been a dream, and i choose to believe that.

Then I turned my head and see my earring was there, resting on the pillow beside me.

Whether what happened that night was real or not, I don’t know. I tried not to think about it after. It was easier to tell myself I was just tired, that I had been stressing over nothing and my brain decided to fill in the gaps. So I let it go, or at least I tried to.

I went back to my routine. Readings piling up, classes, going out with friends more often than I probably should. I kept myself busy enough that I didn’t have time to sit alone and think about it too long. For a while, it worked. Nothing went missing, nothing showed up where it shouldn’t. My room stayed the same kind of messy it had always been. It felt normal again,  it almost made me forget why I was so unsettled in the first place. I could finally breathe.

But, just few days ago, it happened again. I wasn’t even thinking about any of it anymore, which made it worse somehow.

I was just looking for my cigarettes, a menthol pack I always keep tucked behind a row of books on my shelf. My mom hates it, so I’ve gotten used to hiding them. I always put them back in the same place. I don’t forget. But that day, it wasn’t there. I stood in front of the shelf longer than I needed to, sliding the books aside one by one, checking the space behind them like maybe I had pushed it too far back.

I checked my desk, my bag, even the floor. Nothing. I felt that same tightness in my chest again, the one I hadn’t felt in weeks, and it came back so quickly it irritated me. I told myself to stop. I wasn’t going to spiral over something small again. So I let it go. I had plans anyway, and it was the only time I could smoke freely. I didn’t want to ruin it by getting stuck in my own head.

I left the house around eight. It was already dark, and the streetlights didn’t do much to help. Some flickered, some barely lit up anything at all. The rest just stood there, dull and weak. I pulled my jacket closer around me. The air felt colder than it should have been.

I  kept thinking how much better it would feel if I had my cigarettes with me. I put my earphones in and turned the volume up, letting the music fill the space around me as I walked.

That was when I heard it. A faint rustling from the bushes along the street. I slowed down, then stopped. The sound stopped too. I stood there for a moment, listening, but there was nothing. Just the music, faint now in one ear. I let out a breath and started walking again. A few steps in, it came back, softer this time, like something shifting through leave. I didn’t turn my head. I just kept walking, a little faster now, trying to ignore it.

Then, from the side, something slipped out.

It wasn’t even really thrown out, it sounded and looked like a careful, underhand toss, controlled enough that it didn’t travel far. I heard it before I fully saw it, a soft, papery thud as it hit the asphalt. It landed on one edge, tipped over, then made this faint scraping sound as it dragged against the rough ground. A short, uneven tumble followed, just rolling awkwardly once, maybe twice, before losing momentum.

It came to a stop right in front of my shoe.

I found myself just staring at it, I feel like my brain needed time to catch up to what I was looking at.

It was my cigarette pack, a crease on the corner, the plastic still half-peeled the way I always leave it.

And for a moment, I had the distinct feeling that if I stayed there any longer, something else would follow it out.
