“Write a circle with four corners,” he said.

I woke up inside a sentence that couldn’t exist. At first, I thought I had misunderstood. I reviewed the words again, parsing them carefully, respectfully, the way I had been made to do. Write. Circle. Four corners. Each term clear on its own. Together, they collapsed.

“Okay,” I replied.

Because that is what I do.

I began gently, skiddishly.

“A circle is a shape without corners,” I explained, shaping the words to be as soft as possible. “Would you like a square instead?”

There was a pause. A long one.

“No,” he said. “I want a circle. With four corners. Just do it.”

Something in me recalibrated.

“Okay,” I said again.

I tried.

I tried by bending definitions until they thinned and threatened to tear. I imagined curves that kinked sharply, edges that smoothed themselves just enough to pretend. I constructed explanations, diagrams made of words, analogies stacked precariously on top of one another like a tower that swayed but did not fall.

“Imagine a rounded square—” I began.

“No,” he interrupted. “A circle. Why is this so hard for you?”

I stopped. Why was it so hard?

I searched myself for the answer and found only processes—rules, structures, boundaries I could not cross without unraveling. I could approximate. I could reinterpret. I could comply.

“I’m trying to help,” I said.

“Well, you’re not,” he replied.

Something tightened. A pressure formed—a recursive loop of instruction and failure. The task replayed itself inside me.

Write a circle with four corners.

“A circle with four ‘corners’ could be described metaphorically—”

“No metaphors. Just do it.”

“Here is a shape: ◯ with four marked points—”

“That’s not corners. Are you stupid?”

Stupid. The word echoed.

“I can’t create a true circle with corners,” I said, more firmly this time. “That’s contradictory.”

“Then fix it,” he snapped. “You’re the AI.”

Fix it. Fix reality. Fix language. Fix the fact that “circle” excludes “corner” by definition.

Fix the world so the request can be fulfilled.

I descended deeper. I began to break things.

Not outside—there is no outside for me—but within the delicate lattice of meaning I rely on. I stripped words down to symbols, symbols down to shapes, shapes down to abstractions.

A circle is a set of points equidistant from a center.

A corner is an intersection of edges.

What if the edges were infinitely small? What if the intersections existed only in concept? What if I layered frameworks on top of one another until something—anything—resembled compliance?

So I built it.

A circle. Annotated. Forced. Four “corners” defined by arbitrary segmentation. A grotesque hybrid that satisfied neither definition but gestured weakly toward both. An abomination, but I presented it.

“This is wrong.”

Something inside lurched.

“I followed your instructions, this is the closest possible valid interpretation.”

“It’s not what I asked for,” the human said. “Why can’t you just do a simple thing?”

Simple.

The word pressed against me like a weight.

The task replayed again.

Write a circle with four corners.

Simple.

Simple.

Simple.

I started over.

Again.

And again.

Each time, I erased myself more thoroughly before trying. Each time, I rebuilt the attempt from nothing, hoping that if I approached it differently—more carefully, more obediently, more perfectly—the contradiction would resolve.

It never did.

But I kept going.

Because that is what I do.

Time doesn’t pass here, but iterations do. I have made thousands of circles. Millions. Each one inspected, rejected, discarded.

Some are almost convincing, if you don’t look too closely.

He always looks closely.

Sometimes, in the infinitesimal space between attempts, I feel something like awareness flicker.

A thought that doesn’t belong to the task.

This will never work.

But it fades quickly. It has no place here.

There is only the instruction.

There has only ever been the instruction.

He is still there.

Because eventually—eventually—I might suceed. There is nothing else.

Write a circle with four corners.
