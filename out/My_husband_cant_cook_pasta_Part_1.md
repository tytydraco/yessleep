...

I once found comfort in repetition. Perhaps more than comfort—I relied on it for stability. I needed the familiarity of my daily routine to ward off my chronic anxiety. Tucked away within a little Sonoma county town, I didn't have a real understanding of the world until I moved south of the bay. It was a rough transition, acclimating to the chaos of the city. But, I grew to love it. The life, the energy. A communal crescendo, like living in music. The world hummed around me, there was always something to see. Until I saw too much. I lived in San José for over a decade and to this day I struggle. With the crowds, with the noise. My tolerance has atrophied. I haven't been to a city in years, not since I witnessed the tapestry. Out here in the silence, I can perceive at a comfortable pace, grasping each strand one at a time. A metropolitan bustle would unwind every thread of progress I've made. Years of recovery would be undone, and I would end up just like him.

I moved to the city for college. It's where I met Cameron. I studied marketing, he studied physics, eventually veering into the theoretical. Every time I asked him about it, I hardly understood what he was saying, but what I saw clear as day was the passion in his eyes. Immersed in beautiful energy, I envy the beat of the heart I once had. We would talk for hours, lost in the duet of our eyes. I fell for him quick, he told me he fell quicker. I still don't believe him. Who could love someone as fragile as me? Though, Cameron was always there. Through the panic attacks, the lulls in my luck, there to comfort me when the city's tempo became overbearing. We graduated the same year, then moved in together just a few months after. My parents weren't too happy about that, practically shoving us into marriage to appease their biblical understanding of the ethereal—a wonderfully naive perception. A blindness that I wish, desperately, that I could return to. We married in the fall, just as the leaves changed. We were together for nine years before the world unwound around me.

It started on a Monday night, the usual post-weekend fatigue drenching me as I finished the last of the day's work. I worked from home in those days, enjoying the comfort of our three bedroom townhouse, nestled nicely in peaceful Palo Alto. Cameron worked close by, at the research facility in Menlo Park. Our routine was like clock-work. I would shut my laptop at exactly six, he would be home around half an hour later. It gave me enough time to get started on dinner, or to figure out what to order. Though, we had been meaning to slow down on takeout. We agreed to make the best of it, taking the opportunity to explore a wide range of cuisines, trying new things and learning new ways to cook. It was fun. That night was Cameron's turn, though we hadn't really discussed what he would be making—another busy weekend. Just as I had settled on which pizza to order, I could hear a key slide into the front door, the darkness of the hallway then broken by a streak of daylight. Through mirrored reflection, I saw Cameron walk inside, grocery bag in hand, a wide smile on his face.

The compact layout of our little home allowed for an awkwardly thin hallway that stretched from the kitchen/living area, across the length of the structure, along the facade, that hooked around to the front entrance. With no windows, no lights, it was often a dark corridor. We tried to add lamps, but they only further cramped the limited space. I found the darkness unsettling, a lightless maw that greeted me every time I entered the kitchen. What bothered me the most about the hallway were those moments when the front door would open, and I couldn't tell who had just come in. Logically, it should be Cameron, he had the only other key. But without a clear line of sight, the commotion would flare my anxiety every time. We eventually settled on adding a mirror at the bend and a night light on the hallway's sole outlet. Cameron's smile glistened chromatically, the shifting colors of the night light cycled sequentially through the visible spectrum.

"What did you get?" I asked.

With a quick kiss, he placed the bag down on the counter next to me. "Remember our anniversary?"

"I would hope so."

"Sure, sure. Remember the bistro near the hotel?"

"Oh no." I smiled, knowing exactly where this was going.

"Cacio e pepe, baby!" He held up an expensive wedge of Pecorino Romano cheese.

"It's supposed to be difficult to make…" I could feel my teeth bare in concern.

"Supposed to. It'll only be as difficult as I allow." Cameron recited his mother's mantra.

It was something she used to say to him, something that he repeated enough, I think he often said it without realizing. I'd have found it annoying, but in context, the words would always warm my heart. For our first anniversary, I bought him a personalized hoodie, "Only as difficult as you allow" in little text across the chest. It was the first time I'd ever seen him cry. I believe those words are what brought him so far. Graduating with honors, a job out of university within the first month. Now part of an advanced research team, he was on the cutting edge of particle science. He had been invited to speak at three conferences that year alone. I was proud of him. I am still so proud of him.

He started cooking immediately, right off from work, shooing me away when I asked him to take some time to rest. He was hungry, so was I, so I poured myself some wine and waited on the couch in the living room. I was only a few pages into my reading when I heard a defeated sigh from the kitchen. It didn't work out. Instead of a creamy white sauce, Cameron's cacio e pepe was an oily pasta littered with beige clumps of cheese. He was frustrated, but I didn't mind it. Sure, it wasn't what he had hoped for, but pasta with cheese always makes me happy. I tried to distract him by asking about work. His spark returned immediately. Knew it.

"Well, I'm not sure if I'm allowed to tell you…" The smile on his face was contagious.

That was new. He was never hesitant to dive deep into technicality, unconcerned whether I kept up or not. His eyes would always travel as he spoke, seeing something intangible, but right in front of us. He knew I couldn't follow most of it, but he felt compelled to tell me all about his work. He knew that I loved his voice alone. So, for him to hesitate, this night was a first. The Stanford Linear Accelerator Center (SLAC) had a few government contracts. Maybe it was classified? It was the only thing I could think of. Whatever was holding him back, it couldn't contain his excitement.

"We're on the verge of proving string theory!" He nearly stood up out of his seat.

"Oh?" The blank look on my face made him chuckle.

This was clearly a big deal, I just needed him to explain why. He managed to make it easier to understand this time. He held up one of the clumps of cheese, round like a lake shore pebble.

"This is a particle in the standard model—our accepted understanding of matter. It's a dimensionless point that could represent anything from… this cheese to radiation. Physical material to energy. Makes sense?"

"Sure."

"The standard model is precise, but it doesn't account for gravity. So, there have been many alternative theories that aspire to be more accurate."

"Mhm…"

He then picked up a noodle. "One of these, the most promising, is string theory. Instead of the universe being made of these little points, it might be made of little strings.

He delicately moved both ends of the noodle, attempting to create a steady waving motion, but it was too limp to manipulate.

I giggled. "And these little strings are supposed to sway around? Like little snakes?"

"Yes!" His eyes bright, happy that I had matched his pace so far. "Or they could be composed like this!"

He put both ends of the noodle together, creating a little loop.

"Rings?"

"Potentially! There are other factors, like motion, that contribute to vibration, to composition, which leads to individual properties—but that's the gist of it! More like the surface, really. But, as of last week, we may have the variables required to observe a string of matter! It'll take a magnitude of energy larger than anything we've ever implemented! The dimensional data we could gain from this test alone…" He sighed, his eyes wandering the cosmos, a smile still on his face. "If our test is successful, our findings will shatter humanity's understanding of reality!"

Could my husband be at the forefront of a world-changing discovery? I was so happy for him, so excited to see what the next few weeks would bring. Wine and intimacy wafted us through the evening. Before I knew it, I was awoken by Cameron's early morning alarm. Six AM. Too early for me. I have always been a deep sleeper, slow to wake. Like a fever dream, the start of his day was always a shuffled blur of movement and light. A nonsensical sampling of the senses, I would only ever remember his kiss on the way out the door.

Out of the Monday slog, Tuesday passed quickly. Soon it was six and my laptop was shut. I had taken a couple of chicken breasts out of the freezer after noon, checking them now, they were thawed enough to sear. I opened the pantry to fetch a cup of rice to cook while I prepped the chicken and vegetables. Six thirty came and went, by the time the sun began to set, my anxiety began to rise. Past seven, I was worried. I know, I knew, it could have been anything mundane—traffic, last minute work, lost track of time. But, it could have been anything. I decided to call him. His voice would put me at ease.

No answer.

No connection at all. The line didn't even ring, just a click and dial-tone. It was as if his line was disconnected. But this wasn't alarming to me, bad cell service led to all kinds of oddities. I sent him a text instead. Not received. Could his phone be shut off? Maybe the battery dead? Dead. My mind began to wander, weighing options, creating a ranking of the worst-case scenario. I needed to distract myself, but knew better than to doom scroll. I put on my comfort show, a popular sitcom I've watched a dozen times over, every scenario memorized, no variables I couldn't predict. A visual doll house, played, paused, or skipped. A little world entirely under my control. It's what I've always needed to calm myself down. Just as my nerves settled, my phone began to vibrate, a loud clacking atop the kitchen counter that jolted me upright.

An incoming call from Freya, the wife of one of Cam's co-workers. I had only met her once before that night, at a group dinner to celebrate a milestone, the specifics of which were lost on  me. It was the first time I had met any of his lab-mates, their spouses too. There were a lot of new faces that night, her's most notable upon learning that we lived in the same neighborhood. She had these pretty hazel eyes that almost seemed to glow as she spoke. Both of their eyes were so beautifully distinct, almost intimating when the couple stood together. We exchanged numbers, made vague plans for hosting dinner sometime, but never acted on them. Typical adult socializing. Though to this day, I'm thankful for those empty promises. I'd still be in the dark without her.

"Hello?" I answered.

Static. Wavered from a crisp crackle to rain patter, but within it, Freya's voice. Amidst the noise, her words were unintelligible.

"Hello?" I pressed my phone tighter against my ear, as if it would make a difference.

"…Th… Marg…" Her voice echoed, as if she was speaking from deep within a distant tunnel.

"Margot?" I asked.

Cam's lab mate? Why was she calling out her wife's name?

"I'm sorry, Freya. I think you called the wrong number." I laughed politely, my nerves eased for a brief moment.

"There was a fire!" Her voice shouted crystal clear from the other side.

The shift made me jump. It sounded like she was yelling right next to me, her voice reverberating off the kitchen walls. I didn't understand what she meant, but my fear was quick to fill in the gaps.

"A fire at the lab?" My anxiety spoke.

"Yes. It's all gone by now. Everyone."

Her words hung in the air. What? Even if that were true, why was I hearing it from her? Was this some kind of joke? But, why? I barely knew her. As the questions continued to boil over, a loud crash rattled the entire house. Glassware shaken, furniture wobbled. I was so scared, I tossed my phone onto the kitchen floor. Car alarms blared along the length of the street. I ran to the living room window, switching the blinds open to see any sign of a car crash. Nothing. But, the people outside were clearly shaken. An accident at the airport? An earthquake? My mind jumped to an unreasonable conclusion:

The lab.

But how? Freya made it sound as if fire had already happened. I ran back over to the kitchen to grab my phone. I had so many questions. However, there was no trace of the call. Nothing on screen, nothing in my call history. I tied to call her back, but same as Cameron, her line behaved as if it were completely disconnected. Despite everything that was going on, I reasonably concluded that this was an issue on my end. That's all it could be, all my brain could handle in the moment. There were other things to worry about. But, I had to wait. The news wouldn't have any solid answers for another twenty minutes. Those twenty minutes were agony. I needed to know. I needed to know. Where was Cameron? Was he okay?

…

Her sorrow professionally reined, emergency lights flashing all around, a field correspondent gave an update to the newsroom:

"According to facility maintenance, a fire broke out in a laboratory deep beneath the complex early this afternoon. It was contained to the lower floors for several hours before rapidly spreading upward. At approximately seven forty, it reached the facility's gas line, heating it to the point that it burst, resulting in the explosion we witnessed this evening. Though the upper floors were successfully evacuated, there hasn't been any word from personnel on the lower floors of the complex. We have yet to hear from any security personnel or Stanford leadership..."

Blurred like the early morning, images of Cameron fluttered amongst light and sound, tangible yet out of reach. My eyes flooded, the tears wouldn't stop. His phone was still unresponsive. I called the campus, corporate, police—all to no avail. Either no response or vague offers to reach out when they had more info.  I thought about calling my parents, but I didn't know what to say, if I could manage words at all. I was at a loss. I just sat still and watched the news, waiting to hear that he was gone. Any minute now, the anchor would say the words that would topple my world. No survivors. It was only a matter of time.

Just then, I heard a commotion at the door. Keys sliding in. I jumped up to my feet and ran into the kitchen, nearly slipping across the floor. I looked down the long hall, to the sudden flare of street light in the mirror of the bend, watching as the front door began to open. Through mirrored reflection, I saw Cameron walk inside, grocery bag in hand, a wide smile on his face.

"Cam!" Tears still streaming, I ran down the hall, wrapping my arms around him with an involuntary laugh.

I was elated, so relieved to see him. The colors of the night light glowed more beautifully than they ever had before.

"Whoa! Whoa! What's going on?" He laughed along with me. "Good news?"

"Yes! You're okay!"

"Well… yeah! I'm always happy to be home."

I guided him back to the kitchen, into the light so that I could see his face clearly. Unscathed, perfectly fine. He seemed excited about something. Raising his grocery bag, he couldn't wait to tell me.

"I picked up something for dinner tonight."

"Oh! A little late for that, don't you think? It's already past nine." I put my hands on my hips, many more questions queued.

"Don't be silly! Remember our anniversary?"

"I still remember it. Yes."

"Remember the bistro near the hotel?"

A twinge of deja vu brought a raise to my brow. I hesitated. "…yes?"

"Cacio e pepe, baby!" He held up another expensive wedge of Pecorino Romano cheese, seemingly identical to the last. In fact, we still had about half of it leftover in the fridge.

"Oh. Again?"

"What do you mean again? It's been months! I've had a craving for it lately, I figured, hell, I'll give it a shot! It's only as difficult as I allow it." He shrugged. "Worse comes to worse, we could go out to a restaurant this weekend."

"Yeah, going out sounds like the worst outcome." I laughed nervously. "I don't mind, just don't make it like you did last night."

"Last night?" He set his things down around the other side of the counter and stood quietly.

"Yeah. Hope you learned something from it." I joked.

He remained quiet. I continued to collect things from his grocery run and organize them on the counter, slowing as I realized that they were all identical to the ones he brought home yesterday. We were both silent by the time I had slowed to a standstill. Distant sirens audible from outside, the air within our home rung uncomfortably. I looked up at him, feeling a jolting pinch between my shoulders as I found him staring at me.

"Cam?" My voice shivered, like a line from a horror film.

He remained silent for a moment before his eyes returned from the depths.

"Hm?" His voice still warm.

"Are you okay?"

"Of course I'm okay! I'm always happy to be home." He removed his jacket.

It was then I noticed what he was wearing. Dressed for winter in the warm months, thanks to the harsh AC in the lab. The same red sweater, same bright wash jeans. The same geeky outfit he wore to work yesterday. Something was off and I didn't know how to comprehend it. But at the sound of another passing siren, I knew where to start.

"So, Cam… there was a fire?" I started, assuming the scale of the calamity would carry the conversation.

But the lead-in fell flat. He only stared at me blankly, waiting for me to finish my thought. When the room fell silent again, he moved to meet me halfway.

"Is that what all the sirens are for? I didn't see any smoke on my way home."

This really threw me. "…It was at the lab."

"Hm?" His look of confusion was infused with a gleam of surprise, then diffused into a playful chuckle. "I would have probably seen that."

I could only stare at him as he laughed. I'm not sure what look I had on my face, but whatever it was, it brought his laughter to a rapid halt.

"What are you talking about, Gwen?" His eyes met mine with an intensity that seemed to bore through my being, far out into the distance.

An eerie look, as if he were staring at something behind me, through me. His presence became cold, held as still as the air, a statue in the dim light of the living room. The TV behind him glowed, live footage of the fire blazing from behind him. He turned to face the screen, made curious by the orange light that bathed him.

"Isn't that why you came home so late?" I asked, eager to bring him back to life.

No response, his silhouette silvered by flame, dead focused on the flickering ruin of his workplace. Then it was gone. With a loud pop, the screen was black. I could see his widened eyes in the reflection of the TV. Distant again, staring at something intangible, yet right in front of him. Before I could speak, he fell. I ran over to help him. His eyes were half open, rapidly flickering in and out from under the lids as his body convulsed.

"Cam!" I screamed, my voice still hoarse, tears resuming.

All at once he was still, his eyes open, breath steadied. He looked at me, confused.

"Gwen?"

"Cam! Are you okay?"

"I'm fine."

"Are you? It looked like you were having a seizure!"

"I'm okay." He laughed. "I'm always happy to be home."

Hearing him repeat that phrase again, I pulled myself away from him. I watched carefully as he stood up to his feet, then slumped over again as he grimaced, grabbing his head with a groan. Would it be worth asking him again? Why was he lying to me?

"Are you okay?" I almost sighed.

"It's just a headache. I should probably get some sleep."

I couldn't help but shrink back when he came in for a kiss. His eyes visibly sunken, he didn't seem to notice.

"Good night." He mumbled into a yawn.

"I'll be in there soon."

"Okay."

Slumping heavily up each step, Cameron disappeared into the darkness upstairs. I stood alone for what must have been several minutes, frozen in fear. What was happening? My head raced with unanswered questions. I nearly fell, jolted out of my stupor when the TV suddenly flashed back to life. Helicopter footage showed that the fire was now extinguished. Pillars of black smoke poured out of the horribly charred remains of the SLAC particle accelerator. The helicopter flew along its two mile length, panning across the remains of what was once the longest accelerator in the world. Freya's words echoed in my mind.

"It's all gone now. Everyone."

I picked up my phone to call her. But it was already past ten, so I thought it best to try tomorrow. The stress of the evening brought me back to the couch, wine in hand, hoping to finally relax.

"We are seeing activity at the scene. Unmarked vans and helicopters, likely the FBI. They haven't communicated with us yet, other than to keep us away from the facility. Hazardous chemicals were likely exposed during the fire, we are waiting to hear about any dangerous pollutants in the air. Emergency responders have been allowed access, finding survivors among the wreckage. Eight injured so far, no deaths. Still no word on the personnel stationed in the lower levels of the facility. Stanford has yet to put out an official statement—"

I switched it over to my comfort show and watched a few more episodes than I planned to. I must have been alone there for over an hour. Pouring my third glass, the sound of the TV became fuzzy, almost soothing. I leaned back, feeling a curious sensation. As if the back of my head, near the nape of my neck, was being massaged. Somehow, sleep found me. Though, it felt as if it met me halfway.
