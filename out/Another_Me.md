 

Through the midnight moon, I see his face. I see him, in my restless dreams, on that silent night, in bleak December, staring. Dead in the eyes, with long hair of an inky black color, his skin a pallid white, wrinkly as if deceased. I write now, a husk of myself, afraid of what lay in the dark, in the night, what it is that looks like me, human, but not!

 

It began on a dark night in a gloomy November, the rain pattering on the walls. In my home, warmth was scarce, and as I sat in my study observing many a quaint volume of lore, a knock came from the front door.

  
“Who could that be, at this abominable hour?” Said I, rising from my place of study.

Despite my better judgement, I trudged to the door and peeked through the peephole out into the stormy night. On my doorstep was Dr. Toshenkov, an old and trusted acquaintance of mine, and a scientist of much renown throughout the City. He stood, despite the rain, with a smile on his face, one which sent many a shiver down my spine. Yet I thought of him, in the cold rain. He hadn’t a coat, nor an umbrella. Sympathy got the better of me, and I opened the door, its ancient hinges letting out a creak of protest.

“Dr. Toshenkov! What brings you here, on such a dreary night, to my chamber door?”

“Ah, Smith, *comrade,* I bring you tidings of great joy! For I have developed something to cure your ailment forevermore! Please, may I enter?”

Sympathy once more got the better of me. I allowed him inside my abode and called many servants to attend to his needs.

“Need you a drink, Toshenkov?”  Asked I.

“Not now, *comrade*, for I have matters of much importance to discuss with you.” Said he, still that smile on his face.

Just then, a horrid cough overtook me. I excused myself for a moment as I hacked and wheezed, blood spraying in small amounts. After a long moment, and many an attempt at looking presentable once more, I returned to him, and with a sigh of near exhaustion from my recent hacking, collapsed into the plush chair beneath me. Sensing I was in a state of being able to listen, he began to speak.

“Smith, *comrade*, that horrible ailment of yours shall trouble you no more! For I have developed a potent elixir, a cure for your disease. Nevermore shall you be diseased, my friend, for a drink of this, and you shall be well again.”

I sat in astonished silence for many a moment. Could it be true, I wondered?  That I could be cured with just a swig from a bottle? For years, I had suffered from a horrible illness. Could this have been my chance, to be free of it? I looked at the remaining blood from my coughing fit on my hands. Enough was enough, I thought. With grim determination, I looked Toshenkov in the eye. His smile never did fade.

“That is quite an offer you have made, Toshenkov. You say this elixir can cure my ailment?” Said I.

“That notion is affirmed.” Said he.

“And it is safe to consume?”

“Smith, do you not trust me? I would never present an unsafe medicine to you!”

In his eyes there lay what seemed to be genuine intention. An innocent, pure intention. And I could not resist those eyes. They were alluring in a manner which I cannot describe.

“Then,” said I, “I shall attempt this supposed cure.”

“Wonderful!” said he, his smile growing ever wider.

He rose from his place of rest and obtained a small wine glass. With motions of a most fluid nature, he poured a shot-sized serving into the glass, before presenting it to me. Taking it into my hands, I could feel the warmth emanating from the elixir through the glass, a most pleasant one at that, as if holding the hands before a fire. Oh, how irresistible that elixir was! It smelled most pleasant, like a tulip on a spring day. Bringing it to my lips, I glanced at him in my peripheral vision. He had poured himself a glass of wine and was approaching me. He raised his glass and spoke in a tone of much determination.

“A toast, to your future health, and to your happiness forevermore! Cheers!” Exclaimed he.

I raised my own glass and collided it gently against his with a soft clink. I then, without a flicker of hesitation, brought it to my lips, and began to drink. The taste of it was indescribable. It was a bitter, awful taste, enough that I nearly gagged on it.  Yet in my efforts to not appear rude, I did not express these feelings.  He left soon after, disappearing into the rainy night.

 

I did not think about it for some days. I awoke the next morning with an overwhelming relief in my chest, as if a weight had been lifted from my poor, diseased lungs. It had worked, I recall thinking to myself.  So, for some days, I continued with my life, yet feeling like a new man. This continued until the night of December 1^(st), when I was struck with a severe pain in my stomach. Believing I needed naught but bedrest, I recall retiring to my quarters at around eight in the evening. Yet when I awoke early that next morning, I found my window, which I directly recall closing before my retirement, open, and letting in a cold breeze. I thought nothing of it, however, and simply chose to believe it was the wind, and nothing more.

 

From then on, several instances occurred where I felt as if I was not alone, and was being watched inside of my own home. I felt the need to check behind each corner for reasons indescribable. But nothing was ever found, much to my continued paranoia.

 

My paranoia continued to rise until December 10^(th). A feeling, unlike any I have ever experienced, overtook me in the late hours. It was once more, a rainy night, but this time, the storm was of much greater intensity, so much so that I could hear the wind howling from outdoors, as if of some great beast. In my study I resided once more, continuing to read much of the forgotten lore I had read before the interruption from Dr. Toshenkov. Yet, unlike before, I felt as if something was staring at the back of my head. Watching me, awaiting me to make a move. Unable to shake this feeling, I slowly turned my head toward the window present behind me at the time.

And that is when I saw him.

 

Staring at me, in the stormy night, was a man! Yet not any man. He looked like me! Yet, there were striking differences. His hair was long and unkempt, much to the contrary of my own, and the irises of his eyes were a deep red, unnatural and perverse in their appearance. His skin seemed as if it did not fit on his body. It hung off of him in great bloody blobs. Yet despite it all, I saw myself in him. He looked almost exactly like me. He dressed in the same attire, moved in the same fluid manner, had my smile. Terror rushed through each vein in my body, and without a flicker of hesitation, I screamed, a raw, primal scream of terror, before retreating at great speed to my quarters.

 

Upon reaching my quarters, with great haste I shut all curtains of all windows in the room. Outside, despite the howling winds, I could hear him moving, his footsteps a soft clicking against the concrete outside. He seemed to move with no haste. I could not make out where he was. Breathing heavily, and my face perspiring, I remembered the double-barreled shotgun above the mantlepiece. It was of great age, but it was still usable, and I had shells hidden underneath my bed somewhere.

 

With slow movements as to not alert the creature outside to my position, I crawled slowly across the floor towards the mantlepiece. Agonizingly slow minutes passed, as at every sound I heard from the outside, I would stop, and hold still as statues, before continuing in a weeping angel-like manner upon concluding that it was safe. After many minutes of agonizing length, I finally reached the weapon. I reached up, again, slowly and quietly, before wrapping my hands around the barrel.

 

It was then that all hope of silence was lost. Dust from the weapon that had appeared over many generations of disuse came into my nostrils. Unable to control myself, I sneezed, loud enough to alert him to my position. In an instant, I could see his silhouette turn towards the source, and with inhuman speed and agility, he broke the window with naught but his fist. Shards of glass were implanted into his hand and forearm, yet he paid it no mind as he climbed into my quarters. It was then that I saw he was holding a weapon, a knife of extreme sharpness. And he charged, without hesitation towards I.

 

Terror gripped me. I could do nothing. I had dropped the weapon. Only then did I speak, my voice shaking.

“Haven’t you a mind? What are you, foul beast?”  
  
He stopped suddenly, mid-charge. In truth, I did not expect him to reply. Yet he did, his voice a horrible rasp. He stood still, his face shrouded in darkness.

“Another You.” Said he.

My breath hitched. I could not understand. Another me? What sorcery? His voice was calm, collected, a sharp contrast to my own, one of terror.



 No sooner did I faint than I found myself in the rain, outside. The storm battered against me, each drop striking like shards of glass. Yet amidst the chaos, I saw a light. At first, I dared to believe it peace. But I was mistaken. I approached, and soon discerned the source—a window, illuminated from within. Pressing my face against the pane, I beheld my own home. And there, within my study, sat Another Me. He perused my volumes of forgotten lore, calm and composed, before being overtaken by a horrid cough, blood spilling forth. My breath faltered. It was I. Just then, there came a rapping at the front door. He rose. “Who could that be, at this abominable hour?” said he. He approached the door… and peered through the peephole. Dr. Toshenkov stood beyond it.

 

Smiling.
