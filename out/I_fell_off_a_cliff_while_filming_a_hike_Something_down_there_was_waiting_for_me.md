I'm a 22-year-old adventurous vlogger. Or, at least, I was until yesterday.

I headed out to a jagged, sweeping national park to film a 24-hour survival challenge. I was in my element, joking to my camera that it was just me, the mountains, and enough SPF to embalm a mummy. The golden hour lighting was perfectly cinematic, but because I was walking backward to get the shot, I didn't see the ledge fracture.

I fell; rag-dolled down a steep, rocky embankment and slammed into the dirt of Deadfall Basin below.

A friendly hiker further down the trail heard the impact and yelled out to ask if I was alright, and I gave him a wobbly thumbs-up. He waved and kept walking. I brushed off the dirt, grabbed my cracked phone, and laughed off the pain. The brand must go on, right?

Then, the wind kicked up, and I froze.

Farther off in the brush, there was another hiker. He was wearing a dark jacket, and he was completely frozen. He wasn't checking on me, he wasn't moving, and he wasn't blinking. He was just watching.

I tried to shake it off. I patched my scraped knee and went to upload my footage, but I had zero service. I played the clip back instead. I paused it, and a chill crept in: there he was in the background of my video. He was perfectly still, watching me. The wilderness suddenly didn't feel empty anymore.

I kept moving, trying to film a segment on snake safety along a narrow ridge. I stopped cold. He was there again, but this time he was walking straight toward me.

I ducked off the trail into the thick brush, holding my breath like a kid hiding under a blanket. He passed by, expressionless and entirely unbothered. I waited until he was gone, but then I heard the unmistakable buzz of a rattlesnake right at my feet.

I bolted through the tearing brush and crashed right back onto the main trail, slamming into a person. Instinct and panic took over, and I sprayed him full-force in the face with my bear mace.

It wasn't the dark hiker. It was just a guy named Ethan, a solo hiker in cargo shorts. I flushed his eyes out with my water bottle, apologizing profusely, and asked if I could walk with him toward the Skeleton Point campground.

Everything felt okay for a mile. The sunlight was flickering through the trees, and we were joking around.

Then I saw him again. The man in the black jacket.

He was walking from the opposite direction, wearing the exact same clothes, carrying the exact same impossible presence. I urgently whispered to Ethan, pointing right at the man.

Ethan squinted. He looked right at the spot, right at the man. And he told me there was no one on the trail but us.

I stared at Ethan, searching his face for validation, for some shared terror. Instead, his expression shifted to a restrained empathy, like he was trying to decide if he should comfort me or call for a psychological hold. I looked back up, and the dark hiker stepped behind a tree and vanished without a sound.

By dusk, we reached the camp. Ethan looked at my half-built tent, told me he was heading out, and disappeared into the trees so fast it was like he was never really there. I was completely alone.

I grabbed my dead phone and practically ran to the park's General Store to buy a single-use portable charger and some water. Hearing the campers laughing nearby made me feel safe for exactly one second.

Because when I stepped outside, he was there.

He wasn't just standing in the woods; he looked like he was pressed into them, like a smudge on film. I pivoted down another path, and he was closer. He lifted his hand, curled two stiff fingers, and beckoned me forward like he was pulling a string only I could feel.

Every time I turned, he was there, closer. He beckoned me again, not frantically, but like he had done it a thousand times before and his victims always came. It was like the woods themselves were calling me in.

I screamed at him to leave me alone. He vanished. I looked around, realizing I was now the spectacle. The other campers were just standing there, holding up their phones, recording my breakdown to stream it to the world. Mortal fear collapsed into total humiliation.

I asked if they had service, and one guy muttered they had three bars.

I sprinted back to my campsite. I slammed my pack down, ripped open the charger, and jammed it into my phone. The battery icon flashed red and the screen flickered to life. I frantically opened Instagram, intending to post an audio plea for help with my location.

The app glitched. My profile reloaded.

At the top of the screen, in cold, hard text, it read: In Memory of Lana Hill (2003-2025).

My account had been memorialized. It was plastered with digital grief—pixelated flowers, angel wings, and comments from strangers saying, "Wish we could've helped" and "Rest in peace, nature girl". It was a shrine built by strangers.

The screen suddenly went black. In the glossy reflection of the dead glass, I saw my own face staring back.

And right behind me, I saw his.

I didn't scream. I didn't face him. I dropped the phone, tore through the back of my tent, running toward the highway until a passing trucker let me jump into his cab.

I'm sitting in a diner three towns over right now, typing this on a borrowed laptop. I'm physically safe. But my Instagram still says I'm dead. My family won't answer my calls. And every time the diner door chimes, I swear I see a shadow waiting for me outside.
