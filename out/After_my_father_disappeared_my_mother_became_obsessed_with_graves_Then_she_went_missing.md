My name is Maria Brown. I am fifteen years old. If I tell this story to anyone in real life, they will just think I’m crazy, which is why I am writing this here.

About two years ago, my father, David Brown, vanished. The police closed the case, claiming there was no evidence of foul play. His partially destroyed camp was discovered deep in the Arizona wilderness, and everyone assumed that wild animals had killed him. The investigation was officially shut down.

For two years, everyone believed my father fell victim to the wilderness.

Everyone… except my mother.

My mother knew something that no one else did. She screamed at the police, telling them that my father absolutely hated the woods. He was terrified of the dark and open wilderness. How could his camp possibly be found deep inside the Arizona forests if he would never go there willingly? The police had no answer. Citing a lack of evidence, they simply refused to investigate further. The case remained closed.

That rejection completely broke my mother. She cut herself off from friends and relatives entirely. She would spend hours staring at my father’s photograph, waiting for a phone call that never came.

She slowly sank into a deep depression. She stopped talking to people. Eventually, she stopped talking to me too. Even so, I was just glad she was still with me, though it broke my heart how distant she had become.

Then came the dreams. A terrifying series of nights that would change everything.

One night, while I was asleep in my room, I woke up to the sound of sobbing coming from my mother’s bedroom. I crept toward her door. It was locked from the inside. I peeked through a small gap in the window.

What I saw made my blood run cold. My mother was sitting on the floor in the middle of the room. My father’s old coat and clothes were scattered around her in a strange circle. She was moving her hands through the empty air, cradling it as if she were touching a real face. There was no one there. It was just an empty corner of the room. Yet, her voice sounded as though she were begging a physical person.

"David... why are you so wet? Why is dirt falling from your clothes? What happened to you, David? Where are you?"

She kept repeating those words over and over. Then, suddenly, she collapsed onto the floor and fell fast asleep.

That was the first incident, but it was far from the last.

Every few days, my father began appearing in her dreams. My mother became utterly convinced he was trying to tell her something. She told me he spoke to her directly, 

claiming that human beings never truly die. He told her that bodies buried in graves only appear dead because a sinister force imprisons them there, trapping them in an endless sleep. He claimed the same thing had happened to him—a spirit had trapped him inside a grave somewhere, and even he didn’t know his location. He told her that if she performed exorcisms on buried corpses, the evil forces would weaken and reveal where he was hidden.

I remember it vividly; she spoke to me that day for the first time in months. She said, "I saw your father in my dream, Maria. He isn't gone. He is right here, we just have to find him. Don't be afraid, my sweet girl." After saying that, she walked away.

It was around two in the morning when I heard the heavy click of the front door opening. I stepped out of my room and saw my mother walking toward the forest with a flashlight in her hand. I wanted to call out to her, but the words caught in my throat. I couldn't bear seeing her like this anymore. She had never gone out alone at night before. Never.

Grabbing my own flashlight, I followed her into the dark.

She was moving too fast, disappearing into the pitch black ahead of me. I called out for her several times, but my voice was swallowed by the silent trees. Then, suddenly, I heard it—the distinct, wet sound of digging.

I followed the noise, and there she was.

My mother was digging up an old grave with her bare hands. Her fingernails were completely torn apart, and blood was dripping from her fingertips into the soil, but she didn’t seem to feel a thing. She was muttering under her breath, chanting like she was reciting a mantra.

I stepped closer, trembling, and placed my hand on her shoulder.

But my mother… it was like she didn’t belong to this world anymore. As she slowly turned her head to look at me, a gasp escaped my throat. The pupils of her eyes had rolled completely upward—only the blank, milky whites of her eyeballs reflected my flashlight. I had never seen anything so horrifying. Panic seized me, and I ran all the way back to my room, hiding under my blanket.

Everything changed after that night. My mother began slipping out almost every month to dig up graves, desperately performing those exorcisms for answers. I knew it was madness, but I had already lost my father; I couldn’t lose her too. If I reported her, they would take her away to an asylum. So, I stayed quiet.

Our house stood at the edge of town, a few miles away from a church where everyone gathered on Sundays. I wanted to pull her out of her depression, so I started taking her there. But she would just stand behind the church for hours, staring blankly at the tombstones. Eventually, our relatives distanced themselves from us, and I stopped taking her there altogether.

At school, people whispered that I was the daughter of a madwoman. I couldn't take it anymore, so I stopped going. My mother didn’t care; she was entirely lost in her own reality.

Then, things took a turn for the worse. Rumors spread through the town that a grave robber was desecrating plots at night, leaving corpses exposed. The townspeople were terrified. Nobody knew who was responsible. Finally, the local council agreed that every single grave would be secured with heavy iron locks until the culprit was caught.

This restriction made my mother's obsession unbearable. The dreams didn't stop, but she could no longer reach the dirt. One night, she went out again. This time, I didn't follow her. I figured since the graves were locked, she would give up and return home in a few hours.

She never came back.

After two days of agonizing, I called the police. During their search, they found something unexpected deep within the same Arizona wilderness where my father’s camp had been found. They discovered a second campsite—belonging to my mother. Her glasses and flashlight were left behind on the dirt. Just like my father, she was gone.

I was left entirely alone. Both my parents had vanished from my life. No relatives wanted custody of me . Soon after, I was moved to an orphanage where I lived with several other girls. Eventually, I made friends. I started laughing and playing again. For a short while, I genuinely believed my childhood had returned.

But I was wrong. The nightmare was bleeding into my own mind.

The exact same dreams that had consumed my mother began visiting me. Every night, I saw my parents. Wet, black mud poured continuously from their open mouths.

Slowly, I began feeling a restless, uncontrollable urge to dig. Sometimes I would sit by the old, forgotten cemetery behind the orphanage for hours, just staring at the ground. I didn’t tell a soul. I refused to let anyone believe I was losing my mind.

But last night, the illusion shattered.

I woke up at exactly two in the morning. A sharp, burning agony was radiating from my hands. Trembling, I switched on my flashlight to inspect them.

Three of my fingernails had been completely ripped from the skin. Wet blood was pooling over my fingers. Right at that moment, I heard the distinct, unsettling sound of sand sliding and hitting the floorboards.

I whipped around, and my breath caught in my throat. Lying right on the mattress next to me was a decaying corpse. Damp, dark earth was actively spilling from its rotted clothes onto my bed sheets.

I screamed at the top of my lungs—a primal, terrified shriek. Within seconds, the other girls and the orphanage superintendent burst into my room. But the moment the lights flicked on, there was nothing there. Even my fingernails were completely intact, clean and untouched. Everyone held me and tried to calm me down, telling me it was just a vivid nightmare, a bad hallucination born from my trauma, and that I should just sleep it off.

I almost believed them. I wanted to believe them.

But this morning, I walked out to the old cemetery behind the property. One of the old graves had been freshly dug up, the dirt thrown carelessly around it. The coffin was wide open, and the body was completely gone.

And the name on the tombstone matched the face of the corpse I saw lying next to me in the dark last night.

How can they tell me it was just a dream?
