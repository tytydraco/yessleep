This happened three nights ago and it's been bothering me ever since.



I work evenings at a gas station in a tiny town called Morrow. The population is around three hundred, maybe a little more. Maybe less. Nobody seems to know the exact number. Everybody knows everybody, though. That's how places like this work. You learn people's orders before you learn their names. You know what truck they drive, where they sit in church, which dog belongs to which house.



Morrow's small enough that new faces stick out immediately. I've worked at the gas station since I was eighteen. Six years behind the same register means I know pretty much everyone in town.



Mr. Harper comes in every day a little after five when he gets off work. He grabs a coffee and a bag of beef jerky. Same brand. Same order. Every single time.



Denise comes in a couple times a week. She complains about gas prices while buying a pack of cigarettes and swears she's finally quitting after this one.



Luke's a senior at the high school. Every afternoon he stands in front of the energy drink cooler for five straight minutes like he's making a life-changing decision before grabbing the exact same can he bought yesterday.



After a while, you stop paying attention. Not because you don't care, but because everything settles into routine. Most shifts I could practically work on autopilot.



Most shifts are boring. Three nights ago was boring too. Until he walked in.



The bell over the door rang and I looked up expecting another local. Instead I saw someone I'd never met before. At least I don't think I had.



He looked to be somewhere in his thirties. Maybe older. Tall. Broad shoulders. Dark hair that almost touched his shoulders. Stubble. White tank top, dark jeans, work boots. The kind of guy who looked like he could split firewood before breakfast and still have energy left over. Nothing unusual about that, Morrow is a country town. 



The weird part was the feeling I got when I looked at him. Like I'd forgotten who he was. Like there was supposed to be a memory attached to his face and somebody had ripped it out. 



I remember staring a little too long because he smiled when our eyes met. Not the polite smile people give cashiers. Not the awkward smile strangers give when they're caught looking. The kind of smile people give somebody they're genuinely happy to see. Like he'd been waiting to see me. I looked away first.



He wandered around the store for a minute before bringing a coffee and a bottle of water to the register. The entire time I rang him up, he watched me. Not in a creepy way. At least not at first. More like he was studying me. Like he was comparing me to something. Like one of those find the difference games. 



I asked if he wanted a receipt. He laughed quietly to himself. Not at me. Just... amused. 



Then he shook his head. 



"Sorry."



I asked what was funny. For a second he looked like he wasn't sure how to answer.



Then he smiled again.



"Nothing." He glanced down at the counter. "You're just exactly the same."



I remember waiting for him to explain. He didn't. I asked if we'd met before. The smile faltered. Not completely. Just enough that he suddenly looked disappointed.



Then he said, 



"No."



The answer should've ended the conversation. Instead, it somehow made it stranger. Because it didn't sound like somebody lying. It sounded like somebody answering a different question than the one I'd asked.



He paid for his coffee and water and headed for the door. I figured that was the end of it. Then he stopped. His hand was already on the handle when he turned back toward me.



"Connor."



I froze. I wear a nametag, so that part wasn't strange. The way he said it was. Carefully. Like he was testing it. Like he wasn’t sure if it was right. 



"Yeah?" I asked.



For a second he just looked at me. Not at the nametag. At me. Then he smiled. A real smile this time. Relieved. Happy. The kind of smile somebody gets after they scratch one of the lotto tickets out on my counter and win something over ten dollars. 



"Good," he said. "That's still your name."



Then he walked out. I stood there for a full minute trying to figure out what the hell that meant. The thing is, that's not even the part that's been bothering me. The part that's been bothering me is that I've spent the last three days trying to describe what he looked like.



And every time I do, I end up describing some idealized version of myself. Not exactly. Just enough to make me uncomfortable.



I haven't told anybody that part.



Well, I’ve only told my co-worker, Nichole. She already thinks I’m weird so nothing lost there. 



Morrow's small enough that if you tell one person something weird, you'll spend the next month hearing about it from everybody else. So I kept it to myself. Nichole doesn’t really talk to anybody else so hopefully nothing lost there either. 



The thing is, he came back. Tonight. Same time. Same coffee. Same bottle of water. The bell over the door rang and my stomach dropped before I even looked up. I knew it was him. I don't know how. I just did. I’d been on autopilot most the day. Maybe its because I recently buffed the floor and his boots make the floor squeak. I couldn’t tell you. Maybe my internal clock just was anticipating the same time. Everyone here has their routine they follow. Apparently, even new faces. 



This time he stayed longer. He wandered the aisles without buying anything else. Every now and then I'd catch him looking at me. Not staring. Just checking. The way I look back at my door a few times a night to make sure I actually locked it. 



When he finally came up to pay, neither of us said much. I handed him his change. When I dropped it into his hand- he kinda held his hand there a few seconds too long and made it weird. Then he asked me a question.



"Are you happy?"



I actually laughed. Not because it was funny. Because it was weird. Who asks that? Especially a stranger. I told him exactly that.



I said, "That's kind of a weird thing to ask somebody you don't know."



For the first time since he'd walked in, he looked genuinely confused. Not offended. Not embarrassed. Confused. Like I'd said something that didn't make sense. He kinda looked like I hurt his feelings. It made me feel like a jackass.



Then he tilted his head slightly and said:



"You're not a stranger."



I didn't know what to say to that. Neither did he, apparently. Because after a few seconds he smiled again. Not happy this time. Sad. Almost apologetic. Then he took his coffee and left.



I've spent the last four hours trying to convince myself that none of this is worth posting about. Maybe he's just weird. Maybe he mistook me for somebody else with coincidentally the same name.  Maybe there's a completely normal explanation.



But I can't stop thinking about the way he looked at me. Not like somebody recognizing an old friend. Not like somebody recognizing family. Something else. Kinda like on those videos where people reunite with a dog they lost years ago and the dog barks at them instead of coming to them. 



Anyway, my name's Connor. I'm twenty-four years old and I work whatever shifts I can pick up at the gas station. I've been there since I turned eighteen. If you've ever passed through Morrow, there's a decent chance I've sold you a coffee, a lottery ticket, or a tank of gas.



I guess I'm posting because I want to know if anybody else has had something like this happen.



You know that one aunt at a family gathering you barely remember, but she acts like she's known you your entire life? Like she remembers stories about you that you don't remember living through? That's what this guy feels like. Not an old friend. Not somebody who mistook me for someone else. Just somebody who seems way more familiar with me than he should be.



And if he comes back tomorrow, what am I even supposed to ask him? 



Because "How do you know me?" already didn't get me anywhere. 



I can't exactly open with, "Why do you look disappointed every time I don't recognize you?"



Or, "What did you mean when you said that's still my name?"



Nichole says I should just ask him directly what his deal is. That sounds like a great way to sound completely insane. And totally not have some weirdo pull a knife on me or threaten me. 



Anyway. If you've got advice, let me know. 



I'm going to try to get some sleep before my shift tomorrow. Though I have a feeling I'll just end up lying awake thinking about this guy again.



About the way he smiled. About the way he acted like I was the one who'd forgotten something.



And mostly about what he said before he left.



"Good. That's still your name."



I keep trying to imagine a normal reason someone would say that. I haven't come up with one yet. I actually can't come up with any reason. 



If he comes back tomorrow, I'll update.
