You know, some part of me really hoped I’d never have to update this, that it would all blow over, but that sure as hell isn’t what happened. I’m not writing this because of some massive development, I’m writing it because I need to put out another warning. Hell, Im not even sure anyone even has good enough service to read this anymore but I have to try, I can’t let anyone lose someone else to them. Not after losing her.

I guess the internets a better therapist than anyone I’m stuck in here with, so I’ll tell you who she is. Or was, I guess. Her name was Libby, and she was a researcher at the local university before the locusts came. She used to tell me that she couldn’t wait for this to all blow over so she could go back to it. I was never able to tell if she was naive enough to believe that would happen or if she was just an optimist, but I guess we were all a bit of both when this started. I used to ask her about her research sometimes, try to get her to tell me what she studied because I thought being able to tell me about it could maybe help her, but eventually I gave up being the nosy bunk mate. She was beautiful, funny too. In another life maybe we could’ve been together, just two girls against the world. I guess some people might say thats what we were, but I’m not even sure I know what we were to each other.

Sorry, I’m rambling. When I made my first attempt at warning the world the locusts had made it as far as california, and I have no clue where they are now. Libby smashed our radio to bits one day without any warning, said it drove her insane. I’d guess they’ve at least made it to Europe, but we have no way of knowing. The day Libby broke the radio was really the beginning of the end for her. For all of us, really. I guess I’ll start there.

”Libby what the fuck!” I ran into our quarters and dove towards her, trying in vain to catch the radio as it sailed through the air and shattered against the concrete floor.

”no. No no no no. I’m so sorry. I don’t know what came over me. They were talking about some new theory about those damn locusts and I just-“

”Make it look like it fell on its own“

Libby just stood there staring at her hands.

”Quick! Before someone else sees, I won’t let a mistake like this ruin you Libby”

Libby scooped our shattered life line up, sobbing as she positioned everything to look like it could have simply fallen off the table naturally. We left the quarters silently, and although she never thanked me, we both knew she didn’t need to.

The next person to see the radio was an old farmer named John Owens. Everyone tried their darnedest to fix the thing, but it was beyond repair. Everyone could see something was wrong with Libby, the woman wore her emotions like they were ill-fitting clothes, but only I knew what caused them. In the coming weeks the loss of our beloved radio hit us hard. It wasn’t just how we got the news, it was our entertainment. The boredom hit the children the hardest, and the children took it out on us. Ive never seen as many people volunteer to leave the bunker as I did in that first week without the radio. What used to be a possible death sentence just became something else to do. I never went. I don’t think I could have bared to see the world we left behind after so long. Libby never went either, she wanted to see the world but was just too afraid.

A month or so later Libby only seemed to be feeling worse. She got distant, didn’t talk to anyone other than me unless she had to. She started doing something she hadn’t done since the locusts first appeared: she told me about her job. She said she was an entomologist, which was way more detail than the vague position of researcher she had claimed in the past. Before we retreated underground she had been researching the locusts. She said she never learned much, I could tell she was holding something back but I didn’t push.

The day I lost her we had one of the strangest conversations.

“Sam?”

”Yeah Libby?”

”Do you think he regretted it?”

I just stared at her, cocking my head in confusion, not expecting her to punch me in the gut.

”When they nailed him to the cross I mean. Do you think he regretted helping them?”

”Supposedly he cried out, asking why God would abandon him. Sounds like regret to me”

”Sam?”

I didn’t respond this time.

”I want to see the world again. Just one more time“

”What do you mean one more time?”

she didn’t say anything, but the look she gave me told me more than words ever could. She walked slowly towards the hatch, every step seeming to shake the world.

”Libby? Libby get away From there, please!”

She opened the hatch.

”See you later, Sam”

Libby stepped outside. She made it about ten feet before they swarmed her; as quickly as the swarm appeared it was gone, and so was she.

Im sorry to end this here, but I just can’t keep rereading this story, her story, trying to think of where to go next. I’ll keep updating for the foreseeable future, hoping someone might read these posts and do something; but, for now, this is all I have for you.
