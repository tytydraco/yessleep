That damned humming again. 

I once thought it was a soothing lullaby of sorts. It kept the headaches away, you see. I’m always getting these absolutely awful headaches, where it feels like someone is bashing my skull in with a jackhammer. The excruciating pain is always coupled with intrusive thoughts featuring gushing streams of blood, dismembered limbs and spilling intestines. 

L’s beautiful humming makes it all go away.

Nowadays, I can’t stop listening to her melody, but I also hate it. The humming suddenly stopped as my wife shouted, “Watch out!”

I slammed on the brakes, hard enough to send jolts of sharp pain down my spine and up my fingertips. I was more surprised by L’s awareness of her surroundings than her sudden outburst. As for what caused it – a German Shepherd that seemed to have appeared out of thin air— strolling in front of my car.

Suddenly invigorated by a surge of spite, I stepped on the gas. 0 to 60 in 6.3; that was what the car salesman promised me. Nothing impressive, but I didn’t feel the car hit the dog or feel the dog’s body crunch under the car. I knew it - there wasn’t a dog in the first place. All eleven of my fingers, which apparently suffer from decade-old trauma I have no recollection of, continued to ache from the sudden movement.  

The fucking ghost dogs.

Soon, we were cruising again and L’s humming resumed. 

In the distance, I could hear a cacophony of barking and yelping. Dogs. How many dogs were in the town if we could hear them all the way from here? We exited the interstate and pulled into a wide road where desecrated houses began to populate the otherwise barren expanse of dead trees and dried grass. 

“Do you think Annie would want a puppy, dear?” L suddenly asked. Her eyes, a gorgeous sky-blue identical to my own, were as vacant as her smile. As she turned to the silent figure sitting in the backseat, the scars on the right side of her face briefly caught my eyes. “It’s Annie’s birthday tomorrow. Maybe we can get her a puppy…”

I grunted, but didn’t deign to respond.

Oddly enough, the barking and yelping died into an eerie silence as we pulled into Deadwood Valley. The town was in a state of visible deterioration. The few people walking the streets were dressed in styles that went out of style decades ago. Strangest of all, fraying dog leashes hung like nooses from most of the streetlamps.

We drove down the main street, careful to avoid disintegrating memorials and pedestrians that walked down the road like mindless zombies. From what I saw, all the memorials were for dogs and everything was caked with filth. 

What a literal shithole. If I hadn’t found that binder with dozens of printouts and news articles about Deadwood Valley in my wife’s bookcases, I wouldn’t have the slightest idea that a town like this existed. 

A large statue of the town’s founder, OZ Montgomery, loomed over us as we drove under the dog leash nooses and over discarded fliers. *We are Family.* Apparently, that was the Montgomery family’s slogan. Until the entire Montgomery family was wiped out by a revolution. The Montgomery Massacre. 

Small towns and their dramatic stories. 

Once we arrived at the house, I parked and L immediately stepped out of the car to start fussing over Annie. She pulled our backseat passenger out of the booster seat, jostled her around, patted her back, and stroked her hair. All the while, crooning, “I know, baby. It was such a long ride. But daddy has to move here for career advancement and we must support him. We are family.”

I raised an eyebrow. The sheer absurdity of the statement, a stark contrast with our reality, struck a chord within me. I knew what a job was (L had one), but I don’t know what kind of job I would have, if I were to have one. As far as I knew, L and I had a seemingly endless source of wealth. 

As I watch my wife kiss the Raggedy Anne doll and adjust her yellow ballgown, a deep sense of remorse flooded my chest. Our baby, now deceased, was a monstrosity that could not be accepted in this world, I gently reminded myself. I did what I had to do when L couldn’t do it. 

L picked the doll up and said, “Annie needs to use the bathroom.”

I double-checked. L’s eyes were still vacant. She’d started taking a cocktail of pills after Annie’s death four years ago and she’d gradually lost any capacity for coherent thoughts. I rolled down the window and handed L the keys to the new house. 

“Honey, can you pick up some candles later? It’s Annie’s birthday tomorrow.”

As I exited the car, I grimaced. I looked around, hoping to find a local source of the putrid stench but soon realized that it was the air of the town itself that reeked of wet dog and dog piss. Weird. I’d not seen a single dog since entering Deadwood Valley, but there were abandoned accessories for dogs everywhere. 

Behind me, I heard a low, wet growl. 

I turned around, but all I saw was a dead tree, adorned with noose-leash.

No, I corrected myself. I didn’t know how I knew this, but the tree was a Deadwood, one of the several thousand in this town. The tree bore no leaves, flower or fruit, but it was alive and if you cut into it, it would bleed blood-red sap. The sap was sickeningly sweet and the wood was phenomenal for fuel. Discovering how to process Deadwood for fuel was how OZ Montgomery became rich decades ago.

*The face of young boy with sky-blue eyes swam into my mind. He was about seven years old and his clothes exuded wealth. He was pulling on my hand and grinning at me, though there was malice in his smile. “Baby brother, I want to show you something.”*

*Whatever my brother wanted to show me, I knew I didn’t want to see it. Heir to the vast wealth and power of the Montgomery family, O Montgomery was a menace to everyone in the town. Adults knew better and children were protected from him, so O most often tormented the teenagers in the town.*    

*I didn’t want to go, but I went to the garden anyway, because “no” was the worst thing you could say.*

*All of a sudden, O, screamed. “It got free!”* 

*Seconds later, an immense force struck me and I fell to the ground. I’d been shoved into the path of a charging German Shepard. The dog was bleeding, limping, and brutally injured, but fueled by desperate vengeance. Some of the barb wires wrapped around the dog continued to tear into its skin, but the dog promptly ignored me to attack O instead.*

*Such unforgettable screams.* 

*The dog. I vaguely recall it was the dog of a local teenager. Derek?*

Was it a hallucination, a figment of my imagination or part of my repressed memory? I couldn’t tell. Hallucinations were common, along with my headaches. I allowed myself to fall back, into the lush seat of my car.

At this point, you might be asking why I was in this freakshow of a town. 

You see, I have no memories of my childhood or teenage years. The earliest memory I could recall was waking up in a luxurious bed in a large, suburban mansion five years ago. At that point, I was already twenty-three years old. Supposedly. 

A beautiful woman then walked in and told me my name was J. Her name was L, she was my wife and I’d been in a horrible car accident that made me lose my memory. The bandages around my face, the debilitating headache and my atrophied body all backed her claims up.

We lived as a married couple for a year. We had Annie, our beautiful baby who was born *deficient*. Soon after she was born, Annie chewed through L’s breasts with the teeth growing out of her eye sockets and stabbed me with a metal fork. Our beautiful little girl wasn’t meant for this world, but L never forgave me for laying Annie to rest.

That lead to The Mark Incident three months ago.

Three months ago, after the injuries L sustained from The Mark Incident, I was finally able to go through L’s belongings. I figured out that there had never been a car accident, the bandages were from a facial reconstruction and my memory loss was the result of extended hypnosis L performed on me. My only clue about my past was the binder about Deadwood Valley I mentioned earlier. 

A big, bulky binder full of printouts.

As I stepped into the house, I heard for the briefest moments, a faint thumping sound. Like a dog’s tail on loose wooden boards. I looked around, expecting to see the German Shepard hallucination again. There was a dog’s yelp, a warning.

I turned toward the sound just in time to see my wife rushing towards me, with one of my sharpest kitchen knives in her hands. Her eyes were wild. My breath locked in my chest, and for one second, I wondered if there was enough time for my forgotten life to flash before my eyes before I bled out on the kitchen floor.

Who was I? What was my link to Deadwood Valley?

My heart stopped the exact moment the knife’s trajectory ended, with a very sharp tip right at my throat. My wife, with a look of bewildered lucidity in her eyes, whimpered. Her hands started shaking. If her hands shook any harder, she might slice my throat in half without meaning to.

A shallow exhale escaped my throat. I held completely still, fearful that any movement will startle her. It’s incredible how long you could go without blinking or inhaling when your life depended on it. I couldn’t even feel anything despite the trickle of blood.  

“I love you so much,” said L. 

“I love you too, dear.” My voice was surprisingly steady, but the words were hollow. I hadn’t felt anything close to love for my wife ever since The Mark Incident. I’m not sure I ever romantically loved her either.

Luckily, before I needed to think too hard about what to do, the vacant look returned to L’s eyes and her lips twisted up in a loopy smile. The hands holding the knife began to lower. “Who am I?” 

Was I out of danger? Forcing myself to move and not tremble, I lifted my hand to stroke the side of her face, sliding over the scars from a terrible burn that’d healed as perfectly as they could. I said, softly as ever, “Your name is L K\[redacted\]. You are a woman scientist and a pioneer in the fields of behavioral neuroscience.”

“I am a woman scientist,” L repeated. The smile grew wider and her hands fully dropped. The knife fell to the ground, and despite the danger I was just in, I internally grimaced at the thought of my beautiful knife being bent by the fall. L continued, “When I was a child, my family stowed me away in a hidden vault and told me I would not be allowed to live for long because I was a girl. Girls are weak, useless crea…”

A sharp pain emanated from the back of my head and shocked my entire system. I closed my eyes and memories began to form in my mind again. I could see a tall man in a peacoat. He was only so tall because I was short. I must’ve been a child.

*The man was talking. “These peasants will eat you alive from inside out if you give them even an ounce of liberty. They want what we have, and they hate us for having what they want. This is the cycle of hatred. This is the Montgomery curse.”*

The man turned towards me and his resemblance to L was striking. He must’ve been her father. Before I could try to remember more, I was brought back to the present by the sound of loud screams. Ear-splitting, inhuman screams coming from my mouth, caused by the pain in my head.

L was holding me, cradling my head between her hands and humming the magical melody. I lifted my head from the mess of drool and tears I left on her skirt and loosened my death grip on her skirt. From how the drool had partially dried and how the sun had already set, I must’ve passed out for hours. 

“We should go to the supermarket,” said L. Her eyes were more vacant than usual, a sign that she’d taken her medication recently. “It’s Annie’s birthday tomorrow and I want everything to be perfect.”    

Four days later, we finally finished unpacking and I found the time to visit the town center. There were a few functioning stores, mostly for food and produce, but the streets were littered with debris. Faded signs advertised sales for items that are no longer relevant. The air is stale, but putrid with the smell of *dog*.

The sound of metal dragging on concrete caught my attention. I turned my head, and saw a man dragging a metal dog chain. No dog, of course. There was something familiar about the man. As I pause to think, my entire body went cold and goosebumps crept all over my arms and thighs. 

The last time I saw that face, I was burying the corpse of its owner. Mark. L’s intern, who I caught in bed with her in the wing of the house L usually forbade me to enter. To this day, I still don’t know what gave me the self-control to calmly return to the kitchen, heat up the frier and pour hot oil over the two of them. 

The Mark Incident.

Mark died on the spot and L was disfigured. She needed bedrest for weeks after that event. Moments later, I realized I was mistaken. The man bore an uncanny resemblance to Mark, but he was not Mark. The facial structure was similar, but this man was older, much older than me, and definitely blind.

Hesitantly, I struck up a conversation with him.

His name was Derek.

I vaguely remember a Derek from L’s files, a Derek whose father led a revolution, the massacre, against the Montgomery family and ended their tyranny in Deadwood Valley. Derek could have been the next leader, a celebrity, of the town but he refused to pulled himself out of despair and trauma. What he’d seen at the Montgomery Massacre scarred him for life.

Don’t ask why I chose to try to speak to this ghost over any of the other ghosts wandering in this freakshow of a town, but I felt a certain kinship with him. The conversation was difficult. Derek would start sentences, but not finish them. He’d pause in the middle to shriek and scratch his eye sockets. It was all incoherent mumble-jumble until -  

“This is Marley,” said Derek, about the empty dog leash. All of a sudden, his voice was clear and his English was perfect. “Marley is a good boy. I was a lonely kid. The outcast. Marley was and is my first, last and only friend in this world.”

I got the sense that this was an important story to Derek. The man might’ve lost his will to live a good life, but the story of his dog was one that he must pass on to anyone who’d listen.

“Every summer, when O Montgomery returned from his overseas boarding school, he and his gang of sycophants would seek me out and torment me. But summer of 1988 was different. I had Marley and Marley protected me.”

To the sound of Derek’s scratchy voice, I began to remember. I was a Montgomery. I couldn’t remember my birth name, but I was a Montgomery. O Montgomery was my brother. Derek was teenager back then, ten years older than O and twelve years older than me. One of my brother’s many victims.  

“The next day, some Montgomery goons showed up at my door to take Marley away from me. O claimed that Marley tried to bite him and would not give Marley back until he was rehabilitated.”

“My poor Marley suffered so much that summer.”

I could remember some things about that summer now. I was also home from boarding school, but I was more fascinated by books than sunlight or other people. Luckily, I was "too boring" for my brother and he was happy enough to lord over me without physically tormenting me.   

“One day, I sneaked into the Montgomery residence. I freed my Marley from the chains and muzzle, but he was too weak to escape with me. Before I could come up with a backup plan, O arrived. I watched Marley tear him into pieces. It was glorious.”

The scene from my hallucination flashed before my eyes, this time with context. O had told me he found a fun new toy and convinced me to hang out with him. When we arrived at the garden, his stolen dog was mysteriously freed from its constraints. 

*“It got free!”* 

“Two days later, M Montgomery bashed in the head of every single dog and puppy in Deadwood Valley at his son’s funeral. And then…”

Lucidity faded from Derek’s face as I watched.

I, too, remember my brother’s funeral. It was a gruesome affair. There was blood everywhere. The repugnant stench of dog piss, wet dog and smashed brains. I was crying as I looked up, into the eyes of the man I thought was L’s father. “Please, Father. Please don’t make me do this.” 

There was a rock in my raised hands and a dog restrained by a bodyguard in front of me. My hands were shaking so hard that my entire body was rattling. I was supposed to kill that dog, a random amongst the hundreds of pups my father rounded up that day. I was to prove my loyalty and dedication to the Family since I was now the heir.

We are Family. 

The rock fell from my fingers, hitting nothing except my toes. Then, I ran, heart pounding in my throat as someone fired three bullets at the ground next to my path.

Later that day, M Montgomery broke all my fingers. I was not strong enough to be a good heir, my father said. Maybe even *the girl* was stronger than me. I looked at my fingers, finally understanding where the pain came from.

After killing all the dogs in the town, father decided that it wasn’t enough. He’d lost a son, his *heir*. The rumor spread that M Montgomery had hired hundreds of mercenaries to round up and kill everyone’s children.

That was when Derek’s father started the revolution.

Derek muttered, “It always.. Kill all of them. I was going to… you know how… Kill them all. ” 

“But,” I said softly. “They didn’t kill all the Montgomery. You were the one who hid me in the vault behind that painting.” 

The painting in front of the vault my parents used to hide away *the girl*. I also remember that now. I’d grown up thinking O and myself were M Montgomery’s only children. Two healthy sons– exactly what he wanted. But, on the night of the Montgomery Massacre, I discovered that I had a sister (twin sister!) who was still alive. 

When Derek’s father and the rest of the angry mob charged into the room, Derek told them the room was empty. The face that haunts me and keeps me awake at night didn’t just belong to the man my wife cheated on me with. It also belonged to his brother, who once saved my life. 

Mark was twenty-one years old, seven years younger than L and I but more than fifteen years younger than Derek. They couldn’t have been twins but the resemblance was uncanny. “What was the...Kill them all. Couldn’t I? .. Kill all of them.. Doesn’t it?”

“You did. You killed and strung up my whole family except me and L.” I felt no hatred for the man in front of me. This pathetic, sightless man. I decided not to tell him about The Mark Incident. We parted ways and I was left to my own thoughts. I knew more than I ever did about my past, I was more lost than ever. There was a large gap that could only be filled by one thing.

Despite the unease that gripped my chest, I had to visit what was left of Montgomery Mansion.

The sun was about to set by the time I reached the toppling gates of the mansion. The cacophony of barking and whining was unbearable now, like in the day of my brother’s funeral. My headache was equally unbearable and I needed it all to end. I couldn’t fight it. My heart was beating faster and faster and my thoughts were scattered.

Something was calling to me from the depths of the mansion.

Whatever was on the other side of the gate, they wanted vengeance. 

Father? Mother? Brother?

Time felt distorted. I could hear unworldly laughter and whispers. There was so much anger and so much hatred tangled in the air of this ground. A deep wave of dread washed over me; something terrible was going to happen to me. Something wanted to consume me.  

Something on the other side of the gates that I was about to open.

I was deathly afraid, but I was driven by a strangely vivid sense of supernatural urgency. My mouth is dry, and the air is so rotten it scorched my nostrils with each breath I take. There was a horrible feeling. I was watched by hundreds of presences beyond the gate, all harboring insidious intents.   

Then, I heard a bark.

A ghostly German Shepard, on the other side of the gates, barked once more at me and then turned to growl at the mansion. I snapped out of my trance. The sense of urgency vanished along with my desire to open the gates of the mansion. I pulled my hands back, confused.

I suddenly remembered one more detail. *I* was the one who gave Derek the keys to our garden. He was able to sneak into our mansion and free Marley because *I* gave him the keys. That was why Derek hid me behind the painting instead of calling his father in to kill me.

I was the catalyst of the series of tragedies that followed, but, to Marley, I enabled his owner to reunite with him one last time and enabled his vengeance on my brother. Marley had waited, for over two decades, to pay back that one act of kindness, but this growl was the only wake-up call, the only warning, I’d get.

It was all I needed.

I immediately left the premise.

The gates remained close.

We’re back in a real city now, L and I. 

No matter how I try, I cannot remember how we escaped from the Montgomery Mansion twenty-three years ago. But, in the next two decades that followed the Massacre, L pursued a successful education and integrate herself into the science world. I was thrown from one mental institution to the next.

One day, L found me and brought me back into her life. Not as her twin brother, but as her husband. Don’t ask me why. Stripped of my memory and free will, I played that role perfectly and did everything she asked until I had to kill the misshapen creature that L gave birth to. 

Annie’s death and my role in it led to L’s eventual infidelity. Finding L in bed with Mark, killing Mark and subduing L with her own drugs ironically started to free me of my shackles. I was able to go through L’s belonging and eventually found the binder that pointed me to Deadwood Valley. 

This world is frighteningly intertwined, and the answers I found in Deadwood Valley continues to haunt me to this day. No matter where I go, I still smell a foul stench of dog piss and wet dog. Every now and then, I still feel O shoving me into Marley’s path in a desperate attempt to save himself. 

What would have happened if I opened the gates?

There had been so many demons in my life that I was sure one of them would have already claimed what was left of me. I welcome retribution, in whatever form it wishes to take. But, perhaps, there is simply nothing worth claiming in this existence I call my life and even the devil knows it.

L wants to throw a big party for Annie’s birthday tomorrow, so I must go buy candles now.
