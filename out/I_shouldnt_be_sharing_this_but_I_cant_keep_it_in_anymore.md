I find myself needing to write, not so much as a diary, but as a testament of my story, published anonymously on internet forums. The reason: I cannot speak about this anywhere else because I would be breaking the law. I am bound by oath to keep all of this completely secret (with a few small exceptions), but it is killing me inside, and I need to free my mind and my soul from these horrible confessions and stories that my own patients have made me know.

To preserve some sense of morality in myself, I will allow myself to change the names and my place of residence. And if, for some reason, one of these accounts has been told by you, I ask that you understand that I am simply desperate. I cannot sleep at night and I need to tell it, even if only anonymously and to a group of people who most likely do not care or will not believe a single word of what I am about to tell.

Do not worry if you do not believe any of this; just telling it already makes me feel a little more free.

I have twelve years of experience as a psychiatrist, and I have remained sane and consistent. I have kept my practice open for quite some time without stopping. Well, because of the pandemic, I had some sessions through video calls, but I suppose that still counts as continuing.

However, one of my most recent cases has made me break, and above all, it made me remember all those strange and unusual things that have happened in my office.

It was my third session on consecutive days with a sixteen-year-old boy whom I will falsely call Ernesto. His parents brought him to my office because of his constant night terrors, that is, horrible nightmares that woke him up at night and prevented him from going back to sleep.

The session was not going anywhere; I asked questions and he ignored them or answered things that had nothing to do with them. It is well known that adolescence is the most difficult stage to treat, and he certainly made good use of that reputation.

However, I found myself once again sitting in front of him, repeating the questions and suggestions I had made in the previous session, but that day something was different.

“We need to open up a little,” I suggested. “We cannot spend days here without saying anything, just looking at each other.”

“I never asked to come to this place,” he answered.

He did not look me in the eyes, which caught my attention, and he only rubbed his hands on his legs compulsively.

“I know,” I replied, “but your parents think it is the best for you. They are worried, and a worried parent will do everything possible to help their child.”

“You think you can understand everything, doctor, but that is not the case.”

“Why don’t you let me decide that?” I suggested. “Come on, tell me, and I will tell you if I can understand it or not.”

He looked around a little. His shy gaze was almost tender; he looked like a kitten, a very frightened one.

“Will everything I say here stay between us?” he asked.

I nodded. I did it sincerely at that moment; I never thought that in the future I would be telling these things.

He leaned back slightly on my couch, began to look everywhere, then looked at the door to see how far away it was. I suppose he wanted to figure out if what he said could be heard by the people outside.

“No sound leaves this room,” I told him before he asked. I gestured toward the panels on the windows that prevented noise from coming in or out. “Everything you say will stay here. There are no recordings, nothing, only my ears.”

“My friends and I have a YouTube channel about anecdotes or horror stories, we try to create content, you know,” he said, making a pause. “We go to abandoned places or perform rituals that we see in other videos or in stories we read on internet forums.”

I nodded so he would know I was paying attention. It was my duty to make him feel comfortable while he spoke, but it was more than obvious that he was not comfortable; he kept rubbing his hands compulsively. I understood what he meant. I myself have seen that type of content on the internet.

“What is the name of your channel?” I asked, and wrote it down in my notebook when he answered.

“One of my friends, Ramón, had an idea: we would go to an abandoned house and make videos from inside. We all thought it was a fun idea. You know, I believed that all those videos of abandoned houses where things appear are simply staged. We also planned to throw some things or make a ‘shadow’ appear, then upload it to YouTube and let the people who watch the video be the ones to notice it, so that later we could act surprised.”

He paused, looking everywhere except at me.

“Go on,” I said.

“We went in as five people: Carlos, Oliver, Ramón, Rogelio, and me. The first minutes were normal, we just walked through the house hoping not to run into anyone, since in those places it is more the living you have to be careful of.”

“Tell me more about the house,” I asked.

“I only knew that it was a house in the abandoned suburbs, the vineyards, you know the area I mean? Next to the old canals.”

I nodded. That area was known for being nothing more than ruins of what was once a neighborhood of great wealth, but the natural canals began to create floods, landslides, and large pools of water. With them came mosquitoes, dengue, and no matter how much they tried to fix it, in the end they simply gave up. The government helped relocate the families, although they did not really need such support.

We were glad to know the house was empty, although we were completely in the dark. With flashlights, we made sure to go through the entire house looking for people. It was the typical abandoned place: brick walls with dried paint, graffiti, empty spaces where doors used to be. And a lot of trash; metal sheets, barrels, and so on.

“We recorded some videos that we would use in the future; the house was big, with at least ten rooms. We went through everything, threw some objects to create noise and suspense, we laughed while doing it,” Ernesto continued. “That was when Ramón took the next step.”

From his backpack, he took out a box and placed it on the floor of one of the abandoned rooms, among the dust and trash left by people who had been there before, even some syringes that I suppose were used for drugs. From the box, he took out a Ouija board. We all looked at it strangely; it was the first time we had seen one in person.

“What do you think if we use this?” he said.

We looked at each other for a few seconds. I had a bad feeling, but one way or another we agreed, and suddenly we found ourselves all sitting in a circle around the board, in what I suppose used to be the living room of that house.

At that moment, I had no idea where the conversation was going. I have seen movies and also those famous videos the boy was talking about, but I had always been skeptical about all that. It was more out of curiosity that I wanted him to continue with his story.

“I sat in front of Ramón. It was the two of us who started with the board. I do not know the rules of playing the Ouija, but Ramón took out a piece of paper with a name written on it, said it out loud, and burned it. Then he asked me to place my hands on the planchette and told me that no matter what happened, I should not move it, much less remove my hands. I should not press, since the movement had to be soft and involuntary. The others were recording this while we were doing it.”

My interest was growing as the story progressed. Normally I get desperate when patients go in circles without meaning in their stories, but this boy’s account felt real, it felt different, and I just wanted to know what he was going to tell me.

“We started asking the basics: ‘Is there anyone here?’ a couple of times without response. Some of the guys even made jokes, throwing things to create noise. It was at that moment that I began to get scared, the room became cold, I felt a chill running down my spine, as if something else was there with us, and suddenly I felt how my fingers were pulled by the planchette toward one corner of the board, until the words in English: ‘Yes.’”

Ramón looked at me and smiled maliciously, maybe because he was glad that it worked or maybe because he thought I had been the one who did it. The fact is that we had reached the first point, and from there everything changed.

“We began to ask questions and the board answered us, simple questions. ‘Are you a ghost?’ now that I think about it, it sounds quite stupid, however the answer was clear: ‘Yes.’”

“‘What year did you die?’ It said: ‘1869.’ ‘How?’ ‘In Chamula, because of the black stones,’ it answered. ‘What is your name?’ And it answered: ‘Yahval.’”

When my patient mentioned that name, my body filled with chills, as if something evil ran through me. I had no idea why I felt that. I do not know how to explain it. I forced myself to maintain my position as a therapist.

“We asked more questions, many of which I have already forgotten, but Ramón had another idea. He already had something prepared that he saw in an internet forum, and I remember that question perfectly: ‘Do you know the game of…?’”

He paused. I saw him again rubbing his hands compulsively. I had noticed that nervous tic since previous sessions, but now he was doing it so hard that I felt he would hurt his skin.

“The game of what?” I asked.

“I do not want to mention it,” he answered without looking at me. “I am afraid of the word.”

“That’s fine, let’s continue,” I said.

“The board answered that it did know the game, and Ramón asked if it wanted to play it. The answer was the same: ‘Yes.’”

The patient began to cry.

I offered him a tissue, which he took without looking at me. I waited a few seconds until he calmed down a little.

“Can you tell me what the game consisted of?” I tried to get some information, but he simply continued crying.

“According to what Ramón read on the internet; you must ask if it wants to play, and if the board is willing to play, it will tell you yes, which is what it did with us. At that point I still thought Ramón was moving it to create something fake and supernatural.”

He calmed down a little as he told it, and I did not take my eyes off him so he would know I was paying full attention.

“Do you know the board?” he asked me.

“I have seen it, only in movies or images, but yes, I think I know it.”

His fingers began to tremble.

“And how would we know that we are in the game?” I asked.

He looks at me, his eyes red, about to burst into tears.

“I realized when the movement became so sudden that both Ramón and I took our hands off the board. Just like the stupid challenge on the internet said, the board began to count. I just stared, stunned, at how it stood by itself on the numbers: 9… 8… 7… until someone pulled my shirt from behind and I realized that I had to hide.”

“The board was moving on its own, it counted, and then… it comes for you.”

“What comes for you?” I asked, very intrigued.

“It.”

His answer does not satisfy me.

“And what happens if it finds you?” I asked, looking for information.

“That was not on the internet,” he answered with a broken voice, holding back tears. “We all ran to hide. Some laughed, I suppose more out of nervousness… I really was afraid.”

“How would you know if it finds you?” I continued.

“We had no idea,” he answered almost shouting in desperation. After a few seconds, he calmed down.

“I ran looking for the exit, I wanted to escape from that place, but where I remembered the door being there was nothing but an old, worn brick wall. I was sure that was the exit. Either my mind was failing or that house had changed.”

“So I ran to one of the farthest rooms, where there were some metal sheets left behind. I got behind them. I could not hear anyone else, so I assumed that none of them had hidden in the same room as me.”

He paused and looked away from my face. He rubbed his hands again; his eyes were completely broken, red from holding everything in.

“I swear I heard someone enter the room where I was. It was dark, but my sight was already used to the darkness, so I would have noticed someone entering, but I only heard footsteps, very close footsteps. I could hear its breathing, slow and calm, but it did not sound like a person. It sounded like an animal; its throat growled like a rabid dog holding back from barking. It had a nauseating smell, like something rotten. Its steps sounded firm, not like shoes, it was a dry impact, as if something was hitting the floor, it was fast, it did not sound like just a pair of feet.”

“I tried not to make any sound at all, until suddenly everything stopped: the noise, the breathing, the smell stayed a little longer. I heard the voices of the others outside, I knew the time had passed.”

“I came out slowly from my hiding place, still looking everywhere. There was nothing, that presence I had felt seconds before had simply disappeared into the darkness.”

“While I walked, I kept looking for the exit, I still could not find it. I ran into Oliver on the way to the living room, he was sweating, tired, his breathing was agitated.”

“‘I cannot find the exit,’ I told him.”

He looked around with a confused expression.

“‘Let’s go with the others,’ was all he said.”

I remained silent for a moment, analyzing his story. His face was devastated; it was obvious that he was making a great effort to tell me everything.

“And what did you do?” I asked, breaking the silence.

“We all went back to the room where the board was. I asked them if they had seen or heard anything, and they all said no. They laughed, but when they saw my face, they asked me if I had experienced something, and I told them what had happened to me. No one seemed to notice that the house was not the same as when we entered, I suppose it was because of the adrenaline.”

“Instead of accepting it and ending the game, they took it as a challenge and decided to play again. I told them I did not want to, but they all insisted, they told me just one more time and we would end the game.”

“I was terrified, doctor, I really was terrified.”

He broke down crying again. I could feel that his suffering was real, but until that moment I could not find the background of all this. I knew there was something more that he had not told yet, so I wanted to keep asking.

“I did not want to sit in front of the board anymore. Oliver took my place. They asked if it wanted to play again, and the board answered yes. I watched how it moved again to nine and, successively, continued counting down. The Ouija moved completely on its own. We had the camera recording this; surely it would be a success on our channel like no other. I lost a few seconds watching how it moved, my mind began to dissociate and for a moment I could see a shadow, with a humanoid body but abnormally long limbs, kneeling in front of the board moving the planchette. To this day I think maybe it was my imagination. I got lost in that darkness, a scream from one of my friends brought me back to reality.”

“I ran out of that place to the same hiding spot. This time I did not hear or smell anything: there were no footsteps, no breathing, nothing. I simply waited until I noticed the voices of some of my friends. When I heard someone, I came out of my hiding place and went back to the room with the board. The same adrenaline was still there in their faces, we gathered again. However, there was something different this time: Oliver was missing. We looked at each other, searching for his face among us, but he was not there.”

“My patient was no longer crying. His face had become a dark portrait of a person without hope, staring into nothing, mumbling things I could not hear. He was completely devastated.”

“We searched for him in every room. There was no trace of him. The house was just as I remembered it, maybe that change I felt before was my imagination, so we searched outside, in the car, everywhere. We called his phone, which rang in one of the rooms, but it was the only thing we found.”

“For minutes we shouted his name, we looked for him telling him it was not funny anymore, that he had to come out, but he never answered, we spent hours searching and each time we were more afraid.”

“‘We should ask it,’ Ramón said. I was completely terrified; all I wanted was to leave that place.”

“We had some arguments for obvious reasons. In the end, Ramón sat in front of the board, in the same position as that shadow I swear I saw, and asked: ‘Where is Oliver?’ His hands began to move, letter by letter. The only thing it answered was: ‘I have him.’ We asked if it could release him, and it simply said: ‘No.’ We insisted in many ways, and the answer was always the same.”

“We never saw Oliver again,” he paused his story again before continuing after a sigh, placing his trembling hand on his chin. “When his parents asked about him, we told them he had plans to leave the city. They had many problems, so they believed it, although they are still looking for him.”

“But they will never find him?” I asked.

“No,” he answered.

“Now tell me, doctor,” he looked at me directly. “Do you understand it? You, who think you can understand everything, do you understand what happened, do you understand what I feel?”

“I can understand some things,” I answered, I do not know why, from a medical point of view, a possible collective hysteria, “but not completely.”

“I knew it. Thank you very much, doctor,” he said, forcing a smile. “No one who was not in that place can understand it.”

He stood up and ended the session, when it should have been me who did it, but I simply stayed seated while he walked toward the door. I had no interest in stopping him. He stopped at the entrance, with his hand on the doorknob, giving me his back.

“If one day someone comes to ask about me, about that event, that is the official version.”

“Why do you say that?” I asked. “What do you mean?”

He remained at the door, looking at nothing, and I felt a chill that raised my skin, and that same feeling of anguish and sadness I had felt before.

“What the ritual on the internet did not say was what would happen if it found you. The second time we played it found Oliver,” there was something different in his voice, that sadness seemed to have taken a break. “I was hiding behind those metal sheets when the time ran out… or maybe the counting disappeared, because it found him,” he took a deep breath, still giving me his back. “I do not remember anything else after that moment. The only thing is that suddenly we found ourselves again in the room where the board was, I was with the others. The first thing I noticed was the sticky feeling on my hands and all over my body. It was dark, but it was easy to identify what it was.”

“Blood.”

“My clothes were completely soaked; the others were the same. After checking ourselves, we looked at each other with faces full of terror.”

“On the floor was Oliver, with his abdomen open. His organs were scattered all over the floor, or at least the ones I could identify, his heart was not there, you can imagine what happened to it. There was a horrible smell of blood, excrement, and other things I cannot identify. Our faces were covered, and I could still taste it in my mouth, that metallic taste, of blood, of raw flesh, of death.”

I was completely stunned. Now I did not want him to turn because I did not think I could look him in the eyes, but I noticed that he was still rubbing his hands.

“I can still feel his blood on my hands, I can still taste it.”

Now it was me who remained silent.

“Do you want to know what we did with the remains of the body, doctor?” he asked. His voice sounded firm and without hesitation, almost as if he were another person. “Or do you know that he simply did not appear after the second game and we never knew anything about him again?”

“He was never found after the second game,” I answered with fear.

“That is the truth, even though the camera we had recording on a tripod in the room shows us cutting him apart and eating his organs, how after tearing his heart out of his chest we passed it around until we ate it all.”

He turned.

His eyes no longer reflected sadness, but a deep hatred. For a second, I felt the need to stand up from my chair and walk toward him.

“We will see each other again soon, doctor,” he said, and then he opened the door and left.

I remained frozen. I thought I was just very tired. Maybe my mind had been influenced by such a story and there was nothing strange about him. I pray to God that is the case.

But there is something strange about this case that does not let me think clearly.

I have been replaying this conversation in my mind all week, doubting whether I should go to the authorities and tell what that young man confessed to me, but fear forces me to keep it simply as a personal anecdote.

I have done some research; it is true that that young man, Oliver, is missing, therefore the story must be partly real.

The name Yahval is real, I have read something about it on the internet, I still do not have much information, it is also true that there were deaths in Chamula Chiapas Mexico in 1869 caused by obsidian stones…

“The black stones…”
