“One night. That’s how long you’ll need to spend with him.” Mr. Caswell sits at the desk in his home office, the book he gave me resting on my lap, open to page 210. Last Rites.

“Why… why me?” I ask.

Mr. Serling, standing behind me to my right, inhales a sharp breath. The old chair I’m sitting in creaks as I turn to him.

“Sylvie—”

“Sylvia.” I correct Mr. Serling.

“Sylvie, please. Listen to him.” Mr. Caswell says. I don’t correct him.

“Sylvia, you’re the only one close enough to us that we can trust.” Mr. Serling says.

“Close enough to— I barely know you! And if this is real, I have no experience doing this, how do you know I won’t mess it up?” I argue.

“Sylvie.” Mr. Caswell holds his hand out. I reluctantly put mine in it. “The less experience the better. They won’t know you, they won’t know what you’re scared of, they won’t know how you work. Plus, if you truly don’t believe it, then all that will happen is that you spend the night in the same room as a dead body.”

“...F–” I can feel myself trying to stop myself from saying what I’m about to say. “Fine. I’ll do it.”

“Great!” Mr. Serling’s black leather shoes tap on the wood floor as he takes a step towards me, putting a hand on my shoulder. “It means more to us than you’ll ever know.”

Mr. Serling squeezes my hand a little tighter.

“Sylvie, honestly, thank you. You can ask Mia for advice.”

The fact that Mia knew what her uncle’s actual job was this whole time and managed to never tell me doesn’t surprise me anymore.

I close the old, heavy book. I get off the creaky chair. I leave the room. Mia is standing outside, biting her nail, a nervous habit she’s been trying to break since we started college last year.

“So, what do you want to know?” She asks.

  
  
  


Have you ever seen a dead body? I have. Pictures, videos, even in person. Mia is my closest and oldest friend. Her uncle, Mr. Caswell, has a funeral home business with Mr. Serling. Mia, surname not Caswell, and I spent a lot of time with Mr. Caswell growing up, especially in the funeral home. It was probably an inappropriate place for kids to be, looking back on it, but there’s not much I can do about that now.

I didn’t know Mr. Serling that well. For whatever reason, he was never in the funeral home whenever Mia and I went over. I would describe our relationship as purely business-level. 

This story takes place in a Massachusetts town, the one I grew up in. It’s a very small town, small enough that you can’t so much as take a walk without bumping into someone you know. So small that when I heard the news of Mr. Cortes’ death two days ago, I could instantly place exactly where I knew him from. 

The old man who sat on the park bench by the cherry trees, letting their petals fall on the pages of his books on warm, spring days. Nabokov, Bradbury, Azimov. At times, I would notice that he was reading a writer I was learning about in school. On occasion, I’d stop to talk to him about Milton, Rawls, Bierce, or Parnok.

Now he lies in a coffin in Mr. Caswell’s funeral home.

“Well, Miss Sylvie, I’ve lived about twelve lives. I’m pretty sure god forgot to take me on the seas. He can only forget so many times.”

That was one of the last things he said to me. It was during a philosophical discussion of sorts. I suppose he must’ve stayed in one place just a little too long this time.

Anyway, earlier today, Mr. Caswell requested a meeting. I obliged and Mr. Serling joined. They asked me for a favor: spend one night with the dead body of Mr. Cortes and keep him safe. Safe from what? A curse.

Salem was not the only Massachusetts town to host witch trials. Our town did too. As Mr. Caswell told me, his ancestors prosecuted (for lack of a better term) one of these trials. A young woman named Cassandra. Before she was hanged (with a noose made of corded greenbrier vine), she called upon whatever it was that gave her magic to curse the dead. They would no longer be allowed to rest, there would only be endless suffering. When someone dies in this town, Cassandra comes to them three nights past. If there’s no one to stop her, she drags their conscious (or what’s left) to a realm of infinite torture, a punishment for the almost endless torture she endured before, during, and as a result of her trial.

So I am to stay with Mr. Cortes for the night. The book I have been given is a book of instructions, folklore, and history. The ‘Last Rites’ as they were, are the rules to fend off Cassandra’s curse. At the beginning of our conversation, I didn’t believe Mr. Caswell or Mr. Serling. But to see how serious both of them were, how desperate both of them were, I guess I could try to understand, even if I couldn’t initially force myself to believe.

  
  
  


Before I knew it, it was time. Mr. Cortes’ coffin had been moved to a small ceremonial building, with a large stained glass window on the east-facing wall. Mr Cortes’ coffin lays in the middle of the building, on a symbolic shrine of sorts. Small halogen lightbulbs hang from the ceiling. Around the room are standing candelabras, clearly well used candles dripping now solid wax down the long, golden, metal shafts.

“Of Mr. Caswell’s ancestor’s first disobedience, and the fruit of that forbidden curse, whose mortal touch brought Her into the world…” I said to Mr. Cortes, invoking *Paradise Lost*, a book we talked about on multiple occasions. He didn’t respond.

I cracked open one of three energy drinks I brought with me and set it on the altar at the shrine’s head, from which I took a lighter. I walked around the room clockwise, lighting the candles. I returned the lighter to the altar, took the energy drink and the book, and sat down in a chair on the shrine’s left side, to remind myself of what I was supposed to do.

There were tactics to repel Cassandra, but almost none of them were permanent or sustainable.

When I saw Cassandra, I was to first read Caswell’s apology, a remorseful prayer to Cassandra herself. Apparently it works some of the time. The possessions, or ‘Visitations’ as the book refers to them, aren’t the same every time. It’s been so long that she doesn’t really even remember why she takes the dead of the town. Sometimes, when reminded of her death, the apology is enough to stave her off.

If that doesn’t work, I was to remove jasmine petals from a vial and crush them, spread them on my hands, and then light a fire in a bucket, into which I would throw the remainder of those petals. This will momentarily repel her. It has a higher chance of working than the apology, but it’s not as permanent. 

At this point, if the jasmine doesn’t work, she will start to focus on me. The reason that I am alone in this ritual is because she feeds on the living to gain energy. So if there’s two or more people in the building, she has enough power to resist them. 

God, this dress is warm. As part of the ritual, I am wearing a very traditional, very heavy, very light yellow dress with a very tight corset around my torso. It’s a sort of symbolic thing. I didn’t mind. Mia talked me through the ritual as she was plaiting my hair (and tightening the corset). She put a hibiscus flower on the left side of my hair. I felt silly scrolling on my phone and drinking an energy drink looking the way I did. I was dressed straight out of the 1800s and I was scrolling twitter.

It had been a while in the building, and nothing had happened. I had just finished my energy drink when I felt a cold hand lightly embrace my exposed shoulder. I jumped out of the chair, whipping around to look at the visitor. There she stood. I could feel my heart rate rising.

Cassandra, with a quizzical look, stares at me. She stands tall, my eyeline reaching her collarbone. Her face and arms were a pasty white, her body cloaked in a long, white dress, with black shoes poking out of the bottom. Her long, black hair spills down either side of her strangely entrancing face, like a waterfall of ink. Damming that waterfall, on the top of her head rested a woven crown of flowers, the purplish blue petals providing the only color on her person.

Her eyes drift from my face to Mr. Cortes as if she remembered what she came for. A look of determination stretched across her face. She raised her right arm and started moving across the floor. Not walking, running, jumping, or in any other visible way moving, but gliding. I rush to the altar and take out a small piece of paper.

“Cassandra!” I yell, getting her attention: “Cassandra, robbed of innocence, of liberty, and of life, cease your insatiable battle! What Caswell thought of was justice we now know to be the pinnacle of injustice! Your death opened a debt which the families of your murderers have paid several times over. We humbly beg you to show us mercy in the absence of ours. It is only yourself that keeps you from rest.”

My voice shattered the deafening silence of the hall. The four walls that seemed to swallow all noise previous to Cassandra’s visit.

“Bold.” The single word she says echoes all throughout the hall. The apology didn’t work.

She turns back to Mr. Cortes, muttering an incantation. The process of taking a person with her takes quite a while, so I have some time.

Quickly, I went to the altar, pulling from its cabinet a bucket, paper, and a vial of jasmine petals. Though my hands were shaking from the adrenaline rushing through my body, a result of the terror that was setting in, I was able to remove the petals, rub them in my hands, and throw them in the bucket with the paper. I light a piece of paper and throw it in. A light haze emerges from the bucket. The scent of jasmine makes its way to my nose, and evidently, to hers as well.

She recoils and drops through the floor. If I had any suspicions over whether or not she was really, they had been completely squashed.

“What the fuck.” I slump back in the chair. I can feel my braid starting to loosen.

After a second, I look to my left. I can see her hand coming out of the ground by one of the candelabras. As she floats up, her hand gingerly traces the height of the candelabra, fingers gliding from the metal, to the melted wax, to the candle, to the flame. She reaches out for the fire, closes her hand over the wick, and when she opens it, the candle is smothered. She takes the fire from multiple candles.

I make my way back to the altar, and pull out a small vine of greenbrier. I hesitate for a second, but I eventually bring myself to put the small vine in my mouth. I can feel the thorns digging into the inside of my mouth, but the vine is essential for what comes next.

With the only light coming from the small, hanging halogen lights, Cassandra turns to me. She raises her hand and though I am ten feet away from her, I feel myself get pushed over, falling back. As I fall, the brier slightly cuts the roof of my mouth. Before I hit the ground, I am stopped in mid-air.

As I hover, I can feel her get closer. The fear inside me is begging me to move, to flail my arms, to kick, but I can’t. She flies parallel above me. Her hair falls down onto my face. She slowly gets closer.

The first thing the book told me she would do was go in for a ‘death’s kiss.’ Supposedly, the last thing she did in life was kiss Caswell’s ancestor, a sort of mark of death.

I move the brier to the front of my mouth as her hands, cold as a corpse, run along my cheeks. Before her lips make contact with mine, the brier cuts them, a reminder of her painful death.

She recoils and screams. I drop to the floor. I manage to not hit my head on the wooden planks, but I can’t say the same for my lower back. I take a moment to look at Cassandra. She looks like her death has finally caught up with her. In the place of the formerly pale, clear-skinned gorgeous woman in the snowy white dress, sits a decaying body, rotted skin falling off her aged skeleton onto a tattered, greyed, and dirty dress. The crown of flowers was wilted and rotting.

I watched her in front of my eyes gradually shift her appearance to look like I first saw her. Her skin slid back across her skull, the dress mended its rips, and the grease dripped out of her hair.

I checked to see if I could still feel my legs. When I confirmed that I could, I got up. She got up too.

We stare at each other.

She screams. She screams so loud it feels like my head is going to explode.

Instead, the hanging lightbulbs burst. Some of the glass cuts my arm.

Its complete darkness except for the moonlight shining through the stained glass window.

Through the darkness, I find my way back to the altar, pulling a big canister of salt out. 

I glance at the opposite side of the hall to see that Cassandra has gathered a couple of the candles from the candelabras. She puts them in a pile, and hovers her hand over it. I put the salt in a circle around me, and threw some on Mr. Cortes. She mutters something incomprehensible. The wax melts and the puddle catches on fire.

She catches on fire. The far side of the hall catches on fire. I start panicking, but I don’t leave the circle. The fire is bright, almost blinding. I can feel the heat, and sweat starts pouring out of me. 

Through the blinding brightness of the fire, I can see someone walk out. Not Cassandra, but a man. A giant, muscular man, bearing the antlers of a deer. He wears a blue, flowing cloth, and through the fire, I can see his green, piercing eyes.

He slowly walks towards me. The fire spreads quicker. I feel every urge in my body, every deep, pounding terror in the depths of my heart screaming at me to leave. To get out while I still can.

But I don’t. I fall to the floor to keep myself from running. I sit as tears form in my eyes and the fire and the man creep towards me. The fire reaches the edge of the salt circle and…

It doesn’t cross the salt.

The man runs at me.

I instinctually flinch back.

He stops before he reaches the salt. He leans down to look at me. I can’t handle looking into his eyes anymore. I look away as he screams.

I’m propping myself up with my hands. I feel that my hand is on the salt behind me. I stare at my hand. The fire has completely enveloped the circle. I lift my hand and I put it out of the circle into the fire.

It doesn’t burn. 

I look back up at the man. He’s no longer there.

The book instructed that after she focused on me, she would create visions. This is why it was important that she didn't know me.

Cassandra stands above me, in the place of the man. The fire quickly recedes back to the wax pile. The wax pile quickly re-forms itself back into a pile of candles. Her face is redder. Tears are streaming down it. She turns away from me.

She’s not coherent in the noises she proceeds to make, but the emotion was pure confused despair.

Very slowly, a light cuts through the darkness. Beams of red, green, and blue shine through the stained glass window.

Cassandra stares at the stained sunlight. She slowly walks towards me, bends down, and picks up a handful of salt from the circle. She throws it at me. I try to block some of the salt with my hand.

She laughs.

She turns away from me and sinks through the floor.

I get up and walk to the door. I look back at Mr. Cortes one more time. I don’t believe in god and I don’t believe that I just met the devil. I believe I met a human, deeply wronged in her life.

I open the door and let the sunlight touch my skin.
