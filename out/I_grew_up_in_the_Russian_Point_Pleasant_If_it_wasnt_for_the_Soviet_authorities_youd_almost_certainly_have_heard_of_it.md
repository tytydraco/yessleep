I was born and raised in an aul (fortified medieval village) in the Russian region of Dagestan made up of stone houses on the side of a mountain with their front yards on the roofs of the houses below. There was a single school, a single police station, a single mosque, and a single unpaved road serving 400 residents. Many of the old folks had never traveled anywhere else, and even I hadn't until I went off to college. It was lonely, but it was home to everything and everyone I knew.

The older one is in the Caucasus, the more respect they are given, and thus, elders frequently exaggerate their ages. One woman, Patimat, claimed that she was almost 800 and fought against the Mongol invaders in the 1200s, although only little kids believed her. Regardless, she was ancient, possibly over 100, which was made clear by her frizzy gray hair, blindness, and wrinkle-covered face. I'm not sure if she ever had a family-I believe she had a husband who died before I was born and a son who had long since moved away-but she was a grandmother figure to the village children, who'd spend hours at her house listening to stories of war heroes, forbidden romances, and ferocious beasts. There was one such story that she only told to them when they misbehaved-the legend of the goat demon, a goat-headed man with a coat of white fur, muscles big enough to rip limbs off, and claws and teeth prepared to sink into human flesh. "And if you don't stop being so naughty, he might just come for you in the night!" she'd always say at the end, chuckling.

I was fifteen when Patimat passed away in April 1972. Though I hated looking weak in front of Khadizhat, the girl I liked, I cried my eyes out at her funeral, and it took me four or five hours to fall asleep that night. I couldn't stop thinking about her until one June afternoon when a young man named Shamil returned from a hike, his terrified face covered in blood. Shamil, who was about 25, had a wife, was expecting his first child, worked as the village doctor, and was in every way a respectable member of society. He went to the police office and told them that he had been startled by what looked like a nine-foot goatman, which let out a blood-curdling shriek and chased him for several miles, hurtling rocks at him. As he was flown out for treatment, the townspeople discussed what might have happened-we wanted to think rationally, but we knew that he would never lie to us. That night, at about 1 AM, I was awoken from my sleep by the most horrifying sound I have ever heard-imagine a woman's scream mixed with the sound a cat makes when you step on its tail, turned up so loud that your ears physically hurt. At first, I thought I was dreaming, but it was followed by heavy footsteps on the roof and what sounded like chewing noises. Though it only continued for about ten minutes, I spent the rest of the night carefully listening for more, my heart racing.

The next morning, Ayshat, the woman ​who lived above me, claimed she had seen the same goatman Shamil had reported eating a live deer at the same time I had heard the scream. Now it was clear that something was out there. I didn't go to sleep at all that night-I sat wide awake in my bed, listening for the horrible noises. At exactly the same time, I heard them again, except this time, they were even closer. And then, I saw it. Out on the patio, the goat demon was eating a fox and staring out into the night sky. It was about nine feet tall, with muscles so large that its chest must've been five feet wide, and it was covered from head to toe in long, shaggy hairs. Its fangs glimmered in the moonlight, and two curved horns stuck out from its head. I wanted to scream, but I was completely frozen in place-I don't think my heart was even beating. For the briefest moment, it looked at me before disappearing  into the darkness.

It continued like this for months-every night, the entire village would hear the demon's scream, and someone would see it on their patio. A few days after the sightings started, some of my friends went out to kill it, only for their half-eaten corpses to be found at the bottom of a creek. The next week, a young couple met the same fate while having a romantic picnic on the side of the mountain. No one dared go out at night-then a little boy reported seeing the demon in broad daylight, and school was cancelled for a week. One elderly woman even claimed that it broke into her house and killed her husband. At the same time, all sorts of weird phenomena began-strange lights were spotted in the sky almost every night, people received phone calls from long-dead relatives, suicide rates skyrocketed, and the town's water supply began tasting sweet. Perhaps most bizarrely, people began acting erratic and out-of-character. My father went from being extremely clean-mouthed to swearing like a sailor, and fistfights regularly broke out on the street.

All in all, 36 people were killed by the goat demon, not to mention dozens of goats, sheep, and chickens. Several people tried to shoot it, but the bullets bounced right off its skin. In September, while looking through Patimat's house, my friend Magomed came across a book entitled "Rituals to Harness Holy and Unholy Powers" that contained instructions on how to do just about anything-kill someone, make someone fall in love with you, ensure a bountiful harvest-through magic. After showing it to the rest of the village, they agreed to perform an ancient pagan exorcism in hopes that it would drive the demon out. Dressed in all white, a group of men hiked down the mountain, beheaded a goat, and allowed its blood to pour out into the river while reciting prayers in the Lezgin language. The goat demon was never seen again. It wasn't long before the media heard about what was going on-and, for whatever reason, the government did everything they could to prevent them from revealing the truth. A reporter named Giorgi stayed with us for several weeks, but he disappeared upon his return to Tbilisi; most of us still believe he was murdered. In early October, a group of KGB agents arrived at the mayor's house and demanded to speak to the entire village; when he agreed to call an emergency meeting, they told us anyone who revealed the truth would be arrested. That was 54 years ago, and almost everything has changed for me since-I now work as a high school math teacher in Seattle, and I have a wife and two grandchildren. This story has been suppressed for decades, but I believe the time has come for the entire world to hear it.
