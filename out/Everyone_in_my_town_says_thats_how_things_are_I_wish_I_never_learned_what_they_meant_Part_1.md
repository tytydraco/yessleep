I don’t know if anyone here will believe me.

But I need to write this down before I forget what it felt like before I understood.

I think I was eight. Maybe nine.

The sun was high that day. Too bright. Like the sky was trying too hard to look normal.

Ma sent me out to do my usual rounds.

Chickens first.

I hated those things. Meaner than anything with feathers had a right to be.

But that day… they weren’t chasing me.

They were all gathered in the back of the coop.

Quiet.

That’s what I remember most.

Chickens shouldn’t be quiet.

I stepped in, grabbed the eggs, and that’s when I heard it.

A wet sound.

Like something thick… popping.

Not loud. Not sharp.

Just wrong.

Every bird froze.

Then they started screaming.

I turned, expecting to see something—anything—but there was nothing there.

Just a damp patch of dirt where they’d been gathered.

I didn’t stay.

I didn’t want to think about it.

The rest of town felt off too.

I didn’t have the words for it back then.

Just knew something wasn’t right.

A man arguing with a wall.

Mrs. Winthers washing the same shirt over and over.

Dogs that never shut up… silent.

Even the air felt heavy.

Like it didn’t want to be breathed.

I ran into a Marshal on my way to the grocer.

Big man.

Heavy.

Like he’d been carved out of something harder than bone.

His coat dragged through the dust. Feathers tucked into his hat. Gun belts wrapped tight around him.

He carried a sack.

It was wet.

I smelled it before I got close.

Rot.

Sweet and sour at the same time.

I froze when I bumped into him.

Thought that was it.

Thought I was done.

He looked down at me.

Those eyes…

Too deep.

Like there was more behind them than there should’ve been.

For a second, I thought he didn’t see me at all.

Then he reached out.

Tapped me on the head.

And walked past.

I finished Ma’s errands quick.

Didn’t stop to play.

Something in my gut told me to go home.

But the rail yard was on the way.

And it was my favorite place.

It was always loud.

That day…

it was empty.

No kids.

No noise.

No nothing.

Just that smell.

Thick.

Heavy.

Waiting.

I went inside the station.

I don’t know why.

I should’ve left.

I saw Mr. Grant first.

Or… part of him.

His legs were sticking out from behind the counter.

I called his name.

No answer.

I stepped closer.

And that’s when I saw him.

The Marshal.

He wasn’t standing right.

His body didn’t hold together the way it should.

Parts of him looked like they were slipping.

Like something underneath was trying to come out.

The sack was open.

I couldn’t see inside it.

Not clearly.

Just movement.

And that sound.

That wet…

popping.

He turned his head.

Slow.

Like it took effort.

For a second…

I think he recognized me.

His mouth moved.

Tried to say something.

But all that came out…

was that sound.

I ran.

I don’t remember leaving.

Just the feeling of my legs moving faster than they ever had.

I made it home.

Burst through the door.

Tried to tell Ma everything.

She listened at first.

Looked worried.

Asked if I was hurt.

But as I talked…

her face changed.

The worry left.

Then everything else.

By the time I finished, she was just… blank.

She put her hands on my shoulders.

Not tight.

Not gentle.

Just there.

She didn’t really look at me.

Not all the way.

And she said—

“That’s how things are.”

Then she let go.

And walked away.

I went to the sheriff.

The mayor.

Anyone who would listen.

Same answer.

Every time.

“That’s how things are.”

I remember sitting alone after that.

Trying to understand.

Trying to make it make sense.

And that’s when I heard it again.

That sound.

Not from the station.

Not from the coop.

From somewhere in town.

Nobody reacted.

I almost ran.

But I didn’t.

I just sat there.

And listened.

I don’t know how many days passed after that.

I stopped counting.

Didn’t see the point.

They buried Mr. Grant.

Or said they did.

People smiled.

Laughed.

Like it was something to celebrate.

They buried the Marshal too.

Or what was left of him.

People clapped.

That’s when something changed in me.

Nothing felt right anymore.

Food tasted like nothing.

Sunlight felt fake.

Even laughing felt hollow.

But the smell…

That stayed.

I started wanting it.

I went back to the coop.

More than once.

Just stood there.

Waiting.

Breathing.

Nothing ever came.

So I went back to the station.

I found the sack.

I don’t know how I missed it before.

I opened it.

Empty.

But the smell…

It was still there.

I leaned in.

And I breathed.

Deep.

I didn’t stop.

I wear it now.

All the time.

I cut holes in it.

Small ones.

Just enough to see.

A slit for my mouth.

Nothing for my nose.

That would defeat the purpose.

The smell is stronger like this.

Closer.

Heavier.

After a few days…

it changed.

I started smelling things that weren’t there before.

Blood.

Rot.

Wet earth.

And when they mixed…

I don’t have words for it.

But it felt right.

Better than anything else.

The other kids stopped coming near me.

Didn’t matter.

Ma wouldn’t look at me anymore.

That bothered me.

I tried to tell her about it.

She left before I finished.

Same as everyone else.

Nancy listened.

She didn’t run.

Didn’t call me crazy.

She just asked what it smelled like.

I told her to try it.

When I took it off…

everything felt wrong again.

Too bright.

Too loud.

Too empty.

She put it on.

At first she gagged.

Then she breathed.

And she said—

“I get it.”

I think that was the happiest I’d felt in days.

After that…

things moved fast.

Faster than I could think.

I remember saying—

“I want all of it.”

And then we were running.

Through the streets.

Nothing stayed whole.

Nothing stopped us.

It felt right.

Like everything finally made sense.

Until I smelled it.

Something clean.

Too clean.

We followed it.

To the center of town.

The Marshal was standing there.

Like nothing had happened.

Nancy moved first.

I heard it.

A crack.

She hit the ground.

I saw her then.

Really saw her.

She wasn’t the same.

None of us were.

I went for him.

Nothing worked.

He moved too fast.

And the whole time…

he looked at me like he felt sorry.

That was worse than anything.

Then my leg gave out.

Heat spread through me.

Up my body.

Into the sack.

That’s where it hurt the most.

Like something was being taken back.

I fell.

Couldn’t move.

Couldn’t breathe.

For the first time since I found it…

I felt everything.

The emptiness.

The world without it.

The Marshal stepped closer.

Slow.

He leaned down.

And he took it off.

I felt all of it.

Every part of me.

Every nerve.

I tried to speak.

He didn’t react.

He just said—

“That’s how things are.”

Then—

a single crack.

I don’t hear the sound anymore.

But sometimes…

when it’s quiet enough…

I think I can still smell it.

And I think—

it found somewhere else to go
