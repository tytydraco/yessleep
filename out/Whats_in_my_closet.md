I remember running through a dark hall in my dreams as a kid.

I would always wake up at the same point.

I never got to the end of the hall.

Something always stopped me.

The dreams faded around the time I was eleven. I’m twenty-five now. I still have nightmares occasionally—different ones—but never about the hallway.

At least that was true until last week.

I didn’t recognize the hallway at first.

I recognized the presence of it.

The air felt wrong. Heavy. Familiar. And then the smell hit me.

That sickly sweet smell.

You don’t forget a smell like that. It’s the kind that sticks somewhere deep in your memory, waiting. The kind you don’t think about for years—until you’re back in the room where you first noticed it.

In that space.

And it remembers you.

I started running on instinct, just like I always did when I was a kid.

Running and running.

Only this time the hallway didn’t seem to be ending.

There was no point where the panic would spike and rip me awake. No invisible wall. No sudden stop.

So I slowed down.

Then I stopped.

That was new.

When I was younger, I never felt like I had a choice. I ran because that’s what I did. It wasn’t bravery or fear. It was automatic.

Standing still felt wrong.

But I forced myself to look around.

That’s when I saw the doors.

They lined both sides of the hallway, evenly spaced, stretching forward into the dim gray distance.

I don’t remember there being any doors before.

Maybe I ran too fast to notice.

They were ordinary. Almost identical. Plain wood. Cheap hinges. Some were painted slightly different colors, but most were the same dull beige as the walls.

A few of them were slightly ajar.

And one of them had deep gouge marks carved into it.

Not scratches.

Gouges.

Long, uneven trenches torn into the wood like something had tried to claw its way through.

I don’t know why, but I felt pulled toward that one.

The closer I got, the stronger the smell became.

That sickly sweet scent.

There was no mistaking it now.

It wasn’t coming from the hallway.

It was coming from behind the door.

I crouched down slowly.

The gouge marks weren’t random.

They were low.

About three feet from the ground.

As if someone small had made them.

Like a child.

I stared at the splintered wood for a long time.

The grooves weren’t torn outward.

They were carved inward.

Whatever made them—

was trying to get in.

And that’s when something else hit me.

When I was eleven, the dreams stopped.

I don’t remember reaching the end of the hallway.

I don’t remember opening a door.

But I also don’t remember ever running again.

I don’t know how long I knelt there.

I don’t know what made me do it.

But I reached for the handle.

It turned easily.

It was never locked.

And from the other side—

I heard footsteps.

Running.

Toward the door.

I woke up with a violent jolt.

For a moment, I didn’t know where I was.

I forced myself to breathe. To count. To remember.

Then I saw it.

My closet door.

It rattled softly at first.

Just a tremor.

Like something brushing against it from the inside.

I told myself it was settling wood. Old hinges. A draft.

Then it hit again.

Harder.

A dull thud that made the frame shiver.

The knob twitched.

Another impact.

Pounding.

Not random.

Not chaotic.

Measured.

As if something small was throwing its full weight against the door.

Over and over.

The door shook so violently I thought it might splinter off the hinges.

And beneath the noise—

faint but unmistakable—

that sickly sweet smell began to fill the room.

I lay there until it stopped.

Not all at once.

Just… less.

Then silence.

When morning finally came, I waited longer than I needed to.

Then I stood up and walked to the closet.

I opened it.

Nothing inside but clothes and shoes.

No smell.

No movement.

I almost convinced myself it had been a dream.

Until I looked at the inside of the door.

Three deep gouge marks.

Low.

About three feet from the ground.

Cut inward.

I don’t remember ever being locked in a closet.

But I stopped having the hallway dream when I was eleven.

And tonight,

I don’t think I’m going to run.

Or maybe it won’t let me.
