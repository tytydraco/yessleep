I don’t remember adding it to my watchlist.

That’s the part that still bothers me, almost as much as the channel itself.  I *know* I didn’t add it.  I’m meticulous about my queue.  I have a system.  I know what’s on my list.  And on the morning of February 15th, sitting at the top of like it had always been there, was a channel called *Static*.

Not a show.  A channel with its own dedicated page on the platform, the way Netflix or Hulu has a landing page, except this one wasn’t branded with any studio or network I recognized.  It didn’t have a logo or banner image.  Just the name in a font I’d never seen the platform use before — thin, uneven letters, like someone had scratched them into the screen with a pin.  Below it, a row of episodes with no descriptions, runtimes, or ratings.  Just innocuous-sounding titles.

*The Birthday Party.*

*Sunday Service.*

*Backyard.*

*The Basement Tapes.*

Sixteen episodes total, not arranged in any particular order as far as I could tell.

I almost scrolled past it.

I wish I had.

The first one I clicked was *The Birthday Party.*  Twenty-two minutes long.  I remember that because I checked afterward, trying to make sense of what I’d just watched.  Twenty-two minutes shouldn’t be enough time to leave you feeling the way I felt — like someone had scooped something warm from behind my ribs and left cold air in its place.

The episode opened on shaky handheld footage.  It was a home video, unmistakably from the mid-90s.  You know the look, right?  That warm, slightly blown-out quality of a camcorder that cost somebody three paychecks at Circuit City.  Tracking lines shivered at the bottom of the frame.  The date stamp read **06/15/1996** in blocky amber digits.

It was a backyard in the summer.  A folding table was draped in a plastic tablecloth printed with balloons.  Paper plates, a bowl of Doritos, a sheet cake from a grocery store with *HAPPY 7th BIRTHDAY EMMA* written in pink icing that had already started to sweat in the heat.  Kids were running through a sprinkler in the background, shrieking the way kids do when they’re so happy it sounds almost like screaming.

A woman’s voice sounded like it came from behind the camera.  It was warm and a little nasally, the kind of mom-voice that makes you ache for something you can’t name.  She was narrating the way parents do when they’re trying to document everything and enjoy it at the same time.

“Okay, okay, there’s the birthday girl!  Emma, honey, wave to the camera!  Show everybody your new swimsuit!”

A little girl with wet hair and a gap-toothed grin waved.  Emma, presumably.  She was missing her two front teeth and seemed delighted about it.  Normal.  Painfully, achingly normal.

For about nine minutes, it was just... a birthday party.  A dad was working a charcoal grill, swearing quietly when the lighter fluid flared.  A golden retriever ate cake off the ground.  Two boys fought over a Super Soaker.  The camera panned lazily from one small, ordinary moment to the next, and the footage had that quality that real home videos have and movies never quite capture.  The slight un-focus.  The way the person holding the camera keeps forgetting they’re recording and lets it drift toward the ground.  The background conversations you can only half hear, layered on top of each other like radio stations blending together.

I was almost bored.

Then, at the nine-minute mark, the camera swung toward the back fence.  It lingered there for just a moment.  It was probably an accident, maybe the mom adjusting her grip.  But in the far corner of the yard, half-hidden by an overgrown hydrangea bush, someone was standing on the other side of the fence.

I felt the hair on the back of my neck rise.  Someone was *standing there.*  Still.  It had its hands at its sides and seemed to be watching the party.

The camera moved on, and I thought I might have imagined it, because nobody reacted.  The footage continued with Emma opening her presents.  She got a Barbie Dream House and screamed so loud the audio clipped.  Everything was fine and completely normal.  Then, I saw it again. 

A shape in the kitchen window at 11:04. 

A shadow that didn’t match anyone in the yard at 13:22.

And at 16:47, during a wide shot of the kids playing freeze tag, a figure standing at the far edge of the frame, motionless among the running children. It was too tall to be a child and too still.  Facing the camera while everyone else faced away.

No one at the party acknowledged it.  The mom kept narrating.  The dad kept grilling.  The dog kept eating cake.  The footage played out like a preserved memory of a perfect summer day, and threaded through was something that did not belong there, something patient and watching and wrong.

The video ended with the mom turning the camera on herself.  She was a laughing, sunburned woman in her mid-thirties, beaming and squinting into the lens.

“Say bye, everybody!”

A chorus of kids’ voices. “Byeeeee!”

Behind her, in the sliding glass door’s reflection, clear as a photograph, I saw a face.  It was a pale, indistinct oval, there and gone in the space between one frame and the next.

There were no credits at the end of the episode. It was just a hard cut to black and the faint hiss of dead air, like a tape reaching the end of its spool.

Of course I looked it up.  I tried to anyway.

But I didn’t find anything.  There wasn’t a single result on Google, Reddit, Letterboxd, IMDb, or anywhere else.  I searched “Static channel,” “Static streaming,” “found footage birthday party 1996,” “Emma birthday party horror,” but there was nothing.  I searched the platform’s catalogue directly, and the search returned zero results.  But when I went back to my watchlist, there it was.  The channel page with the same row of episodes.  All sixteen of them, waiting.

I tried to share it, but the share button was grayed out.  When I copied the URL, my browser returned a string of characters that didn’t look like any web address I’d ever seen — a bunch of long unbroken chains of numbers that scrolled and scrolled when I pasted them into a document.

My roommate, Casey, was in the kitchen making tea when I carried my laptop out to show her.  I set it on the counter, turned the screen toward her, and pointed.

“Look at this weird channel — *Static*.  It just appeared on my watchlist.  Have you ever seen it?”

She looked at the screen.  Then at me, slightly confused.  Then back at the screen.

“What channel?”

“Right *there*.”  I tapped the screen. “At the top of the list.”

Casey picked up her mug and took a sip.  She gave me the look she usually reserves for when I tell her I can’t find my sunglasses and they’re perched on top of head.

“Jane, the top of your list is a documentary about cheese.  *Curd Your Enthusiasm*, seriously?  A channel called *Static* is lot less weird than that.”

I turned the laptop around.  No, the cheese documentary was in the second position.  And what’s wrong with wanting to learn about caseiculture?  I was standing, looking down at my own screen, *Static* sat above it, clear as anything with the title scratched in white.

She couldn’t see it?

We laughed.  She told me to go outside and touch some grass.

But that night, lying in bed with every light off and the apartment ticking and settling around me, I opened the app on my laptop, navigated to the *Static* channel page, and scrolled to the second episode.

*Sunday Service.*

The thumbnail had that same washed-out quality.  This time, it was a church interior, shot from the back pew, amber date stamp glowing in the corner.  **03/08/1998.**  There were rows of heads bowed in prayer, and in the far left of the frame, half-cut by the edge, someone in the congregation appeared to be sitting backward in their pew and facing the camera.  Facing me.

I snapped the laptop screen closed, heart pounding.  Every rational part of my brain was composing a list of very logical reasons not to watch it.  But the other part — the older part, the part that still listens for sounds in the basement, the part that understands on some primal level there are things in the dark that already know your name — that part was urging me to watch it.  I knew I shouldn’t, but I opened my laptop up and pressed play. 

*Sunday Service* was seventeen minutes of a church camcorder recording from a small Baptist congregation somewhere in the American South.  White walls, sun shining through stained glass windows, hymnals in the pew backs, and a choir in maroon robes singing a song I recognized.  The camera operator must have been a church volunteer, dutifully recording the sermon for homebound members.  You could see the tripod wobble when they adjusted the framing.

The pastor was a heavyset man with a kind face.  He preached about mercy and forgiveness. He said that God sees us even in our darkest rooms when we think we’re alone.  The congregation murmured *amen* and fanned themselves with programs.  There was no backward-facing person among them.  It was so deeply, specifically real and almost familiar that it hurt to watch.  These were real people in an actual church on a Sunday morning in 1998, and someone had recorded it.  Now, it was here, on this channel that might not exist for anyone but me, on my screen, in the dark.

I almost convinced myself it was just an old tape.  Some digitized church archive that had gotten swept into the algorithm, like the other home movie of Emma’s birthday party, by mistake.  The footage was grainy and mundane, and I was so caught up in my musings about that simpler time and place I forgot about the figure I had seen in the preview.

Until the hymn.

The choir stood for the closing number.  It was something traditional with the word *glory* in it, and as they sang, the camera slowly began to pan to the right, away from the choir and pulpit.  Toward the side aisle, where the pews gave way to a narrow corridor that led to a back room.

The corridor was dark.

Not unlit-dark or shadow-dark.  The kind of dark that looks like it has texture, like you could reach into it and feel it push back against your hand. And standing at the threshold of that darkness, just barely visible, was a figure facing the congregation with hands at its sides.

The same posture as the one behind the fence at Emma’s birthday party.

Everyone was standing and singing, hymnals in hand.  Nobody looked toward the corridor. The camera held on the figure for eleven seconds.  I don’t know why I counted.  Then, the tape cut to static.  Real static, the roaring gray blizzard of a dead signal, and buried in it, so faint I had to lean in to hear it, was a sound.

Not breathing this time, but a whisper.

I couldn’t make out the words.  I played it back four times with the volume maxed, my ear pressed to the speaker, and each time I caught a different fragment, or at least I thought I did.  Something that might have been there or might have been nothing at all, just the audiological pareidolia of a frightened mind looking for patterns in noise.

The episode ended abruptly.

I closed my laptop and stared at the ceiling until the room started to turn gray with early morning light.  Somewhere outside, a bird began to sing.

Over the next three days, I watched four more episodes.

I need you to understand that I didn’t want to.  Every time I opened the app, my stomach clenched and my hands went cold and some deep, sensible part of me screamed to close it and delete the app.  But I couldn’t stop.  It was like pressing on a bruise or picking at a scab or standing at the edge of something high and feeling gravity whisper *what if you jumped*?  The tapes were in the app waiting for me, and the not knowing somehow seemed worse than the knowing. 

*Backyard* was a cookout in what looked like rural Virginia in the summer of ‘93.  Somebody’s dad teaching a kid to throw a football.  For the first twelve minutes, it was aggressively wholesome.  Beer cans in koozies, a woman in a lawn chair reading a mystery novel, neighbors talking about fishing.  Then, at 12:15, the kid threw the football into the tree line at the edge of the property, and when the camera followed it into the shadows between the pines, there was a dark shape.  It wasn’t standing this time.  It was sitting cross-legged on the ground, relaxing just inside the trees like it had been there all afternoon.  The kid jogged right past it to retrieve the ball without acknowledging it.

The camera lingered for three seconds, then whipped back to the cookout.  The dad was laughing about something.  The world was normal, and the shape in the trees was already fading from the frame almost like it had never been there.

*The Basement Tapes* was different.  It was shot in a finished basement, and judging from the décor, it seemed to be from the ‘90s like the other episodes.  The room had wood paneling, shag carpet, a console TV with rabbit ears, and there was no one in it.  Not at first.  The camera sat on a surface, filming the empty room, and for eight straight minutes, nothing happened.  Then, so slowly I wasn’t sure it was happening at all, the tracking lines at the bottom of the frame began to creep upward.  The image destabilized, and in the static between the frames, for just a flicker, there was a different room I didn’t recognize.  It looked like a concrete room without windows.  There was something on the floor that might have been fabric or…

The tape corrected itself.  The basement was back and normal and empty.  But the camera had moved so that it was facing a different wall, one that hadn’t been visible before, and on that wall, written in what I chose to believe was marker, were three words:

**YOU FOUND US.**

I closed the app.  I closed the app and deleted it and cleared my cache and restarted my laptop, and when the home screen loaded and I looked at the spot where the app had been.  It was back.  I opened it and saw the *Static* channel was still there.

Eighteen episodes now.  There had only been sixteen.

I started taking pictures.  I needed proof that I wasn’t imagining it.  So I went back through every episode I’d watched and paused on every frame where the figure appeared, and I took a picture of each one.

They came out wrong.

The screenshots captured the footage fine.  But where the figure should have been, there was just... a smear.  A slight flaw in the image every single time.  The birthday party fence: an almost imperceptible smear.  The church corridor: the same kind of smear.  The tree line at the cookout: smear.  It was as if whatever was in those frames existed in a format my phone couldn’t translate.

I tried video recording instead.  It was kind of the same thing.  The playback looked normal, the rooms and people and the warm amber light of the time and date stamp, but the figure was gone.  It wasn’t smeared like in the picture.  It was just absent, as though it had never been there.  I watched the recordings side by side with the originals on the *Static* channel page, flipping back and forth, and it was the most disorienting thing I’ve ever experienced.  In the original: figure.  In the recording: empty space where a figure should be.  As if it only existed inside the channel and could only be seen live, in the moment of watching, like it needed my eyes looking directly at it to be real.

I told Casey.  She listened, which I appreciated, and then she told me I’d been working too hard, which I did not appreciate.  She suggested I talk to someone, which meant a therapist and was probably fair.

I didn’t talk to someone. I watched the next episode.

*Piano Recital.* **12/11/1994.**

A middle school auditorium with folding chairs and a battered upright piano on a low stage with a curtain that didn’t quite close all the way.  The camera was handheld, sitting in someone’s lap by the look of it.  You could see the edge of a knee, a program clutched in fingers, the occasional jostle when the person shifted in their seat.

A boy who was maybe twelve years old walked to the piano.  He had brown hair, a clip-on tie, and the kind of terror that only a child performing for an audience can experience.  He sat down and adjusted the bench with the exaggerated precision of someone stalling for time.  Then, he began to play.

It was Chopin, Nocturne in E-flat major.  I recognized it because my grandmother used to play it on Saturday mornings when I spent the night at her house, standing in the kitchen in her bathrobe with the record player going.  The memory hit me so hard and so fast that I had to pause the video and sit with it for a moment.  The boy was pretty good.  His tempo wavered and he fumbled a transition in the second phrase, but he played with the earnest, teeth-clenched concentration of someone who had practiced this piece a hundred times and was now praying his fingers remembered what his brain was too scared to.

The audience was sparse.  Parents, mostly.  A few grandparents.  Someone’s younger sibling was kicking the back of the chair in front of them.  The camera stayed on the boy for the first four minutes, and then drifted the way cameras do when someone’s been sitting in a folding chair too long toward the left side of the auditorium, where the emergency exit was propped open with a rubber doorstop.

There was a hallway just beyond the door.  It had fluorescent lights, half of them dead, casting that queasy institutional glow that seems to illuminate every public school in America.  There were linoleum floors and rows of lockers.  At the far end of the hallway, where the light gave way to shadow, I could see the figure.  Hands at its sides, facing the auditorium.

I wasn’t surprised anymore.  That was the strange part.  I’d crossed some threshold between fear and recognition, and what I felt now was closer to a grim, exhausted familiarity.  *There you are.  Of course you’re here.  You’re always here.*

But this time the figure moved.

Not a step or gesture, nothing so dramatic.  Its head tilted.  Just slightly, a few degrees to the right, the way a person tilts their head when they’re listening to something.  Or the way a dog tilts its head when it hears a frequency humans can’t detect.

The boy kept playing.  The Nocturne swelled into its middle passage, and the figure in the hallway tilted its head.  It seemed to be listening.

Then the camera shifted back to the stage, and the boy hit the final chord — a little too hard and loud, the sound of relief more than artistry.  The audience applauded, and someone shouted, “Way to go, Simon!”  The tape suddenly ended.

There was no static this time.  No whisper or dead air either.  Only the screen cutting to black.

I sat in the dark for a few minutes, lost in thought.  The figure had *listened.*  Whatever haunted these tapes had stood in a dark hallway in a middle school in 1994 and listened to a twelve-year-old play Chopin, and something about that made me feel deeply unsettled.

What does it want?  What does any of this mean?

I didn’t sleep that night.  I opened my laptop and started searching again, using different terms — “streaming channel no one else can see,” “show that only appears for one person,” “found footage channel haunted.”  Page after page of nothing.  Creepypasta wikis.  Nothing like what I was watching.

I was about to give up when I found the post.

It was buried on a forum I’d never heard of — not Reddit or 4chan, nothing with a name I recognized.  One of those old-school message boards with a dark background and small text that looked like it hadn’t been redesigned since 2006.  The kind of place you only find when you’re fifteen pages deep into a search engine at 4 AM.

The thread was titled: **“does anyone else have this channel”**

No capitalization.  No punctuation.  Posted eight months ago by a user called **tape\_head\_0.**

The post read:

*i know this is going to sound insane and i know no one is going to believe me but i need to know if this is happening to anyone else. about six months ago a channel showed up on my streaming service. not a show. a channel. it’s called Static and it has episodes that look like old home videos from the 80s and 90s. there’s something in them. in the background. a person or a figure or something that nobody in the videos can see. i’ve tried to show it to people and they can’t see the channel at all. it only shows up for me. the episode count keeps going up. please if anyone else has this i need to know i’m not losing my mind.*

Four replies.

The first was from someone called **no\_signal\_99**, posted five months ago:

*I have it. I’ve had it since March. The figure is in every episode. It’s always watching. It never interacts with anyone in the footage. The people in the tapes can’t see it. I’ve watched 23 episodes. There were 14 when it first appeared. Now there are 31.*

The second was from **vhs\_ghosts**, posted three months ago:

*Found this thread after searching for two weeks straight. The channel appeared on my account in July. I tried creating a new account to get away from it. It was already there on the new account when I logged in. Same episodes. Same order. Has anyone else noticed that the footage is getting more recent? My first episodes were from the early 80s. The latest ones are from 2005, 2008. It’s moving forward.*

The third reply was from **tape\_head\_0** again, posted six weeks ago:

*Update. I’m at 40 episodes now. vhs\_ghosts is right — the footage is getting more recent. The latest one was dated 2019. And there’s something else. In the newer episodes, the figure isn’t just in the background anymore. It’s closer. In the 80s tapes it was always at the edge of the frame, barely visible. In the recent ones it’s in the room. Standing behind people. Sitting in chairs. I watched one from 2016 where it was standing directly behind a woman while she washed dishes and its face was right next to hers, almost touching, and she had no idea. I can’t stop watching. I’ve tried. I can’t.*

The fourth reply was from **no\_signal\_99**, posted nine days ago:

*Has anyone gotten to the episode with no date stamp? I need to know what happens after the episode with no date stamp. Please. If anyone has gotten past it, tell me what happens. I can’t watch it again. I’ve watched it three times and each time*

The post ended there. Mid-sentence.  No period.  No follow-up.

I checked **no\_signal\_99**’s profile.  Their last login was nine days ago — the same day as the post.  Nothing since.

I checked **tape\_head\_0**.  Last login: six weeks ago.  The day of their final update.  Nothing since.

**vhs\_ghosts**: last login three months ago. The day they posted. Nothing since.

Every single person who had responded to the thread had posted once or twice and then vanished.  The profiles were still there, the posts were still visible.  They had simply stopped logging in. Stopped being online entirely, as far as I could tell.

I created an account.  My hands were shaking so badly I had to retype my password three times.  I clicked “Reply” and stared at the empty text box for a long time, the cursor blinking like a heartbeat, before I started typing.

*I have the channel. I’ve watched seven episodes. The figure is in all of them. I have questions. Is anyone still here?*

I posted it and sat back in my chair and waited.  The forum said there were eleven members online.  The view counter on the thread ticked upward — 112, 113, 114.  People were reading it, but no one replied.

I refreshed the page every few minutes for an hour.  Then every few minutes for another hour.  Around 5 AM, with the sky going gray and the birds starting up again, I refreshed one last time.

There was a new reply. It wasn’t from any of the previous users.  It was from an account created that day, minutes before the post.  The username was a string of numbers I didn’t write down and couldn’t find again later when I tried.

The reply was one line:

*You’re at seven. Stop at seven.*

I sat with those words for a long time.

*You’re at seven. Stop at seven.*

The account that posted the reply had been created four minutes before the message appeared.  No other posts.  No profile information.  Just a string of numbers for a username that I wrote down on a sticky note and stuck on the corner of my nightstand, where it stayed for two days before the adhesive gave out and it drifted to the floor and I never found it again.  When I went back to find the account, the profile page returned a 404.

But the message was still there.  Glowing on my screen at 5:17 in the morning.

I closed the laptop.  I went to bed.  I lay in the dark and stared at the ceiling, and for the first time since the channel appeared, I did not watch another episode. 

I made it through the next day.  I went to work.  I ate lunch at my desk.  When I came home, Casey was making pasta and we sat on the couch and watched a cooking competition show that was so ordinary it made me feel almost normal again.  Casey glanced over at me at one point and said, “You look better.  Less haunted,” which she meant as a joke, and I laughed because what else was I going to do, and I went to bed that night feeling like maybe I could just … not.  Maybe I could leave the tapes alone.  Maybe seven episodes was enough.

I made it through the second day, too.  Barely.

On the third night, I opened the app. 

I told myself I was just going to look at the channel page.  Not watch anything.  Just … check.  The way you check your phone after a fight even though you know there’s no new message. 

The episode count had changed.  When I’d last looked, three days ago, there had been eighteen episodes.  Now there were twenty-two.  Four new episodes had appeared while I wasn’t watching.  The channel was growing on its own, adding to its library, including new tapes alongside the old ones whether I was there to see them or not.

I scrolled through the new titles.  Most followed the same pattern as the others — simple, domestic, the kind of names you’d give home videos if you were organizing them on a shelf in the living room.  *First Snow.*  *Thanksgiving.*  *The Waiting Room.*  But the eighth episode, the one that had been next in line when I’d stopped, was called *Graduation Day.*

I pressed play.  I want to be the kind of person who didn’t press play.  I am not that person.

The footage was different immediately.  Sharper.  The warm amber haze of the earlier tapes was gone, replaced by the cooler, flatter quality of a mid-2000s camcorder.  The date stamp read **06/03/2007** in small white digits, not amber.  Even the font of the timestamp had evolved.  The tapes were getting closer to the present, just like **vhs\_ghosts** had said.

It was a high school graduation, an outdoor ceremony on a football field.  There were rows of white folding chairs baking in the sun, a temporary stage with a podium draped in the school’s colors.  The camera was handheld, seated somewhere in the middle of the family section, and whoever was holding it kept adjusting the zoom with the uncertain enthusiasm of a parent who hasn’t quite figured out digital.

A man’s voice came from behind the lens, “There she is.  Third row, next to the tall kid.  Jess, wave!”  A girl in a cap and gown, maybe a dozen rows back in the sea of graduates, raised her hand and wiggled her fingers without turning around, the way teenagers acknowledge their parents in public with a minimal gesture of love. 

For the first ten minutes, it was a graduation with the typical speeches about futures unfolding, a valedictorian who made a joke about senioritis that got a bigger laugh than it deserved, and a the principal mispronouncing names while parents cheered loudly.  The camera drifted between the stage and the rows of graduates, and I kept scanning for the figure in every shadow and every edge of the frame. 

But I didn’t see it in the background this time.

I saw it in the audience.

At the eleven-minute mark, the camera panned right to follow a graduate walking across the stage, and as it swept past the family section, there it was.  The figure was sitting in a folding chair a few rows ahead and slightly to the left.  Among the parents with its hands in its lap, shoulders straight, facing the stage like everyone else. 

It was sitting with them.  In the bright June sun, surrounded on both sides by people in lawn chairs with programs and water bottles and disposable cameras, and not one of them looked at it.  A woman in a sundress next to it was clapping and cheering, her elbow nearly brushing its arm, and she didn’t even seem to notice it.

In the older tapes, the figure had been a shadow.  Something I could almost explain away if I tried hard enough, like a trick of the light or an artifact of the degraded footage.  But in the cleaner, sharper image of a 2007 camcorder, it was different.  I could see it clearly for the first time, and what I saw was wrong.  The proportions were almost right.  The posture was almost human.  But something fundamental was off, something my brain could register but not name, like a note played slightly out of tune in the middle of a chord.

At fourteen minutes, the graduates began filing off the stage.  They streamed down the center aisle between the family sections, caps askew, diplomas raised, grinning and whooping.  The camera followed Jess as she walked past the row.  “That’s my girl!” the dad shouted.  As the camera tracked her, the figure entered the frame again, still seated while everyone around it stood. 

Its head was turning.

Slowly.  Not following the graduates or the movement of the crowd.  Turning toward the camera, toward whatever was on the other side of it, which in 2007 was a proud father with a camcorder, and in my apartment at 1 AM was me.

The camera held on it for five seconds.  I don’t know why I counted.  I couldn’t see its features.  It’s hard to explain.  The image wasn’t blurred exactly, but it was as if the space where a face should be refused to resolve into focus.  Then the footage cut to black, and the episode was over.

No static.  No whisper.  No dead air.  Just a clean cut to nothing, which was somehow worse, because at least the static had felt like an ending.  This felt like a pause.

I went back to the forum.  I needed to tell someone — **tape\_head\_0**, **vhs\_ghosts**, **no\_signal\_99**, anyone — that the figure had looked at the camera.  In all the other episodes it had been watching the people in the tapes.  In this one, it felt like it was watching me.

The thread was gone.

Not just the replies, the entire thread “does anyone else have the channel” was gone like it had never been there.  **tape\_head\_0**’s original post, the replies from **no\_signal\_99** and **vhs\_ghosts**, my own post asking if anyone was still there, the warning from the account made of numbers — all of it was gone.  The URL still worked.  The page loaded.  But where the thread had been, there was just the forum’s default empty state, the dark background and the small text and a message that read: *This thread has been removed or does not exist.*

I searched the forum for “Static.”  No results.  I searched for “channel.”  Nothing relevant.  I searched for each username individually and got three blank profile pages that confirmed the accounts existed but showed no post history, no activity, no trace that any of them had ever written anything at all.

My own post was gone too.  My account still worked.  I could log in and see my username in the corner of the screen, but my post history was empty.  As if I’d never typed those words.  As if I’d never, at 4 AM with shaking hands, asked the void if anyone else could see what I was seeing.  Had I imagined it?

I almost closed the tab.  But out of habit, or maybe compulsion, I checked the “Recent Posts” feed one more time.

There was a new thread.  It had been posted two minutes ago by an account I recognized.  It was the same string of numbers that had replied to me before and told me to stop at seven episodes.  The thread had no title or body text.  It was just a single reply from the same account, posted one minute after the thread was created:

*You didn’t stop.*

I could feel my heart pounding as I stared at those three words until my vision blurred.  I closed the forum and opened the *Static* channel page, because apparently that’s what I do now.  I make very bad decisions as a lifestyle. 

The episode count read twenty-three.  It had been twenty-two when I’d pressed play on *Graduation Day* less than twenty minutes ago.

I scrolled down to the newest episode.  The thumbnail was small, the way they all were, a single still frame in the row.  But something about it snagged my eye the way a hangnail catches on fabric.  The color was different.  Not the warm amber of the ‘80s and ‘90s tapes, not even the cooler palette of the 2007 footage.  This was pushed toward blue — a cold, digital blue, the color of a screen glowing in a dark room.  And there was no date stamp.  Every other episode had one, those little digits anchoring the footage to a specific moment in time.  This one had nothing.  Just the image.

The thumbnail showed a room.  A small room, shot from above and at an angle, like a security camera or something mounted near a ceiling.  I could see the edge of a couch.  The corner of a bookshelf.  A coffee table with something on it that might have been a laptop.

I leaned closer to the screen.  The image was too small and too compressed to make out any details, but the arrangement of the furniture, the way the light fell across the floor, tugged at something in the back of my skull.  Not recognition exactly.  More like the feeling you get when you’re driving and a song comes on that you haven’t heard in ten years, and for the first two bars you can’t name it, somehow you already know it.  The melody is already playing in your chest before your brain catches up.

I didn’t click on it. 

I sat there instead, in the dark, in my apartment, with my laptop open on the coffee table casting its blue light across the room, and I watched the episode count.  Twenty-three.  I didn’t touch anything.  I didn’t scroll.  I just watched the number. 

It changed to twenty-four.

I hadn’t refreshed the page.  I hadn’t touched the keyboard or the trackpad.  The number just … increased, the way a clock ticks over to the next minute.  Silently and inevitably.  And sitting there watching it happen, I understood something that I think **tape\_head\_0** and **no\_signal\_99** and **vhs\_ghosts** had all understood before they went quiet. 

The tapes aren’t for you to find.  *You* are for the tapes to find.

I closed the laptop slowly.  The room was dark.  The apartment settled and ticked.

And somewhere behind me in the dark hallway, in the space I couldn’t see without turning around, I heard the softest sound.  Something like the hiss of a tape reaching the end of its spool.  And then, just as quickly, nothing at all.  Had I really heard it?

I didn’t turn around.

I’m writing this from a coffee shop three blocks from my apartment.  It’s 11 AM on Friday.  There are people around me.  There is sunlight coming through the window.  Everything is normal and bright and I can hear someone ordering an iced chai latte with pistachio cold foam, and there is nothing wrong.

Except that when I opened the app this morning to check, the episode count was twenty-nine. The newest thumbnail was that same blue-tinted room, the same angle from above, and this time I could make out a shape on the couch.  A person.  Curled up with a laptop, face lit by the screen.

I’m going to post this here because I don’t know where else to put it, and because I need someone — anyone — to tell me what happens next.  The people who knew are gone.  The forum thread is gone.  The warning I didn’t listen to is gone.

All that’s left is the channel.  And it’s still growing.
