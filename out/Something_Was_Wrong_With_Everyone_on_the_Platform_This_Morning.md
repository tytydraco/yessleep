I come from Singapore and we have a particular relationship with our phones in this country.

I say this without judgment. I am as guilty as anyone. The moment I step onto the MRT platform, the phone is out. It is a reflex, like reaching for an umbrella when clouds gather. We have optimised ourselves for the commute: earphones in, shoulders slightly forward, the small bright rectangle held at the precise angle that signals to the world that we are occupied and not to be spoken to.

I have commuted on the Purple Line for eleven years. I know the rhythms of it. I know which carriage to board for the shortest walk to the exit at my destination. I know the particular shuffle of the morning crowd, the way people press forward without quite touching, the collective exhalation when the doors slide open. I know how it sounds: the recorded announcement, the chime, the low ambient sound of people not quite talking to each other.

I know what normal looks like on that platform. That is why I noticed when it wasn't. And I wish, with some regularity, that I had been less observant that morning.

It was on a Tuesday. I know because I had an early meeting, which is why I was on the platform at seven fifteen instead of my usual eight. The crowd was thinner than I was used to, which might be why it took me a moment to register what was wrong.

Everyone was on their phones.

That wasn't the wrong part. That was just a regular Tuesday morning.

The wrong part was the screens.

I noticed it first on the man standing two metres ahead of me. He was holding his phone at the usual angle, tilted slightly upward, both thumbs resting on the screen. His face had the glazed, pleasantly absent expression of someone deep in a feed. But his screen was black. It wasn't dimmed. It wasn't locked, and it sure doesn't show a dark background with a faint time display. Black. The way a screen looks when it has been switched off entirely.

I looked at the woman beside him. Same thing. Black screen, face forward, the posture of engagement without the content.

I looked around the platform. Every single screen was black.

Something happened in my chest before my mind had time to process it. You know that feeling when you do a freefall? My body understood what it was looking at before I did, and my body, in that moment, wanted to leave.

I am a reasonably rational person. I am a communications professional and that means I am attuned to body language, pattern, and the ability to distinguish signal from noise when it comes tp human behavior.

The first question I had in mind was whether there  a network outage. If the mobile network had gone down simultaneously for all carriers, every phone on the platform would be a black rectangle. It would explain the screens. It would not explain the expressions.

They were smiling.

Not all of them in the same way. The man two metres ahead had a small, private smile, the kind you get when you recall something pleasant from long ago. The woman beside him was smiling more broadly, her teeth slightly visible. Further down the platform, a teenager in a school uniform had her eyes half-closed and her lips curved in an expression of deep, sleepy contentment.

None of them were looking at anything. Their eyes were open but unfocused, directed at the black glass of their screens without appearing to see it.

I stood very still and watched them for a long moment.

The platform was silent in a way that platforms are not silent. The usual ambient noise was absent: no one tapping at a screen, no tinny audio leaking from someone's earphones, no one clearing their throat or shifting their bag. The announcement chimed and the recorded voice named the arriving train and the doors slid open, and people filed in with the usual efficiency, and not one of them looked up.

I should have walked away. I want to be clear about that. Every reasonable instinct I had was pointing toward the exit, toward the surface road, toward anywhere that was not this train.

But I had an early meeting. So I followed them in anyway, because I did not know what else to do.

Inside the carriage, it was the same.

I found a sit near the door and watched. Forty, perhaps fifty people in my carriage alone, all with their phones out, all with black screens, all with that particular expression of quiet, private satisfaction. A few had their earphones in. I could not hear anything coming from them.

The man sitting beside me was someone I recognised, a neighbour from my block who I saw occasionally at the coffee shop downstairs. We had never spoken beyond the nod of people who share geography without sharing lives. He was holding his phone in both hands and smiling at the black screen with an expression I had never seen on his face in waking life. He looked, for lack of a better word, peaceful.

I leaned forward slightly and looked at his screen.

It was not entirely black.

There was something there, at the very edge of visibility, the way an image becomes visible on a piece of paper when you hold it at an angle to the light. I couldn't make out what it was from where I was sitting. I leaned closer.

The man's eyes moved.

He didn't turn his head. His expression didn't change. But his eyes shifted, slowly and smoothly, from the screen to my face. He looked at me with the calm, unfocused gaze of someone who is mostly somewhere else, and his smile remained exactly as it was.

I sat back hard. The woman beside me shifted at the movement. My pulse was loud in my own ears, and I became aware, with some embarrassment, that I had stopped breathing at some point between leaning forward and sitting back. That is the part I have never been able to rationalise away: not the black screens, not the smiling, but the fact that my body registered something in that gaze that my mind still cannot name.

He looked back at his screen.

I rode three stops past my destination before I got off. I don't know why. At each station, more people boarded, and each one had their phone out by the time the doors closed, and each screen was black, and each face arranged itself into that expression of gentle, sourceless contentment.

I walked back to my office along the surface road. It took forty minutes. I was late for my meeting. I told my colleague the train was delayed, which was not a lie exactly.

That evening, I took a taxi home.

I have thought about it every day since.

The rational explanations have not held up. I checked: there was no network outage recorded that morning, no reports of unusual incidents on that line, no posts on community forums about anything strange. The commute happened and was forgotten, the way commutes are, by everyone who was on it.

What I have not been able to explain, and have not tried to explain to anyone, is what I saw when I finally looked at my own phone that morning. I had taken it out at some point on the train, the way you do, the reflex. I don't remember doing it. I only noticed it when I was standing on the platform of the wrong station, watching the train doors close.

My screen was black.

And I was smiling.

I can feel it still, at the edges of my morning. The faint memory of a peace so complete it had no room for anything else.

That was three weeks ago. I need you to understand what I mean when I say it has not stopped.

I have started arriving at the platform earlier. I tell myself it is because I want to avoid the crowd, but the crowd has nothing to do with it. I stand at the edge of the yellow queue box and I take out my phone and I wait. Most mornings, the screen stays bright. I scroll through the usual things. I board my train. I get off at the right stop.

But some mornings, the screen dims on its own, and I feel something arrive at the edges of my chest, something that is not quite peace but is in the same family as peace, and I understand, in those moments, exactly what expression was on those commuters' faces.

Last Thursday I found myself at a station two stops in the wrong direction with no memory of boarding. My phone was in my hand. The screen was dark. My thumb was moving across the glass in small, slow circles.

I was not distressed. That is what I cannot explain to you. I was not frightened or confused or in any discomfort. I felt the way you feel at the end of a very long sleep, when you are not yet fully returned to yourself, and the world seems gentle and far away.

The doors opened. I got out. I took a bus home.

This morning, a woman I have never seen before was standing on the platform when I arrived. New commuter, I think, the uncertain posture of someone still learning the rhythms. She was watching the crowd the way I once watched it, with the mild alertness of a person cataloguing unfamiliar territory.

She looked at me for a moment. Then she looked at my phone.

I looked down. The screen was black. My thumbs were still. My face felt strange and I can't explain why. I raised my hand and touched the corners of my mouth.

I was smiling.

I took the earphones out of my bag. I put them in. I pressed the button for the next train. She was still watching me when the doors closed.

I don't know what it is. I don't know if it is something that spreads, or something that waits, or something that was always here and the phones are just the newest surface it has learned to use.

What I know is this: some mornings the platform is very quiet, and the screens are dark, and the faces around me have that expression, and I am part of it now in a way that I cannot undo.

I am not sure I want to.

That is the part that should frighten.
