Hello there, you.

I’ve been a taxi driver for years now, but never has someone quite like you sat in my back seat.

“Will you take me home? I live at 14 Willow Creek Drive… the house opposite the park…”

Your words drag and blur together, slowed by the alcohol. You shouldn’t be out like this. Not alone. The world is full of people who don't have your best interests at heart.

“I’ll have you there in no time,” I say. My voice is practiced, steady the sound of a man who is exactly who he claims to be.

The car pulls away, tires humming against the damp asphalt. You sink into the seat, shoulders swaying slightly to the radio, your eyes half-closed. You’ve already forgotten I’m here. To you, I’m just part of the machinery. Part of the car.

I watch you in the mirror. You’re not like the others I pick up at 2:00 AM. No glitter, no stumbling heels. Just a button-up shirt… crisp, deliberate. Neat, almost careful.

Different.

You reach up and undo the top button, tilting your head back to let the air hit your throat. My grip tightens on the wheel. *Is this for me?* You could have asked for the A.C., but you chose to show me that instead.

“Ugh… you’re going to hate me…”

I glance back. Guilt is written all over your face, soft and messy. I wonder what you’re about to say and if I’ll even be able to hear it over the sudden rhythm in my chest. Your blue eyes are wide, glassy with drink, looking right into mine.

“I… I forgot my phone at the party… could we…?”

Are you making puppy dog eyes at me? You’re looking at me like I’m your hero. How could I ever say no to those eyes?

I laugh a soft, harmless sound and swing the car around. In the rearview mirror, I catch your smile. It’s small. Knowing. You think you’re playing me. You think you have the power here.

We pull back into the neon glare of the club. You lean forward, touching my shoulder just a graze of fingers on fabric as you tell me you’ll be right back.

*What are you doing to me?*

Time passes. Too much time. When you finally return, the light has gone out of your face.

“I can’t find it. Will you help me? I’ll give you the number… you call it, and I’ll listen for where it rings…”

Again with the eyes.

I unlock my phone and hand it over. It feels like handing you a piece of my soul. You start typing, then pause, looking up at me through your lashes.

“If you’re going to have my number… I should probably know your name.”

“Luke,” I say.

“Veronica. But my friends call me Vee.”

“Am I a friend?”

You smile. “More like… an acquaintance. You haven’t earned the Vee privilege. But since you’ve been so helpful… you can call me Ronnie.”

A privilege. I like that. I’ll earn it. I’ll earn all of them.

You hand the phone back. For a moment, it feels heavier than it should. Before I can say anything, you turn and hurry off again with small, quick steps.

I look down at the screen. You saved yourself as “Cute taxi girl.”

Do you even know what you’re doing to me?

I snap out of it. Right. The call. I press the number, already imagining different reasons to hear your voice. A better reason. A normal one. The kind where you’d want me to call just to say goodnight.

The line clicks. Your laugh spills through the speaker effortless and bright.

“I’m coming back to the car!” you chirp.

That sound… it does something to me. Something I can’t quite explain. Then I see you running back, waving slightly. Careless.

I notice the way the fabric of your shirt moves. *Are you not wearing a bra?* Ronnie, once we live together, I will make sure you never leave the house like this. The thought of someone else watching you, the way people look, the things they think… makes my stomach turn.

They don’t deserve to see you. One day, I won’t let them.

You climb back into the car, smiling like nothing in the world could touch you. But I can. And I think… deep down, you know that.

You slide back into the seat, out of breath, holding your phone up like a prize.

“Found it,” you say, dropping it into your lap.

Of course you did. I nod, a little slower than I should.

“Good,” I say. “That’s good.”

But something about it doesn’t feel good at all. The engine hums as I pull out of the parking lot again. This time, there’s no reason to stay. No reason to turn around. No reason to delay.

So why does it feel like something’s ending?

You unlock your phone immediately, your face lighting up in that soft, digital glow. Your thumbs move quickly typing, scrolling, laughing quietly to yourself.

Not at me.

I watch you in the mirror for a moment too long. You don’t notice. Or maybe you do… and you just don't care.

“Long night?” I ask.

“Mhm,” you reply, distracted. “I’m exhausted.”

Your attention never leaves the screen. That bothers me more than it should. You were talking to me before. Looking at me. Smiling at me. Now I’m just… the driver again.

I take a turn. The correct one, this time. You don’t even look up.

“Do you always work this late?” you ask suddenly, still staring at your phone.

“I don’t mind it,” I say. “You meet… interesting people.”

You hum in response, barely listening. My fingers tighten slightly around the wheel.

“I’m glad you picked me up, though,” you add absentmindedly. “Some drivers are weird.”

I almost laugh. Almost.

“Yeah,” I say. “Some are.”

The silence stretches again, heavier now. The kind of silence that presses in from the windows. We pass a streetlight. Then another. Willow Creek is coming up.

Too soon.

I glance at you again. Still smiling at a screen. Still somewhere else.

“Do you always trust strangers this easily?” I ask.

That gets your attention. You look up, meeting my eyes in the mirror for just a second. There’s a pause. A tiny flicker of something is it realization?

Then a small shrug. “I mean… it’s your job, right?”

*Your job.*

I nod slowly, turning onto your street. Of course. Just my job.

The houses line up neatly quiet, dark, and unsuspecting. The park sits across the road, empty and shadowed at this hour.

“Here’s good,” you say, gathering your things.

I stop the car. For a moment, neither of us moves. You reach for the door.

“Hey,” I say.

You pause, looking back.

“Be careful, alright?”

You smile politely. Not warmly. Not the way you did before.

“Yeah,” you say. “You too.”

You step out, closing the door with a soft, final click. And just like that, you’re gone. Walking up the path. Not looking back.

I sit there longer than I should. Watching. Memorizing.

14 Willow Creek Drive. Opposite the park.

I already knew that, of course. I've known for weeks. But now I know the sound of your laugh through a phone.

I look down at my screen. Your contact is still open. *Cute taxi girl.* My thumb hovers over the name.

You said I hadn’t earned it yet. The Vee privilege.

I smile to myself and shift the car into gear.

“That’s alright,” I murmur to the empty back seat.

“I’ve got time. I wont miss your yoga classes tomorrow morning. I'm sorry for missing them today, Ronnie, but...you saw me yesterday...I don't want to scare you...”

I pull away slowly, the headlights cutting through the dark of the park, already planning my outfit for tomorrow. I like looking good for you, even if you don't see it. 

  
Goodbye for now, Ronnie.
