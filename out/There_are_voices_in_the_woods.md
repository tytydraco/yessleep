It was May twenty‑first when I finally decided to send in an application. I had been going back and forth on whether or not I should sign up to do the dream job of my childhood. When I was younger, I thought being a camp counselor would be amazing — working out in the woods, doing something I loved, and even getting paid for it. But over the years, the idea of having no internet, no sleep, and barely decent food started to seem like a pretty bad deal. Still, it seemed better than making burgers at the time, so I sent in the application.

Within an hour, I got an interview, and shortly after that, an offer: two hundred and fifty dollars a week. At the time, that seemed like a pretty good deal, so I signed all the paperwork and waited for summer to begin.

Shortly after June arrived, we went over most of the basics — CPR, first aid, all that — and everything seemed to be moving smoothly. But about halfway through the season, in week five, we hit a roadblock.

Every week, two cabins with about ten kids each and four counselors would set out into the woods to camp. Everyone brought sleeping bags, pillows, and all the necessities for the night. One way or another, something always went wrong — storms, failed fires, you name it. This week was no different.

There was a kid in our cabin named Tommy. He was a good kid, but tonight something was off. I asked him what was up.

“I need to go number one,” Tommy said.

“We’re in the woods,” I replied. “Find a tree or something.”

“I can’t. He’ll get me,” Tommy muttered.

I stared at him, thinking it was a joke. Finally, I asked, “What is going to get you, Tommy?”

“You know… the guy out there. Haven’t you heard him yet?” he asked.

I looked at him, dumbfounded, waiting for a smile or something to tell me he was kidding. “No. What are you even talking about, Tommy?”

“Never mind. All that matters is I’m not going out there,” he said.

I brushed it off and tried to find someone to go with us to take him to a port‑a‑john about a mile out, around the lake. None of the campers wanted to go — some looked terrified, presumably because of this ‘man in the woods.’ The other counselors begged me and the counselor on break to take Tommy before we left. I convinced my co‑counselor Jerry with the offer of my last white Monster in my backpack.

So we started our trek. We’d pass the girls’ group on the way, so we figured we’d get some clarification on this weird rumor.

We were about 300 feet from the bathroom when we reached the girls’ campground. We heard laughs, yells, stories, gossip about the boys, and songs being sung. But when we rounded the corner, everything was silent.

Sleeping bags, tents, hammocks, and a smoldering fire sat untouched. Food was left out. Not even the animals had touched it.

Tommy’s eyes went wide. “It was him! I told you he was real! He got them, and he’s gonna get us next!”

Jerry rolled his eyes. “They probably just went down to the lake for a cookout.”

“But the food is still here,” Tommy whispered.

“Stop worrying, Tommy,” I said. “They’re probably on a night hike. The teens do it all the time.”

“Okay… I guess,” Tommy said.

Luckily, the porta‑potty wasn’t far. We walked Tommy down and waited outside.

I whispered to Jerry, “What the hell, man? Why weren’t they there? What could’ve happened?”

Jerry whispered back, “I don’t know. Just stay calm. We can’t scare Tommy. We’ll get back to the campfire and radio leadership.”

Night was falling fast. I flicked on my flashlight — something moved. I gasped as a raccoon climbed a tree. Jerry laughed as Tommy walked out.

“What’s so funny?” Tommy asked.

“Oh, nothing. We were just joking about last week,” Jerry said.

“I think it’s time to go back,” I said. “I don’t want it pitch black while we walk.”

We headed back toward the girls’ campground. Everyone was back like nothing had happened.

Jerry freaked out, yelling about how they didn’t tell anyone where they were going and how worried we were. The girls burst out laughing. One of the counselors, Emmily, explained:

“We decided to scare the boys, but it didn’t work. We thought you found us, but clearly we scared some of you tonight.”

We stared in disbelief. We couldn’t believe we fell for it. We took some water and headed back as they continued giggling.

As we got farther from the fire, it got harder to see. My flashlight was dying, so I started swapping batteries. Tommy suddenly shouted, “Look! He’s right there! I saw him!”

Jerry groaned. “Tommy, there’s nobody out here but us. You just can’t pee in the woods like everyone else.”

“Leave him alone,” I said. “We’ve been through enough tonight.”

“No, for real,” Tommy insisted. “I saw him. He was right over there.”

“Well, Tommy, there’s nothing I can do right now. I don’t have service, and the radio is back at camp. We have to get there to get help.”

“Okay… but keep your eye out,” Tommy stammered.

We made it back with no more sightings. I saw the bright fire and heard the usual whispers of the group getting ready for bed. As we got closer, a loud static noise came from the campground — but it was empty again.

The noise stopped. We searched around and found the radio. Jerry flicked it on.

“This is Jerry. Has anyone heard anything about the boys’ sleepout group?”

A moment passed.

Then Emmily replied, “Nope. What, did they go missing too?” She was clearly mocking us.

“Yes, Emmily, they did. Was this some kind of joke again?” Jerry snapped.

“Good one, Jerry. I’m not that dumb,” she said, followed by giggles. “Have a good night, guys.”

“No — don’t you dare turn the radio off!” Jerry yelled.

No response.

We were on our own.

We tried to reach leadership, but it was too late. They were probably asleep — or gone.

We decided to go directly to the girls’ camp.

We grabbed water and headed into the dark. All of us were petrified. We hoped it was a joke, but deep down we knew it wasn’t.

Near the girls’ camp, Tommy whispered, “What if they aren’t there again?”

“Don’t worry,” Jerry said. “This is all one big joke.”

“But what if it isn’t? I saw the man earlier. I think he caused all this.”

“Everything is going to be fine. This was just a joke taken too far,” Jerry said.

We reached the girls’ camp and found the counselors’ setup. Emmily was on the ground, staring into the darkness like she was watching something.

“Emmily?” I said.

She shrieked and tackled me.

“Emmily, stop! What happened? Where are the other counselors?” Jerry asked, pulling her off.

“I don’t know,” she said, shaking. “The girls kept talking about something in the woods. I thought they were joking. But then he struck. It was like lightning — taking one after another. All of them but me. He just stared at me for what felt like hours… then he was gone.”

She kept darting her eyes around. Tommy began to cry. Jerry tried to console him while I talked to Emmily.

“We need to get to the lodge,” Jerry said. “Call the cops. Get out of here.”

“What if he finds us?” Tommy asked.

“He’ll definitely find us if we stay here,” I said.

“But—”

“We’re going, Tommy. No questions.”

We made our way back toward the cabins. Suddenly we heard noises — shouts, yells. Tommy sprinted toward the cabin. I bolted after him, but Jerry grabbed me, saying it was too dangerous. I shoved him off and ran after Tommy.

But when I looked back, Tommy was gone — and a shadow stood in his place.

“Get away from this place and never come back,” the shadow said. “Do not speak a word of what you saw today, or I will find you. And you will regret even thinking about this day.”

“What are you?” Jerry asked.

The shadow stepped forward. It wasn’t a man. It wasn’t human. It looked like a deer standing on its hind legs, but instead of hooves, it had malformed hands covered in deer hide, with antler‑like appendages replacing the fingers. Its eyes were empty. A thick black liquid dripped from its mouth like oil. It bucked its head and shrieked before disappearing into the woods.

We ran to the lodge without looking back. Fear, grief, and confusion hit me all at once. What was that thing? Why did it let us go? Where were the kids? Were there any survivors?

Then we saw it.

The lodge was ablaze. Kids shrieked. Adults yelled. The building collapsed, and then there was only the crackling of fire.

We stood frozen. I fell to the ground and passed out. When I woke up, there was a huge gash on my head, and Jerry was dragging me away from the ashes.

We made it to the parking lot. Emmily started her car, and we drove off. Once we reached the country roads, we finally got service. Jerry dialed 911 — and a misshapen deer jumped into the windshield.

That’s when we knew we were taking this to our graves.
