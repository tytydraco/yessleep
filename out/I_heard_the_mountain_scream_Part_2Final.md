I made it to the other side of the town and entered the forest, and after walking another mile or two, I was standing at the foot of the mountain. 

Now that I was closer to the holes, I noticed how peculiar they were. They weren’t just holes that someone could’ve made using a hammer and chisel. These holes were circular, and that’s not to say that the holes vaguely resembled circles; they were flawlessly made circles, as if they had been drawn using a pair of compasses.

I watched as a man exited one of the small holes carrying a sack over his shoulder. Being as short as he was, I could have mistaken him for a 3-year-old; only the abundance of hair plastered on his face told me otherwise. He effortlessly climbed down the mountain, walked over to a covered wagon, pulled off the cover, emptied his bag into the wagon, and covered it again before heading back up the mountain and into a different hole.

I cautiously walked over to the wagon and lifted up the cover myself. Instantly, I smelled the rotten potatoes and spoiled raw chicken. The stench was so overwhelming and unyielding, my head was knocked back as soon as I lifted the cover. Peering inside, I saw what looked like a pile of red apples without stems. The same thing I had seen on Five’s table the night before. I reached in and grabbed one of them for myself. It was heavy like a baseball and squishy like a sponge. It was unlike any apple I’d ever felt. Even a rotten apple wouldn’t have such a weird texture. There was no doubt that it was those things that smelled so bad. I looked at the hole wherefrom the man had crawled out and decided I needed to see it for myself.

Using the rocky surface to my advantage, I began scaling the mountain, reaching the hole, which sat about 3 meters above the ground. I pulled myself up to look into the hole, securing my foot on a rocky ledge. The hole couldn’t have been more than forty centimeters wide. It was no wonder that a person of his stature was the one crawling into the holes. Only a person as small as him could crawl into it without getting stuck. Looking around, the holes all seemed to vary ever so slightly in size by a few centimeters. I came to realize that not only was the entry a perfect circle, but the tunnel it made into the mountain was also a perfectly straight cylinder. There were no bends, nor were there any rocks sticking out at an angle. It was like someone had just drilled into the mountain with some enormous electric tool. They were certainly not made by nature, and they were certainly not just made by the people of Ipshire. I focused all my senses on picking up anything. There was a hint of the same stench I had experienced from the wagon as well as Five’s kitchen. When I listened closely, I heard something. Though it was subtle, I heard strained breathing mixed with painful moans, and this time I was sure it wasn’t just the wind. It was then I saw it. Something deep inside the hole, obscured by darkness, moving around.

I pulled out my camera and aimed it into the tunnel. With my heart galloping in my chest, I turned the flash on and released the shutter. What I saw in the brief flash of the camera remains vivid in my mind to this day. I’m not sure I would have believed what I saw had it not been for my camera capturing the exact same sight.

Far down in the tunnel lay a man. Due to the width of the tunnel, the man’s arms were completely pressed up against his body, leaving only his head to move freely. The darkness had taken his sight long ago, leaving only white marbles in his eye sockets. On his face were three enormous wounds sending streams of blood down his face. His toothless gums were completely exposed due to what I can only describe as a thoroughly broken jaw, complete with a deep crack from the middle of his lower lip down to his chin. I wondered if he was even aware of where he was or what was happening to him.

And as my brain was stuck in a state of fear, I felt a great rock strike the side of my head. Out of utter shock, I lost my footing and fell down three meters onto the ground, landing on my stomach, feeling the entirety of my lower intestines being pushed up into my ribcage. My camera landed a few meters away, destroyed on impact with the ground. I turned myself onto my back, desperately wheezing, my lungs screaming for air.

“What do you think you are doing?!” A voice came from up above.

I looked up to see the same man climbing down the mountain.

“I’m sorry.” I said, coughing the words up from within my beaten lungs. “I was just curious.”

The man approached me, the sunlight in my eyes making his face hard to read.

“You looked into the hole, did you not?”

“Yes, but I didn’t see anything, I swear!”

He began laughing. “You know, you are quite the lousy liar.” Now I could make out his face: a malicious grin reaching from ear to ear. “Your nervousness sticks to you like dew on grass.”

I was stricken with panic; I didn’t know what he was going to do to me.

“I didn’t mean any harm; please just let me go.” I tried to get up, but he kicked my feet away from under me.

“Oh, you think I can let this pass?” He pulled out a knife, making a horrid grin that exposed every crooked yellow and black tooth in his mouth. “I think those eyes have seen too much.”

He grabbed me by the wrist. And it was then, for the first time, that I looked into the obsidian eyes. I did not see a human. I did not see a soul anchored within his iris. I saw the baptismal font filled with muck, fruits in the forest rotting away, and the townsfolk feasting on their apples, all while the mountain kept screaming and the church bells rang for an audience of none.

“Perhaps you would like your own hole.” He said. “I can surely find a tunnel that fits just right.”

I tore away from him. I stumbled to my feet and ran out of the forest towards Five’s cottage, all while the sounds of the man’s cackling echoed throughout the silent forest. Blood streamed into my eyes as I bolted through the door to the cottage. Ignoring Five sitting at the table, I headed for the bedchamber. I crammed everything into my bag as fast as I could: flick knife, sleeping bag, and everything I had. Just as I was about to leave, I heard a familiar voice coming from the doorway.

“You seem to be in a rush.” Cottonmouth said.

“I’m sorry, sir. I have to get back to the mainland.”

Cottonmouth approached me, pulling out a napkin from his pocket.

“Look at yourself.” He wiped the blood away from my face. “What happened?”

“There was a man by the mountain that threw a rock at me.”

“Oh, you are talking about Fourteen.” Cottonmouth said. “Yes, he can be quite the jester. Got an apple when he was still too young, a real blunder on my part. He never really matured, I suppose. You need not pay attention to what he says. I will gladly go and talk to him if you so desire.”

“No, it doesn’t matter.” I threw my backpack over my shoulder and walked out of the cottage. “I have appreciated the hospitality, but I’m leaving the island, and that’s final!”

That’s when I felt Five’s hand wrap around my wrist before she put me in a rear wrist lock, forcing me to my knees, leaving me with only one free arm. She dragged me out of the cottage, and Cottonmouth followed. He sighed.

“I would have preferred to do this on different terms, but I see now that more drastic actions are necessary.”

“Sir, what are you doing?”

“I am giving you the chance of a lifetime.” Cottonmouth began. “Every so often we get visitors on our island. They come to explore our island, like you, but once they find that there are other people, they change.” 

I could only answer with a look of horror.

“They become infatuated with conforming us. They come marching in with a fallacious way of life, which they intend to force upon us, poisoning our society, only because they see the people of Ipshire as primitive and simpleminded! They want us to establish a bond with the mainland and rob us of our individuality.” Cottonmouth got very close to my face, his own face stricken with vehemence and solemnity. “Those people have to be weeded out!” 

“What’s that supposed to mean?” I was overcome with panic. “You kill them?”

A devious smile crept onto Cottonmouth’s lips. 

“No, of course not. They are merely given a different purpose.” He turned his head towards the mountain before turning back to me. “Besides, no one truly dies on Ipshire anymore.” He must have seen the fear in my eyes or heard my heart through my chest, because his demeanor quickly switched to something more friendly. “But do not fret, for you are not one such person.” He said. “You are worthy of something much better.”

Cottonmouth turned around and started walking. Five pushed me in the back, signaling for me to follow him. With every attempt to pull away, it felt like my shoulder was about to pop out of its socket; it was no use. I only had one other shot. I looked to Five who was staring right ahead at Cottonmouth. I took advantage of her absentmindedness and reached my free arm around, sticking it into my backpack and reaching for the flick knife, trying not to be noticed by Five as I was dragged along. I saw the other townsfolk starting to follow us like paraders, and I suppose that would make Cottonmouth the grand marshal. Suddenly, he stopped walking. Five brought me up close to him so that I was up against his back. The sound of townsfolk shuffling to their positions filled the air. I was desperately reaching for the knife, but it was stuck somewhere under my sleeping bag. Silence fell once the people had gathered in a big circle around us, just like the day before. I had my free hand deep inside the backpack and moved it ever so slowly to stay unnoticed. A long pause came and went before Cottonmouth broke the silence.

“Mainlander, the reason I have brought you out here, in the middle of the town, amongst my fellow inhabitants, is because His Eminence has deemed that you are a lost soul?” He said.

Turning around on his feet, his face was all the way up in mine. I could feel the knife’s hilt with the tip of my index finger. Just a bit longer and I would have it. I had to buy myself some time. 

“I’m not sure as to what you’re referring to, sir.” I said. “Can you elaborate?”

“A LOST soul.” Cottonmouth answered. “A person so disoriented in their own life, so bedeviled by their own insecurities, that they cannot break free of the toils that keep them tethered to the ground. Like a leaf flowing down the river: unable to change its direction, jealous of the fish who so effortlessly defy the stream. They do not have a way of life that they can enforce upon others.”

The hilt was slowly coming free of the sleeping bag.

“Sir, I don’t know what you’re talking about. I don’t feel disoriented in my life.”

Cottonmouth pulled away from me and turned around, facing the townsfolk. He started to laugh. It was a hearty laugh straight from the stomach. It wasn’t long before the townsfolk joined him in unison. I felt my body shaking as Five bounced up and down from her own snickering. Cottonmouth suddenly shot his arm into the air, and the townsfolk abruptly fell silent. He turned towards me again, smiling soothingly.

“You have only gotten worse at lying.” He said through a grin. “I know your kind. You wake up every day in a bed that you hate, to work on something that you have lost all passion for, with people whom you only feel bitterness towards, and then you go home to sulk yourself to sleep, just to repeat it all the following day; a day which always feels just slightly more insignificant than the one before.” His smile twisted into a stern expression. “You keep telling yourself that you are going in circles, only because you refuse to see that it is a downward spiral. One day you will wake up without an inkling of significance to your name. You will have no idea of what you are doing, but you will continue your routine anyways, hoping for a prize at the end of the tunnel.”

For a moment I stopped moving altogether. I didn’t know what to say. I stared blankly at him.

“But Ipshire can change that!” Cottonmouth dug into his pocket and fished out one of the apples from the mountain. “Just a little bite, and all your problems will wash off your back.”

I started panicking, desperately reaching for the knife.

“Please, sir, let me go.”

“Let me imbibe you for all eternity with all the greatest luxuries that you so dearly deserve.” He held the apple to my face, the stench coating the inside of my nostrils. “Let me take you to the endless pastures. Break the yoke that has entrapped you for so long!”

I finally managed to grab the hilt of my knife. In a split second, I pulled my hand out of my backpack, flicked the knife open, and propelled it into Cottonmouth’s neck.

He kept staring. He didn’t even pull back out of shock. There was no blood flowing. He didn’t fall to the ground with his hand desperately trying to cover a squirting wound. I let go of the knife, leaving it stuck in the man’s neck.

“You bring them all the way to the divine gates …” He said casually as he pulled the knife out of his neck. “And yet, they still yearn for the agony of their own life.”

For a moment, I didn’t believe my eyes. Though I had seen the blade pierce the skin and even felt resistance as the blade punctured it, there wasn’t as much as a scratch on Cottonmouth’s neck. With a mere swipe of his hand, three of the townsfolk began walking towards me. The first one grabbed my free arm and held it tightly. Then another one grabbed my head, and the last one started to pull down my lower jaw, stretching the corners of my lips so far that I thought they would rip. I couldn’t pull away. Once again, I felt the smell of rotten potatoes and spoiled raw chicken penetrate every orifice of my face. I felt as bile forced itself up through my throat and ran down my chin. I clenched my eyes shut as tears streamed down my cheeks, preparing for the worst.

It was then that something truly strange occurred. 

I tasted the saltiness of chicken, the sweetness of strawberries, the umami of lobster, the sourness of oranges, and the bitterness of dark chocolate; it all somehow played together in a symphony of tastes like I had never had before. It was the greatest thing that had ever passed my lips.

I opened my eyes once again, and no longer did I see the town of Ipshire, which I had come to know. I saw pristine white cottages spread out on emerald-green pastures, where the tall flowers swayed to the subtle breeze that blew across the island. The townsfolk, who had once been devoid of beauty, now stood before me looking as if Michelangelo himself had chiseled them from marble. Their malnourished appearance had abandoned them, and their dirty garments had been replaced by spotless white garbs which seemed to shimmer in the balmy sunlight.

I looked to Cottonmouth, who, as the only one, appeared unchanged in his fancy suit. He dangled the rest of the apple in front of my face. I felt as the grip around my arms and head disappeared. Without hesitating for another second, I grabbed the apple from his hands. With every single bite, I felt warmth spread through my body. I felt my cheeks simmer and saliva form underneath my tongue. After swallowing the last bite, I sat in silence, appreciating the aftertaste before it swiftly left my tongue.

“How do you feel?” Cottonmouth asked.

“I feel better, sir.”

“Good.” He grabbed my hand and caressed it gently. “This is but a taste of what a life in Ipshire can offer you. I have no intention to imprison you. It is all up to you. Tell me that you wish to go home, and I shall guide you to the shore. Ask to join the congregation, and you will never suffer another day in your life.”

I sat on my knees in front of him, turning my options over in my head.

“I cannot just leave the mainland.” I told him. “I cannot leave my family behind.”

“Do they support you?” Cottonmouth looked me in the eyes. “Would you miss their presence?”

“They are my family.”

“The blood of the covenant is thicker than the water of the womb.”

I was dumbfounded. A few minutes before, all I wanted was to get away, but things had changed; I had seen the other perspective. I was between a rock and a hard place. I felt tears running down my face once again; my thoughts were ripping me apart.

“I wish to go home.” I sniffled. “It would be the only right thing to do.”

“If that is what you want.”

Cottonmouth helped me to my feet and interlocked his right arm with my left. He swiped his other arm into the air, and the periphery of townsfolk disassembled. Cottonmouth and I walked arm in arm through the forest, and, for the first time, I did not hear the church bell in the distance. We arrived at the shore, where I pushed my boat into the water. Just as I was about to climb in, Cottonmouth grabbed me by the shoulder.

“Remember, Ipshire is a home for any lost soul who wishes to find its purpose.”

I nodded before getting into the boat. I picked up the oars and started my retreat to the mainland. For a while, no matter how far away I got, the black silhouette on the shore was still easy to make out. It was like he knew. 

Knew that I had my doubts.

Knew that I wanted to turn the boat around.

But I did not do that.

I sailed away till the island was just a speck on the horizon, letting the screeching motor distract me from the many thoughts whirling around in my head. It was after a while that I turned the motor off and sat down in the boat, taking a break. I cried until my eyes could give no more and screamed till my throat gave out. Deep down, the only thing I longed for in that moment was another apple. I gripped my hair and tried to breathe in and out as best I could. I looked at the sky and saw that the sun was rapidly moving westward, and I still had a long journey ahead of me. I wiped my eyes, turned the motor back on, and started sailing once again.

I never wrote my article. There was no point. I had neither pictures nor artifacts. The fact that I was the only person to ever return from the trip only served to cast doubt upon any anecdote I attempted to share. For the longest time I tried to push every memory of Ipshire away. I begged for my thoughts to leave me alone. I begged for a minute of peace. I wished that Ipshire was gone.

But Ipshire refuses to leave me alone. In the evenings, when memories fail to leave me alone, I roll around in my bed, unable to sleep. I have tried to pretend that everything is normal, tried to go back to how my life was before, but that is not possible. My apartment is duller than I remember. There is always a gloomy feeling in the air. Nobody seems happy these days.

I have been feeling the yoke pressing down more and more with each passing day, and I do not know how much longer I can take it. I have caught myself thinking of the island more times than I would like to admit. I think about the significance of my own life. I think how nice it would be to taste their apples just one more time.

I am starving as I write this. Every day I try to force down food, but everything tastes the same: rotten potatoes and spoiled raw chicken. Some days I can push down some food without puking everything up; those days are best-case scenarios. I think my stomach has begun digesting itself.

It is in these cumbersome moments that I have come to realize that the choice I was given was merely illusory. My life on the mainland is no longer viable, not since that trip. Ipshire calls to me like a lighthouse does a ship.

I have thought it through, and I see no reason to fight the urge anymore. In Ipshire I can belong. Just another sheep in the herd as the days march on. 

That sounds nice right about now.

To whomever may find this, I write to let you know of my decision. You deserve to know why my apartment stands empty. I hope that you can find some comfort in these words. I do not wish for anyone to worry about me. If you ever find yourself trailing the lonesome path paved with your own grievances and frustrations, I hope you are able to deviate and find a better path than I did.

The island has called out to me for months, and there is nothing I can do but give in to the urge. Do not waste your thoughts nor breath on me, for I am already gone. I hope that you can understand, even though, deep down, I know that will never be possible.

Do not try to find me; it will not end well for you.
