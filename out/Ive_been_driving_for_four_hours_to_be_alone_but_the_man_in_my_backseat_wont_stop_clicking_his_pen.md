I’ve been driving for four long hours and haven't seen a *single* pair of headlights. I should be happy, but a sinking part of me knows something's off. That’s what I wanted when I quit my job and deleted my socials—to finally be alone. Alone in a world that wouldn't allow it. But when I peered into the rearview mirror, a man with a clipboard was sitting in my backseat, dressed in a gray suit with a perfectly trimmed beard and mustache.

I slammed the brakes, bringing the car to a grinding halt. Nausea washed over me as I crashed forward in my seat. But in that split second— before everything went dark—the man didn't move with the car. It was as if some ungodly force was holding him in place.

When I came to, the world was a vibrating mess of doubles. Two steering wheels. Two flickering dashboards. I tried to swallow the metallic sting of blood in my throat. And my tongue of sickly metallic copper pennies.

"Have you ever heard of Christopher McCandless?"

The voice was clipped, professional. It didn't wait for an answer.

"He thought he could escape the scourge, too. Drove further than you did. All the way to the Alaskan brush, in fact."

I heard the scritch of his pen—a dry, rhythmic sound that cut through the ringing in my ears.

"*You see,* he didn't die of starvation, George. That was the official story. In fact, he *never* died. You are welcome to go see him. With my permission, of course."

'Look, Mr...'

"The name is Davenport," he interrupted. The voice was flat, completely devoid of warmth. "Names are just labels for assets, George. Assets, which mean something to someone, somewhere. You gave yours no meaning. But don't worry, I'm here to take it for myself."

A week ago, I wasn’t some "unclaimed asset." I was just a ghost in a polyester vest. I spent my days navigating the university—a concrete hive of twenty thousand students whose voices blurred into a constant, sickening drone. By nightfall, I was a pair of hands for a retail giant. I was an in-store shopper, a human cursor moving through aisles of fluorescent hum and floor-wax, picking out items for people too lazy to fetch them for themselves. I didn't see people anymore; I only saw a maze of unpredictable variables, beings that, by their very nature, could disrupt my peace in a moment's notice.

The disruption happened on a Tuesday. I was scanning a third-choice substitute for a brand of organic milk when a man cornered me. He didn't ask for help; he barked at me, his face a mottled, angry purple, screaming about a missing coupon like I had personally stolen it from his pocket. I stood there, watching clear viscous globs of spit fly from his lips, and I realized I didn't hate him—I hated the fact that he was allowed to exist in my space at all. To begin with.

I didn't argue. I didn't even put the milk down. I simply walked toward the automatic doors, left my scanner on a shelf of discounted cereal, and stepped out into the rain. I was tired of being a witness to a blind world. I wanted the world to be a void. Something I could control and achieve pure, uninterrupted peace in.

"So, you want to be alone, right?" Mr. Davenport said in the back of my car.

I gripped the wheel, my hands trembling. "I just wanted peace. I just wanted them to *go away*."

"They never went away, George," Davenport whispered. I heard the scritch of his pen, louder now, like it was writing directly onto the back of my skull. "You just stopped paying attention."

He leaned forward, his grey sleeve brushing my shoulder. "You thought you were driving into the deep dark? Into the shapeless, infinite void?"

He reached over and flipped the high beams on.

The blackness didn't just break; it shattered like glass. The highway was gone. The trees were gone. I was sitting in my stationary car in the middle of a crowded, sun-drenched terminal. Thousands of people—the scourge of the Earth—were swarming around the vehicle, faces pressed against the glass, voices muffled in a deafening roar of 'unpredictable' life.

"You’ve been truant from the human race for too long, George," Davenport said, clicking his pen with a final, metallic *snap*. "Your private life has been seized. From now on, you are public property."

I tried to scream, to hide my face, but I realized my hands weren't moving. I wasn't a man anymore. I was a statue. A permanent fixture in the middle of the crowd I hated, forced to watch every face, hear every breath, and feel every brush of skin against the metal of my cage until the end of time. I used to fear the end; now I realize the true horror isn't that life stops, but that the paperwork never does. This month I am not writing a letter to him saying I've learned my lesson, I'm writing it to *you all*. Don't be the ghost that I was. And don't take this as a "self-help" guide, but as a *warning*. The auditor is always watching. And when you lose purpose, he'll find you a new one. It's his job.
