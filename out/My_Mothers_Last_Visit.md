*“Come see me as soon as you can,”* the note read.

I dropped it on the table and considered it while I sipped my coffee.

My mother and I hadn't spoken in almost 15 years- but I already knew why she wanted to talk now.

The nursing home called last week. My mother was dying of cancer.

I took another sip. Could I forgive her?

I would have to try. Take the high road and offer forgiveness, absolution even, to the woman who abused me so she could die with a clear conscience.

I got in my car. I drove in silence through the rain, a highlight reel of painful memories running through my head.

The trees and mist eventually parted, revealing a hulking, red brick facility that was as ugly as it was depressing. 

I climbed the steps and braced myself to see my mother.

The staff seated me at a beige table in a private waiting room. Eventually a nurse wheeled her in.

I knew she would look bad, but it was shocking to see how she had transformed over the years. The skin around her face had become saggy, wrinkly and loose, and her hair was completely white.

But her eyes? They were still full of hate. Almost as much as mine.

We stared at each other for a moment, daring the other to speak.

“Where’s the boys?” She asked finally. “They were supposed to be here.”

She peered beyond me, looking for my brothers. Even as an adult I was a disappointment to her. 

“Your note didn’t say that,” I countered.

“I didn’t want *you* to come. I thought you would pass the note along. They aren’t taking my calls,” she hissed.

I'd forgotten how cruel she could be. I opened my mouth to reply but she continued before I could get a word out. 

“My own children won't come see their dying mother,” she mumbled hatefully, fumbling with her pockets.

“I'm here,” I said loudly. 

She glared at me. “Oh, please. You can’t *wait* to get rid of me. You abandoned me in this stupid place.” 

I refrained from mentioning that it had been a group decision to put mom here, and that we all paid for it.

 “Now you’re just waiting for me to die. Well, I guess you don’t have to wait much longer.”

I frowned. “What are you talking about?”

I now saw she held something in her hand; quickly, too quickly, she slashed it across her throat. Blood poured onto her neck and saturated her thin nightgown.

I jumped back, shocked. The blade slipped from her hand and clattered on the floor. A razor, stolen from the clinic.

The door burst open. The nurse looked accusingly at me as she rushed to help. “What have you done?” She screamed. An alert buzzer began to wail.

My heart sank. Suicide - my mother’s final abusive act.

I slipped out the door as more staff rushed in.

A startling thought popped in my head.
