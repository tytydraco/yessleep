“*Inhale, through the nose. Deep. Now exhale. One more time with me. Inhale. Exhale.*”



Sarah Sloan’s whisper echoed around my head since we passed the New Mexico state line. At the first sign of my whining, my mom plugged me into Sloan’s *Sublime Spaces*, one of the many relaxation or meditative “audio experiences” my mother bought for me when she got an email from my school about me being disruptive. At nine years old, I just liked to make friends.



I sat in the back of my dad’s ‘83 Ford Fiesta. Every summer, he would pile my mom and I into the sewage colored sedan and we would embark on the great American road trip. He used to tell me, “You are who you surround yourself with.” He must have found it important that I surround myself with him. Looking back, all of those summer road trips blend together. All except for summer 1989, when my mom finally convinced my dad to take us to Roswell, New Mexico.



I sat, starving and squeezing my tiny bladder as tight as I could in an effort to keep my pants dry. I really had to pee. My mom and dad were singing along to some tune in the front seat. My mom’s dark bangs danced as the desert air whipped in through her rolled down window. I gazed at her face through the rearview, in my head imagining her lips aligning perfectly to the words coming through my ear buds. “*One….Two…*” Sloan beckoned me to relax through the player and count with her, but I knew what would happen if I did. My bladder cramped. “*Three…Four…Five…*” I balled both of my fists and pushed them hard into my stomach, my face curling into a grimace.  “*Six…*” A few drops wet my underwear.



I ripped out the headphones and, louder than I intended, yelled out, “Can we stop! I have to go! BAD!”



“I haven’t seen any exits, bud.” My dad shouted back over some Tom Petty track.



“No exits?” I thought to myself. “Where *am* I? How can they be so calm? We are stuck in this desert and there are *no exits*?” Maybe, I thought, mom and dad were so calm because they had already come up with some sort of escape plan. I found comfort in that. I threw the ear buds back in and stared out the car window.



The New Mexico sun seemed to cook the sand a shade or two darker brown as I watched out of the window. As we drove and I stared at the sand the desert began offering me a distraction from my aching bladder. A delightful, but hardly present, smell. The odor of dad smoking a rack of ribs on the back porch on a misty fall Sunday morning before the football games. The sun baked the smell, too, and soon its pungence was making my mouth water. 



“*From the top of your head to the heels of your feet, release your tension.*” Through Sloan’s bad advice I saw my dads nose twitch at the smell.  “Good,” I thought. “Not a mirage.” I knew deserts could make people hallucinate from the movies. Luckily, this smell seemed to be real.



The fragrance filled the car and soon my hunger pains almost matched the discomfort in my bladder. Through the windshield I saw that around a distant bend in the highway, a thin wisp of graywhite smoke billowed into the atmosphere. “Yum!” I thought to myself, “That must be the barbecue!” I imagined my face covered in dark sticky barbecue sauce. I pictured myself sucking the grease from my fingers, my bladder relieved from a streetside restroom. Finally, an exit.



We grew closer to the bend in the highway and the stench of barbecue surrounded me. With every breath my lungs filled with the stench of salty, smoky meat. We started to come around the bend. The silhouette of the massive smoker started coming into my view, black from the backlight of the setting New Mexico sun. It triumphantly blew savory smoke into the sky, and I imagined bratwursts and brisket and pulled pork and best of all gnawing meat off the bones of saucy, messy ribs. 



“*From the tips of your fingers to the tips of your toes, relax.*” I could almost bear to listen to Sloan’s sleepy voice and release my stress.



It took another quarter mile for me to realize what I was looking at. The sun set just low enough on the horizon to reveal the smoker was the carcasses of at least two vehicles. I could make out a pickup truck and a small sedan, and from their crumpled frames it seemed they suffered from a head-on collision. The truck rested halfway atop the crunched remains of the sedan. Aluminum and steel twisted and reached from the pile, the limbs of the sedan reaching out from underneath the truck as if begging for just one more moment of life. The entire mass was ablaze.



“*Find the most comfortable spot you can in your chair. Your sofa. Your bed. Wherever you may find yourself listening*.” 



We kept approaching. The passenger of the sedan was unrecognizable. A charred black form, its mouth agape and flesh burned away to showcase stark white teeth. It must have had its legs on the dashboard. Its blackened feet sat pressed on a dash that was shoved towards it so quickly that it pushed its knees through the top of its skull, almost straight through the eye sockets.



*“Set your head back. Rest your arms wherever they may feel comfortable. And just relax.”*



The windshield of the truck was completely missing. We grew closer. The truck was empty. When we passed the wreckage, I finally saw the man face down in the sand. There was no wind to disturb the long sand divot behind him, a bloody moat that his corpse dug and already began to fill with blood. 



*“Breathe with me. In. Then out.”* 



I didn’t need to pee anymore.



I am well into my forties now, and I can still depend on the nightmares. When summer approaches and the air begins to warm, I can count on my sleep bringing the same visions that I have had ever since witnessing that horror on the highway. 



My family and I, we coast by, and even though the car is running and my dad is pressing on the gas and the wheels are turning the car will not move. The charred corpse in the Sedan stares at me, its toes pointing at me from legs that dangle out of its eye sockets. Its bones begin to stretch from the tips of its fingers and toes. Perfectly smoked meat, greasy and moist with sweet sauce, clings hot and juicy on the skewers of its dead fingers, and I stare with my mouth watering and stomach growling. 



I awake drenched in sweat. And, every time, I have to go down the hall to the kitchen and make myself a sandwich before I can fall back asleep.
