I haven’t worked here that long. Maybe that’s why I didn’t know better.

Sorry for any typos. My hands are shaking as I’m typing this. I just needed somewhere to write it all down, to make me feel like it was real—but as the hours go by I start to wonder if I imagined it all.

So, I work for the government. Started a few weeks ago. Don’t get it twisted—this isn’t the top-secret, high-clearance federal shit with real mystique. I work for the state, at the department of public health. I’m an assistant to the primary data analyst. Going in every day and preparing reports, attending meetings, sending emails… your usual office slog. I was just excited to have a stable 9-5, especially one that put my statistics minor to use. I’d been jumping between retail jobs since graduation last year. 

I set up my cubicle with the usual— folders, pictures, a few books and a cheap hand-me-down clock that has some characteristic damages from so many moves. It took them a while after I started to get me a computer. The government runs slow like that, as I’m sure all of you know. For the first couple days, I just twiddled my thumbs and tried to look busy while playing card games on my phone. Everyone in my department was really nice about it, apologizing for the inconvenience and telling me I could do whatever. That’s probably why, when IT finally got me my tech, I was eager to overcompensate for lost time. Look, I’m not a super suck-up, but you want to make a good first impression at a new job, right?

This time of year, summer is sneaking in but hasn’t quite crossed the threshold yet. The government center is well-kept as it can be for a building of its age, but the HVAC isn’t exactly cutting-edge. It was having a hard time keeping up with the springtime temperature swings. I came in wearing a thick wool cardigan, but had shed it over my chair by ten a.m. that morning. Various other cubicles had coats and jackets strewn on the edges, victims of the cold morning subdued by afternoon heat. 

I’m not sure how it is in other places, but here almost all our statewide agencies are in the one giant building. On top of that, the health department is one of the largest, so our section takes up half a floor by itself. If you’ve ever worked an office job of a decent size, you know a field of gray cubicles can cross a huge berth and you can barely see the sunlight through the windows at the end—blocked by private supervisor offices, naturally. The state pinches every penny possible. Don’t worry, your tax dollars won’t pay for so much as a decorative wall hanging in our office or a new keyboard that isn’t missing the asterisk symbol. It’s stark, sterile, and just as monochrome as you think, but there’s good people, okay? No one works these jobs for the money. 

That’s why it wasn’t until after 6:30 that the last other employee left. It was Aisley— not from the analytics department, but epidemiology. She’s an older woman who was always kind to me, stopping to say farewell on her way out every night. Not hard to do since my cube is the very last one on the end before the employee door, but still. It was one of the few fleeting interactions I had that wasn’t about a deadline. Tonight, she insisted I didn’t work too much longer, that I was too young for that and it would give me wrinkles like hers. I told her I just had a couple spreadsheets to clean up, then I had a date with my Nintendo Switch. The thick back door clunked as it latched behind her.

Long quiet began its heavy reign. In the distance, I could see tinges of rich red coloring the painted metal edges of the office doors. The sunset crept through blinds and bent distortions across the mottled carpet like broken fingers. Some of them reached, skeletal and long in the golden hours, right toward my dark corner of the floor.  

I shivered, pulling my cardigan back on. The static of the wool prickled, fibers raking across my goosebumps. The clack of my outdated keyboard echoed louder in the tenfold quiet. I tapped my tan shoes against the floor. 

I finished pruning some columns from one county report and closed it out. My dusty blue clock ticked at me, hand twitching under a hairline crack in the face. 6:47. A chunk of paint on the side was peeling off in the shape of a smile. I plucked it,, leaving a gaping wound of exposed black plastic. 

One more sheet, I told myself. Just some equation writing for the statewide flu records. My boss had said he wanted the charts as soon as I could do them, and I was still keen to impress. 

*Vrrrrrrrr.* I jumped in my threadbare office chair. Just the heat kicking in as the night came on strong. The red-oranges outside had cooled into a purple and blue bruise. It left no more warm traces across the floor. I ran a hand down my lanyard, fiddling with the latch on my ID badge. 

I was really struggling with one of the equations and my bar chart wasn’t displaying the way it was supposed to. I tried searching up answers online, but the crappy public internet had chosen that time to sputter out, leaving me bereft with a spinning load wheel. 

I didn’t notice anything was wrong until I stood up. I remember now, I was thinking it would be a good idea to visit Andrew’s desk and try to find his Excel guidebook, if only to kill the time until the Internet kicked back in. But as soon as my head breached those dust-gray walls, all purpose exited my brain. 

There were cubicles. And there were cubicles. And then there were more cubicles. 

I imagine you’ve been to a coast before. But I’m not talking about the fru-fru tourist ones with dots of buoys and gentle rocks easing land into the ocean. I’m talking about the drop-offs, the oceans so abrupt and so sheer the vast edge is like a cliff into another reality. My family took us all to Iceland one spring, right before my older sister moved across the country. We hiked a long route that eventually took us to a blisteringly cold beach. Frigid ocean spray cut our cheeks open, tar-black sand melting into colorless water against a colorless sky sick with clouds. A horizon line didn’t exist—the sea just went and went and went.

What I saw in that office seemed to go even further than that ocean. It seemed to reach infinity, uncaring, and then march past it. 

I blinked, hard. I crunched my eyes together so tightly that I felt a flake of my cheap mascara crawl under my eyelid. 

Still the field of stiff geometry remained. 

Okay, sure. I must have had too much caffiene after lunch and now the stress and darkness were teaming up on me. After all, this office was *big.* Like, so big that it took you a solid few minutes to walk straight across it. 

I crept back toward my desk and sank into my seat. I breathed, counting to ten like those Instagram posts tell you to do. I stood up, kept my eyes fixed to the ground, and began walking straight down the hall to where I knew Andrew’s cubicle would be. 

The carpet began to shapeshift under my feet. Tacky swirls twined into each other, forming the distinct lines of a smile, a frown,  a face of rage. If you asked me to draw it out now, I couldn’t possibly. I couldn’t even describe it right. It never looked the same way twice—but human beings have a gut reaction to the cues of another’s face, and in that floor I saw desperate life trapped in the polyester fibers. 

I kept walking.

I must have reached his desk by now. Surely, I’d even passed it and I would feel really silly when I knocked into a director’s office door. 

I looked up.

I was in front of my cubicle. 

Okay, fuck the book. I clearly just needed some fresh air, right? I spun around, ready to peel out the staff door. I know distinctly that I turn right out of my cubicle to leave. I’ve been doing it a few weeks already.

In that direction stretched endless cubicles. 

But mine was still the last one on the end. I was overtaken by nausea—the mind isn’t meant to comprehend space like that. It was like being inside a gyroscope, where every turn forces you back into alignment. 

I was hyperventilating now. Hands shaking, I grabbed my phone from the desk. The black screen laughed back at me. Dead. Good as a brick. 

You never realize how much noise even the quietest office has. Phones ringing here and there, people chatting through Zoom meetings. Feet tapping, an occasional sneeze. The hum of machinery. 

I realized, then, all those things I was missing. I had never been in silence so complete. So absolute. So perfect. The more I stared into the churning ocean of parallels, the less it seemed to define itself. The speckled light gray of the ceiling smoothed out, taking on the static felt of the cubicles. The walls soaked up darkness from the swirling gray carpet. I felt bile push up my throat again. 

I glanced at my dusty clock. It stared back—6:47 p.m.

I probably could have tried something else. I could have breathed again, could have tried to find a mirror or a weapon or checked that my stupid old clock hadn’t finally just broken for good. 

I ran. 

Tears pooled down my face, turning black in the wake of my stale makeup. I felt the hot sting as they beat against my mouth. My ID badge flapped left and right, lanyard raking across my neck as it flailed. My breath came hot and short, pitiful little gasps choked into hiccups. The gray, gray carpet smiled and cried and begged and pleaded and screamed and laughed. 

My ankle caught. I folded down, down, down, for ages and ages I tumbled until the rug burn scraped my ankles. 

I swung my head around—desperate to keep moving, unsure why. I yanked my knees up, but they wouldn’t separate themselves. Perfection crept over the soles of my beige flats until I couldn’t tell where I ended and emptiness began. 

Nothing pursued me relentlessly. Nothing crept into my lungs. Not one thing at all. I heard my throat choke on it, and then I heard nothing entirely. 

My hands weren’t sinking into the carpet with my legs. They weren’t anything at all. I only felt the lack of them because my head was still something, and I knew my name knocking around inside. 

“Please,” I said, “I—“

*Beep! Click.* 

The badge scanner chirped. The employee door was heaved open. 

“‘Scuse me, forgot my—“ Aisley stopped, dropping her handbag with a gasp. 

I couldn’t blame her. There I was, collapsed on the floor, hot tears clogging my eyes, sweater torn and shoes scuffed. An Excel sheet shouldn’t do that to someone—though I’m sure it has on certain tough days.

“Oh my god, are you okay?” She rushed toward me, squatting down. “Should I call 911?”

It took a moment for me to remember. The world had not reassembled back in front of me gradually, only blinked into somethingness between seconds. The weight of my body felt invasive.

“I—no, it’s—I’m okay.” I heard my own words as if hazy, forgotten of their form. “The stress just—got to me.”

Her dark eyebrows furrowed, unbelieving, but she didn’t press. “Okay, okay. Can I walk you to your car?”

I nodded, swiping stained tears off my cheeks. “Sure. Let me just turn off my c-computer.” 

My knees buckled the first time I tried to stand. The second time took. 

Aisley hovered outside my cubicle while I closed everything down with shaking hands. I glanced at my clock—nearly 7. When I stood with my bag, I searched the far wall for office doors, finding them diminutive but present. I exhaled, the air running thick and heavy in my nose. Too full. 

We made the trek to the parking garage, Aisley bidding me goodnight once I was tucked inside my car with the engine humming. The noise grated my ears in a new way. Even now, curled up in the corner of my living room, I’m noticing how much *stuff* I have in my apartment. It overwhelms me. I keep staring by the door, where I kicked off my work shoes in a pile. I thought they were tan. I know they were tan. 

They smile at me now, gray and swirling.
