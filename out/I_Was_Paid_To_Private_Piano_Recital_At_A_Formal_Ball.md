The car picked me up, a Rolls Royce with a crimson leather interior and a strikingly handsome chauffeur wearing a black three-piece, matching tie, tanned leather drivers gloves, Chanel sunglasses and a flatcap. The hat was odd. I expected a chauffeur to wear a fedora, a trilby, even a bowler, but not a flatcap. 

 

The Flatcap instructed me to don a blindfold over my mask, which I did, and we settled into travel. 



I was hired as a concert pianist for the evening and had been paid an exorbitant sum to perform a private recital at an unknown location. My instructions were that the concert would be played to a formal masked ball and that I should wear a raven costume, or more specifically. a tuxedo, tie, and a domino mask embellished to look like a raven. 



During the drive, I envisioned myself playing the night’s composition. It was composed with Ravel’s Gaspard *De La Nui*t in mind. It was a demanding piece, and the rehearsal window was smaller than I liked, but I welcomed the challenge.

 

As we travelled, I recalled the notes, feeling the keys press underneath my fingers, transported—until the sedan slowed and the chauffeur commanded me to remove my blindfold. As I settled into my sight, Flatcap opened my door, and I stepped out onto a competently cemented surface. 

 

The circular driveway surrounded both sides of the road, with fastidiously manicured topiary trees lining both borders. The garden’s grandeur hinted at a desire to tame the wild natural land beyond the garden. To punctuate this, wrought iron lampposts lit the area with an unnatural golden hue. Beyond the lights lay the authentic darkness, and beyond that might have been anything. 

 

I expected opulence, but I hadn’t expected this level of wealth; the building was a pleasure palace, two marble domes atop a central plan building—everything in perfect proportion—Byzantine post and lintel construction held up by colossal marble columns. It was a blight on the lushy forested land, not something I expected to exist outside of Europe. The building was so completely out of time and space that it confused my senses, but there it stood, a cream-coloured cancer erected amid a majestic forest of pine and fir. 

 

Up a smooth marble staircase lay a set of bronze-tinted oak doors that swung open to receive me. The building, in its blinding intensity, waited to swallow me up.  The chauffeur signalled to climb the stairs with a leather-gloved hand. 

 

“Please, your hosts await.”

 

Walking the stairs, the silence emanating from the building and its surrounding was odd. The night’s usual chaos was tamed. There was no breeze rustling the shrubs. No bird song. Not even a hum of the indoor lighting. Nothing. 

 

While I pondered this, the car had disappeared. Atop the oak doors, an apse held a tile mosaic. The tesserae compiled to portray a peacock: purple, blue and green tiles accented with white and gold ones.

 

Turning, I greeted the chauffeur’s doppelganger. “Hello, sir. Please follow me.” The person who received me was similarly handsome and wore a flatcap as well. Otherwise, they were attired in a grey tweed suit with a grey owl mask.

 

The new Flatcap entered the oak doors, and I followed. So, the hosts were those who hired employees based on their resemblance and charisma. Twins rarely looked as fetching as the two Flatcaps did. The chauffeur and butler were like two dolls pressed from the same plastic mould, hired for their uncanny resemblance to one another. 

 

Inside the narthex, the walls were adorned with statuary, bronze and modern, impressionistic bodies and abstract faces absent of features; the metal was purposefully oxidized and reflected purple, blue, and green. I wanted to stop and stare, but the butler continued walking and since I was a guest, I followed him closely. 

 

The ceiling opened as we entered the building’s nave. The guests dressed in finery, mostly ball gowns and tuxedos, costumed as birds of paradise: pink flamingos, parrots, toucans, macaws and more. 

 

In contrast, the servants dressed as I was, in black or grey: crows, ravens, rooks, owls and jackdaws. 

 

As we strode into the building, the guests parted before us. They gawked me with wide eyes, drinks in their hands. From the excited chatter that arose, they had awaited my arrival. The hall grew silent as I approached a raised platform. 

 

Atop the nave-adjacent aisles were more bronze statuary that stared from deeply recessed alcoves. Unlike most classic sculpture, who stared skyward, these statues stared to the earth and acknowledged those that peered up at them. Beholding their blank faces, the bronzes’ aura caused me to shiver. Their eyeless faces peered through to perceive something within me, perhaps a soul, if such a force was there.

 

The partygoers stopped and stared at me. I could hear their breath, the slight clink of fine crystal and the blood flowing in their arteries. The bubble of silence outside the building had expanded inside. 

 

The thought of my music being lost to this silence worried me. As a pianist, everything I do is predicated on the fact that when I press a key, that key hits a cord which resonates into sound, producing a note. A series of notes makes a string of notes which constitute a song. The thought of the silence absorbing my art made each step forward a struggle. 

 

I rubbed my throbbing hands. My hands had cramped in rehearsals for this performance—phantom pains, like they had been cut off and replaced with gossamer and silk. It hadn’t affected my performance, but I was worried it would continue. In this industry, if your hands disintegrate, so does your career. 

 

This was a private event for exclusive clients. Patrons gossiped. A tepid concert would ruin my career.  

 

At the platform’s base stood my hosts, a man and a woman dressed as peacocks. The man’s three-piece suit was opalescent green embroidered with blue and purple designs, while his mask was translucent vinyl material shaped into feathers, with a golden beak. The woman wore a slighter version of the peacock mask and an emerald gown with a blue/green/purple phosphorescent train spun to appear like peacock feathers. 

 

Their eyes glimmered with mischief.

 

 “A pleasure to have you this evening,” the woman said, “This celebration will hinge on your performance. But first, some housekeeping. I have instructions.” The first instruction was in the contract. “You must have one of your ankles cuffed to the piano.” She cleared her throat. “The other, our surprise for you, is that you are playing a piano duo. I know you are primarily a soloist, but tonight demands the two of you play together.”

 

There was always something either humiliating or infuriating with clients like these. The fact was, they didn’t appreciate the thousands of hours invested in my musical education, or the standards to which I held myself. This was an insult to my profession and my integrity. 

 

Then, I noticed another platform further down the nave. Even with a costume on, I knew the pianist as Peter Dieuwke, an egotist whose cup overflowed with cruelty. Seeing me, he bowed and allowed a smug smile to stretch across his thin lips. Everything about Peter was thin, from his fingers through his frame. If I were a conductor, this man would be my Salieri. We no longer auditioned for the same parts but walked in similar social circles. The only good thing about having him here was the crowd that separated us. 

 

She continued, “Just so you know, the music will be discordant. Please concentrate on a precise performance tonight.”

 

“I’m not friendly with Dieuwke.”

 

“Name your price.” 

 

I did, and she paid it. 

 

“So that is the price for your discomfort?” She smiled and I noticed gemstones inlaid into her teeth. The smile spread wider, showing her crimson gums. “Please attach yourself to the piano.” 

 

I asked that no one touch me as I played, and my host nodded. Then I attached myself to their gorgeous Steinway and Sons model D. There was a lock, but since no one examined it, I left it open. 

 

The familiar sheet music reminded of the role that I found myself in, the role of entertainer. Performance is my entirety; I am a master pianist, totally in control of my recital. That said, the time to perform had arrived. 

 

My vision travelled across the room, to my rival. Peter met my eyes with disdain and a hollow-eyed grin. Our fingers twitched over our Steinway’s keyboards. There was no one to count us in.

 

All around us the costumed idiots stared, waiting for us. The stillness of the space made seconds stretch endlessly out while the crowd and statues watched. Peter raised a hand performatively, and together we began in perfect synchronisation. 

 

Being a concert pianist, I was used to the audience watching politely, enraptured, but this crowd writhed to our music, twisting their bodies together, dancing with orgiastic enthusiasm. The air suddenly tasted of salt and champagne. 

 

Beginning the first movement, Peter and I played bitonal music, mine in C major and his in F-sharp major, a two-piano sonata. The notes merged like two separate oceans pushing into one another and integrating. I closed my eyes and allowed myself to perform this well-rehearsed opening and listened. Both pieces were classically composed, but by different authors; I ascertained this from how were written. Everything aligned oddly in rhythm and syncopation, but there were odd moments of integration.

 

Dieuwke performed with a ribald enthusiasm, his bravado extended through his hollow gaze through his skeletal fingers. His body convulsed with the actions of his arms as his long mop of curly hair flopped in time. There was this notion that pianists needed to carry emotional charisma into their work, and Peter accomplished that. In comparison, I relied on technical mastery and moved little. 

 

The grand building filled with impeccable acoustics and costumed dilettantes, the fundamental excess of the partygoers, from their designer outfits to their awkward tangos, fuelled my anger into a hissing propane tank; I loathed them singularly. Rage filled my sacrum and emerged into fluid hand arrangements, flowing like a red tide through me. 

 

The second movement trended quieter, but the heat from the surrounding crowd coalesced as the music pulsated. A radiant wave of senselessness undulated along invisible strings of music, an ocean of flesh rippling beneath my perch. 

 

There was a quick call and response section between Peter and I that tore through the crowd, separating it into halves, one below his dais, one below mine. Their forms moved chaotically, limbs flailed spasmodically, as the two pianos discharged two distinct tempos; the thing was, to my ear, our pianos had manifested unique notes that infested the crowd’s intellects that warped their bodies into unknown perspectives: flesh melding, breaking, stretching the reality I had known. 

 

The effect this had on me wasn’t immediate, but thinking back on it now causes knotted thoughts—like a loud flash of light affects your eyesight. 

 

Halfway through the movement, sweat spiralled down my face, and the room lights flickered. I stared up at the statuary that manifested a staccato aurora; the acid-etched bronze writhed in the shadows, the statuaries’ blank ‘faces’ twisted with newfound features: gaping holes filled with gnashing teeth, peacock irises, and flared nostrils. Bronze limbs thrashed with satisfaction as their unearthly openings gaped to feast on the crowd’s maniacal energies. 

 

By itself, the music was even-tempered, but when the two pieces combined, the harmonics unmoored minds to allow human minds to comprehend an aspect of the universe our brains naturally ignored. 

 

My psyche was not as affected as the crowd’s. An unknowable force that reviled humanity possessed them. I cannot say this with certainty, but the crowd’s maddened faces and perverted silhouettes were those affected by a celestial loathing.

 

I wish now that I had pulled my hands from the piano, but I observed Peter laughing at our decomposing reality, enraptured and maddened by the phantasmagoria, and I shamefully continued. 

 

Reflecting on this now, I believe the Music induced me to perform; it interfered with my thoughts and compelled me to play. The Music allowed the sanity to continue. The crowd of onlookers was not so blessed. Their minds were flayed by the notes played.

 

The Music transformed everything it touched. I played a magnificent scale, suspecting the Music had appropriated my nervous system. Then, I realized that this intellect contrasted so sharply from human beliefs and sentiments that it didn’t consider us. We were motes of dust to be swept from the mantle of its existence. 

 

The third movement began with a jarring rhythm change, as blood poured from the partygoers; the Peacocks were dismantling the servants with instruments of torture that had appeared from beneath their garments. Their mouths were open with perfect white teeth bared—their animalistic natures overtaking any rational thought.  

My fingers themselves felt slick with that blood, but on second consideration, it was perspiration from a terror that burned the base of my spine, my armpits and the pits of my knees. 

 

Who had created this music? If my theory were right and it belonged to multiple composers, then why did the two pieces interact to create this symphony of unravelling? Had the Music written itself? Or been channelled through the mind of agonized composers? Surely, these were harmonies of forsaken muses and murdered inspiration. 

 

The music crested, coming to the crescendo. 

 

The music consumed this pleasure palace, I accepted this idea of Rex Mundi as real, and alive within the music I played.  

 

Despite this, I could not discern how to stop and continued until the music crescendoed before spiralling into a torrid tempest of irrationality, like the muttered prayers of doomsday prophets. 

 

Now, the floor roiled with oceans of gore. The Peacocks were slick with thick black coagulated blood. The hosts waded through myriad corpses as the Music spilled into them with the cruel chaos and emergent mortal energies of the dead—they were recipients of its malevolent blessing. 

 

The statues continued thrashing in the flickering light, more alive than dead. 

 

I had attained a point where I could no longer discern reality. The room was unmoored, floating in the spaces between of the notes written on the sheet music before me. It was entirely possible that nothing existed beyond our room but a cascade of white paper dotted with notes that couldn’t exist. The music wasn’t reasonable—it was a cacophony with a core of exquisite symphony that spoke to the tapestry of all realities. 

 

We moved into the fourth movement. The performance removed me from the surrounding terror. My hands worked separately from my mind, as my eyes witnessed our descent into the Rex Mundi’s grasp. 

 

The statuary’s limbs twisted like origami, elongated, multiplied, and disappearing into infinity. They danced a dance that could only be danced by a being that existed outside of human senses. 

 

Those conscious in the crowd were psychotic and sinister ritualists: rotted and sharpened teeth, bloodstain blooms, experimental medical equipment, and coal-fused hearts. 

 

I thought, for a flittering moment amidst that nightmare realm, of my parents, who pushed me to perform, every note an angel arising to join the heavens. 

 

Peter and I arrived at the most demanding section. Here, I begged the Music to take me, for the world I knew had collapsed, leaving only the unrecognizable, a psychopath’s mind embodied. The chaos had ascended beyond the physical; the marble pleasure dome around us liquefied, the bronze statues dematerialized, mouths twisted into eternal screams, as the Music further broke reality. It did it with such apparent ease that I worried for all reality. All around me were the drops of what remained of the building, fragmenting like pearls in the sky. A statue’s arm elongating along the horizon into an abyss that yawned open, waiting for collapse. 

 

Despite this, I continued to contemplate; if the Music lived, it needed the earth’s atmosphere, the piano (the shape, its strings, the keys) our ears and our brains to comprehend it. And my fingers. Without me, this ‘aether’ didn’t exist. Peter and I had built this song and created a nest where the Music could burrow, nest, and multiply. This song seemed ready to spread through everything, dissolving, or consuming everything within its reach.  

 

Those left in the crowd thrashed and moaned. The peacocks were the only survivors still visible. Their ears, eyes, and mouths erupted with blackened filth. I could feel thin rivulets of the same substance pour from the openings in my face. The Music hadn’t spared me of torture. 

 

Reality balanced on the precipice, a forest fire that had blown through the foundations of the stars, and what was left would transform everything. The Music arrived through the bottom of a cosmic lake’s sediment, travelling through the water, sending ripples upwards it came, about to birth itself into celestial nothing. 

 

My hands continued across black and white keys, ensorcelled, the bones suddenly curved like the arms of a praying mantis as the unravelling continued. 

 

Across the room, Peter’s body thrashed as we hit one final crescendo; his back arched to near-breaking, his body trembled with effort as his eyes bulged like overripe watermelons. 

 

I brought my head crashing down into the piano, interrupting how my brain interpreted the music. My hands relaxed. The Music had lost its hold on me. I pitched forward, feeling an onrushing heat as the infernal energy that had been compiled came rushing back into the fragile remnants of reality, lifting me upwards from my seat and then throwing me down into the piano, where unconsciousness awaited.  

 

On awakening, I found my hearing deafened. Blinking, I saw the bodies of the evening’s participants strewn around the dais—lifeless. The building around me lay in ruin, enormous fragments of marble creating a moonlike landscape. Beyond the edges of the broken building, the forest and night swallowed what remained. 

 

Peter’s body lay brutally twisted underneath his piano. 



Anxiety wracked my body, and to calm myself, I began to hum a song I could no longer hear, something familiar. The sky lit with a rainbow hue.
