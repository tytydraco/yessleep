# 

I've been married to Renee for twenty-three years. We got married the summer after she finished nursing school and I got hired on at the mill. Her mother threw the wedding in her own backyard in Morganton. We bought this house from her mother's estate five years back. Four bedrooms, front porch, a pecan tree out back that drops nuts on the tin roof every fall and scares the hell out of you if you forget it's the season.

I'm telling you all this so you understand I know my wife. I know how she breathes when she's asleep. I know she grinds her teeth when she's stressed and sleeps with one foot sticking out of the covers and snores a little, a soft whistle, when she's on her back.

She's been whispering my name in her sleep for about three weeks now. I counted.

The first time, I thought she was awake. I thought she wanted something. I rolled over and said, "Renee, what." She was out cold. Mouth slack. Breathing even. I figured she was dreaming. I told her about it in the morning and she laughed and said well who else would I be dreaming about. Kissed me. Made me pancakes. Normal day.

The second time was four nights later, and I didn't go back to sleep after. She said my name three times. The first was regular. The second was a little off, its like she was drawing the vowels out. The third one, she put the stress on the wrong part of the word. Like she was trying to remember how it went. Like somebody practicing a foreign word they'd heard once.

"Renee," I said. Louder. "Honey."

She didn't move.

I got up and went to the kitchen and drank a glass of water at the sink in the dark. When I came back she was on her side, turned away from me. I lay there for a long time. I thought, people talk weird in their sleep. I thought, her mother did that same thing toward the end. Everything's genetic. Go to sleep, Tom.

I didn't go to sleep.



I want to be careful here because I know what this sounds like. I know it sounds like a man making a mountain out of something normal. So I'm going to tell you what I noticed, in order, and you can decide.

**Night six:** she said my name with the stress on the second syllable. The way somebody says it if they've only ever read it off a page.

**Night eight:** she said my name. Then she said Clarence. Then David Ray. Then somebody called Melvin. I don't know any Melvin. She said them in that same practicing way, trying each one out. She was facing away from me. I did not move.

**Night ten:** she said my name from the wrong side of her face. I know what that sounds like. I'm telling you she was turned away from me, mouth pressed down into the pillow, and the whisper came from the side of her face that was up. From her ear. Or her cheek. Or that place on the jaw where you feel your pulse.

I'm a practical man. I need you to understand that. I don't watch horror movies. I don't believe in ghosts. I fix small engines on the weekend and I read Tom Clancy. I am the least spooky man you will ever meet.

Nights ten through fifteen I stopped sleeping.



Here's some other stuff.

She's been eating more. Three big meals a day and snacks between. Not gaining anything. Renee has always been a picky eater, half a sandwich, she's done. Last Sunday she put away a whole chicken thigh, potatoes, green beans, got up and cut herself a piece of pie, ate that, and cut another.

Her handwriting on the grocery list has changed. Not a lot. The way she does her capital R used to have a loop at the top. Now it doesn't. I noticed because I stopped at the list to add batteries and I thought for a second it was somebody else's list. It wasn't. The rest of the handwriting is hers. Just the Rs are different.

She's been humming a song I don't recognize. Slow, not quite a tune. Five notes that go down, and one that goes back up. She hums it in the shower and while she folds laundry. I asked her what it was last Tuesday. She looked at me like I was speaking Russian.

"I don't know that song."

"You were just humming it."

"No I wasn't."

She wasn't lying. Renee can't lie. It's one of the reasons I married her.



Saturday night I woke up at 3:17 and she was standing at the foot of the bed.

I'm going to describe this carefully because I've been over it in my head a thousand times and I want to get it right.

Her eyes were open. She was looking at me. The bedroom was dark, but the streetlight out front puts a little bit of light through the edge of the curtain, and I could see her face.

She was not breathing.

I watched her for a long time. Her chest didn't move. Her mouth was open a little. She didn't blink.

"Renee."

Nothing.

"Honey, you're sleepwalking. Come back to bed."

Nothing.

I sat up. Slow. I didn't want to spook her. The covers slid down and the cold got at my legs and I kept watching her face and her face kept watching mine and neither of us breathed. I reached out with my right hand. The distance across the bed felt like a mile. I touched her arm.

She was cold.

Not like skin-cold. Not like she'd kicked off the covers and was cold to the touch. Cold like the handrail on the front porch in January. Cold like meat out of the fridge.

She turned her head toward my hand. The rest of her didn't move. Just the head, slow, in little stops, like a cheap animatronic. She looked at my hand on her arm. Then she looked at my face. Then she smiled.

Renee has a good smile. I have been looking at it since 1999. I know every tooth in it. I know which one got capped after she fell off the porch when she was twenty-two. I know the way her lip catches a little on the right side when she's really grinning.

This smile had all that. Every detail. It was her smile, exactly. But it was on her face like somebody was wearing it. Somebody who'd studied it carefully and was trying it out for the first time, proud of how good they'd gotten.

She said my name.

She said it right. First time in three weeks. Perfect.

Then she said, "I'm almost ready."

Then she climbed under the covers and was asleep in thirty seconds. Breathing normal. Warm.



I waited until she left for work Monday morning and I went through the basement.

I don't know what I thought I'd find. Her mother's old files from when she was a bookkeeper. Christmas ornaments. A box of photos from the seventies, kids on a porch squinting at the sun. A cedar chest locked with a key we'd never found.

I broke the lock with a pry bar.

There was a manila envelope sitting on top of everything inside, my wife's name on the front in her mother's handwriting.

**FOR RENEE. IF SOMETHING HAPPENS.**

One piece of lined paper inside. A list of names. Thirteen of them. All men. Dates next to each, going back to 1961.

My wife's father is on the list. He disappeared when she was four. Her mother never talked about it, and I never pushed, because I'm the kind of man who respects when a woman doesn't want to talk about something.

The last name on the list is Clarence Hobbs. He disappeared from Burke County in 2009. I remember. It was in the paper. He went out to check on his dogs and he never came back in.

Underneath the list, in her mother's handwriting:

*It's her. It has always been her. She does not know. I thought maybe it skipped a generation. I was wrong. Tom, if you are reading this, I am sorry. Go tonight. Don't pack. Don't tell her. Just go.*



I have been sitting at the kitchen table for six hours.

Renee will be home from her shift at 7:04. It's 5:58.

I love this woman. I have loved her for more than half my life. I built a porch swing with her father before he died the first time, before I knew he'd died the other way. I held her hand when we got the results that said we weren't going to have kids. I know the sound of her car on the gravel.

I would know. Wouldn't I have known.

The humming started a few minutes ago. I thought it was outside. I thought it was the neighbor's kid. I went to the window and the road was empty.

It's coming from upstairs.

She isn't home yet.

I don't know what to do. I don't know who to call. I don't know what I would say if I called anybody.

I think I'm writing this because her mother wrote her letter and it didn't save her any, but maybe writing it lets you do something instead of nothing in the hour you have.

The humming stopped.

Somebody just turned on the shower.
