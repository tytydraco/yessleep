I found the group by accident.

Or at least that is what I kept telling myself for the first few weeks.

Three months ago my therapist told me I needed “community.” At that point I was sleeping maybe twenty minutes at a time, waking up soaked in sweat and half convinced somebody had just leaned over my bed and whispered my name. I had every light in my apartment on all night. I stopped shaving. Stopped answering texts. Stopped going anywhere unless I absolutely had to.

I told him I was not a group person.

He said, “It isn’t that kind of group.”

Then he wrote an address on the back of his business card and slid it across the table.

St. Bartholomew’s Community Center  

Basement entrance  

Thursdays at 7:00 p.m.

That was it.

No group name. No number. Nothing.

It should have bothered me more than it did.

The first time I went, I almost left when I saw the chairs.

Twelve metal folding chairs in a loose circle under fluorescent lights that made everybody look washed out and sick. The basement smelled like burnt coffee, mop water, and something wet trapped behind the walls. There was a folding table in the corner with stale donut holes and one of those dented silver coffee urns that always sound like they are thinking to themselves.

Near the door was a clipboard with a sign in sheet.

No title. Just first names and dates.

I almost walked back out after I wrote Evan.

Then the woman across from me said, very calmly, “The first tooth didn’t hurt as much as the second one.”

Nobody reacted.

Not the old man with both hands around a paper cup. Not the guy in the hoodie staring at the floor. Not the woman in scrubs rubbing her thumb over her knuckles. Not even the facilitator, Martin, who had a legal pad in his lap and the kind of quiet face people use at funerals.

I laughed.

It came out louder than I meant it to.

Everybody looked at me.

The woman touched the side of her mouth. “The first one cracked. That was mostly shock. The second one... I felt the root go.”

My stomach turned over so hard I had to grab the chair.

Martin gave me a little nod. “You must be new.”

A couple people smiled at me, but not because anything was funny. More like they had seen this before.

Then the woman said, “He pushed my face into the sink after the third one. That’s when I swallowed the blood.”

I made it about three seconds before I staggered to the trash can and threw up.

Nobody came over.

Nobody looked surprised.

When I sat back down, sweating and embarrassed and still tasting stomach acid, Martin clicked his pen and said, “Thank you, Dana.”

That was my first night at the support group for people who remember dying.

I did not know that yet.

I figured it out when the old man started talking.

“My name’s Carl,” he said. “Last week I remembered the stairs again.”

He took a breath that sounded like it hurt. The skin at the base of his throat was puckered and shiny, like old scar tissue.

“I always thought I fell,” he said. “That’s what the dream felt like. But I didn’t fall. Somebody had my ankles.”

Nobody in the room moved.

“He dragged me down slow,” Carl said. “On purpose. My back hit every step. My neck too. I remember seeing the basement light upside down between each hit. Yellow. Dark. Yellow. Dark. By the time I got to the bottom I couldn’t feel my legs. I thought that meant it was done.”

He smiled a little then, but there was nothing friendly in it.

“It wasn’t. I heard him come down after me. One step at a time. He had something metal in his hand. I know because it kept tapping the railing.”

The guy in the hoodie flinched so hard his chair scraped the tile.

And for one second, one ugly impossible second, I knew exactly what Carl had looked like at the bottom of those stairs.

One arm bent wrong under him. Mouth open. Eyes full of tears. Trying not to scream because screaming made it worse.

The image hit me so hard I tasted pennies.

I swallowed until it faded.

Martin looked around the circle. “Anybody else getting something from that?”

The guy in the hoodie raised one hand.

“My name’s Josh,” he said without looking up. “Mine was the closet.”

Dana closed her eyes.

Carl stared into his coffee.

Josh rubbed his hands together inside his sleeves before he spoke again.

“It was small. Real small. I could stand if I kept my head tilted, but not straight. There were coats in there. Damp ones. I remember breathing through wool and dust and old wood. I kept thinking if I stayed quiet, if I just shut up and waited, somebody would open it and laugh and tell me I was an idiot.”

He stopped there and swallowed hard.

“Nobody opened it. Every time I hit the door, something hit back from the other side.”

I felt my stomach tighten.

Not from the words.

From the smell.

Mildew. Old wood. Wet fabric. Human breath in a space too small to keep breathing in.

Not in the room.

In my head.

Josh finally looked up. He was maybe twenty two, maybe younger. Hard to tell. He looked worn down in a way that made age unreliable.

He studied my face for a second too long.

“Sorry,” I said.

I did not mean to say it.

The word just came out.

A couple people glanced at me.

Josh’s expression changed. It was not fear exactly. More like recognition arriving before the rest of him was ready for it.

Martin wrote something on his pad.

The meeting kept going.

A woman named Renee remembered waking folded into a bathtub too small for her body, knees forced up into her chest, water gone cold around her skin. She said every time she tried to move, her heel slipped against something slick in the porcelain and she thought it was soap until her foot caught on a fingernail.

A man named Peter remembered choking on feed pellets with his jaw wired half shut while somebody kept shoving more in and telling him, in a gentle voice, You always waste what you’re given.

A young woman named Mara, maybe nineteen or twenty, said she remembered a hymn playing through the wall while she died. Her older sister sat beside her holding her hand so tightly I could see the tendons standing out.

When Mara hummed two bars of it, I felt something in me drop straight through the floor.

I knew the tune.

Not a little. Exactly.

I knew where the singer dragged the last word.

After the meeting I cornered Martin by the coffee urn.

“What is this place?”

“It’s a support group.”

“For what?”

He glanced at the sign in sheet and then back at me. “For people who are remembering.”

“That’s not an answer.”

He shrugged a little. “Might be the only one you get tonight.”

I should have left.

I went home and stood in front of my bathroom sink for almost an hour instead.

Then I came back the next Thursday.

And the one after that.

I told myself it was curiosity.

That was not the whole truth.

By then some part of me had already started recognizing the shape of things.

The third time I went, I noticed the stack of old sign in sheets clipped beneath the current one.

When Martin stepped away to refill the coffee, I pulled the stack out just enough to see the page under it.

At the top, in faded black ink, was my handwriting.

Evan

I checked the next page.

My name again.

And the next.

And the next.

Not every week.

But enough.

Some signatures were neat. Some slanted. One looked like whoever wrote it was shaking so badly they nearly tore through the paper.

I shoved the stack back into place when I heard Martin’s shoes on the tile.

I did not hear much of the rest of the meeting after that.

When it ended, Josh stopped me near the basement stairs.

“How long have you been coming here?” he asked.

“I haven’t. This is my third time.”

He stared at me. “No.”

I laughed because I did not know what else to do. “What do you mean, no?”

“I mean I’ve seen you before.”

“Here?”

“Not here like this.” He looked pale all of a sudden. “Before before.”

Something cold moved through my chest.

“There was a man in my memory,” Josh said. “I never saw all of him. Mostly his hands. Sometimes his mouth.” He swallowed. “He kept saying he was sorry.”

“A lot of people say sorry.”

“Yeah,” Josh said. “Not like you do.”

That night I dreamed of stairs.

Not falling.

Dragging.

My hands around warm ankles. A body hitting wooden steps one after another. Yellow light. Dark. Yellow. Dark.

And in the dream I was trying not to cry.

Not because I was horrified.

Because I thought I had to do it.

I woke up on my bedroom floor with splinters in both palms.

There are no wooden stairs in my apartment.

After that, the memories started coming in sideways.

Dana talking and my jaw aching deep under the gums.

The smell of bleach in a grocery store turning into the smell of diluted blood.

An old hymn in a coffee shop making me stop dead because I knew I had heard it once through a wall while somebody begged me not to shut a door.

I stopped sleeping almost completely.

I stopped going to work.

My therapist’s office went straight to voicemail for three days.

On the fourth day I drove there and found the suite empty. Furniture gone. Nameplate gone. The receptionist next door said nobody had rented that office in over a year.

I laughed in her face.

Not because it was funny.

Because I could feel something in me starting to split and I did not know what else to do.

The next Thursday I got to St. Bartholomew’s early and waited in my car until Martin unlocked the basement door at 6:31 p.m.

He did not look surprised to see me.

“We need to talk,” I said.

“We are talking.”

I followed him downstairs. In daylight the basement looked even worse. Water stains in the ceiling tiles. Paint bubbling low on the walls. That same smell of damp paper and old dust. He straightened the chairs, set out the clipboard, plugged in the coffee urn.

“Who am I?” I asked.

Martin looked at me. “That usually isn’t the first question.”

“Who am I?”

He rested both hands on the back of one chair.

“I know the name you use when you come here,” he said. “I don’t know if it was the first one.”

“When I come here? You mean now?”

“No.”

I wanted to hit him.

Instead I said, “How long?”

He reached into a file box under the folding table and handed me a manila folder so full it bowed in the middle.

Inside were photocopies of old sign in sheets.

Dozens of them.

Years worth.

My name kept appearing.

Not just Evan. Sometimes E. Mercer. Sometimes Evan M. Once just M in a pressed hard scrawl.

There were notes too, in Martin’s handwriting.

Subject exhibits early stage recognition.  

Subject responds physiologically to sink, stairs, hymn stimulus.  

Apologetic language emerges prior to conscious memory.  

Monitor closely if Mara begins recall of basement door.

There were photographs.

Not bodies.

Objects.

A cracked molar in an evidence bag.  

A stained square of wallpaper with tiny blue flowers.  

A bent length of wire.  

A rust brown tire iron.  

A legal pad warped by water damage.

I dropped the folder.

“What did I do?”

Martin glanced toward the basement stairs and then back to me.

“You ended cycles.”

I stared at him.

“That is the kindest version.”

I grabbed his shirt before I really knew I was moving.

“Tell me.”

He did not fight me.

That was worse.

“When they start remembering together,” he said, “it spreads. The memories bleed. More people begin waking up with things they were never supposed to know. The group helps contain it.”

“So I kill them?”

He did not answer.

He did not need to.

I let go and stumbled backward into a chair.

“No.”

“You usually don’t remember the beginning,” Martin said quietly. “Usually not the middle either. By the end, you always remember enough to know what you are. That’s why you come back.”

“Why would I come back?”

His mouth tightened. “I think part of you hopes someone stops you first.”

The others started arriving before I could say anything else.

Dana with store bought cookies. Carl with gas station coffee. Josh with his hood up. Peter smelling faintly like cigarettes and cold air. Mara with her older sister.

I stood there with the folder open at my feet and watched them greet each other in those tired ordinary voices.

It made me feel sick.

Josh saw my face first.

“What happened?”

Nobody answered him.

Mara looked from Martin to me and then to the sign in sheet.

She frowned.

Not scared. Thinking.

That was when everything changed.

Dana said my name.

Not Evan.

The other one.

“Mercer.”

She said it softly, like she was not sure where it had come from.

The room changed immediately.

Carl dropped his coffee.

Peter sucked in a breath.

Josh took one step back.

Mara’s sister whispered, “No.”

The fluorescent lights buzzed louder.

And inside my head, something let go.

Dana at the sink, cheek pressed to stained porcelain while broken teeth clicked in pink water. My left hand in her hair. My own voice shaking as I told her I was sorry.

Carl at the bottom of wooden stairs, spine bent wrong, looking up at me while I came down holding the tire iron because the first strike had not been enough.

Josh trapped behind a closet door, hitting it weakly while I stood on the other side with my forehead against the wood, crying harder than he was.

Peter on his knees in a feed shed, choking and convulsing while mash spilled from his nose.

Mara—

I stopped there.

I couldn’t.

The room had gone dead still.

Dana was crying.

Carl looked like he might throw up.

Josh had gone white.

Mara was staring at me.

Then Martin said, in a voice so tired it barely sounded human, “You all still think this room is for you.”

Nobody moved.

He looked around the circle.

“It never was.”

Something under the floor knocked once.

Every head turned.

Then again.

Then a third time.

Three slow knocks from beneath the concrete.

My skin went cold.

Mara’s sister pulled her back toward the stairs.

“What did you do?” Dana whispered.

Martin did not answer her.

He was looking at me.

And suddenly I knew why.

Because the knocking felt familiar.

Not from outside a door.

From underneath one.

From the wrong side.

Carl stared at the floor and started shaking his head. “No.”

Josh said, “No. No, no.”

Martin said, “When all of you remember together, he opens.”

“What does that even mean?” Dana asked.

“It means the deaths were not the point,” Martin said. “They were delays.”

The floor knocked again.

This time the far wall answered.

Three more knocks.

Not echoing.

Answering.

The room came apart.

Dana backed into the chairs so hard one went over. Carl started praying under his breath. Peter covered his ears. Mara’s sister dragged her toward the stairs, but Mara dug her heels in.

Then Josh said, very quietly, “I remember after.”

Everything stopped.

He was looking at me, but not like I was just the threat anymore.

Like I was only one piece of something much worse.

“I remember killing you,” he said.

My stomach dropped.

Dana looked at him. “What?”

“I remember holding him under the water in the church baptismal,” Josh said. “Martin kept telling me not to listen if he started apologizing.”

Carl made a terrible sound in his throat.

Then he said, “I remember the tire iron.”

Dana turned to him.

Carl’s hands were shaking so hard coffee was dripping off his fingers.

“I took it from him,” Carl said. “I remember hitting him with it.”

Peter whispered, “Oh God.”

Dana backed away from all of us and covered her mouth. “I locked him in the bathroom upstairs,” she said. “The one with the sink. I held the door shut while he begged me to open it.”

Every person in that room looked at every other person, and the same realization moved through all of us.

It was not just that I had killed them before.

They had killed me too.

Again and again.

Different ways. Different nights. Same room. Same song in the walls. Same ending bought off for a little longer with more violence.

The sign in sheet fluttered.

No wind.

Just the paper moving by itself.

Then the top page changed.

Ink spread through it from underneath, like old writing soaking back to the surface.

Dana gasped.

Carl crossed himself.

At the top of the page, above the names, one word appeared.

DOOR

The first pain hit my mouth.

One of my molars came loose so suddenly I bit down on it. I spat it into my hand with blood and spit hanging after it.

Then my back spasmed.

Not like a cramp.

Like impact.

One hit.

Then another.

Then another.

As if my spine was taking stairs from the inside.

I screamed and dropped to one knee.

My jaw locked half shut.

Cold water spilled from my nose onto the floor.

Bathwater smelling water.

Peter started retching.

Dana screamed.

Bruises rose around my throat while we watched them happen, finger shaped and darkening.

Mara was the only one not panicking.

She was watching Martin.

Martin moved fast then. He slammed the basement door shut and threw the deadbolt.

Carl shouted, “What are you doing?”

“Buying time.”

The knocking under the floor became pounding.

Something hit the wall behind the coffee table hard enough to crack the plaster. A wet brown stain spread through the paint.

Josh grabbed the deadbolt and tried to wrench it open. Martin shoved him away.

“If that door opens now,” Martin snapped, “it won’t just be him this time.”

That shut everybody up.

Even with blood filling my mouth and my back feeling like it was being climbed from the inside out, that line cut through everything.

It won’t just be him this time.

Mara spoke then.

Quietly.

“You’re lying.”

Martin turned toward her.

She pointed at him.

“He’s not the one it uses.”

The room seemed to hold its breath.

Martin’s face changed. Not guilt. Not panic. Recognition.

Mara’s sister grabbed her arm. “Mara, stop.”

But Mara kept looking at Martin.

“I remember your office,” she said. “Before the church. Before the group. Before him.” Then she looked around at all of us. “You’re the one who taught him to say sorry.”

For one second, nobody moved.

Then the far wall burst.

Not all at once.

A fist sized hole first. Plaster blew inward. A smell came through with it so foul and wet and old I nearly blacked out.

Then a hand pushed through.

Gray skin. No fingernails. Knuckles scraped raw.

Then another hand.

Then another.

Behind me, the sign in sheet rustled.

I turned.

There were thirteen chairs.

I know there had only been twelve.

I counted them the first night.

Now there were thirteen.

And the last one was occupied.

At first I thought I was losing my mind.

Then it moved.

It was me.

Or close enough to me to be worse than a stranger.

My face, but water bloated. Jaw caved in on one side. Hair stuck to the skull. Shirt dark with old blood. One eye milky and collapsed. Martin’s legal pad sat in its lap under rotted fingers.

Dana screamed.

Carl stumbled backward and knocked over two chairs.

Josh pulled Mara behind him.

Martin did not move.

That scared me more than anything else in the room.

Because he looked like he had been waiting for this.

The thing in the thirteenth chair smiled at me.

Then it spoke in my voice.

“You always remember this part too late.”

The wall behind the coffee table split wider with a sound like ribs opening.

More hands came through.

Human hands.

Large ones. Small ones. One with a bracelet sunk into the flesh. One missing fingers. One so waterlogged the skin had peeled back over the nails.

The room erupted.

Dana crying.

Mara’s sister screaming for somebody to open the door.

Josh dragging chairs into a pile like that might help.

Carl saying no over and over like he thought repetition might undo what was happening.

And Martin just standing there, staring at the thing in the chair like he knew it.

Like he knew exactly which version of me it was.

Then the thing looked at him.

Its smile widened.

And for the first time that night, Martin looked honestly afraid.

It opened the legal pad with a wet deliberate motion. The pages stuck, then peeled apart.

It looked down.

Then back at me.

“Ask him about the house,” it said.

Martin shouted, “Don’t.”

Too late.

The thing tilted its head. Water dripped from its hair to the floor.

Then it said, very clearly:

“The basement is where he keeps the ones who remember. The house is where he makes them.”

Everything stopped.

Not the pounding.

Not the screaming.

Not the hands forcing through the broken wall.

Us.

Every person still standing in that room went still.

I looked at Martin.

He had gone pale in a way that had nothing to do with fear.

Josh found his voice first.

“What house?”

Martin said nothing.

Dana took one shaky step toward him. “What house?”

Still nothing.

The thing in the thirteenth chair started to stand.

Its knees bent the wrong way before snapping straight with a wet popping sound.

I wish that sounded fake to me when I read it back.

It took one step.

Then another.

Its shoes left little crescent puddles on the tile.

When it got close enough, I could smell it.

Standing water. Mildew. Pennies.

And under that, wet wallpaper.

Tiny blue flowers.

My stomach dropped.

Because I knew that wallpaper.

I did not know how.

But I knew it.

The thing leaned close enough for me to see one of my own loose teeth stuck in the torn meat of its lower lip.

It whispered, “You left us there.”

Then it pressed something cold into my hand.

A key.

Old brass.

House key.

I looked down at it.

When I looked back up, it was smiling wider.

Behind it, the hole in the wall split all the way to the floor.

Hands started pouring through.

And from the dark on the other side, somebody started humming the hymn.

That was when Martin finally moved.

Not toward the door.

Toward me.

He grabbed my wrist so hard I nearly dropped the key.

“If you want any of them alive by morning,” he said, “you need to remember where the house is.”

Then the lights went out.
