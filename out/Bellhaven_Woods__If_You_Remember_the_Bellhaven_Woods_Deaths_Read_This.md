If you were on paranormal forums in the early 2010s, or those old true crime boards before they all got bought out, you’ve probably seen threads about the Bellhaven Woods murders.

Every October somebody reposts the same blurry newspaper scans or police photos or fake transcripts onto Reddit threads and paranormal forums and YouTube compilations with titles like “The Bellhaven Woods Mystery Explained” or “Top Five Real Demon Possessions.” Iris Mercer’s name shows up constantly on paranormal blogs. Usually beside edited pictures of the woods or screenshots from old local news broadcasts with the contrast turned all the way up so her eyes look black.

Most of those people are working with the same fifteen pieces of publicly available information because there was barely anything released in the first place. 

Here's what the public record actually contains. On October 14th, 2009, a high school senior named Malcolm Reyes was found dead in his van, which was parked on a fire road about a quarter mile into the tree line off Route 9. He was the fourth person to die in Belhaven that year under circumstances the sheriff's department described as "under active investigation.” Before Malcolm there had been a woman found at the reservoir, a retired teacher, and a sixteen-year-old boy whose case was closed within a week. All four deaths happened within about a two-mile radius.

Approximately five days after Malcolm's body was discovered, the body of Iris Mercer — at that point the presumed suspect in his death — was found by a search team about half a mile into the woods. Her death was ruled a suicide, though the coroner's report noted that the body showed signs of having been moved from the original place of death.

Local sources described a string of behavior in the weeks leading up to October that didn't make it into any official account. Strange activity around Saint Germaine's Church on the east end of town, where the congregation had tripled in size over the course of about six months and where several longtime members had stopped attending after raising concerns they declined to specify publicly. Three separate noise complaints in the same week about "gatherings" in the woods near the Route 9 fire roads, which the sheriff's department responded to and then filed no report about.

Law enforcement barely spoke about the investigation after the first few weeks. The coroner’s report was partially sealed. Half the witness statements were either lost, withheld, or reduced to one-sentence summaries that raised more questions than they answered. The case was technically closed when Iris died.

You can still find archived forum threads where people argue about details that were never confirmed one way or another. Why Iris’ body had been moved. If Malcolm fought back. Whether there were symbols carved into the trees near the site where she was found. If Saint Germaine’s Church paid to keep certain documents out of public records. Most of it spiraled into conspiracy theories eventually.

Those who were directly involved decided a long time ago to stay quiet. I was one of them. I kept that decision for fifteen years because I didn't think the truth would help anyone, and I think now that I was wrong about that too. My name is Kolby Hatch. I was seventeen years old in the fall of 2009. I moved to Bellhaven the summer before junior year, a little over a year before the deaths. This is what actually happened in that town.

I need to tell you about Jensen before I tell you about any of this, because he is where it starts, and because people who only know my reputation from that year tend to have the wrong idea of it.

Jensen Schaefer was my best friend from sixth grade through the end of junior year. We met because we sat next to each other in earth science and both failed the same quiz and were assigned to redo it together after class, and we spent the whole hour talking, and after that we ate lunch together every day for two years. At the time I had dark curly hair down past my ears because my father had given up trying to make me cut it, and I wore old band shirts so consistently that teachers started recognizing me by them. By sophomore year my nose had already been broken once from a fight at a middle school football game and healed slightly crooked afterward. 

In November of our sophomore year, an upperclassman named Wyatt Briggs decided that Jensen was worth tormenting. By March, it had been going on long enough that Jensen went home one Tuesday and swallowed most of a bottle of his mother's Lexapro.

He survived. I found out the next morning when his mother called our house at six a.m.

I don't have a lot to say about the two days between that call and what I did to Wyatt Briggs in the student parking lot. I found Wyatt on a Thursday afternoon and I hit him until someone pulled me off, and when it was over his cheekbone was fractured and I had broken two knuckles. His father was a vice principal in the district, so the whole thing got handled quietly. I left voluntarily as part of an agreement, which kept it off my permanent record and kept Wyatt's family from having to explain why their son had spent months making another kid's life a living hell. What it didn't keep from spreading was the story itself. According to other students, I had apparently put Wyatt in the ICU, which was not true, but you can't correct a rumor.

Needless to say, I was relieved when my father decided to move us across the U.S. a month after.

The town of Bellhaven was three blocks of downtown and then neighborhoods spreading out in every direction, older houses mostly. Two gas stations. A diner called Patsy's that had the same menu it'd had since 1987. A Dollar General. A laundromat that also sold lottery tickets. Six churches, which I didn't count until later, when it started to matter.

That summer was quiet. My aunt lived three houses down on Caldwell Street and came over for dinner twice a week and asked me about my plans. I gave her answers and we got along fine. My dad was relieved to be somewhere smaller and slower, but I think he was also relieved that I seemed to be handling it, or performing handling it convincingly enough that he didn't have to ask. I rode my bike around and learned the town. I stayed out of trouble because there was no trouble available to get into, and that was probably what my dad had been counting on.

The high school was called Bellhaven Regional High and sat on the west end of town with a parking lot full of beat-up cars and a marquee sign out front that still said CONGRATULATIONS CLASS OF 2008 by the time school started in late August. I walked in on the first day and immediately understood that the rumors had arrived before I had.

I had been hoping that four hundred miles of distance might mean starting clean. It did not. A couple seniors asked outright whether the rumors were true within my first week there. One kid in gym class asked if I had ever been arrested before he even asked where I moved from. But honestly, the adults were the worst of it. I remember one teacher stopping me after class during my second week because another student had claimed I threatened him in the parking lot. The threat turned out to be me telling him to move his truck because he was blocking my car in. The teacher still spent ten minutes explaining that Bellhaven High had “zero tolerance policies regarding intimidation.”

Bellhaven was small enough that I probably could have laid low and waited for the story to fade. I was considering doing exactly that when Summer Okeke-Pruitt sat down across from me during the second week of school without asking.

She set her tray down, dropped into the seat, and looked at me across the cafeteria table. Summer wore oversized clothes almost constantly — hoodies with the sleeves pushed halfway over her hands, huge jackets once the weather cooled off, loose jeans — and her long twists were tied back that day with a faded blue bandanna.

“Okay,” she said. “I have a question. Did you actually fracture his orbital socket or were people just being dramatic?”

I blinked. “...It was actually his cheekbone.”

"Okay, I figured." She picked up her fork. "Taylor Breck said orbital socket and Taylor Breck has never been right about anything since the sixth grade, so."

I didn't say anything for a second because I was waiting for the pivot — the ‘so what's wrong with you?’ It didn't come. She just ate and looked around the cafeteria, then looked back at me like she expected us to have a normal conversation, which we did.

We talked about her, mostly, because she kept asking questions and I kept answering them and by the time the bell rang I had learned that she'd been at Bellhaven since freshman year, that she ran cross country badly and did it anyway, that she had a complicated relationship with her parents that she described as a work in progress, and that she had strong opinions about which gas station had the better hot food, which was the Sunoco on Route 9 and not the Shell, no contest.

Summer introduced me to Malcolm two weeks in, on a Friday afternoon, which is when I found out about the van.

It was a 1993 Chevy in a yellowish beige that had oxidized down to something closer to the color of an old newspaper, and it ran about sixty percent of the time. He'd bought it for four hundred dollars from a guy his cousin knew and spent six months making the back of it livable. We spent more hours in the back of that van than I can account for, parked in various lots and river pull-offs and dead ends.

The day I met Malcom Reyes, the van was parked in the student lot at the end of the school day. He was sitting on the hood eating chips when Summer walked me over. He had locs down to his shoulders and wore bright sweaters even when the weather probably didn’t justify them. He was a few inches shorter than me, but I still felt intimated when he looked me over before nodding at the van behind him.

"You can get in if you want. We usually just hang here for a while on Fridays."

"Malcolm's van is Bellhaven's third-best hangout spot," Summer said, climbing up onto the hood next to him.

"Second," he refuted. "The quarry is first and then there's us and then the k-mart parking lot."

"The k-mart parking lot is not a hangout spot."

"Tell that to the kids who are always there."

I got in the van. The back was set up with a secondhand mattress wedged between the wheel wells with a sleeping bag folded on one end, Christmas lights strung along the ceiling, a little Bluetooth speaker balanced on the flat part above the wheel well. A hand-drawn map of Bellhaven and the surrounding county was taped up next to it, marked in different colored pen, though I couldn't read what the markings meant from where I sat. 

Two other people were already in the back. One of them I didn't know yet — Cecile Vanek, who was cross-legged on the mattress with a textbook open in her lap and a cigarette burning in her other hand. Her hair had been bleached almost completely blonde except for one uneven purple streak near the left side, and she wore long sleeves with short denim skirts and heavy boots that thudded against the van floor whenever she shifted position.

And then there was Iris.

I've spent a lot of time over the past fifteen years thinking about how to describe Iris Mercer to people who didn't know her, and what I keep running into is the problem of the ending. Given her perception now being what it is, she’s stopped being a person and instead is either a victim or a monster, depending on which version the storyteller needs.

I’ll tell you what half these posts don’t say about her: She grew up in a strict religious household. By the time I met her she'd created distance from a lot of that, or was in the process of creating it. She'd still go on Sundays or whenever she felt she needed comfort. She'd done ballet for several years as a child and stopped when her family moved. She had a documented aversion to pain, her own and other people's. She didn't watch horror movies. She winced when you stubbed your toe. She crossed the street to avoid aggressive dogs. 

The day I met her, she was sitting in the back of the van with her knees crossed, an open bottle of purple nail polish on the floor beside her as she painted her nails. 

She had long dark curls falling forward every time she tilted her head down, and there was a small silver cross necklace resting against the front of her dress. Most days after that she wore long skirts or dark tights or oversized cardigans over dresses that looked half thrifted and half homemade. At the time I mostly just registered that she looked very sure of herself in a way I wasn’t used to people being.

After Cecile greeted me, Iris looked up long enough to say, “Hi,” before turning back to her hand.

Cecile and Iris had known each other since seventh grade, Malcolm told me. They had what I can only describe as a frenemy dynamic in the classic sense of the word. Despite this, they fell into routine with the rest of our group like it was nothing.

In the following weeks, after school or on weekends, we'd all end up parked somewhere in Malcolm's van: the lot of the Kmart that had closed in 2003, the pull-off where you could see the whole valley on clear days, sometimes just Malcolm's driveway with the back doors open and the string lights on and the speaker playing whatever Summer had decided we were all listening to that week. Cecile would do homework on the mattress, one leg folded under her, making corrections in pencil. Malcolm would be half-in and half-out of the front, trying to fix whichever thing had most recently stopped working. Summer would be talking, as usual. And Iris would be reading, or would have her head tipped back against the door and her eyes closed.

My reputation settled into something manageable by mid-September. The adults stopped watching me so heavily. The students sorted into two camps pretty cleanly: the ones who decided I was going to start something, and the ones who found the story interesting and wanted to talk about it, which I was equally uninterested in. There were a handful of people in between who, like Summer, seemed to have moved on, and those were the ones I ended up talking to. I'd been in Bellhaven for two months. I had somewhere to be after school. I was starting to feel, for the first time since moving to town, like I might actually be okay.

The quarry was about four miles out on Route 9, past the grain elevator and a stretch of fence where someone had been tying ribbons to the wire for years, red and yellow and orange and white, weather-faded and fraying at the edges. Malcolm had shown me the turn-off on a Wednesday, a gravel road that dipped down through tree cover and opened onto the old limestone cut: two levels of flat rock shelf above the water, a rusted ladder someone had anchored into the lower shelf with concrete a long time ago, and the water itself, green-black and deep enough that you couldn't see the bottom on even the clearest days.

It had been a working quarry until the mid-eighties and before that a swimming hole going back further than anyone could trace. Half the adults in Bellhaven had stories about it. The other half had stories about why you shouldn't go. It was private property in the technical sense, but the fence had been down for years and nobody had ever tried to repair it, so in practice it was just another teenage hangout spot.

We were there on a Saturday in late September, all five of us. Malcolm had a cooler in the back of the van, and Summer had a portable speaker that she'd zip-tied to a belt loop because she'd learned the hard way that leaving it unattended on the rock shelf meant it got knocked in eventually. Cecile was sitting at the edge of the upper shelf with her feet hanging over. I was in the water. Iris was on the lower shelf, sitting with her knees drawn up and a book resting in her lap.

"Are you getting in?" I called up.

She looked down at me. "The water's green."

"It's fine."

"Kolby. The water is visibly green."

"It's algae. It's natural."

"I don't want algae on me."

"You're not going to get algae on you."

"You don't know that," she said, and looked back at her book.

Summer cannonballed off the upper shelf and hit the water six feet away from me and came up shaking water out of her braids. Malcolm was already in, floating on his back with his eyes closed and one arm under his head, entirely at peace with the algae situation.

"Iris," Summer said, treading water. "Get in."

"I'm good."

"You're always good. Get in."

Iris looked up from her book. "Has anyone ever told you that you make it very hard to say no to you?”

"Yes.” Summer grinned. “Get in."

A beat. Then Iris put her book down, stood up, and jumped off the lower shelf, almost entirely silent and barely disturbing the surface. She came up near me, pushed her hair back, and looked at Summer with a sarcastic frown.

"Happy?"

"Very," Summer said.

Cecile came down from the upper shelf eventually and sat at the edge and put her feet in and that was as far as she was going, she said. Malcolm climbed out and sat next to her. I floated in the middle and Summer had found a section of the old quarry wall where the limestone had eroded into a set of rough handholds and was trying to climb it, with no particular success.

I looked over at Iris. She'd drifted to a part of the quarry where the rock wall curved inward and made a shallow alcove, out of the main current. She was looking up at the tree line.

"What are you looking at?" I asked.

"The ridge." She paused. "You can see the roof from here."

I looked. Through the trees, just barely, was a line of dark roofing, overgrown and listing slightly, visible only because the September light hit it at the right angle.

"It’s the top of the Bellhaven facility, I think," she said, more to herself than me. 

I'd heard about it by then. Bellhaven State Psychiatric Facility had operated from the forties until a fire in the mid-seventies took out part of the building and prompted the state to close it rather than rebuild. Before the fire there'd been something like two hundred patients and a staff of about sixty. Afterward there were lawsuits, sealed records, a rezoning of the property that nobody from the county could fully explain when pressed. The building was still standing, or most of it was. Teenagers had been daring each other to go in since before we were born, apparently.

"Have you ever gone?" I asked.

"No." She kept looking at the roofline. "Summer thinks it's stupid to be interested in it."

"Summer thinks it's stupid to be interested in a lot of things."

Iris looked at me. "She's not wrong most of the time," she admitted. "She has a better sense of what things deserve attention than almost anyone I know."

"But..?"

A pause. "But I think sometimes a thing deserves attention even if you're not going to do anything about it."

I floated on my back and looked up at the sky. "What do you think is in there?"

"I don't know." She pulled her arms through the water slowly. 

Above us on the shelf, Malcolm said something to Cecile that made her laugh, and Iris glanced up at the sound of it.

The church thing had been going on all summer, apparently, but I started hearing about it in September.

Saint Germaine's was on the east side of Bellhaven, past the Dollar General and the auto parts store, in a building that had been a different church before it changed hands sometime in the early nineties. It was a small congregation historically: forty or fifty people, nothing remarkable about it. 

There had always been rumors around Bellhaven about Saint Germaine’s becoming increasingly intense and insular over the last year or so, though “rumors” makes it sound more dramatic than it usually was.

But sometime around spring, it started getting bigger.

I heard most of it secondhand, from Malcom, who had heard it from his mother, who had a coworker whose sister-in-law had gone to Saint Germaine's for years and then stopped. The new pastor had come in about eight months before. Nobody I asked could tell me where he'd come from or who'd brought him in. The congregation doubled by June. People started driving in from outside Bellhaven to attend the Sunday services.

"What's wrong with that?" I said. We were in the van, idling in the school parking lot after hours. 

"Nothing on its own," Malcolm said. He had one knee up against the back of the front seat, turned to face the group. "It's the way people talk about it. Like– my mom's coworker, right, she was there for fifteen years, even baptized her kids there. She left this spring and she won't say why."

"That's it?" Cecile said. She was examining her nails, half-dismissively.

"There's other stuff.” He continued. “Tommy Soviero’s parents pulled him out of school for a week in September to do some kind of retreat out in the woods that was through the church. He came back and Emily said he was different. Like she was talking to him through a wall."

"Emily’s dramatic," Cecile said.

"Emily’s not dramatic."

Cecile made a face that communicated polite disagreement.

"Okay," Cecile said finally, and got out her own homework, and that was the end of it.

.The last Friday before we went to Bellhaven State, we were all at the quarry again, but thanks to the weather, the water was too cold to get into. We sat on the upper shelf and watched the water go dark below us. The tree line was orange and red and just beginning to go bare at the edges. From this angle you could see more of the facility than you could in the months before: the roof clearly visible now through the stripped branches, and what might have been a window, or a gap where a window used to be.

“I met this guy named Gerard Fitch yesterday,” Summer began. “He stopped me outside the pharmacy because he saw me taking pictures of the old Bellhaven State sign. He worked maintenance at Bellhaven State Psychiatric Facility from the late sixties until seventy-four.”

That got everyone’s attention.

Malcolm looked up. “Did he actually talk to you about it?”

“Sort of.” She tucked a strand of hair behind her ear. “He wouldn’t talk about patients at all. The second I asked, he shut that down completely. But he talked about the building itself.”

The wind moved across the quarry hard enough that I could hear it whistling through the trees below us.

“He said there was a sub-level beneath the east wing that wasn’t included on the official floor plans.” Summer added.

“What the hell does that even mean?” Iris asked.

“It means either somebody built part of the place off-record or somebody deliberately removed it from documentation later.” Summer looked back up at us. “He said the staff called it the annex.”

Nobody interrupted her after that.

“He said the only way into it was through a staircase on the third floor of the east wing.” She paused briefly. “Which apparently bothered him even back then because who builds basement access on the third floor of a building?”

Malcolm frowned. “Did he ever go down there?”

“Twice.”

“He said he got sent to inspect plumbing because the east wing kept having pressure problems.” Summer looked uncomfortable now, like she regretted asking as many questions as she had. “The first time he went down there, he said he came back up with a migraine so bad he threw up in the parking lot afterward. The second time, same thing. He told me after that he refused to go back and nobody ever pressured him about it again.”

Iris finally looked up from her notebook. “What did he say was down there?”

Summer shook her head slowly. “That’s the weird part. He said he couldn’t really describe it properly. But he told me that his grandson was out near the facility property a few nights ago with friends.” She glanced between us. “They saw lights inside the east wing.”

Malcolm immediately shook his head. “It’s just other teenagers.”

“That’s what the sheriff’s department said.” Summer nodded. “But Gerard said the kid came home terrified. He said they heard voices coming from inside.”

“What kind of voices?” I asked, intrigued despite myself.

Summer glanced between  us before looking back out across the quarry. “He said his grandson described it as chanting.”

Cecile rolled her eyes. “That sounds fake.”

“I know it does.” Summer said.

“No, I mean specifically fake. Like somebody trying to scare people away from the property.”

“Maybe.” Summer shrugged. “But Gerard believed him enough to call the non-emergency line.”

Iris looked up from where she’d been absentmindedly peeling the label off a beer bottle. “And?” 

“And the deputy told him kids break in there all the time and it was probably nothing.”

Nobody said anything for a few seconds after that. The quarry was quiet except for the distant sound of traffic somewhere beyond the trees and the occasional splash of water against the rocks below us.

Iris exhaled slowly through her nose. "So what are we saying here?"

Summer looked at her. "I want to go see."

Cecile cut in: "Obviously you do."

"Don't you want to know what's in there?"

"I know what's in there," Cecile said. "Mold. Asbestos. Teenagers from another school." 

"I'm going," Summer ignored her. "Anyone who wants to come can come."

Cecile looked at the ground. "Fine," she conceded. "But I get to pick the day."

She picked a Saturday, twelve days later. Cecile's logic, which she explained, was that Saturday gave us all of Sunday to recover from whatever stupid thing we were about to do, and that mid-October was late enough in the season that we wouldn't have to worry about other people being out there with the same idea, because only genuinely stupid people went into derelict buildings in the cold. 

We parked on the fire road just past the Route 9 turn-off and covered the rest of the distance on foot, three-quarters of a mile through the tree line. 

The facility appeared through the trees in pieces — the roof first, then the upper windows, and then the full thing as we came out through the last stand of trees onto what had been the access road. Three stories of dark brick, the paint peeling away in long vertical strips. Some of the windows were boarded, but maybe half of the boards had been pulled away or broken through long before us. A cracked concrete path ran from the tree line to the main entrance, weeds up through the cracks and the weeds themselves dead and frost-blackened now, and on either side of the path the grounds had been absorbed back into the woods over thirty years, the lawns the facility had surely once maintained now indistinguishable from the surrounding undergrowth.

It was larger than I'd expected. I'd built a picture from Summer's description and Gerald Fitch's account and what I'd glimpsed through the trees, but somehow the real building was still bigger than all of that. 

The window we found on the west side was low, its frame and glass long gone, easy to step through. Summer went first, then Malcolm, then Iris. Cecile went after, and I followed her.

Inside, it smelled of mildew and something else I couldn’t place. Whatever it was, it wasn't fully identifiable or quite right, and it got stronger as we moved further from the window.  

Four flashlight beams went in four directions and found the first-floor corridor: long, doors on both sides, most of them open, peeling paint and water damage on the walls, and at the far end a section of collapsed ceiling that had brought part of the floor above down with it into a rubble heap that blocked the hallway entirely. The patient rooms off the corridor were small. A rusted metal bedframe sat in most of them, its mattress long gone.

We moved down the corridor toward the collapsed section and found a stairwell branching off to the right before we reached it. Luckily, the stairwell was intact, concrete with plaster debris that crunched underfoot and made it impossible to move quietly. We went up single file.

The second floor was darker than the ground floor due to most of the windows being boarded, so what light came through the gaps was thin and directionless. Our flashlights made it workable. The corridor up here was wider and the rooms varied: a common area at the far end, a long rectangular space where tables had been and most of them had collapsed, and off the main hallway a room that had held equipment bolted to the walls and a central unit, all of it eaten by rust. I stood in the doorway of that room for a moment and tried to identify what the equipment had been and couldn't.

We were moving through the common area when Iris stopped.

Her weight shifted and her head turned as she looked toward a direction that wasn't any of us. The change in her posture was so deliberate that it took me a second to process that she had stopped rather than turned.

"What?" I paused.

"Did you guys hear that?"

The rest of us went still. The building moved around us — the creak of stressed structure, wind finding its way through the broken upper floors and carrying the cold with it, somewhere a loose piece of metal tapping against something else. 

"Hear what?" Summer tilted her head.

Iris was quiet. Her head was still turned toward the far wall, toward the corridor that ran deeper into the east wing. "Someone speaking," she said. "Like– very far away."

We listened.

Nothing.

"I don't hear anything," Malcolm frowned.

She turned back to us. Her expression was difficult to read in the flashlight. "Never mind.”

The third-floor stairwell was at the far end of the corridor and it was worse than the one below. The railing had been pulled away or had rusted free of its anchors and was simply gone, and the concrete steps had cracked and heaved as the building settled.

Summer found a candle on the landing before we'd gone five feet.

She crouched over it with her flashlight close. White pillar candle, half-burned, set upright in a small glass dish with a rim of solidified wax around the base. She held the light at an angle and looked at the drip pattern down the side.

"Someone burned this recently," she said. "If this had been sitting through a winter the wax would've yellowed. It hasn't." She looked up at us. "Someone was up here in the last week. Maybe less."

"Don't touch it," Cecile said. She was standing with her flashlight on the corridor ahead of us, not on the candle, and I realized she was covering our backs. She'd been doing it, I thought, since the second floor. Keeping herself between us and the corridor behind, angled to see what we couldn't.

"I'm not gonna," Summer said, and stood.

The east wing corridor on the third floor was a different world from the rest of the building. The floor simply stopped existing in a section about fifteen feet wide: the planking and the subfloor had fallen through and you could see straight through to the second floor below, and above, through the gap where the ceiling had gone, a clean rectangle of night sky showing stars.

We went around the gap along the narrow intact section of floor that remained against the wall, one at a time, backs to the bricks, and didn't look down.

The room beyond the gap had once been a patient room. Unlike the rest of the third floor, somebody had reused it recently.

Its rusted bedframe had been dragged back upright against one wall. Someone had cleaned sections of the floor recently enough that the dust broke around shoeprints instead of covering everything evenly. There were blankets folded in one corner.

The candles in the room were in a row along the baseboard: eight or nine of them, same white pillars, varying heights. Between them, arranged with an attention to placement that was clearly not random: a glass bottle, old, the pharmaceutical kind with a cork stopper, the label faded past legibility but the bottle itself still held something dark. A length of dark fabric folded into a precise square, placed beside the bottle. And above all of it, pinned directly into the crumbling plaster of the wall with what looked like dissection pins, a piece of paper. Handwritten. Dense with text that covered the page front to back, the writing small and deliberate.

"What language is that?" I frowned.

Nobody answered. I looked at Iris. She was reading it, her eyes moving line by line and lips moving slightly.

"Iris?"

"Some of it's Latin," she looked up. Her voice was steady. "My church used Latin for the liturgy and I studied it for two years, I can read it." She looked back at the paper. "The parts that aren't Latin I don't recognize.”

"What do the Latin parts say?" Malcolm asked.

A pause. Iris's eyes moved over the paper. "Consecration language," she said slowly. "Dedication. The kind of phrasing you'd use to give something over, I think." She stopped. "To transfer custody of something."

"To who?" Summer questioned.

Iris said nothing for a moment. "It’s probably named in the non-latin section, but I can't read it… I don't know." She looked at the bottle on the floor, at the dark cloth.

"We need to keep moving," Cecile said.

"Cecile's right," Malcolm agreed, but he looked at the paper for another moment before he turned away.

Within a few minutes, Malcolm had moved ahead of us down the third-floor corridor. He was shining his light at the wall, and the rest of us went to see what he'd found.

It was the door to the annex stairwell at the far edge of the damage, exactly where Gerald Fitch's account had placed it. Green-painted metal, the paint reduced to a few stubborn flakes, the door itself pushed fully inward and resting against the wall, open, as if someone had gone through it recently and left it that way. A regular institutional door. What it opened onto was a stairwell that went down: narrow, a single flight, and at the bottom a wider space was visible in our flashlight beams. The smell coming up from it was strong enough that Cecile put her sleeve over her nose without comment.

The sub-level annex was a single long room with low ceilings and stone walls, much older than anything above it, with a floor of fitted stone rather than the concrete and planking of the upper building. At the far end, the clear structural skeleton of a chapel — the altar stone intact, a relief carving above it that was older than the facility by at least a century, rough-cut, a cross at the center with shapes radiating outward.

And the room was in active use.

The candles here were a deliberate arrangement across and around the altar surface, varying heights, most of them burned significantly down through multiple sessions. The wax had pooled and rehardened in layers, building up. A metal bowl sat at the center of the altar, dark with residue. Photographs were arranged around the base of the bowl. I looked at one and my light caught a face I recognized — Tommy Soverio. There were others I didn't know. A dozen, maybe more, the photos overlapping at the edges, and beneath them pages of the same dense handwritten text from the corridor upstairs.

Malcolm was looking at the photographs. Summer had crouched near the far wall with a stack of papers balanced against her knee, flashlight moving line by line. Cecile had stopped pretending she was interested in any of it altogether. She stood facing the stairwell we had come down from with her arms folded.

It took me longer than it should've to realize that Iris wasn't beside me anymore.

At first I thought she had just moved farther behind me while Malcolm and Summer argued quietly about whether the dates on the papers meant anything. I could still picture exactly where she had been standing a minute earlier.

Then I realized I couldn’t hear her breathing and turned around automatically. She was gone.

For a second my brain genuinely refused to process it. I swept my flashlight across the chamber once, then again slower. The room was too small for somebody to disappear inside it, nowhere large enough to hide, nowhere to go except the stairwell leading back upstairs.

“Iris?” I said.

Nobody answered.

Malcolm looked up immediately. “What?”

“She’s not here.”

Summer stood up so fast the papers slipped from her lap and scattered across the floor. Cecile turned fully toward us, flashlight jerking upward toward the corners of the chamber.

“What do you mean she’s not here?” Summer asked.

“She was standing right there.”

All four of us looked at the same empty section of stone floor.

Summer swung her flashlight slowly around the room, breathing harder now. “She was literally behind me like thirty seconds ago.”

We had all been facing different directions. Somebody should have seen her leave. The room was silent enough that even shifting your shoes against the stone echoed.

I went to the stairs, calling her name. "Iris?" The word came back off the stone walls, flattened and then doubled in a sequence that didn't make acoustic sense.

Malcolm and I went up the stairs, leaving Cecile and Summer in the annex. The staircase suddenly felt much narrower than before. 

The third-floor corridor was completely dark when Malcolm pushed the green door open. Our flashlight beams cut across warped flooring and peeling wallpaper and exposed brick. I had mine moving before I was fully through the doorway, sweeping instinctively for movement.

That was when I saw Iris.

She was standing maybe fifteen feet down the corridor on the intact stretch of floorboards near the end of the hall. Facing the wall. Her flashlight hung loosely from one hand, switched off. Her other palm rested flat against the exposed brick beside her. She was completely motionless.

Every instinct in my body told me not to get closer.

Malcolm spoke first: “Iris.”

She didn’t respond immediately.

For a few seconds nothing happened at all. Then she turned around slowly.

The flashlight beams hit her face directly. Her pupils contracted sharply against the light. She looked from Malcolm to me without recognition at first, and I swear to God there was a moment where her face looked completely unrecognizable to me.

And then it was gone and it was Iris, looking a little pale and startled.

"Sorry," she said. "Sorry– I needed air. I felt sick."

"How did you get past us?" I asked. "We were at the stairs."

"I don't know. I just– I had to get out, it was– " She looked at her hand against the wall, as if noticing it was there. She pulled it back. "I'm sorry. Can we go?"

"Are you okay?" Malcolm took another step forward.

"I'm fine. I just need to be outside." She looked at us, half-begging. "Please."

We went back downstairs to collect the papers and photographs Summer had dropped. None of us spoke much while we packed everything up.

The trip back through the building felt longer than the trip in. Every sound made me jump. The creaking floors. Rain tapping weakly somewhere inside the walls. The shifting flashlight beams catching torn wallpaper and making it look, for half-seconds at a time, like figures standing in doorways.

By the time we were through the tree line and back on the fire road, I could feel myself starting to breathe normally again.
