I’ll skip the introductions. At this point, you know who I am and, if you don’t, just find out [here](https://www.reddit.com/user/AHauntedBarista/comments/1qkz8i2/drowsy_spectre_the_mimic_saga/). I waited in my apartment through the night, only allowing myself to relax once Smiles stopped knocking. I found myself looking at maps online, searching for my location so I could confirm what I already knew was true; that there was nothing out where I’d found Barrow. No buildings, no mansion or hotel. Something supernatural was the obvious answer but that was never a good thing. I am left to wonder what Tall Ben wanted me to see; the fog, or the place beyond it.   
And I am left to wonder why either would be important to show me. Whether the giant died out there, I do not know, but Smiles appeared unharmed. Amber’s “reverse vampire” claims were not entirely honest and Carlie’s assessment was all the more likely. He was a mimic, like the one that had attacked the cafe months ago. Maybe he was the same one...  
But no, that couldn’t be. Nothing came back after entering the bathroom. But they had to be related in some way.  
These mimics weren’t a new thing. We have had issues with them for years but they were one off’s. Constant attempts at joining staff, or getting into the shop through invitation. Rules kept them at bay but they were getting desperate. I worry that they want more than to just feed. Their end goal seems to be to join the staff in some fashion and I cannot imagine what end that serves. It isn’t just finding victims; there is a plan, there is premeditation. Motives.   
I can’t help but feel that what happened in Fraeria has something to do with all of this. Even if it doesn’t, maybe there is a clue surrounding Selene’s actions. Maybe… maybe Barrow is right. No, I know he is right. Selene betrayed everyone. I didn’t want to believe it, I went chasing anything that could confirm it wasn’t true. But that doesn’t mean Barrow is trustworthy.   
Last I spoke about Fraeria, I said I had a plane to catch. That I was investigating the blue mark situation. That is an important detail, those marks that we all had. Five total that seemingly showed up without cause. But  I never got to that plane. I searched for Selene, but it was all remote. I haven’t been able to leave for years. For the longest time, I didn’t know the restriction was physical. Every time I tried to go– whether chasing a piece of evidence, or leaping at a chance for answers– something would stop me. My own nerves, some delay in flights, bad weather; I never actually left.  
Always chasing leads, but finding them snipped before I got started. Now I know that I couldn’t have left even if I wanted to. The roads go nowhere. How long that has been the case, I can’t say.  
So let’s retrace my steps to find out where it all went wrong. Three years ago, back in Fraeria.

\---------------------------------------

Em’s wandering was as effective as I had hoped. Though she just picked a direction and walked, her instincts served as a compass to take us right to the city. That is where things took a sharp turn. It was very different than how we had left it. I guess it looked like nature had reclaimed it, as if a thousand years had passed without a single person stepping inside. Vines covered in briars wanderedthrough the city, growing through windows and wrapping around light poles. Massive, thorny vines as thick as a horse, crushing rooftops and piercing entire buildings. The green, celestial eye that had hung above the city was gone.   
And the center was gone. Instead of the citadel structure at its heart, there stood a vast, plant structure. A grand briar, larger than it had any right to be, greater in size than any tree I had ever seen. Colossal, and the heart of all the vines that choked the city.  
Had I not *just* been there, I would have thought it was some relic of the ancient past. Old and forgotten. Selene was somewhere in there. Barrow too.   
Em was the first one to point out the change on our wrists. The marks that had been present when we left were worn off, faded away completely. I don’t know if that was a good thing or not. Nomic, Em, and I did the only thing we could do. We descended the hill and stepped into the city where the road coming from ended in patches of grass and dirt. Not a single soul remained. Shattered glass, ruptured sidewalks, and toppled buildings served as shelter to absolutely nothing but vines. No bodies, either. Like everyone had just vanished.  
Em started shouting for her dad, but the hairs of my neck stood on end. Something was watching. I was careful to avoid the vines scattered across the road, whether small or big, as we made our way towards the center.  
Nomic called for the others, yelling for Selene and Barrow but there was no response.  
I stayed silent. I was worried something else would answer instead. We continued straight through the city, headed directly for the heart as we had before. There the invasive briar took root, digging deep into the ground where it tore open roads and sidewalks like paper. At some point the road disappeared completely within those vines. We had to choose to turn away or enter the heart of the briar city.   
“I don’t feel him.” Em’s voice was shaky. Could she sense her father before? “He’s… gone.”  
“Your father is not in the city?”  
“No. He is… *gone* gone.”  
If he wasn’t her father, I wouldn’t have felt a shred of pity. He had tried to imprison me and turn me into a pet. He got us into this trouble too. He was a horrible creature, but she was not. “That doesn’t mean he is dead.” I tried to reassure her to little effect.  
“You must be Axel.” There was someone inside the twisting briars. I looked closely, watching the shifting shadow of countless overlapping vines. The longer I stared, the more those shadows appeared to take shape. The person that stepped forward was once one with the tangled briar, separating from them only at our arrival.  
I did the only thing I could think of. I asked it what it was.  
“Trove.” His revealed white teeth that seemed a tad too narrow, as if to be more effective at gnawing bones and mincing meat. I remember him being unusually tall, built narrow and dressed in a brown suit.  “Trove Briar.”  
Hands folded behind his back, standing upon the long arms of the sprawled vines without care; I knew that he didn’t belong in the city as it was before. The name, it was too tongue in cheek.   
“What happened?”  
“This?” He threw out his arms. “You ask me as if I had a hand in it. T’was my sister’s doing. She wants you to know that she didn’t have a choice, and that there is no use in chasing her. You can’t be following where she is going, not anymore.”  
“Selene? You are Selene’s brother?”  
“Selene.” He laughed, one too deep for humor. “An old nickname. Is that the one she used?” Trove looked me up and down, losing his smile. “I don’t know what she saw in you, human, but rest assured it will be forgotten.”  
Before I could speak, he raised a finger. “But, before I go, a deal is a deal. My dearest sister leaves you a parting gift.” He lifted an arm, splitting his skin so that his veins could leap out like writhing vines. They reached for something out of sight.  
Its revelation came with a sick crunch. The body of Rykar, the inconvenient hazard of the cafe, had been split and snapped so badly that I hardly recognized him. It looked like a truck had dropped on him.   
“Where is she?”  
“Selene?” He kept staring at Rykar’s body. “All his dreams and ambitions, realized by my sister instead.” Trove clicked his tongue. “Out of the briar box, as it were. Fret not, Mr. Axel, she will be far happier outside than in.”  
He tried to leave back into the vines, but I wasn’t the only one with questions. Em’s father was missing. She spoke up and I can’t remember exactly what she said, something about her father. Part of it was a threat, part of it was desperation. All accusatory and Trove didn’t seem like the type of creature that appreciated her tone. He halted as a silhouette and my stomach dropped. We were not equipped to fight anything, let alone a creature that gloated over the devastation of an entire city.   
Trove came back out, his hands folded behind his back. I seem to remember his shadow looming further than it should have, his anger or disgust stretching it to subdue us.   
“Your father, like the rest of your breed, is gone.”   
I couldn’t do anything. No weapons, no tricks, only the instinct that something bad was going to happen. Em sensed it too and she retreated from Trove, but she only made it a few steps before those tendrils exploded from Trove’s arms and stomach. They wrapped around her neck as he lifted her up.  
Nomic was closer than me and did the only thing any of us could do. He went to shove Trove to free Em. He didn’t think for a moment, his instant reaction was to help. And, if it weren’t for the fact that his head was pierced by a single vine from Trove’s arm, I would have fought too. But my manager was gone and we didn’t have rules for any of this. Honestly, I froze, and I won’t be able to forgive myself for it.   
Trove killed her. It wasn’t pretty. He killed Em, he killed Nomic, and he gave me the most shallow of bows. Then he was gone, back where he had come from, into the briars and thorns.   
My tongue was stuck. I could have asked for answers like Em had, I could have tried something. Instead I just watched him go. Standing there, I realized there was nothing left for me to go back to at the cafe. These were the bulk of the employees– Nomic, Em, Barrow, Selene. There were others but hardly enough to keep it going. Besides, without Selene, what were we supposed to do?  
I couldn’t look at the bodies. Not because I would be overcome with sadness, but because their eyes were accusing. I’d seen too many people die already. A hole opened up in my heart when tears didn’t come. Like Barrow, it was as if I was numb and could not feel. What kind of person had I become?   
I might have stood there for ten minutes, or it could have been an hour. If I couldn’t go back, then I could only go forward, and that would bring me into the briars. That is where Selene went, if Trove was to be believed. It sounded like she went in willingly. Selene protected us, though, and I can’t see why she would do something like this. She’d lived up to her claim, she had cured the marks on our wrists, but I am left to wonder if that served her more than us.   
So I stepped into the heart of the briars.   
Tight corridors of twisting vines and thick brush threatened to cut my arms and face with each movement. Even breathing was risky as thorns the size of my fingers seemed to reach out, desperate to cut me through my clothes as I crept through. It smelled of spring, somehow pleasant despite the circumstances. I saw no sign of Trove or Selene. Other than whistling wind, it was completely silent. Not even the songs of birds or insects found a way into the briar. It was like a spider’s web, meant to catch and hold me.   
But it couldn’t because I was careful. Barrow, however, was not so lucky. I entered a chamber– one big enough for me to walk without grazing thorns. He was there on the other side. He wasn’t dead, I don’t think he can die. If I were him though I would rather have been killed. Thick thorns dug in deep to his arms and stomach, clutching him close to the wall, holding tight. One was growing through his chest and each time he breathed his ribcage traveled along it with a sickening squelch. I couldn’t help Em or Nomic. I didn’t know if I could help him.   
And I wish I would have left him there. It would have been much easier than finding a way to cut the vines and knowing what I do now, he would have deserved it.   
Within the rubble caused by the briar’s wakening, I found a large stone. They didn’t wriggle and scream– honestly, I had expected them to– and behaved as any plant would. Fibrous, hard to break, but crushed eventually under the stone. Sweating, sore, I eventually broke enough vines away to see Barrow sag down. His weight did the rest and the thinner vines snapped so he could fall.   
I found a good place to grip, dodging the larger thorns, and pulled as hard as I could on the vine in his chest. If he was going to die it would happen vine or not and it couldn’t stay there. He didn’t groan as it came out and I was reminded that he couldn’t feel pain at all. What would amount to severe torture to anyone else was nothing to him. He opened his eyes as if he’d been taking a nap.  
“Shit.” I remember that being the first thing he said, followed by. “Where is Selene?”  
 “I don’t know.”  
Barrow was calm as ever. “She left. She left without us.”  
That echoed what Trove said. “Left where?”  
“Left, as in left this prison.”  
Despite all my time at the cafe until then, I hadn’t heard anything referred to as a prison. Both Trove and Barrow were using that language but Selene never did. “What prison?”  
“We are trapped here. She found a way out and pretended like she was going to help us. I caught on, tried to beat her there first. I didn’t.”  
I could only assume he was talking about the city. But, when I asked him if that is what he meant, he shook his head. “No, Axel. This entire place. When did you last leave the cafe? When did you last travel? Visit another state? Another *city*? You can’t because we are all in a box and she just took the only way out.”  
That was obviously ridiculous. I didn’t travel because I didn’t want to, or so I thought. “If that is true, then you were going to take the door instead? What about us?”  
“It is only good for one person.”  
“What about us?” I asked again.  
He shrugged. “I’ve been here longer than you. Almost as long as Selene. It only seems fair I go too.”   
“So this is a prison and you were going to just… leave us here?”  
“Yes. But now we are going nowhere.”  
And he seemed entirely indifferent about it. “Em and Nomic are dead.”  
“I assumed everyone was.”  
“Oh.” Of course he did, and of course he didn’t care. He couldn’t feel anything, I know that, but it seemed exceptionally cold even for him. “And you don’t care?”  
“I don’t know. I don’t even care that Selene left. She just beat me to it. Played it well. But how are you alive?”  
“Trove didn’t kill me.”  
“You weren’t caught in the vines?” That made him hesitate. “Who is Trove?”  
“He came out of the briars. He said he was Selene’s brother. He killed Em and Nomic! Killed him because we came back for you and Selene!”  
“Our marks are gone. That should have killed us.” He looked at his own wrist. “Myself included.”  
The side effect of not feeling was not knowing when to stop talking. “Opening the door would have gotten us killed?”  
“Five people, one to open the door and four to die. That is what Selene was planning to do. It was going to happen anyway, if that makes you feel better, and apparently it didn’t kill anyone.”  
Was he deaf?! “It got Em and Nomic killed!”  
“Apparently not. Something else killed them.”  
“As a result of what you and Selene did!”  
He shrugged. “Maybe.”  
“If this is a prison, is any of this real?” I point the way I came, mustering every bit of self control I had to not put Barrow back in the vines. “Are they actually dead?”  
“This world is mostly an illusion, I think. But the people? The things that creep and hurt? Those are all real, all trapped with us.”  
“And how did we get here?”  
He stared off for a long time. “I don’t remember.”  
“How did *I* get here?”  
Again, he shrugged. “Don’t know.”  
“How can you possibly know any of this, then?”  
“Because I listen and I snoop when no one is looking. Because, unlike you, I seek answers without asking questions. I don’t expect someone else to do the work for me.” He rubbed his chin. “Though I wonder if it can be done again. The door, that is.”  
“I thought you said it was gone.”  
“Doesn’t mean there isn’t another one. We just have to make the key again.” I didn’t like the way he was looking at me. “We just have to find the spell she used for the marks.”  
I took a step backwards. Careful, slowly, hopefully subtly.   
“If it means a way out, maybe seeing Selene again, will you help me?”  
“You said it was supposed to kill me. I’m not letting you sacrifice anyone.”  
“That is pretty much the point of the cafe, Axel. Rykar was racing Selene, I think, but she got there first. Found four people stupid enough to go with her wherever she wished. Four to have the mark. We can do it again and I think we can do it faster. We just need to find another door.”  
“No. I won’t help you with that.”  
“I don’t need your help. The cafe needs a new manager with Selene gone. You can either work for me and with me, or be fired. You can see how quickly the forest will eat you alive.”  
The sharper side of his indifference hit hard. If he had control over the cafe, it would be very easy for him to bend the rules to the harm of others. There was one rule about the shop that he was forgetting, however, and he showed too many cards. Despite being unable to feel, his intentions still mattered. They shifted to direct harm, to a desire to sacrifice, and that would halt him at the door.   
“Okay.” I lied. “I’ll go with you back to the cafe. We can talk about it.”  
“We can.” He nodded. “Won’t do us very much good, though. Talking has gotten us nowhere.” Then he set off as if nothing had happened at all. “Let’s get back, then. We’ll need to talk to Richard and figure out a hiring scheme.”  
But it was getting late. “How do we get back?”  
“Walking.”  
“But I thought… if we were out after sunrise, that we…”  
“I wouldn’t believe everything Selene says.” He waited for me at the first bend. “Are you coming?”  
We passed the bodies of my friends. The briar was… eating them, for lack of a better word. Their bodies were sinking into the ground, wrapping in new vines, slowly joining the colossal interloper. Barrow passed them without so much as a glance and I had to keep up. He was determined, focused, and marched straight for the cafe. Whether he would be allowed to enter or not, I didn’t know, but something told me I would have to get there first.   
And if he knew it was a race, he would most certainly win. So I followed him out of the city and to the treeline. If there was a door out, he would not be allowed to so much as touch it.   
Though distracting him with conversation might have been a good idea, I found I had nothing to say to him. He betrayed me. He got Em and Nomic killed. Him and Selene both, if his claims were to be believed. As it stood, I was certain of only one thing; Barrow himself claimed he was willing to see us all killed to unlock that door of his. A confession was far worse than accusations, and Barrow was willingly open about his complete lack of remorse.   
We were silent all the way to the treeline. I had begun to recognize certain aspects of the forest from my trip to Em’s house, and when we went and fought with Tom. The cafe was close and a terrifying realization came upon me just as we approached the treeline. The Drowsy Spectre was closed and I didn’t have a key to get in.   
“It’s locked.”  
He pulled a keyring from his pocket. “Selene wasn’t the only one with a key. I made a copy a while ago, just in case. How do you think I found out what she was up to?”  
Tampering with the store after hours seemed dangerous, but it didn’t mean anything anymore. He tossed the keys back in his pocket and we entered the sidewalk leading up to the parking lot. I was behind him, each step shaving away precious time while I tried to figure out what to do. I could lock him out, but only if I got inside first. There was no way that was happening if I needed *him* to unlock the door!   
Barrow can’t feel. I couldn’t trick him or manipulate him, or appeal to any sort of reason that would matter. Then it clicked; Barrow can’t feel. The key was in his pocket and I wondered if he truly couldn’t feel anything at all. The wind, the breeze around us, or my fingers digging into his pocket. I wasn’t a good thief or pickpocket by any measure– never even tried it before– but if I used Barrow’s weakness against him…  
I closed the gap subtly as we got closer, silent as I could, casually as someone as nervous as I. My heart raced knowing that he was far more capable of harm than I was. Would he even flinch, choking the life from me? Would he even care at all? I saved his life, I think, but it was time to save mine.  
I leaned forward, keeping pace with him, and stuck out my fingers. He was about my height, nearly six feet, and a slightly quicker gait saw my hand slip into his right pocket. He didn’t so much as flinch despite all my shaking. My twitching fingers would have alarmed anyone else immediately. But the key to the store came out cleanly and he didn’t turn when I straightened. We were passing across the lot. Closer with each step.   
Getting the keys didn’t solve the problem. I had to be faster than him too. When he got to the door, I hurried to the left. He watched me go, his cold eyes were *almost* curious, but he didn’t give chase. Barrow had no reason to with the key that was in his pocket. He’d run to catch me once he realized it was gone.  
I sprinted as soon as I was out of sight, racing for the back of the store and praying both locks were the same. The key slipped in just when I heard the sound of shoes slamming into the pavement, rapidly approaching the corner I had just turned. I threw open the door and slammed it shut on Barrow’s arm.   
Anyone else would have howled in pain.   
“Axel.” He tried and, for the first time, he sounded like one of them. Like something from the woods. “Axel, let me in.”  
A chill went up my spine. I used my entire bodyweight on the door, holding it tight as I reached with my other hand for the chain above my head. I slipped the chain lock in place, all while Barrow argued calmly, and only then could I step away. He couldn’t fit his shoulder inside.   
Finally he stopped, pulling his arm away. “You won’t find a way to get to her, Axel. Not without my help.” His voice remained behind the door. “You need me.”  
I didn’t. I needed Selene. I needed Em and Nomic. So I closed the door and hid in the shop alone.

Turns out we don’t need four employees to run the store. I did it with two, then with three. Months went by and we found a fourth. I don’t know if something changed, or if Selene only made that rule so she’d have her chance to mark us. Richard was no help at all and barely seemed to react at all when I told him what happened.   
But he gave me one solid piece of advice– add a new rule to the list; don’t speak to Barrow. So far, that has kept us from allowing him to get what he wants. Either four marks for another door, or something equally as bad. I didn’t believe his claims about Selene for the longest time, or his claims that we lived in some kind of prison. I’ve seen the end of the road, though. I’ve gone to the edge of the forest and it is getting smaller. If we are all trapped and there is a door out, I’d like to find it. I want to take the way Selene did and find her. But I won’t sacrifice anyone and I won’t leave four behind to the mist that eats.   
And, out there, there are mimics from the briar. Whether they belong to Trove or spawned from that city, I don’t know, but I think they are looking for the same thing. A door to get out.  
I manage the Drowsy Spectre and we serve the odd and supernatural but, sometimes, I have to protect them too. Neither Barrow or any mimic will use the cafe to escape and if there is another way to get out, I’ll find it.
