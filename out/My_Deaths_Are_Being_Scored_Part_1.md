The moment I saw that little locket, I knew I had to buy it.
It was sitting in the window of a dusty antique shop, catching the light just right, and I instantly thought, Brooke would love this.

It was small, silver, delicate, exactly the kind of necklace she’d wear. The front was clean and polished, and it had this tiny button that was supposed to pop it open.

Supposed to.

In reality, it didn’t move at all. No matter how much I pried at it, twisted it, pushed the button, nothing.
Even the shop owner tried, then shrugged and said, “Probably seized up.”

Honestly, I didn’t care. It looked beautiful, Brooke’s birthday was coming up, and I figured I’d just have a jeweler fix it. Seemed like one of the best impulse buys I’d ever made.

At least until that night.


I’d had the locket for a few days. Whenever Brooke came over, I hid it, our relationship was still new, we didn’t live together yet. But that weekend she went back to her parents’ place out of town, so I finally had time to mess with it. I watched videos, read forums, cleaned it, oiled the hinge, nothing. 
The damn thing stayed firmly shut.

I was lying on the couch, scrolling through local jewelers on my phone, when I heard it:
A tiny click.

I froze.
The locket was sitting on the coffee table.
It had opened.
Just… opened. Quiet as a whisper.

I grabbed it immediately, completely stunned. I’d fought with this thing for hours. And now it just decided to open by itself?

I lifted it and looked inside.

It was empty.

Or almost empty.
Someone had scratched something into the silver interior.


“Die with style.”


I actually read it out loud. My own voice sounded strange in the quiet room.
I just stared at it for a while, trying to wrap my head around what kind of sick joke this was. Who the hell engraves that into a locket?

One thing was certain: I needed to get that message removed before Brooke ever saw it. She’d freak out.

I set the locket back on the table and decided I’d deal with it tomorrow. I was exhausted.


The next morning it was exactly where I’d left it.
Same message carved inside. Same perfect, mocking little scratch marks.

I sighed. Great. I’d definitely bought trouble.

Whatever, I'd figure something out. First I needed coffee, so I headed out.

The morning was calm, barely anyone on the street.
I reached the edge of the crosswalk, checked both ways, and stepped forward… and that’s when I heard the horn.

Just one instant. A flash of movement. The bus. The driver’s terrified face.

And then the impact.


I blinked down at the little locket.
It was sitting in my hand exactly the way it had that morning… except now there was an ugly scratch carved into its shiny silver surface.

04:00:00
That was all it said. A timer.

I couldn’t wrap my head around it. What the hell happened to me? A minute ago I’d been hit by a bus. I felt my body break under the weight of it.
And now… I was back. Sitting in my apartment. Alive.

The locket clicked again. The same soft click as the night before.

Only this time, it wasn’t empty.

A tiny, yellowed scrap of paper was inside. At first I thought it was just debris, but then I saw the words scratched onto it:


“The first was a gift. The next one you earn.”


I just stared at it. What did that mean? What was this thing?

I tried to continue my day like normal, well, as normal as I could. I went out to get coffee, but I walked like a paranoid lunatic, jumping at every sound. People definitely stared.
But nothing happened.
I made it home. The locket sat on my coffee table like nothing was wrong.
The timer scratch was still there.
The paper was still in my hand. I must have read it fifty times, and it never made any more sense.

Around noon, I got hungry, but I couldn’t bring myself to go outside. Something felt wrong. Like something was getting closer.

And then…
I heard it.

A deep, warped voice, coming from everywhere and nowhere, from inside my skull and under my skin:


“You didn’t keep the time. Your soul is ours.”


“What the hell…?” I whispered.


Then it hit.

There’s no way to describe this pain.

It wasn’t physical. It wasn’t even emotional. It was like fire erupting inside me, like something was ripping through my core, tearing at whatever makes you a person. I screamed, thrashed, knocked over furniture. My limbs jerked on their own, my vision blurred, and my body threw itself toward the open window.

The next thing I knew, I was hitting the asphalt below.
Someone screamed.
Someone else shouted for help.

And the worst part?

Dying, feeling my bones shatter, my lungs collapse, my blood pooling out, hurt less than whatever that burning, soul-deep agony had been.


I was back on my couch again.
The little locket rested in my palm, shining just as clean and silver as the first time I saw it.
And I honestly thought I was losing my mind.

I had died. Again. And I’d started over. Again.
What the hell was happening to me?

The locket looked a little different this time. A new time was scratched across its surface:

15:00:00

Same style as before. Same cold, perfect carving.

Then it clicked open.
Another soft, mechanical little sound. The same yellowed slip of paper was inside. I grabbed it immediately.


“You barely made it! Don’t exceed the time, or your soul is ours!”


My stomach twisted. Pure panic washed over me.


“Fuck… FUCK!” I shouted, my whole body shaking.


I fumbled for my phone and called Brooke. I didn’t know what else to do. I just needed someone, anyone, to tell me I wasn’t insane.


“Hey, Ant! Why are you calling so early? What’s wrong?” Brooke answered, cheerful, completely unaware of the nightmare I was in.


“Brooke… Brooke, something’s really wrong,” I blurted out, struggling to breathe. “I died. I actually died, and then I woke up here again. They want my soul...something wants my fucking soul! Brooke, I don’t…”


Silence.


“Anthony… what are you talking about?” she asked. Her voice had changed. Quieter. Careful. Like she was afraid of what I might say next.


“Brooke, I’m telling you! Listen to me! The locket… it opened… and there was this message… and now it happened again… Brooke, I don’t know…”


More silence.
She didn’t believe me. I could practically feel her pulling away, trying to figure out if I was having some kind of breakdown or playing a sick joke.


“Anthony… I’m going to hang up now,” she finally said, slowly, like she was talking to a cornered animal. “Just… calm down, okay? Whatever’s going on, just breathe. We’re at my mom’s for lunch. I’ll talk to you later this afternoon, alright?”


“Brooke, don’t… Don’t hang up! Please! I died, a bus hit me, and I’m still here! Brooke, just listen to me!”


But she ended the call.

My arm dropped to my side. I stood there in the middle of my living room, shaking so hard I thought my knees might buckle.

And I couldn’t even be angry at her. Because everything I’d just told her… sounded completely, absolutely insane.


I made my way back toward the antique shop.
I kept the locket clenched in my fist the entire time. The place was annoyingly far, took me almost an hour just to get there.

I turned the corner onto the right street…
Closed.

I felt my stomach twist. I rushed up to the door and almost punched it when I saw the little sign:

Closed on weekends and Mondays.


“You’ve gotta be fucking kidding me…” I muttered.


“It’s closed,” an old man said behind me. He was smoking outside the used bookstore next door.


“Yeah… I can see that,” I mumbled. “Sir, do you happen to know where the owner lives?”


He shook his head instantly.


“No idea,” he said, dragging on his cigarette.


I hesitated, then held up the locket.


“And this? Ever seen one like it?”


He squinted, leaned forward, studied it for a second.


“Not really, kid,” he said, scratching his cheek. “But whoever carved that crap on it? Ruined it. Looks like a shitty stopwatch now.”


He flicked his cigarette onto the street and disappeared back inside.

I stood there on the sidewalk, frozen in place, staring down at the locket.

A stopwatch? A timer?
Was that… possible?

I was heading home while browsing the internet on my phone, looking up information about the medallion. 
The shop had no real info online, and the locket didn’t match anything I could find. One site guessed the design might be 17th-century, but that didn’t help me at all.

Then my phone rang.
Brooke.


“Brooke! Thank God!” I answered immediately. “Sorry about earlier, it’s just… everything’s been so weird.”


“Anthony… are you feeling any better?” she asked, sounding nervous.


“Not really,” I admitted. My voice shook. “Something’s really wrong. I bought a locket. For your birthday.”


“Oh… that’s really sweet,” she said.


“But wait… this locket is evil. It wants me to…” A shout cut me off. I hadn’t even realized I’d wandered straight into the middle of a move.


“Watch out! Jesus Christ!” someone screamed.


I looked up.

And that’s when I saw it.

A refrigerator, an entire fucking refrigerator, falling out of an upstairs window, straight toward me.

I can’t describe what it feels like when a fridge hits you in the face.
The impact is too huge, too surreal.

But right before everything went black, one thought flashed through my mind:

Who the hell moves a fridge through a window?


I touched my face as I came to on my couch again.
The locket was glowing in my hand like nothing had happened.

Meanwhile I was trying to mentally process the fact that a fucking refrigerator had exploded my skull and turned my brain into paste.

That’s when I noticed the locket again.

01:00:00


“One hour…?” I muttered.

Was it really a timer? A stopwatch? But what was it counting down from?

The locket clicked open again.
The yellowed slip of paper was inside, waiting for me. I pulled it out with shaking fingers.


“You can’t tell her! This one is punishment! You have one hour!”


My stomach clenched. I stared at the carved numbers. One hour.
I was in deep, deep shit. I didn’t even know what kind of shit, it was too far beyond anything I understood.

I sat there for a few minutes, just listening to the silence.
Then I realized something:

If I wanted answers, I had to test the rules of this thing.

I swept everything off my coffee table.
Set the locket down. Set my phone beside it with the stopwatch running.

And I waited.

Every second felt like a knife.
I didn’t move. Didn’t breathe. Just watched.

50 minutes.
51.
53.

At 55 minutes, the feeling hit me… that horrible, familiar sense of something approaching.
Something massive.
Like when animals sense a storm coming, except this was a thousand times worse.

And when the timer hit 56 minutes, the voice returned.


“You did not keep the time. Your soul is ours!”


I tried to brace myself, but nothing can prepare you for that.

It felt like my insides were detonating. Like every imaginable agony was chewing through my soul at once. My body convulsed on its own, twisting, jerking, spasming.
I screamed. I shrieked. I couldn’t stop.

And somewhere deep inside, I knew:

This pain wouldn’t end. Not ever. Unless I made it end.

Luckily, I’d prepared for that…

The kitchen knife was right there on the couch.

Through the unbearable torture, I pushed myself to my knees and grabbed it.
There was no other option. This wasn’t living. This was execution.

With one clean motion, I stabbed myself in the throat.
Then again. And again. And again.

Eventually I collapsed onto the coffee table. Through fading vision, I saw the phone screen:

1 hour 2 minutes.

Two minutes.

Two minutes was all it took for me to choose death over whatever the hell that soul-tearing agony was.


I was back on the couch again.
The locket sat in my palm, waiting for me.

But this time… waking up felt different.
Before I even opened my eyes, I had the briefest sensation that I’d been somewhere else. Somewhere dark. And I heard something.

One sound. Soft. Barely there.
Like someone clapping.


A chill ran through me.
I looked down at the lid of the locket.

24:00:00

Twenty-four hours. A full day.
Just because I stabbed myself in the neck like a fucking pig.

The locket clicked open. I grabbed the familiar yellow scrap and read the new message.


“Now that’s progress! Just a shame you had to be warned…”


A cold wave washed over me.

As horrifying as it was to admit it… whatever this was, it wanted me dead. Over and over.
Someone, something, was enjoying my death throes.

I didn’t have a second to waste.
I had to find a way out of this.

I strapped on my running watch and started the stopwatch. I wasn’t taking any chances. I needed to know exactly how much time I had left.

Then I started researching.
Hard.

Forums, articles, obscure websites, digital archives, library books, old myths, cursed objects… anything.
Nothing. Not a single mention of anything like this.
No stories. No folklore. No warnings. And the locket? Total mystery.

By the time I looked up, I had six hours left.

It was getting dark. I sat on the couch smoking my tenth cigarette of the day.
Didn’t really matter. I’d have to kill myself again by the end of the night anyway.

That’s when I had the thought.

The most insane idea I’d had since all of this started.

If they wanted entertainment… I’d give them entertainment.

I had to get back into that antique shop. I had to force the owner to tell me what the hell this thing was.
But the place was locked until Monday, and I had no idea where the guy lived.

So I had two options:


1. Kill myself in a special enough way to earn enough time to survive until Monday.

2. Or find out where the shop owner lived.


I had less than four hours left.
I figured the smartest move was to earn more time.

I filled the bathtub.
Got in.
The toaster, plugged in, sat on the edge of the tub.

I took one deep breath.

And pushed it in.

There was one blinding flash.

Then everything went dark.

Back on the couch.
Back with the locket in my hand.

5 hours.

I slammed my hand on the table.

“What the fuck?!” I screamed into the empty apartment. “That wasn’t enough?! The electricity blew out my eyes! My brain fucking cooked!”


I kicked a chair across the room.
The locket clicked open again. The yellow slip of paper waited.

I tore it out.


“You can do better, Anthony. Too many people choose this option.”


I slammed the note back onto the table.

I bit my lip, thinking.
The pattern slowly made sense.

It wasn’t creative enough. Too common. The “audience” was bored. I needed something bigger. Something better.

I sat down and made a list, going over how much time each death had earned me.
Slitting my throat was still the highest.

I walked to the window and looked down at the street. What would be dramatic enough to buy me days?

My eyes drifted to the restaurant across the street.

And then… the idea hit me.


I stormed into the restaurant.
The servers froze mid-step.
I marched straight to the kitchen like I owned the place.

The cooks stared at me, bewildered.


“Sir, you can’t be in here!” one of them said.


“Back the fuck up!” I barked, yanking out the knife.


“Whoa—whoa—easy!” He raised his hands.


The others backed away, terrified. They didn’t know I wasn’t here to hurt them.


“Oil. Where’s the oil?” I demanded, sounding like a complete maniac.


“Uh…there… down there,” one of the kitchen kids stammered, pointing at a cabinet.


Huge metal cans lined the bottom shelf.
I grabbed one, popped it open with the knife, and dumped it all over myself, my head, my chest, my arms.
The oil soaked everything, dripping down my clothes and skin.

The cooks just stared, shocked, not even understanding what they were watching.

Then one of them kicked the knife out of my hand.
The others lunged at me.

Chaos broke out.

They tried to hold me down, but the oil made me slip out of their grip every time.
Three of them were on me, but I twisted, writhed, thrashed, it was impossible to restrain me.

Until I slammed backward into one of the hot ovens.

The oil basically exploded.

I went up in flames instantly.

The fire tore across my skin, eating through every layer.
I choked on the heat, barely able to scream.
The cooks were shouting, panicking, one ran for water, one grabbed a phone, one was crying.

And I burned. I burned.
I felt myself burning alive.


The sound of applause hit me the instant before the darkness faded.
Just a sharp, sudden burst of clapping, then I was back on my couch.

The locket sat in my palm again, staring back at me.
79 hours.

For a second, I almost felt… happy.
Almost.

But the memory of burning alive, choking, blistering, melting, still clung to me like smoke.
Even so, that was still better than letting the timer run out.

The locket clicked open.
I pulled out the yellow slip.


“Well done, Anthony. You’re almost at the peak!”


I exhaled, disappointed.
Part of me hoped that killing myself in such a brutal, chaotic way, so publicly, might’ve finally earned me freedom.

But no.
At least I was closer.

And I realized something else:

How many people watched me die mattered. The more witnesses, the more time I earned.

And now I had 79 hours.
I needed to get my shit together.
I needed a plan.

And most importantly… I needed to get back to the antique shop.


It was the most stressful weekend of my life.
I kept the stopwatch on my wrist running the entire time. I talked to Brooke as much as I could, but I didn’t dare tell her anything after what happened last time. I couldn’t risk her thinking I’d lost my mind again.


And now I was standing in front of the antique shop.
I saw the owner, the same mustached, middle-aged guy who sold me the locket, cleaning behind the counter.

I didn’t wait a second. I shoved the door open.


“Oh…” he jumped. “Good morning, sir. I didn’t expect anyone this ear…”


“Shut the fuck up,” I snapped. “What the hell did you sell me? What the fuck is this locket?! Tell me!”


The man stumbled back behind the counter, terrified.
He clearly had no idea what I was talking about, even though I was practically shoving the silver chain in his face.


“S-sir, please, calm down,” he stammered. “I have no idea what you’re talking about.”


“THIS!” I nearly slammed it into his nose. “Where did it come from? I bought it here, and my life has been absolute fucking chaos ever since!”


He blinked at me, wide-eyed and trembling.
He probably thought I was about to beat him or tear the shop apart.


“I… I don’t know like this,” he said weakly.


“Then FIND OUT,” I roared. “Now, for fuck’s sake!”


He hesitated.
Of course he did, he wasn’t supposed to give out customer info.
But I looked insane enough to do… anything.
And the clock was ticking.

I had 57 minutes left.


“I’ll check! I-I’ll look,” he said quickly. “I just need to go get the book from the back.”


I knew he might call the police or just kick me out. That’s why I brought the bag of tools.


“You asked for this,” I muttered.


I pulled out the hammer sticking out of my backpack, and smashed it into the nearest shelf so hard the whole thing buckled.
Vases, old watches, silver trinkets, and God knows what else crashed to the floor.

I hit another cabinet.


“No! Please!” the man screamed.


“TELL ME WHO BROUGHT IN THE LOCKET!” I shouted, sweeping an entire table of junk onto the ground.


He didn’t wait any longer.
He dove behind the counter, grabbed a thick ledger, and flipped through it with shaking hands.


“Here!” he pointed at a line. “A woman named Cathlyn brought it in. Here’s her number!”


I snatched my phone and took a picture of it instantly.


“Sorry,” I said, out of breath. “But I had to.”


Then I bolted out the door as fast as if I were trying to catch a bus.
I had 49 minutes left to get to the next part of my plan.

I called the number as I ran, panting.
Nothing.
No answer.
I sent a message. Then another. Then another.

No reply.

And just like that, I had 10 minutes left.

I didn’t have another second to waste.

I had reached the tall office building.

Work.
My boss had been calling nonstop since yesterday, wondering why I hadn’t shown up.


I was already in the elevator heading up.
I worked on the eighth floor. The guys at the front desk looked surprised to see me walk in, but I didn’t care enough to acknowledge them.


“Anthony? I thought you didn’t work here anymore!” Paul, one of my heavier coworkers, joked as I passed.


I ignored him. I had a much bigger plan today.

I sped down the hallway, people whispering and staring from behind their desks.
I definitely didn’t look work-ready, just a ragged hoodie and beat-up jeans. I looked more like a homeless guy who wandered in than a data analyst.


“Anthony, what the hell are you doing? And where have you been?” my boss shouted the moment I stepped into his office.


I walked up to him and punched him so hard he toppled over with his chair.
I heard the entire office gasp, and half of them cheer. Everyone hated Richard. He was a miserable, arrogant prick.


“Fuck you, Richard,” I said calmly. “I’ve been waiting eight years for that promotion, you asshole. But whatever. I’m done.”


I pulled out the hammer.
Some of the onlookers screamed.
Richard started begging.

But I wasn’t here to do what they thought.

Instead, I reached over and slammed the fire alarm.

The sirens erupted instantly.

People standing near the door bolted for the exit. Exactly what I needed, more people out in the street.


“Please, Anthony, don’t hurt me!” Richard whimpered on the floor, blood dripping from his nose. “I’ll give you anything!”


“Shut the fuck up,” I growled.


I walked to the huge side-opening window and pushed it wide open.
This office was perfect for what I had planned.


“Alright, you fat bastard,” I said, turning back to him. “What’s the code to your safe? I know you’ve got one in here. I’ve seen it. So don’t lie.”

“W-what? I… I don’t have a safe,” he stammered. “I don’t know what you’re talking about…”


Richard was a cockroach.
I’d watched him steal from the company for years. I knew damn well he had a safe hidden in the room.

And honestly? I was glad he denied it.
Gave me an excuse to let out some anger.

I raised the hammer and brought it down on his knee.
He screamed.


“FUUUCK!”


“Where’s the safe?” I asked calmly. “Or should I break the other leg?”


“Aaah… there! There, wait!” he howled, pointing toward a cabinet. “The key’s right here…oh God…”


I snatched the key from his shaking hand and rushed to the cabinet.
One glance at my stopwatch, three minutes left.

The safe was stuffed with cash.
Eight years’ worth of embezzlement, probably. A small fortune.

I threw open the safe, grabbed a huge stack of bills, and tossed it out the window.
Then another.
And another.

The wind whipped the money into a chaotic storm above the city.


“What the fuck are you doing?!” Richard screamed, clutching his knee. “I thought you wanted to keep it!”


“I don’t need it,” I shrugged. “And nobody else is getting it either.”


I stepped back toward him.


“Now hold this.”


I shoved a thick rope into his hands.
Then pulled the other end from my bag, a noose.

I’d bought it over the weekend, when I came up with the plan.

I tied Richard’s hands together with one end of the rope.


“Hold on as tight as you can,” I told him, pulling the knots hard.


“What the fuck are you doing?!” he screamed.


I lifted the other end. The noose dangled from it.


“No… don’t. Don’t you fucking… NO! NOOO!” he shrieked. “PLEASE DON’T!”


I smiled at him.

I had thirty seconds left.

I slipped the noose over my neck.

Took a deep breath.

And jumped out the window.


I won’t pretend it felt good to hang outside the eighth-floor window, choking to death.
But in some twisted way… there was a spark of satisfaction in it.
At least I’d gotten one last shot in at that asshole Richard.

I could hear him screaming for help, groaning as he tried to keep his grip on the rope, my rope, while I dangled in the air, suffocating.

I didn’t last long.
In my final seconds, I saw the entire building’s worth of employees standing on the street below, pointing up at my flailing body.
A hundred-dollar bill drifted past my face, caught by the wind.

Then everything went black.

And again…
applause.

Louder this time. Clearer.
Someone even whistled, like the crowd at the end of a great show.

It was intoxicating.

Then my eyes opened, and I was standing outside the building.

Right there in front of my workplace.
I glanced at my watch, and realized only ten minutes had passed since I’d walked inside to kill myself.

What the hell?

I felt the cold metal disk pressing into my palm.

144 hours were carved across the surface.

The performance had been worth it…
I just respawned from a different point.
Apparently everything that happened over the weekend still “counted,” and as far as the world was concerned, I hadn’t shown up to work yesterday at all.

The locket clicked open.


“Well done, Anthony. That was beautiful. Now the real game begins.”


I stared at the paper, furious.
They wanted more. More deaths. More pain. More chaos.
They were going to tear my life apart piece by piece, and I couldn’t stop them, because if I resisted, I’d get reset.

Then my phone buzzed in my pocket.

A message.
For a second I didn’t even process who it could be from. 
It was an address. And a single sentence:


“Meet me there.”


At the bottom was a name:

Cathlyn.

I think…
I finally knew what I had to do next.
