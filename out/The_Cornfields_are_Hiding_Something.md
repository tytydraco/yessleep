Cornfields are ridiculously terrifying, and I don’t care what else you have to say about it. Picture this: So-Cal kid graduates UC Berkeley with master’s degree in fine arts (I’m an oil painter) and doesn’t know what to do with his life, can’t afford to live in LA, so goes off to live with his aunt and uncle for a while. Cute story, right? Well, that aunt and uncle happen to live on a farm in southern Minnesota, also known as hell on earth.

I thought Minnesota had big cities and deep forests, but this part of the state was flatter than a McDonald’s cheeseburger. I knew I’d be living in a farmhouse, but I thought we’d be closer to civilization than we really were. The only bright side was that I arrived there in June, so at least there wasn’t any snow.

The corn was up to my shins when I walked past the fields for the first time. Aunt May (real name) came down the farmhouse steps to meet me and gave me a big hug before I could get my bags out of my rotting Toyota. Uncle Bill (also real name) slowly descended the steps with a smile on his face and a limp in his right leg. He hobbled over to join us while May gushed about how good it was to see me again and inquired exhaustingly about my travels. Uncle Bill offered a massive paw and shook my hand like a dog flogging its limp chewtoy, a genuine smile on his face all the while.

“Good to see you, bud.” Was all he offered before May’s doting drowned him out.

“We’ve got the guest room all set up for you, come on inside! You haven’t been here since you were real little—do you remember last time you were here?” She asked, grabbing my bags and heading off toward the house without me. I was barely a year old last time I was here, so no, I did not remember. While May trotted off to the house, still asking questions over her shoulder, uncle Bill patted me on my own shoulder and we followed her at his pace.

“It’s good to have you with us,” Bill said. He was resting his hand on my shoulder and when he took a step would use me as a makeshift crutch to help him along, but he did so gently rather than using his full weight, which I greatly appreciated. “It’s going to be a busy year; not much rain so far, so I’ll need to make sure the fields are irrigated. You don’t have to help, you’re not here to work, but if you don’t see much of me, that’s why. But if you need anything—seriously need anything—I’ll always make time, alright?”

“Thanks, uncle Bill.” I answered. I detected no hint of Bill or May putting up fronts or facades; they seemed genuinely happy to see me even though I’d be of absolutely no use to them in their farming endeavors. That evening, as we settled down to supper, chatty May and quiet Bill exhibited a surprising intellectual acumen as we talked about the last few years of my life since I had last seen them. My mom (Bill’s sister) had been the artsy child of the family; she moved to California with a boyfriend (my father) who subsequently dumped her to escape the backwardness of flyover country. While I had spoken to my extended family on the occasional holiday, I had always imagined them to be simple neanderthals eking out a wretched existence beyond the bounds of civilized society. Uncle Bill, however, guarded a sharp mind behind that round, bearded face and small, bright eyes, and asked probing, meaningful questions in the course of our conversation. Aunt May, perhaps not so forwardly intellectual, listened with enthusiasm to everything I had to say about my art and the dreams I hoped would carry me far away. The anxiety of meeting my aunt and uncle again for the first time relaxed, and I felt like I was home.

I had to piss that night, but entering the bathroom revealed a toilet on its side with an array of plumbing utensils scattered around the floor. Uncle Bill informed me that the guest toilet had broken the week before and he wasn’t able to fix it in time, but I was welcome to use the master bathroom downstairs. However, as it was two in the morning, I wasn’t about to disturb a farmer and his wife to use their toilet. Instead, I crept down the staircase and slipped out the backdoor.

Despite summer being in full swing, the June night was cool thanks to the breeze that blew from the West. I approached the cornfield, my only light the orange sodium lamp that illuminated the driveway from a telephone pole, and prepared to water the crops when I was seized by the sudden, cold grip of an ancient fear. I could see, silhouetted against the half moon, the whole landscape spread out in every direction like a scroll unrolled across the earth. The horizon surrounded me, darkness beneath distant stars: anything could be out here with me. The hairs on my neck prickled as a primeval fear from my pre-human ancestors informed me that I was not alone, and that fear spoke true: a pair of eyes in the shin-high corn, then another pair, and another.

I froze, my heart pounding as a cocktail of cortisol and adrenaline gave form to the creature in the crop: I had no idea what lived out here, were there wolves in Minnesota? I think so! Bears? No, the eyes are too low to the ground. Mountain lions? Are there mountains in Minnesota? A trembling hand pulled my phone from my pocket and shook it, igniting the flashlight to reveal the monster of my dreams: a stout raccoon and her posse of babies blinking sleepily at the sharp, white light. The critter made no noise but seemed annoyed by the artificial sun blinding her children, so I held it off to the side. The raccoons trundled sleepily onward, completely unflinching at the human presence which the farmstead had accustomed them to over the course of generations, and continued on their nocturnal business.

The next morning, looking out over the endless landscape stretching to the horizon, I was at once awed by the sheer scale of it all and also embarrassed at how fearful I had been ten hours before. The corn concealed no secrets, the farmhouse stood as the only object of note in a sprawling sea of emptiness, so no wonder my mind—so accustomed to bustling cities filled with people and buildings and meaning—had found the need to fill the void with something else besides, with monsters from the periphery of the map. But it was, after all, only us.

Uncle Bill worked the fields that summer while I helped aunt May around the house for half the day and painted in the yard for the other half. I painted the great expanse of nothing that we were marooned in, I painted my uncle’s shiny new tractors and his shitty old tractors and machines that I could not recognize. I painted the grizzled, tabby barn cat, Kaiser, who refused to let me close but watched his new visitor with keen and guarded intent from the cover of the many barns and machine sheds that dotted the farmstead grounds. Kaiser was not a trusting creature, though he let May pet him and I once saw Bill pick him up dotingly (I swear I heard the critter purr when he did so). As a rule, however, Kaiser never left the farmstead. He preferred to lurk in the sheds or under the house, and he never, ever trespassed close to the cornfields which, grew taller and taller every passing week in spite of the oppression of the sun. One day, when the sun was beginning its descent, I stood at the boundary of the knee-high corn (“knee-high by the fourth of July” was an expectation my uncle’s crops had failed to meet), and I heard the unusual sound of the habitually silent Kaiser squawking at me. I searched in vain for the critter until I saw him poking his head out the door of a nearby barn. He ceased his caterwauling when I noticed him. His eyes seemed to look past me into the fields, scanning left to right. I had seen him pose like this before when he hunted, but his ears were pinned back to his head and his back was arched; Kaiser was not the hunter this evening. “It’s alright buddy,” I offered, leaving the field to approach the petite groundskeeper, but the moment I stepped toward him he retreated into the barn and disappeared. I can’t remember seeing Kaiser again after that night.

The summer likewise disappeared, sweeping by faster than I had anticipated, and my plans to relax and evaluate my future slipped away with it. I was no closer to solving the future than I had been when I first arrived, and I cursed myself for my ineptitude. When August arrived, my indecision was keeping me up at night, and all the short-form content in the world couldn’t still the little voice in my head that kept prodding me to get my act together.

On one such August night, I sighed exasperatedly and tossed my phone to the side when social media lent me no solace from my self. With another groan, I heaved myself out of bed and slunk down the farmhouse stairs to piss in the field again. The August nights were far hotter than June had been; the cool Western breeze blew instead warm air against my face and the smell of dusty earth along with it. The corn, burnished bronze against the warm sodium light, was taller than me now, the sea had risen to choke out sight of the horizon like the floodwaters of Noah rising against the ark. Stepping beyond the threshold of the farmhouse, that ancient fear fermented again within my bowels, gripping my heart and squeezing it faster, faster, faster as icewater flushed my veins. I hesitated before taking another step down the farmhouse stairs but reminded myself of just how alone we were out here. I approached the wall of green which threatened to choke out the stars and prepared to lend them water to ward off the heat of summer when my heart suddenly palpitated: eyes between the stalks, shin-high, looking at me.

After the first moment of ancient panic, I laughed with relief, remembering the night in June and many others since that I had seen the little raccoons lazing through the fields about their business. In the two months since that day, I had not seen another critter except for Kaiser and the occasional rodent that the cat’s aging frame had spared from extinction, an increasingly frequent phenomenon as Kaiser’s absence grew louder in the closed ecosystem of the farmstead.

I squatted down to get closer to the raccoon’s height and made that sound people use to attract animals that sounds like kissing and spitting at the same time, offering an empty hand to the eyes. The eyes looked up at me, but their host did not move to approach. It struck me as odd then that only one set of eyes stared back from the void; had the mama’s babies grown up and moved along to their own fields somewhere? Or maybe this was not a raccoon at all but our long-lost groundskeeper? I reached for my phone but remembered that it still lay cozy in bed where I should be right now.

“Kaiser!” I whisper-shouted to the critter, making the sound again. “Come here buddy!” Kaiser was not my buddy, but he was familiar. The eyes refused to move or blink. A burst of warm wind swept over the corn and painted the air with the smell of dust and heat and…

Death?

The stench hit me like a slap in the face. I sputtered in surprise at the reek of rotting flesh, like the exhale of a dog that just finished eating a slab of meat or an unfortunate squirrel which trespassed in its yard: warm and wet and hot and dead.

The eyes did move; they moved up. They moved slowly upward through the corn, up until they were level with my squatting frame, then to standing height, then to the top of the corn, then higher still until it looked down on me from over the eight foot stalks of green. Where were the stars?

Primordial fear animated the rational animal that had squatted in the grass with a visitor it did not know; it propelled me back through the yard, back into the farmhouse, back up the stairs to my second-floor bedroom, back under the covers, back into an ancient past when monsters still lived beyond the walls of civilization and children knew well not to venture into the wild at night.
