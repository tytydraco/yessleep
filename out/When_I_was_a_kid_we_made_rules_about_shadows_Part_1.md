When I was a kid, dusk lasted forever. Especially the summer Billy disappeared from under the streetlight.

Not really forever, but it felt like it. It was still only twenty minutes, like always, but when you're thirteen and it's summer vacation, those twenty minutes are the best. It was like the world was letting you stay awake a little longer than you were supposed to.

At that time of day there was still enough light to play outside without getting yelled at. The sidewalks held onto the day's heat, warming the bottoms of your feet through sneakers worn thin from hours of running, skipping, playing. 

The air wasn't cold yet. There were no crickets. No wind. Just that stillness before night finally settles in. As an adult I liken it to getting into a car that has been sitting in the sun for a few hours. It warms you to the bone without getting the repressive sun beating you down.

Dusk was great, and was the best time to play hide-and-seek. 

In daylight, hiding was impossible. Someone always spotted your shirt behind a tree or your shoes sticking out from under a car. But at dusk, the shadows hid you. They stretched long across the street and crawled over lawns and driveways. You could disappear in the shadows without really being lost. Or so I thought.

It was a few weeks before school started again. That's probably why we stayed out so late that summer. Nobody wanted it to end.

Billy and I had been best friends since second grade. Our moms trusted us enough to let us roam between the houses on our block as long as we stayed where the porch lights were visible. During the summer, that basically meant we were free.

There were other kids out that night too. Bikes abandoned in lawns. Someone's baseball glove in the gutter. Normal summer stuff.

I remember Melissa talking about the missing kid first.

"You know they still haven't found him," she whispered.

That got everyone's attention immediately. Kids love scary things when the sun is almost down.

"My brother said they found his bike in the woods," another kid added.

"My cousin said they found blood on it."

Billy rolled his eyes and looked at me. "They're making stuff up again."

One of the older boys lowered his voice.

"My uncle says something takes them."

The little kids immediately crowded closer.

"What thing?"

He shrugged. "Nobody knows. People hear stuff at night though."

"What stuff?"

He waited just long enough to make everyone uncomfortable.

"Scratching."

A few of the younger kids got visibly nervous after that. One girl said she had to go home. 

Billy leaned toward me and muttered, "They're idiots."

I laughed, but lacked the confidence of knowing whether or not scratching things were nabbing kids at night.

The light was changing now, and the streetlights came on one by one. That was usually the signal. Time to go home.

"Michael!" my mom called from somewhere down the street.

"There it is," Billy sighed.

We started walking our usual route toward the corner where we'd split up. My house sat one direction, Billy's the other, past the Miller place and an empty lot with the overgrown weeds. 

We'd had the same routine for almost two years. One of us would run to our corner first and stand under the streetlight while the other watched. Then we'd switch. 

It was silly, but still, we did it every night.

Billy stopped at his usual spot and pointed dramatically down the street.

"Ready?"

"Ready."

Then together, like always, we said, "Get there before your shadow does."

Billy sprinted first. I watched him run under the streetlight at the opposite corner, shoes slapping pavement. Once he reached the light, he turned and threw both arms into the air victoriously.

"Too slow shadow!" he yelled.

I ran next, laughing as I crossed the street toward my corner. When I reached the light, I turned back toward Billy. He waved. I waved back. 

Then my mother called my name again from the front porch. I looked over my shoulder toward the house.

"Coming!"

When I turned back, Billy wasn't waving anymore. He was looking backward, past his house at something farther down the street. I remember that his expression had changed. He looked confused. 

Behind him, stretched across the sidewalk by the streetlight, was another shadow. At the time, I thought it was probably his sister. Rachel snuck out sometimes with older kids and came home late,. I figured maybe she'd walked up behind him, trying to scare him.

My mom called again, louder this time. I looked back toward the porch. She was standing in the doorway now. When I turned around again to say good-bye to Billy, he was gone. His corner was empty. I remember just standing there under the streetlight, staring.

Billy had always messed around. Hidden behind bushes. Ducking behind parked cars. Trying to scare me. But something felt different. I could see his house down the block. The porch light was on, the TV flickering blue through the window. But no Billy.

My mother called my name one more time, sharper this time, I could tell she was mad. Suddenly I didn't want to stand out there anymore. I turned and ran home. I burst through the door breathing hard enough for my mom to notice.

She just told me to wash up because dinner was almost ready. I remember standing at the sink pretending to rinse my hands while staring out the kitchen window toward Billy's street. Part of me expected him to come sprinting around the corner laughing. I waited an extra second. He never came.

During dinner my parents talked about normal adult things while I pushed food around my plate pretending to eat. I kept thinking about the way Billy had looked over his shoulder, like he'd heard someone call his name from far away.

After dinner I sat in my room listening for the sound of bikes outside or kids yelling in the street. Nothing. I laid in bed trying to block everything out and hold on to one thought, Billy. But sleep finally won out. 

A few hours later, voices woke me up. Adult voices. I stayed still in bed listening to them through the hallway. My parents were downstairs talking to someone at the front door. Even half asleep, I recognized Billy's father immediately.

I crept closer to my bedroom door and tried listening, but I could only make out pieces.

"...checked everywhere..."

"...wouldn't just leave..."

A few seconds later my bedroom door opened and my mother stepped inside.

"Were you with Billy tonight?" she asked quietly.

I sat up. "Yeah."

"Did you see where he went afterward?"

"We ran to the streetlights."

"The streetlights?"

"It’s… our thing."

She nodded, but I knew she wasn't really listening.

"He probably wandered off somewhere," she said. "Maybe hiding at a friend's house."

Billy did that sometimes when his parents fought. Usually he'd ask to sleep over. Sometimes, when it was really bad, he’d just hide in his yard. I hesitated before asking the question that had been sitting in my stomach since hearing the older kid talk about it.

"What if the thing got him?"

My mother frowned. "What thing?"

"The thing the older kids were talking about."

For a second she just stared at me. Then she came and sat on my bed.

"There is no ‘thing’," she said. "He’s just being Billy. Tomorrow you'll be outside playing with him again."

My mother stayed in the room until I laid back down. She left the hallway light on after she closed the door, something she hadn't done in years. I pulled the blanket tighter around myself and tried not to think about the streetlight. About the empty corner. About the shadow behind Billy that didn't seem, I don’t know, right.

When morning finally arrived, the neighborhood already felt off. 

After trying to eat breakfast, I walked outside and down the street toward Billy’s house. As I rounded the corner I immediately saw the police cars. Half the neighborhood was outside watching. Kids stood clustered together on sidewalks while adults spoke in hushed voices nearby. Billy's mother sat on the front steps crying into her hands while an officer talked to her. 

One of the neighborhood kids leaned toward me.

"They still can't find him."

"What?"

"He never came home."

My stomach dropped. At that exact moment, Billy's mother looked up and saw me standing there. She pointed. Two police officers started walking in my direction. One of them knelt slightly so he could look me in the eye.

"You're Michael, right?"

I nodded.

"We heard you were with Billy last night."

"Yeah," I squeaked.

"Can you tell us the last place you saw him?"

"The corner," I said quietly, pointing down the street. "Under the streetlight."

The officer exchanged a glance with the other cop.

"Was anyone else there?"

I swallowed.

"I think maybe his sister."

"Rachel?"

I nodded.

The officer frowned slightly. "Did you actually see her?"

And for a moment, I almost told him. About the shadow and the way Billy had looked behind him. About how wrong everything suddenly felt. But I was thirteen years old, and even then it sounded stupid.

So instead I just said, "I think she was there."

The officer stood back up slowly.

"Okay. Well, if you remember anything else, tell your parents so they can contact us."

I nodded again. Then he paused before walking away.

"The strange thing is," he said, "Rachel's missing too."
