Something strange is happening in the old house at the edge of my hometown. My most unreliable friend told me about it but he’s not lying. I don’t want to tell my parents or the police because nobody would believe me, and I don’t have proof that anything nefarious is happening yet. To fully grasp the depth of the situation you first need know some of my town’s history. More specifically you need to know the story of at least one of the three families that lived at Woland Manor.

  
Egress Island was founded in 1733, but there’s something here that is much older. I’ve lived on the island my whole life, which means I know the stories well. They’re the kind of stories that can only be born in a small town. I think it’s because there’s enough  
 room for life to breathe and minds to wander. If a big city never sleeps and its’ people never slow down, then does the old house at the end of the street still give you goose bumps when you walk by? Maybe a little, but it’s been demagnetized. Woland Manor,  
 on the other hand, has been energized by generations of youthful apprehension.

  
To tell the story of Woland Manor is to tell a story with no beginning. It’s a road with many forks that all dead end at a cold and disconcerting place.

  
The first story that I heard was of the last family that lived there. The Webb’s were the American dream personified. Beautiful, wealthy, self-made, they were what any decent family aspired to be. Mr. Webb was an architect with an eye for real estate. He had  
 been hired to design the new City Hall building for Egress Island, and upon his first visit, he fell in love with the small island town.

  
While the rest of the country had been feverishly building strip malls and sprawling suburbs, Egress Island was left on a slow historical simmer. It took many years to get where it is today, methodically growing like a coral reef into the strange and beguiling  
 place that its’ people call home. The buildings are of a quality and craftsmanship that you rarely see replicated in modern times. Narrow alleyways, cobblestone streets, and small tucked away shops and restaurants feel like they were designed for the scale  
 of a person. Everything here was built from the salt of the earth, and it calls people of a kindred spirit. Upon visiting you either become home sick for a place you just discovered, or the town itself seems to slip from your mind altogether, like a wave retreating  
 back into the ocean.

  
Mr. Webb began talking to a real estate agent about buying a house on the Island. Synthia Weiss was “the” real estate agent in the area back then. She showed Mr. Webb all the best houses that were available, but nothing was quite what he had envisioned. He  
 wanted something grand and unique, he wanted a home that would captivate his guests, and above all he wanted a house that had a story, a house that embodied the rich history of Egress Island. Mrs. Weiss, always willing to do anything to make the sale, thought  
 of just the right place for Mr. Webb. In the end she got the commission she wanted but her career was never the same after what happened. The whispers that blood was on her hands followed her for the rest of her life.

  
When the locals found out he was buying the Manor they tried to talk him out of it, they told him what had happened to the people that lived there before, but he laughed it off. He was going to design the remodeling himself, superstitions be damned.

  
He couldn’t find a single contractor in the area that would step foot in the house, so Mr. Webb was forced to hire someone from out of town. Once the renovations began, there was a curious dread that hung over the island. An expectation that one day some hapless  
 construction worker would turn up dead, or more likely just go missing. However, much to everyone’s surprise nothing dark, ominous, or even out of the ordinary seemed to happen. Instead, day by day, week by week the old manor was slowly being resurrected.

  
Apprehension was beginning to dissipate, and cobwebs were being shaken from the town’s collective nerves. Maybe the locals were simple and foolish after all. A small town still believing in the boogey man while the rest of the world exchanged their myths for  
 pragmatism. Mr. Webb was an injection of modernity, and his boldness was contagious. A few gossipy old ladies even mustered up the courage to walk to the edge of town just to get a glimpse of the renovations. They took slow uneasy steps down the thickly wooded  
 driveway and peaked around the last bend. Their eyes went wide as they beheld the transformation. A Victorian masterpiece that weighed heavily on even nature itself.

  
News traveled fast of how unrecognizable and beautiful the house had become. Mr. Webb was so proud of his new home that he told everyone he knew that he and his wife would host the grandest party the town had ever seen once they settled in.

  
At last, the finishing touches were complete and the move in day had arrived. They worked all day and once evening had come the last moving truck rumbled down the driveway and out of site. Mr. Webb, his wife, and their two children were now alone in their new  
 home, or at least that’s how the story goes.

  
No one spoke to them all weekend. Presumably they were busy unpacking and never ventured into town. Some were planning to bring a welcome gift but they conveniently all had an excuse when it came down to it. A few people called to check in on them, but no one  
 answered. A small worry started to form in the back of people’s minds, what if something bad had happened. What if everyone was right to fear that house. Maybe their instincts were dead on, and they should have trusted the stories they all knew to be true  
 in their hearts. They should have forbidden anyone live in that house. Instead, they let an outsider dispense with their stories like a child that has outgrown their favorite toys.

  
A whole week came and went, but nobody saw or heard from the Webb’s at all. Worry escalated into panic and after several concerned citizens called the police station the Sheriff decided he would go out to the Manor himself. He would later say that he knew something  
 was wrong the minute the house came into view. Maybe it was just his imagination or some sort of visual placebo, but he could have sworn that he saw a large black cat sitting on the very top of the chimney when he pulled up. It was there for a brief second,  
 looking down at him, but when he got out of the car, it was gone.

  
The Sheriff, now with a lump in his throat, marched up to the house and rang the doorbell. No one answered. He called out for Mr. Webb, but no one responded. Walking around the front porch he began to peer through the windows, hoping to see signs of life. When  
 he reached the dining room window, he leaned in close to get a better look. On the dining room table sat four untouched meals, putrefied, and starting to sweat. He scanned the table and creased his brows, not sure of what he was seeing. A spasm of disgust  
 shot through his back and forced him upright. At the head of the table the chair was toppled over, and in front of it, on the tabletop, was a long silver fork whose back end was lodged with brute force into the wood. At the tip of the fork, raised high in  
 the air, was a human eye, skewered, and looking in his direction.

  
The Sheriff ran to the front door prepared to kick it in, but there was no need, it was unlocked. His gun was drawn and steady as he crossed the threshold. In all his long years of service, there had never been a murder under his watch. Upon entering he noticed  
 a peculiar sensation, small almost imperceptible pops rippled across his body. The chain reaction subtly prickling his skin and quickly rolling off again. He chalked it up to nerves and adrenaline and kept moving. Stalking from room to room he searched for  
 any trace of the Webb’s. One after another they came up empty, and with each failure a hopeless nihilism grew and gnawed at his insides. Deep down he knew there was no point, they were just the latest dead end in the tragic story of Woland Manor. No one was  
 home, and there was no trail that even the keenest blood hound could follow. He did, however, make note of several unusual findings.

1.  As I already mentioned, the human eye impaled on the fork that was jammed into the tabletop.
2.  A broken pitcher of what looked like dried sweet tea on the kitchen floor. The shattered glass splattered with blood.
3.  A large hole in the wall at the back of the kitchen.
4.  The kitchen sink was coated with blood, as was the garbage disposal.
5.  In the powder bath on the main floor there was a slow leak in the toilet that had now had time to cover the entire bathroom floor and half of the foyer. The shower curtain was ripped down, and a large bloody crack was hammered into the shower tiles.
6.  Blood from the dining room table, the kitchen, and the powder bath all led to the fireplace and into the firebox.
7.  There was no sign of a fire, no ash, and no residue, but the Webb’s were gone.

  
That’s all we know of what happened to the last of the three families that were unfortunate enough to live at Woland Manor.

  
I’ve never been inside the house myself, but I have seen it. The closest I got was to the last bend in the driveway. I remember walking for what felt like an eternity, winding through the woods and checking over my shoulder the whole way. Every kid gets dared  
 to look at that house at some point in their life. Most of them don’t go. When I think back, it’s almost like recalling a dream. I was in the twilight world, things were quiet, and nothing felt right. I stood behind a giant oak tree, rigid as the trunk itself,  
 and peered around the edge. I didn’t want to be out in the open for some reason. It was as if I was trying to sneak a glance of the house without it seeing me back. Vines were creeping up its sides, shutters were falling off, but it was still beautiful in  
 a terrible kind of way. Something about the scale of it was oppressive, like a cold lead blanket draped over my stomach. I dreamed about that pale white chimney for weeks, I swear it was so tall that it almost touched the moon.

  
I never thought I would go back, that is until I saw Gavin last week.

  
My friend Casey and I had just left the movie theater and were walking down main street towards the coffee shop. I was cutting up about the “horror” movie she’d insisted that we see when she grabbed my hand in surprise. Up ahead a commotion had exploded onto  
 the sidewalk. A few people gasped and stepped aside. A large woman sturdied herself on a light post while shouting dignified obscenities at a man that was sprawled out on the concrete.

  
He bounced up, all lanky and apologetic, scrambling to hand her the bags she had dropped.

  
“Is that Gavin?” asked Casey

  
He turned to face us before I could answer. Eyes lighting up with recognition as he jogged our way.

  
“Jax, Casey! You’re not going to believe what I just saw.” Gavin glanced over his shoulder and pulled his hoody over his head. “Some weird shit is going down.”

  
He was right to say we were not going to believe him. Just a week ago, I asked him to help me move some furniture at my grandma’s house and ever since then he hadn’t shut up about how he’s convinced that she’s doing cocaine because her craft room was too organized.  
 I think he’s smoked himself into a constant state of paranoia but he’s still a good friend. We’ve been getting into trouble together since kindergarten.

  
“What happened back there?” I pointed behind him

  
“I was looking at this” he held up a crumpled piece of paper with a picture of a cat on it, as if that meant something to me, “and I accidently walked into that fat lady. She straight up shouldered me into the ground”.

  
“Alright… well we’re going to the coffee shop, so you’ll have to tell us there.”

  
Casey and I stood in line as Gavin picked out a table. After we got our order, we made our way to meet him at the far back corner of the shop.

  
I handed him the large black coffee he asked for, and Casey and I nestled down across the table from him.

  
“So, what did you see?”

  
He leaned in close, his long unkempt hair partially covering his face, and smiled. I’d seen that smile a thousand times before, and I knew exactly what it meant, he thought he’d discovered something fantastic.

  
“You guys know ole Ms. Wellers, right?”

  
I shuddered, “Yea she owns the antique store off 7th street. Place always gave me the creeps.”

  
“Exactly. So, I was in the alley way across from her shop and”

  
Casey raised her hand like she was in class, “Wait… why were you in the alleyway?”

  
“I was selling drugs to one of your teachers, want to guess which one”.

  
She slumped a little and cocked her head. Casey had been homeschooled most of her life, and Gavin often left her at a loss for words.

  
I rubbed the back of my neck, “If I had to guess, I’d say he was taking the trash out after closing at Big Joes Pizza.”

  
“Yea your probably right, if anybody asks, that’s what I was doing. Anyways, I was standing by the dumpster when I heard the door creak open at the antique shop across the street. It was Ms. Wellers, and she was carrying a cage”.

  
I leaned closer, “What was in it?”

  
“A cat, grey with a white face and white paws.” He took out the crumpled flyer and slapped it down on the table.

  
“This cat”

  
Sure enough, on the flyer was the same cat he had described and at the top in large red letters was the word MISSING.

  
Casey perked up, “Oh, she found the Dansby’s cat, Mr. Whiskers! That’s great!”

  
Gavin shook his head and lowered his voice, “No, she didn’t find Mr. Whiskers, she trapped him, and trust me, she had bad intentions”.

  
I stared back at him, “Why do you say that?”

  
“Well Ms. Wellers never liked me, and I’ve never trusted her, so when I saw her take off late at night with a cat in tow I figured I’d see what she was up to. She headed East, and it just seemed so strange to me. I know she lives in the apartment above the  
 shop so she wasn’t taking it home; I heard her mention that she lived there one time when I was a kid, my mom would take me to the antique store and talk to her forever, she really likes her for some crazy reason.”

  
“Yea same here”, I nodded

  
“Anyways, I was keeping my distance when I noticed the flyer posted on a street sign. The Dansby’s cat was missing, and it was the same damn cat she had in the cage. Thing is, I know where the Dansby’s live, Philip buys an eighth every now and then and I drop  
 it off under the big rock in their front yard. It’s not far from the antique shop but she was going the complete opposite direction. I kept following her, but it was tricky because that side of town is dead at night. I was keeping close to the buildings, trying  
 to stay in the shadows, and out of site. One time she turned around and almost caught me, but I slipped into the door niche of the post office at the last second. It must have been a full minute before I heard her start walking again. I let her build a little  
 distance before I rounded the corner and picked back up. She was way down the road now, fading in and out of view as she walked under the streetlights. The old hag leaned under the weight of that poor cat and hobbled all the way to the edge of town and when  
 she got there, she stepped off the curb and walked into the night”.

  
“Maybe she was going to release the cat in the woods or something. It might have been getting into her trash. I wouldn’t be surprised if she didn’t see the posters, she’s old as hell”.

  
Gavin shook his head, “I thought the same thing, but she didn’t do that, she just kept going, and there’s only one thing in that direction…”

  
He waited for us to fill in the blank, Casey did the honors.

  
“Woland Manor…”

  
I crossed my arms, “There’s no way she was going out there”.

  
Gavin grinned like he’d just pulled off the world’s greatest heist “Oh yes there is. I followed her all the way to the bend in the driveway and watched her walk up the front steps and inside the manor.”

  
“You’re full of shit”.

  
“Nope, not this time, and when she came back out, the cage was empty.”

  
Casey scrunched up her face, “Are you saying she killed Mr. Whiskers?”

  
“I don’t know”, Gavin threw his hands back, “all I know is she went in there for about 20 minutes and when she came back out Mr. Whiskers was gone. I crept back down the driveway before she could see me and got the hell out of there.”

  
“And did you get any of this on video?”, said Casey

  
“No, my phone doesn’t do video”, Gavin held up a Nokia brick phone that was probably older than him. “Old school phones like this have less radiation so they don’t fry your nut sack, plus it doesn’t have a camera for the government to spy on me with.”

  
I spoke up before he could go any further down that rabbit hole, “So, what are you going to do now?”

  
“Hell, if I know”

  
Casey hugged her coffee closer to her chest, “Well, if that really happened, we should tell somebody”.

  
“Who would believe me? If you haven’t noticed, I don’t exactly have a glowing reputation around town.”

  
He was right, I couldn’t think of a single authority figure that wouldn’t roll their eyes at Gavin repeating this story. He was the class clown, the bad influence, the social contrarian. He was my friend and even I had my doubts. Ms. Wellers, on the other hand,  
 might have been a bit eccentric, but she was perceived as a nice, long-standing member of the community that none of the adults seemed to have anything bad to say about.

  
I mulled it over, “If you’re not screwing with us and Ms. Wellers is actually up to something, then we need proof”.

  
Gavin nodded with a distant look in his eyes; I could tell that a plan was forming.

  
“Ok this is what we do, we go to the manor tomorrow, during the day, and see if we can find any evidence of whatever the hell she was up to. If we do, great, we call the cops and bust the old broad, but if we don’t then we regroup, do some investigating, and  
 keep an eye on the antique shop”.

  
I was about to respond when Casey interjected, “I’m in”.

  
“You are?” My last girlfriend hated Gavin and was constantly nagging me about hanging out with him. Casey and I weren’t officially together but it seemed to be heading in that direction, and that was fine with me, for being a goodie goodie, she was unusually  
 down to earth.

  
“Yea, why not? Somebody’s got to do something. Plus, what else are we going to do, it sounds kind of fun, like a cozy mystery. Except I hope Mr. Whiskers is ok…”

  
Gavin raised his eyebrows, “Ok then, lets meet here tomorrow morning, around nine, then we’ll head out to Woland Manor and see what we can find.”

  
We went our separate ways. Gavin back to his house and Casey and I back to hers. I was under strict orders from her dad to bring her home before 11:30 and we were pushing our luck.

  
She stayed close to me as we walked, the uneven cobblestones jostling us together.

  
She looked up at me. "This might sound bad, but I'm kind of excited about going to the manor tomorrow."

  
"Nothing to feel bad about. I know what you mean. It is kind of exciting. Have you ever seen it?"

  
I was probably more nervous than excited, but I wasn't about to tell her that.

  
"Nope. This'll be pretty much the most rebellious thing I've ever done. Don't tell anybody. If it got back to my parents, I'd be grounded for a month."

  
I held up my free hand. "I won't say a word."

  
"Hopefully Gavin doesn't say anything either."

  
"Don't worry about that." I pulled out my phone. "I'll text him right now and let him know."

  
I walked her to her door, and we paused at the top step.

  
"I guess I'll see you in the morning." She held my eyes for a long moment.

  
"Yeah, I'll meet you here around a quarter to nine, and we'll walk to meet Gavin."

  
I could feel the pull between us, drawing us closer. I was just about to lean in when the door swung open. Her dad stood there, arms crossed.

  
Good thing I held off. He already doesn't like me.

  
We said our goodbyes, and I headed home, the night's chill settling in around me.

  
Now that I was alone, the unease crept in. It's only in these quiet moments that I find myself glancing over my shoulder. As I turned onto the road to my house, I looked back. And there it was: a large black cat, sitting dead center in the street, staring right  
 at me.

  
I hope that's not a bad omen.

  
Guess I'll find out tomorrow.
