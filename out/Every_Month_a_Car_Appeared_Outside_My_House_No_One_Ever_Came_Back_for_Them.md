In 2018, I fled from the busyness of city life. I spent my prime years on Wall Street, dedicating my days to protecting the financial interests of my clients, until it broke my mind. At the age of 31, I suffered from a nervous breakdown. And as long as I remained in the city, my mind would remain broken, so I left.

 

I packed up my studio apartment, turned in my two weeks at the firm, and moved into the very heart of the Midwest. Small-town life cleared my mind. I got a job at the local bank, and with my savings, I was able to purchase an old Victorian manor, in desperate need of repair.

 

Life was good, exactly the change I needed; the neighbors were friendly, the old lady next door even baked me banana bread as a welcome gift. It was the sort of place where people wave at you across the street as you walked by, and the workers at the local supermarket remembered your name. Months passed, as I grew accustomed to life in the slow lane, but soon cracks began to form in this pleasant façade. It started slowly. I can’t quite remember the moment I noticed that something wasn’t quite right, but before long, it was clear as day.

 

Every month, a car with an out-of-state license plate would be parked and left in front of my home. No one would ever return to the vehicles, and they would eventually be towed away. But every month, just like clockwork, a new one would appear, and a few days later it would be towed away. Every car was different; some were expensive sports cars, others were rust buckets. Some were perfectly clean and empty; others were packed to the brim with boxes and bags. The only thing they all had in common was that they all came from out of state, and the owners never returned.

 

I’ll never forget the first car I noticed abandoned in front of my home. It was a bright red 1992 Corvette with plates from New England. It was parked alongside the road, mere feet from my mailbox. The first day it sat there, I hardly noticed. I figured my neighbor across the way had a friend or maybe some family in town. But when no one returned to the vehicle by the end of the second day, I started to pay closer attention. The rest of the evening, I watched the car from my living room window as I watched some evening tv. In the hours I sat there watching, no one ever returned to the Corvette; plenty of people passed by on the sidewalk, but none of them even so much as acknowledged the bright red sports car. It was as though it were invisible. As I headed off to bed that night, I made a mental note to go take a closer look at the car in the morning.

 

The next day, I was awoken by the annoying sounds of the town’s tow truck. Peeking out of my bedroom’s blinds, I saw the large, rusty truck back in front of the Corvette, drop its ancient iron hook, and carry off the abandoned vehicle. After that, I didn’t think much about the whole incident until it happened again exactly one month later.

 

The next car was an early-2000s minivan, bearing a plate from the pacific northwest and a Grand Canyon bumper sticker. When I saw it, I knew that there was something off, and I had to get to the bottom of it. My first plan was to see if any of my neighbors knew why strange vehicles kept showing up in front of my home. Across the street from me was the home of my elderly neighbor, Nancy, who rarely left home. I felt certain that she would have seen who had parked the car along the road. Crossing the street, I rang the doorbell and waited for her to open. Her eyes lit up as she recognized me.

 

“Why, Carter, what a surprise, how are you, young man? Won’t you come in for a bit?” she said in her cracked but warm voice.

 

“Hi, Ms. Nancy, good to see you. I wish I could stay, but I’m on my lunch break.” I said declining her invitation as graciously as I could.

 

“Oh well, another time then, how can I help you?” she replied.

 

“Well Ma’am, I’m just curious if you know anything about the car parked in front of my house? Like who it belongs to and are why it’s there?”

 

She leaned slightly to the side to see the van behind me.

 

“That car right there, son?”

 

“Yes, Ma’am, that’s the one.”

 

“Oh umm, I can’t say that I know anything about it. Do you think it belongs to someone around here?” Her tone became a little more serious, and her volume dropped slightly.

 

“No, I don’t, it’s got plates from the other side of the country,” I said.

 

For a moment, a flash of wild energy flickered across her old eyes before she responded, “Oh, then I’m sure it’s nothing to worry about, probably just someone passing through.”

 

I looked at her for a moment and said, “Ms. Nancy, did you happen to see who parked the car there? Maybe you could help me track them down.”

 

“Oh! I’m afraid not deary, I must have been napping when they parked there, but don’t worry, I’m sure they are probably at the bar, or maybe out smoking with some teenagers, you know how young people are nowadays.” She said with a forced chuckle.

 

I stared at her, and she smiled back. After an awkward moment, she said

 

“Well, thank you, Carter, for stopping by, but I really must be going. I hope you have a pleasant day.”

 

With that, she shut the door. And as I turned to leave, I heard Ms. Nancy locking the door. Puzzled, I looked back at her home just in time to see her pull the curtains shut. I figured I’d ask another neighbor, so I went next door to the home of Brad, a divorced 40-year-old man who worked weekends at the local gas station. He answered with a gruff

“What do you want?”

“Umm, hey,” I said, caught off guard, “I was just wondering if you know anything about the car parked in front of my place?”

 

He didn’t even look at the car before he answered. “Look, new guy, the cars that go there aren’t bothering anyone, so why don’t you drop it and worry about something else, like the rest of us?” he had clearly had too much to drink.

 

“Cars?” I said, “So this has happened before?”

 

He stared at me, eyes full of malice, before muttering, “Just mind your own business.” And slamming the door.

 

“So much for Midwest nice,” I said under my breath as I walked away.

 

A day and a half later, the tow truck came and took the van away. At this moment, I decided that if another car showed up the following month, I would go to the sheriff's office and ask for any information regarding the strange cars. And sure enough, exactly a month later, a beat-up Honda Civic from the east coast became the third strange car parked in front of my house.

 

The day it showed up, I walked the 5 blocks to the small county sheriff’s office, wedged between the quaint town hall and a store that sold greeting cards and not much else. I entered the small law enforcement office and was greeted by the middle-aged woman at the front desk. I smiled back at her and said,

 “Hi there, is Sheriff Kelly in today?”

 

“Why yes, he is. Let me check if he’s available right now. Why don’t you have a seat, and I’ll go check.”

 

I nodded and sat in one of the three chairs filling the small waiting room. Two minutes later, she returned and beckoned to me, saying,

“Alright, the sheriff will see you now, down the hall, second door on the right.” She sweetly chirped.

Thanking her, I entered the hall and gently knocked on the Sheriff’s door.

“Come on in,” bellowed a deep voice from beyond the door. I opened the door as Sheriff Kelly rose to shake my hand. Sheriff Kelly was a man meant for a different time; he would have done well in the era of cowboys and outlaws. The stocky man had the appearance of a man who thought of himself as the hero. This small town’s only guardian against the harsh evils of the world. His handshake was firm, and his weathered face had a kind look to it, though deep in his eyes was the ambition and shrewdness that all politicians have.

“Carter, isn’t it?” the Sheriff said with a smile.

“Yessir, that’s me,” I replied

“Well, son, what can I do for you today? Are you settling in well?”

“Um yeah, Sheriff, things are going good, but I’ve noticed something strange happening in front of my house.”

He raised his eyebrows and stared at me. “Go on, Carter.”

“Well, it’s the strangest thing, Sheriff, once a month, a random car is parked and abandoned in front of my home.”

I could sense disappointment in the Sheriff’s tone, “The road is public property, Carter; it’s not that strange that vehicles are parked along the side.”

“Yessir that alone isn’t too strange. But these cars are always out of state, no one ever returns to them, and then they are towed away two days after they show up.” I protested.

 

His tone became slightly more agitated as he retorted, “That’s the town policy, sir. Cars left on the roadside longer than 48 hours are towed, which keeps our roads clear and helps provide the local tow truck with some additional business.”

 

I was a bit flustered by that answer; it never crossed my mind that this could be so explainable, “but” I stammered, “but why doesn’t anyone ever return to their car? And why does a new one show up exactly every month?”

 

It was clear that the Sheriff was over this conversation; his tone was now firm and aggressive, as he stood up and crossed the office to stand directly in front of me. “Carter… have you ever considered that sometimes people come here because they want to disappear? You’re not in the big city anymore; you’re in the Midwest, people born here want to leave, and those who come here are trying to disappear, just like you wanted to. now unless you have a real concern, I’m going to have to ask you to leave.”

 

I swallowed as I stared into Kelly’s eyes. A moment passed before I said, “Thank you for your time, Sheriff Kelly.”

I turned to leave as the Sheriff said, “Be safe out there, Neighbor.”

As I walked home, I didn’t know what to think. I felt certain that something weird was going on in this small town. And the sheriff’s dismissal made that all the more clear in my mind. It must be a cover-up of some kind; my neighbors wanted nothing to do with it, and the cops ignored it. But doubt entered my head as I wondered, ‘What if I’m losing it? What if it really is nothing unusual, and my imagination was getting the best of me?’ I had already suffered one mental break in my life; what if it was happening again? Could I trust my understanding of what is happening? ‘No.’ I told myself, trying to banish the doubt, ‘This is real. I just need proof, proof that I’m not crazy.’

 

I was so deep in thought that I didn’t notice when I passed my house. I just kept walking, and evidently, the sidewalk came to an end, right in front of the town library. Looking at the library building, which was far older than any other building in town, I decided to go inside. My neighbor, Brad, had let it slip that the strange cars were not new. Maybe something in the town’s history would give me a clue.

 

The Library smelled of damp wood and old paper. Though it was small, the space had been neatly decorated as if it were a grand Victorian library. The walls were painted a dark green with finely stained mahogany wainscot. Beautiful lamps and leather wing-backed chairs dotted the empty spaces between hand-carved floor-to-ceiling bookshelves. The atrium was home to a large, ornate wooden desk, behind which sat a thin man who appeared to be in his mid-60s. His choice of a white button-down and a blue bow-tie told me that he took his role as town librarian very seriously.

 

“Hello there, young man,” he said with a warm buttery voice. “Is there anything I can help you with today?”

“Umm, yes, actually, I’m new to town, and I’m curious to learn more about my new home.”

He smiled widely, “well you certainly have come to the right place, and welcome to our community. So what information would you like regarding our town?”

I thought for a moment before responding, “Maybe we can cover the founding and early history of the town?”

“Ah, well, that is certainly a good place to start. The township of Buckhorn was founded in 1823 by a close community of German immigrant farmers, who brought with them generational farming knowledge. Which, combined with some of the nation's best soil, protected this community from several famines and the Great Depression. Even when other farming towns struggled, here in Buckhorn, we have always been blessed with bountiful harvests.”

I nodded, trying my best to appear interested. “Does this library have the town records dating back to the founding?” I said rather abruptly.

He raised an eyebrow before saying, “We do have the town records and minutes there on the back shelves; however, records of the first decade were lost in the town fire of 1892, but we have supplemented the records with information from surrounding regions of the time.”

“That’s good to know, thank you.”

“Can I help you with anything else?” he said

“No I don’t think so, you’ve been very helpful, thank you.”

With a nod, he returned to the book he had been reading as I walked to the back of the library.

For the next several hours, I poured over records and historical sources, most of it was unimportant, but by the end, three odd bits of information stood out like a sore thumb.

First, though Buckhorn claims it was founded in 1882, the surrounding communities claim that Buckhorn was originally called Ramshorn and has been around since 1860. The neighboring town of Asheville, founded in 1850, had the following in its records: ‘In the spring of 1860, a group of strange German settlers homesteaded in the prairie to the North, though they spoke German, their dialect was unknown to the German folk of our town. They had many odd practices, and though their farming was outdated by many decades, the land they tended seemed to flourish. In the fall, they formally came together as a township called Ramshorn.’ Later on, the record states that ‘in the winter of 1892, a fire destroyed the majority of the town of Ramshorn, though the townsfolk would rebuild in 1894 under the name of Buckhorn.’

Second, I researched the history of my house and found that the lot was the location of the original town square. Up until 1940, the land beneath my home was the center of town and was home to a statue only referred to as the ‘old statue’. Only a small note in the 1941 town ledger mentioned it: ‘Old Statue removed 1940 – relocated.’

And third, I was able to find several newspaper articles from around the county and the state that noted the high number of disappearances that happen in the area surrounding Buckhorn. One headline from the early 1980s read ‘Several motorists were reported missing after last being seen refueling in Buckhorn. Local Sheriff assures residents there is no cause for alarm.’

That must be where the cars come from. I stared at the headline for several minutes; it wasn’t the only one or the newest. I looked around to make sure the librarian wasn’t looking before pulling out my phone to take some pictures of the records. This was crazy; there are hundreds of missing people last seen in or around Buckhorn. But where were the police? Why wasn’t anyone looking? I knew the answer, but didn’t want to believe it. The police must be in on whatever is happening. I couldn’t trust anyone, not the police, not my neighbors, not anyone from Buckhorn.

“Find what you were looking for?” The librarian stood right behind me and startled me with his words.

“Yeah, it did, thank you, it’s been a big help.” I quickly said

With a thin smile on his face, he replied, “Always happy to help.”

Incredibly uncomfortable, I tried my best to get out of this conversation, “well I really must be going, it’s nearly time for supper, thanks again!” I said as I headed for the door.

His eyes watched me move across the room, and blankly he said, “Be safe out there, friend.”

Once outside, I took off running and didn’t stop till I reached home. Behind my locked doors, a plan began to form in my mind. I needed to get evidence. Maybe if I could film one of the cars being dropped off in front of my house, I could send it to the FBI, and they would come to investigate. And so, I planned to get a camera in the morning and headed off to bed.

I wasn’t going to shop in town anymore, I doubt they would have a good video camera anyway, instead I drove 45 minutes to the nearest Wal-Mart. It was nice to get away for a bit, to not have to worry about weird neighbors or strange cars. At Wal-Mart, I purchased the nicest camera 100 bucks can buy, along with some other equipment, such as a tripod and spare batteries. Leaving Wal-Mart, I decided to stop by a local gun store. I’ve never owned a gun, but figured it might be good to have one in case things got weirder. So I picked out a Sig P365, had the man at the front show me how to operate it, and headed home as a new gun owner.

The next day, the Civic was gone, which meant I had exactly a month to get ready. I planned to film nonstop, starting a few days before the next car was supposed to show up. I placed the tripod in the upstairs window, looking down on the road. The goal was to catch on film the person who would park the car there, maybe the FBI could identify the driver, and confirm whether or not it was the car's owner.

This month, the people around town changed. The friendly façade was set aside. People looked at me as if I were a terrorist. Instead of a friendly wave or a warm greeting, most people would look away from me, and those who did look in my direction looked with anger and malice. Word had gotten around that I had been looking into the town’s history. And the general public didn’t like that, and they let me know by denying me common courtesy.

The month passed slowly, but soon I only had a few days left to wait. I set up the camera and left it rolling as I headed off to bed. At 3 AM, I awoke to the sound of shattering glass. Adrenaline pumped through my veins as I slowly walked downstairs, gun in hand. My front window had been smashed by a brick. I cautiously checked every room of my house to be sure I was alone. And then rushed upstairs to review the footage. In shock, I watched as the camera revealed that my elder neighbor, Nancy, walked out into the middle of the road holding a brick, before wildly throwing it through the large living room window, and quickly shuffled back into her home.

Paranoia and anger boiled within me. I didn’t sleep the rest of the night, as I prepared to confront Nancy for what she had done. Morning came, and I stormed across the road and banged on Nancy’s door. “Nancy! Get out here! I know what you did!” I rocked the door with my fists. “I know you're in there! Come out now!” For 5 minutes, I slammed my hand into her door.

“Everything alright?” a voice boomed from behind me. I turned around to see Sheriff Kelly, wearing a smirk on his face.

Taking a deep breath, I replied, “yeah Sheriff, just a little neighborly dispute.”

“Well, son, probably good to put it to rest, if we can’t get along with our neighbors, probably can’t get along with anyone.” I noticed his hand rested on his sidearm.

“Right, Sheriff, sorry about the noise.”

“It’s quite alright son.” He turned and looked at my smashed window. “Might want to get that fixed, boy, don’t want it to get drafty in there.” He said as he walked away.

Sighing, I returned home. Tired from the night before, I returned to bed. It was midafternoon when I woke up, and from that point on, I watched Nancy’s home like a hawk. She had to come out sometime, and when she did, I would be there waiting. Night fell, and Nancy hadn’t left. I reviewed the tape and confirmed that Nancy hadn’t left when I was asleep. I planned to watch her house all night, especially since the next car would appear in the morning.

At 1 AM, a light in Nancy’s home turned on. All day, the house sat dark, but now her whole living room was lit up. A moment later, her garage door began to open. I watched intently as her old Cadillac rolled off the driveway. Curiosity got the better of me as I grabbed the camera and gun before dashing to my own car. I tailed Nancy for over an hour. The elderly woman did several laps around town. She slowly cruised down every street Buckhorn had to offer. I nearly called off my pursuit, but then Nancy’s car took a last-minute turn onto the county road out of town. The road carved through the empty country lit only by the headlights of our two cars. Surely she knew she was being followed; no other cars were out at this time of night, but I didn’t care. I had to know where she was going.  

After 15 minutes, she slowed before taking a left-hand turn onto a dirt road that led off into the timber. Brush and old growth pressed in on the road, scraping the sides of our cars, but Nancy kept on driving. The road continued deeper into the woods, and Nancy drove on for another 20 minutes before stopping. When she did, she pulled over to the side of the road as best as she could. She sat in the cab for a moment or two before she exited the car as calmly as if she were going to the grocery store, then she slowly turned and stared into my headlights for a brief moment before walking off into the woods.

I sat in my car for a few moments after Nancy walked off, unsure if I should follow. But soon I decided I needed to know where she was heading, so with the camera in hand and gun in my coat pocket. I stepped out of my car, and with a deep breath, I followed after that crazy old woman.

The path was easy enough to follow, though it was dark; the night mode on my camera offered some visibility. Nancy was on a rough trail of some kind, one she seemed to know well. It weaved its way through the dense forest, before reaching a clearing after 400 yards or so. Tall grass filled this clearing, but it was better than trees; the makeshift trail was far easier to follow.

As the path continued, it came upon a large opening in the grass, a circle of dirt free from any sort of vegetation. In the center of the circle sat a massive stone statue of some sort of creature. The thing was roughly twenty feet tall and absolutely terrifying.   It had the shape of a man, yet the feet of a goat, and scales covered its arms; its head was similar to a bull's, and two enormous bat wings draped over its shoulders. Though carved in stone, its eyes were piercing and angry. And the base of the statue had a plaque which read ‘*zu einem unbekannten gott’.* I stood staring at the statue in awe and horror for what felt like hours, but suddenly realized that Nancy was not here. I looked through the camera and saw that the trail continued on the opposite side of the circle. Eager to leave that horrible statue behind, I continued along the trail. Before long, a shape emerged in the distance; there, on the edge of the forest, was an old chapel. It looked as though it had been here since the pilgrims; it was a gray colonial-style chapel with a pointed steeple. The thing was falling apart, the weather had worn away its paint, parts of the roof were caving in, and its windows were all broken. But through glassless windows I saw a gentle light, like the flittering of candles.

 I crept my way up to the entrance; its hefty doors were long gone, leaving the building wide open. I peeked into the chapel, and what I saw still haunts me. it was a classic church layout; the back of the chapel had a raised platform with a closed door in the back wall, yet there was no pulpit upon the platform, nor was there a cross anywhere in the building. Candles sat upon the stage, bathing the building in gentle candlelight. The rest of the space was filled with rows of pews, ornately hand-carved ones, and in the pews were people. Not many, no more than two dozen scattered throughout the rows, all of them had their backs to me, facing the front. As I peered in on this bizarre sight, one of the figures turned and looked at me. It was Nancy. She beckoned me to come to her. I swallowed as I slowly stepped in, and she gestured for me to sit down next to her as I appeared. As I did, she smiled sweetly at me. looking down she saw the camera in my hand, which she quietly pulled from my hand, and whispered in my ear.

“you won’t need that anymore.”

We sat in silence, I glanced from side to side, and realized everyone here were people from Buckhorn, random people I’d see out walking, the mailman, the owner of the diner, the crossing guard, they all sat in silence. Suddenly after several long quiet moments, two men entered from a side door near the front, carrying a third. The man they carried was shirtless, bound, and crying. He begged and pleaded that they stop and free him, but no one listened no one ever acknowledged him, but the two men forced him into a kneeling position with his back towards the congregation as he faced the door in the back of the chapel. As the man wept loudly, Nancy leaned over to me and whispered,

“This one is from out west, and he’ll help us have a bountiful harvest.”

“What is this?” I managed to squeak out

“It’s our offering to Yorath.” She said with a toothy smile.

“What?”

“Yorath cares for us, gives us a good harvest, and all he asks is that we do what he wants.”

She explained, “This is what he wants.”

 Cold beads of sweat made their way down my forehead, and the room slowly filled with chanting in some ancient language. The chants continued growing louder and louder, and then as quickly as they started, they stopped. The room was eerily quiet, apart from the low whimpers of the man up front. Then, with a loud creak, the door on the back wall slowly swung open, and behind it was pure darkness; the room became terribly cold, yet the man up front was drenched in sweat. I could see that something was happening to him; it started in his lower back, a strange wiggling movement, then, with a sound like a bursting pimple, his lowest vertebra burst through the skin of his back, then another and another. Before long, his spine curled up towards the ceiling as more and more vertebrae popped from their place. The poor man screamed in pain as his spine was forcefully peeled from his back. As his spine pushed through the back of his neck, he gurgled and choked on his own blood, as his intact spine snapped off his skull and rocketed towards the ceiling. The now silent man crumbled into a fleshly pile. In a panic, I jumped from my seat and screamed. I ran to the exit, but was blocked by the large shape of a man.

“Where you off too son? Man, you look like you’ve seen a ghost.” Sheriff Kelly said with a smirk as he swung a right hook into my cheek. blood leaked from my mouth as I fell to the floor.

 dazed I tried to crawl my way to my feet, as the Sheriff walked aggressively toward me. Instinctively, I pulled the pistol from my jacket and fired, hitting the Sheriff twice in the chest. As Kelly fell, and before anyone else could move, I bolted out the door I didn’t stop to see if he got back up. The trail was difficult to follow in the dark, and I stumbled and fell several times, but I didn’t care. Eventually, I found my way back to the road. Jumping in my car, I sped down the dirt road and turned back onto the blacktop, heading away from town. I will never go back. I don’t care that everything I know is in that town; I’m never going to Buckhorn again.

I started over. I moved out west, got a new job, even my own apartment. For a long time, things were good.

Until last week.

I came home from work and taped to my apartment door was a note.

It said, “Hi there, Neighbor.”

I don’t think this is over. I don’t know how they found me, or how long they’ve been watching. I haven’t seen anyone yet, but I keep thinking about those cars…

If you’ve ever seen something like that, cars that don’t belong, people who won’t talk about it just leave. Don’t ask questions. Just go.
