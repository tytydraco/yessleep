It’s taken me more than half a year to write this. Mainly because I held out some semblance of hope that the police would do something with my statement. I think at this point it is safe to say that my hope was unfounded and my boyfriend told me posting to this site might have some catharsis. And maybe, just maybe, someone else might have experienced something similar. The only update I received from law enforcement, however, was that the man I described to them never existed. His name wasn’t found in any police database, or any birth record for that matter. Even the composite sketch went unidentified from the departments of Colorado to the sectors of Nevada. By all accounts, Jeremy Kitsch was a ghost. Seeing as what happened that day, I think he’s something much worse. 

I was 26 at the time and had just moved to the Los Angeles area. If you know anything about California in general, you know it’s not cheap to live there. Especially for a recent college grad with no entry level job offers in my field. I don’t know what compelled me into becoming a history major, but it certainly wasn’t making money. Not trying to be cynical by any means, I love historical research more than anything, but when you start working for shitty tips at Benjy’s dive bar, you start to wonder where society's priorities lie. We don’t dwell in the lessons of the past anymore, instead we fly towards the high speed future with robots to wipe our asses and clean our feet. Los Angeles was very reflective of that mentality, in its glass clad penthouses and billboard sex appeal. Can’t say I didn’t enjoy the overpriced coffee though.

Benjy’s had cut my hours that month so while I had a lot of time on my hands I had a lot less money in my pocket. Simultaneously, I tried to apply my credentials for a job I actually wanted and would take walks in various parks with free entry. I was in Runyon Canyon that day, my normal spot since it was closer to my apartment. The mid-April sun was bright and oppressive so I was taking a lot of breaks on my journey. One of those breaks was at the very zenith of the highest hill. I silently thanked fate for making the bench wood instead of metal, but the surface was still scalding. Despite this I managed to breathe deeply and take a deep drink from my water bottle that had managed to keep cool under the oppressive sunlight. Closing my eyes, I hadn’t noticed the man sitting next to me on the bench at first. Once I opened them and lowered my water bottle, I jumped in my skin, my hand instinctively reaching for my key-fob with a can of pepper spray attached. 

“Sorry miss, I didn’t mean to startle you,” the strange man said politely. He was tall and lanky, with a dark tailored suit and a patterned tie. His hair was dark brown and greased close to his head with what appeared to be hair gel, but his skin was so shiny I almost thought he had slicked it back with sweat. Despite that, he reeked of cheap cologne with not an ounce of body odor seeping through. His smile was the most unsettling thing about him, stretching his cheeks up to the frame of his sunglasses. His gums were a bright pink and his teeth an even brighter white. I had seen plenty of people with veneers before, but the shine off them was next level. 

“No, it’s fine,” I gasped, assuming he just wanted to take a load off like I was. I moved slightly to give him more room and his eyes seemed to follow me under his sunglasses.

“I don’t mean to trouble you but I couldn’t help but notice that you’d be the perfect model for my upcoming photo shoot.”

His smile never wavered, the reflection off of his teeth starting to become blinding. 

“I’m sorry, who are you?”

The man reached into his pocket effortlessly, pulling out a white card between his pointer and middle finger. 

“Jeremy Kitsch,” the man said as I took the card from him gently, making sure not to come too much closer to him. I turned it over in my hand and saw his name printed in black ink and below it his phone number and occupation along with the address of his studio. “I’m an upcoming modeling agent developing my portfolio. I focus on the natural look and intend to bring a new perspective to the industry. Your freckles and round facial structure will be an inspired addition in my collection.”

I remember thinking that he was describing me like a piece of meat. 

“I’m not a model. I don’t have any experience in this type of thing.”

“Inconsequential. In fact, I prefer it that way. Professionals are much too stiff and insistent on wearing makeup. I’ll tell you what, come to my first shoot tomorrow evening and I’ll pay you 400 dollars an hour for 5 hours.”

I remember my jaw almost dropping at the number he described. 1k for just a couple of hours. Not only would that help pay my rent that month but it would take me off my diet of microwave meals for at least a couple of weeks. But I obviously wasn’t that stupid, he was obviously offering that much because it was probably something involving nudity or sex. At least, that’s what I thought at the time. 

“This isn’t a porn shoot is it?” I remember saying bluntly. Jeremy’s lip twitched at that, almost as if he was offended at my saying so or at the very least, annoyed. But that was only an instant before he laughed charismatically. 

“Goodness no! Nothing of the sort!”

“No nudity?” 

“The opposite, in fact my collection is a simple and yet modest ensemble. You will feel even less vulnerable than you do now.”

I remember hating how he had said that last part. Yet, despite it all, I couldn’t help but consider his proposition seriously. I was starting to get really strapped for cash and there was only so much that I was willing to ask of my friends and parents. Besides, I remembered thinking, it would only be until I found a better job. 

“I’ll think about it,” I finally replied. I remember his smile growing even wider, something I still think was inhuman to this day. 

“Excellent. If you decide to accept, the photo shoot will take place at the studio address written on my card. Be sure to get there at 5pm sharp, no earlier and no later. Buzz the gate and I will come out to escort you and remember to not wear any form of makeup.” 

I nodded, rubbing at the edges of the thin card’s material and slipping it into my pocket. I stood up to thank him for the opportunity but within the span of seconds he had vanished. I remember thinking that he must have gone the other way down the hill but I could still smell the stench of his cheap cologne on my walk home.

Now, it's important to mention that I couldn’t afford a car, one of the luxuries stolen from me by staggering student loan debt payments. This resulted in using public transportation most of the time but on the rare occasions that there was no way to get somewhere, I would ask to ride with my roommate Sam. Sam was an eclectic person, somehow built like a bodybuilder and simultaneously volunteering at our local dog shelter. They always lent a helping hand to me and my other roommates at the cost of talking our ears off about their latest niche interest. We still keep in touch but after partially witnessing what happened to me that night, even their friendship for me didn’t hold them back from supporting my move to Oregon wholeheartedly. 

The topic of that night was cults, the universe's idea of irony. I remember them talking about the Love Has Won cult for a majority of the 30 minute trip to Jeremy’s studio. Sam kept on detailing their philosophy on aliens and how the leader's skin had turned blue. I remembered hearing about the case shortly prior, but I let them talk about it anyway. It was intriguing to hear about how Sam thought about theological higher powers. 

“I think it’s all just a puppet show for some being we have no concept of quantifying,” I remember them saying. “I mean, sure there's some balance to things, but that’s only because this thing likes seeing how that notion fucks with people. How it can make us think that justice is real and that truly good people exist. That the good people get what they deserve and the bad people get what' s coming to em’ y’know? Hell, it might even like the bad people more. Those are the people that have an easy time convincing themselves they’re gods.”

“Jesus Sam, that’s a bit nihilistic.”

They shrugged.

“It’s more realistic to me than anything else.”

When we pulled up to the studio the sun was still low on the horizon. It was a gorgeous view when I stepped out of the car, the warm light seeping over the city and the ocean blue in the distance. It was that time of the year where the air smelled of rain no matter what the weather was and the earth bloomed into the senses. You could feel it best at dusk, the perfect time of day.   
I checked the dashboard clock to make sure it was 5pm sharp and hummed seeing that it was just a few minutes before. Leaning over the windowsill, I looked Sam over. 

“Are you sure you’re okay with staying out here for this long?” I asked.

“Dude, I was the one that suggested it and besides, if the guy tries to murder you, you’ll be dead meat if you don’t have a getaway driver,” Sam said with a more serious tone than I’d hoped. 

“Let’s hope he’s not that type of weird.”

“Diane, if he is any type of weird, you call me. You don’t hesitate, you don’t doubt, if he pulls anything, you call me and I get you the fuck out of there.”

Sam had their stern moments but they were few and far between. I swallowed hard and pushed up from the windowsill. 

“Okay. Well, the guy seemed pretty insistent about the time so I guess I better get going.”

Sam nodded and I turned toward the direction of the studio. They had parked down the driveway to be at least somewhat inconspicuous, so I had to walk a short ways. Once I reached the metal gate I finally got a good look at the studio. It looked like a standard mini-mansion with the sleek, modern exterior. I hated those types of houses. Clean, clinical, hollow. To me they just looked like white boxes stacked on top of each other, with boring box windows leading to boring lives. Yet, the boring lives were filled with money, something I lived upon seeing the next bill or cent of.

I gently pressed the buzzer, listening to its chime before looking up at the security camera lining the gate. I considered awkwardly smiling but thought better of it. There was a subtle click and then the metal gates opened slowly into the driveway. I understood that it was my time to enter and slowly walked toward the house. It was strange to see that the yard outside was rather overgrown with weeds and tall grass. The upstairs lights were on and out of the corner of my eye I saw someone silhouetted in its light. I could make out a woman, not too much older than I was, with blonde hair down to her thighs and a long face. She wore a sheer white gown that reached all the way down to her feet. She stared at me curiously, appearing to acknowledge my presence with a small smile. I almost raised my hand to wave but just as I was about to Jeremy opened the door. 

“I am so glad you could make it! Thank you for your timeliness and remembering my instructions,” he said with a wide smile. Even in the low dusk light I could see the shine off of his skin and teeth. 

“Don’t mention it,” I replied with a weak smile. Jeremy ushered me with an outstretched hand into the house entrance. When I looked back to the upstairs window, the woman was gone. 

As I entered the studio I noticed Jeremy was wearing a dark red suit with a black undershirt. He still had his sunglasses on despite the time of day. Without hesitation he politely instructed me where the bathroom was and that my change of clothes would be waiting for me. I remember nodding a lot, trying not to become distracted by how dark the rest of the house was outside of the foyer. I could see a modern kitchen on the left and a dull sitting area on the right with a large flat screen television. The staircase, which I assumed led to the main studio area, was illuminated by a cold hanging chandelier composed of suspended ball bulbs. I shivered just looking at it, as it reminded me of a bacterial cluster despite looking so sterile.

The bathroom wasn’t much better, reminding of the oppressive lights of working in retail during high school. I could almost feel the buzz of the bulb's electricity circuit through my brain as I pulled on the dress. It was almost identical to the one I saw the woman wearing upstairs except it had a creamier color. I glanced at myself in the mirror. Somehow I looked more elegant than I had felt in a long time. The double chin I had suddenly seemed beautiful, the split ends in my long red hair were now even, and even the surgery scar on my chest glowed in the light. It was strange yet empowering. Sure I had felt euphoric before, but this was deeper. More instinctive. The hunger to emerge, despite not knowing what that meant.

The steps I took out of the bathroom felt like I was floating. It didn’t feel good but it didn’t feel bad either. It was confusing, like waking up with a light hangover. Like the first time hearing music underwater. Somehow Jeremy’s tightened smile looked more human than it ever had and the perspiration on his skin shined like streetlights under the sheen of tears. Looking back, I’m convinced that I was drugged. That’s the only logical explanation to why I felt so docile after hyping myself up with adrenaline filled anxiety. There must have been something in the air of that bathroom. I’d heard of gas drugs before but they were expensive. Though, I suppose that makes sense considering how loaded Jeremy seemed. He put a gentle hand on my shoulder that I’m surprised I didn’t lean away from, and guided me upstairs. At the edge of the stairs, the corridor was dark. Three rooms lined the hallway, two on the side in front of me and one on the opposite side. Only the door we stood in front of had light seeping through the bottom. It was the same light that I had seen through the upstairs window outside. 

But as we entered the room I realized that didn’t make sense. It couldn’t make sense. This room had no windows and yet it seemed to take up the entire second floor. There was no lamp or overhead light to create such a warm glow, and yet it radiated from every surface. Ten women stood in front of me, each standing eerily still. As if they were waiting for something grand, a beautiful prize sparking smiles to grow on their faces. Instinctively, I felt myself smiling too. They stood in two rows of five all staring at the deep red curtain that lined the back wall. It looked like the curtains at the Chinese Theater, the place I had seen *The Favorite* premiere when I first came to Los Angeles. At that age I was obsessed with art films like that, and to see it at such an opulent theater was like walking into a dream. 

“Our last model has arrived,” Jeremy announced behind me. I didn’t look back, but I wish I had. “Let us begin the shoot.”

With a clap of his hands I had somehow reached my place in one of the lines. One of the women stood at the very front now, taking her place as the 11th model outside of the uniformity. It was her, the woman I had seen through the window just minutes before. She was standing with her back toward me but I could see her long blonde hair tremble. 

“All await the captured eye. With shutter bulb glowing bright. Only her will can make it right. To bathe the world in rose-colored light. She shatters screen of fantasy. For our will so mote it be,” he continued, his words absorbed into the walls like blood in cloth. I remember not caring how nonsensical his words were, everything felt so far away. Suddenly, I didn’t feel his presence behind me, like a hollow space in the air. But I couldn’t move. Not even my head. Not even my mouth. I couldn’t stop eagerly smiling. 

The room was silent for many moments and the curtain was deathly still. A growing panic was emerging within my paralyzed body. Unlike the lone model, I couldn’t even shake, but I imagine she had been in this state for longer than I had. Perhaps some of her autonomy was coming back. I tried with all my strength to move my right hand into the underwear belt where my phone was tucked, but not even my finger would budge. The only thing about my body that seemed to obey me was my rapidly beating heart, almost bursting out of my chest with panic. 

I didn’t have long to sort my frantic thoughts before a loud noise was heard. It sounded like a camera shutter combined with a light bulb blowing out. I could imagine none of us saw what resulted in the noise at first, until the blonde-haired woman's head fell off of her shoulders in a burst of blood. It descended to the floor so gracefully that I didn’t think it was real at first. Her body fell in the same way, like a pile of flowers. I could hear the women in the front rows whimper at first but then those horrified exclamations turned into a sickening laughter. A laughter I found emanating from my own throat. I begged my body to stop. To scream, to cry, to do something that wasn’t exalting at this woman’s death. But I couldn’t. I just couldn’t stop laughing. 

Our mockery only lasted for a few moments, but it made my throat sore. We all stopped simultaneously as a resounding clap was heard. Instead of from behind me it sounded from everywhere, piercing as a whistle. I had moved positions in the line but this time I was in a third line of three. The model who stood alone this time had her hair cropped short to her scalp in a buzzcut. Her brown skin had begun to drip with sweat and the bottom of her dress began to stain with the blood of the other women. Before the shutter sound resounded again one of the women in the first line bent her hand in some effort to save the lone model before it snapped back to her side. It was in vain, before our eyes she was cleanly bisected at the torso. This time I could hear a slight moan come from her throat before she shuddered dead. We all began to laugh again. 

I felt sick, as if I were inside my own stomach trying to claw my way out. This process repeated until I was the only one left. Clap. Two rows of four. Click. The pile of bodies grew higher. Clap. a circle of seven. Click. The blood had reached my feet. One of the women had managed a scream before clamping her teeth down on her tongue. Clap. Two rows of three. Click. There was a large splatter on my dress. I could almost reach my phone, but as if pulled by a magnet, my hand would go back to its side every time. Almost all of us had begun shaking. A circle of five. Two rows of two. A triangle of three. A row of two. A person called me. Alone in the room. The blood and viscera went up to my heels and covered my dress until it was stained a deep red. There would be no one to laugh when I died.

I knew I could move when I started crying. I don’t know how or why it happened, but my hands, feet, arms, and legs were now under my control alone. The feeling had been gradual but once full control was back it felt as if a rubber band had snapped. My body was shaking violently all over and now that my feet were no longer balanced, I slipped on the warm pool of blood. Everything became less numb and looking into the eyes of the dead, glassy and blank, I had to stifle a gag. I began to hyperventilate, scuttling back away from the blood and gore. Whatever was killing the models seemed just as confused as I was since there was no noise in the room other than my heaving breaths. But it was not scared, that much I could sense. I frantically turned my head to the direction of the door but where I had entered was gone. Only the blank, glowing walls. I was trapped, and the tears began to flow violently. 

“Fuck. No,” I sobbed before remembering my phone in the band of my underwear. I finally lifted my dress over my stomach and swiped at my phone screen with bloodied fingers leaving smears of red. It was immediately clear that there was no service here, wherever here was. I cursed again and screamed futilely, throwing my phone into the puddle of blood in frustration. Once I saw the curtain in front me shift for the first time, the fear tightened my heart in my chest once more. 

It was the first time I had realized there was a split between the cloth, dividing the curtain into two instead of just one long curtain. Whatever had been behind it was now pulling one side of the curtain back. Now this is the part of the story that made the police's faces turn sour. I knew they had stopped believing any of this was real when I described the inexplicable severing, but when I described what happened next, they seemed more inclined to believe my theory that I was on a hallucinogenic drug. Sure the drug theory could account for why I couldn’t move but I know what I saw was real. I know when I saw its bony hand enter the light I wasn’t hallucinating. It was as certain as knowing if you were drunk or about to throw up. I knew what I was seeing was real. Which is what made it all the more terrifying when I saw its face.

Its hand alone was bigger than the average person as it plucked up one of the women who had been bisected vertically. The nails on its fingers were green and rotting. Its arm was long and its skin was mummified, aged, and thin. Even then, I could make out tattoos. Letters of a language I had never seen before, not even in my years of academic research. But it was its face, its face that made me know that, no matter if the thing tried to attack me or not, I had to get out of that place. The face wasn’t there but instead revealed exposed organ tissue and socket, cut cleanly as if by a laser. This reached down to the lower area of the mouth where a human jaw lay intact but instead of a normal lower jaw there was the lower jaw of a cow. Exposed in white bone. As it lifted the carcass high, its jaw distended unnaturally, revealing a tunnel of teeth that was its throat. Each row clicked like the shutter I had heard so many times prior and as it did so the body was diced into more and more pieces, each falling into the creature's esophagus and visibly moving into its stomach. 

My stomach turned, and the need to vomit was so strong that I couldn’t help but give in. The bile mixed with the blood into an unpleasant orange sludge. I stared at it for a long while, trembling all over in fear and squeezing my eyes shut. I had to get out of that place. I had to find a way or I was going to die. As if by divine insight, I remembered the blonde woman in the window. How could she have stared out at me before in this place? What if the window was still there? I gathered myself to my feet, still breathing heavily. If this didn’t work, I would be stuck here. If I didn’t try, I would be stuck here too. Without hesitation, and with all the fear, I ran toward the left side of the room. I remember slipping many times before I reached the wall, but each time I did I went faster. Harder. Crying out for each and every one of the women that had been silenced.

There was a crash, a shattering of glass. The next moment I was free falling into the night, the overgrown grass of the studio yard cushioning my fall somewhat. I still ended up with my right leg broken and my left arm twisted, but somehow, through the pain, I was able to run. Luckily the gate to the house was still open and I could limp down the dark street to Sam. To safety. To home.

After I spent the night at the hospital the police came to gather my statement. They said that Sam had told them the address of the house on our way to the hospital and they had gone to investigate. The upstairs window was broken which corroborated my story but there had been no sign of anyone being in the house. No bodies. No blood. Not even my discarded phone. The house had been abandoned for years and there had been no records of a Mr. Kitsch, or anyone for that matter, buying or leasing the property. Later they had the blood on my dress tested and confirmed that it had been from multiple missing women in the area. Their bodies have still not been found. They still don’t know how I escaped but Sam says because whatever that thing was got greedy and couldn’t hold control over me long enough to kill me. 

After my physical recovery I made the effort to leave Los Angeles. Once I secured an archival job in Oregon, I made the preparations to move to a small house with plenty of roommates. After that night, it was hard to be alone. Luckily my trauma therapist has helped me through that gradually. My boyfriend and I met about 4 months ago. It was hard to be vulnerable with him at first but I eventually told him everything last week. It felt nice to be believed. Nice to be heard. He’s a writer so I also have him to thank for helping me make this story into something at least slightly palatable. He also helped me come up with fake names for everyone so that was reassuring. Though, we didn’t feel the need to fake a name for Jeremy. Both of us know that by now he is somewhere else, with a different name and maybe even a different face.
