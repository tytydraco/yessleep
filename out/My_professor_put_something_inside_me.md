It was 8am Monday morning. Comparative physiology. If you’ve been there, you know how much of a slog it can be.



Everyone was either asleep or in a clouded haze. The professor was droning on about sleep cycles in mammals and how they adjust to different light sources across seasons. Then he said something different.



“As we all know, the human brain, unlike those of other primates, can function at homeostasis with only 30 minutes of sleep a night, on average,” he said, his flat expression denying the absurdity of the claim.



I looked up in confusion. Was he making a joke? His face and body language looked like that of a mortician at the end of a 12-hour shift. I looked higher at the projected slides on the board.



An intricate diagram of the nervous system in the human head was displayed. A complex map of intersecting lines with arrows pointing at different parts was overlaid above the brain and eyes. I didn’t understand what this meant. It didn’t really look like any diagram I had seen before and his statement was odd.



Before I could get a better look, he clicked his remote and moved on to the next slide. It had nothing to do with what I had just seen.



I looked around at the classmates next to me and saw no one batting an eye.



Thinking I was probably just misunderstanding the class material, I approached the professor after class.



“Professor Davis, can you explain that diagram again? The one with the human brain? Something about sleeping?”



“Uh, which slide do you mean? We didn’t cover anything about human sleep behaviors. It was just about different species of bears.” He looked at me, his brow furrowed.



“What about that one slide? With the brain? You said something about how long humans sleep for. Right?” 



“No, I’m not sure what you mean. There definitely wasn’t anything like that today. Maybe you were dreaming!” he chuckled at himself. 



“Yeah… maybe.” I gave a fake laugh. “I guess I'll just check the notes again when I get home.”



He laughed again as I walked out of the lecture hall. 



Later that day, when I got back to my dorm, I was quick to open my laptop and check the powerpoint. I studied every slide carefully, and found nothing. It really was all about bears. Had I just fallen asleep? Dreamt it?



I could’ve sworn it happened. That diagram was off. That network of lines over the brain. What was it?



Although slightly shaken, I tried to dismiss it.



But that very night, I felt it. Something inside my brain. My skull was a hard-boiled egg being peeled. I felt a mild itch beneath the skin on my scalp that I couldn’t relieve.  And it kept me up. I stared at the ceiling for hours, trying to just shut my brain off and fall asleep. But I just couldn’t.



By 6am, I gave up. I sat up in bed and rubbed my eyes. My body felt so tired. But my brain just wouldn’t stop moving. I got up and dressed myself for the day.



Despite feeling tired the whole day, Tuesday went pretty normal. My body was eager to lay in bed again but my mind was dreading it. I was already convinced that it had something to do with that class. And the next lecture was in the morning at 8am.



Another restless night.



When I finished pretending to sleep, I nervously slogged myself into clothing and walked to comparative physiology. 



The lecture was covering adaptations mammals in humid environments make to better survive and work their bodies more efficiently. It was all normal for the first few minutes.



“Of course, the human body tends to blink less when the individual is adapted for equatorial, humid environments. Like here.” He looked around, briefly meeting my gaze. “The eye is able to function with practically no eyelids whatsoever, actually.” 



My eyes darted to the slide but he clicked away too fast. I couldn’t see it. I stared intensely at the screen, a pit in my stomach forming. What was there? I paid close attention to the notes.



Whatever it was, the effects of my insomnia took precedent. My eyes felt heavy, almost like they were burning. I realized then that I hadn’t blinked since I looked up. When was that?



I checked my watch. *8:46*.



That *couldn’t* be right. I must have been staring straight at the screen for at least… 30 minutes? My eyes began to sting intensely.



When I tried to will my eyelids shut, nothing happened. The usual comfort of darkness wouldn’t appear. I felt tears beginning to form around the edges. 



I turned to the guy sitting next to me. He was asleep. I grabbed his shoulder and shook him.



“Hey man, did you see that slide? About the human eye?” The desperation in my voice leaked through more than I intended.



“W-what? I wasn’t really paying attention.” He put his head back down.



I was starting to feel crazy. Maybe I was? 



No. There's no way. 



My eyes burned something fierce. I waited out the clock. When 8:50 struck, I promptly stood up from my desk and marched to the front of the room on struggling feet.



“Professor Davis. How long does a human being need to sleep?” My tired voice was cracking.



“Um, I’m not sure what you mean. Eight hours a night?” He smiled briefly, fading fast when he looked into my eyes. “If you’re struggling about the upcoming midterm, you can always come to my office hours.”



“No… I, I, just that thing you said today. About blinking? The eye? Humidity? Something?” I stared at him, gripping his desk to support my aching body.



He frowned. “Are you feeling alright? You look sick. You know you can go to the student health–” 



I turned and walked away while he was still speaking. He wasn’t going to help me.



It was unbearable by the time I got home for the evening. My eyes felt like molten metal was being poured on them. By then the tears had long since dried up. My mind was racing. I didn’t know what to do.



Davis had something to do with it. I knew it. He was doing something to me. I just wasn’t sure what exactly. 



I spent the rest of the night trying to focus on studying for a different class’s exam I had the next day. Needless to say, it didn’t work out. The next morning, I bombed it. I could hardly hold my pencil straight. My mind was in a dense fog with no end in sight.



The rest of Thursday was no different. If anything, it was just worse. I could tell people were avoiding me now. Diverting eye contact when talking. Staring at me when they thought I wouldn’t realize. 



The day came and went in a grey haze.



8am. Friday. Comparative physiology. 



I was slumped in my desk, barely able to sit without falling over. The lecture was about colonial organisms. The man-o-war. Then he pivoted.



“Just like man-o-war, our bodies are in a close relationship with an organism.” He clicked the remote.



“Filaria habitans,” he said, the new slide glowing above his head. It depicted a full map of the human nervous system alongside a secondary network of tubes and lines that was dispersed across the entire body. It seemed to coalesce into a tumor-esque core in the stomach.



“This organism, made up of 200 miles of tubes, originates within the stomach, when we eat certain foods that contain its eggs. The core of filaria will grow inside the host, contributing to the health of their gut microbiome, until it reaches a certain size.” His pale skin and dead eyes gave him a hollow appearance as he spoke.



I raised my hand.



“Yes?” He pointed to me.



“What are you talking about? What is this? This is *not* real. Am I the only one paying any attention?” I looked around, but no one batted an eye.



He chuckled. “They don’t care. They won’t listen to you.”



“W-what? What's happening? Why can’t I sleep anymore? Why can’t I blink?” Only then did I realize my fingernails were digging into the desk, bleeding.



“It’s inside of you, *right now*. It always has. When it reaches its apex, it will have to *come out*.” He clenched his jaw.



I stared at him in disbelief with my distorted, dry vision.



“Anyways, let's move on,” he said, clicking the remote and going to the next slide. It was back to the man-o-war.



I felt it in my stomach. A mass. The size of a grapefruit. Shifting, groaning. I stood up and walked out of the lecture and went straight home.



I was nauseous by the time I got to my room. The world was starting to spin. The speed with which all this had progressed was too much. I felt like I was going to vomit.



I collapsed to the floor and started to dry heave. The grapefruit in my stomach was shifting up and down, coming closer and closer to my throat. Mucousy spit was trailing out of my mouth, my knuckles white as I gripped the carpet.



I felt my throat expand to its limits and further, burning as the mass tore through. My jaw cracked and snapped as the thing squeezed onto the floor with a wet plop.



It was pale and veiny, roughly ball-shaped. It was covered in these tiny white hairs that extended outwards in all directions. It all shined in my bedroom light, drenched in stomach bile and spit.



I closed my eyes. Relief flooded through in a cool wave and I saw nothing again for the first time in years.



I’m writing this all out after having slept for the past 15 hours straight. It was the best sleep of my life.



Maybe if I hadn’t seen it, none of this would’ve happened.



If you’re a student, maybe it's okay to fall asleep in class sometimes.
