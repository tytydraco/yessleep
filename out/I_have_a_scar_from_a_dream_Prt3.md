It’s been seven days since I said I was going to check on Paul.

Seven days of telling myself I would update when I had something solid. Something concrete. Something that didn’t sound like the ramblings of someone who hasn’t slept properly in almost a week.

I don’t have that.

What I have instead is a feeling that everything around me has thickened. The air in my apartment feels heavier now, like it’s holding onto something. Even the light coming through the blinds in the morning seems duller, less sharp. I sit in the same chair, at the same desk, looking at the same walls, and it all feels slightly rearranged even though nothing has moved.

I tried not sleeping the first night. I thought if I stayed awake long enough, whatever was happening at 5:00 AM wouldn’t have a chance. I made coffee at midnight and again at two. The kitchen light buzzed faintly above me, that cheap electrical hum I’ve always ignored. The fridge clicked on and off. Pipes shifted in the walls. Normal sounds. Small, domestic sounds that used to mean safety.

At some point I must have blinked too long.

When I opened my eyes, I was standing in the hallway.

Barefoot.

The front door was open, and cold air was slipping in around my ankles. The sky outside was that washed-out grey-blue you only see right before sunrise. Not quite night. Not quite morning. The world holding its breath.

I don’t remember unlocking the door. I don’t remember walking there. My feet were coated in sand. Fine, pale sand packed into the creases of my skin and stuck along the sides of my heels. I stared down at it for a long time before I bent and brushed it away with my hand.

I didn’t look at the clock.

I already knew the time.

It was 5:00 AM.

It’s always 5:00 AM.

The second night I moved my bed into the center of the room. I don’t know why I did that. Something about the wall felt wrong. Like turning toward it in my sleep meant giving something my back. I dragged the frame across the floor until it sat like an island in the middle of the room, exposed on all sides. I set up two cameras. One facing the bed. One angled toward the desk and the safe underneath it.

When I watched the footage the next morning, I expected chaos. Thrashing. Sleepwalking in the dramatic way movies show it.

Instead, I watched myself sit up at 4:59 AM as calmly as someone waking from a good dream. I swung my legs over the edge of the bed and stood. My movements were slow and deliberate, almost gentle. I walked to the desk, knelt down, opened the safe, and removed the disk and the photographs.

I laid them out carefully across the surface of the desk. One by one. The desert. The lake. The truck. The boot prints. The ground-level angle.

I picked up the photo of the Wrangler and held it inches from my face. I stared at the eyes in the mirror for a long time. Then I tilted the picture slightly, as if trying to catch a different angle of light, like I was studying a flaw in the print.

And then I smiled.

Not wide. Not exaggerated. Just a small, knowing curve at the corner of my mouth.

I set everything back in the safe, locked it, returned to bed, and lay down on my back with my hands folded over my stomach. At exactly 5:07 AM, I closed my eyes.

I do not remember any of that.

I went back to Paul’s apartment again yesterday.

The eviction notice was still taped to his door, curling at the corners. The date on it made my stomach drop. Over a month ago. I stood there running my thumb over the cheap paper like it might smudge or change if I pressed hard enough.

On my way out of the building, I stopped at the mailboxes without really thinking. I don’t even know why. Habit, maybe. The hallway smelled like cleaning solution, sharper than usual, and the corkboard near the entrance looked different.

Someone had cleared it.

The usual layers of faded yard sale ads and lost pet flyers were gone. The board looked newer somehow. Less cluttered. Like someone had finally decided to tear everything down and start fresh.

There were only a few papers pinned up now.

One of them caught my eye because it was crooked.

Just slightly.

The top corner curling inward.

I stepped closer to straighten it without thinking.

That’s when I saw the photo.

It wasn’t large. It wasn’t dramatic. Cheap ink bleeding at the edges.

But I recognized him immediately.

Paul.

The same uneven part in his hair. The same flat expression he makes when he doesn’t know someone’s taking a picture.

The words MISSING were printed in bold at the top.

The date underneath made my stomach tighten.

Six weeks ago.

I stood there longer than I should have, staring at it like it might rearrange itself into something else if I gave it time.

The bottom of the flyer had those little tear-off strips with a phone number.

Most of them were gone.

I don’t know how long it’s been there.

Maybe it was buried under older notices before.

Maybe I just never had a reason to look.

Maybe I didn’t want to.

I drove to the lake again.

The access road stretched out in front of me, the desert on either side flat and endless, the horizon blurred by heat. The mesa still rose up in the distance like it always has, carved and patient and indifferent.

When I stepped out of the car, the air hit me in the chest. Dry. Metallic. Sun-baked. The kind of heat that settles into your bones and makes everything feel slower.

I walked to the disturbed patch of earth.

It looks smaller now. Less obvious. Like time is smoothing it over, the wind doing quiet work to erase whatever happened.

There are two sets of boot prints leading up to it.

Two.

One set deeper, pressing harder into the sand.

The tread pattern matches mine exactly. I checked. I placed my boot beside one of the impressions and watched the edges line up perfectly.

That doesn’t prove anything.

Lots of boots look the same.

That’s what I told myself as I crouched down and ran my fingers along the faint drag marks leading toward the water. They’re subtle. Almost invisible unless you’re looking for them. Like something heavy was pulled a short distance before being lifted.

I found the butter knife again near the shoreline. Half-buried. The handle warm from sitting in the sun. I picked it up and turned it over in my hand.

It’s such a small, ordinary thing.

I pressed the flat of the blade against my own throat, just enough to feel the cool metal against skin.

Nothing happened.

No flash of memory. No surge of guilt.

Just wind moving across the lake and the distant sound of something shifting in the rocks.

When I close my eyes now, I get fragments.

Not full scenes.

Just sensations.

A raised voice.

Paul’s voice.

Angry. Then afraid.

The truck door open.

Sand under my boots.

The weight of someone pushing back.

The warmth on my hands.

The sound of breath catching.

I open my eyes every time before it goes any further.

I won’t let it.

Last night, I didn’t set up cameras. I didn’t try to fight sleep. I lay flat on my back and stared at the ceiling until the dark softened into early morning.

I don’t know if I slept.

I must have.

Because when I checked the clock, it was 5:08 AM.

My hands were resting on my chest.

There was dried mud under my fingernails again.

I went to the bathroom and scrubbed them until the skin around them turned red.

Then I looked at the scar.

It’s darker now. More defined. Like it’s healing wrong. I traced it with my finger, following the thin line from collarbone to jaw.

For a second, something inside me shifted.

Not a clear memory.

Just the understanding of motion.

Arm forward.

Pressure.

Resistance.

A body not mine, struggling.

I stepped back from the mirror so fast I hit the door behind me.

“No,” I said out loud.

The word sounded small in the tiled room.

There had to be someone else there.

There were two sets of prints.

Two.

I remember that clearly.

Two.

I am not the only one in this story.

I can’t be.

I’m sitting at my desk now. The sky outside is turning that same pale grey-blue.

It’s almost 5:00 AM again.

My hands are steady as I type this.

The footage doesn’t matter.

The prints don’t matter.

The knife doesn’t matter.

The missing poster doesn’t matter.

There had to be someone else there.

It can’t be me
