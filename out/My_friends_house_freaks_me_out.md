As soon as I stepped into Joe’s house, I felt unwelcomed. A force, the wind perhaps, pushed the front door open every time Joe nearly closed it. Bemused, I watched him struggle to close the door.

“It’s been a while, my friend. Thanks for inviting me over.”

The exterior of Joe’s house looked bloated with rot and weather damage, but the inside was as familiar as it’d ever been. Black tea lights crowded most surfaces: window sills, the dining table, the stove top and even the warped floorboards.

Strips of fraying fabric covered in rusty-red sigils were threaded up all over the dimly lit kitchen. The symbols looked like a mishmash of Arabic and Hiragana, but spaced like Thai script. I’d never figured out what was this monstrosity – I just assumed it was some witch doctor things from Joe’s mom.

A thick candle slumped awkwardly on top of mini-fridge on one side of the room. The flame flickered and curl upward, dangerously close to the curtains. I could almost feel the heat, a cozy contrast to the snowy cold outside. “Woah. That’s a fire hazard, isn’t it?”

Joe, who finally shut the front door, took off his jacket. “What’s you talking about?”  

I gestured across the room — then stumbled back, recoiling. I shook my head and rubbed my eyes. My hands stayed in front of my face, defensively. There was no flame, no smoke, no sign that the candle was lit recently. I jerked my head around. Joe was still taking his boots off behind me.

The candle was at least ten feet away from us.

No one else was here.

After some seconds of hesitation, I approached the candle and touched the wick. Ice-cold. Though I could not precise why, I felt unwelcomed. Someone, something, was watching and judging my every move. A presence in this house did not want me here.

As I pondered the feeling, something wrapped around my torso, pulling me with so much force I toppled over. The dense presence pressed in, encasing me from all sides and knocking the wind out of my lungs. My vision swam.

I gasp for air.

“I’m much glad we can hang out,” Joe’s hearty voice boomed next to my ears. “I will kick your ass to the dark side of Pluto where the sun don’t ever shine.”

The smile returned to my lips as I returned the hug and thumped him on the back. “Only in your dreams!”

*Staying up until 5AM to cram for college diagnostics was messing with my head.*

The only part of the room that wasn’t covered in candles, dried herbs or talismans was the front of the fridge, where magnets held up yellowed pictures of a couple. Papa B and Mama B were high school sweethearts. Although the whole town saw the ambulances and police car, Joe was tight-lipped about what happened to his parents.

“Lays or Doritos?”

I grinned when Joe held up two bags. This was how it always went. “I can’t decide…”

Joe beamed and opened both bags in one gesture. “If yo can’t decide, yo git all.”

That’s what Mama B used to say. There was a strange echo in Joe’s words, but I paid it no mind as I invited myself to the pantry and grabbed three more bags. Plus, two packs of cookies. My parents scorned processed food, but Joe’s house had it all. Delightfully, I stuffed my face with chips and cookies.   

“Child, how's bouldering goin?”

I paused on my chomping. Mama B used to call me "child" but it was weird to hear it come from Joe, who was younger than me. And what was up with that weird echo? “Bouldering?”

“Yeah bro, you were hella bragging about going bouldering with an Olympic sportster last week I calling you. Friend of yo rich dad or something.” 

Unease pricked the back of my neck. My head pulled back in confusion. My dad was well-connected, yes, but I’d never gone bouldering in my life. While somewhat athletic, I stuck to sports that didn’t take my feet off the ground.

And I haven’t spoke on the phone with Joe for months.

“Maybe you got me mixed up with someone else?”

“Nah, it was you, bro,” said Joe.  

We headed to his bedroom. After I got into high school, my parents were stricter about my after-school activities and friends. Since I was leaving for an Ivy League next week, Joe and I decided to catch up, *really catch up*, with bottomless soda and an all-nighter on our favorite first-person shooter.

While opening the door to the basement stairs, I heard a metallic *clang*. Joe screeched at me, “Bro!”

The… *display* behind the basement door was not here the last time I went to Joe’s house. With morbid fascination, I observed the taxidermized small creatures, a shrine of bones and rows of metallic bowls holding things I don’t have a name for.

I’d knocked over a bowl holding a veiny egg.  

I moved aside and let Joe scoop the sludge and broken shells back into a vessel. Then he knelt and bowed before the shrine. He murmured a foreign dialect in a soothing voice, petted the various small creatures and picked up rodent skulls to kiss.

The expression on Joe’s face changed.

For a moment, it sounded like Joe was pleading. Begging for permission.

Again, I felt the house urging me to just leave.

“Um…” I tried to ask if there was anything I should do to atone for my carelessness, but before my unease took too strong of a hold on my psyche, Joe put down the skull he was kissing and smiled with relief.

He dabbed himself with some red powder from one of the small bowls and stood up. “Mama gits mad if her space mess up but she good now. We can game.” 

A chill washed over me. The feeling of being unwelcomed returned, so powerful that I felt as if a force was trying to push me out of the house. I was busy with high school and AP classes when Papa B and Mama B killed each other, but I still heard about it.

A bloody affair, right here in this house.

My fingers tightened over the bags of chips and cookies I’d grabbed. Maybe I should cancel the evening and drive somewhere else? But Joe even forgave me for not attending his parent’s funeral and I wouldn’t see him again for at least a year.

Mama B was a witchdoctor, I reminded myself. Don’t judge.

I remembered Joe’s shining smile when I said I could come hang out.

How he’d jumped for joy. Nobody had been so happy to hang out with me before.

We settled in, behind a fort of junk food and sugary pop. We each grabbed a controller.

Despite the claustrophobic set-up of Joe’s basement bedroom, I felt gleeful. My mom would skin me alive if I touched my own controller with cheesy, MSG-laden hands. Actually, she’d give me a dirty look just for touching video games. Joe’s household had no such restrictions.

As we fired at enemy soldiers, we crunched on chips and spoke with our mouth full. Joe unleashed a series of perfect headshots. “College, bro. That’s huge. Sometimes, I wish I was free…”   

I landed some headshots of my own. I wasn’t allowed to game much, so Joe had a clear advantage and his score was almost doubled of mine already. “You can still apply. Just redo your last year of high school and apply to state uni.”

Something hissed into my ears. I shrugged away the cold fingers biting into my shoulder, thinking that it was just in my imagination. Adrenaline surged. There was no way I could beat Joe, but I didn’t want a fraction of his score.

“Nah, yo don’t get it,” said Joe. He easily stole my next kills. “Me mama…”

“A college degree will open so many doors for you,” I insisted. I pocketed some explosives and calculated a proper time to use them. “Remember how you scored perfect scores on all your tests in elementary school? You’re smart and don’t need to waste all your potential in this dead county.”

A wistful smile spread over Joe’s face. “Me potential…”

There was an explosive, chiming *crack*, as if someone had just shattered something fragile, porcelain or glass, on the floor tiles above us. The sound did not come from our game. My heart started to beat faster. We were alone in the house, weren’t we? I turned my face up, “What was that?”

I distinctly remembered closing the bedroom door, but the door was wide open now.

*Leave*. Now.

I stopped mashing buttons on my controller. My other friends were out of town. Mom and dad wanted the house to themselves tonight. I could get a hotel room, but how was I supposed to tell Joe that I wanted to ditch him because his house freaked me out?

“Some birds chirping real loud.” Joe shrugged, looking completely unbothered. His score was now triple of mine, and he looked so happy. “I already got meself one real job. Like me mama say, I don’t need no college degree.”

That weird echo in Joe’s voice again.

I took a deep breath and tried to focus on my aim. I’d wanted to bag groceries when I was a freshman in high school but my father told me it wouldn’t build my resume. “Don’t you want to see a real city though? Join a respectable field and develop some skills. Contribute to society and make a salary. Help people.”

The room went completely dark and silent.

The low hum of the console, the music from the map and the lights from the halogen bulbs disappeared in an instant. I heard that your other senses heightened when you lose one of them. While I couldn’t see anything in the pitch darkness, I could suddenly taste something coppery in the air. Something earthly with a faint trace of rot hovered near me.

My fingers curled around the controller. The basement stairs creaked and there was a soft slithering sound. Air displaced around me. A weight settled on the couch next to me, making the springs underneath the cushion screech. The dip caused by whatever heavy life form settled down next to me grew, pulling me slightly towards the right side of the couch.  

I held myself completely still, bracing for impact, but nothing touched me.

“Blackout. Always going on in this streets,” Joe said apologetically. His voice sounded far away.  “Hope you not too bothered.”

I wanted to argue that a blackout shouldn’t cause the candles in his bedroom to flicker wildly and go out, but the electricity came back just as quickly. And, of course, the candles never went out. Light flooded the room with a rushing sound of every faucet in the house turned to the max.

I whipped my head to my right, where Joe had made himself comfortable.

Now he rolled his eyes and stood up again.

As Joe then explained something about faulty pipes and went to turn off the faucets, I calculated how quickly I could get out of the house. Six seconds for the fourteen flights of stairs and five seconds to cross the kitchen above.

But what could I say that wouldn’t make Joe think I was leaving because I didn’t like him?

It was just one night. A sleepover, like when we were kids and Mama B would come tuck us in.

What could go wrong?

*Scrape.*

I don’t know when I fell asleep, but hours of gaming and a full belly of take-out did me in. The television was off now, behind the open bags of junk food and crusty controllers. A nightlight dimly lit up the cramp bedroom. I was in Joe’s bed and Joe was snoring away in a sleeping bag on the ground.

I needed to use the bathroom, but something made me uneasy.

A shuffling sound.

I knew there were no windows behind the window curtains in the basement, but they shifted, moved by non-existent wind. I repeated to myself that there were no eyes peeking at me from behind the curtains. Damn. I’d hoped to sleep through the night and wake up at a perfectly normal hour to hightail out of this house and never come back.

*Scrape*

The bedroom door slid open.

Right before my very eyes, the bedroom door opened on its own. A steady, controlled turn of a wooden door on a wooden frame. What the hell was I even supposed to try to do here, against whatever was coming through the door?

At first, it was just blurriness, a warp in our three-dimensional world. Then I saw the bones – misaligned bones suspended by the sequence of distorted space. A bead necklace that I remembered Mama B used to wear hung in the mass. The negative space that formed her mouth stretched wide and thin with fury.

*Scrape*

Nothing had ever looked at me with such hatred.

Oh, the rage this *something* emits. Her presence, so violently unnatural that reality bent itself to accommodate it, seethed. Immediately, I knew that she was the source of the uninviting presence I’d felt the whole day. Why was she so angry? What did I do?

*Scrape.*

A knife dragged along the floor. Painstakingly slow, as if the fabric between the real world and the spiritual world was not an easy one for this something to bend. With each *scrape*, the knife was dragged an inch closer towards me.

She was a couple of feet inside the room now and the space she’d occupied was distorted, as if her presence broke all the rules of physics that kept us safe and grounded. I calculated my options. If she needed a physical knife, did that mean I could run into the space behind her and exit the basement?

Could I bring myself to run closer to her, though?

*Scrape.*

The shape of the distortion tears itself apart with twitching spasms and drags itself back together in jagged intervals, beckoning me to try. I managed to croak out, “Joe…”

I made an attempt to push myself off the bed, but something held me motionless. I looked down. Inches of wax clung to my skin in thick heavy folds. All the candles in the house had melted into a singular mass encasing my body up to my neck. I strained and scratched with my fingernails, but the wax, cool to touch, pressed back, resisting even the shallow expansion of my chest.

*Scrape.*

How could I free myself from this cocoon of hardened wax?

If I woke Joe up, could he protect me from his dead mom? Could he stop this something? I remembered the egg I broke. I’d swear it on my life, if I could keep it, that I would never disturb her shrine again. Heck, I’d even give Joe that Nintendo DS he’d wanted for ten years.

*Scrape.*

My eyes darted to the ground. Joe was still in his sleeping bag. His eyes were half-opened, but snores reverberated from the dark hollow space of his mouth. Now was not the time to ponder if the absence of half his teeth was related to a jar of teeth I found in his bathroom earlier. “Joe…”

Wax tightened on my throat, choking my breath out. The something did not want me to make any sounds.

*BANG.*

The thunderous banging came from above my head. Approximately where the front door was. The something, whose knife was inches away from taking my life, *howled*. Her knife fell to the ground with a soft clatter. Her icy breath scratched my face, peeled back my lips, scraped off the surface of my teeth and dug into my eye sockets.

A rancid stench pierced my nostrils and I felt liquid drip down to my lips.

My teeth rattled in my gums, giving away with a slick *click* when my tongue slid over them.  

Without needing to manipulate a physical object, the something was fast. She dashed away from me, out of the room, in a matter of seconds. The room looked normal again. My throat finally worked. “Joe! Help me!”

*BANG. BANG. BANG.*

Loud crashes shattered the night, each more thunderous than the last. Joe, who’d somehow managed to sleep peacefully through the entirety of his dead mom trying to slit my throat, suddenly sat up with fear in his eyes. “Papa’s home.”

I coughed, trying to get the aftertaste of her breath out of my system and spat out blood. I thrashed, throwing a pillow off the bed. Pillow? I was in a normal bed again.

Bed. Mattress. Pillow. Blanket.

No wax, except for what I accumulated under my nails.

I rubbed my face and my palm came away wet. Fluid continued to trickle from my eyes, my nose and my ears. My phone, along with the keys for my car, were upstairs. Upstairs, where distinctive sounds of ghoulish combat jumped from one corner of the house to another.

The entire house shook with the force of the two somethings fighting one another.

After spitting out another mouthful of blood, I whispered urgently, “Joe, we gotta go.”

Joe stared back at me blankly. “Mama could have helped him with his business again, but Papa didn’t *believe*. Papa tried to take me to gram’s.”

I instantly clamped my mouth shut.

The watchful, disapproving, presence. The weird stuff that happened whenever I encouraged Joe to pursue his dreams. Whether it was to finish his last year of high school, go to college or be a medical doctor. The something was so angry at me because, just like her husband, I tried to take her child away from this house.

The cacophony upstairs continued. Hollow, concussive thuds in between ghostly wails.

The custody battle that continued after death.  

Joe appeared perfectly at home right where he was.

I threw the blanket off me and bolted to the basement door, jerking it open. I ran up the stairs, taking them two at a time and kicking down the dried carcasses of small animals in Mama B’s shrine. I paused to tear my jacket off the hook.

The somethings were fighting each other in the kitchen. Candles all over the house lit and snuffed with no patterns. The glass windows bent for a split second before snapping back into place. Nothing looked solid anymore.

The door was on the other side of the kitchen.

When crashes sounded from another corner of the house, I took my chances.

Something clawed my back and shoulders open and I felt blood slide down my legs, but I already had the front door thrown open. I propelled myself forward, into the dead of the night. The front yard shook from the impacts warping the house.

My car was right there. I fumbled pulling the key fob out of my jacket but didn’t drop it.

From the house, I can hear Joe cackling. “Bro, yo ass peed me bed! What you did, you peed me fucking bed! No wonder yo ass dashin—”

An accusation like that would normally hit differently. Right now, I had no capacity to care. Once my seatbelt was fastened, I yelled at my steering wheel. “Drive home!”

The car started.

Once I was a good distance away and on a familiar highway, surrounded by other cars, my heart rate started to settle down. Pain built up in a steady thrum. My teeth still felt loose, but the bleeding from my orifices stopped. The gash on my arm bled freely. I gingerly touched it, feeling something more than skin, and winced.

My brows furrowed in confusion.

Gritting my teeth against the pain, I dug into my flesh, feeling around until my nails closed around something solid. I tugged the intrusion out of my body, scowling at the additional blood that spilled, and uncrumpled the macabre fragment. My brain struggled to process the new information.

“Hey Google..”

The internet responded to my query quickly. Decades ago, Papa B and Mama B had a set of twins who died exactly at the age of one. Papa B’s business flourished after the death of the twins but trickled into a halt after the birth of Joe. By the time Joe was a teenager, the B family was impoverished. *Mama could have helped him with his business again, but Papa didn’t believe. Papa tried to take me to gram’s.*

I stared down at the old photograph of Mama B in a hospital bed holding a pair of newborn twins. Her eyes, looking straight at the camera, were unhappy and dead. Full of hatred and rage, just like the something that tried to kill me. 

Originally, I thought everything happened because even being dead couldn’t stop Mama B from trapping Joe in generational poverty. But was it worse than that? The way Joe continued Mama B’s rituals. The unworldly echo whenever Joe said things that Mama B used to say... or, would have said. 

Joe… what was going to happen to Joe?
