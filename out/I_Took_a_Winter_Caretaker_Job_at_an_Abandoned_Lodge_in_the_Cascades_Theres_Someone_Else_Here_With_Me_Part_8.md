\------

He said my name again, as if to test the mouthfeel. “Ethan Vale.” This time, it was less a greeting than the start of an entry in a logbook, an old habit at the surface even now. He leaned back against the wall, boots flat on the corridor floor, and waited, perhaps to see if I would move. I didn’t. I kept my back to the opposite wall, legs awkward and braced, every nerve aware of the thirteen steps and the door at my shoulder and the long, uninterrupted line between my body and his.

He took my lack of movement as permission. He drew in a slow breath — so quiet I almost missed it — and began, “I’ve lived here for two years.” No preamble, no softening. His eyes never left mine.

I said nothing.

“It’s the longest I’ve spent anywhere in my life. I picked this place because there wasn’t another building within six miles, and because I just...wanted to get away. To hide." He tapped the wall behind him, then let his hand fall limp over his knee. "When the wind drops and the boiler’s off, you can hear every part of yourself that you’ve tried not to know about. At first, I thought the silence was enough. It isn’t. It turns out you can make a vacuum so complete that it pulls everything else in, and then the only thing in the room with you is the thing you thought you’d escaped.”

He gave me a small, tired smile. “I mean, you know that already, I assume.”

Still I said nothing.

He waited to see if I would. Then he went on. “I started following you about a month after the…after Sarah. My sister.” No hesitation at the name, though it seemed to take an effort. "Don't worry," he said as he noticed my face tense up. “I didn't hide in any alleys or peer through any of your windows. I had an internet connection, as everyone does. I listened to your podcast, the interviews, the Reddit Q&As, and your Twitter account." He allowed himself a low, gruff laugh that held no humor. "You never even tried to hide.”

I flexed my fingers into a fist, squeezing so hard the nails were cutting into my palm, and forced myself to relax.

He continued, “I got to know your rhythms. Your favorite coffee place. The days you went to the hardware store, which always seemed to fall on Mondays or Thursdays, depending on how hungover you were."

I winced. After the podcast fell apart, the bars in the city were the only form of solace I could find. People may hate you, but the bottle loves all who drink from it.

His voice was calm, almost distant. Like he was reading a report. “I built a file. I used spreadsheets. I didn't have a plan, but it was easier to reduce you to a set of cells and columns than to think about the forty-seven seconds you never stopped playing.”

I felt a new pulse of heat under my skin, but I didn’t move.

“The day you said yes to the caretaker job, I was watching from the end of the block. You put the manila envelope in your bag and checked twice that no one had swapped it out, even though you knew I couldn’t have. The level of care was almost touching.”

He laughed again, a brittle, unamused sound. “I left the keys to the truck in the ignition the morning you got here. I wanted to see if you would drive away, just for the hell of it. You never even checked the glove box. You just…walked in, set your bag down, and went straight to the logbook.”

My throat felt thick, and the cold in the hallway bit harder with every word. I realized I was leaning forward, just slightly, like an animal caught between freeze and bolt. He had *seen* me come in on that first day?

“I watched you work. Logging boiler pressures, sweeping the corridor, and rearranging the woodpile in the shed. I wrote the second list of chores when it seemed like the first one was getting too easy. I moved your tools by one peg, just to see how long it would take you to put them back. Thirty seconds, usually.” He raised his eyebrows, faintly impressed.

Now he looked down, not at the floor, but at his own hands. They were callused and raw at the knuckles, as if he’d spent a week splitting kindling with no gloves. “I kept expecting to feel…something. Satisfaction. Triumph. Closure, I guess. But all I ever felt was colder. Empty.”

He met my eyes again. “You can say something, if you want.”

I couldn’t. I wouldn’t.

He shrugged, as if that made sense to him.

He told me about the hardware store listing, how he’d written and printed it himself, then posted it at midnight because he knew that was when the cleaning crew ran the lobby and would leave it up, undisturbed, for at least a day. “You were the only one who replied in handwriting,” he said. “All the others were emails, or DMs, or comments in the thread. Yours was a letter, and it didn’t even try to sell yourself. It just said you needed silence, and you would do any work that came with it.” He shook his head, as if this detail still surprised him.

He recited every step of the plan: the envelope, the key, the contract, the hand-drawn map. The warnings about the road. The note telling me to leave my recorders behind, which, he said, “I figured you’d ignore, but you didn’t. You put them in storage. I checked.” He seemed pleased, but not in a vindictive way. More like a craftsman noticing a joinery that had come together without glue.

“The hard part,” he said, “was the waiting. Once you were inside, once the snow closed the road and the boiler started up, I had to decide how much to do. Whether to just let you sit here until it broke you, or whether to…intervene.” He didn’t say the word haunt, though it hovered between us, unsaid.

“At first, it was small things. Lists. Moving objects. The photograph at the foot of the stairs. But nothing worked. You’d just fix the list, reset the tools — you filed the photo away like it was a tax return." For a moment, a bit of quiet anger flared in his voice, but he quickly suppressed it. "For a while I thought you might outlast me.” There was a kind of reluctant admiration in the way he said it. “But then you started writing in the logbook. Not the first week. Not even the second. But by the end, it was all there. The real stuff. The things you didn’t put on air, or in your apology tour, or in any of the interviews.

“I read every page,” he said. “I annotated a few. But mostly I just waited for you to admit to yourself what I already knew.”

I found my voice, if only for one syllable: “What?”

He didn’t miss a beat. “That you were sorry for what happened to your career. You were sorry for the discomfort, for the inconvenience, for the awkwardness of having to give interviews and go on leave and eat shit in every comment section from here to the sun. You were sorry for all of it. But you were never sorry for her.”

The corridor, already cold, became glacial.

He didn’t look away. His voice, if there was ever a hint of menace before, was on full display now. “You called her a mistake. An accident. The 'way things unfolded.' But you *never* said her name until tonight, and even then, only in the context of what it had cost you. Not her. Not anyone else. Just *you*.”

For a long time, nothing happened. There was no sound from the woodstove or movement in the corridor. Not even a twitch of muscle in either of us. I opened my mouth to say something, but the only thing that came out was the white vapor of my own breath. I felt my jaw tighten, then unlock. I tried to conjure a response, but every word I thought of was immediately disqualified by the part of my mind that had edited podcasts for a living. Nothing was true. Nothing would help. There was no phrase, apology, or rhetorical trick that could take the weight out of what he’d said. So I sat there and let the accusation be what it was. The raw fact at the center of the last three years.

The machinery in my head, which had, for weeks, been running diagnostic after diagnostic on every sound, every footstep, every cold spot and flicker of light, finally shut off. In its place was only the specific gravity of what he had said. Not a punishment — just the truth, left in the center of the room like a stone in a bowl.

He didn’t say anything else. He just sat there, cross-legged, the lines in his face carved deeper by the firelight, and waited to see what I would do next.

For the first time since Sarah Harrow’s last call, I had nothing to say.

\------

The corridor held its silence for a long time, and the longer it held, the more complete it became — a kind of mutual armistice, a border at which both sides could stare each other down and decide if there would be a next move. My mouth was open just enough to dry out the tongue. I watched the way the lamp’s shadow edged the stubble of Daniel’s jaw.

He let the silence expand to its breaking point, then did something that caught me completely: he took a single, slow breath, and when he spoke again, his voice was not the flat, forensic monotone of before. There was a catch at the start, almost a wince, and he didn’t look at me but at the tabletop between us, as if he were addressing a third party sitting there, something that needed to be heard with exacting clarity.

“Sarah liked to make puns,” he said. “Not clever ones. She’d just wait for a word in a conversation, then build out the most terrible joke possible, and let it sit until someone else acknowledged it. She was always patient. She never explained her jokes. She didn’t need to.” He flexed his hands, one after the other, then folded them, knuckle to knuckle, on his lap. “She drank her coffee with three sugars and never apologized for it. She once wrote a Yelp review for a coffee shop just to publicly shame them for under-frothing the milk, and the manager printed it and taped it above the register.”

His voice got steadier, but the edges of the words were jagged. “She called me every Sunday, for years. We’d talk for exactly forty minutes, because she said that was the limit before it turned into a hostage negotiation. But she always called back twenty minutes later. She’d pretend she forgot something, or that she needed a recipe I’d never given her, or that she’d remembered a joke she had to tell me before it expired.” He stopped, not for effect, just to let it land.

“She was afraid of moths,” he said. “Not spiders or the dark or anything else you might expect. Moths. She’d leave the porch light off if she got home after dusk. She’d make me check the laundry room in case one flew out of the vent. I don’t know why, and she never explained it, but she was genuinely convinced they were obscene.”

He looked up now, and the look on his face was not anger, not even grief — just the stubborn, exhausted need for someone else to finally understand a thing that had never made sense. “She was going to adopt a greyhound that summer. She’d already picked out the name. She wanted to call it ‘Legs’ and buy it a racing jersey and take pictures until it hated her.”

The table between us was covered in the objects he’d arranged earlier, but now I saw the way he’d built a kind of altar: her photograph, the transcript, the album, the page with the three-line memorial. All of it triangulated under the circle of light. The frost on the window behind him was black and absolute, nothing beyond it but the world he’d abandoned in favor of this one, these four walls, and this last-ditch version of justice.

“I’m not telling you this because it will change anything,” he said, voice close to breaking and then recovered. “I just want you to know she was real, in a way you never bothered to check. She was not a mistake. She was not a lesson for your audience. She was a person, and you made her into a voice in a box, and that was all she was to you.”

He fell silent again, but it wasn’t the same silence as before. The air was charged now — not just between us, but inside every book on the shelf, every page in the album, every inch of the corridor where Sarah had never set foot but had, through him, been made present.

I stood in the doorway, and my hand closed into a fist. The cold was complete. The only warmth in the space radiated from the lamp and the woodstove and, almost unbearably, the sound of his voice still decaying in the air.

Daniel looked at me a long time, then at the objects in front of him. He reached up, slow and deliberate, to his breast pocket, and withdrew a matte black audio recorder — the same model I’d used in the early days of the show, back before we had budget for real gear. He set it on the table between the photographs, fingers lingering on the power button.

He said, “I want you to hear it once. No edits, no music. Without your voice over it.” He didn’t ask permission. He pressed play.

There was a split second of nothing — true digital silence — and then Sarah’s voice came in. It was rough, not at all the same as the version I’d processed for the show. I’d remembered it as shaky, a kind of smudged terror, but here it was raw and trembling and human in a way that made my knees go weak.

She didn’t say her name at first. She said, “I don’t know if this is going to get out. If it doesn’t, I’m sorry. I’m so fucking sorry.” The words overlapped a little, like she was trying to say them all before she lost her nerve. There was a sound of motion, a scuff, a low thud. “He’s in the hall. I can hear him.” The voice was not just scared — it was struggling to fit inside the language, to keep up with what was happening.

She said, “I think if I stay quiet maybe he’ll go.” A pause, a shudder of static. “I don’t think he will. I think he’s listening too.”

I heard it truly for the first time, not as content or a recording, but as a voice I’d never deserved to hear. She said, “I tried 911 but it’s—” and then she laughed, a wet, broken laugh, the kind you only hear when you've run out of options. She tried to make the joke, tried to say something that would be funny on the other side, and then lost it. There was a sound like her hand over the mic.

And then, in a whisper so low it was almost missed, she said: “If you hear this, The Hollow, I guess you got me. Please. If you’re listening, send help.”

The last seven seconds were just breath. Then Sarah's voice. "He---Hello?" A slam, a scream, and the file went dead.

The recorder clicked off, a tiny mechanical sound that felt like a thunderclap in the silence.

I didn't look up. I couldn't look at Daniel, or at the photos, or at the window, or at my own hands. The sound of her voice, unedited, unscored, was so much more devastating than the forty-seven seconds I’d built my own legend on that I felt, for a second, as if my heart had simply stopped. I realized, in that moment, that this was what Daniel had been building for two years, with his logbook annotations and his corridor steps and his slow, patient rearrangement of my entire sense of what the lodge was. This was the end of the process, the final, clean cut that leaves nothing for the wound to close around.

He did not speak again. He let the silence do its work.

The brass key, forgotten on the floor between us, lay where it had fallen, indifferent to whether it opened a door or locked it.

On the other side of the frost-black window, the mountains were coming into focus, faint and blue and unreal. Here, in the silence, there was nothing left to do but listen.

And so I did.
