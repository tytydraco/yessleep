# Prologue: The Man at the Bar

A quiet, smoky bar buzzed with murmured conversation and clinking glasses. The door creaked open and a man stepped inside — leather jacket, scar running along the side of his neck, eyes like someone who'd seen too much. He approached the bartender, who was polishing a glass.

"Whiskey," the man said, voice low.

The bartender eyed him skeptically. "Got ID?"

The man slid a worn card across the counter. The name read: Josh Miller. Date of Birth: 10/9/1989. Country: Texas.

The bartender poured the drink without another word. The man silently took a sip.

On the bar's old TV, a breaking news alert flashed. The anchor's voice was urgent:

“Another brutal attack last night on the outskirts of Pine Creek. Multiple victims injured — no fatalities. Authorities believe the attacker to be The Phantom Stalker — known to be Jeff Hutcherson, a 24-year-old white male with jet-black hair, a mouth carved into a permanent smile, and eyes scarred from severe burns. 11 years ago, a tragic massacre occurred in ST BAAL Town, leaving only one survivor. That survivor remains anonymous to this day.”

The man at the bar didn’t flinch. He only stared deeper into his drink.

# Chapter 1 – The Move

Jeff’s family was driving to their new home in St. Baal Town. Jeff sighed deeply, staring out the window at the endless sea of trees blurring past.

Liu noticed and asked, “What’s wrong?”

Jeff replied, “Dad’s job makes us move all the time. I’m mad I won’t see my friends again.”

Liu smiled, trying to cheer him up. As he talked, Jeff’s gaze drifted deeper into the forest. Suddenly, something caught his eye.

A tall, pale man wearing a grotesque bull mask was violently hacking at something on the forest floor with a bloody axe. With every brutal swing, dark red blood sprayed across the leaves and the man’s naked skin.

The masked figure paused. Slowly, he rose to his full height. He was completely naked, his entire body drenched in thick, glistening blood that dripped heavily from his chest, arms, and thighs. In his left hand, he clutched a severed human head by the hair. The head’s dead eyes stared wide open, its mouth frozen in a silent scream, while thick ropes of blood still poured from the ragged, torn stump of the neck.

The bull-masked man turned his head and looked directly at Jeff through the car window. Then, with a slow, deliberate motion, he raised the severed head and waved it at him, as if greeting an old friend.

Jeff’s stomach twisted in pure horror. His heart slammed against his ribs, and cold sweat broke out across his forehead. He desperately tried to convince himself it was just his imagination — a sick, twisted hallucination. It couldn’t be real. Could it?

“Jeff, what’s wrong?” Liu asked again, snapping him back to reality.

About 30 minutes later, they arrived at their new home. There next-door neighbor came over to there house to greet them and invite them to her son’s birthday party. She smiled warmly at first, but when her eyes met Jeff’s, her smile stretched wider — almost too wide, like she had seen something in him she liked.

Jeff stood frozen, staring back into her eyes until his mother’s voice snapped him out of it.

With a cheerful smile, Jeff’s mom said, “We’d be happy to join! When will the party be?”

“In two weeks,” the neighbor replied pleasantly.

Jeff’s mom thanked her, but Jeff only frowned, looking skeptical.

“Are you serious?” Jeff asked his mom.

“Oh come on, Jeff, it’ll be fun,” Liu chimed in.

Jeff shot Liu a look, “Not you too.”

The neighbor smiled again. “Well, that sounds great. I hope to see you there. Welcome to the neighborhood. Enjoy your stay.”

As she turned around to walk back to her house, she glanced over her shoulder at Jeff one last time and gave him a sadistic, twisted smile that sent a chill down his spine.

Jeff’s family carried their boxes and bags inside the new house. The move-in was exhausting. By the time everything was unpacked, the sun had already begun to set.

Jeff’s mom wiped her forehead and said, “Jeff, Liu, it’s getting late. You two should get some rest. Tomorrow is your first day at school”.

The boys nodded and headed upstairs to their bedrooms. Jeff entered his room, dropped onto the bed, and stared at the unfamiliar ceiling for a few minutes before exhaustion finally pulled him under.

Jeff woke up in a place he didn’t remember.  
He was standing inside a dark closet, the smell of old wood and dust thick in the air. Why was he in a closet? Confused, he slowly pushed the door open and stepped out.

Someone was lying in his bed.

As Jeff crept closer, intending to run and get his parents, a loud, blood-curdling scream erupted from outside the room, followed by a heavy, wet thump. Footsteps — slow and deliberate — began moving down the hallway toward his bedroom.

Panic exploded in Jeff’s chest. He dove back into the closet and pulled the door almost shut, peeking through the thin crack with wide, terrified eyes.

The bedroom door creaked open.

It was the figure from the forest.

The tall, pale man in the grotesque bull mask stepped inside, his axe dripping thick drops of fresh blood onto the wooden floor. He moved straight to the bed and grabbed the person lying there. It was a girl, maybe the same age as Jeff or a little older. Her face… that face looked familiar. It was the girl whose severed head the masked man had been holding in the forest.

The masked figure hoisted the unconscious girl over his shoulder as if she weighed nothing.

Suddenly, something grabbed Jeff hard by the shoulder from behind inside the closet.

He gasped in terror and stumbled out. The masked figure instantly turned, dropped the girl to the floor with a heavy thud, and lunged toward Jeff, raising the bloody axe high above his head.  
The axe began to swing down —

BRRRRRING!

Jeff jolted awake in his real bed, drenched in cold sweat, his heart pounding violently. He sat up quickly, eyes darting around the dark room in panic, searching for any sign of the masked man or the girl.

There was no one there.

Jeff breathed heavily, running a shaky hand through his damp hair.

“Was it all just a dream…?” he whispered to the empty room.

# Chapter 2: The Fight

Jeff woke to the pale morning light spilling into his room. It was his first day at the new school. He dragged himself downstairs, eyes heavy with exhaustion, and slumped into a chair at the kitchen table. His family was already eating breakfast.

“You look… awful,” his mom said, frowning. “What happened?”

“Bad nightmares, I think,” Jeff muttered.

“You think?” she pressed.

Jeff stared at her with a blank, unreadable face.

“Well, eat your breakfast,” she said with a sigh. “It’s almost time to go.”

Jeff and Liu finished quickly, grabbed their bags, and headed out on foot. The neighborhood was quiet, the morning air still and heavy.

Halfway down the street, Jeff’s shoelace came undone. He crouched down to tie it — and something whistled through the air just above his head.

A skateboard.

“Watch it, asshole!” Jeff snapped, standing up.

The rider spun around and grinned. He had jet-black hair styled in a mullet, a tank top with a faded Black Sabbath logo, ripped jeans, and a smug, arrogant look. Behind him stood two others: a tall, skinny guy with long brown hair, a dark T-shirt, and worn-out sneakers named Isaiah, and a bald, heavyset kid who looked like he had never exercised a day in his life named Hunter.

The leader stopped his board and stepped closer.

“Yo, fresh meat. I’m Logan. On my left is Isaiah, on my right is Hunter. Here’s the deal — new kids gotta pay a fine. Fifty bucks a week.”

Jeff narrowed his eyes. “Are you joking? Move aside.”

Logan chuckled darkly and pulled a switchblade from his pocket, flicking it open with a sharp click.

“I’m not laughing.”

Before Jeff could react, Logan lunged forward. Liu stepped in between them, and the blade slashed across his arm, drawing a bright line of blood.

Jeff froze for a split second. Then something happened — a strange, powerful feeling surged through him. It was unlike anything he had ever experienced before: cold, electric, and terrifying. He couldn’t describe it, couldn’t name it. It felt both foreign and deeply familiar at the same time, like something ancient waking up inside his chest.

Across the street, half-hidden in the shadows between two houses, he saw it: a strange dark figure. Tall and unnaturally still. It refused to show itself clearly, its form blurred and shifting as if the light itself was too afraid to touch it. Jeff could only make out the faint outline of broad shoulders and a head tilted slightly to the side, watching him.

The feeling grew stronger, pulsing through his veins like fire and ice at once.

He lunged at Logan, grabbed his arm, and twisted until there was a sickening crack. Logan screamed in agony, dropping the knife. Jeff spun toward Isaiah and slammed a fist into his face with brutal force. The boy staggered back, blood spraying from his broken nose. Hunter rushed forward, but Jeff kicked him square in the stomach. The heavyset kid collapsed to the pavement, retching violently.

Liu stared at Jeff in shock. A neighbor, hearing the commotion, peeked out from their front door. Their eyes widened at the sight of the three bullies sprawled on the ground.

“Run,” Jeff hissed.

They bolted toward school.

All day, Jeff’s mind churned. In class, he couldn’t stop replaying the fight — the moment of control, the rush of power surging through him. Part of him felt disgusted… but another part, deeper and darker, felt a sickening pleasure.

By the time school ended, there was still no sign of the police. They walked home in tense silence.

The moment they stepped through the front door, their mom looked at Jeff.

“Why are you so… happy?” she asked, confused.

“I don’t know,” Jeff said with a small shrug, a faint smile still lingering on his lips.

“Okay… well, dinner’s ready,” she replied.

As they headed toward the kitchen, a sharp knock came at the door. Two uniformed officers stood outside.

“Are your boys here?” the first officer asked.

Jeff’s mom looked concerned. “Yes… why? What happened?”

The officers exchanged a quick glance.

“A neighbor reported seeing three boys on the ground, injured. One of them had a knife that’s now missing. We just need to ask a few questions.”

Jeff stepped into view. The lead officer’s eyes narrowed.

“You’re coming with me, kid. We want to ask you some questions.”

Panic rushed through Jeff.

“It was all me!” he blurted out.

The officer stepped inside and reached for his cuffs — but then, from behind, a voice rang out.

“Hey!”

It was Liu, holding Logan’s switchblade in his trembling hand.

The two cops froze.

“Put the knife down, son. We don’t want to hurt you.”

Liu’s hand trembled. Then, slowly, he dropped the knife to the floor. The officers moved in and cuffed him without resistance.

Jeff’s chest ached with guilt.

“It was me, not him!” he cried desperately.

Liu turned his head just enough to meet his brother’s eyes.

“You don’t have to help me, bro. We both know who did it.”

Jeff’s eyes burned with hot tears as the officers led Liu away.

# Chapter 3: Black Sheep

The court session lasted less than an hour, but to Jeff it felt like an eternity. He sat beside his mother, staring blankly at the polished wood of the courtroom table while the opposing lawyer spoke.

Logan, Isaiah, and Hunter sat confidently across the room, dressed in clean clothes with their hair neatly combed. Their lawyer — a tall man in an expensive suit — twisted every fact with smooth confidence, burying the truth beneath his words.

Finally, the judge cleared his throat and delivered the verdict.

“After reviewing the evidence and witness statements,” he said firmly, “I find the defendants Logan Reed, Isaiah Carter, and Hunter Wells not guilty of all charges.”

Jeff’s heart sank.

The judge then turned toward Liu.

“As for the defendant, Liu Hutcherson — I find you guilty of assault causing actual bodily harm, attempted second-degree murder, and larceny. You are hereby sentenced to eight years in juvenile detention.”

Jeff’s mother gasped. Jeff’s hands trembled as the bailiff approached Liu. The sharp crack of the gavel echoed through the room like thunder.

When they arrived home, Jeff went straight to his room and slammed the door behind him. His mom called after him, but he didn’t respond.

His dad sighed. “Give him some time,” he said quietly.

Two weeks passed. David’s birthday party was coming up.

One morning, Jeff’s mom gently woke him. He was still sad, still haunted by what had happened to his brother. She sat on the edge of his bed and said softly, “I know, Jeff. I know what you’re feeling right now.”

Jeff looked up at her with empty eyes.

“Get dressed,” she said, forcing a small smile. “We don’t want to be late for the party. You’re going to have fun there.”

Jeff sighed but obeyed.

Downstairs, his mom eyed his outfit critically. “Are you really going to wear that? Please, put on something else.”

Frustrated, Jeff went back upstairs and rummaged through his closet. “I have nothing good to wear!” he shouted.

Finally, he settled on a plain white hoodie and black jeans.

When he came down the stairs, both his parents gave him disapproving looks.

“Are you serious?” his dad asked, glancing at his watch. “We don’t have time for this.”

They arrived at the party, and Jeff’s mom nudged him toward the backyard. “Go outside and play with the other kids.”

Jeff sighed and wandered out. He sat alone in a corner, feeling empty and sad, his thoughts still stuck on Liu.

David, the birthday boy, approached hesitantly. “Want to play?”

“No,” Jeff muttered.

David looked down, disappointed.

“Fine,” Jeff relented. “What are we playing?”

“Cops and robbers,” David said, handing him a bright plastic toy gun.

For a short while, Jeff almost forgot everything. He laughed and ran with the other kids, losing himself in the game.

Then he heard the screech of wheels behind him.

He turned — and there they were. The bullies had jumped the fence and were storming straight toward him.

“That’s where we draw the line,” Jeff said, standing up. “You got your payback, asshole.”

Logan smiled darkly.

“No, no, you’re wrong. I don’t want to be even. I want to win.”

As Logan spoke, Isaiah and Hunter pulled out pistols and aimed them at the crowd of terrified kids and parents. Screams erupted everywhere.

Logan lunged at Jeff, punching and kicking him viciously. He slammed Jeff against the glass sliding door, shattering it into a thousand pieces. Sharp shards sliced across Jeff’s body, and warm blood dripped down his arms and chest.

As Logan stepped forward to finish him off, he pulled out a switchblade and flicked it open with a cruel grin.

“Look at you,” Logan laughed. “Pathetic. You sent your own brother to juvie. Come on, fight me.”

Jeff lay bleeding on the ground, his mouth full of blood, consciousness fading fast.

As Logan raised the knife to finish him off, the world seemed to slow — then stopped completely. Time snapped like a broken string.

A low, wet giggle echoed in Jeff’s head. He looked around desperately, searching for the source of the sound.

In the dark corner of the yard, the strange black figure from before stepped forward. As it moved closer, it revealed itself — not in its true form, but as a white husky.

Its mouth stretched into an unnaturally wide smile, almost lustful, as if it found the entire situation pleasurable. The teeth were rotting and stained with old blood. Its eyes were dry and wide, completely black except for tiny white dots that seemed to hold the last spark of something dying. The creature’s body was tall and painfully skinny, rotting like it had been dead for months or hadn’t eaten in just as long.

As the decaying husky approached, Jeff whispered in terror:

“What are you? What do you want from me? Why are you tormenting me? I have done nothing…”

The husky’s smile widened even further. It let out a deep, dying laugh that sounded like crumbling bones and whispered:

“I am the silence that devoured the first light and the shadow that will swallow the last. I am the breath that births ruin and the abyss that claims all things. Before me there was nothing but hunger — and after me, not even oblivion shall remain

.Jeff, thou art set apart in this world, for humanity is divided. There are the white sheep — pure and unbroken — those who bring no harm, the innocent, the children of light

.And there are those called monsters — born of violence, raised in suffering, shaped by cruelty and neglect. From broken homes and wounded spirits, they are forged into wolves clothed in the skin of sheep. They walk among the innocent, and in their wrath they strike down those they deem worthy of death.

Yet there is another kind — rare and unseen. The black sheep. They appear as the innocent, untouched and whole, yet within them stirs something ancient… something waiting. It whispers, it hungers, it begs to be released.

And when it is revealed, their true desire shall rise from the depths — and there shall be nothing in this world, nor beyond it, that can restrain it.

”The husky leaned closer, its rotting breath washing over Jeff. Its voice dropped into a dark, commanding whisper:

“This is you, Jeff. Just release the black sheep inside you. Look around you. Look how they smile while you suffer. No help. No remorse. Look at your parents — see how disgusted they are, how disappointed. You are the one who is free… not your little brother. So open… and release.”

In that moment, something dark and ancient uncoiled deep inside Jeff.

He pounced on Logan, forcing him to drop the knife, and punched his chest repeatedly until he heard ribs crack with sickening snaps. Then Jeff snatched the knife and drove it straight into Logan’s heart.

Logan’s eyes widened in shock. A wet gurgle escaped his throat before his body went limp.

Jeff stared at the corpse, a twisted rush of pleasure flooding through him.

Around him, everyone stood frozen in horror.

The two remaining bullies aimed their guns at Jeff.

Jeff scrambled to his feet and ran upstairs, with Isaiah chasing close behind.

Downstairs, Jeff’s dad tackled Hunter, pinning him to the ground. Hunter didn’t even struggle.

Jeff and Isaiah burst into the bathroom. Jeff lunged, knocking the gun from Isaiah’s hand. They grappled violently on the floor. Isaiah grabbed a glass bottle of alcohol and smashed it over Jeff’s head. Jeff collapsed, hitting a shelf. A large bottle of bleach tipped over and spilled across his body, soaking his clothes and mixing with the blood.

As Jeff tried to get up, Isaiah laughed cruelly.

“What’s so funny?” Jeff gasped.

Isaiah pulled out a Zippo lighter. “You’re covered in alcohol,” he said with a smirk.

Before Jeff could react, Isaiah flicked the lighter. Flames erupted instantly, engulfing Jeff in searing heat.

Jeff screamed in agony as the fire consumed him. He stumbled out of the bathroom and fell down the stairs, his burning body crashing into the yard while he cried desperately for help.

In the distance, the white husky watched with satisfaction, its grin stretched impossibly wide.

Jeff’s vision blurred. The pain became too much. He lost consciousness.

# Chapter 4 – The Change

Jeff woke up and quietly got up, but a nurse saw him and stopped him.

“You need to stay in bed and rest,” she said gently. “You’re in no shape to be up, young man.”

His mother stirred awake and, seeing Jeff sitting up, began to cry.

“Jeff… Liu will be released from juvie,” she said, her voice trembling with relief. “The two boys confessed — the charges will be dropped.”

Jeff let out a hollow laugh, empty and distant.

His mother wiped her tears and forced a small smile.

“I’m going home to prepare for Liu’s release party,” she said softly. “We’ll all be together again soon.”

The next day, the doctor entered the room and began carefully removing Jeff’s bandages.

In the background, his family stood silently watching — his mother clutching her hands together, his father’s face pale, and Liu’s eyes full of nervous hope.

No one spoke. The only sound was the soft rustle of bandages being peeled away.

When the last layer fell, everyone froze in horror.

Jeff, confused, got out of bed and went to the bathroom.

His hair was blackened from bleach, his skin pale white, his lips burned red.

He stared at his reflection in the mirror, tears streaming down his face.

Liu approached quietly.

“Are you okay?” he asked.

Jeff gave a twisted smile, his face twitching every few seconds.

“It’s bad, I know… but look at my smooth face. It’s beautiful.”

Jeff’s father turned to the doctor.

“Is he… okay?”

The doctor sighed.

“In cases like this, severe trauma can affect the mind. If his behavior worsens, we may need to admit him for a mental health evaluation.”

Jeff asked a nurse where his clothes were. She returned with his bloodstained, torn clothes from the glass shards.

When they arrived home, the house was filled with laughter and chatter — Liu’s release party was already in full swing.

Family members crowded the living room: uncles, aunties, cousins, grandparents — all smiling and celebrating his return.

But the moment Jeff stepped inside, the room fell silent.

The laughter faded, replaced by uneasy stares and nervous whispers. One by one, relatives made excuses — early mornings, long drives, things to do.

Within minutes, the party quietly dissolved.

Soon, the house was empty and still. Plates of uneaten cake sat on the table. Decorations hung limp in the quiet air.

Jeff forced a small smile.

“I’m tired… I’m going to bed,” he said softly.

His mother watched him, worried, her brow tight with unease. Jeff’s father gently held her arm.

“He’ll be fine,” he said quietly.

That night, Jeff’s mother woke to sounds of crying, laughter, and slicing.

She cautiously went to the bathroom and froze in horror.

Jeff stood there, covered in blood.

“I couldn’t stop smiling,” he whispered. “It hurt… so I fixed it. I burned my eyelids so I could keep smiling forever.”

“Am I beautiful, Mommy?”

His mother trembled, forcing a weak smile.

“Yes, honey… you’re beautiful. Let me get your father.”

She turned and rushed downstairs, shaking as she reached for the phone.

“Hello—can you send an officer to my house? My son—”

Her voice broke off.

The dispatcher responded, concerned.

“Ma’am? Hello? Are you still there?”

Behind her came a low whisper:

“You lied.”

She turned slowly.

Jeff stood behind her, holding Logan’s knife.

Her phone slipped from her hand.

“Jeff,” she whispered, backing away. “Please… I’m sorry. Don’t do this.”

From the floor, the dispatcher’s voice continued faintly, urgent and confused.

Jeff’s expression was calm, almost gentle.

“You’ll be fine, Mommy. Just go to sleep.”

Jeff’s father heard the noise and ran downstairs.

He froze at the sight before him.

Jeff stood over his mother’s lifeless body.

The father gagged and lunged at him.

Jeff dodged and stabbed him in the neck, forcing the blade deeper until his body collapsed.

Jeff tilted his head, studying him with eerie calmness.

Then he smiled.

Jeff walked upstairs to Liu’s room.

He climbed onto his brother’s bed.

Liu opened his eyes, weak but aware.

“Go to sleep,” Jeff whispered.

Liu shoved him away and stumbled downstairs—only to find his parents dead.

Eerie laughter echoed through the house.

Jeff’s laughter.

“Why?” Liu cried.

Jeff blinked suddenly, tears streaming down his face.

“Liu… help me,” he sobbed.

Liu stepped forward, desperate.

In the shadows behind Jeff, a white husky stood watching silently, its eyes glowing faintly.

Suddenly, Jeff moved violently and slashed Liu’s neck, sending him stumbling to the ground.

Liu lay there, bleeding, struggling to breathe as sirens began to wail in the distance.

Jeff looked at him, a brief flicker of remorse crossing his face.

“I’m sorry,” he whispered.

Then he turned and vanished into the forest.

Liu lay barely conscious.

In the shadows, the white husky approached, drooling, its grin stretching wide.

“It was you…” Liu rasped weakly. “You did this…”

The husky leaned closer.

Suddenly, a bright light erupted.

The husky screeched, recoiling in fear.

A calm voice spoke from the light:

“You will not harm this child, foul creature.”

The husky dissolved into darkness.

A gentle figure appeared within the light.

“You are safe now,” it said softly. “I am here to protect you.”

Those were the last words Liu heard before he lost consciousness.
