\[PART 1:\]

I’ve been a 911 call operator for about five years now. I don’t love it. I get to hear the worst parts of society while having my grim imagination fill in the gaps with bleak visuals; it is not ideal. But I have rent and a useless art degree to pay off. Plus, having insurance in America is a luxury, so I’ll take what I can get. I live in a small, rural county with just one call center, my agency. I work the night shift, which isn’t so bad after your body gets used to the nocturnal lifestyle. There are usually about four of us working nights. We all spread out as far as we can in such a compact space, shielding ourselves from the scrutiny of our co-workers as we all slack off.   
I clocked in at 7:00 pm, taking a seat at the outdated desk that sat in the back right corner. The furniture hasn’t been updated since the eighties. All of our department’s budget goes towards updating our technology, so we’re left with back pain and the loudest chairs you have ever heard. The decades-old desks remain as a time capsule for all those who have worked here. Scratches, marker stains, and paint peeling away to reveal the cold metal interior set up an appropriate tone.   
I spend most of my nights sending out emails and working on my portfolio, trying to put myself back out there as an artist. It hasn't been going terribly…just very slow. A few uneventful hours pass. The typical nightly calls of drivers being intoxicated and potential break-ins. I contact dispatch and assure the panicked individuals on the other line that an officer will be with them soon. I try to stay on the line for as long as I can, but they usually hang up after they’re promised the appearance of the authorities.   
It's been about thirty minutes since I received any calls. I can hear the low grumbling of snoring coming from the front of the room. Sounds like things haven’t been exciting for old Frank either. Frank is a nice, older gentleman. He’s been working here for over thirty years, which is evident from his prominent dark circles and sagging basset hound-like face. We’ll have small talk here and there in the break room. He’ll give me updates on his progress of refurbishing vintage cars and show me photos from Facebook Marketplace of his potential new buys. I pretend to listen and care to make him content, and he pretends to listen and care about my struggling art career. Most of the time, he keeps to himself and minds his business, which is typically of the night-shift staff.   
Slumped down in my stiff chair, I mindlessly scroll through my email once more. Admittedly, I have been obsessively rechecking my inbox and previously sent messages. The anxiety of rejection eating away at my stomach lining. The high-pitched tone of an incoming call pulls me away from the ever-rising tide of my career failure. Quickly sitting up, I adjust my headset and answer the call. “911, what's your emergency?” I waited for a reply, but the only response was shallow breathing, labored as if free was causing their lungs to tremble. “911, what's your-”

“Please p-please help me,” her tone was hushed but urgent, the tears audibly noticeable. “He keeps getting closer to my house, my daughter is home, please send someone here now..!”

“Okay, ma’am, calm down. Can you give me your address and describe the man to me?” I switched tabs on my monitor, getting ready to signal the first officer I saw on screen. This wasn’t entirely out of the ordinary for an emergency hotline, but it was definitely not a common occurrence.

“I live on Old 150, across from the orchard. It’s dark, but I can see him a little bit from my porch light.” Her voice was still hushed, but the promise of safety gave her more confidence. “He’s…he’s really skinny…his skin looks like it ’s-it’s too loose. Like it's falling off of him. His gums are showing, and his eye sockets are so red-I don’t know if he even has eyelids. He’s so dirty and…wet? His hair is long and stringy…like it's falling out as we speak.”

“Okay, that’s good, ma’am. Stay on the line with me. Can you tell me your name, and is anyone else in the house?” I quickly connected dispatch with the address and short description. I could feel my spine tense in haste, fearful that if I wasted even a second, something terrible would happen, and it would be all my fault.

“My name is Rita, and it’s just my daughter and me. My husband left town for a few days to visit his m-mom.” She choked back her sobs. “He can see me, I don’t know how, it's so dark, but he can see me. I can feel his eyes on me, oh my god.” I could hear the flood of tears. “Oh god!” she screamed even louder. I could hear her move the phone away from her face as she stifled a scream.

“What’s going on, Rita? Talk to me, please.” I felt sick. My bones physically ached from my muscles tightening around them. I could hear the guttural anguish in her voice. A sob that could only be created by the most painful feeling, grief.

“That's his shirt! He has Phillips clothes! No, please!” Her pained cries caused a lump in my throat. Without hearing his name before, I just knew he was her husband. In that moment, I could feel her pain, her grief, and the terrible thoughts that flash through your head. The grim thought of what terrible things Phillip experienced. Though I couldn’t see her, I saw her as she trembled, her knuckles turning white from her grip on the phone, knees buckling from emotional exhaustion. I could feel my heart in my throat as I clenched my teeth, moving impossibly close to my monitor. I have had my fair share of disturbing calls…but nothing like this. I’m not religious, but I am conjuring every once of spiritualism in me to pray that this guy is just an elderly man that wondered off. 

“He fucking moved!” She shrieked, her voice hoarse from crying “oh god, oh god,” her voice was now completely raised to a yell.

“Mom, what's going-” I could vaguely make out the few words of what sounded like a young child, her daughter.

“Rita, grab any weapon you can and hide. Take your daughter and hide,” I was yelling, now gripping the cold, hard desk. The hair on the back of my neck was standing up, feeling as though I was also being watched. “Rita-,” The line went dead. I sat there for a second in shock, my brain unable to process my next move. “No, no, no, no,” I quietly repeated to myself in a panic. My heart thumped in my head as my peripheral vision darkened. I was running off pure adrenaline. My hands shook furiously as I went to contact dispatch once more. I heard the familiar high-pitched alarm of a call silence my heartbeat. Taking a deep breath before I answered.
