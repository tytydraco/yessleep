I was twelve the year my town fed my sister to the wall, and I’m old enough now to know it wasn’t a miracle.



“Why do we have to go to the building?”



“You know why, Adam.”



“It’s stupid. You don’t believe it. I don’t.”



“But everyone else does.”



“But how does a wall just open?”



“I don’t know.”



“Someone has to open it.”



“Maybe not.”



“No, Ashley. It cannot open on its own.”



“Hey, you don’t know that for sure. Have you seen anyone opening it? Why is it always so clean when it closes up? And don’t say, I don’t believe in it. I do. I just don’t think it's sacred. Taking a young woman isn’t anything beautiful as the minister would like you to believe.”



“But you said.”



“Adam, stop it. We need to go faster; we’re already late.”



I rolled my eyes. I hated it when Ashley turned into mom mode. She was much cooler as an older sister.



The building stood at the end of the boulevard, a large iron structure with few windows on either side, covered in dark gray tiles that swallowed the light. It had multiple floors reaching almost to the sky, towering over the buildings around it.



The whole city was modeled after it. It made everything look dark and depressing, like a rainy autumn day that never left.



As we approached it, my hands began to sweat. People already had their idea of us, two siblings with no parents. To them, it screamed bad news. Coming late only reinforced it.



As soon as we entered the building, a blast of cold air hit me, making me shiver. We walked through the gray, empty hall to the chapel.



The deep, loud voice of the minister echoed through the tall steel doors. Two large stacks of hay stood beside it, bound with white ribbon.



Ashley stood in front of the door and began swinging her hands around, moving her lips, pretending to be the minister. I began laughing uncontrollably, and she immediately stopped and put a finger in front of her mouth, telling me to shut up. This was the older sister I loved.



“You need to act cool, Adam.”



“Why would you make me laugh then?”



She smirked and opened the door.



My smile died instantly. The whole town turned around, staring at us, their lips pressed, eyes narrowed, hushing between each other. On the stage stood all the young girls from the town, nervously looking around.



“Ashley, Adam. I was afraid we’d have to send someone for you.” 



The minister paused. 



“The wall opened,” he then said in a deep, slow voice, holding a pouch in one hand and a stack of hay in the other.



We looked at each other.



“Come, Ashley. The girls had already drawn their tokens.”



My legs began to shake.



“Ashley, no,” I whispered.



“It’s okay, Adam,” she said, swallowed, and slowly walked to the stage.



The minister took the pouch from the table and handed it to Ashley. She pulled the last paper and stood back next to the other girls.



“Unwrap.”



The air inside felt thin. My vision narrowed. I could feel my heartbeat in my temples.



I watched each of the girls unwrap the small paper and sigh with relief.



Then came Ashley’s turn.



She slowly unwrapped the paper. 



Its crumpling echoed through the room.



Her eyes went wide, her mouth opened, and she let out a silent gasp.



No.



Not Ashley.



“The wall has chosen.”



My world began spinning. The people clapped. Ashley wept softly.



I couldn’t take it anymore. I ran to the stage screaming.



“No, not my sister.”



But the townsmen stood up and grabbed me.



“Let her go!” I screamed.



“Adam, please, don’t,” Ashley said through the weeps.



“It’s an honor.” “You should be grateful.” “It will keep the city safe,”  echoed through the crowd.



The pressure in my head was too strong. I wrestled my right hand out and punched the man closest to me.



He returned the hit.



Copper filled my mouth. Stars burst behind my eyes.



He punched me again, and my world went dark.



Sounds came back first, low and rhythmic.



My vision was blurred.



I tried to move but couldn’t; two men were by my side, holding me.



The wall was open.



Inside, Ashley stood in a long white lace dress, barefoot. 



They had already dressed her.



Flowers trembled in her hands, hay was stuffed in her mouth.



Tears poured down her cheeks. She stared right at me, her eyes still compassionate, loving.



“Ashley…” I murmured.



“He’s awake,” someone yelled.



A sharp knuckle hit my temple.



When I opened my eyes again, there was no Ashley. No people. I lay on the cold floor, the taste of copper still in my mouth.



Before me was the wall, sealed with the dark grey tiles, silent. 



A strand of hay lay stuck between the tiles.
