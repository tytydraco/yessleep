Waiting for a train to pass on another line. A crimson leaf upon a railroad track, fallen from the trunk of a red maple. The leaf is swept off by the adjacent train’s gust. Can’t explain it, but I know it won’t return.  
The certainty lands before the explanation ever could.  
Such a long train. The Great Gatsby, sophomore-year summer reading. Wondering what erudite parents gave me that name. Didn’t they know better? My parents were far from literati acolytes. Dad knocked up Mom in high school. Some crass music was probably playing when he did it.  
Still in purgatory. Somewhere, a bitter ex-husband—Levi’s belt clutched—requests “Cocaine Blues” as a fuck-you to his ex-wife. Bet the song belted out when my parents conceived me. FM 97.2 played “A Boy Named Sue.” Despising the country music but can’t change stations. Genetic revenge.  
Train end in sight. Jay. Gats. Predetermined consequence of a summer reading assignment. Happy plus little plus accident equals “Milkman.” Wishing my parents had been versed enough to dodge my social funeral.  
Out of purgatory, toward a shadow purgatory. Work. Passing over the railroad tracks, the Ford F-150 in front advertises with a bumper sticker: DILF’n Ain’t Easy. Another. Thumb and ring fingers tucked in, other ones erect. Two in the pink, one in the stink? asking the truck.  
Stoplight change. Crank the steering wheel left onto Irote Ave. Almost miss my second turn, still processing the bumper stickers. Jerk the wheel right and park behind the squat municipal building that triples as police station, courthouse, and library—because in this town even the books are doing community service.  
Get out of the car and push through the entrance doors, inadvertently to the rhythm of “Cocaine Blues.” “Mornin’, Gat,” and “Morning, Detective.” Cops call me Gat. I’ll take it over Gatsby jokes. Hang up my winter jacket and settle into the office.  
Something feels off. I write it off, shaking my head. Scribble the F-150’s plate number and a quick association in my notebook—habit. In case the driver turns up dead. Or in case I need a laugh later. Also in case a pervert case surfaces. I do not explain the habit to myself. I never have. Someone raps on the door. Too fast. Too clean. Urgent.  
“Detective.”  
“Yes, Reynolds?”  
“A man came to see you. He says he has urgent business that you both need to attend to.”  
Both?  
“Who is this man?”  
“I don’t know. He says he knows you.”  
I sigh. Another nutcase. “Let him in.” What business could a man have that we both need to attend to? Thought registered. Reynolds holds open the door. A man pale as I am. An uncanny reflection.  
“Mornin’, Jay,” he voices, like a friend.  
“Morning, Mr—”  
“Mr. House. Mr. Robert House.”  
The name doesn’t fit the room. My prior intuition was right. Today’s a strange day. Robert House belongs somewhere else. A video game. An autocrat. A man who doesn’t age. A man who doesn’t leave his tower.  
Caught off guard, I forget his impudence. “Have a seat, Mr. House.” Mr. House drops into the chair.  
“Let us cut the crap and get to it,” he says, abrasively. “You establish connections easily. Are we right?”  
We? This man is operating under assumptions I don’t share.  
“Yes,” I say. “It’s part of my job.”  
“But it goes further.”  
“What do you mean?”  
“You were a straight-A student throughout your education, since middle school.”  
“Yes… yes, I was. How do you know that?”  
No reply.  
“In fact, we also know that you did not study throughout school and received these grades.”  
“How can you possibly know that?”  
My voice starts to tremble. No response again.  
“Who is we?”  
“We are a group interested in you, Jay.”  
Without permission, Mr. House takes out his phone and photographs the notebook. The F-150. The association.  
“What are you doing?”  
He looks at me, faintly irritated. “What is necessary,” he says, “to ensure world safety.”  
World safety?  
“What is necessary… for world safety?”  
House doesn’t respond.  
“May we grab a coffee?”  
“Uh, sure.”  
Mr. House leaves the room. He only refers to himself plurally. Is this plural a tic? Or a refusal to be singular. Sometime during the ten minutes he’s gone, three police officers run out the back door and into their patrol cars. Sirens blare.  
Mr. House returns, stirring his coffee oddly meticulously, though it appears to be black. “You’re going to want to come with us. There’s something important you need to see.”  
He’s already walking toward the municipal exit. I’m not riding with him. I take my own car.  
By the time I reach it, House pulls out ludicrously fast. If an officer were present, he’d be ticketed. I struggle to keep up. Just before the local sanatorium, I see an ambulance, a fire truck, three police cars—and the F-150 from this morning.  
Fire rises from the car’s carcass like blooming red maple leaves. Firemen douse the flames. After the smoke subsides, I peer inside to see what remains of a man in the front seat.  
Agape mouth. Cigarette-ash gray, entirely. Fragments of clothing cling lifelessly to him. A faint color—borderline illegible—of a Hooters shirt is seared into his chest. He has no pants. Singed off in the explosion. The bumper sticker reads: Ain’t Easy.  
A stray beam of sunlight strikes the corpse and refracts into my eyes. My vision pools with tears. I stand there, mutely crying, until one of the firemen asks if I’m all right.  
I can’t respond.  
I do not ask how this happened. I do not ask why.  
The last thing I remember is falling down, feeling like I’m forgetting something I was supposed to be.
