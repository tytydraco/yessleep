My sister brought home a stray white cat.  
It started with a stray white cat.  
We’re a normal family—five of us total. My parents, my older sister, my younger brother, and me. We live in a quiet neighborhood, the kind of place where you leave your back door unlocked and assume the worst thing that can happen is a rainy weekend.  
Then, my sister found it.  
It was an old cat. Too quiet. Its fur wasn’t a normal, healthy white; it looked pale and dead, like it had been washed in ash instead of water. We took it in because we felt sorry for it. At first, nothing seemed wrong. It ate its food, slept in the corners, and did regular cat things.  
But within a few days, the atmosphere in the house shifted.  
The cat started acting... wrong. It would sit completely upright for hours, staring intensely at empty spaces in the room, tracking movements that none of us could see. Sometimes I’d catch it staring directly at me, and a cold shiver would run down my spine. It felt like the silence in the room was watching me through its milky eyes.  
Last night, we finally snapped. The cat was sitting at the top of the stairs, completely rigid, making this horrific, low humming sound that didn't sound like a purr at all. Frustrated and honestly terrified, my dad grabbed it, marched downstairs, and threw it out into the front yard, slamming the heavy wooden door shut.  
The second that thing crossed the threshold, reality broke.  
Through the frosted glass next to the door, we watched its silhouette change. It didn’t turn into a monster right away. It became a man. Not normal but man  
It grew tall, horribly thin, its limbs stretching out in ways that defied human biology. Under the moonlight filtering through the window, its skin looked completely wrong—leathery and synthetic. It moved stiffly, jerking its joints as if it was learning how to walk like a human from a distant memory.  
Then, it started begging.  
A voice came through the door. It was soft, trembling, and deeply unsettling because it sounded *almost* safe. Almost familiar.  
*“Let me in,”* it whimpered. *“I just want to stay.”*  
"Go away!" my dad yelled, his voice cracking. We all backed up into the living room, screaming at it to leave.  
The more we rejected it, the more its patience cracked. The soft voice dissolved into a guttural, furious roar.  
And then it grew.  
Right before our eyes, its shadow expanded exponentially, like reality itself was struggling to contain its mass. It shot up, towering above the trees and the roofline, until its massive, distorted shape blurred against the night sky. With terrifying strength, the entity lifted a massive boulder from our retaining wall and hurled it at the house.  
The first one tore through the roof, exploding wood and plaster into the air. Dust choked our throats as we screamed, shielding our heads. A second boulder struck the ground right in front of the porch, splitting the earth open.  
But the physical damage wasn't the worst part. The worst part was the isolation.  
Our neighborhood is tightly packed. The sound of a roof collapsing should have brought out every single neighbor. But looking through the broken window, the street was dead. No lights turned on. No one came outside. It was like the entire world outside our property line had been completely erased. We were totally trapped inside its attention.  
Then, it shrank back down. It took on a humanoid form again, but it was cruder this time—a melting, oil-like caricature of a person. And it wasn't alone anymore. Another entity, a secondary presence that felt inextricably attached to it, materialized right beside it.  
We kept screaming through the wreckage for them to leave, but my dad suddenly went pale. He gripped my shoulder, his fingers digging in so hard it bruised.  
"Look at his face," my dad whispered, his voice hollow. "Look carefully."  
I forced myself to look past the oily distortion of the entity's face. My stomach dropped into a bottomless pit of pure horror.  
I recognized him.  
The scent of grease and stale tobacco suddenly filled my memory. It was the roadside mechanic. The one who had fixed my car a few weeks ago when I broke down. I remembered how he had looked at me back then—staring just a little too long, with a deep, unsettling envy. He hated my life. He envied my normalness, my family, the easy life I took for granted.  
Now, he wasn't just a man anymore. Something was wearing him. Or something had learned him.  
This wasn’t a random haunting. It was personal.  
They didn't try to smash the front door down. They didn't rush us. They just stood there in the dust, waiting.  
Because spirits like that don't need force. They need permission.  
And I realized, with a sickening jolt of adrenaline, that permission doesn't always sound like a door opening. Sometimes it sounds like a mistake. Sometimes it comes from a younger sibling who gets scared and cries out. Sometimes it comes from someone who is just too tired, confused, or terrified to think straight.  
A few minutes ago, the shouting outside stopped.  
The entities are no longer outside. They're in the house.
