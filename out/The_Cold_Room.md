I'm writing this because I don't know what to do. I've always been a realist, never giving credit to claims of the supernatural. But now I don't know what to believe. I've been shaken to my core, and I know that if I tell anybody what I just saw, I'd immediately be sent for a mental evaluation.

I'm a police officer. I have been for about 12 years and have never once—not once—seen anything that couldn't be explained.

Last Saturday we brought in a man for homicide. Mid-thirties. Short hair. Brown eyes. Average-looking. Turns out the sick bastard killed his four-year-old daughter with an ax. He called dispatch before he even attacked her.

I don't normally do interrogations, but there was an emergency, so I was sent in.

The moment I walked in, I noticed he was nervous. Fingers tapping his pant leg. Eyes darting around the room. Camera. Table. Door. His pupils moved in a triangular pattern.

He watched me walk to the chair across from him. His eyes followed my legs like he was studying how I moved.

I asked the usual questions—name, address—taking notes before getting to the hard part.

He looked me straight in the eye.

“It wasn't her.”

“Excuse me?”

I put my pen down.

“It wasn't her. It was in her body… but it wasn't her.”

He started crying. Real crying. The kind you can't fake. I could tell he believed what he was saying. Plenty of killers try the insanity card, but something about the way he looked at me made me hesitate.

“I hit her first,” he said. “But she kept coming. Then I used the ax… and she kept coming. It wasn't her.”

I wrote everything down.

“How do you know it wasn't her?”

He swallowed.

“I couldn't find her anywhere in the house. I thought she'd run into the woods behind it, so I went looking. When I came back to grab my phone to call 911… she was standing in front of the house.”

His voice shook.

“I hugged her. Told her she scared me. I barely noticed how cold she was. Thought she’d just been outside too long.”

“We went inside. She didn't say a word. Emily was always talkative. Always followed directions.”

“I started a bath. That's when I noticed she was just… looking around. Like she'd never been inside before. I told her to get clothes from her room. She didn't know where to go.”

He wiped his face.

“She hadn't spoken once.”

“The bath finished filling. I realized the garage door was open, so I went to close it. I told her again to get ready.”

“When the door shut, she was standing in the doorway staring at me.”

“I got frustrated. I yelled. Asked if she heard me.”

His breathing quickened.

“She ran at me. Not like a child. Not like an animal. Like she'd never used legs before. Like bones were optional.”

“I pushed her away. When she hit the ground it sounded like… like slapping mayonnaise on bread.”

He gagged slightly.

“She stood up immediately. Her legs were twisted in front of her. Her body lifted like her spine didn’t exist… then snapped straight.”

“She ran at me again.”

“I grabbed the ax.”

“I hit her.”

“She got back up.”

“I hit her again.”

“She got back up.”

“Again.”

“And again.”

“It felt like forever.”

He broke down completely.

I was disgusted. Either he believed this, or he was the best liar I'd ever seen.

He'd already confessed on camera. I left the room, finished my paperwork, and went home.

The next day I came in still thinking about the crime scene photos. Still angry.

First thing I heard:

He killed himself. Strangled himself with his shirt.

Coward.

That's all I thought. A man kills his daughter and can't face consequences.

Lunch came around, and I decided to stop by the morgue. I figured I should speak with the mortician after everything they'd had to handle.

The moment I walked into the lobby, something felt wrong.

No one there.

Not a single person.

Usually at least someone sits at the desk or works in the back.

I kept walking.

I'd been back there a hundred times.

The cold room doors lined the wall.

One was open.

I don't know why I walked toward it.

Inside was Emily.

Or what should have been Emily.

The body was completely deflated. Like a discarded skin suit. Torn open where the ax strikes had been. Empty. Like an old coat with holes worn through it.

I pulled the tray farther out.

That's when I heard a thud from the chemical closet.

I couldn't even call out.

I walked to the door slowly.

The mortician stood inside.

Just standing there.

Looking around the room like they'd never seen it before.

Then they stepped forward.

Their leg lifted wrong.

Like there were no bones inside it.

Like they were guessing how walking worked.

I ran.

I'm not proud of it, but I ran.

I got in my car, locked the doors, locked the windows.

And now I'm sitting here typing this.

I don't know what to do.

I don't know if I'm losing my mind.

And I don't know if I left the morgue door open…

or if something opened it behind me.
