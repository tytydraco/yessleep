
 
The listing on the dark-web-adjacent job board was simple: Night Watch. Krusty Crewing Distribution. $4,000/week. No experience necessary. Must be comfortable with "unconventional" silence. I was drowning in debt, so I ignored the red flags. The facility was a windowless, concrete monolith built over the exact site where the original "Krusty Krab" flagship restaurant had burned down in a "freak grease fire" in 1999. My supervisor, a man whose skin looked like wet parchment, handed me a flashlight and a heavy iron key. "The mascots stay in the 'Fun Zone' basement," he croaked. "If you hear them laughing, turn up your radio. Don't let the frequencies match."
 
 
The interior of the warehouse didn't match the exterior. The hallways were curved like the inside of a shell, and the walls were coated in a thick, translucent slime that smelled of old pennies and saltwater. My station was a glass-walled booth overlooking a "recreation" of the original restaurant. It looked perfect—too perfect. The tables were set, the grills were polished, and the air was freezing. I felt like a specimen in an aquarium, being watched by the darkness beyond the glass.
 
 
At 1:15 AM, the monitors flickered to life. I hadn't turned them on. Camera 12 showed the "Mascot Storage." In the corner sat the Spongebob suit. It wasn't the friendly version from the cartoons. It was massive—nearly eight feet tall—with a porous texture that seemed to pulse. It was slumped against a crate, but its eyes—large, plastic, unblinking yellow spheres—were fixed directly on the camera lens. I zoomed in. There were small, black veins spider-webbing through the plastic of the eyes.
 
 
I found a cassette player in the desk. It contained a tape labeled "TOM B. - VOICE SESSIONS - DO NOT REPLAY." Tom was the original voice actor hired for the live mascot shows in the 90s. The tape started with him laughing, doing the iconic high-pitched chuckle. But then, the laughter didn't stop. It went on for four minutes, getting faster and more desperate, until it turned into a wet, choking sound. "The foam," Tom’s voice whispered through the static. "The foam is breathing for me now. It’s so cold in the suit, but the grease is so warm."
 
 
Movement on Camera 4. The Patrick mascot—a bloated, faceless pink pyramid of heavy fabric—was standing in the middle of the dining room. It hadn't been there ten seconds ago. It didn't move like a human in a suit; it moved like a starfish, its limbs undulating in a slow, sickening wave. It was dragging something behind it—a heavy, rusted anchor that left a deep groove in the floorboards. There was no one inside the suit. It was hollow, yet it had weight. It had intent.
 
 
I heard music. Not the upbeat tiki music from the show, but a distorted, slowed-down version played on a cracked clarinet. It was coming from the vents. I looked up and saw a grey, four-legged shadow scuttling across the ceiling of the warehouse. It was the Squidward mascot, but its neck was elongated, stretched out like a piece of dead taffy. Its eyes were wet and human, weeping a thick, black bile that hissed when it hit the floor. It wasn't walking; it was skittering.
 
 
I broke into the "Manager’s Office" to find a weapon. Instead, I found a medical file. It detailed "The Secret Formula." It wasn't for food. It was a chemical compound used to bond organic matter to synthetic fibers. The original restaurant hadn't burned down; it had been a laboratory. They weren't making burgers; they were trying to create "Eternal Entertainers"—creatures that didn't need food or sleep, only "The it.
 
Squeak. Squeak. Squeak. The sound of rubber boots on linoleum. It was coming from the hallway directly behind my booth. I didn't turn around. I looked at the reflection in the glass. The Spongebob mascot was standing in the doorway. It was vibrating so hard the image was blurred. Its square mouth opened, stretching wider than the suit’s frame should allow. A row of jagged, needle-like teeth made of calcified coral lined the darkness of its throat.
 
 
I grabbed my radio to call for help. Instead of the supervisor, I heard a chorus of screams. It was the sound of a thousand children screaming in a theme park, but the pitch was being shifted up and down rhythmically. A voice cut through the noise: "Who lives in a nightmare under the sea?" The response was a guttural, bass-heavy roar that shook the glass of my booth: "THE ONE WHO WATCHES."
 
 
The floor began to vibrate. A heavy iron hatch in the center of the dining room floor—the "Grease Trap"—swung open. A thick, black sludge began to pour out, and with it, hundreds of small, animate spatulas. They moved like insects, clicking their metal heads against the floor, swarming toward the stairs. I realized the warehouse was a hive, and I was the only thing in it that was still "raw."
 
 
I found a VHS tape in the manager's safe labeled "SDR-86: THE RETIREMENT." I played it on the tiny monitor. It showed the mascot characters in a dark room. They were standing around a man tied to a chair. It was Tom, the voice actor. The Spongebob mascot leaned over him and literally melted its yellow foam over his head. Tom’s muffled screams turned into the cartoon’s laughter as his body was absorbed into the porous material. The tape ended with the mascot turning to the camera and saying, "I'm ready for a new skin."
 
 
I ran toward the emergency exit, but the air became thick and humid. I entered a section of the warehouse encased in a giant glass dome—the "Treedome." Inside, a mascot of Sandy the Squirrel was hanging from a dead oak tree by its neck. But it wasn't dead. The suit was twitching. The glass helmet was filled with salt water, and inside, a human face was pressed against the glass, eyes wide, lungs filled with brine, still blinking, still alive after.
 
The warehouse lights flickered to a deep purple. I ran through a storage area filled with glowing, translucent umbrellas hanging from the ceiling. They weren't decorations. They were stinging. Each time I brushed past one, I felt a jolt of electricity that carried a memory—a memory of being a child, laughing at the TV, while something behind the screen was hungrily counting my heartbeats.
 
 
I reached the loading dock, but the door was welded shut. Next to it was a massive vat labeled "CHUM." It wasn't fish guts. It was human clothing—shredded uniforms, security badges, and shoes. Thousands of them. I realized I wasn't the first "night watchman." I was the seasonal "delivery."
 
 
The Patrick mascot cornered me in the breakroom. It leaned forward, and the seam in its chest split open. There was no person inside, just a hollow cavity lined with pulsing, purple flesh and rows of suction cups. It tried to pull me into itself, to make me its "spine." I managed to stab it with a letter opener, and it leaked a fluid that smelled of the deepest, oldest part of the ocean.
 
 
The building began to groan. The "anchor" Patrick was dragging wasn't just metal; it was connected to the foundation of my own mind. I started seeing things—bubbles rising in my vision, the sound of a foghorn blowing inside my skull. The mascots weren't just physical; they were a memetic infection. The more I looked at them, the more "underwater" I became.
 
 
I finally met "The Boss." In the main office sat a massive, red, armored shape. It was the Mr. Krabs mascot, but it was three times the size of a man. Its claws were real crab shells, sharpened to razors. It didn't speak; it just clicked its claws in a rhythmic code. Snip. Snip. Snip. It was counting the money it had made from selling the souls of its "crew."
 
 
I fell to my knees, gasping. My lungs felt like they were filling with sand. I looked at my hands; they were turning yellow and porous. The "Spongebob" entity was standing over me, its hand—soft, yellow, and damp—touching my forehead. "Don't worry," it whispered in Tom's perfect, cheerful voice. "The costume doesn't hurt after the first hour. You just have to forget how to breathe air."
 
 
With the last of my strength, I grabbed a flare gun from the emergency kit. I didn't aim for the mascots. I aimed for the overhead sprinklers. But they didn't spray water. They sprayed the "Secret Formula." The mascots hissed in delight as the chemical bath hit them, making their foam skins grow and expand, knitting them together into one giant, multi-limbed monstrosity of felt and flesh.
 
 
I woke up on the shore of the beach, miles away from the warehouse. My car was gone. My phone was gone. The warehouse was a pile of ash, the "grease fire" having finally finished the job. But when I look in the mirror, my eyes aren't the same. They have small, black veins in the corners. And every time I try to speak, I can only produce a high-pitched, echoing laugh. I am "ready." And they are coming back for the rest of the cast.
