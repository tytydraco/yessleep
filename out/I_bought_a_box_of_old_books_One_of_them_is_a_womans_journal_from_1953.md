Hey everybody! So, I love to collect old, forgotten literature of all kinds. My house actually came with this massive built-in bookshelf when I bought it a few years ago and it has become the home to most of my collection. Collecting these old books and things has become a bit of an obsession but I guess it’s better than crack or something lol.

 But the other day I went to a local flea market (The best place to find whatever old relic you could possibly want) and I came across a stand that had a large cardboard box absolutely FULL of old books. On top was a piece of paper that said:

“$25 for all.”

 I easily got the lady talked down to $15, which I was pretty proud of myself for.

When I got home I laid all the books out on my living room floor. Most of them were ones you’d expect to be in an old attic. Some farm manuals, cook books, a couple old church hymnals, stuff like that. 

But there was some other fun stuff in there! Books like Dragonwyk, The Big Clock, and Peyton Place (some pretty neat finds if you're in the same niche hobby).

 But, there was a journal in the box as well. It has a dark, brown leather cover. It kind of felt like an old baseball glove. The opening page has a name written in neat cursive.

Ruth Calloway.

And even though it may be wrong to share what this lady wrote in her journal on the internet, especially someone who has probably been dead for decades. But after reading through some of it, I think that it needs to be shared. 

So that’s what this post is for. I will be typing out her entries exactly as they are written. I just want to share the, odd, things that appear in this journal. Let me know what you guys think!

May 3, 1953

This morning has been very quiet. A kind of quiet that settles over the farm once the wind decides to rest for a while. I sat at the kitchen table looking across the fields with my coffee I had made just after sunrise. The dew that rests on the grass reflected the burnt orange sunrise beautifully. 

I have decided to write things down again. The house had felt horribly empty the past few months, and I thought putting words to paper might help pass the long hours.

Thomas always gave me grief for keeping things bottled up inside all the time. Perhaps he was right about that. As he was with most things.

I go out to his grave every morning after tending to the chickens. The ground beneath that large oak is still a bit uneven, though the grass has started growing over it some. I brush away the loose dirt that tends to gather at the headstone and I sit with him awhile. I pray and speak with him. 

It’s strange how this place felt so full of life and now suddenly feels large and hollow.

The farm is struggling a bit this season, though I suppose that is nothing new. The soil seems to do as it pleases, no matter how hard a person works it. I never was much for doing the dirty work on the farm. Still, I will keep trying.

Times come that I think about leaving this place, but I do not believe I could ever truly do it. Thomas always loved this land. If I were to go, it would feel as if I left him here alone.

I suppose it will just be the two of us for a while longer.

May 6, 1953

The chickens were restless today. The eight of my hens fussed and paced more than usual as I was gathering eggs. At one point all of them went silent as they stared out of the hen house toward the cornfield. I am unsure if it is because of the storm that the strong winds are blowing in or if maybe I have a slimy fox prowling around. 

During my visit with Thomas I noticed the grass had grown some more since the last rain. At least this land has shown it can grow something. I try to be thankful for the revelation.

While I was sitting there after our prayer, I noticed the wind moving through the far end of the field, bending the tops of the stalks back and forth. But strangely, it didn’t seem to reach the house or yard around it. 

I sat there suddenly aware that the air felt completely still.

I remember thinking how peculiar it looked. The field swayed and danced while everything around me remained quiet.

It did not last long. After a few minutes the wind came through the yard and everything seemed to return to normal. Perhaps living in this quiet house alone has led me to simply notice small things more than before. 

I suppose when someone spends too much time alone their mind begins to wander.

\*\*\*

May 7, 1953

The storm last night has changed everything. 

It rolled in just as any other storm before it. I sat at the kitchen table with only my reading lantern and a book. I have never been much for storms. Thomas always knew that and would stay close to keep me at ease. 

I sat with my book attempting to read as the loose boards of the barn smacked violently against themselves. Storms tend to have a way of making a place feel smaller. The whole house seemed to settle around me as the wind continued to push and pull against the walls. I would close my eyes from time to time trying to imagine Thomas’ hands gripping my shoulders for comfort.

It must have been sometime past midnight when I first heard it.

I believed it to be the wind at first.

But then it came.

A small sound. Thin and wavering. Like crying. Muffled by the storm.

I sat there a moment longer, listening closely. The storm was beginning to quiet, though the rain still fell steady on the roof.

Then I hear it again.

Crying.

It was coming from outside.

I took the lantern and stepped onto the porch. The rain had slowed to a light drizzle, though the air still smelled heavy and wet from the storm. 

Then I heard the sound again.

There, resting beside the railing, was a bundle wrapped tightly in an old brown blanket.

I cannot say how long I stood there before moving.

When I knelt down and pulled the blanket back, I saw the face of a newborn child staring up at me. He couldn’t have been more than a few days old.

As soon as I lifted him into my arms his crying had softened, as though the warmth had comforted him. His eyes opened slowly and locked in with mine. My heart felt as if it melted completely.

There were no tire tracks in the mud near the house. 

No footprints along the path.

Nothing to show how someone could have brought him here in the middle of such a storm.

I stood there for a long time beneath the porch roof, listening to the rain and watching the calm begin to hit the fields after the storm.

He’s sleeping in a small basket near the stove while I write this. I don’t know who left him here.

But I cannot bring myself to believe it was done by accident.

\*\*\*

May 7, 1953

As soon as the sun rose, I grabbed the baby and made my way out to the porch to investigate. Just as I had believed last night, no footprints. No tracks. I couldn’t figure out how someone would have gotten the child to the house.

Am I wrong to feel unbothered by this?

Thomas and I had always wanted children. We had always dreamt of a yard full of babies. For whatever reason the Lord must not have seen it as the right time. But now? The mysterious appearance of this child must be a sign of some kind. Tears were building in my eyes as I looked to the sky and thanked God for the immense blessing he has given me. I looked down to the sleeping baby in my arms and the revelation hit me like nothing ever had.

Samuel.

My father was the one who told me the name Samuel meant that the Lord had heard someone’s prayers. Just as Hannah’s prayers were heard in the Bible, it seems perhaps mine have been heard as well.

\*\*\*

May 10, 1953

Word travels quickly in a place like this.

Many of the folks in town had seen Samuel and me going to the general store to get some baby supplies. Before I had even finished grabbing what I needed, Mrs. Pritchard and her son had spotted me near the counter. Her eyes nearly jumped from her head when she saw Samuel in my arms.

She asked where he had come from.

Before I could properly think, a lie about Samuel belonging to my sister in Kentucky slipped out.

I said that my sister had fallen ill and she needed someone to look after the boy for a while. I do not have a sister and lying like that made my stomach curdle.

Mrs. Pritchard did not seem entirely convinced of my story, but she nodded and accepted all the same.

Her son, Todd, seemed quite enamored with Samuel. His eyes were locked on him as if he was studying him. 

Samuel let out a small whine in my arms. Todd raised an eyebrow as he pulled on Mrs. Pritchard’s sleeve.

“He talks funny Mama.”

Mrs. Pritchard and I chuckled and it broke the tension. 

\*\*\*

By the time Sunday service began I could feel the eyes on Samuel and me. The whispers began as soon as we entered through the doors. 

A widowed woman living alone does not suddenly appear with a newborn child without stirring questions. 

After the service had ended a group of them gathered near the church steps.

Mrs. Pritchard being one of them. Along with old Mr. Jenkins and the Williams sisters. They seemed to try their best to sound polite, though a mixture of curiosity and judgement filled their eyes.

They asked about Samuel again. Where he had come from. How long he would be staying. 

I told them the same story.

My sister.

Kentucky.

Just until she was well again.

They seemed satisfied enough with my explanation so Samuel and I made our way to the car. As we walked I could see Todd with a group of four or five other children. I couldn’t tell what he was saying but he was subtly pointing his eyes in our direction. The other children would steal glances as well. Some of them with an awe filled look.

I looked down and could see that Samuel’s eyes seemed to be glancing back at them as well.

I ignored the children’s glances and got Samuel and me back to the farm

May 13, 1953

Things have really started to turn around for the better here on the farm. 

This morning I went out to gather eggs from the chickens as I normally do. When I opened the door to the henhouse I nearly dropped my basket when I saw what was waiting for me.

I had to count twice to be certain.

16 eggs.

I couldn’t believe it! Those hens have not produced like that since Thomas was around to tend to them properly. I stood there for a long moment simply looking at them before gathering them into the basket.

Samuel sat in the small basket I had made for him near the coop door as I worked. His quiet eyes just followed those chickens as they made their way through the yard. 

Afterward I went to tend to the garden behind the house. 

The beans have begun sprouting far quicker than I had expected! The rows that seemed weak and on the verge of dying only a few days ago now appeared strong and bright green! Even the soil felt softer as I weeded the area around them. 

I even noticed that Mr. Jenkins’ cornfield across the yard seemed to be fuller than I remember them being by this time of year!

Thomas used to tell me how the land can always surprise you when you least expect it. 

I took Samuel with me as I walked along the edge of the flourishing cornfield. The joy in my heart felt almost too great to contain. 

For a moment I thought I had heard something in the field. Something moving deeper in the rows as I ran my hands over the sharp leaves.

Samuel grew very quiet in my arms and I noticed his eyes were fixed deep into the rows of corn as well.

But as I looked I couldn’t see anything. 

Just the wind and the corn. But all the same my motherly instincts are beginning to settle in because I brought Samuel back inside after catching Thomas up on the good news. 

I suppose it may be a foolish thought, but I cannot help but feel like there is something good that has come to this land. The farm just feels fuller somehow. As if the land has finally been able to breathe again.

And it all seems to have stemmed from the enormous blessing that has been the presence of Samuel.

\*\*\*

May 18, 1953

The farm’s good fortune has continued these past few days. 

The hens have yet to slow their egg laying and the garden has grown strong enough that I only needed to spend time keeping ahead of the weeds. Even the weeds seem to be thinning out more and more. 

Samuel has taken well to the fresh air. We’ve been going out in the evenings to check the rows one last time before sunset. 

The corn has now grown tall enough that the wind makes the stalks dance like long rolling waves. The sound even reminds me of the ocean Thomas had told me about after one of his trips down south. 

I stood there for a while watching the field wave in the wind. 

That’s when I noticed the scarecrow.

It stood far enough out that I could not make out many details. 

At first I assumed that the farmer must have placed it out there to fend off any birds coming for his crops. I do not remember seeing it before, but there has been enough going on that I easily could have missed it.

Still, something seemed strange.

The wind blew heavily through the field, sending the corn swaying back and forth. Yet, the scarecrow did not seem to move.

Not even a little.

Eventually the wind changed and the corn shifted enough that I couldn’t see the scarecrow clearly from where I was standing. 

When I brought Samuel back into the house I couldn’t help but glance back to the field once more. For a brief moment I could have sworn the scarecrow seemed a row or two closer. But with the dimming sunlight I could not be certain.

\*\*\*

May 21, 1953

The farm is still flourishing. The farm is flourishing too well, actually. I pulled 30 eggs from the chicken coop this morning. 

30 eggs.

8 hens.

It is not the worst thing because it seems the folks around town are going through dry spells with their flocks. So, I have been able to provide some eggs by setting them at the end of the drive. But, it is not just their chickens.

Everyone’s gardens seem to have died off or are barely hanging to life. While mine, as well as Mr. Jenkins’ attached field is growing out of control! I have already been able to harvest many of my vegetables and they have somehow started showing blossoms once again!

I am grateful for the blessings God has given me, but my prayers have turned to questions on why only me? All while the rest of the town is struggling.

I believe the stress has grabbed a strong hold on me, because that scarecrow seems to have moved a few rows closer to the house. I don’t know why Mr. Jenkins would move it only a few rows. I would not think that would make much of a difference.

\*\*\*

May 24, 1953

Mrs. Pritchard stopped me after the church service again. 

She was asking me about what I am doing to my garden, and what I am feeding my chickens. She was not asking curiously. She seemed to be asking in animosity. 

I wish I could have given her more of my attention but I could not help but notice her son, Todd. He was hiding behind Mrs. Pritchard. Holding tightly to her dress. 

His eyes that used to look at Samuel with curiosity, or even infatuation, are now showing fear and unease. I look down to Samuel and his eyes are locked on to Todd. 

I thanked Mrs. Pritchard for her time and Samuel and I made our way to the car. 

On the way I saw Mr. Jenkins. He chuckled as I approached and he joked about how we were the only ones in town who seemed to know how to grow a sprout around here. I played along knowing I came to ask him about something very specific. 

The scarecrow.

Mr. Jenkins says he never put up a scarecrow.

Once Samuel and I arrived back at the farm, the scarecrow wasn’t in the field anymore.

\*\*\*

May 26, 1953

This morning, I brought Samuel to the porch so I could drink my coffee. As soon as we stepped outside, I noticed there was a line of flattened grass near the porch. When I went to investigate, I came to the realization that the line made a perfect circle around the house. 

It was as if something had been walking around the house the night before. 

As I was inspecting the path, I heard Samuel in his basket on the porch. 

He was looking towards the cornfield. 

Smiling.

Laughing.

I turned to face the field, and the scarecrow was back in the field. 

Closer.

It was standing maybe a dozen rows away from the yard. 

I could see the shape more clearly now. It resembled a man in the broadest sense, but the proportions were wrong. It was tall and thin, almost stick-like. Its arms hanging low enough that they nearly brushed the top of the corn. Its joints seemed like they would bend in different directions. But in multiple places like a twig. The wind blew the stalks, and yet the figure remained unchanged. I realized being this close that the corn wasn’t even touching the figure. It was as if the corn was bent away slightly. Trying to give it space.

What I had previously assumed to be clothing, seems to be strips of something dark somehow clinging to its frame. I tried to make out a face but its head was tilted in such a way that I could not see one clearly. I stared at it trying to figure out what strange material it had been made from. None came to mind.

I started looking for the post beneath it, a wooden beam a scarecrow ought to be strung up on. But the longer I stared, the more certain I became that there was no post at all. 

Samuel and I have yet to go back outside. 

\*\*\*

May 27, 1953

I did not sleep well last night. 

I stayed up far too late washing dishes that had already been cleaned and sweeping the floor three times over. I kept thinking that if I stepped back outside to investigate I could possibly find some sort of explanation. But the thought of going anywhere near that thing caused my stomach to tighten into a knot.

As the evening went on I tried to settle Samuel down for the night. The poor boy seemed restless. I’m sure the anxiety flowing through me didn’t sit right for him either. 

The sun had almost disappeared behind the corn field when I built up the courage to take a look outside. 

The figure was still there. 

It did not appear that it had moved at all since the last time I saw it. I stood there for several minutes watching through the window.

That’s when Samuel began to fuss in his basket. 

It was not a cry but almost a low humming noise.

I nearly dropped the curtain when I noticed the figure beginning to move.

It did not walk like another human would. Instead it folded itself forward through the corn, slow and deliberate. As if a folding ladder were alive and moving through the corn. 

First it leaned its long torso too far forward, then one of its legs bent at an odd angle. And suddenly the rest of its body was several rows closer

It folded again and again.

Once it got just inside of the yard it stopped. Now disturbingly still. It seems to be almost 10 feet tall. 

I could not see its face at first. That was until the long curve of its neck leaned in closer. 

Its face was missing any discernible features. It had indents of eyes, a nose and mouth. But there were no details. As if someone had begun forming a face from clay but was not able to finish.

Samuel began giggling and cooing in his basket.

The figure stopped moving as soon as soon as the laughing began.

Suddenly the figure lowered itself. Slowly, its long limbs folded beneath it until it rested on the ground. It stayed there for several minutes, completely still.

Almost as if it was kneeling. 

It was close enough now that I could see that its skin was grey and smooth, almost like rubber stretched around something underneath. And it had these black, thin, leathery strands hanging off of it. Rustling softly with the wind.

Samuel eventually grew quiet and fell asleep.

When I turned back to the figure it had resumed standing. It didn’t seem to be interested in coming any closer. 

It was as if it was… waiting.

I wrapped Samuel in my arms and we sat together in Thomas’ television chair. I had not sat in that chair since Thomas passed. I just sat there and held Samuel. Trying to remain calm until morning.

When morning came I looked back out the window. The same figure was in its place. But behind it, scattered through the corn, stood dozens more of the figures. 

All facing the house.

They all looked very similar to the original. Yet, they had their own unique qualities. Whether it was different places or directions, their limbs bent. Some had more or less of the leathery strands flowing in the wind.

The view is both mesmerizing and terrifying.

\*\*\*

May 29, 1953

None of them have moved. 

At least as far as I can tell.

Samuel and I had a different group of visitors today. This morning the yard began to fill with some of the vehicles from town. Cars and trucks pulling in with purpose.

Mrs. Pritchard, a few of the other farmers, and even Sheriff Jed.

They gathered near the porch yelling and screaming about how I must have poisoned their fields or am doing something illegal to my garden in order for it to flourish the way it has.

I did not dare to step outside. But I looked across the house to see if the figure was still standing in the yard. The mob of people must have noticed my glance and they followed suit. 

When they saw the figure I had a feeling of relief come across me. Because that means I have not lost my mind. 

But also a feeling of fear grew in my gut.

Sheriff Jed took the lead as the group of them stepped to the corner of the house for a better view. Their whispers and questions were not loud enough for me to make out. But the gasps when the figure took a slow folding step were discernible.

The sheriff quickly raised his gun and fired a single shot at the figure.

It didn’t flinch.

Though the sound of the gun made Samuel begin to cry. 

Suddenly the figure folded forward rapidly across the yard, covering distance much faster than before. The crowd scattered to the cars and began speeding out to get away. But the figure was able to catch one of the farmers’ trucks before he made it off the property.

It’s long, scrawny arm wrapped underneath and all the way around the cab of the truck. The metal collapsed with an awful grinding sound until the entire cab looked like nothing but a crushed tin can.

Even through the scene before me I realized that Samuel had stopped crying.

The figure placed the destroyed truck down and in its originally deliberate pace folded its way back to its place in the yard. 

Once it settled in its desired location I saw something else.

The rest of the figures in the field began to fold closer to the house until they were all at the edge of the corn.

May 31, 1953

The town folk have not returned.

The road out in front of the farm has remained empty as well. But I know that word has to have spread fast about what happened here. The farmer’s truck remains in the yard where the figure had crushed it. 

The figures have not left either. 

They stand at the edge of the corn sun up to sun down. I counted 37 of them at sunset. By the time the sun came up again there were more.

They have not wandered.

They have not spoke.

They simply face the house.

Watching.

Samuel slept for most of yesterday. He had been strangely calm the last couple of days. Once he woke this morning, I carried him to the window so he could feel some of the afternoon sun. 

The moment he looked to the field the figures began to move.

Not toward the house.

Downward.

One by one they fell into that kneeling pose that the first one performed a few days ago.

Samuel let out a joyful laugh at the sight before us. I should have been frightened by the disturbing nature of so many of those things kneeling in the field. But instead I felt something else.

Relief.

Almost as if I was beginning to understand what they are. At the very least understand that they have not come to harm my sweet Samuel.

Just after sunset I could hear movement outside. When I looked out the window I saw that several of the figures had moved closer throughout the yard. 

Almost all of them are out of the field now.

A few by the barn. 

One by the oak tree where Thomas’ grave lies.

And the first figure stands outside Samuel’s window.

Samuel was laying in his crib looking out at them. Reaching for the glass. Laughing.

I do not believe that these figures have come for the farm. 

Or the land.

Or even me.

I believe they came here for Samuel. Possibly even sent by God.

But tonight, for the first time, I think I understand something else. 

They haven’t gathered here to take Samuel. 

They are waiting.

That’s the final Journal entry. There’s nothing else after May 31st. No explanation as to what happened to Ruth or Samuel.

I tried doing a google search on them but I really couldn’t find much other than a farmhouse fire in the early 60s outside of town. 

One other thing I noticed when I finished reading. The back pages have impressions on them. As if someone had written on one of the page’s extremely hard. I couldn’t quite make them out at first but I think I figured it out.

It looks like someone wrote the same sentence over and over again.

“They are still here.”
