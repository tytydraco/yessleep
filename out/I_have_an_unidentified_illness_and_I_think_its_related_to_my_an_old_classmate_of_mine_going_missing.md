It was exactly 06:00, and I was installing a motion detector next to the door. I had bought it a few days prior, hoping I wouldn’t need it but knowing better. I could hear Beth’s footsteps approach as I drilled in the last screw attaching it to the wall. She didn’t say anything, but I could feel her glaring at me with a gaze sharp as obsidian.

I waved a hand in front of it and the device erupted into a loud beeping that stung the ears.

“Liza, what do you think you’re doing?” Her voice sounded more tired than annoyed.

I looked at her with a neutral face. My exhausted expression told her enough.

I knew this wouldn’t stop the sleepwalking. It’d just make my exhaustion worse in the long run, but it would keep me from ending up at the lakehouse for at least a couple of days.

After the detector was set up, I spent the rest of the morning nursing a cup of coffee while trying and failing to recreate the spiral from last night as Beth stared at the newspaper in her hand.

“One for you,” she said, “Ten across. A type of rock transformed under heat and pressure.”

There was a waver in her voice, like she was trying too hard to pretend everything was normal.

“Metamorphic,” I said, putting the drawing on the—

Where there had once been a pile of notes, there was now nothing. I looked around the room properly for the first time and saw the absences I had not noticed before. My notebooks, gone. Timeline, gone. Geological reports, gone. Everything was gone. I clenched my teeth together.

“Beth,” I said, forcing a smile. “Where are my notebooks?”

Beth didn’t look up from the paper. “In your desk. I did some cleaning.”

I grumbled, moving to my room and opening the drawer. Nothing. I slammed the wooden surface with the palm of my hand and cursed.

I walked back into the kitchen, where Beth gently turned a page without acknowledgment.

“Wasn’t in the desk.”

“Hmm,” she hummed. “Strange. Maybe I put them somewhere else.”

I sighed. “Beth, please.”

She finally lowered her paper a little.

“This is all I have,” I said, wiping away sleep from my eyes, “I worked on those for a year. Where did you put them?”

Beth remained motionless for a moment. After a small eternity, she finally dropped the paper. Her eyes were red. She hadn't been reading at all.

We didn’t speak much the rest of the day. While Beth had returned my belongings, I wasn’t in any mood to talk to her. As we made our way to Henderson’s party that evening, both of us remained silent. We took the four turns and found it sitting there, same as always. I didn’t have the energy to point out how impossible it was.

The porch was smaller than I remembered from the outside, or maybe it was the crowd funneling through the door that made it feel that way. I watched people cross the threshold ahead of me, laughing, jostling, unbothered. Normal people. Normal night. The queue moved forward and the two of us got closer to the door.

She squeezed my arm.

“Beth, I’m fine,” I said curtly, hesitating for another fraction of a second, then stepping through.

I was fine, briefly.

Then, the world tilted. The floor lurched under me like the deck of a ship cresting a wave, and I had to grab the door frame with both hands just to stay upright. My vision fragmented at the edges and there was a sound I couldn't locate, somewhere between a frequency and a pressure, like the inside of my skull was becoming a smaller space. I was aware, distantly, of Beth saying my name.

Then it passed. Like a door closing. I straightened up. Beth was watching me with an expression she was trying to keep neutral. I smiled at her. She avoided my gaze.

To the naked eye, the interior looked superficially ordinary. Bare concrete and loose tools gave the impression someone had intended to renovate. Just beneath that, I could feel it. There was some quality in the air that gave me the sense that the building was breathing at a frequency just below hearing, as if something vast was very close, separated from us by a membrane so thin you could press your finger against it and feel it give.

I tried to shake off the feeling, pulling the orange bottle of prescription medicine from my pocket and taking one. It didn’t hit as hard this time.

Twenty minutes after entering, Catherine appeared at my elbow. She'd clearly spent time on her appearance – freshly curled hair, careful makeup. The mascara decorated serious eyes that moved to the walls and then back to me.

“You came,” she said, barely above a whisper as she led me to a different corner of the house.

“You invited me.”

She nodded slowly, pensively. “Did you feel that, when we entered?”

I looked at her sideways and whispered back. “Yeah, I thought it was just—I mean, you, too?”

Another nod, “Liza, I don’t think we should be here.”

I sighed, rubbing my eyebrows with one hand. “You and me both,”

I glanced back at Beth who was staring at us intently.

“There’s something really, really wrong about this place,” she continued with a low voice. “I don’t know how to put it. I’ve never been good with words, but I—”

“Everything okay?” Beth asked as she practically stepped between us.

“Yeah, we’re good! Just catching up,” Catherine answered, the cheeriness back like she turned on a switch.

Beth glanced between us. “Ethan’s here. Do you want to talk to him?” I looked at Catherine. Her smile faded slightly, but she nodded all the same. “Sure, you go on ahead.”

Beth led me by the hand as we weaved in between the people, nearly spilling someone’s beer before speeding on with a quick apology.

Ethan Walsh was leaning against the counter, beer in hand. His eyes were narrow, his cheeks a little sharper, and a goatee had fully formed around his mouth, which took getting used to the most of all.

“Liza Thompson,” he said, setting the red plastic cup aside. “I can hardly believe it.”

Beth let me go as if dropping me off at an after-school day care. I rolled my eyes at her and bumped fists with Ethan. I did my best to focus on our conversation, but my gaze kept drifting.

I couldn’t stop watching the walls. The way certain corners seemed darker than the light justified. The way sound behaved slightly wrong in the left half of the room, voices arriving a fraction of a second late, like an echo with nowhere to echo from. The pattern was everywhere, in the furniture, in the wallpaper, in the arrangement of M&Ms in the bowl next to the drinks.

Out of the corner of my eye, I saw Beth continue to watch us as she talked with a mutual acquaintance. I snapped the rubber band around my wrist as I watched her.

Catherine had come back. I could see her hovering beside me, waiting for me to finish talking to Ethan. I turned to her and she was again staring at me with a serious expression.

“Liza, can you come for a moment? It’s important.” I glanced back at Ethan for a moment, excusing myself.

The two of us continued to a far corner of the living room as I kept Beth in the corner of my vision. I passed a wooden partition with rectangular holes arranged in a familiar pattern.

“You were about to say something earlier,” I said. Catherine looked up at me without turning my way as we walked. I continued. “I need to be honest with you, I’ve known about this place for a year now, and every attempt to classify the materials has failed. It doesn’t make any sense, but—I don’t know.”

I looked at her more directly now. She studied my face with doe eyes.

“Cath, I think we should just forget it, okay? It’s just a house,” the words rang hollow. I wanted Beth to be right. No matter how much I did, though, my mind could not agree.

Catherine’s gaze narrowed as she looked at me. She held up the palm of her hand without a word. In the middle sat something small and cream-colored.

I stared at it for a moment. “What is—”

“It was out back by the cement mixer.”

A molar. Human. My nostrils flared as I felt something tearing at the seams of my cognition. I looked back up at Catherine. She kept a straight face, but her eyes were glistening. Beth was back again.

“Hey, sorry to interrupt.” She wasn't sorry. “Liza, you've been inside for a while. Do you want to get some air? It's getting a bit stuffy in here.”

I looked at her. “I'm fine.”

“It'll just be a minute.” Her hand found my arm.

Catherine's smile didn't move but something behind her eyes did. “We were actually just in the middle of something.”

“Oh, I know! I just thought—” Beth gestured vaguely at the room. "It's warm in here and Liza gets—"

“She said she’s fine. We’re just finishing up.” Catherine smiled.

A thump came from above. I stared at the ceiling for a moment, then shook my head. Beth withdrew her hand. “Okay.” She smiled at Catherine with the particular warmth people use when they mean the opposite. “Sorry, you two carry on.”

She didn't leave. She drifted a few feet away and found something to look at on her phone.

Catherine watched her go, then turned back to me. Whatever she had been about to say had receded somewhere behind her eyes. She looked down at the molar in her palm, closing her fingers around it slowly.

“There's something I need to tell you,” she said, quieter now. “About Ronnie. About why I—”

“Liza.” Beth again, closer than she should have been. “Can I get you anything? How about we go get a drink?”

Catherine's expression tightened. I felt something tightening in me too as I glanced between them.

“Beth, she has a drink. She’s holding it,” Catherine said, pleasantly.

Beth looked at her for a moment. “I know, I just figured she’d be tired of that cheap beer by now. I know my sister.”

The silence between them lasted exactly long enough to be uncomfortable. That's when we heard it. Another thump from above. All three of us looked up at the same time.

Footsteps passed above us. They moved from one side of attic to the other. Then, a second pair followed. Slow, deliberate.

We weren’t the only ones looking. The once noisy crowd had died down to a whisper as everyone looked at each other and the ceiling in confusion.

I noticed it for the first time then. The house, despite having a second floor, lacked any and all staircases or ladders to get up there. Almost as if, at one point in its existence, the house had decided that the second floor was over and done with and erased them all. Or perhaps it had been built like that from the start? I clenched my fists, stepping a little closer to Beth and Cath.

Someone cleared their voice, interrupting the silent mumbles of the crowd.

Henderson, wanting to be the tough guy in front of the girls, had climbed onto a table and loudly announced he was going up there to check it out. He held a metal folding ladder under one arm. I strode over expressionlessly.

“If you’re going, then I’m coming too,” I said, more as a command than a suggestion.

Henderson looked down and smiled. I pursed my lips and looked up at the ceiling as I tried to keep my legs steady.

A considerable number of guests stood around outside in a circle as Mike set up the large folding ladder at the front of the house and leaning it into one of the dark windows. I could have sworn it was closed before.

We ascended the ladder slowly. I glanced back down at Beth for a moment, who nodded my way. I cursed under my breath, inhaling before finally hopping over the edge of the windowsill and nearly bumping into Henderson. He handed me a flashlight and continued down a doorway on the left.

“I’ll do this side; you go check out the other. Tell me if you see anything… interesting.” He called out. I cursed Mike under my breath, too, then continued to the doorway on my right.

The air was stuffy but carried an undertone of something sweet. Ripe figs, old paper and something else that I didn't have a word for. I continued inside, flashlight sweeping across floating dust.

The room was an office once. A desk, a collapsed cabinet, papers curled at the edges like dead leaves. I moved the flashlight slowly across the walls and stopped.

There was a staircase.

Not a real one. A drawing, covering most of one wall from floor to ceiling, rendered in something dark that might have been charcoal or might have been something else. It was just like the drawing in the notebook. It spiraled downward from the top of the wall, each step slightly narrower than the last, disappearing into a vanishing point at the bottom that the eye kept trying to follow and couldn't quite reach.

I stood there looking at it for a long time. The vanishing point at the bottom of the stairs kept pulling my eye downward and I kept letting it. The spiral was the same one. The same inverted Fibonacci, the same wrong curve. I had drawn it a hundred times without being able to get it right and here it was, perfected, even better than what I had unconsciously drawn into the notebook.

At the base of the stairs, someone had written five words.

The door goes both ways.

As if on cue, I heard the creaking of wood from the other side of the attic, snapping me out of my trance. I stopped and listened for a moment.

“Henderson?”

No answer.

I crept over to the other side of the attic, past the window where we had come in. The room was smaller than the one I had been in. More broken furniture lined the walls. I noticed a drop of blood against the windowsill, opposite the one we had entered, and wondered if whoever or whatever was up there had already fled when we entered. I scanned around the room and realized Mike was nowhere to be seen.

I double checked, shouted his name again. Nothing. On the ground, there were footsteps in the dust leading to a chest at the far corner. I approached cautiously. Each step made the wood under my feet creak. The feeling from before was back, stronger now. I could feel the house breathing into my ears, making my head buzz. Finally, I shone my flashlight over the edge of the sturdy studded wooden box, desperate to see what he had found.

There were polaroids. It took me a moment to register what I was looking at. Pictures of children. All of them. Dozens of photos of sleeping kids, taken in the dark with a flash. I mouthed a curse as my heart pounded hard enough to make my chest ache.

I pulled one out that drew my attention. Something about it gnawed at me. I brought the flashlight closer.

A black-haired girl, eyes fully closed. A bedroom I didn't recognize at first. Dark walls, a window with curtains I couldn't quite place. Something stuffed and worn sitting beside the pillow.

Something finally clicked as I realized what it was.

I'd had him since I was four. I'd called him Mr. Muffins. I’d loved that bunny to pieces. My mom eventually had to sew a new eye on, but the replacement didn’t fully match. I hadn't thought about it in years.

I didn't move for a long moment, just staring at its button face.

That was my room. That was my bed. The child sleeping in it, face soft and unguarded in the flashlight's glow—

That was me.

The world tilted. I couldn't tell if it was the house or something colder, something that started in my stomach and moved outward. I turned the polaroid over with a hand that wasn't quite steady. A date on the back. December 2011. The month that—

I turned it back. I looked at my own sleeping face for a moment longer than I wanted to before sticking it in my pocket with a trembling hand. I continued digging through the box. So many children, so many—Something else caught my attention. One different from the others.

It was a photo of a rusty metal staircase, not unlike the drawings. It was standing in the middle of a forest. That was bizarre enough, but the forest looked wrong somehow. I couldn’t describe it any better than that it looked like someone had done their best to create trees from a description without ever seeing one themselves. I turned this one around to see the date.

“7 Apr. 23.”

Today.

I cursed and took a step back, making the board behind me creak. It was identical to the sound that had brought me here.

I covered my mouth as I saw it.

Out of the corner of my eye. In the corner of the room. It sat, barely visible. I could see its chest slowly rising. It was not Henderson.

I dropped the polaroid and ran back to the window. I climbed out and slid down the ladder, promptly tripping onto the dirt as I reached the bottom, another wave of vertigo hitting me. I grabbed my waist with both arms as people approached me.

The one in the middle crouched in front of me. “Liza. Liza.” I blinked. The crowd was gone. The lights inside were off, replaced by a flashing, colored light illuminating Beth’s silhouette.

“Jesus, you’re back. You’re okay, you’re okay.” She embraced me, and I could see the remains of dried tears in the corners of her eyes.

I looked up at the other two individuals. Police officers and one paramedic. I was illuminated by the red and blue lights of their patrol car. People were talking to me. I think I answered. The paramedic held fingers up in front of my eyes.

My head spun, a splitting ache making the edges of my vision throb and I finally put forth the question that had been gathering.

“What—” I shook my head, “What time is it?”

The taller of the two officers looked at his watch. “3:47 AM.” I blinked.

The paramedic shrugged, putting their things back into their bag and getting back into the ambulance as the officer stepped closer.

“Liza Thompson, we would like to ask you some questions regarding tonight's events. Would you mind coming to the station with us?”
