“This house has a pulsating heart. Like it’s a living beast. We need to destroy that heart to leave.”

The hot southern lady tried to explain what she’s learned over the centuries of being here.

She said that this was originally just a hill. Then a decrepit house appeared. The cornfields appeared around her over time. The house was built up and improved through no action of her own. She could see how the world developed around her, and she had no role in its improvement.

All she brought with her was that book.

“So what do I call you? You’re like, God and Satan, right?”

I was met with silence upon my questioning.

“So like… if you’re God *and* Satan… I could call you. Gotan… No, no. Sod! Sod works for me.”

Again, she didn’t respond.

“Alright, Sod. What do I need to do?”

Sod was tight-lipped. 

“Assumptions are all I possess.”

And a fat ass. Wait… I probably shouldn’t think of her like that. Women are more than that. But is God a woman? Is that why the world is fucked up?

I shook my head from those sexist thoughts and tried to focus on the situation at hand.

“What’s the plan, then?”

She walked back over to the porch and sat back down in the rocking chair before speaking again.

“I’ve made it to the heart, I’ve tried to destroy it, but nothing *I* do has any particular effect on the beast.”

“So… this house has a heart? That’s a little… stupid, no?”

Sod gave me a blank stare as she picked up the disgusting book to read it again.

“Whenever you are ready to go inside, I’ll be here.”

I wasn’t sure what she meant, so I strolled around the house. It was fully furnished with family portraits of some incredibly ordinary-looking people. Notably, I didn’t see any photos of Sod.

I checked the bedrooms. No heart.

I checked the bathrooms, took a quick tinkle, but no heart.

I checked the attic. It was covered in dust and slightly creepy, but no heart.

I then stared at the basement door. I’ve never been a fan of basements, so I figured I’d save it for last, and if my knowledge of horror tropes means anything, the heart would be down here.

I flicked the switch to the basement light. A singular lightbulb illuminated the darkness below the steps. I could hear its faint hum from the top of the stairs.

One foot met the first step, and it creaked—we were hitting all of the horror trope boxes here. A few steps followed, and then I was officially in the basement.

The floor was concrete, and the lightbulb was working overtime to fight the darkness; shadows still crept in the corners. There were cardboard boxes in piles and random tools scattered on industrial shelving. It smelt of mildew and paint.

Investigating the area, I quickly discovered that there was nothing of note in the boxes except for old family photos and random junk the family didn’t feel like putting up.

I checked in the washer and dryer, because that was the last place I’d put a heart for a house. Now that I say that, I believe this situation has officially jumped the shark.

After not finding anything, I approached the southern woman, still reading her creepy ass book on the porch.

“Alright, where’s the heart?”

I said, a little out of breath, but tried to hide my exhaustion. Climbing those stairs took more out of me than I expected, but I didn’t want to look like a wimp in front of Sod.

She simply shook her head.

“Isn’t here.”

Instantly, I was annoyed.

“What do you mean it’s not here, you’d just said—” Then it dawned on me. It wasn’t *here* here. It was in that other place she showed me. “Oh, no. Oh, no, no, no. I’m not going back there.”

She shrugged.

“I hope those… sandwiches… can hold you over for the rest of eternity then.”

I looked back to Jayco, the gasless queen that she was, and knew I had already used half a loaf of Wonderbread. My original plan was to eat the rest of the bologna rolled up with some mustard when I ran out. 

I guess there was a lot of corn. But I’d rather die than eat corn without butter. The fact that the cornfield was actually a collection of eviscerated human flesh sacks made it a bit unappetizing as well.

There is a moment in every man’s life when he is faced with adversity. That there is a challenge, and one must rise to it. Conquer it. Grab life by the horns, and show her no mercy. Staring at Jayco, my love, my life. I knew what I needed to do. Not everyone in their lifetime gets to experience what I am about to experience. Hell, I may be the first person in *history* to go through something like this. I wasn’t about to squander this opportunity, as I have squandered many opportunities in the past.

I walked back to Jayco, grabbed the rest of my Wonderbread and bologna, and made my way down the curved path, back to the road.

Don’t judge me, okay? You didn’t see what I saw. That is some S-tier level spooky nonsense, and I know who I am. I know what I am capable of, and I am *not* the hero.

Unfortunately, I was being railroaded by destiny. I did successfully make it to the road, but after walking a few hundred feet, I realized this endeavor was pointless.

I even weakly held my thumb out for a bit. But it got to one point where I’d turn around, and I saw the hill, then I looked forward, and the same hill was still there. So I walked back up the winding, dirt path, defeated and confronted Sod.

“Don’t say anything.”

She didn’t even look up from her book.

“I wasn’t going to. Have you found the courage, or are you still a coward?”

*Coward?!* Well, of course, I’m *still* a coward, but I don’t like to hear that from a hot woman.

“Can I die doing this?”

“I’d be more surprised if you didn’t die.”

“Am I the first person you’ve seen here?”

“Yes.”

“Do you think I’m attractive?”

I gave her a smouldering look, and I even bit my lip for good measure.

She didn’t answer me.

“The heart is in the little girl's room upstairs.”

All business, I see.

“Okay… what makes you think I can destroy it?”

“I don’t.”

It was at this moment that I realized that we let hot people get away with too much. I don’t care if she said she was God and the Devil or whatever; this is *absolute* discrimination.

“Why do you want me to try?”

She flipped the page. From where I was standing, it was a mess of bloodied symbols, schizophrenic drawings, and moving pictures like a television show.

I pretended I didn’t see anything.

“Because, *Mortal*. Unlike you, I want to get out of here. I don’t particularly want to read the same tome for all eternity.”

She said my title with emphasis this time. Whatever could that mean? Could my human charms be working on her?

“Yeah… um…”

She rolled her eyes before I said anything coherent. Without hesitation, she snapped her fingers. The sky turned an abyss black. The horizon was swallowed by red. The howling of tortured souls echoed throughout. An audible heartbeat could be heard, especially when standing on the porch of the house.

Sod was now ugly, but I pretended that it wasn’t Sod. My Sod was the curvy, voluptuous southern belle that’s *probably* into me, at least a little.

“You’re hideous,” I said without thinking.

“Let he who is without sin be the first to cast a stone.”

I blinked, not understanding her meaning.

The heartbeat continued in my ears; it was impossible *not* to notice. The painted wooden porch beneath us now barely held together with grey, charred wooden planks and rusted nails.

The windows were now shattered, so I decided to catch a glimpse of the new interior. My eyes quickly met a corpse hung by a noose, convulsing uncontrollably.

“Are we supposed to go inside?”

“No, I decided a change of scenery was in order.”

Her sarcasm wasn’t lost to me. 

I swallowed hard, bearing the weight of the task before me. I took one last glimpse at Jayco, knowing this could be the last time I saw her, and opened the front door.

But before I could do that, Sod put a hand on my shoulder.

“I know you lack intelligence, but you mustn’t go in alone.”

I felt her bony fingers grip my shoulder, trying to stop me from making a mistake.

“You’re coming with me?”

Sod shook her head.

“No, take this.”

She held out a curved blade. Its hilt felt warm in my hands. The blade slithered like a serpent. I awkwardly held the dagger.

“What am I supposed to do with this?”

“You’ll understand soon enough.”

With surprising dexterity, Sod threw open the door, pushed me inside, then slammed it shut. I was now trapped within the house.

I did what any brave person would do and immediately started banging on the door I was forced through. I’d even be so bold as to say that when I beg for help, I do sound pretty masculine and heroic.

“*OH MY GOD, SOD! PLEASE LET ME OUT! I CHANGED MY MIND! I CAN EAT CORN FOR ALL ETERNITY! I ACTUALLY LOVE CORN! SOD PLEASE LET ME—”*

A giggling sent a shiver up my spine.

I turned my head to meet the voice.
