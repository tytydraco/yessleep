The Wishing Well appears when you’ve stopped pretending. When the excuses have run out. When even self-pity feels pathetic. When it appears, it does only for you, somewhere inconspicuous like a park, an old road, or at the basement of your home. Someone could walk past it a dozen times and they wouldn’t notice. But to you, in that instant, the air thickens. It presses against your skin, seeps into your lungs. You might hear the faintest hum or a whistle coming from the well. It'll never tell you to approach. It waits. Always waits. After all, it's the only place left for you to go.

The rules are not that complicated, but I've seen far too many cases where a person messes it all up by breaking them. So here they are.

**1. Approach alone.** Others cannot perceive it. Their eyes will slide past it as if it were invisible. And please, don't try to force someone to see it. I remember the case of a particularly depressed teenager who'd noticed the well and thought he was hallucinating. He had been diagnosed with schizophrenia but he was on his meds. He tried to verify it with his sister because he thought his meds weren't working, and that didn't go well. The well is meant for you, and if you have someone else forcefully perceive it they might get irreparable mental damage.

**2. Offer a tribute.** Not money, not anything material, not the things you wish you could throw away. Something precious. Something that leaves a hollow in your chest when it is gone. Memories, objects, mementoes of someone long past.  Nothing that you wanna discard. Negative emotion is like poison to the well.

Human sacrifices have been noted to work exceptionally well, but the rule about negative emotions is even stronger in this. The recoil will be bad. At best, you'll never see the well again. At worst, the well dweller might get angry. You don't wanna see that.

**3. Speak only your wish**. If you try to be too precise, the well will grant the wish—but sometimes not as you imagined. It'll try to fight back. Vagueness is just as bad. The well twists outcomes into cruel distortions. Just try to hit that sweet spot, or be so precise that the well can't find a loophole. Another way to prevent a distorted result is the quality of tribute. It always appreciates good offerings, and it'll show its gratitude in this step.

**4. It may wait.** Minutes, hours, days. Be patient. If you go outside a certain radius, it'll disappear. It's testing your desperation. I think it gets some kind of sadistic pleasure out of that.

**5. Six months.** Once the wish is granted, the tribute is only a down payment. You have six months to deliver the rest. Time stretches. It bends. Each day becomes heavier than the last. The price is never low. Try to pay it back.

**6. Nothing else matters.** Precision is survival. Timing is living. You may think my instructions are reliable. Perhaps. But try to think on your own head. It'll try to trick you every step of the way. Be wary of that and be cleverer than it.

I have a few cases for you, so you know even better what not to do. The first is Aaron Paulson, who found it behind a shuttered strip mall. Bankruptcy had hollowed him out. The office smelled of stale coffee, burnt paper, and wet carpet. His fingers trembled as he sorted letters and notices, the edges of each sheet sharp against his skin. He'd offered a tribute carefully chosen: the guilt of abandoning a mentor, the man who had guided him, trusted him, taught him everything. He'd drawn him there and pushed him down.

The well accepted it slowly, savoring every ounce of regret. Within a week, his debts vanished. Errors corrected themselves. Partners and benefactors appeared in plenty. Aaron thrived. Like I said, the well always appreciates good tribute.

Then the countdown began. Days stretched. Each sunrise felt duller. Every step, every breath, reminded him that six months had begun. And when the time was up, he could not bring himself to pay. The well left—and Aaron’s world collapsed in ways the well had anticipated: contracts vanished, employees resigned, investments turned to dust. That was just the beginning, though. The well-dweller hates unpayed debts more than anything else. It came for him. They say he was attacked by a vicious bear while trying to run from it in the forest. We know better.

Anna found it outside a fertility clinic. She gave a memory: her mother’s voice, soft and protective, the lullaby that had steadied her heart for decades. The well drank it slowly. It cherished the tribute, and she forgot everything about her mother.

She asked for a child. She got one.

Then the six-month countdown began. The well demanded that she leave the child. Not kill it, not give it back. She had to abandon her family. She could not release the attachment. When the six months ended, the well left—and Anna’s world collapsed. They say it was a fire. Her house was consumed by it, burning her family away before help could arrive.

The game is not simple. Every wish is layered. Everythings a trap. You may believe what I write here. But perhaps the rules will bend for you. Perhaps they won't. Perhaps the very act of reading this, is the first move in a game of chess you will never winy.

I have watched them all. Aaron. Anna. Daryl. Glenn. Rick. Others whose names I have long since forgotten. I have catalogued their choices, their failures. I type this now from the the well. Stones press beneath my fingertips, cold and damp. The reception down here is so terrible. It took me *hours* to fucking post this.

Oh, look! A new visitor approaches. Her heartbeat is ragged, breath even shallower. I just can't wait to devour all that juicy tribute. I want her hope, her guilt. All for me.
