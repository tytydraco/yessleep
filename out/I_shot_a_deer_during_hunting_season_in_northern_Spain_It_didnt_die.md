I grew up in a small village south of Burgos, the kind with more sheep than people and a bar that opens before the sun. Hunting isn’t a hobby there. It’s just something you do, like chopping wood or fixing fences.

Last November, during deer season, my uncle asked me to join him for an early hunt. He’d been uneasy for days, complaining that the animals were “behaving badly.” That was his phrase. Not sick. Not aggressive. *Badly.*

We went into the forest before dawn. The air smelled like wet leaves and frost. No birds. No wind. That should’ve been my first warning.

Around six, we found tracks—deer prints, fresh. Too fresh. They led off the path toward an old clearing the villagers avoid. No one talks about why. They just don’t go there. Charles stopped walking.

“Do you hear that?” he asked.

I did. Footsteps. Slow. Heavy. Not the light, nervous sound of a deer—more like something remembering how to walk as one.

That’s when I saw it. A deer stood at the edge of the clearing, facing us. It was tall and unnaturally thin, its ribs pressing against its skin like fingers from the inside. Its head tilted slightly, studying us. The eyes were wrong—too forward, too focused. Human eyes trying to pass as animal eyes.

Charles raised his rifle, then hesitated.

“Don’t” he whispered. “It knows.”

I was already raising mine.

I aimed for the chest and fired.

The shot tore through the forest. The deer staggered—but didn’t fall. I worked the bolt automatically, the metal clicking loud in the silence, chambering a new round. I fired again. The second shot hit higher. The deer’s body twisted in a way no animal’s should.

Still standing.

My breathing was loud in my ears as I chambered a third round. My hands didn’t feel like mine anymore. I pulled the trigger one last time.

That’s when the deer lifted its head—and its mouth began to open.

It stretched wider than possible, the jaw splitting, skin tearing wetly at the corners. The shriek that came out wasn’t pain. It was rage. A sharp, metallic scream that felt like it was being forced straight into my skull. The sound made my vision blur. My teeth vibrated.

I dropped the rifle.

Behind me, Charles screamed my name.

When the sound finally stopped, the clearing was empty.

The deer was gone.

So was my uncle. The Guardia Civil said Charles must’ve wandered off during the hunt. No blood. No tracks leading away. Just his rifle, found neatly propped against a tree at the edge of the clearing.

Mine was recovered too.

It hadn’t been fired.

Deer season was cancelled in our village the following year. Officially, it was “low population numbers.”

Unofficially, people started seeing deer standing too close to houses at night. Too upright. Watching. Opening their mouths in silence, as if rehearsing. Last night, something stood outside my parents’ home. I recognized the careful rhythm of its breathing before it spoke.

It used my uncle’s voice.

And it sounded like it remembered the shots. prior.
