I shouldn’t have opened the cellar in Bachmut.
But then again, in the winter of 2024, curiosity is a luxury killed by the need for warmth.

My name is Marek. I am—or was—a volunteer medic. I’ve spent the last year patching up holes in boys who looked like they should be sitting for exams, not bleeding into the grey Ukrainian slush.

We were taking shelter in the skeletal remains of an old winery on the edge of the city. The shelling was constant, a rhythmic, bone-deep thrum that makes the teeth ache.

"Marek, help me with this," Stepan wheezed, tugging at a rusted iron ring set into the floorboards.

"Leave it," I said, wiping the copper-scented grime from my surgical kit. "The drones will see the heat signature if we go deeper. It's a tomb down there."

"It’s a bunker," he countered, his eyes wide with the frantic exhaustion unique to the front lines. "And it’s quiet."

He was right. The cellar didn't just feel underground; it felt outside of the war. When we finally pried the hatch open, the air that rushed out wasn't the stale, damp scent of a basement. It smelled of scorched honey and old copper.

We descended by the flickering light of a single tactical torch. The walls weren't brick. They were lined with glass jars—thousands of them—filled with a viscous, crimson fluid that refused to settle, even after decades.

In the center of the room sat a man. He didn't look like a soldier. He wore a tuxedo, pristine and black, standing in stark contrast to the filth of the 21st century. His skin was the color of a fish's belly, and his eyes were sewn shut with silver wire.

"You’re late," the man whispered. His voice sounded like dry leaves skittering over a grave.
"Who are you?" Stepan leveled his rifle, his hands shaking. "Russian? Wagner?"

The man laughed, a wet, bubbling sound. "I am the vintage. And you... you are the harvest."

Before I could scream, the silver wire on the man’s eyelids snapped. From his sockets, dozens of translucent, needle-like appendages erupted.

They didn't strike like a knife; they wove through the air like silk. One pierced Stepan’s throat.
I watched, frozen, as the gore began. It wasn't just a killing; it was a distillation. The needles pumped rhythmically, drawing the blood out of Stepan’s body so fast his skin turned translucent, then grey, then collapsed like wet parchment. His internal organs were liquefied and siphoned through those terrible glass tubes. I heard his ribs snap as the pressure turned his chest into a vacuum.

"The soil of Europe is always hungry, Marek," the creature said, calling me by name. "The Great War, the Second, and now this lovely little tragedy. You think the politics matter? The borders? No. We just need the friction to keep the wine flowing."


I didn't run. I couldn't. I watched him fill a fresh jar with the essence of my best friend. The creature drank a single drop, and his skin bloomed with a healthy, terrifying pink glow.

"Why let me live?" I sobbed, staring at the pile of hair and fingernails that used to be Stepan.
The creature smiled, revealing rows of needle-thin teeth. "Because a sommelier doesn't work the fields. He oversees the cellar."

He handed me a silver needle. It was warm. It pulsed against my palm like a heartbeat.

I’m writing this from a recovery ward in Poland. They think I’m a hero. They think I’m the sole survivor of a horrific Russian thermobaric strike that "evaporated" my unit. They see my shaking hands and think it’s PTSD.

But I have a secret.

I’m not a medic anymore. I’ve been traveling the hospitals of Europe—Berlin, Paris, Warsaw, Belgrade—visiting the "hopeless" cases, the ones the war broke beyond repair. The doctors thank me for my "comforting presence."

But they don't see what I do under the sheets. They don't see the silver needle I carry.

To be honest, I didn't serve a monster.I’ve realized he was lying. He didn't need the war to feed. He started the wars because he's a connoisseur of specific flavors. 

And as I stand over the bed of a wounded boy in Warsaw, I realize I’m not just a servant. I’ve developed a palate. The blood of the innocent is sweet, but the blood of those who have killed? It tastes like vintage champagne.

I’m not trying to stop the war. 
I’m making sure it spreads. 
I need the cellar to stay full, and I’m getting very, very thirsty.
