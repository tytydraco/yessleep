She was a sweet old lady, somewhere in her nineties, as frail as could be. It was one of the reasons I let her into my home a week ago. Her glossy eyes blinked as if they were sending signals straight to my heart. How could I say no?

"Please, ma'am, I'm so sorry to bother you..." she said, looking up at me. Her back was hunched, making her seem even smaller.

"You see, I used to own this house. My husband... he-he built it."

I slowly looked around, but she didn't seem to be with anyone. I'm not going to lie—her request completely caught me off guard. I'd only been living there for a couple of months myself.

"Um, I'm not sure... I'm only renting, and I just don't know if—"

She cut me off before I could finish.

"Renting? No, no, no..." she said, reaching out with a frail hand to gently touch the wooden doorframe. "No, this house is meant to be someone's treasure. Rent? No..."

She let her hand fall away, her fingers lingering for just a moment before she stepped back.

For a second, neither of us spoke.

The old woman offered me a small, apologetic smile before turning toward the walkway. Her steps were slow and uncertain, each one seeming to take more effort than the last. The afternoon breeze caught the edges of her faded cardigan as she shuffled away, her shoulders slumped with quiet disappointment.

A knot formed in my stomach.

"Ma'am..."

She stopped but didn't turn around.

I sighed.

"Come on in."

She slowly looked back over her shoulder, her expression softening.

"Are you sure?" she asked quietly.

I gave a small nod. "Yeah. If this really used to be your home... I'd feel awful sending you away without letting you see it one last time."

For a moment, she simply stared at me. Then a warm smile spread across her weathered face, the kind that deepened every wrinkle around her eyes.

"Thank you," she whispered.

I stepped aside and held the door open.

She paused at the threshold, resting her fingertips against the old wooden frame again. Closing her eyes, she drew in a slow, steady breath, almost as if she were taking in a scent only she could recognize.

"It's exactly as I remember," she murmured.

I smiled awkwardly.

"Would you... like some tea?"

Her eyes met mine.

She nodded.

"Yes," she said softly. "That would be lovely."

I led her inside, carefully watching as she crossed the threshold. Her gaze drifted from one room to the next, lingering on the walls and windows with quiet fascination, as though every corner held a memory waiting to be rediscovered.

She followed without a word, her gentle smile never quite leaving her face. As I filled the kettle at the sink, I glanced back to make sure she was comfortable.

She hadn't sat down.

Instead, she stood in the center of the room, slowly turning in place, her fingertips brushing lightly across the wallpaper.

"It hasn't changed as much as I thought it would," she whispered.

Something about the way she said it sent a faint chill up my spine.

I'd only been renting the place for a couple of months.

How could she possibly know what had—or hadn't—changed?

I set the kettle on the stove and reached for two mugs, but by the time I turned around, she was gone.

"Ma'am?"

My voice echoed softly through the hallway.

I found her standing in the living room, her fingertips gliding across the mantel as though she were greeting an old friend.

"This is where our Christmas tree stood," she said with a wistful smile. "Every year... right here."

I didn't have the heart to interrupt.

She moved on before I could respond, shuffling into the dining room. Her eyes swept across the empty space.

"Our table sat beneath that light," she murmured. "My husband insisted on building it himself. It leaned a little..." She laughed quietly to herself. "...but he swore it gave the table character."

I smiled politely and followed a few steps behind.

She never once asked permission.

She simply wandered from room to room as though she already belonged there.

The hallway.

The bathroom.

The little linen closet.

At each stop, another memory surfaced.

"Oh, this used to squeak."

"My grandson hid in here during hide-and-seek."

"We painted this wall three times before agreeing on the color."

Some of the details couldn't possibly still be visible.

Yet somehow she knew exactly where everything had once been.

Eventually she stopped outside my bedroom.

She rested a trembling hand against the door.

"My room," she whispered.

I hesitated.

"Oh... actually..."

Before I could finish, she pushed the door open.

The room looked nothing like the rest of the house. My clothes were draped over a chair. Books were stacked on the nightstand. A half-finished cup of coffee sat beside my alarm clock.

She stepped inside anyway.

Her expression softened into something almost peaceful.

"I've missed it."

I gave an awkward laugh.

"I'm sorry, but this is where I sleep."

She didn't answer.

Instead, she slowly walked to the side of my bed and rested one hand on the mattress.

Her fingers pressed gently into the blanket.

For several long moments, she simply stood there.

Then, almost absentmindedly, she began to rock.

Forward.

Back.

Forward.

Back.

The movement was slow and rhythmic, barely noticeable at first. Her eyes remained fixed on the bed, her lips moving just enough that I couldn't tell if she was whispering something or merely breathing.

The room suddenly felt much quieter.

"Ma'am?"

She didn't respond.

She kept rocking, the motion growing ever so slightly more pronounced, as though she'd forgotten I was standing there.

A chill crept over my arms.

"Are... are you okay?"

Without answering, she stopped.

The silence that followed felt strangely heavy.

Then she reached beneath her chin and untied the faded scarf wrapped around her head.

For a second I simply stared, confused.

Then she folded it carefully and laid it on my dresser.

"Ma'am?"

Next came her cardigan.

My stomach tightened.

"Hey... what are you doing?"

She didn't seem to hear me.

She slipped off her shoes one at a time, placing them neatly beside the bed.

I stepped closer.

"Ma'am, I think there's been a misunderstanding."

Still no response.

She lowered herself carefully onto the edge of my bed with surprising familiarity, as though she'd done it a thousand times before.

Then she drew the blankets back.

My pulse quickened.

"Ma'am."

I reached out, gently placing a hand on her shoulder.

"I don't think you should be doing this."

She looked up at me.

Her eyes were clear.

Not confused.

Not frightened.

Just... tired.

"I'm only going to rest for a little while," she said softly.

"You really can't."

I tried to help her back to her feet, but she resisted with quiet determination, clutching the blanket against herself.

"No," she whispered. "I'm home."

The words hung in the air.

I stood there for several seconds, unsure what to do.

She didn't appear aggressive.

She didn't appear frightened.

If anything, she seemed perfectly content.

I tried one last time.

"Please... let's get you sitting in the living room instead. I'll make that tea."

She simply smiled.

"I've waited a long time to come back."

Then she closed her eyes.

I backed slowly out of the room, pulled out my phone, and dialed 911.

The dispatcher answered almost immediately.

"911. What's your emergency?"

"I... I don't know if this is really an emergency, but there's an elderly woman in my house."

"Is she threatening you?"

"No. Not at all. She came to my door saying she used to live here. I felt bad for her and invited her inside, but now she's gone into my bedroom and climbed into my bed. I think she might be confused... maybe she has dementia or something. I've tried asking her to leave, but she won't."

The dispatcher asked for my address and assured me officers were on their way.

"Just wait outside if you feel uncomfortable," she said.

So I did.

I stood on the front porch, watching through the storm door as the house sat unnaturally still.

The tea kettle began to whistle inside.

Neither of us moved to silence it.

Ten minutes later, flashing red and blue lights washed across the front windows.

Two officers stepped out of their cruiser.

I explained everything as calmly as I could, though I could hear how unbelievable it sounded coming out of my own mouth.

One of the officers gave me a reassuring nod.

"We'll take care of it."

The two of them entered the house.

I waited on the porch, hands shoved deep into my pockets.

The kettle had stopped whistling.

The entire house had fallen silent.

A minute passed.

Then another.

Neither officer came back out.

An uneasy feeling settled over me.

Finally, the older officer appeared in the bedroom doorway down the hall. He stood there for several long seconds before speaking quietly to his partner.

The younger officer disappeared into the room.

When they emerged, neither looked at me immediately.

The older officer removed his hat.

"I'm sorry," he said gently. "It appears she passed away."

I stared at him.

"What?"

"We don't believe she suffered," he continued. "From what we can tell, she simply... slipped away."

My mouth went dry.

"She was talking to me five minutes ago."

"I understand."

He glanced back toward the bedroom.

"Would you happen to know her name?"

I slowly shook my head.

"No."

The officer nodded solemnly.

"We'll see if we can find out who she is."

Behind him, my bedroom door remained open.

From where I stood, I could just make out the edge of the blanket.

It looked as though someone had tucked her in.

That was last week.After everything was over, I tried convincing myself it had simply been one of those bizarre, once-in-a-lifetime experiences people tell at dinner parties years later.



But every night after that, I found myself staring at my bedroom door.

I couldn't bring myself to sleep in the bed.

I tried.

The first night, I lasted maybe twenty minutes before dragging a blanket into the living room.

The second night wasn't any better.

Every time I closed my eyes, I pictured her lying there peacefully beneath my blankets, as if she'd always belonged there.

So I bought new bedding.

New sheets.

A new comforter.

New pillows.

I stripped every inch of that bed until nothing remained that she'd touched.

It didn't help.

The room still felt wrong.

Like it remembered.

A few days later, I gave up entirely.

I went out and bought a brand-new mattress, a new bed frame, and even rearranged the entire bedroom. The bed that had once sat beneath the window was moved against the opposite wall. My dresser traded places with the bookshelf. I even changed the lamps around, hoping that somehow making the room look different would make it *feel* different.

It was exhausting.

But it worked.

Or at least I thought it had.

For the first time in nearly a week, I slept through the evening without waking every hour.

I remember thinking, *Finally.*

Then, sometime in the middle of the night...

I felt the mattress sink beside me.

Not much.

Just enough to wake me.

Still half asleep, I assumed I'd imagined it.

Until I felt the unmistakable sensation of someone climbing into bed next to me.

The blankets shifted.

Weight settled against the mattress.

Every muscle in my body locked.

I reached for the lamp beside my bed and switched it on.

The room flooded with warm light.

There was no one there.

I let out a shaky breath.

Then something drifted lazily through the air in front of me.

A faded floral head scarf.

It floated downward, turning once before landing softly on the floor beside the bed.

I froze.

It wasn't tossed.

It wasn't blown.

It fell exactly the way fabric falls after someone removes it from their head.

My heart hammered against my ribs.

"No..."

I whispered it more to myself than anyone else.

I searched the room.

Behind the curtains.

Under the bed.

Inside the bathroom.

Nothing.

When I finally worked up the nerve to pick up the scarf, it felt perfectly ordinary.

Soft.

Cool.

Old.

I stared at it for several seconds before carrying it straight outside and dropping it into the trash bin.

I spent the rest of the night trying to explain it away.

Maybe I'd dreamed the whole thing.

Maybe the scarf had somehow been tucked beneath the new bed when it was delivered.

Maybe the movers had found it somewhere and left it without telling me.

None of those explanations made much sense.

Especially since I'd watched the police bag every one of the old woman's belongings.

Still...

Lack of sleep does strange things to people.

That's what I kept telling myself.

The next morning, I almost believed it.

Until the hammering started.

*Bang.*

*Bang.*

*Bang.*

It echoed through the house like someone was driving nails into wood.

I set my coffee down.

"Hello?"

The hammering stopped.

I searched every room.

Nothing.

No television.

No neighbors working outside.

No maintenance crew.

As soon as I returned to the kitchen—

*Bang.*

*Bang.*

*Bang.*

This time it sounded like it came from upstairs.

I ran up the steps.

Silence.

The noise seemed to move every time I did.

Living room.

Hallway.

Attic access.

Bedroom.

Always just out of reach.

After nearly twenty minutes, I was starting to wonder if the sound was somehow inside the walls.

Then I heard it again.

Directly behind me.

*Bang.*

I spun around.

The bedroom.

Specifically...

The closet.

I swallowed hard and walked toward it.

The hammering had stopped.

My hand wrapped around the doorknob.

For a few seconds, I simply stood there, listening.

Nothing.

I slowly pulled the door open.

Before I could even react, something small burst past me.

A little boy.

He darted out of the closet laughing hysterically, the sound bouncing off the hallway walls as he sprinted past.

I caught only the back of him—a flash of suspenders, scuffed shoes, and a little plaid shirt.

He moved so quickly that I never saw his face.

By the time I reached the hallway...

He was gone.

The laughter stopped as abruptly as it had begun.

The house was silent again.

I checked every room.

Every corner.

Every window and door.

Locked.

Empty.

That was the moment I stopped trying to convince myself I was overtired.

I called my friend Melissa.

She answered on the second ring.

"You sound awful," she said before I could even speak.

"I think... I think something's wrong with this house."

There was a long pause.

Normally she'd have laughed.

Instead, she said quietly, "Tell me everything."

So I did.

From the old woman...

To the bed...

To the scarf...

To the little boy.

When I finally finished, Melissa was silent for several moments.

"I know someone," she said at last.

"What do you mean?"

"A medium."

I almost laughed.

Almost.

A week ago, I would've hung up.

Now...

"I'll bring her over this afternoon," Melissa continued. "You don't have to believe in any of it. But if it'll give you some peace of mind, it's worth a try."

I looked down the hallway toward my bedroom.

For just a second, I thought I heard a child giggle again.

"...Yeah," I said quietly. "I think it's time."

Melissa arrived that afternoon with the medium.

Or, at least, the woman who *called* herself one.

She introduced herself with an overly dramatic smile, jangling bracelets, and enough rings to cover nearly every finger. Before she even stepped inside, she announced that she could "feel tremendous spiritual energy radiating from the property."

That should've been my first clue.

Within minutes she was waving a bundle of smoking sage through the hallway while muttering what sounded like random phrases stitched together from half-remembered television shows.

"The spirits acknowledge me..."

"...negative vibrations..."

"...the portal is closing..."

She stopped in my living room and suddenly gasped.

"Oh my."

Melissa looked concerned.

"What is it?"

The medium closed her eyes dramatically.

"There is a little girl here."

I frowned.

"A little girl?"

"Yes."

"Not... an elderly woman?"

She hesitated.

"...She's here too."

"And an older man?"

Another pause.

"...Several men."

By that point, I wasn't scared anymore.

I was irritated.

She spent another twenty minutes wandering around my house, occasionally stopping to announce that she had "cleared another entity."

Then she handed me a small bag of crystals and suggested I sleep with one beneath my pillow.

For seventy-five dollars.

The moment they left, I knew I'd wasted my time.

Melissa apologized repeatedly.

"I really thought she'd help."

"It's okay."

No...

It wasn't.

That evening, I sat at my computer and started searching.

Not television psychics.

Not social media influencers.

People with actual reputations.

I spent nearly two hours reading reviews before I finally found someone who seemed... genuine.

Grounded.

She wasn't claiming to remove demons or guarantee miracles.

She simply described herself as someone who investigated reports of hauntings and historical properties.

I filled out the contact form.

Attached everything I'd written about the house.

And hit **Send**.

She replied less than an hour later.

*"I'll call you tomorrow morning."*

It was the first hopeful thing I'd read all week.

That hope lasted until sometime after two in the morning.

...

*Bang.*

I opened my eyes.

Silence.

Then—

*BANG.*

The sound shook the wall behind my bed.

I sat upright.

Another strike.

Closer this time.

*BANG.*

The hammering wasn't moving anymore.

It was coming from somewhere downstairs.

I grabbed my phone.

Its screen cast just enough light to guide me into the hallway.

Another blow echoed through the house.

*BANG.*

I crept down the staircase.

Halfway down...

I stopped.

The living room was glowing.

Not from my lamps.

Not from the television.

Soft, colorful lights flickered across the walls.

Red.

Green.

Blue.

Gold.

Christmas lights.

The room should've been empty.

Exactly where the old woman had pointed a week earlier.

Glass bulbs.

Tinsel.

Handmade decorations that looked decades old.

For one impossible moment...

It looked less like my living room...

*BANG.*

The hammer struck again.

I turned toward the dining room.

An older man stood with his back to me.

His shirt hung over the back of a nearby chair, revealing a broad, aging back dusted with sawdust.

A workbench had somehow appeared where my dining table should have been.

Wood shavings littered the floor.

The smell of fresh-cut pine filled the room.

He worked quickly, driving nail after nail into a wooden chair.

His movements were hurried.

Almost frantic.

Without looking up, he muttered to himself.

"Running out of time..."

Another nail.

"I told her I'd finish it..."

Another strike.

"Christmas morning..."

My breathing became shallow.

He paused.

Slowly...

He turned around.

His face was kind.

Weathered.

The face of someone who had spent a lifetime working with his hands.

For just a heartbeat, he looked confused to see me standing there.

Then recognition spread across his face.

"Oh."

He smiled warmly.

"You startled me."

He picked up the chair.

"I've got to finish this before Christmas."

"My wife deserves at least one surprise this year."

The smile disappeared.

His eyes narrowed slightly.

"...You're not my wife."

Another.

Still carrying the hammer.

"I..."

His voice faltered.

"...Who are you?"

I turned and ran.

I don't remember crossing the yard.

"I need the police!"

"Ma'am, what's happening?"

The attic.

The closets.

The backyard.

"What do you mean?"

"If I were you..."

So that's where I am now.

Sitting in my car across the street.

Writing all of this before I can convince myself none of it happened.

I've already contacted my landlord.

Whether they believe me or not, I don't care anymore.

I'm actively looking for any legal way to get out of my lease.

Because after tonight...

I'm done sleeping in that house.
