I’m a Sheriff’s Deputy. The county I work in/for has many isolated and rural areas, and just like so many other police departments, we are short staffed. I work 19:00 to 07:00 (7pm to 7am). It’s not uncommon for us to be on a call at a place where backup is 25 minutes away at minimum. It’s just part of being a deputy. You learn to do things on your own. 

There’s an old building in the northwest corner of the county that was formerly a slaughterhouse. It’s vacant right now, but the company who owns it has plans to renovate it and use it for other things. Because of this, there’s a security system. We get calls for the motion alarm going off all the time, at least twice a week. Every time I’ve responded to this location for the alarm, I’ve found all the entrances secure. No windows broken, nothing. Last night was different. 

I was dispatched to the alarm at 02:40 and arrived on scene at 03:04. I checked a few of the doors and it seemed like it was going to be another case of a false alarm. But then I grabbed and pulled the handle of the only door I hadn’t checked yet, and it opened. 

“912 to county, I have an unsecured door here”.   
“County to 912, you want a 19?” (19 means backup unit) 

I paused for a second and considered the following things: The closest unit was over 20 minutes away, and by the time they arrived, the potential intruder would likely be gone. And that’s the point where things became interesting. 

“912 to county, I’ll advise. I’ll be clearing the building”. 

Now, in a perfect world, you never clear buildings by yourself, especially one this size. But for the reasons I already explained, I decided to do so anyway. I pulled the flashlight from my outer carrier, drew my gun, and went inside. 

“SHERIFF’S DEPARTMENT, IF YOU’RE IN HERE MAKE YOURSELF KNOWN! DO IT NOW!” 

I repeated these announcements as I progressed through the building and entered different areas to ensure I would be heard. I was just about done clearing the place, and that’s when I saw her. An older woman, about 5’6” and maybe 120lbs. Her back was to me, she had ZERO clothes on, and she was facing a table. She didn’t turn around when commanded to, she just kept doing something with her hands in front of her. I approached her slowly from an angle, continuously telling her to show me her hands and to turn towards me, but she ignored me. I finally got to a point where I could see what she was doing with her hands, and it looked like she was writing on a piece of paper. I closed the distance, all the time asking what she was doing and why she was in there. I got close enough to touch her and put my hand on her shoulder and pulled it to make her face me. 

It was my grandmother. A woman who, last I knew, lived across the country from me. She had grown up in the area but moved away to live with other family due to her Alzheimer’s after my grandpa died.

“Grandma, what are you doing? Why are you here? What’s going on?” 

She began crying, and acted like she didn’t know who I was. Which, due to her declining mind, made sense. 

“My husband used to work here. This is where I met him, when I was 22. He asked me out to the movies right where we are standing, I had to come back one last time and see the spot my life changed”.

“Grandpa? I didn’t know he ever worked here. But what do you mean last time, are you okay?”

And with that, she pulled a knife out from under the paper she had been writing on and sliced her throat. I had seen enough injuries resulting from violent crime to know she wasn’t going to survive it. She bled out right there on the floor before EMS arrived.

I didn’t believe what I was experiencing. That it was her, that she was there, that she ended her life this way, any of it. But then I looked at the paper she had been writing on. 

“Blake, I’m sorry you had to watch me do this. But I can’t live this way any longer, I’m scared all the time and can’t remember hardly anything. This place holds my favorite memory, and I have to go see your grandpa again. It’s been too long” 

Somehow, she knew it would be me that responded to the call. I haven’t slept since.
