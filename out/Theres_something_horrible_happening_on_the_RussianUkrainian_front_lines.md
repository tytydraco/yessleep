Sounds of weeping February wind filled the darkness behind the thin glass panes of my window. Cold air pressed itself against the wooden walls, seeping through every crack I didn’t bother to caulk. 

A potbelly stove stood quietly in the corner, last pieces of firewood gone a couple of days ago. I would’ve gone and chopped some in the woods, but I for the life of me cannot find my axe. 

A different “source of warmth” stood on the table, under the yellow light of a slightly swaying lightbulb. Two dirty glasses stood beside it, waiting to be refilled with the cheapest, shittiest vodka you can buy in the local grocery shop. 

I grabbed a clove of garlic and bit on it, the burning sensation fading quickly as a bite of stale bread followed. 

A door opened with a groan. A sound of heavy footsteps on a wooden floor accompanied the slam of closing it shut, cutting off the stream of cold wind that rushed inside the house.

Anton has returned from his trip to the outhouse, although I suspected that he might have relieved himself on the random snow pile not even halfway there. He landed heavily on the stool opposite of me.

“Another one?” he asked, and I silently grabbed the bottle, filling both our glasses.

Vodka burned my insides, causing me to exhale sharply. Anton, with his glass already half-emptied, suddenly said: 

“Do you remember Vitalik?”

It took me a moment to recognize the name: “The one who joined the police?”

Anton nodded and continued: “Yeah, well I met him last week in Tula. And guess what he told me? He’s going to sign a contract.”

“For SMO?” I asked, mildly surprised. Vitalik didn’t seem like a reckless person to me. Not reckless enough to sign up for the special military operation at least. “Police not paying him or something?”

“Thats the thing” Anton lowered his voice as if someone could hear us. “His bosses told him that war’s gonna end soon. Likely in a month or two, before he’s gonna even get to the frontlines. But the payment for signing the contract is the same, so you get the money and then just return home”

Anton straightened. “I think I’ll sign up too. They say that you can get 4 millions if you sign up in Moscow”

He pulled out his phone. Cracked screen lit up with the advertising poster. The text read “Backlines contract service! 4 million roubles on a sign up, plus 200 thousands monthly pay”, accompanied by a picture of a happy soldier. 

“And you believe it?” I asked, curiosity stirring inside me. Four millions was a life changing sum. Finally getting out of this fucking place, to live like a human being. “You’re sure about the war ending soon?”

“I’m sure that there are no idiots sitting up there,” Anton answered, pointing up. “If the war is gonna end soon, they’ll cut the payment soon too. I think it’s our last chance to cash big”.

My mind sluggishly began working through the haze of alcohol, fueled by hope and greed. Vitalik was the sort of person who always seemed to be in the right place at the right time. When the three of us finished school in a bigger village a dozen kilometers from here, he was the only one who got into college. I tried to enroll in one too, but failed the exams and spent a year in mandatory military service instead. Vitalik was drafted after college and got into a cozy paper-pushing position. The idea that he had some insider information wasn’t unbelievable. 

On the other side, Anton was never the brightest head. Orphaned at 16, when his drunk father fell asleep with a cigarette and burned down their house, he always seemed busy with some new enrichment scheme, hustling here and there. I’ve never seen him stop, relax or take it slow with life. Like a squirrel in a wheel, always running. Even now, looking at me, he was constantly shuffling around, tapping his foot, checking his phone. Eventually, he took a bottle and poured more vodka into his glass, which I didn’t notice him emptying. 

As for myself, after the army I got a job at a local metal doors factory. Without college I could only get a welders assistant position, earning barely enough to survive. I never knew my father, and my mother died last year from liver failure caused by excessive drinking. I didn’t doubt my fate would eventually be the same as hers.

“Alright,” I said, turning to Anton, “Let's go to Moscow and sign up.”

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

The next day we took a bus to Tula. There, we climbed the train station fence and boarded a train to Moscow. We had to jump out and run towards the opposite end of a train on certain stations to avoid conductors, but otherwise the four hour long journey was uneventful.

In Moscow, we got off before the main terminal to avoid tall fences and an abundance of guards. After exiting the smaller station through the gap in the fence we headed to the nearest metro station.

There, behind the glass wall of the station we immediately saw it. A simple wooden desk stood to the side from the entrance, with the same posters Anton showed me the day before. A single man sat behind it, bored out of his mind, scrolling Tiktok on his phone. 

A loose crowd of people were entering and exiting the station, their constant stream pulsing with the invisible rhythm of arriving metro trains. I studied those who passed me. Kids returning from school, wearing bright coloured jackets and hats with “cat years”, laughing loudly and carelessly. Women in makeup, wearing white coats and high leather boots. Men with suits visible from under their jackets, walking with all the confidence.

None of them even looked in the recruiters way. None of them needed to. They had their own comfortable lives, their stable well-paying jobs. Their little flats with central heating, with fast internet and delivery of anything and everything at any time any day.

The bus stop nearby had a constant motion of electric buses arriving and leaving. In my village, the rusty soviet pazik ran the route twice a day.

A bubbling black tar of envious hatred started to rise inside me. I wanted to grab them, to shake them until they dropped their expensive phones and looked me in the eyes. I wanted to scream at them for living a comfortable life while their countrymen had to struggle every single day. While I had to struggle. I wanted to take everything they had and to deprive them of everything. I wanted to kill them all and die myself.

I turned away, grabbing Anton by the shoulder. “Let’s get this over with”.

We approached the recruitment table and the man turned his face to us with a surprised expression. 

“We want to sign a contract,” I said.

“For the backlines,” Anton added.

A smile creeped across the man's face: “Of course!”

He jumped up and brought two chairs to us so we would sit at the table. After that he brought two stacks of papers and pens. “I’ll need your passports.”

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_



Five weeks later, our training course at the base near Kursk was coming to an end. A moment when we got our assignment an unpleasant surprise revealed itself.

“What do you mean we are being sent to Dobrinka? It’s the frontlines! We didn’t sign for it!” I almost screamed at our sergeant. 

“You did, in fact, sign for it” he replied, with steel in his voice. “You signed a contract with the Ministry of Defence. There’s no clause in that contract that allows you to choose where you are being sent. From the moment you sign it, you are a property of the army. So unless you want me to shoot you in the head, bury you in the dirt and write you off as deserter, you’ll shut the fuck up and follow orders!”

That treat left me speechless. I’ve heard the rumors, and there was a suspicious pit in the ground at the edge of the forest beyond the base borders, where nobody had been allowed to go. Some said that was sort of prison, that they put in those who would refuse orders to make them more obedient. Some said that not everyone got out. 

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

Anton kept to himself on the way towards the frontlines. I didn’t pry him, my own thoughts of despair were a burden on my shoulders heavier than the equipment and rusty kalashnikov I’ve been given. No ammunition of course, that we’ll only get before the fight. Probably.

The truck we were riding in was packed with soldiers just like us. The back of it was open to the cold air and we were desperately wrapping ourselves up trying to keep warm. Another car with soldiers followed us, but they were actually armed and making sure none of us decided to jump and run. 

From the back of the truck I’ve seen what used to be beautiful fields of Ukraine, now a barren land, tortured by bombs and decimated by fires. Ruins of wooden houses were seen in the distance, reduced to the suggestion of a village. I tried not to wonder what happened to the people who lived there. The last rays of the setting sun shined on something in the ruins, making it look like a bright fire. I wondered for a moment if a glass window could have survived the destruction of its house.

As we arrived at the camp near Dobrinka, we were escorted towards our barracks. Well, in reality it was just a wide trench dug up in frozen earth, covered with a camo tarp. Piles of dry straw were used for beds. Armed guards patrolled the camp and two of them stood at the entrance of barracks. 

The coldness of night was agonising. As I laid on the straw, shivering and befogged, I felt gentle shaking. 

“Hey, are you asleep?” Anton whispered.

I turned my face in his direction, not being able to tell if my eyes were even open in complete darkness.  
  
“What?” I whispered.  
  
“I have a plan”, Anton’s voice got closer. Next thing he said so quietly, I could barely discern it: “We have to run. No way in hell we can survive like this. We have to go back.”  
  
“We cannot go back,” I whispered in return, matching his volume. “There are barrier detachments, they shoot on sight!”  
  
“Then we have to surrender to ukrainians, some guys said they won’t exchange you as a prisoner if you ask.”

I wanted to answer something when the sound pierced the night air. Horrifying whistle, raspy and gurgling, reminiscent of a scream. It sounded distant yet felt like it was getting closer. As abruptly as it began, the sound stopped.

“What the fuck was that?” I asked, sitting up fully awake. 

“I dunno, some kind of bird?” Anton mumbled, “Maybe a bittern? I heard they sound scary”

I wanted to believe it was a bird, I did. But the feeling that sound invoked in me. Like a cold finger scratching on my very soul. I didn’t lay down, didn’t rest until the first light pierced holes in the tarp above me. 

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_



Our captain was in an upbeat mood the next day. He stood proudly before the formation, smiling in satisfaction.  
  
“Today!” he proclaimed “Today you will liberate Dobrinka. You will strangle the khokhols holding this land!”   
  
He balled his hand into a fist, shaking it at the imagined opponent. “The enemy holds the village center, and our task is to push them out into the open fields. We begin the assault in 15 minutes! You will prove yourselves as true defenders of Motherland!”

“Are we gonna get ammunition?” Some guy in the first line asked. 

The captain looked at him, the anger flashed in his eyes, before shifting to a fatherly expression: “Unfortunately, we don’t have enough supplies. But don’t worry, our brave veterans will cover you. Don’t worry, as our intelligence says that khokhols are almost out of ammo.”

I knew what was going on at that moment. We were going to be sent into one of those “Meat assaults”.

As we got marching towards the village, I got closer to the right flank, trying to not raise suspicion. Anton kept near. Armed soldiers were following our group at some distance, the message clear - “turn around and be shot”. Then the gunfire sounded ahead.

We were close to the village when the shooting started, and I immediately dashed forward to the nearest building. The ruins of a house only had two wooden walls left, but it still gave enough cover. Behind me I heard screams of pain and more gunshots, I guess the armed guys did provide some cover fire. As I reached the ruins, I ducked near the empty window frame. A moment later, Anton ducked beside me. 

Looking back, I saw some of our soldiers lying on the ground. Some screamed in pain, bloodied hands caressing their wounds, others were lying motionless. Those still on their feet rushed forward to the cover of other buildings, while the armed guys kept their distance and slowly approached, firing periodically.

As one of them looked in our direction, I knew we had to keep moving. I jumped around the corner of the house, getting out of their sight, and kept forward towards the next building. Anton’s footsteps followed me, but I could not spare a moment to look back. I moved further to the right, past the remains of a small wooden fence and into the overgrown yard. 

I landed into chest-high grass and started crawling forward. In a couple of moments I emerged out of grass near another house, this one having all four walls standing. 

I stopped for a moment to catch my breath as Anton emerged from the grass. Sounds of gunshots and screams were still too close for my liking, so I forced myself to keep going. As we moved around the house crouching, a wide road opened before us. In a distance to my left I glimpsed barricades, likely a village center. Across the road were more houses, the furthest rows of them looked intact. 

I met Anton’s eyes, he nodded. I counted down: “On three, two, one!” 

And we dashed across the road like madmen. A couple of shots sounded from the barricades as we made our way through. And then, as we were close to the cover, Anton cried in pain. Reaching the wall of a house, I turned back to see him on the ground, clutching his leg. I quickly reached and pulled him behind the cover, a couple of shots hitting the ground where he just was. 

As I pulled him further from the road, behind the cover of the house, his cries of pain turned into wheezes. I inspected his leg. The bullet hit him in the calf, blood quickly pooling on the ground. Having nothing to clean the wound with, I tore a piece of cloth off his pants where the wound was and fixed it with my gun belt, applying as much pressure as I could. Leaving the gun on the ground, I helped Anton up.   
  
“We must keep moving,” I said, helping him to limp further. Luckily, other houses were placed in a way that provided uninterrupted cover from the Ukrainian positions. As we slowly moved forward, the sounds of battle were getting quieter, but never ending. 

Finally, we reached the one house that could provide a temporary shelter. Only half of its roof had collapsed, and its walls stood strong, making it safe to hide inside. I carefully laid Anton down, his breaths shallow, and collapsed nearby. 

“I..,” Anton whispered, his voice raspy and dry. I unscrewed my flask open, held his head up and slowly poured water into his mouth. After a couple of sips he started coughing and I pulled the flask away. 

“I’m sorry,” Anton said, after his cough subsided. “I’m sorry for pulling you into this. If I knew..”

“Hey, hey,” I tried to sound soothing: “It’s not your fault. We’re gonna get out of this. We’re gonna survive”  
  
“I cannot run,” he shook his head: “I don’t think I can make it very far like this. If it comes to it, leave me.”

“I’m not leaving you, stop talking bullshit,” I scolded him, a tight lump forming in my throat.

Anton chuckled and got quiet. I took a gulp of water myself and laid on the floor. As exhaustion got its hold on me, I felt darkness eating away at my consciousness. 

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

I came by with a gasp and sat up, nausea hitting me immediately. When my head stopped spinning, I took a look at Anton. He was very pale, the blood had soaked through the cloth on his wound. He groaned quietly as I checked his pulse, his skin cold to the touch.

Then I looked outside. The blue colour of the sky was darkening, the last rays of sun bathing the ruined village with rich orange light. Looking around I saw a bright glint nearby. A square of fiery light a couple of buildings away. Again I was surprised that the glass pane survived in the otherwise ruined village. And then I noticed something moving in the light. A dark spot in the center, getting bigger and bigger until the light only took one edge of the window.

Suddenly, in a single fluid motion, the darkness poured out of the window, growing in size in the process. I looked in shock as it took the shape of a four-legged creature, reminiscent of a horse, with a man-shaped lump on top where the horse’s head should’ve been. The creature straightened, looking about five meters tall. It raised its hands with awfully long fingers, each ending with long spearlike protrusion, and let out a screech. It was the same horrible whistling sound we heard last night, except it was so unbearably loud. 

The icepick of pain pierced my skull and I desperately tried to cover my ears. I don’t know how long the screech lasted, but I came to consciousness lying on the floor. As my hearing slowly returned I heard the sound of tapping hooves approaching. A dread filled me, and I crawled to the darkest corner of the hut, hiding behind the fallen piece of roof.

From my hiding place I could see the other half of the house and Anton lying near the wall, unconscious. As the taps were getting closer, I found myself unable to move a muscle. Then, on the canvas of the darkening starless sky two burning eyes appeared. The creature looked inside the house. I was hoping that it would mistake Anton for a corpse and that it wouldn't search for me, but my prayers weren’t heard. A massive long hand stretched through the hole in the roof and grabbed Anton, pulling him outside of my view.

And then the screams started. The wet sounds of tearing flesh, the cracking of breaking bones, the most horrific screams I’ve ever heard from a man. All that accompanied by low guttural chuckles. And it kept on and on and on. I could not move. I could not make a sound. All I was able to do was to listen, to be a witness of this never ending torture.

After an eternity of suffering, the screams stopped. The chuckles died down, and with the wet splash something dropped on the ground.

Then, in the distance, a man screamed. Gunshots filled the air, followed by more of those bone-grinding whistles. The creature near me whistled too, and the sound finally granted me the mercy of senselessness. 

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

When I woke up, the light of day was shining through the holes in my cover. The complete stillness in the air, the silence convinced me that it was safe. I crawled out and went outside to the gruesome scene. Body pieces were thrown around, blood splattering the ground and the walls. Giant deep hoof footprints in the ground proved that the creature wasn’t a hallucination. I avoided looking at Anton’s remains for too long.

Instead I went towards the village center. Even from afar I could see that the barricades were completely trampled, blood and gore indicated the fate of its defenders. I decided not to linger and moved further west, away from the Russian camp. I had a nagging suspicion that soon their forces would march into the village and I didn’t want to get caught by them. I guess they would just send me into the same thing again.

About an hour after I left Dobrinka I heard the high-pitched buzzing of a drone. I quickly raised my hands and when it approached stated my desire to surrender. I was escorted to the nearest Ukrainian camp, where I was detained.

When asked I told them everything. All of it. They gave me the choice: prison or joining the Free Russia Legion. I know what I have to do. I understand now, that nobody willing to do that to their own people can be allowed to act.

All the money I had on my yet to be closed bank account I’ve sent to somebody they told me. In return, they gave me the old smartphone with a local sim card. Now I’m writing this as a warning to my fellow Russians to not trust anything the government promises you. And as a message to the world witnessing this war and trying to isolate themselves from it. Evil cannot be contained, it must be stopped. Only then we could truly have a peaceful sky above our heads.
