I don’t really know how to start this. I just need to tell someone because I haven’t stopped thinking about it.

I went camping alone last week. Three nights at a state park about an hour from my house. Not remote. Other campsites nearby. Families. RVs. Normal.

The first night was completely normal.

I set up my tent, built a small fire, cooked some food, had a couple beers. Before bed, I left things how I usually do. Chair a little crooked by the fire ring. Cooler resting but not latched. Trash shoved into a grocery bag on the bench.

Nothing messy. Just not organized.

I zipped the tent from the inside and went to sleep.

When I woke up the next morning, everything looked… cleaned.

The chair was upright and centered.

The cooler was fully latched.

The trash bag was tied and placed on the table.

The fire pit had been cleared and the half-burned logs stacked neatly beside it.

I stood there trying to remember if I’d cleaned up before bed.

I couldn’t picture doing it.

But I also couldn’t prove I hadn’t.

I told myself maybe I’d straightened things without thinking. Or maybe a ranger had come through early. It felt stupid to overthink it.

The second night is when it stopped feeling stupid.

Before bed, I left things deliberately out of place.

I knocked the chair over on its side.

Left the cooler cracked open.

Set my boots loosely beside the tent instead of inside.

Left a stick half in the fire ring.

I remember feeling ridiculous, like I was testing something that didn’t exist.

I zipped the tent from the inside and went to sleep.

When I woke up, I unzipped the tent slowly.

The chair was upright.

The cooler was sealed.

The stick had been placed with the rest of the firewood.

My boots weren’t where I left them.

For a second I thought they were gone.

Then I saw them.

They were directly in front of the tent entrance.

Side by side.

Toes pointed inward.

Like someone had taken them off before stepping inside.

I just stood there staring at them.

Animals don’t do that.

They drag things. They scatter them.

This was careful.

I checked the ground for footprints, but it’s mostly gravel and hard dirt there. Nothing clear. Just my own from the day before.

I tried telling myself a ranger had come through.

But that didn’t explain the tent zipper.

The night before, I’d left it slightly cracked at the top for airflow.

That morning, it was fully zipped.

From the outside.

I don’t think you can pull it closed that last inch from inside. I tried, and I couldn’t.

That’s when the feeling changed.

Not panic.

Just this heavy awareness that I wasn’t alone out there.

The third night, I didn’t build a fire.

I stayed in the tent.

Around 2 or 3 in the morning, I woke up halfway.

I didn’t hear footsteps.

I didn’t hear breathing.

But I felt watched.

Close.

Like someone was standing just outside the fabric.

I held my breath and listened.

Nothing.

Eventually I fell back asleep.

When I woke up the next morning, I didn’t unzip the tent right away.

I just lay there for a minute.

When I finally opened it, the campsite looked perfect.

Cleaner than either of the other mornings.

The fire ring was cleared completely.

The sticks were stacked neatly.

My chair was centered in the clearing.

Facing the tent.

Not toward the fire.

Toward where I had been sleeping.

I felt sick.

I packed quickly.

Before getting in the car, I looked back one last time.

That’s when I noticed the ground where my tent had been.

There were two flattened areas in the dirt.

One where my sleeping pad had pressed down.

And one directly opposite it.

Closer than the fire ring.

Like someone had been sitting there.

Facing me.

For a long time.

I didn’t get out to inspect it.

I didn’t want confirmation.

I drove home.

I haven’t gone back.

The part that won’t leave me alone isn’t the cleaned campsite.

It’s my boots.

Standing upright.

Toes pointed inward.

Like someone had stood there.

Looking at me while I slept.

If someone wanted to hurt me, they had three nights.

Instead, they straightened my campsite.

They zipped my tent.

They lined up my boots.

And I don’t know why that feels worse.
