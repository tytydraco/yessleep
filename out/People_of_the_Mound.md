‘Twas some years ago that the Butler family moved into Ballydara. Being from out of town, it took some time for us locals to acclimate to their presence, but that time passed quickly. They were a fine sort of people, the kind that goes to church every Sunday, kids who didn’t get in trouble at school, and parents who got on famously well. Having been all around Ireland, both North and South, the family had many tales to share of the places they’d stayed. Mr. Butler became regarded as a paragon of the town, loving merry jokes about his golden spectacles, while Mrs. Butler volunteered at the church often, and their two children were well-loved by their teachers. Despite all that, however, they never truly became part of the community. This can be ascribed to their approach towards the local legends.

Ballydara is out west in Donegal, away from the hustle of cities, and flanked by hills. On one hill in particular grows a nest of ringforts, very old and far predating the town itself. We Ballydara folk have often whispered that *na daoine sídhe* owns those lands, for is it not true that on some nights can be heard the most beautiful singing and music? Do we not see lights hanging on those mounds, bouncing up and down as if they were dancing? Do not weary travellers sometimes hear a woman calling out to them as they pass? And has not a few reckless souls gone to visit the mounds, then never come home? That has been the case since the days of my grandfather’s grandfather, and his grandfather’s grandfather. For long generations, we Ballydara folk have not dared set foot upon those mounds. Nonetheless, those hills have found themselves hosting foreigners, travelers, and wanderers, all interested in our legends but not our warnings. The Butler family proved no exception.

It happened one day at the old pub that Mr. Butler was speaking with some of his new friends, discussing his time farming the lands, and how he’d been growing new crops, but whenever he looked upon those hills so near his property, he wondered who owned them and if he might buy them. His friends stopped drinking their beer, just a little uneasy, and asked if he meant the hills where the mounds dwell. He said yes they were. They tried to dissuade him from the notion, explaining how those hills are still occupied, that no amount of money would empty them, and that he’d be better off finding another field to buy.

Well, Mr. Butler weren’t having it. He was a man who only believed his own eyes, and he’d never seen *na daoine sídhe* before - not in Galway, not in Dublin, not in Belfast, and not in Ballydara. His friends tried to convince him to be careful and cause a fuss over nothing, but a fuss was already kicking about in his head. After that visit to the pub, he went home and started making some calls, tracking down whoever owned those lands. After a few fruitless searches, he was all but ready to give up when the phone rang a few days later. It was a man claiming to be the proprietor of those hills who asked if he was still interested. Mr. Butler said yes and was sure he had more than enough money to pay for it. Imagine his surprise when he found he had too much money, which is to say, it did not cost as much as he’d expected. In any case, with it procured, he sent out ads promising good money to people for work on the hills. All he needed to do was set up a few fences, and he’d have plenty of room to raise flocks of sheep and cows.

Now, you will usually find someone who’ll work any job for any price, but there wasn’t a man or woman in all Ballydara who’d set foot on those hills, nor for a few miles. Eventually, Mr. Butler hired out a crew from out of the province, and they set to work building the fences. But queer things did happen; some mornings, the crew would find the wooden posts and barbed wire they’d just put in lying neatly on the ground, the hole it had been forced into filled back in with fresh grass. Other nights, the crew complained of hearing noises outside; little kids laughing at them and what sounded like foxes screaming through the night. One worker even swore he saw a woman in a long white dress, seeming not to walk but float over the ground. The man was so startled by it that he never returned to the site afterward, leaving his coworkers short a hand. So Mr. Butler had to hire a new fella to come down and help, while also installing security cameras to catch sight of this strange woman. This new fella was a Belfaster, a young man with good looks. Some of the girls in the town were swooning over him, and one or two of the lads. But nobody’s swooning for him on the work site. It’s his first job, and he’s not exactly what anyone would call a diligent worker. He’s slacking off, he’s making bad jokes, he’s distracting the others. The crew starts to complain about him, but Mr. Butler just tells them to leave it be. He’s already invested a lot in this venture, and he’s not about to hear any more complaints.

One workday, the crew is out by the fences, slamming them in tight. The Belfaster is drilling wire into the fence when he stops and turns to the ringfort, an old, desecrated thing covered in grass. The other workmen ask him what’s wrong, and he says he heard someone calling his name from over there. It was a woman’s, soft, airy, and sultry. None of the other lads had heard it, so they told him to stop mucking about and get back to work.

‘Twas another few minutes before the Belfaster stopped again and turned to the ringfort. This time, he swore he heard the woman calling him, begging for him to come and see her. The workman tell him to stop his joking and to leave it off. He swore he wasn’t joking, but they wouldn’t hear it. All they wanted to hear was his mouth shutting.

Another few minutes passed before the Belfaster jumped in the air and turned towards the ringforts. This time, he swore that a woman was calling his name, whispering all sorts of lovely things to him. One of the other workmen was about ready to clobber him when he stopped too, going white as a sheet. He says he heard a woman say his name, long and low.

Now the other workmen are getting a queer feeling, and they all set down their tools and go investigate the ringfort. They searched every inch of the place, from top to bottom, and couldn’t find anyone. And when they come back to the fence, what do they find but that all their tools have vanished!

The foreman of the crew called up Mr Butler and bluntly informed him that they wouldn’t be completing their work now, explaining what had happened. Mr Butler, now, he was furious over this, and that night at the pub, he ranted and raved with his friends about all those silly old superstitions that should all be dead and gone now. Mr Butler is sure that this weren’t the work of na daoine sídhe, but hooligans. Now he’d gone to the guard, but they didn’t find any evidence of suspicious fellows, and had more important things to do than look for missing tools. So he says to them all, “I’ll be there myself, tomorrow night, and I’ll find whoever’s been sabotaging me! If anyone here is brave enough, come with me, and I’ll pay you a hundred euros each!”

No one would take that offer. No matter how much money is offered, there are some things no one will do. For Ballydara, visiting those mounds was one of them. Mr Butler searched up and down the townland, but not a soul would accept. Once again, his friends tried to convince him otherwise, but he refused to listen and, cursing them all, went off to see if any in the work crew would come with him. The only one who did was the Belfaster. Mr Butler’s plan then was to sit in the mounds overnight, catch whoever was stealing from him, and bring them before the guard. For his good devotion, the Belfaster would be given two hundred euros.

On that night, people did see strange lights up in the mounds, but they weren’t dancing lights. They were still, like streetlamps in the old days, burning white. No music was heard, nor singing, nor birds singing, nor even a fox screaming. Only the crying of the cold wind.

When her husband did not return the next morning, Mrs Butler became very anxious. She asked some of her friends to come up to the mounds to see what was wrong. Now, her friends were sensible people, and like all sensible people, weren’t going to turn away a frightened woman. So the group of them go up to the mounds, and what do they find but the Belfaster, shivering against a tree, his shirt torn open, his eyes as mad as a rabid dog. When he saw the women approaching, he screamed like a lunatic and tore away, running out into the field. The guard caught him later, gibbering about nonsense, and he was taken to a hospital, where, so I am told, he still resides.

As for Mr Butler? He wasn’t to be found. There was a large, extensive search. All of Ballydara joined in, combing every inch of the land. His friends even searched the mounds. Others searched as far as the bogs. It went on for months, volunteers coming in from every part of Ireland, for Mr Butler had made many friends. The guard deployed sniffer dogs, but those dogs kept going to the mounds, and a few even tried to dig under them. Despite all this effort, not even a shoe turned up.

I am sad to say that Mrs Butler and her children were forced to move later, too close to the memory of their beloved father and husband. They still live comfortably off the money Mr Butler had built, but money is all they have left of him, and money is no substitute for a loved one.

There is one final addition to this tale. A few years after Mr Butler vanished, a pair of tourists came to Ballydara, interested in seeing the mounds where a man had vanished. Suppose that’s something we’ll have to put up with now. When those two did visit the mounds, one of them was tramping along the edge of a ringfort when she stepped on something. Perplexed, she bent down. It was a set of gold-rimmed spectacles, sticking out of the earth as if vomited. When they turned it into the guard, they collected it as evidence but did not reopen the case.
