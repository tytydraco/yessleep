Today, my daughter came running toward me. Her face was caught between terror and confusion. She spoke in a single breath.

"Dad! They are outside!"

I didn't understand what she was trying to say. We lived alone at 3217 Farland Street. My wife had passed away two years ago. Maybe three.

"Who are?" I asked.

"Don't you know them?" she said. "The kids who wear masks made of goat's flesh."

I had no idea who she was talking about.

"It's probably just some neighborhood kids playing a prank," I told her.

She looked at me perplexed. "But we live alone, don't we?"

I told her to go to bed. It was getting dark anyway, and the whole story sounded like something she had invented. But she rarely lied. I stood in the kitchen after she went upstairs, turning that over in my mind.

I went outside.

The sun was setting, bleeding the sky a deep blue. At the corner of my eye something moved. I turned, but there was nothing. There was only the empty yard, the tree line.

And the silence.

Then I saw it. A small figure lying in the grass near the fence. I walked over and crouched down.

When I picked it up, I went very still.

It was a statue of Baphomet. Small, dark, and carefully made. Someone had left it deliberately, oriented so it faced the front door.

I stood there holding it as the last of the light left the sky.

\---

As soon as I entered the house, I set the statue on my desk. Its face was now oriented toward the window. Toward outside.

I went upstairs to check on my daughter. She was still asleep.

Something wasn't right. Or was it? I stood in the hallway turning it all over, the statue, the figure in the yard, my daughter's story, trying to find sense. There was probably one. There usually is.

I shook it off and went to give her a goodnight kiss. As I leaned down, I noticed a few brown hairs clinging to her dress. Both she and I have black hair. I assumed it was probably just loose threads from the fabric, kissed her forehead, and went back downstairs.

The statue was still facing the window. I stood there looking at it for a moment. I had the brown hairs with me, so I opened the window, and let them go.

The smell hit me immediately. Rotten meat, thick and close. Something dead just outside in the dark. I shut the window and latched it.

I went to bed. I don't remember deciding to stop thinking about it. I just did.

\---

Later that night, I didn't sleep. It wasn't my daughter's story keeping me awake. It was something simpler and harder to name: the feeling of being watched. Not from a hidden angle. Directly. The way I feel a person's eyes on me across a room before I turn to confirm they are there.

I lay still for a long time, staring at the ceiling.

The window.

I had closed it after the smell, but I hadn't drawn the curtain. The glass was just sitting there, open to whatever was outside in the dark. I got up, went to the desk, and pulled the curtain across without looking through.

Then I heard it. A hard sliding sound behind me.

I turned around.

The room was empty. But the statue had moved. It was no longer facing the window. It was facing the door's window panel.

I stood there in the dark looking at it. I told myself the curtain had disturbed the air when I drew it. I told myself the desk was uneven. I told myself a lot of things, standing there in my socks at two in the morning.

I left the statue where it was and went back to bed.

The feeling of being watched was gone now, at least. Whatever had been outside the window, it wasn't there anymore.

But then, if it was the window giving me that feeling, why was the statue facing the door?

I don't think I slept. But at some point the night ended anyway.

\---

The morning came harsh and dull.

What woke me wasn't the sunlight. It was my daughter screaming. I heard her run downstairs and followed immediately, heart already ahead of my body.

She stood in the kitchen. Her clothes were covered in the same brown hair I had thrown out the window the night before. She was shaking. I stood in the doorway looking at her and felt everything from the past twelve hours press down on me at once.

I needed to think. I needed to be calm.

I asked her what happened.

"I don't know," she said. "I was sleeping and then I woke up and it was just... it was everywhere." Her voice broke on the last word.

I changed her clothes and held her for a moment. Then she pulled back and looked at me.

"Is it the kids?"

My body went still. Not because of the question. Because of something behind her.

The statue was facing the window again.

I asked her if she had touched it. She said she hadn't even noticed it was there until now.

I picked it up, walked to the front door, and threw it into the road as hard as I could. It hit the asphalt and skidded. I stood there waiting for it to shatter.

There were no visible cracks.

That was when the smell hit. Worse than the night before. Much worse. It was inside the house now. Thick and immediate, like something dead in the walls. My daughter almost puked. I brought her to my room and told her to stay, then followed the smell back.

It was strongest near the kitchen sink. I moved toward it slowly, eyes fixed on the cabinet drawer beneath.

I told my daughter to stay in my room. She was standing in the doorway anyway when I opened it.

In the cabinet where the silverware should have been, a dead goat’s head was tucked neatly among the ladles. Its body torn off. Its eyes hadn't been removed; they had been dried, turned into hard, cloudy marbles. Part of the skin had been meticulously flayed away in strips, cut with some precision, fashioned into the shape of small masks.

My daughter didn't stop screaming for a long time.

\---

It was at that moment I realized I was just as scared as my daughter.

Whatever this was, it had made a place for itself here.

“We are leaving this goddamn place,” I said hastily. “Don’t pack anything. We need to move fast."

No answer.

I went to grab my keys from the hook by the door. My Mustang Shelby was in the driveway. We could be on the road in thirty seconds if she would just move. But there was a sense of something lingering just around me.

Then I heard it.

Faint laughter. Childlike.

It grew deeper with every second, twisting into something far darker. My mind went numb. My temples burned; ice-cold and scorching at the same time. The pain was unbearable.

I finally reached the front door and pulled it open. 

The statue of Baphomet was gone.

“They said I should wear it.”

The words echoed slowly through the hallway. I realized then; my daughter hadn’t said a word until now.

I turned around.

She was standing on the stairs. The Baphomet statue sat on the step beside her, upright, facing me.

I looked at her.

Something covered her face. Not a cloth or plastic. Something that didn’t belong on a child. 

She was the one wearing the goat mask now.

The laughter wasn't outside anymore.
