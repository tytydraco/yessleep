The floorboards in my Victorian fixer-upper don't just creak; they groan, like the house is trying to digest the twenty years of neglect it endured before I bought it.

I didn’t mind the noise. As a single guy in a new town, the silence of the countryside was actually more unsettling than the house's "character." But three weeks ago, the character started getting specific.

It began with the mirror in the master bath. Every morning after my shower, I’d wipe a circle through the steam to shave. And every morning, I’d find a second handprint already there—smaller than mine, positioned right where someone would stand if they were leaning in to look over my shoulder.

"Real original, ghost," I’d mutter, trying to laugh it off. I’m a rational person. Old houses have moisture patterns.

Then came the humming.

It wasn't a spooky, ethereal wail. It was a low, melodic baritone—someone humming "The Man I Love." It would start just as I was drifting off to sleep, coming from the empty guest room down the hall.

One night, emboldened by a second glass of bourbon, I walked into that guest room. "Look," I said to the darkness. "If you’re going to haunt me, at least pick a more upbeat tune. Maybe some Gaga?"

The humming stopped. The room went cold—not 'drafty window' cold, but 'liquid nitrogen in the veins' cold.

Then, a voice. Soft, raspy, and sounding like it was being squeezed through a throat full of dust.

"You have his eyes."

I froze. "Whose eyes?"

"Elias."

I did some digging at the local library the next day. The house had belonged to the Miller family in the 1940s. The son, Julian, had been a local recluse. He never married. He died in that guest room in 1949, officially of "heart failure," though the town gossip column hinted at a broken heart after a "close friend" from the city stopped visiting.

That night, I woke up to the smell of tobacco and expensive pomade. My bedroom door was wide open.

Standing at the foot of my bed was a shadow. It wasn't a blur; it was a man in a sharp, double-breasted suit, his hair slicked back perfectly. He looked beautiful, and he looked absolutely miserable.

"I'm not Elias," I whispered, my heart hammering against my ribs.

The shadow moved closer. He didn't walk; he blurred the space between us. He leaned over me, and for a second, his face flickered into focus. He was young, maybe twenty-five, with eyes that looked like shattered glass.

"I know," he breathed. His hand—pale and translucent—reached out and brushed my cheek. It felt like a piece of dry ice. "But you’re the first one who’s ever let me stay."

I should have been terrified. I was terrified. But there was a loneliness in his expression that mirrored the same ache I'd felt since moving here.

"You can stay," I said, my voice trembling. "Just... no more handprints on the mirror. It's creepy."

The ghost of Julian Miller actually smiled. It was the saddest thing I’d ever seen.

That was a week ago. Since then, things have been... quiet. Better. He doesn't manifest often, but sometimes I’ll find my coffee moved to the side just the way I like it, or a book left open to a poem about longing.

But last night, I found a note on my nightstand. The paper was yellowed, brittle, and smelled of 1940s tobacco. The handwriting was elegant, looping script.

Elias came back tonight, it read. He told me he’s lonely where he is. He says there’s room for three.

I haven't looked in the mirror yet today. I can hear two voices humming in the guest room now. And the front door, which I know I locked, just clicked open.
