I’m from Chicago. Concrete, noise, people everywhere. Two years ago I burned out hard—quit my job, ended things with my ex, just needed quiet. Found this cabin online: cheap, 40 acres, middle of nowhere. Sounded like the reset I needed. Packed my laptop, some basics, and drove out here. Thought the scariest thing would be spotty cell service and raccoons in the trash.



First few months were weird but okay. Too quiet at first, but I adjusted. Worked during the day, scrolled at night. Deer sometimes came close to the porch. I even posted a couple pics like “new life unlocked” or whatever.



Then the feeling started. Like eyes on me. Always from the trees.



Little things first—twigs snapping too deliberately. Footsteps that matched mine then stopped when I did. I blamed wildlife. Bought a trail cam. Nothing useful ever showed up.



Then the breathing.



Three weeks ago I woke up around 2 a.m. Fan on low, room black. Heart already racing. Under the fan noise—slow, deep breathing. In… out… raspy and wet. Coming straight through the bedroom screen, right above my head. Like someone standing there, face almost touching the glass. I didn’t move. Couldn’t. It breathed for what felt like ten minutes straight. Then nothing. Crickets came back like a switch flipped.



I told myself it was my imagination. Stress. Bad sleep. Recorded it the next night—audio was just fan and my panicked breathing. But I knew.



Started sleeping on the living room couch. Kept the bedroom door shut. Figured if it wanted the window it could have an empty room.



Didn’t help.



Two nights ago it was at the back door. Soft puffs against the wood, like it was pressing close and sniffing the cracks. I sat up, phone in one hand, shotgun in the other (birdshot only—I bought it online because the realtor mentioned “occasional wildlife”). Twenty minutes of that slow breathing. Then it left. Leaves crunching away.



Last night it got me.



I was in the kitchen boiling water for tea. Lights low. Tiny cabin—one main room, bedroom off to the side. Kitchen window looks straight into the woods. No curtains because who’s gonna be out here?



Turned around for a mug. Froze.



Breathing. Against the glass. Loud. Close.



I killed the light. Stared at the dark window. My reflection looked like a ghost.



Inhale. Exhale. Right there.



I stepped up. Cupped my hands. Pressed my face to the cold glass to see past my own reflection.



It peeked.



Face inches away. Human-shaped… but wrong. Skin grayish and too tight, stretched over bones that didn’t line up right. No hair. Eyes huge, solid black, no whites, reflecting the faint moonlight like wet holes. Mouth too wide, lips thin and cracked, teeth uneven and sharp. The neck was long—too long—like it had to bend to fit under the window frame. It was crouched or hunched, but still tall enough that the top of its head was cut off by the top of the pane.



It looked like a person. But it wasn’t.



It tilted its head slowly, the way people do when they’re curious. Then it opened its mouth wider and breathed again—deep, deliberate, fogging the glass in slow bursts. I could smell it through the screen. Wet earth. Something dead underneath.



One long arm lifted. Fingers way too many, joints bending backward. It pressed the palm flat to the glass. No sound. Just pressure. The wood frame creaked a little.



I screamed and stumbled back. Hit the counter. Mug shattered. The face jerked away fast—melted into the dark trees like smoke.



I ran to the bedroom. Slammed the door. Dragged the dresser in front of it. Sat on the floor with the shotgun across my lap all night. Didn’t sleep. Kept hearing that breathing in my head, looping.



This morning I checked the window. No fingerprints. No scratches. Nothing. Just me in the reflection, looking like I haven’t slept in years.



But I saw it. Those black eyes. That stretched skin. That wrong body.



I’ve been reading about skinwalkers since I moved here. Not because I believed—just because the locals sometimes whisper about “things in the woods that wear people.” Old stories. Navajo stuff mostly, but people say they show up out here too. Imitate voices. Watch from the edges. Wait until you’re alone. Until you look back.



Tonight I can’t stay inside. The walls feel too thin.



I’m grabbing the shotgun, the flashlight, and I’m walking out to the tree line. I’m going to stand where it stands every night. I’m going to wait.



Because if it’s breathing that close, if it’s peeking like that, it’s not just watching anymore.



It’s waiting for me to invite it in. Or for me to come out.



If I don’t post again tomorrow… don’t come looking.



Stay inside. Lock your windows.



Whatever it is—it’s not human. And it knows I’m alone.
