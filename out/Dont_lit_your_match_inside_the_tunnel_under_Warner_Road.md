If you ever end up near a tunnel around Niagara Falls, DONT LIT A MATCH.  

It was my first day at my new job. I had recently been promoted to railroad inspector for Niagara Falls. I had been working around the rails since I was just 12, selling lemonade to people who are waiting for the train during summer and offering warm drinks in the winters. I was always fascinated with the trains and that led me to learn more about the railroad. By the time I was old enough, the railway felt less like a mystery and more like a path, and eventually I got a job as a ticket agent at the station. I loved my job and never cut corners and never missed a shift.  

That's where I met my future wife, Eliane, at the Niagara Falls train station and I fell in love with the first look. She was at the station one evening selling tickets for a local theatre show. Short blonde hair, freckles, warm brown eyes that made me forget what I was doing long enough for customers to start yelling at me. She noticed, asked if I wanted to go to the theater with her and a week later, I proposed to her with an engagement ring. Elaine accepted it on the spot. We got along very well.  

The inspection job paid well, and we could finally buy our dream house together. The inspection of the Grand Trunk Railway line wasn’t an exciting job, but it was necessary for the safety of trains. My duties were simple, look for uneven joints, loose fasteners, obstructions and anything that didn’t match the normal must be reported. I carried the standard tools, a measuring gauge, a small hammer, a notebook, and a railroad lantern. It was a windy day at Niagara Falls. I wrapped myself deeper into my coat. A gift from Eliane. “She is an angel” I thought to myself and smiled while touching my engagement ring.  

By late afternoon, I was working my way toward the stretch near Warner Road. I had heard about the place. Everyone had. From the way people got quiet when Warner Road is mentioned. The railway ran over the tunnel, not through it. The tunnel itself served as water drainage and passage for farmers. But now days no one dared to go there. “What stories people makeup when they are bored on the train” I mockingly muttered to myself.  

The wind howled through the trees as the sun sank low. I didn’t notice the train until the operator blew the horn. I jolted, lost my footing on the wet gravel, and hit the ground. Mud smeared my coat “Eliane won't be happy” I mumbled as I was getting up. Checked my hand; My ring stayed clean at least. The lantern rolled over to the tunnel entrance when I fell. “Just my luck” For a second I considered leaving it and heading back. But the light was dying fast, and the wind made everything feel colder than it should have. I took out a box of matches from my pocket and walked towards the lantern. I struck a match. The flame jumped to life bright, confident and the wind killed it instantly, as if the wind had been waiting. I exhaled through my nose, irritated. Picked up the lantern and stepped deeper into the tunnel, just far enough to escape the breeze. I struck another match; this time the flame was held, trembling but alive. I leaned toward the lantern’s wick. I lit up the lantern, closed the lantern lid, picked it up, and looked around. 

The tunnel was surprisingly quiet and warm. I looked around the stone walls, old markings, uneven and muddy ground. The tunnel was short; I could see the other side clearly. There was a disgusting smell in the air, like a half burnt carcass. I heard something from the other side; it was not clear what it was. “Anybody there?” I yelled. “Must have been the wind” I whispered to myself.  

And then it started. A woman’s scream. It was raw and full of hatred. It filled the tunnel so completely it felt like it came from the stone itself, vibrating through the limestone and into my ribs. I covered my ears with both hands and started going out. Scram stopped; the silence was worse. The smell of burnt corpse was stronger now, I looked back, my biggest mistake. A woman stood just beyond my reach, too tall, too still. Her skin looked pale in the lantern's glow, but her eyes were black; nothing but pure darkness lived there. Her hair hung in scorched strands, like it had burned and never finished burning. Looking at hand, my ring. Her jaws opened not like a person. Like snake getting ready to sallow its prey. And the scream tore out of her again. I wanted to scream but I couldn't, as if I was getting choked. I fell to the ground again. This was not NORMAL. 

A few days later I woke up at a local hospital. That night they sent a group after me since I failed to check back in with the station. They told me I was out on the ground; my coat was burnt. Elaine left flowers next to my bed. She convinced me to write this and warn people about that place. Don't go inside the tunnel near Warner Road, and if you must go inside, DONT light a match.
