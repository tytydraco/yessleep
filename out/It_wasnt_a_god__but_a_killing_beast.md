**THE DISTURBED YEMU**  
*by Galaxy Master*

Jill Jorning was a cattle shepherd who lived in the valley of Teja Terhum — a village blessed with rising, flourishing beauty. The meadows stretched long and endless until they met the grand sea. Teja Terhum was nicknamed “Buluri,” the gifted land, for its unmatched glory.

Jill never grew bored grazing his muscular beasts. He was as curious as the shores that longed to swallow the entire meadow, which stretched across countless acres. Yet he had never been able to climb the forbidding mountain that towered over Teja Terhum.

One day, driven by pure wonder, he joined the restricted Hoja — the mountain wanderers. These people spent half the year in the arched valleys and the other half in the high, harsh mountains. They were as strong as a pride of lions and as clever as orcas, but they never accepted villagers or so-called civilized folk among them.

Jill’s curiosity knew no bounds. To everyone’s surprise, the Hoja accepted him. The climb was tough and bone-breaking. Jagged rocks tore at his hands and feet, and the stinking terrain tested his limits, but Jill refused to let his curiosity die. While his sister Nora stayed behind to care for the cattle, he pushed onward until he reached the icy peak.

At the summit, his breaths came shallow and warm. The land below looked even more blessed than his sweet meadows. He accepted his destiny and sat on the edge of a rock.

The Hoja did not rest or celebrate. Instead, they brought fish meat, covered it with mud along with some oily fruits, and offered it at sunrise. Jill watched as the men retreated to higher grasslands, where a group of eerie scarecrows stood. The Hoja worshipped them. They placed the offerings before the figures with deep respect.

Jill thought they had lost their minds. These scarecrows were far creepier than the ones in his village — twisted and unnatural. What truly held his gaze was the strange, glowing stone embedded in each of their chests. He stayed motionless, eyes fixed on them even when he closed his eyelids.

He spent several days with the Hoja. Then the dream came.

One of the scarecrows — which they call the **Yemu** — began calling to him, urging him to bring it down to his land. The vision filled him with growing frustration. He desperately wanted to return home but could not leave.

When three members of the Hoja left to hunt a special fish called garnils — the sacred food they fed to the Yemu — Jill saw his chance. In a rush of madness, he lifted one of the scarecrows with his bare hands and retraced the dangerous path back to the village. It took him two exhausting days. He drank from mountain rivers and ate the stolen garnils to survive.

Upon reaching Teja Terhum, he planted the Yemu firmly in the middle of the grazing grounds to declare it the guardian of his land. The figure stood tall and imposing. Satisfied, Jill left it there and returned to his cattle.

But the next morning, two of his cattle had vanished.

He suspected the Hoja had discovered the theft. Yet the Yemu remained exactly where he had placed it. As days passed, Jill found himself staring at the scarecrow for hours, especially at the beautiful, hypnotic stone in its chest.

Strange misfortunes soon began to plague the village. Kema, a respected man, suddenly tried to kill his own mother — an unthinkable crime in Teja Terhum, where even bandits spared women and elders. Cattle started disappearing. When Jill fetched water, he discovered the river had turned salty, as if the ocean had crept inland from the upper lands. The crops would surely fail.

Alarmed, he warned the villagers. They rushed to find solutions, but none worked. The wind grew violently strong. Cattle began dying without drinking the poisoned water. Misery spread.

Then Jill noticed Kema acting even stranger. One evening, he heard Nora screaming — Kema was grabbing her by the throat. Jill struck Kema hard with a stick, forcing him to flee. But the horrors only worsened.

During a violent thunderstorm, Jill went to secure his cattle in the stable. There, he found Kema brutally killing one of the animals. When Jill attacked, Kema fought back with unnatural strength, sending him crashing toward the lake. In the pouring rain, Jill pushed Kema into the river. As Kema grabbed his hand and beat him savagely, Jill stabbed Kema’s hand with a knife. The man vanished into the dark, churning waters.

The next day, Kema’s mother wept bitterly — her only child was gone. Nora collapsed in grief. Jill could not bring himself to tell the truth. Doing so might also bring punishment upon his sister.

As the village suffered, Jill grew convinced the Yemu was the source of the curse. He kept his suspicions silent, determined to prove it first.

Late one night, during another raging thunderstorm, Nora woke him in panic. She had heard cattle screaming inside the stable. When Jill rushed to check, he witnessed a nightmarish scene: three of his own cattle were viciously attacking a fourth, tearing it apart until its legs were severed from its body.

Enraged and terrified, he ran to the Yemu and shouted at it. The figure remained motionless — until he began rocking it violently. Suddenly, the Yemu seemed to enjoy the chaos. In fury, Jill started dancing wildly in the storm. A thunderbolt struck the valley, and as he fell, the Yemu slowly turned its head and stared directly at him.

Horrified, Jill scrambled to his feet and ran toward the house. There, waiting for him, stood Kema — soaked in blood, one eye missing, his fists bony and deformed, his face a haunting mask of rage. With unnatural power, Kema threw Jill against the rocks. The possessed cattle attacked from behind while Kema came from the front. Jill’s toe was ripped off in the brutal struggle.

Fighting desperately, Jill began to dance again — this time with fierce, ritualistic power. His movements synced perfectly with the raging storm. Thunder clapped in time with every step. As he completed the final movement, a massive lightning bolt struck the Yemu, setting it ablaze. The heavy rain could not extinguish the flames.

Kema and the possessed cattle began burning as well, slowly turning to ash. Jill rushed forward, grabbed a blade, and severed the Yemu’s head. Its unearthly cry finally stopped.

The thunderstorm cleared instantly.

The next morning, the sun rose over a peaceful valley once more. The river ran fresh and clear again. The villagers danced with joy and relief as life returned to normal.

But Jill lay wounded on the grass, his body broken. Though the land had healed, the mental peace he once knew had been lost forever.
