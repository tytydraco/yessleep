**Part one: The Best In Show:**



It was another hard winter… not that that was surprising for our small town. Our side

of the Dales hadn’t seen a winter without snow for as long as anyone could

remember. In our old market town, we never used to let it get to us, though. We were

all in it together and everyone had everyone’s back.

I should mention now that at that time, when I was still a kid, I didn’t really appreciate

the role of the Dacre family and their importance to our town. They were “the first

family” – practically local royalty.

Keith, Helen his wife, and their twin daughters Rebecca and Isabelle - the Dacres

were certainly the wealthiest in town with Keith owning the biggest source of

employment in the area, “Dacre’s Machinery Ltd.,” supplying farming and agricultural

equipment to most of the Yorkshire Dales and Cumbria.

Helen was also well-established, owning a beauty salon in the centre. Helen would

be in every day the salon was open, chatting cheerfully to everyone who came in.

This also allowed her to get to know nearly everything about nearly everyone in the

community. To say we had never spoken, she nevertheless knew everything about

my life…

She was always a showcase for her own salon – perfectly set hair, flawless make up

and elaborately decorated nails, long before nail bars were found on every street.

Before Christmas, she’d always have reindeer and snowmen or some such fancy

painted onto her talons…

The girls, Rebecca and Isabelle, were in my little sister’s year at school. Ordinary,

annoying girls – like I thought of every girl back then. Just with posher toys than most

of us.

It was the Dacre family that started off the competitions, my mum told me.

Apparently, Keith’s dad started the tradition. Or his dad’s dad… Every winter we

would host a myriad of competitions to keep spirits high and attempt to smother the

blues some felt at that time of year. There was “best soup in town”, “best chilli in

town” and the usual Christmas pudding and Christmas cake bake-offs. The local

wool shop organised a “knitwear catwalk” bombarding people with colourful jumpers,

throws, blankets, and a seemingly bottomless supply of hats. No vested interest

there, then. Every pun intended.

It all changed when I was about twelve. The last year before it all stopped.

The main competition, that almost everyone took part in, was the “Best snowman

competition”. You will have seen scarecrow trails and scarecrow competitions in

villages? Well, we reckoned on getting creative with the predictable snow, using the

cold to warm the hearts of the community. Every household would chip in a couple of

quid and then make snowmen in different shapes and sizes. On a given day, four

appointed judges would walk around the town and score them. The winning

household would win half of the money paid in, with the other half being donated to

the winning household’s charity of choice.

This particular year, however, the Dacre family would be absent. They had decided

that they would book a pre-Christmas trip to Lapland so the girls could meet Father

Christmas. They wouldn’t shut up about it, and back then, I just thought that they

were annoying, spoilt little brats.

Keith informed the competitions’ committee that this year, as they weren’t going to be

there for the snowman competition, that he would match the charity donation and

give that to the charity the winning household picked.

The evening before they set off for Lapland, the Dacres had a meal together in the

local pub. They were all excited for the adventures that were to come, and Keith was

in such a good mood he left a three-hundred-pound float behind the bar for everyone

to have a Christmas drink on the Dacre family, much to the joy of everyone there.

Especially my father. They left in good spirits to trudge through the snow to the “big

house” that looked down on the town from way up the hill.

A few days later and most of the town, as they did every year, had built their various

snowmen. All were in their front gardens or on the community greens for those that

didn’t have gardens, like me. My sister and I had sweated over ours for ages. This

was the first time I’d managed to get a third layer onto it. To us, then, it was a work of

art. In retrospect, it was terrible.

Most snowmen had a sign, often with a random pun, relating to the theme of their

build. I put a grass skirt and a blonde wig on ours and called it “Hips Don’t Lie” after

the song released by my pre-teen crush on Shakira. I thought my design was ruined

by the fact my mum wouldn’t let me put a bikini top on it.

So, the streets were buzzing with people walking around, excitedly looking at the

builds and talking about which ones were their favourites. The competitions’

committee judges, including my mum, were out in their stride, making sure to view

each one and mark their check lists and then they would leave a little board on a

stick next to each build with a score out of ten.

The committee consisted of four, as I said, but this year they were accompanied by

my sister and I, as mum wouldn’t leave us in the house alone after the last time…

The judges were always quick to get out and view the snowmen as soon as possible

on the elected day, so the weather didn’t ruin the details. They made their way to the

second green, and I struggled to keep my mouth shut as two of the judges awarded

our masterpiece a pitiful three points.

Then, we were dragged up the hill to survey what wonders the Dacres has created.

They had a montage, a family of four large and elaborate snowmen in the front

garden of their huge house, all of which were wearing ornately patterned fluffy hats

and scarves, knitted at the wool shop. The colours contrasted to the cold features of

their faces, consisting of rocks and pebbles for eyes and mouths. And, as everyone

else, they had put a sign at the bottom of the display, this one reading “Best in

Show!”

Twelve-year-old me was livid – it didn’t even look like they had used their own snow:

the figures were surrounded by immaculate, pristine snow, unmarred by footprint or

dog wee, like we had to contend with on the green. But even as I opened my mouth

to complain, my mother’s stern look silenced me. It wasn’t worth risking her wrath

just before Christmas. Presents were at stake.

June, however, another of the judges and owner of the wool shop, cooed over them.

“So festive! Such beautiful craftsmanship in the traditional snowflake patterns!” as

she wrote “10/10” on her score card. This was the first time I had come to

understand the concept of match rigging...

“I don’t like those,” whispered Meredith, my little sister, clutching at the front of my

jacket as she looked at the two snowmen representing the twins. “They’re spooky.”

I thought she was daft, feeling uneasy. After all – they were just snowmen!

Nevertheless, I gave her a brief hug. She was only six, after all, and mum was

watching.

The judges conferred, worked out the average score, and marked the board. The

Dacres’ snowmen had an average score of 9/10. Certain to win. I had calculated that

the kitty to the winners this year would be about a hundred and twenty, after the

charity donations. Nothing to the Dacres. But to someone like me…?

Like I said - I was livid.

We all began to trudge back down the hill towards the town, the cold stare of the four

snowmen seemingly still unnerving Meredith as she and I looked back one last time

at them. The bloody Dacres. Even their bloody snowmen looked down on the rest of

us…

That evening, I shared my annoyance with my friends whilst playing “Halo 2” on X-

Box live. I told them about the fixed voting – perhaps slightly exaggerating how mean

the judges had been about their efforts as well as mine – making out that only my

mum was anything like fair. Then, we came up with what we thought was a great

idea.

Each of us claimed to be going round to another’s house for a game, instead,

meeting at the dark side of the green. The stars were glimmering in clear patches of

sky, whilst the moon was surrounded by a nimbus of high, thin cloud, as our band of

five positively scampered excitedly up the hill.

As we passed through the gates onto the drive, amazed that they weren’t locked, a

sense of our own intended wrongdoing made me pause. But only momentarily. With

childish glee, we committed the funniest, most shocking deed that our juvenile brains

had dreamed up: we spray-painted willies and boobs on to the snowmen. Did I

imagine the snowmen staring down at us balefully? I think I did…

Did we then get distracted by what felt like acres of deep, crisp, pristine snow? What

kid could resist? Before we knew it, we were playing our own version of “Halo” in the

garden, using trees and benches as defence posts. Shrieking and giggling, our

voices echoed in the crisp night air.

Carried away, I reckoned I could go up against all of them like the Masterchief. As a

flurry of snowballs pounded relentlessly into my face, I realised just how wrong I was!

Barely able to see and get my bearings, I stumbled over towards the snowmen, to

take shelter behind their bulk.

Reaching out from behind the montage to launch further missiles, I suddenly felt my

foot slip from under me, and I slid down the side of the nearest snowman.

My mates, of course, laughed hysterically as a chunk of the snow from the arm fell

with a heavy splat on top of my head. Winded by the fall, I didn’t realise how

suddenly that laughter stopped. As I tried to move, I felt a sharp scratch close to my

eye and then a sensation of the snow being raked from the top of my head. As I

finally sat up, still feeling an odd pressure on the top of my head, and more snow

gave way, I became aware of the horror on my friends’ faces. All were seemingly

staring at my head. Or above my head. I tipped my face slowly back. There were the

unmistakable, elaborately-manicured fingernails of Mrs Dacre raking through my

hair.

In absolute terror, I kicked out blindly at the ground, the snow, the figure, and with

what seemed like a groan, the rest of the snowman - and its macabre contents – fell

back, away from the next snowman along. Disturbed, further snow fell away from the

second figure, and the tall, dead form of Mr Dacre was revealed, his face still

contorted in horror.

I remember the sound of my own hysterical screaming ringing through the night

before we charged, still shrieking like possessed beings, down the hill, rousing the

sleepy town, chased by the wave of lights suddenly turned on in each house, as our

terror disturbed their peace.

All I remember of that Christmas now is the sleepless nights when I would feel, again

and again, the manicured nails of Mrs Dacre comb through my hair. That, and the

endless questioning by police.

My parents’ silent compassion regarding the matter was one of the worst things: their

lack of anger just emphasised the true horror we had encountered.

The town’s spirit died that day. No competition could ever be brought back without

evoking the ghost of four murder victims.

Because, although no-one was charged, it had to be murder. They couldn’t bury

themselves in elaborately adorned carapaces of snow.

It was the lack of answers that scared the town the most. Forensics revealed no

cause of death. Or deaths. No drugs in their systems. No stab wounds. No petechial

haemorrhaging. Their home surveillance footage claimed to be unaltered,

untampered with, yet the snowmen seemingly appeared between one sweep of the

grounds and the next, suggesting everything was done within less than three

minutes. Which is impossible. No footprints – other than ours – were found. There

was no sign of anyone other than the Dacres entering their grounds that night.

Nothing was ever the same after that. As I said, I had never appreciated what the

Dacres did for the town. People lost heart: their little paradise was ruined.

Employment opportunities dwindled and many, my father included, lost their jobs and

were obliged to move away to find work. That’s what we ended up doing, too. A

couple of my mates from that night stayed: the five of us were never all together

again. The big house never found a buyer. If you had enough money to afford that,

you wouldn’t be moving to a dying town

Why bring this up now?

I had an email from an old mate who’s still in the town, one who was with me that

night. All it had was a picture of what was once the Dacres’ garden. There was a

montage, a family of four large and elaborate snowmen in the front garden of the

huge, abandoned house, all wearing ornately patterned fluffy hats and scarves. And

the figures were surrounded by immaculate, pristine snow, unmarred by footprint.

A sign at the bottom of the display, read “Still the best in Show!”
