


As a casual learner and reader, I’m the kind of guy who needs books beside me to read and draw. I’m not searching for treasure; I’m searching for the perfect book that fits me. Ironically, finding it turned out to be far tougher and more grotesque than any actual treasure hunt.

I had been wandering in and out of libraries and bookstalls, but none of them had what I was looking for. Even the famous ones didn’t meet my expectations. I had heard about \*Great Expectations\*, which felt like it might be my type, though I had never cared much for it despite all the praise calling it a diamond.But I wanted to give it a try.

I live in the countryside, where the ground is brittle and always stays cold. I couldn’t even use the left bathroom because it weakens the ground and could make the old house collapse.

I m a member in a community called Cornen. They owned a small, old library filled with hundreds and thousands of untouched, valuable, old books. No soul ever seemed to visit it.

I needed to go there to fetch something and, hopefully, find the book I was searching for. As soon as I entered, I met a chubby woman in her fifties wearing old-fashioned, weird clothing. She looked like a walking boulder. I didn’t pay her much attention until she walked towards me. Her scent was decent, but her hair looked scrambled and thin. She wore a stupid band and her name tag read “Chatlie.”

She asked what book I was looking for. I told her. Without much fuss, the not-so-busy mistress turned her full body and moved away. As she walked, the distant humid weather seemed to grow heavier, and her footsteps strangely synced with the low buzz of lightning. She took out a book and handed it to me. Her face looked dull, as if she had some beef with me. She grinned and said, “Great expectations? You hadn’t better choice, dear. The books have been untouched for years. Read it, my sweetheart. This won’t make you smile, as it didn’t make me.”

I glanced at her. Her lips were dry and cracked. I left the place feeling something was off.

Back home, I bathed, dressed nicely, and placed the book on the table with excitement. I was ready to try a new path. I had always been tormented by those romantic books. Today, I thought, would feel different.

I opened the book and enjoyed the first few pages, but then something felt wrong. The book didn’t sit well with me. As I turned the pages, my heart grew weaker. Finally, I closed it. My collar was wet with sweat. My mind kept repeating, “What the hell did I just read?”

It wasn’t anything like people said about \*Great Expectations\*. This was some sort of Japanese horror piece. The climate outside didn’t match the eerie atmosphere the book created. Someone had clearly pranked me. I turned to the cover and last pages, looking for any label or sign that it was the wrong book, but there was nothing.

I returned to the library and gave the book back to the woman. She had an even wider smile now. As I turned to leave, she stopped me and said, “Didn’t you believe me, son? I told you this book wasn’t meant for you! Ah, but I have a solid one that will help you out.”

She turned, grabbed an old dusty book, and handed it to me with a strange “ahh” smile. I took it and smiled back. The cover alone said enough, but I never judge a book by its cover.

I returned home as the clouds grew thicker and darker. I soon realised it was another horror genre book, but I didn’t care and proceeded to read. The first few pages hooked me, but it quickly got worse. The story dove into a paranormal nightmare that left me deeply unsettled.

I turned the pages until I reached page 142 and stopped. It couldn’t get any worse than this. It was a clever topic, but definitely not meant for me. The story was about a girl and her father living in a mansion. Their life was plagued by paranormal events. The father started acting strange, and then came the shower scene — he melted like wax while showering in boiling hot water for hours. The daughter escaped and ran off somewhere… and then the pages went blank.

I cursed myself and decided to return the book, taking some pie with me as a peace offering. I wouldn’t suggest anyone read or go through this stupid book.

On the last page, written in bad handwriting, it said:  
“Charles Dickens was a fraud!”  
Below it was the name of an author I had never heard of: “Chatlie Williams, 1865.”

I threw the book on the table. A sudden wind blew through, moving the chair. I quickly closed the window and sat down to eat dinner.

Then someone knocked on the door.

I thought it must be the neighbour’s gardener needing a fresh bag to pile up the old leaves before the storm hit. I opened the door, but it was a little girl standing there in the dull evening. Her face looked strange and bitter.

“I don't want to go home,” she mutered.

Her condition was terrible, as if she had been running for a long time. I let her in and gave her a place to sit. She looked exhausted. I tried dialing the rescue team, but the phone wouldn’t connect. The room felt cold, so I turned up the flames in the chimney, keeping them controlled.

While pouring tea for her, I asked where she lived. She stayed quiet at first, then finally spoke.

“I’m from a mansion,” she said. Then she leaned closer and whispered, “My father is dead. He died during a shower. He had been acting strange for a few weeks… and then he melted away while showering.”

I couldn’t think straight. I pulled my chair back and gulped, the air suddenly feeling thick in my throat. I turned around, terrified she might kill me or bring her half-melted father here. But she was just sitting there, crying.

I felt trapped. I closed the room door, but when I turned back, the book on the table was flipping its own pages. It stopped at page 142.

“What now?” I exclaimed.

Suddenly, the door pushed me into the hall. The girl was standing there, pointing her finger towards the bathroom with a low, sobbing voice.

“My father is right there… bathing.”

My feet felt something strange — the floor was getting warm. As I looked towards the bathroom, I heard water leaking. I rushed over. Boiling hot water was pouring out. The walls were cracking. My mind went blank.

I hit the bathroom door several times, trying to stop the water before it collapses the entire house. I could hear a man growling inside. The hot water was spreading into the hall. In panic, I did the only thing I could think of — I slammed the book shut.

After reading so many books in my life, I brought this cursed one to the home shrine where my beloved Jesus sat. I placed it there and sat down. I heard footsteps and when I turned slowly a human figure appeared with a half-melted face and eyes popping out. He gripped my throat tightly and yelled, “I was bathing and you didn’t let me!”

As he choked me, the Jesus statue began moaning in pain. The tighter he gripped, the louder the statue moaned. It was an unsettling moment. Then the figure suddenly disappeared into the darkness.

I was left traumatised, coughing violently.
 The Jesus statue went silent. When I stood up and touched it, I felt a heartbeat syncing with mine. The moment I took the hand off from  it, I fell unconscious.

I woke up the next morning. Whatever had entered me was still there. I stood against it until everything fell silent again.

I took the book and went back to the library. This time, the woman was nowhere to be seen. Only a man was there, talking to another person. I approached him, returned the book angrily, and said, “Where is that old filthy woman that bit—”

I was at the peak of my anger and wanted answers. The librarian looked confused and replied that they had never hired a woman and also he closed the library yesterday afternoon and the day before as well.

I tried to argue, but it was useless. As I handed over the book, he looked at it strangely and said, “This book? Did you read it? It hasn’t been taken out in decades. It was buried among those useless old piles of newspaper. How did you even manage to dig it out?”

I tried to explain, but I knew what I had seen was unreal, so I stayed quiet.

“Did you read all the pages?” he asked.

In a high tone, I replied, “This book was never completed. It’s blank!”

The other person leaned in, flipped through the pages, and said, “Who told you that? It’s full of words down there.”

I wasn’t even surprised anymore. This might have been fate. I mentioned the strange woman to the librarian, but he had no idea who she was. He only said that many people found the book so disturbing that they stopped publishing it.

I still wonder who that woman was and why she recommended this cursed book to me. But at that moment, I just left it at the doorstep.
