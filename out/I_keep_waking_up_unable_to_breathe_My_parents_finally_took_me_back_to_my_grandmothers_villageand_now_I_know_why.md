I jolted awake, my chest tight and my lungs refusing to fill.

At first, I thought it was just another bad dream.

But then I tried to breathe again.

And nothing changed.

The room was dim, barely lit by the faint glow of my lamp, but everything felt wrong. My body was freezing—too cold for a warm summer night. My feet felt like ice, and my hands were already starting to shake.

It was happening again.

I forced myself upright and twisted into that strange position I had learned over the years—the one that sometimes helped me get just enough air into my lungs.

Then I started forcing my breathing.

Loud.

Deep.

Desparate.

Sometimes it worked.

Tonight, I wasn't so lucky.

My chest tightened even more. My heart started racing so fast it felt like it was trying to escape. My breathing grew louder and harsher, but no matter how hard I tried, it wasn't enough.

I wasn't getting enough air.

I was going to die. 

My vision blurred.

Darkness crept into the edges of the room.

And somehow, I felt even colder.

My whole body started shaking.

Suddenly, I jumped out of bed.

Not by choice.

My body just reacted.

I grabbed the water bottle next to my lamp and drank quickly, hoping it would fix something—anything.

It didn't.

I ran.

My bedroom door was slightly open. I pulled it wide and stumbled down the hallway. 

"Wake up!" I tried to shout, but my voice came out broken.

By the time I reached their room, my mother was already getting out of bed.

My father didn't even ask what was wrong.

He already knew.

He rushed to the drawer, grabbed a lighter and a bundle of African incense, and dropped to his knees.

The smell filled the room immediately—thick, earthy, almost suffocating.

Then he started chanting.

Low.

Steady.

Repetitive.

Clan names.

"Forgive me," he said.

"I should have done this sooner."

"Please...just give us more time."

More time for what?

Usually, this is where my breathing would start to come back.

Slowly.

Then eventually, it would be like nothing happened.

But this time...

It didn't.

It got worse.

Everything after that happened fast.

We threw on whatever we could and rushed to the car. My father drove faster than I had ever seen him drive before, his hands tight on the steering wheel. 

My mother sat beside me in the back seat, holding a brown paper bag to my face.

"Breathe," she kept saying. "Slowly...slowly...."

It wasn't working.

But I convinced myself it was.

I had to.

We reached the clinic. 

And just like that...

I could breathe again.

The moment the car stopped, the pressure lifted.

Like it had never been there.

As big as I was, my father carried me inside.

The nurse looked at me, confused as she placed an inhaler over my face.

She asked me questions.

I answered.

"If you were really struggling to breathe," she said, "you wouldn't be able to talk."

I felt anger rise in my chest.

I wanted it to come back.

Right there.

In front of her.

Just so they would finally believe me.

But like always...

they found nothing.

No asthma.

No illness.

No explanation.

On the drive home, my parents argued.

Louder than usual.

More desperate.

"This is your fault," my mother said.

"You knew this would happen."

My father stayed quiet.

"You said leaving would fix it," she continued. "You said changing everything would help."

"I thought it would," he finally said.

"You were wrong."

Silence.

Then-

"He needs to go back."

That's when something began to make sense.

Ever since I was born, things between them had been falling apart.

My father started drinking more.

At first, it was occasional.

Then it became every night.

Their arguments became normal.

Loud.

Endless.

And somehow...

I always felt like it was because of me.

That night, my mother packed a bag.

"He's going," she said.

"I'm done watching him suffer."

My father resisted at first.

Then he gave in.

But not without warning.

"If he goes...we can't go with him," he said. 

"We're not allowed near that house anymore."

In the morning, we left.

When we reached the village, my father stopped the car far from my grandmother's house.

He didn't even look in its direction.

"You'll find it," he said.

My mother hugged me tightly before letting go.

Like she wasn't sure I'd come back the same.

I walked alone.

The village was quiet.

Too quiet.

No cars.

No voices.

No animals.

Not even the sound of wind.

Then I saw her.

An old woman walking toward me.

Staring at me.

When she got closer, she grabbed my hand suddenly.

"There you are," she said.

Calling me by a name I didn't recognize.

"We need to hurry."

She led me to a house surrounded by bushes and trees. 

As soon as we stepped inside, she locked the door.

"You must never be outside after seven pm," she said.

"The things that walk, then...do not like to be seen."

Then she smiled.

Like nothing was wrong.

That night, I couldn't sleep.

I was tossing and turning.

The silence felt even more unbearable.

No insects.

No distant sounds.

Nothing.

Eventually, I needed to use the bathroom.

She had left a bucket in my room.

I ignored it.

I went outside.

The air felt heavy.

Thick.

Wrong.

I turned on my phone's flashlight.

And that's when I saw it.

Standing near the trees.

It looked like a man.

But his head...

It was tilted at an angle no human neck should allow.

He didn't move.

He didn't speak.

He just stood there.

Watching me. 

I called out—

"Don't say a word."

I froze.

My grandmother stood behind me.

Her face was pale.

Her voice shaking. 

She pulled me inside and locked the door.

Three times.

Then she looked at me.

"That's not a man," she said.

She paused.

"That will be you...if you accept what you were born to become."

The thing outside...

was my great-uncle.

The one I was named after.

He refused his calling to become a witch doctor.

And now...

he waits.

"For someone to take his place," she said.

"Or for someone of his name to finish what he didn't start."

That night, something scratched at my window.

Slow.

Delibarate.

Like long nails dragging across glass.

In the morning, we found something outside.

A piece of cloth.

Old.

Burnt.

Covered in soot.

With marks pressed into it.

Like fingers. 

My grandmother told me to bury it.

At the edge of the property.

Then she said:

"Tomorrow...we begin your initiation."

So before you name your child after someone in your family...

Make sure you know who they were.
