The first time I dreamed of Ashdown, I was nine years old.

In the dream, I stood at the edge of town beside a wooden sign with white peeling letters:

**WELCOME TO ASHDOWN**  
**POPULATION 2,091**

Someone had scratched a line through the number and painted underneath:

**2,092**

I woke before I could ask what that meant.

The dream came back every few months after that, then every few weeks, then, by the time I was thirty-four, every night without fail. Always the same town. Always dusk. Always that strange amber light, as if sunset there had been stretched too long and gone stale.

There was the diner with the cracked green stools. The church with no cross on top, just a bare steeple like a pointing finger. The school playground wrapped in chains although it was never locked. The gas station with one broken pump. The narrow rows of houses, each with trimmed lawns and curtains half drawn, as if the whole place had decided privacy was a performance.

And always there were people.

Not random dream-people with blurred faces and shifting features. No. These people were fixed. Repeating. Familiar in the way coworkers become familiar, or neighbours you don’t like enough to know. The woman at the diner who polished the same glass forever. The mailman with one clouded eye. Three boys on bicycles who never moved from the corner but somehow were always watching me arrive. An old man in a barber shop chair with a towel around his neck, turning slowly to look at me before the dream ended.

Sometimes they spoke.

“Not yet,” the woman at the diner would say.

“Wrong road,” said the mailman.

The boys never spoke, but they smiled too wide.

My therapist, because naturally this became therapist material, called it recurring anxiety imagery. Stress. Symbolic landscape. Unresolved blah blah, all the usual polished nonsense people come up with when their brains start wallpapering over cracks. I nodded, paid her, and kept dreaming.

Then my mother died, and the dreams changed.

The town remained the same, but the people were warmer now. Expectant.

“We’ve missed you,” the barber said once.

The woman in the diner set a cup of coffee in front of me and said, “You’ll stay longer next time.”

I woke with the taste of burnt coffee in my mouth.

After that I stopped telling anyone.

Two years later, on a wet Thursday in October, I took a wrong turn trying to avoid a highway closure outside a place called Gap Creek. My phone had no signal. The GPS map froze and turned into a blank gray square. Rain hammered the windshield. I drove for twenty minutes through pine forest and then the road curved downhill, and there it was.

The sign.

**WELCOME TO ASHDOWN**  
**POPULATION 2,091**

A line had been scratched through the number.

Underneath, in faded white paint:

**2,092**

My hands locked on the wheel so hard my knuckles hurt.

I should have kept driving. That would’ve been the sane option. The healthy option. The option for people who don’t wander into their own nightmares because curiosity has better marketing than self-preservation. But I slowed. Then stopped. Then, because apparently I have the survival instincts of a moth with car keys, I drove in.

Everything was there.

The diner. The church. The schoolyard. The gas station with one broken pump.

No dream distortion. No foggy edges. No sleepy strangeness to excuse it. The place was exact. Even the pothole in front of the post office, filled with rainwater and dead leaves, was where it had always been.

A boy on a bicycle lifted one hand as I passed.

I nearly drove into a mailbox.

He was one of the boys from the dreams. Older now, maybe sixteen, but unmistakable. Freckles across the nose. Sharp chin. Smile too broad.

He waved like he knew me.

At the diner, I pulled over, mostly because I thought I might vomit. The bell above the door rang when I stepped inside.

Every head turned.

Not in the casual way strangers glance up. Not the flicker of curiosity reserved for newcomers. This was recognition. Immediate and collective.

The woman behind the counter put down the glass she was polishing.

“Well,” she said, and smiled as if I’d finally shown up for a dinner reservation. “You took your time.”

The room stayed silent.

My mouth had gone dry. “Do I know you?”

A little laugh rippled through the diners. It didn’t feel friendly, or cruel. If anything, it felt...tired.

The woman poured coffee into a white mug without asking if I wanted any.

“You know us,” she nodded. “Even if you don’t remember us when you’re awake.”

I backed toward the door. “I’ve dreamed about this place.”

“Yes,” said a man in a booth by the window. “That’s how you kept visiting.”

The coffee smelled exactly like it did in the dreams. Burnt and bitter.

“This isn’t possible.”

The woman shrugged. “What does possible have to do with it?”

That should have been funny. Under better circumstances I might even have appreciated it. Instead I felt the room tilt.

“Who are you people?”

A chair scraped. The barber stood from a corner booth. He no longer had the towel around his neck, but it was him. Older, thinner, but unmistakably himself. His eyes sadder than I expected.

“We’re your town,” he said.

I stared at him.

He tried again, slower, like to a child. “We are where you were supposed to be.”

The room seemed to shrink around me.

“No,” I said. “I grew up in Marrowfield. I know where I grew up.”

A silence fell then, genuine and heavy.

The woman behind the counter looked at the barber. The barber looked at me.

Then the old man in the booth closest to the door spoke for the first time.

“Marrowfield where they done sent you. Where you get sent, boy, ain’t never the same thing as where you’re meant to be.”

Sent me.

I went cold, from the inside out.

The barber moved carefully, as if approaching a stray dog. “There was an accident,” he said. “A long time ago. Your mother was driving. It was winter. She skidded off Black Pine Road.”

I shook my head before he finished. My mother hated driving in winter. She told that story often. The ditch, the broken headlight, the miracle of walking away.

“She left,” he said. “You didn’t.”

Something in me lurched violently, like a hidden gear finally catching.

I heard a sound and realised it was me laughing. Small, breathless, ugly. “That’s insane.”

“Is it?” said the woman.

I looked around the diner and saw no pity in their faces. Only patience.

“No,” I said again, but the word had gone weak.

The old man by the door leaned forward. “Every night, you come home to us. And come morning, you get sent right along back. We thought that some time you’d remember enough to stay.”

“Stay where?”

“Home. Ashdown,” he said. “With us.”

The bell above the door jingled behind me though no one had entered. Cold air brushed the back of my neck.

I turned.

Outside, through the rain-streaked glass, I saw my car parked at the curb.

And in the driver’s seat, hands fixed on the wheel, head slumped slightly forward, was me.

Not exactly me as I stood now. Me as if seen through old glass. Paler. Slack-faced. Sleeping.

Or dead.

The whole diner had gone quiet.

I couldn’t breathe.

“That’s not real,” I whispered.

The woman’s voice softened. “That doesn’t make it false.”

I stumbled backward into a table. Cups rattled. Someone caught my elbow, steadying me with familiar kindness, and for one horrible second I recognised the touch.

My mother used to hold me like that.

I turned.

She stood beside me in a blue raincoat I had not seen since childhood, her hair damp around her face, her expression exhausted in the particular way only mothers can manage, as if love itself had worn grooves into her.

“I’m sorry,” she said.

Everything in me split open.

“No,” I said. Then louder: “No.”

Her hand tightened on my arm. It felt real. Warm. “I couldn’t let you go.”

I wanted to scream at her, to demand an explanation, to ask what kind of mother traps her child inside a life built over a grave. But under the fury was a truth I understood instantly and hated her for understanding too:

she had done exactly what mothers do.

She had chosen the child over reality.

Chosen my breath over my death.

Chosen delusion over burial.

And somehow, impossibly, she had made that choice stick.

“All these years,” I said.

Tears filled her eyes but never fell. “Every night you found your way back here. Every morning I sent you back before you could remember too much.”

“Why?”

“Because once you remember fully,” said the barber quietly, “the waking world won’t hold.”

Outside, the version of me in the car lifted its head.

It turned slowly toward the diner window.

Its eyes were open.

Empty.

Waiting.

My mother’s fingers trembled on my sleeve. “You have to choose now.”

Of course. Always a choice at the end, because the universe loves the illusion of choice.

I looked around Ashdown, at the people who knew me, at the town I had carried in my sleep for twenty-five years. At my mother, who had lied to death itself with such ferocity it had almost become truth.

I looked at the thing in the car wearing my face like a borrowed coat.

I understood, then, what the changed population sign meant.

Not that the town wanted one more resident.

That it had been counting me all along.

2,092.

Not arriving.

Returning.

The bell above the diner door rang again.

This time, when I turned, the road out of town was gone. There was only the rain dripping through the pines as dusk stretched its rotten gold light over Ashdown like a curtain refusing to fall.

My mother whispered, “I’m sorry.”

I believed her, but belief didn’t warm me.

The thing in the car smiled at me through the glass, and everyone in the diner looked away.
