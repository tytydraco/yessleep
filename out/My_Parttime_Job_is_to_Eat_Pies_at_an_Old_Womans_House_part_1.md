

I didn’t believe the ad when I saw it either. I thought it was a prank- something a family member set up to mess with their grandma, but no. It was legit. 

“PAY FOR PIES. $50 PER PIE FULLY EATEN.”

It requested that any interested party send a resume to a specific PO box in town and email the listed address to notify them of the drop-off. The ad was stapled to the front of the bulletin board at the edge of campus and didn’t have much in the way of showmanship. It had no fancy graphics or arrangements. It was all in Times New Roman, bolded, and very plain. I hadn’t seen it before that day, but even if it had been there, I doubt I would’ve paid it any mind. It was one of dozens of old event posters, club ads, and military recruitment posters. In a sea of infographics and colors, it was a lost piece of printer paper. However, to a starving college student, this was an offer too good to be true. 

I was strapped for cash and too ashamed to ask my folks for money, so I plucked the paper off the wall, sent in my resume, and emailed the listed address. It was AOL, if you can believe it, and within a day, I heard back from them. 

“Hello there!” it said. “Thank you for filling out the application. I’d like to see you in person for an interview as soon as you are able. When works best for you? “

I sent back my availability, set up a time, and before I knew it, I was traveling across town and down a quiet street to an unfamiliar address. The buildings where this person lived were small, but quaint in their own way. It wasn’t far from downtown, so everything had an old fifties feel to it, along with people in their fifties to match. It was a clean, quiet, and respectable neighborhood, and the place I pulled up to looked about as plain as a two-story house could be. It had a little thatched roof, a brick chimney, and a set of wooden stairs that led up to a red front door. The outside was painted a dry, sun-bleached yellow, and everything had an age to it only gained by existing in the elements for decades. 

I wasn’t the only person there, which I expected since I figured someone else would’ve looked into a fifty-dollar offer to eat some pies, but the vehicles I saw weren’t what I expected, nor were the people who walked out of the front door. 

As I slowly pulled up to the building and prepared to park, I saw a middle-aged man in clean business clothes rush professionally down the stairs and get into what looked to be a Road Runner. It was a surprising thing to see in a neighborhood like that, and way too nice a car to be anyone looking for a pie-tasting job, but as it pulled out of the driveway and revved down the street, my crappy little sedan took its place. I watched the car zoom down the street and out of sight before I mustered up enough courage to walk up to the front door. My nerves were interrupted on my way up the stairs as a soft, croaky voice said:  
“Hello, dearie.”

Waiting for me at the top of the stairs was an elderly woman in a flowery dress and a cardigan. She had a slouch to her posture, but moved as spryly as a young hiker. She approached me with an outstretched hand and, instead of shaking it, squeezed it kindly. 

“You must be Jason,” she said with a smile. “It’s wonderful to meet you, dear. I’m Constance, but you can just call me Mrs. Connie.”

“It’s very Nice to meet you, ma’am,” I told her. She chuckled sweetly and put a hand on my face.

“You poor thing, you’re too thin! We’ll soon fix that. Come in, Honey. Come, come- do you like tea? Coffee? I can’t have it myself because of my heart-”

She continued like that for a bit over tea and coffee. 

The inside of her house felt like something out of a Hallmark card. Flowery wallpaper, yellowed with age, adorned the walls, and throughout the narrow halls were pictures and memorabilia from decades past. She had wooden frames that held old pictures of her family.

She asked me some questions in between the ramblings. Basic things like where I grew up, what I was studying in college, and whether I went to church. I told her I didn’t really attend church, and something in her demeanor changed. She clinked her ring finger against her mug as she pried.

“Do you believe in evil, Jason?” She said it so calmly that it took me aback. I didn’t really know what to say, so I covered my ass as best I could. 

“I mean, I suppose. I’m not sure I’ve ever thought about evil. It’s just kinda…. I don’t know. It just is. I know it when I see it or hear about it, but-”

“What about sins?” she cut in again, a pleasant smile still on her lipstick-drawn mouth. “Do you believe in those?” 

“You're trying to get me to join the JWs, aren’t you?” I said, half joking. She laughed heartily at that and took a sip of her tea. 

“No, no, no, I’m not a JW,” she said, “But in my line of work it's typically an important factor when it comes to helping my clients.”

“What exactly do you do?” I asked. She looked off in thought for a long while before saying:

“I guess you could call me a counselor of sorts.”

“A counselor?” My mind went back to the yellow Road Runner and the man who’d driven it. “You’ve got some interesting clients then.”

I giggled at my own joke, but she only smiled and said: “Everyone has a guilty conscience about something, honey.” She added another sugar to her tea and stirred the glass meditatively. I don’t know why, but the clank of her spoon in the glass unnerved me. It was some untraceable feeling that lingered at the top of my spine and caused me to ever so slightly tremble. 

“Well, that’s a whole different conversation,” she said with a lilt. “You’re here for my other passion: feeding starving children such as yourself! Look at you, you’re rail thin! Come to the kitchen with me, and we’ll get this all sorted out. There’s one last part to the interview before I hire you…”

I followed behind her, half dazed from the lingering tingle in my back, but still hanging on her words. She was strange, to be sure, and I still didn’t understand the nature of my potential job, but I sat at the kitchen table nonetheless. It was a small room, and the table wasn’t any bigger than a nightstand, but it felt cozy. It honestly looked like something straight out of the 50s. The tablecloth was checkered white and red, and between a knife and fork sat a beautifully made pie with a golden crust. The scent of cherry wafted under my nose and made my mouth water. I hadn’t eaten anything that day.  

Connie pulled out a chair and basically shoved me into the chair. She poured me a glass of milk and patted me on the head like a kid. I would’ve been more weirded out if I hadn’t been so hungry and if she hadn’t been so elderly. 

“You get $50 for every pie you finish,” she told me. “When you’re done, you can just put the tin in the sink. I like to recycle those when I can.” 

My eyes were locked in on the pie. It was possibly the most perfect example of a pie I could think of. I know that’s weird, and I probably sound fat as hell, but sweet mercy… I started eating it immediately and with fewer manners than I was raised with. It was gone in less than five minutes, and the glass of milk went soon after. For the first time in a month, I finally felt full. It was a glorious feeling that I tried to bask in before falling into a sugar coma, but it was interrupted.

To the right of me, over maybe a yard and a half gap, was a door I hadn’t noticed before. It was white with missing chips running up and down its face. The handle was tarnished and nearly rusted from age. I asked Connie what it was, but she waved her hand and beckoned me away. 

“That’s just the basement, honey,” she told me. “There’s nothing down there but some cob webs and old boxes. Here, come lay down on the couch. You need some rest before you head on out. Here-” she shoved a fifty into my jacket pocket. “You earned that. Oh, here, dearie, lie down.”

I didn’t argue, and allowed her to lead me to the couch. I slept for a lot longer than I meant to. It was almost dark by the time I woke up. I was still full and wondered what on earth had made that pie so good. I’d had good pies before, but something about hers had made me near ravenous. I didn’t understand a bit of it, but I also didn't have the brain power to even try and tackle it. I saw a note on the coffee table. Scrawled across in the large letters of a woman losing her eyesight were the words:

“HAD TO RUN OUT. KEY UNDER MAT. PLEASE LOCK UP. COME BACK TOMORROW, AND WE’LL GIVE YOU ANOTHER. YOU DID SO GOOD! I LOVE A MAN WITH A HEALTHY APPETITE. YOU’RE HIRED!” 

I rubbed the large bill between my fingers and stared at the brilliant green numbers on its corners. It was too good to be true, but the money was real. Cherry lingered on my breath, and my stomach burbled with content. I got up to leave and almost walked straight out of the door, but something in the kitchen caught my eye. 

At the basement door, piled on the linoleum floor, were stacks of empty pie tins. They were dirty, covered in crumbs and fruit syrups, but noticeably fresh. I remembered the old lady saying she liked to reuse tins, so I figured that’s all it was. However, there was something else in one of the tins. Upon further investigation, there was one shiny, bright object nestled in the crumbs. I picked it up and wiped it off, but before I could get a good look at it, the front door opened. Connie called out. “You’re still here, Jason?”

“Yes, Ma’am!” I replied. “Sorry, I just woke up!” 

“Oh, it’s no worry at all, dear, “ she said, coming into the kitchen. “I figured you’d crash after that much sugar. I’ll need to make something more savory next- oh my goodness! I’m so sorry, I thought I cleaned those up!” She rushed past me and put the tins in the sink before I could see if anything else was in them. “I’m so sorry, but I’ll need to get these all cleaned before bed. Are you coming back tomorrow?”

I was hesitant, but still had the fifty in my fist. “Yes, Ma’am. I will, probably around three. My classes get out around then.”

She explained to me that she had occasional errands during the day, but she was going to leave a key under the mat for me. If I finished a pie when she wasn’t there, I’d just stack the tins on the counter, and when we’d eventually meet up, she’d pay me the full amount. I still didn’t and don’t understand why she was willing to pay for someone to eat her cooking, but I figured she was just lonely and didn’t have any actual grandkids to spoil. 

I felt a little bad taking the money, but I bid her goodnight all the same and shook on the arrangement. I was getting paid to eat. It was every college kid’s dream, and I got it. I was still riding the high of the whole agreement when I remembered the object from the pie tins. As I pulled up to my dorm complex and made my way inside, I finally got to inspect it. Imagine my confusion when I stared at a bright pink gel fingernail in the gleam of those sterile white building lights. White polkadots adorned its edges, almost disappearing with its sheen.

I almost dropped the damn thing and began chastising myself for being such a weirdo. I’d basically gone through an old woman’s trash and found one of her missing nails. It didn’t look like anything special, and I’d held onto it for whatever reason. As I ascended the stairs of my building and prepared myself for an overnighter for Chem midterms, by chance I glanced at the noticeboard and stopped in my tracks. 

There was an image of a girl, probably only a year or so older than me, smiling and throwing up a peace sign. On her fingers were the same shade of pink, lined with white polka dots. Below her smiling image, on this homemade poster, was the word “MISSING.” 

It said she’d disappeared about three weeks ago, and her family and friends were still trying to locate her.

I can’t tell if I’m overreacting or underreacting. On one hand, I don’t know if this is the exact same nail. Plenty of women probably had this pattern. I thought about asking Connie, but I really don’t want to jeopardize this gig by offending her. I mean, I’m still unsure what the implication of this is. It’s not like I ate a meat pie or anything, so there isn’t any Sweeney Todd shit going on, but still. What would I even tell the cops? I found a nail in a pie tin in a random woman’s house? 

As I’m typing out all of this, I started cross-searching this girl’s name online. I haven’t done a lick of Chem work, but I have found her Instagram. It was open to public viewing and had the same generic stuff you’d expect a regular college girl to have. Posts with friends making cute faces, funny pictures of her dog, all benign shit except for one. 

Five weeks ago, she made a post with some crappy inspirational quote about love and harmony, but that didn’t make me stop in my tracks. What made me tense and start to spin was what was in the photo with her. 

She was posing against a yellow Road Runner.
