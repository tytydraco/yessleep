At twenty-seven, I was completely burned out. The endless cycle of concrete, fluorescent office lights, and meaningless corporate emails had eroded something vital inside me. When my grandfather, Silas, passed away and left me his isolated cabin deep in the Oregon wilderness, I didn’t see it as a burden. I saw it as a lifeline. My family thought I was losing my mind when I packed my life into a few boxes, quit my job, and drove out into the deep, dark sea of pine and Douglas firs.

The cabin sat at the dead end of an unpaved logging road, miles away from the nearest trace of civilization. The first day was pure, exhausting, but therapeutic physical labor. The place had been empty for months, covered in a thick layer of dust and the sharp smell of old pine sap. I spent hours sweeping, clearing out cobwebs, and opening the heavy wooden shutters to let the crisp, winter air circulate.  
By the afternoon, the temperature dropped significantly. I found an old, heavy iron axe in the shed behind the cabin. For nearly two hours, I split thick logs of cedar and pine, the satisfying, rhythmic thud of the blade echoing through the dead silent forest. My hands blistered, and my muscles ached in a way they hadn't in years, but for the first time in a decade, my mind was quiet.  
Later, as the sun began to dip below the jagged tree line, painting the sky in bruises of purple and orange, I brewed a pot of thick, black coffee. I took a heavy ceramic mug and stepped out onto the front porch. The air was so cold I could see my own breath mixing with the steam from the coffee. Wrapping my hands around the warm mug, I sat on the old rocking chair and just listened. There was no highway drone, no sirens, no notifications. Just the wind sighing through the canopy and the absolute, beautiful weight of solitude. I felt completely safe.

It wasn't until that night, while cleaning the kitchen counter to prepare dinner, that I found it. Tucked beneath an old cast-iron skillet on the stove was a single sheet of yellowed notebook paper. It was my grandfather’s shaky, uneven handwriting.  
The note read: "If you are reading this, it means the cabin belongs to you now. You can live here for the rest of your days, completely free. But you must swear to follow one rule. If you ever hear rain hitting the roof on a night when the sky is completely clear, do not look out the windows. Do not peek through the blinds. Draw every curtain, turn off the lights, and stay in bed. The rain isn't water. And it desperately wants to see who is looking."  
I shook my head and laughed, tossing the paper into the kindling basket next to the stone fireplace. I knew Silas had become a bit of a recluse in his final years, and I dismissed the note as the lingering paranoia of an old man who spent too much time talking to the trees.

For the first two weeks, life was perfect. I established a peaceful routine. I woke up early, stoked the fire, drank my coffee on the porch, hiked through the woods, and read by the firelight at night. The isolation felt liberating, not terrifying.  
Until that Tuesday night.  
I was lying in bed, staring up at the massive glass skylight my grandfather had built directly into the sloped wooden ceiling. The night sky was breathtakingly beautiful. There wasn't a single cloud in sight. The moon was a sharp, brilliant silver crescent, and thousands of stars blanketed the dark void.  
Then, I heard it. A single, heavy, wet drop struck the glass directly above my head.  
Within thirty seconds, that single tap erupted into a violent, roaring downpour. It sounded like a massive thunderstorm was tearing through the valley, heavy sheets of water violently pelting the roof and the glass skylight.  
But as I looked up at the glass, my chest tightened, and a cold wave of pure adrenaline flooded my veins.  
The skylight was bone dry. There was no water running down the pane. No ripples, no splashes, no moisture. Through the crystal-clear glass, Orion’s Belt and the silver moon were still shining brightly in a completely cloudless, star-filled sky. Whatever was making that deafening noise, it wasn’t falling from the sky.

My grandfather's words flashed in my mind like a siren. The rain isn't water. It wants to see who is looking.  
Panic seized me. I tore off my blankets, threw myself out of bed, and rushed through the pitch-black cabin. Moving with a desperate, shaking speed, I pulled the heavy wool curtains shut over every single window, completely sealing myself inside the dark wooden box.  
The moment the last curtain clicked into place, the sound changed.  
The roaring downpour on the roof instantly ceased. The silence lasted for three agonizing heartbeats. Then, the sound reappeared, but it was no longer coming from above. It was against the outer wooden walls of the cabin.  
It sounded like thousands of heavy, wet, fleshy drops slamming against the logs. But it wasn't random. The sound was moving horizontally. It was circling the perimeter of the house, dragging itself along the wood in a slow, calculated rhythm, mimicking the cadence of a storm. Like a massive, unseen, wet entity was crawling around my walls, looking for a gap.

Then, it reached the heavy oak front door. A thick, wet, sickening impact shook the wood.  
I fell to my knees in the center of the living room, blindly grabbing a heavy iron poker from the fireplace, gripping it until my knuckles turned white. I checked my phone. No service. Just a dead screen. The only thing alive in that forest was the rhythmic, wet slapping against the door.  
And then, the voice came.  
It didn't sound like a throat with vocal cords. It sounded like something trying to speak through a throat filled with stagnant water—a wet, bubbling, suffocating whisper that vibrated through the wood of the door. And the worst part was the cadence. It was trying to sound like my grandfather.  
"It's pouring out here" the voice gurgled, a disgusting wet hiss spraying through the small gap beneath the doorframe. "I forgot my keys in the truck. It's so cold out here. Please, just open the door. Let me in."  
I stared down at the floorboards in horror. A thick, dark, viscous fluid was slowly oozing under the door. In the dim light of the dying fireplace embers, it didn't look like rainwater. It looked like ancient, decomposed mud, and the heavy scent of copper and rotting iron immediately filled the small cabin, making me gag.  
I froze. I didn't move. I barely allowed myself to breathe. I knew with absolute certainty that if I made a single sound, if I whimpered or took a loud breath, whatever was on the other side would know I was right there.

The gurgling thing disguised as my grandfather kept weeping, begging, and scratching at the door for two straight hours. It cycled through different voices—my mother’s voice, my own voice, and then back to Silas—always asking me to just take a peek outside.  
When the very first pale rays of dawn finally pierced through the Oregon trees, the scratching stopped. The wet, heavy sounds slowly recated, trailing away into the dark woods until there was only silence.  
It has been three days since that night. I haven't stepped foot outside. I tried scrubbing the dark stain under the front door, but the wood is ruined; it smells permanently of copper and rot.  
The weather forecast on my battery-powered radio says tonight is going to be completely clear. Not a single cloud in the state. The sun is setting now, casting long, bloody shadows across the room. And as the darkness rolls in, I can already hear the first few wet drops gently tapping against the back wall.

Part 2 

The tapping on the back wall didn't stay there for long. Within minutes, it multiplied, spreading across the logs like a thousand crawling insects. The battery-powered radio on the kitchen counter gave one loud, high-pitched shriek of static before dying completely. The shadows in the cabin seemed to stretch, closing in on me as the last remnants of twilight vanished from under the doorframe.  
The copper smell returned, but this time it was suffocating. It coated the back of my throat, thick and metallic, making every breath a struggle. I retreated to the farthest corner of the kitchen, clutching the iron fireplace poker with both hands, my knuckles white and aching. I refused to look toward the windows, keeping my eyes glued to the floorboards.  
That was when the liquid under the front door began to change. It was no longer just slowly seeping in. It was pooling, expanding across the linoleum like a living, breathing entity. In the dim glow of the dying embers, I could see the surface of the black mud bubbling, rippling in sync with the wet slaps outside.  
Then, the noise above me shifted. The roaring rain concentrated entirely onto the glass skylight directly above my bed. It didn't sound like drops anymore. It sounded like heavy, wet fists violently pounding on the glass, desperate to break through.  
A sharp, deafening crack echoed through the bedroom.  
My stomach dropped. I risked a glance upward. A thin, spiderweb fracture had appeared right in the center of the skylight. Through the crack, a single, thick drop of that dark fluid fell, landing on the wooden floor with a sickening hiss, burning into the wood like acid.

The rain isn't water. It wants to see who is looking. If the glass shattered, I would be forced to look. It would be standing directly above me, looking down. Desperation took over. I pulled off my flannel shirt, tore it into thick strips with my teeth and hands, and tied it tightly around my eyes. Total darkness. I couldn't see anything, but my hearing became agonizingly, terrifyingly sharp.  
The glass shattered. A heavy shower of sharp fragments and thick, viscous mud rained down into the bedroom. But it didn't just splash. I heard the wet, heavy sliding sound of something massive dropping from the roof onto my bed. The wooden beams of the cabin groaned under an immense weight. Whatever it was, it was now inside the house.  
It slid down from the bed, dragging its wet, heavy mass across the floorboards. It was moving toward the kitchen. Toward me.  
Then, the voice came. It was right there in the room, just a few feet away from my face. It shifted between voices rapidly—my grandfather’s voice, then my mother’s, and finally, my own voice.  
"Look at me," my own voice gurgled, wet liquid dripping onto the kitchen floor. "Just one look. Open your eyes. The storm is over."  
I pressed myself deeper into the small pantry closet beneath the kitchen stairs, pulling the door shut. I bit down on my own hand, breaking the skin, doing anything to keep from screaming as the wet, heavy dragging sound circled right outside the closet door all night.

When the morning birds finally began to chirp outside, the heavy breathing stopped. The suffocating weight in the air evaporated. I slowly untied the blindfold.  
The cabin was in ruins, covered in black, foul-smelling mud, but it was empty. I didn’t care about packing. I grabbed my car keys from the counter, threw open the front door, and sprinted out into the morning sunlight, desperate to escape this living nightmare.  
I ran to my car, my heart pounding with a sudden surge of hope. I reached for the door handle, ready to jump in and slam the gas, but as I looked down, my hands froze. My entire body went numb.  
All four tires were completely flat. They weren't just deflated; they looked shredded, sliced open from the rims, sinking deeply into the thick, black mud that now blanketed the entire driveway.  
I stood there in the middle of the bright morning, paralyzed, staring at my only escape route completely destroyed. The sun was fully up, shining brightly through the pine trees. It was a beautiful, clear, cloudless morning.  
And then, I heard it.  
A single, heavy, wet drop struck the center of my car's windshield. Followed by another on the hood.  
It wasn't waiting for the night anymore.
