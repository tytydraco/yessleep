I'm a psychiatrist used to seeing everything in my office. Almost everything has an explanation, and I find it. But what I lived through is something I cannot explain through medicine or psychiatry. It all began a few years ago, when I had a patient in consultation with typical post-traumatic stress disorder. He relived an accident he'd had some years before, over and over. In time we made progress and he came to understand that the memories and the dreams were just that — dreams.

There was a period when I stopped seeing him. He quit coming to my office, which tends to happen when a patient improves. It's a reminder that I help more than I charge. Then one day this patient came back. He had never stopped having recurring dreams that tormented him daily about his accident. Even so, he had returned to tell me his dream once more. Irritating, but it's my job.

We sat in my office, facing each other in two very comfortable chairs I've had for a long time. I have no secretary, so he didn't need to be announced. I had the slot open in my schedule and even though he showed up unannounced I had to see him. The poor man looked different.

"Doctor," my patient began, "I dreamed the same thing as every night. I'm leaving my house as usual, heading to work, walking a little distracted, watching the clouds in this hypnotic way. In the dream I'm in no rush at all, and then I simply feel a blow from the side. A car runs me over and I wake up completely frightened. But it's been strange lately."

"Why have you felt different?" I asked, with some curiosity.

"It's been different lately because…" He paused for a moment. "I've noticed that my chest doesn't tighten and I don't wake up sweating. And honestly… nothing like that has ever happened to me since I started having this dream every night. With the therapy I've been losing the fear and anxiety, but it's not true that I feel sweaty or my heart in my throat."

"That's good news," I said with genuine enthusiasm. "Post-traumatic stress brings many symptoms, and the fact that you're overcoming them is something to be glad about."

Before answering he took a few seconds. I watched with quiet unease the tangle forming in my patient's mind. It's common to see patients who debate whether healing is good or bad for their lives — many times they turn their symptoms into part of their identity. But this man had a different attitude. He was genuinely worried about not feeling the way he was supposed to feel. And then he said something that left me cold.

"Doctor," he said with frank drama, leaning forward in his chair, practically folding himself toward me, "I have never woken up sweating or agitated with a tight chest."

I raised an eyebrow and leaned back slightly. He saw my eyes open a little wider than normal and then he said: "Doctor, I have no pulse. I can't hear myself breathing. Doctor — I'm not alive."

I opened my eyes even wider and with a half-formed smile grabbed him by the shoulders and eased him back into his chair. He felt cold. Then I stood and moved to my desk, inviting him to sit across from me. This served a dual purpose: establish distance and bring the session to a close.

"I understand what you've been through," I said with the weight of seriousness I'd built over the years. "Dreams press hard on our psyche and tend to distort our reality when they're this repetitive. I want you to hold on to reality, to see the importance of your symptoms receding. Hold on to that."

He stared at me with a furrowed brow and narrowed, almost pleading eyes, and scratched his head nervously. He was genuinely frightened — it wasn't the first time we'd arrived at a feeling like this. Cotard's syndrome is very rare to develop, and rarer still for it to appear following post-traumatic stress disorder. It's simply not common. At all.

His face changed in an instant, as if he'd reached some conclusion, and he stood up. He shook my hand — it felt a little strange — and I didn't try to stop him. I'd see him again soon. I offered to increase the dose of his medication, but he indicated that he was indeed improving, and disappeared through the door.

That same night, before going to sleep, I looked at my hand. I'm a man of slender build, pale, quite tall, so seeing my hand pale has never surprised me. I've considered I might have Marfan syndrome or some connective tissue disorder. That night my thin fingers weren't what caught my attention — it was my wrist. I felt the urge to check my own pulse. I made a fist and closed my eyes. This patient wasn't going to take root in my head. The crazy one isn't me.

Over the following days I tried to carry on with my normal life, going to work as if nothing had changed. But I was aware that, in truth, everything had changed. One day I felt much more energetic — I didn't even remember the urge to smoke. I'd been reaching for a cigarette every morning for years. But that day was different. I hadn't smoked. I hadn't eaten breakfast either. And then it hit me. I looked at my trembling hand — pale, as if not a drop of blood lay beneath the skin. I had to do it. I searched for my pulse while holding my breath, fingers in the right place. Seconds passed and I felt nothing. Yet I still wasn't breathing. Minutes passed and I felt no need for air. And then I woke up.

I breathed deep. It was a relief to feel myself damp from sweat. I touched my carotids — on each side they jumped, announcing vitality. I was alive. It was 3 a.m. I stayed in bed a few more hours, and when the urge to urinate hit me I stood up too fast. My temples began to pound, my chest wouldn't stop tightening, everything started to spin. A few minutes later I got up from the floor slowly. I'd fainted — I told myself I shouldn't have stood up so quickly. I'm getting old. I drank some water and went straight to the mirror. As I held onto the sink and rubbed my face, recognizing myself, I noticed something behind me. Someone. A tall shadow passing quickly across the hallway. In the back of my mind: it's a spirit, it's a demon, or worse — it's nothing, and I'm losing my mind. I decided not to follow it. I went back to bed and waited for the alarm.

At 7 a.m. I got dressed and went to work. This time I made sure I wasn't dreaming by reading my schedule — if I'd been asleep I wouldn't be able to read — so I began my day without changes. Again there was a two-hour gap in my schedule, something that happens during the week but unusual for that long a block. I usually leave myself small gaps of barely ten or twenty minutes. Never two hours. I felt my heart in my throat.

As time passed I grew more anxious, waiting for that patient from a few weeks ago to show up. I needed to check his pulse, to feel that he was breathing. I couldn't keep feeling like I was losing my mind over nothing. Then the hour arrived. I stood up from my desk and tried to distract myself at the window — in truth I was watching who entered the building. Then I saw something very unusual. Crowds of people crossing the avenue, almost a mass of bodies — women, men, children — not a single vehicle in sight. I couldn't understand what was happening, so I sat back down and tried to distract myself with something else. I forced myself to look at my notes. My hands felt heavy, unresponsive. I began to notice more light coming from the avenue, and then someone came through the door. Without making a sound.

It wasn't my former patient. I definitely didn't know this person. A woman of incalculable age, older than I was, crossed the room without introducing herself and immediately began to speak. "Doctor, thank you for seeing me. I need your help." The word *seeing* echoed in my head, since she had let herself in without asking.

"I feel like something's wrong," the old woman continued. "My children haven't spoken to me in a long time. They're abandoning me — they took all my things from the house, left me with only what I had on, and then simply left me alone. I haven't eaten anything in days. I haven't slept a single night. I don't understand why they did this to me. I came because I can't remember their names, or even my own. I think I was called María, or Ana, or Clementina — but I can't remember. Give me something for the memory, for the dementia. I need it."

I genuinely tried to diagnose her. The deterioration was apparent. I looked at her steadily and offered her water, hurried to pour it into a paper cup, then approached and took her hand, trying to help her sit. She was cold. She looked haggard, on the verge of tears. I began taking in the details — her skeletal face, her thin body — and the more I looked the louder one thought grew: *was I looking at a ghost?* I sat back down and instinctively searched for my own pulse. Felt nothing. I reached for my notes and could see my schedule. I was reading it perfectly: the appointment listed was "María or Ana or Clementina" — literally what she had just told me. I would say I felt a chill, but I felt absolutely nothing. I looked her directly in the eyes. She began to cry. No tears fell.

"When were you born?" I asked with care. She looked at me and stifled a sob with her hands. She couldn't remember. That was clear. I didn't want to ask her anything more. I placed my hands face-up on the desk, as if asking her to give me hers. I held them — outwardly offering emotional support, but in truth searching for her pulse. I found nothing. She was dead.

"Ma'am… uh…" I tried to say her name, but neither she nor I knew it. "I want to help you. I'll speak with your children. Tell me where you live." Her eyes went first to her right, searching for an answer, then to her left, trying to give herself an explanation. She couldn't remember. She kept sobbing without saying another word, tried to speak, but couldn't produce a sound. I stopped asking. She was locked in something serious. If I had been in my right mind I would have called an ambulance immediately — a woman with no pulse, no memory — not calling for help would be absolute medical negligence. I have no excuse. I was genuinely disconnected from reality. I was losing my mind.

While I sat lost in my thoughts I didn't notice what was actually happening. When I looked back at her, what I saw made no sense. I'll try to describe it as best I can: she had become a coil of shadows. If I looked at her from the corner of my eye she was still the old woman, but looking directly at her was unsettling — she had a frequency that was uncomfortable to see. As if something pulled her toward the door she'd come through, she vanished. A moment later I stood quickly and when I reached the door she was gone. Suddenly I felt the full weight of my body. My chest was tightening again and my temples ached.

While I was unconscious I dreamed something very brief. I was standing in front of the bathroom mirror, rubbing my face. Again a shadow on my back, crossing the hallway. This time I followed it. Someone was standing upright at the end of the hall. A tall man, very well dressed — the kind of elegance that accumulates over centuries. Dark suit, vest, tie. He didn't need to say his name. In fact he said very little, in a voice that resonated in bones rather than ears. He said something about the mind, the soul, the ego. Something about the memory that holds them. Then he crossed the hallway in an instant. I couldn't move. He left a pocket watch inside my jacket and disappeared.

I woke up with the sharp pain of a headache. I had truly fainted and hit my head. But I pulled myself together well enough, checked the clock on the wall — not much time had passed since morning — and a reasonably ordinary patient would be arriving soon. There was no trace of the old woman in my schedule. I couldn't remember her name, whether it started with M or A. Nothing in my notes about any old woman. There had never really been a two-hour gap in my schedule at all. I considered whether I'd been dreaming, looked out the window — no crowd. The recent dream was a vivid memory. I reached for the watch. There it was. Cold, heavy against my lapel as if made of lead. I opened it: the hands weren't marking the hours. They moved slowly backward, counting the exact minutes remaining until the next patient would cross my door.

I'm writing this because I know there are more of them out there. If you know someone who has stopped eating not from lack of hunger but from forgetting; if you have a family member who insists their pulse has stopped, or that they can hear the weeping of a funeral no one else hears — don't take them to a regular hospital. They don't need medicine. They need a diagnosis that will let them rest. My schedule has an opening today at three in the afternoon. I'll be waiting. After all, the ego is the last thing to die — and I'm the only one who knows how to help them say goodbye.
