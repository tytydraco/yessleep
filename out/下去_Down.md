Zhang Bianlu looked at me. He stepped back and said, low: "Go. Now. Go back. Get out of here."

"Are you serious?" I was getting mad. "You ask me to come, and the second I get here you tell me to leave?"

A middle-aged woman with a white mourning cloth tied around her head came over, smiling, carrying a bowl of noodle soup. She set it in front of me. "Xiao Lu — what's wrong with you? Your friend just got here and you're already kicking him out? Eat first. I'll deal with this kid."

She pushed Zhang Bianlu away. He looked back at me, frowning, trying to signal something with his eyes.

I didn't care. I ate the noodles.

Drank the last of the broth and stood up, looking around the courtyard. People everywhere, all busy. The kitchen was separate from the main house. People with mourning cloths moved between them, between the front gate and the main house. Everyone in a hurry — but no one was carrying anything.

What were they so busy with?

I saw Zhang Bianlu walking toward me. I was about to get in his face and ask him what the hell — when he missed a step at the main house threshold.

He fell hard.

And then I watched him get pulled. Like something was dragging him — *down*.

Whoosh.

I snapped awake. Outside the window: blue hour before dawn. Pretty. I didn't care. Got up, went to the bathroom, came back to bed. Work in a few hours.

Exhausted. Feels like I didn't sleep at all last night.

I got to work this morning barely conscious. The new coworker handed me a coffee — thanked me for teaching him stuff. Then he handed me a courier envelope.

It's been a little over two months since I came back from Zhang Bianlu's hometown. I still don't know why he kicked me out that day. And he never came back to work. My manager had me hand his tasks over to the new guy.

I opened the envelope.

It was a letter from Zhang Bianlu's mom. It said: He's been unwell for over a week now — bedridden. Could I come see him? Tucked into the envelope was also the old diary, cover half-rotted away.

First page: clear as day. Thirteenth day of the third month, 1996.

What did it say? I can't quite remember now. I'll ask for another day off tomorrow and go see him. We haven't talked in two months.

I'm so tired. Gotta sleep.

Someone knocked on the door.

I went to open it.

A friend. Which friend? I can't remember. Doesn't matter — it's late, he's crashing here. We'll share the bed. Talk tomorrow.

Stop pulling my blanket —

I sat up.

I remember now. Zhang Bianlu told me about this.

I watched the friend get pulled away.
