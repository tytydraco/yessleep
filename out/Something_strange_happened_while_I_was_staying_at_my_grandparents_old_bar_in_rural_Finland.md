Hey Reddit.  
This is my first time posting here, and English is not my first language, so sorry if my writing isn’t perfect.  
A few years ago I stayed with my grandparents in a small town in eastern Finland. The town is tiny. One grocery store, one church, lots of forest. The kind of place where everyone knows everyone.  
Their house is attached to an old bar called Heikin Baari, named after my grandfather Heikki. He owned it for decades before closing it down years ago.  
The strange thing is that he never really stopped taking care of it.  
Every evening he still goes downstairs, wipes the counter, checks the tables, and locks the front door before coming back upstairs. I always assumed it was just routine. Something old people do when they’ve spent half their lives in the same place.  
I arrived during a snowstorm.  
The drive there was miserable. Snow everywhere, terrible visibility, and roads that looked like they disappeared into the forest.  
By the time I arrived I was exhausted.  
That first night I woke up around one in the morning.  
I heard voices downstairs.  
At first I thought it was the television.  
Then I realized it sounded more like a group of people talking.  
Nothing dramatic. No screaming. Just normal conversation.  
A few laughs.  
A chair moving.  
The sound of glasses touching.  
Exactly the kind of noise you’d hear in a small local bar.  
I got up and listened for a minute.  
Then I heard someone say:  
“Is Heikki still working tonight?”  
That made me pause because that’s my grandfather’s name.  
The second I opened my bedroom door, everything stopped.  
Completely.  
No voices.  
No movement.  
Nothing.  
I remember standing there for a while trying to decide whether I’d actually heard anything.  
Eventually I went back to bed.  
The next morning I checked the bar.  
It was empty.  
Dusty.  
Cold.  
Exactly what you’d expect.  
But there was a smell in the room.  
Beer.  
Old cigarette smoke.  
Wet winter jackets.  
It wasn’t strong, but it felt fresh somehow.  
What really bothered me was a set of wet footprints behind the counter.  
Not muddy.  
Just wet.  
Like somebody had recently walked in from the snow.  
I followed them across the floor until they stopped near the old jukebox.  
When I touched the jukebox, it was warm.  
Not hot.  
Just warm enough to notice.  
I told myself there had to be a reasonable explanation.  
Old electrical equipment maybe.  
Heat from somewhere.  
Anything.  
Later I mentioned it to my grandmother.  
She immediately stopped smiling.  
Then she asked:

“You heard them already, didn’t you?”

I asked who she meant.  
She didn’t answer directly.  
Instead she said something that stuck with me.  
She said that during bad winters, people used to get stranded on those roads.  
Especially in the 80s and 90s.  
The bar was often the only place nearby with lights on late at night.  
A lot of people stopped there during storms.  
Some never made it home afterward.  
I thought she was just telling old local stories.  
Then, a couple nights later, I heard the voices again.  
This time I went downstairs.  
The bar itself was dark.  
But I could have sworn I heard people talking inside.  
Not loud.  
Just enough to hear.  
I stood outside the door for maybe thirty seconds.  
The conversations stopped.  
Then I heard someone cough.  
A deep, rough cough from inside the room.  
I opened the door immediately.  
Nobody was there.  
The room was empty.  
The thing that finally got to me happened on my last night.  
There was another snowstorm outside.  
The wind was hitting the building so hard it rattled the windows.  
I couldn’t sleep, so I went downstairs to get water.  
When I passed the old bar entrance, I noticed a light coming through the front windows.  
Not bright.  
Just a faint yellow glow.  
For a second I thought my grandfather had left a light on.  
When I looked through the glass, I saw him standing behind the counter.  
He wasn’t doing anything unusual.  
Just standing there.  
Looking toward the tables.  
Like he was waiting for customers.  
I almost opened the door.  
Then I noticed something.  
He wasn’t alone.  
There were shapes sitting at a few of the tables.  
Not clear enough to make out faces.  
Just dark figures.  
Maybe coats hanging over chairs.  
Maybe shadows.  
Maybe people.  
I honestly don’t know.  
Then my grandfather turned his head toward me.  
I stepped back instinctively.  
A second later the lights were gone.  
The room was completely dark again.  
The next morning I told him what I’d seen.  
He didn’t laugh.  
He didn’t seem surprised either.  
He just sat quietly for a while before saying:

“Some people never got to leave properly.”

That was all.  
When I left later that day, I looked back at the building one last time.  
My grandfather was standing in the front window of the bar.  
Watching me leave.  
I waved.  
He waved back.  
But for a moment I thought I saw someone standing beside him.  
Just inside the darkness of the room.  
Waiting.  
Maybe it was a reflection.  
Maybe it wasn’t.  
I still don’t know.  
What bothers me most isn’t what I saw.  
It’s what my grandmother said before I left.  
She told me that my grandfather still opens the bar every evening because he doesn’t want anyone arriving from the cold to find the door locked.  
I laughed when she said it.  
She didn’t.

One last thing before I end this post.

A few years have passed since all of this happened.

My grandfather passed away last winter.

My grandmother couldn’t stay in that town alone after that, so she sold the house and the old bar. She lives much closer to me now in the city, and honestly she seems happier there.

As far as I know, Heikin Baari was bought by a young couple from another part of Finland. They renovated most of the building and reopened it under a different name.

I haven’t been back since.

A few months ago, though, I was talking to my grandmother about the place. I asked her if she thought my grandfather really believed what he used to say about people arriving from the cold.

She was quiet for a long moment before answering.

Then she said:

“Your grandfather never believed they were ghosts.”

I asked her what he thought they were.

She looked out the window and smiled sadly.

“Customers.”

That was the end of the conversation.

Sometimes, especially during heavy snowstorms, I still think about Heikin Baari and the people my grandfather might have been waiting for all those years.

The strange thing is that after he died, nobody in town ever reported seeing lights in the bar at night again.

At least not until the new owners moved in.

A few months after they reopened, I saw a review online from someone passing through during a winter storm. It was mostly positive: good food, friendly service.

But one sentence stuck with me.

“Funny old place. When we arrived after midnight, the owner asked if we were the first customers of the night. We weren’t. There were already a few people sitting quietly in the corner, but when I looked again, the tables were empty.”

I’ve never told the new owners any of this.

And I don’t think I ever will.
