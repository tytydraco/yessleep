Last spring, my parents got new neighbors. 



Roger, who had been next door since before I was born, was put in a nursing home just down the hill after it became apparent that the progression of his dementia made him forget to eat. The in-home nursing staff came by once a day to heat him a standard issue pre-cooked meal that they brought with them, and then they left before making sure that he actually ate it. When the other neighbors noticed the way his clothes started to sag, one of them contacted his son on Facebook to let him know what was going on. Apparently, he didn't visit much. 



Roger used to be a kind but quiet man who loved to poke around in the garden, and prior to his illness, he always took pride in keeping it in pristine condition. Hedges trimmed, grass mowed every other day during the warmer months and neat flowerbeds dutifully watered. The son paid someone to come do it when he could no longer manage it himself, but only when it got absolutely necessary. The flowers withered and died within a few months.



I was visiting my parents when Rogers son, accompanied by staff from the nursing home, came to take him away. We were sitting on the front porch in the uncharacteristically warm april sun when they came dragging him out of the house, him trying to claw his way back inside all while screaming an incoherent stream of words such as no, ruin it, too small, get out. His son was on the phone, seemingly more upset about something work related than his fathers desperate attempt at pawing himself out of hell.



When the son came by a few days later with what appeared to be a moving company, my mom called out to him from over the fence. She asked how Roger was doing and what was going to happen next, and he told her that Roger still had a hard time settling into his new surroundings and that the house had been sold before the realtor even had a chance to schedule an open house. Someone had simply seen the ad on their website no more than a day after it was posted and called to say that they were interested in buying it. 

When my mom asked him about the buyer, he said that he'd never met them. They had sent someone to sign the papers for them, and the son was notified that this person was also going to be picking up the keys for them on the move in-day. All he knew were their names and that they were brothers, both in their forties.



Then, no more than two weeks after Roger moved out, there were suddenly three cars crammed into the driveway. Two more were parked on the street alongside the fence surrounding the yard at all times. No one had seen either the brothers or any moving trucks coming or going, and yet, they seemed to be moved in and housing a few guests as well. From the street you could see some simple furniture and a single light bulb hanging from the ceiling in the kitchen. The wall dividing it from the living room had been knocked down, making the ground floor mainly one big, open area. Nothing had been done to clean up afterwards, crude edges still visible where the wall had once been. Eventually, the walls on the upper floor were knocked down as well, leaving the house in a constant state of remodeling that remained for the rest of its existence.



Things then got progressively weirder as time went on. On several occasions, my parents heard multiple dogs howling and barking, sometimes for hours on end. Suspecting that the dogs were being neglected and left on their own for longer than what would be considered reasonable, my mom went over to peek through the windows one day and not only did the ground floor at least appear to be completely empty, there were no signs of any dogs living there. No bowls in the kitchen, no toys, no nothing.



Another time, my dad woke up in the middle of the night to the sound of a rager going on next door. The music was loud enough to wake the devil and when he poked his head out in driveway to see what was going on, he said that a loud thumping was coming from the house, as if a crowd of people were all jumping in sync but in a rythm completely different to the one from the music. Not a single light was on in the house and when the cops arrived to break up whatever black out-type of party they had going on, the music was abruptly cut off but no one answered the door. No movement inside, no beer cans in the kitchen, no cigarette butts in the driveway.



Roger ran away from the nursing home a few times and each one he would be found silently sobbing and moving around the backyard of his old house, heartbroken again and again by the dead flowers and the dried up guppy pond. The last time my mom was the one who found him there late at night, and so she calmly went up to him and ushered him towards the driveway where they would wait for the cops to come get him. As they were passing the front door, he shied away from it and told her that he couldn't go in there, that he wouldn't fit. He kept throwing anxious glances over his shoulder towards the dark and silent house while they sat out front and waited.



Naturally the house scared the shit out of me, so when my parents asked me to house-and-dog-sit for a week back in february I very reluctantly agreed. The brothers and whatever guests they had still shone with their absence and it had become the new normal, the occasional sounds coming from the house had become more of a nuisance rather than a creepy occurence to my parents and the rest of the street.



I slept with the lights on for the entire week and whenever I accompanied the dog on his late night patrol of the backyard I made sure not to look towards the house for too long, scared to spot the vague outline of someone standing in any of the windows or find Roger crying in the snow. The dog would stop and growl sometimes as he passed the hedge dividing the two backyards, to which I replied cowardly by quickly going back inside and calling for him through gritted teeth. 



One night, I heard laughter coming from the house. The kind of laughter you'd hear passing by somewhere there's a big birthday celebration going on, only ten times louder. I didn't even bother joining the dog outside that time, knowing that finding pitch black windows staring back at me yet again would only freak me out further.



The last night can only be described as an infernal fever deam. At around 2 A.M I woke up to the dog barking, his hackles raised, the smell of smoke assaulting my senses. Any illusion I'd ever had of me acting pragmatic in a state of genuine crisis went out the window as I grabbed the 40 pound dog with both arms and ran outside wearing nothing but the clothes I slept in and my dads slippers. I ran toward the couple next door and it wasn't until my fists were banging on their door that I noticed the screams and the heat burning at my back. 



Black smoke and flames billowed out of Rogers old house, accompanied by the screaming of what must have been a hundred different people. The fire and the panicked voices seemed to be roaring for dominance as I stood there, holding the shaking dog by the collar, now joined by the elderly couple who stood beside me with their hands covering their mouths. The street was swarming with firefighters in just a few minutes and curious people from neighboring streets came over to take in the spectacle. A teenager aimed his phone toward the house with an awestruck look on his face as his mother stood with her hands on his shoulders and shouted to no one in particular, 



"Jesus, why isn't anyone coming out?"



But we knew why. It had been nearly a year of no one coming or going, of sounds that in no way should be pouring out of a house so desolate and yet apparently so filled with invisible life.



We were all left scratching our heads when we found out that not a single body had been found in the rubble, and for some reason, Roger never came back.
