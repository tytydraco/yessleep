The day began like any other, which is to say, without warning. I had no idea what the hell was going on— One moment I was somewhere else, the next I found myself sitting in the back of a police car, staring out through smudged glass, trying to make sense of it. I wasn’t handcuffed. That seemed important. If I wasn’t restrained, then I wasn’t under arrest—at least, that’s what I told myself. No one spoke during the ride. After several long minutes, we arrived at a facility I didn’t recognize. Before stepping out, I gathered my belongings—a pistol, and a briefcase heavy enough to suggest it carried more than paper. Inside, they handed me forms to fill out, asked for my name, and the name of the officer who had brought me in. When they couldn’t find him in the crowd, they exchanged a glance and waved it off. *We’ll handle it,* they said. They always do. I was led straight back out again.

A black car was waiting this time, its windows tinted to opacity. Two men in suits sat in the front, their eyes hidden behind dark lenses. One of them handed me a portfolio. In the corner, stamped in clean, unforgiving letters, was a single word: CONFIDENTIAL.

That was the moment things began to settle into place. I wasn’t a suspect. I was… a detective?

They gave me instructions, precise and minimal: *Enter the Mumbai Hotel. Take the stairs to the fourth floor. Room 242. Open the case once inside* I simply nodded, there was nothing else to do., and we continued the ride in silence.

About twenty minutes later, the car slowed beside a vacant lot. An abandoned cinema loomed to one side, its façade peeling like old skin. Next to it stood the Mumbai Hotel—if it could still be called that. Its faded salmon exterior and bulbous domes gave it the silhouette of a decaying Taj Mahal. One of the men opened the door for me. “Good luck,” one of them said. I didn’t yet know what I was investigating. A murder? A disappearance? Something worse? Was I even working with the police? The car pulled away before I could decide.

I lingered at the entrance, watching until the vehicle vanished. Then I turned my attention to the surroundings—the empty lot, the dead cinema, a small Chinese shop tucked into the corner like an afterthought. I stepped inside and asked if they had seen anything unusual. The owner didn’t speak English. So much for that. I returned to the hotel. Its windows were so clouded with grime that the interior might as well not have existed. After a moment’s hesitation, I stepped inside. And everything changed.

The lobby was nothing like the exterior. Soft pastel colors bloomed across the walls. Art nouveau furniture curved elegantly beneath towering paintings. Gold accents shimmered in the light, catching my eye no matter where I looked. It was excessive, but deliberate—like a stage set designed to convince you of something you couldn’t quite name.

At the front desk, a man in a green suit slept with his head tilted back, mouth slightly open. I let him be. The stairs called to me. I went around him and took the key from the wall behind him, it felt heavier than it should have. Old, too—its metal worn smooth by time. A small tag hung from it, engraved with the number: **242**

The climb was… strange. It didn’t feel like four floors. It barely felt like anything at all—just a handful of steps, as if distance had been quietly folded in on itself. The air grew different as I ascended. Thicker. Dreamlike. Like walking through someone else’s memory.

At the door, I hesitated. I wasn’t alone. Figures lingered in the hallway, watching. Silent. Waiting. “Hello,” I said. Some smiled—too eagerly. Others withdrew, doors closing with soft, final clicks. I slid the key into the lock. It wouldn’t turn. I pushed, twisted, forced it— “Don’t turn it,” a voice said behind me. “Just open it.” I froze, then did as instructed. The door gave way.

The room inside was… underwhelming. Neither clean nor dirty. Neither expensive nor cheap. It was balanced in a way that felt intentional, almost unsettling. A small kitchen sat tucked into one corner. The bed was plain. Everything was… *acceptable.* Too acceptable. I dropped my belongings onto the bed and stepped back into the hallway. “I was expecting something better,” I muttered.

The tenants were no help. I knocked on door after door, asking questions, searching for anything useful. Instead, I got fragments: “No one asks for that room.” “I’ve lived here for years. Never seen anyone go in. Or out.” One couple lingered longer than the rest. I joked about the place being strange. One of them laughed. The other didn’t. “This is a refuge,” they said. “For people like us. You’ll find everything here—killers, people with abilities, those who’ve lived far longer than they should. Even the occasional celebrity.” I studied their faces. “Strange,” I said. “You noticed.” Murderers? Is that why I’m here? How is it that I don’t remember? I thanked my hosts for their help and left. 

Back at my door, something finally clicked. The building was wrong. From the outside, it looked like there were only two floors. Inside, I was standing on the fourth. The spacing between rooms didn’t make sense. The walls bent at odd angles. How had I missed it before? *Some detective.* I went back inside.

And everything had changed.The room was no longer ordinary. Gold gleamed from every surface. Victorian furniture lined the walls. Silk cascaded from an enormous bed like a waterfall. A jacuzzi sat in the center, absurd and opulent. Beyond it, towering windows revealed a breathtaking view. I stepped back out into the hall. **242.** The same door. The same number. Inside again. Luxury. I needed answers. The briefcase. Of course.

The moment I opened it, everything came rushing back.

Memories didn’t return gently—they crashed into me, overwhelming and absolute. I wasn’t a detective. Not in the way I’d assumed. I worked for something else. Something without a name. We investigated anomalies. Classified them. Studied them. Determined whether they were dangerous. Room 242 was one of them.

Sleep took me without warning. Heavy. Total.

When I woke, I felt… whole. As if every lost hour of sleep had been returned at once. Years of exhaustion erased in a single moment. Outside, the sun had barely moved—perhaps two hours had passed. Music pulsed faintly through the floor. A party. In a place like this.

I changed quickly and stepped into the hall. Someone was waiting. An old friend. “We’ve been waiting for you,” he said. “You’re the last one.” I stared at him. I hadn’t seen him in years. Me? The room below mine was alive with people—faces from another life. Friends I hadn’t had time to miss. My work didn’t allow for that. Months. Years. One anomaly at a time. China had taken five. A statue that moved when unseen. Harmless. Or so I thought.

But being surrounded by people I once cared deeply about made me feel good for the first time in a long time. I spoke with a few of them, and while talking with one of my best friends, a little girl caught my attention. She couldn’t have been older than twelve.

Then I saw her. A girl. Too young to be there. I followed her out into the stairwell. “Hey!” I called. She turned. Smiled. “Hi, Dad.”

The world tilted. “No,” I said. “You’re mistaken.”. “Not yet,” she replied. “But you will be.”

What followed made less sense than anything before. And yet… it felt true.

“She sent me here to help you.”

“She? Who is she?”

She spoke of the room. Of *her*. Of something that could create life—projections, she called them. Conscious, thinking beings. Entire universes, contained within impossible boundaries.

Then the hallway went silent. The party vanished. Only us remained.

She laughed as if hiding something. “Your room! I’ll explain. She knows what you’ve been through and what you will go through. You shouldn’t fear her—she just wants to help you. But she wants you to have a little fun and get some rest first. She knows your job is dangerous and lonely.” My confusion only grew.

“She can create beings outside of herself and project them. They have consciousness and memories, like real people. But that’s all she can do—at least outside. Inside, she can create entire universes with certain limits. She thinks you will help her understand those limits.”

“Come,” she said, taking my hand. “Let’s go to China.”, leading me to the door. “I’ve always wanted to go to China. Eventually we will, and it will be an important place for both of us—but try it.”

“What else was in China?” All the anomalies had disappeared long ago. “To China, then.”

The door opened onto the mountains. Endless green, stretching beneath a clean sky. A village in the distance. A road without end. 

And there—

At the summit—

The statue. Gone. And the village was dying without it.

I had said before that the statue wasn’t dangerous. In fact, it seemed to help people at night while they slept. It repaired small houses and watered the rice fields. At the summit, it appeared to have its own home, furnished, with a small altar outside where villagers left offerings. They called it the guardian of the mountain.

But now, long after it had disappeared, the village looked almost abandoned. There was no one to protect them. “What did I do?” Had I driven it away after staying so long nearby? 

“It wasn’t your fault,” she said softly. I hadn’t realized I’d spoken. 

“The villagers began to demand more and more from it, so it chose to disappear. But it wasn’t your fault. In a few years, you’ll understand everything—why they’re here and their place in the universe.”

I had more questions than answers.

“What about you?” I asked.

“I’m just a temporary construct—or maybe I’m really your daughter, you know, from the future” She chuckled. “I don’t know yet. That’s something she also wants to find out. She wants to know how all of this is possible, where she came from—something we all wonder. I think it’s time to go back.”

So she is aware of her own existence. “SHE” can create living beings outside herself—and what seem to be entire universes inside.

Once again, she took my hand and led me back into the hotel hallway.

“I can’t tell you everything,” she said. “Not yet. But you need to rest. You have to test her limits. And yours too.” Wait… my limits?

“Hey, what do you mean by—”

Then she was gone. I stood alone in the dark hallway.

“Tomorrow,” I whispered. Then, softer: “I’d like the first room back. The simple one.”

The door opened. I stepped inside. And closed it behind me.
