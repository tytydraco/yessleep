***4:01pm - December 13 – 1944***   
*Western Front, Belgium*

I grip this pen with frostbitten fingers.

The cold has settled into the trenches like a living thing. My skin is split and pale, numb to the point that my limbs no longer feel like mine. The pressure aches, but I have learned that pain endured is quieter than words left behind. Silence is what truly rots a man, and silence is what dominates this trench of ghosts and snow.

To my darling, Maggie, if this ever finds you, I am sorry. Not for coming here. I no longer believe choice survives once a thing has already begun. I made my choice, and I will die with it. I am sorry for the things I will never explain. For the stories I cannot tell you. For the way the world looks to me now.

War comes with many things: fear and sorrow, but also strength. I arrived a boy. I became a man. And now I have become something thin and watching.

As the shell that I am in this trench, it seems above all else, I miss my wife.

Always yours,   
*Private O’Brian.*

***9:22pm - December 14 – 1944***   
*Western Front, Belgium*

Lucky died today. Alfie, as you would have known him. He earned the name back in the fall, after walking away from shellfire that ought to have had him. We swore his charm turned bullets aside. Perhaps it did, for a time, but lead has a memory.

Lucky’s chest was never strong, and the cold finished what the guns could not. We didn’t notice for a time. He went silent, then still, his soul frozen along with his body. He was a good man. Slim. Maybe without Maggie’s extra pudding I’d already be frozen beside him.

May heaven bring him as much luck as life.

We hear movement from our neighbors. Thirteen of us stand tonight.

A goddamned icicle,   
*Private O’Brian.*

***11:48am - December 15 – 1944***   
*Western Front, Belgium*

The Germans advanced at dawn. Shapes moved through the white like stains spreading through cloth. Our Vickers answered. We held our calm. It is strange how quickly a man learns the difference between sounds meant for him and sounds meant for someone else.

We live in the trench. Fifty feet from the Germans, sometimes less. Close enough to hear them cough. Close enough to see their faces when I close my eyes. I used to believe they were my enemies. But they fall as any man does. They shiver in the night as we do.

I no longer believe I have enemies; only frozen men here for reasons we did not understand, fighting for a chance to avoid the future we all see.

If it could all end, I would be the first to walk away.

But death evades me another day. The cold does not. The war does not.

Still here,   
*Private O’Brian.*

***1:15am - December 16 – 1944***   
*Western Front, Belgium*

Something unnecessary has been burned away. I no longer fear death the way I feared leaving home. Death is a direction, and maybe it will be warm.

If I am honest, and I intend for these pages to demand honesty, I think I am already halfway gone. What remains of this life is watching, understanding, and trying, in these last borrowed moments, to name the world as it truly is.

We advance at sunrise. I am unsure if this finger can squeeze its iron trigger. We will see if this parchment sees my pen again.

As I am,   
*Private O’Brian.*

***2:26pm, December 16, 1944***   
*Western Front, Belgium*

We went over at dawn.

The snow was shallow where we climbed out, then deeper once we moved. It caught at our boots and tugged at us like hands. My senses narrowed to snow, cold metal, and the breath of the men in front of me. The Germans were quiet that morning. I thought that might mean mercy.

It did not.

The guns found us halfway across. Tracers cut the flurry open in bright seams, melting men down where they stood. The noise was enormous and distant at once, like being underwater during a storm. One of the lads screamed, I do not know who. Orders dissolved. We ran because stopping meant death.

Billy, to my left, was struck in the throat. He fell without sound, hands opening and closing, reaching for help that would never come. I fired my rifle without aiming. I do not know if I hit anything at first.

Then a German came up in front of me, close, closer than I thought possible. He was younger than me. I think he was shouting something, I’m not sure, the sounds always bleed together in gunfire. I remember the blue of his eyes. I pulled the trigger because the distance between us demanded it. He fell backward into the snow, and I stepped past him without looking again.

That was the seventh man I’ve killed.

Sarge said killing in war is not a sin, that our guns cleanse the land of the devils of the east, but the snow is always stained red, no matter what flag is worn.

We were already scattered when an explosive shell landed. Something hot tore through my ankle. I did not feel pain at first, just a searing heat, then the warmth of my blood.

I crawled. I crawled back through snow that had already filled with shapes. Men I had spoken to hours earlier lay facedown or twisted, their packs and flesh torn open.

By the time I reached the trench, only I remained.

Twelve did not.

Alone,   
*Private O’Brian.*

***7:00pm, December 17, 1944***   
*Western Front, Belgium*

The trench has not changed, but I have.

The silence of this day wraps me in a blanket of cold void. I waited hours for another man, another survivor, to drop into the trench alongside me, but it is just me left in this grave.

My foot was an unrecognizable shape of boot, blood, and frost. Leather and scab had become one. I had to slice the leather with my blade and peel it off like the skin of an orange.

Tiny bits of shrapnel protruded from my crooked ankle. I tugged the large bits from their sockets with shaky, purple fingers. I don’t know if I should’ve removed them or not; Specs was the medic, and I am a gun. But bleeding out feels like a mercy compared to the feeling of metal grinding on bone.

Night is coming now, and with it, the true cold. The candle I write beside is dripping down to its final stretch of wax. I am bleeding. I am alone. I am unsure if I will ever feel the warmth of the sun again.

Morning has become something that is no longer promised.

Four-fifths of a man,   
*Private O’Brian.*

***7:26am, December 18, 1944***   
*Western Front, Belgium*

We kill each other to preserve a future that none of us will live to see, trading the sun of today for a handful of coin, blood, and power.

Both sides are the same, just boys shivering in different colors. I cannot regret the decisions I have made, but I am finally starting to understand the world, and my only reward is that I must soon leave it.

I think I hear something in the wasteland. A man in pain. We will see if I can stand.

Good god, I miss you, Maggie.

A pawn,   
*Private O’Brian.*

***10:33am, December 18, 1944***  
*Western Front, Belgium*

I found another soul. I had to crawl to reach him. My leg is a heavy anchor I must drag through the red-stained white. I followed the sound of a low, rhythmic whimpering until I found him.

He was slumped against a splintered crate, practically invisible against the fog, except for the German flag stitched on his shoulder. I instinctively reached for my blade, but then I saw his eyes. They were a deep blue that I recognized instantly.

The boy from the advance.

I had shot him square in the chest, but he was still breathing. Beneath his tattered tunic, my bullet was wedged into a small, golden locket. The lead had crushed the metal and distorted the face of a woman inside. Perhaps it was his mother, or maybe his own Maggie, but the locket had turned the killing blow into a slow one.

I didn't see an enemy. I saw a mirror.

I don't know how I mustered the strength to get him over the lip of our trench, but I did. We fell into the mud together, two heaps of wool and blood.

The flame of my fire was small, a tiny orange defiant against the world of blue and gray. I boiled the last of the water and dropped in the final leaves of tea.

The scent of it seemed to wake him.

He looked at the steam, then at me. He scrambled backward against the trench wall and frantically patted his pockets, I assume for a weapon. I quickly reached out a shaky hand and offered the steaming cup. He stared at me a moment, his breathing shallow and quick. Then the tension left his expression along with his body; he too had no fight left.

He tried to speak, a jagged string of German that I couldn't understand, but his hand found mine. His fingers are as purple as my own.

"O'Brian," I whispered, pointing to my chest. "Wilhelm," he coughed.

We are sitting here now, shoulder to shoulder, leaning against the frozen earth.

We do not speak, for there is nothing left to say. We wouldn't understand each other anyway. The borders, the flags, the reasons we were told we were different, they have all been burned away by the frost. There is only the warmth of another man leaning on my shoulder.

We have both killed, neither of us is more righteous.

The snow is falling heavily now, filling the trench. It feels like a blanket. Wilhelm’s breathing is slowing, becoming a soft echo of mine.

I hope you find this, Maggie. I hope you know that in the end, I wasn't alone. I was with a friend I was supposed to hate but chose not to.

I think I now see the world as it truly is.

It is love.

A frozen trigger,  
*Private O’Brian.*
