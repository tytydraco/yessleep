Before I left the house, I opened the notebook one last time. The paper felt warm under my fingers — unnaturally warm, like it had been held against someone’s skin. The ink from yesterday still carried that faint, sickly smell of burned meat. I told myself it must have been there all along. But my stomach twisted anyway. Why did it feel… alive?

Downstairs, Mom looked up from the kitchen counter with a gentle smile I barely recognized.

“How are you? Did you sleep well?”

The glass slipped from my hand and shattered across the tiles. Cold water splashed over my shoes.

“Don’t worry, my child,” she said softly. “I’ll clean it up. Come eat your breakfast.”

I stared at her. Mom never called me “my child.” She never smiled like that. My chest tightened. “I… already ate,” I mumbled, and hurried out before she could say anything else. She had never been this nice. Never this interested. What the hell was going on?

At the bus stand, I was rough-sketching in my pad when a stranger stopped beside me.

“Your art is amazing, kid. Keep going.”

He gave a quick smile and walked away without another word. I stood there blinking, heart beating a little faster. Strangers didn’t just say things like that to me. Deep down, it felt good — like someone had finally seen the hours I poured into those lines. But why today? Why now?

When I reached school, Anne was waiting near the entrance. We fell into our usual rhythm, complaining about how much school sucked.

“You don’t have to stress about exams next month,” she said. “You’re representing the school in the fest now. Lucky you.”

“Yeah… I guess.” I rubbed the back of my neck. “Hey, I’m thinking of making Georgi my sub-partner so he can help with sketches at night. You okay with that? You wouldn’t have to worry about classes either.”

She studied me for a second too long. “I’ll think about it.”

Then Georgi jogged up, grinning like an idiot.

“What are you guys bitching about me?”

I forced a laugh. “Nah. I want you as my sub-partner. You help with my sketches, and you get your night adventures at Pic de Bugarach.”

“Done! Done, bro!” His eyes lit up with real excitement. “But you gotta let me explore that creepy mountain stuff. I’ve heard some wild stories.”

I went to the art department, cleared it with Ms. Elato, then headed to the library. During lunch, Georgi dropped into the chair across from me after returning a book.

“So, that creepy notebook you bought — the one with the nightmare story? You brought it today, right? Give it to me after school.”

“Yeah, sure,” I said, trying to sound casual.

Then, out of nowhere, Liya appeared at our table. My heart slammed against my ribs. Face burning. Palms instantly sweaty. I felt myself turning red.

“Hey,” she said, voice soft. “How are you?”

My tongue wouldn’t move at first. “I… I’m good,” I managed, voice shaking.

She stepped closer and rested her hand lightly on my shoulder. The touch sent a rush through me — the kind I’d imagined a hundred times while sketching her from afar — but something about it felt… off. Too perfect. “Are you really okay?” She glanced at Georgi. “Is he really okay?”

Georgi smirked. “He never talks to you, so yeah — he’s nervous.” Then the traitor bailed, leaving us alone.

Liya sat down beside me. We talked about the art fest and the “Introspection and Desire” theme. Her eyes actually stayed on mine. “I know you’ll make something good,” she said with a small smile. “Hey… any plans this weekend? We could grab coffee and brainstorm ideas.”

We shook hands. Her grip lingered a second longer than it should have. Then that smile again — pleasant on the surface, but it didn’t reach her eyes. It felt unpleasant. Forced.

“How was your day?”

The words hit like ice water. Same exact phrase as the notebook last night. My stomach dropped.

She scribbled her number on a scrap of paper and slid it over. I fumbled in my bag for something to write on — and pulled out the notebook instead. The one I had definitely left at home. My blood ran cold.

She gave a little wave and left. I sat there frozen, chest tight, no explanation that made any sense.

I headed for the exit, mind spinning, when I spotted Dad’s car waiting at the school gate. He never picked me up.

“I thought I’d grab you — I had a half day today,” he said as I climbed in, tense.

He asked about school, my friends, what I’d been doing lately. He actually listened. “That’s pretty good, the fest. People will finally see your art and respect it. If you need any help, I’m with you. Don’t worry about studies or exams. Do what you love and you’ll never have to work a day in your life.”

Before I got out at home, he turned to me with this weird, flat smile.

“How was your day?”

I was shaking by the time I stepped inside. Mom called from the kitchen, her voice bright. “Dinner’s ready! I made all your favorites. You’ll love it.”

Her smile was wide, but her face had no real expression — just a creepy, blank mask.

“I… I’m not really hungry,” I said.

She tilted her head, still smiling that empty smile.

“Okay. How was your day?”

Mom. Liya. Dad. All three of them. The exact same question. Like they were reading from the same fucked-up script.

I didn’t answer. I ran upstairs, slammed my bedroom door, and locked it behind me. My hands were trembling as I ripped the notebook out of my bag and flipped it open.

Fresh ink, calm and elegant, waited on the new page:

“I’m glad that you enjoyed your day.”..
