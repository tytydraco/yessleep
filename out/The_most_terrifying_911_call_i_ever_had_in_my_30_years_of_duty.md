I’ve been a 911 operator for almost eight years.

You hear a lot of things in this job—car crashes, break-ins, people screaming, people dying. Most of it eventually makes sense once officers arrive. There’s always an explanation waiting on the other end of the line.

That’s the part that lets you sleep at night.

Three months ago, during a late shift, I took a call from a man driving alone on a forest road. He said he’d seen someone who looked badly injured and wanted to report it. At first it sounded routine—maybe a lost hiker, maybe someone from a nearby base.

I stayed on the line with him while units headed out.

About ten minutes later he started describing things that didn’t make sense.

Then the screaming started.

The responding officer radioed me when he reached the location.

What he found out there… also didn’t make sense.

The call logs from that night were archived, but I kept a copy of the transcript. I’m not supposed to share it, but I haven’t been able to stop thinking about it since.

I was the last person to speak to both of them.

This is exactly what happened while I stayed on the line:

That’s where the official record ends.

The report says the officer’s radio signal was lost due to interference. Backup units arrived about forty minutes later. They found the patrol car parked where he said it would be.

They never found the officer.

They never found the caller either.

Just the van.

It was lying on its side exactly the way he described, claw marks cut deep into the metal panels like something had raked across it over and over again. The back door was gone completely. Not bent. Not broken.

Gone.

There was blood on the road, just like the officer said. A trail of it leading straight into the trees.

The search lasted three days.

Nothing was ever found.

Officially, the case is listed as two missing persons and an abandoned vehicle.

But here’s the part that’s not in the report.

About a month later I drove out there myself.

I know that sounds stupid. Everyone I’ve told has said the same thing. But when you’re the last voice someone hears before they die—or disappear—you start to feel responsible. Like you left something unfinished.

So on my day off I drove out to Forest Service Road 12.

It’s even quieter than it sounded on the phone. The trees grow so thick they block out most of the sky. During the day it’s dim. At night it must be completely black.

I parked roughly where the van had been found.

I remember standing there and realizing how silent the forest was.

No birds. No wind. Nothing.

Then I noticed something on one of the trees near the road.

Long scratches. Deep ones. About shoulder height.

There were more further in.

Like something very large had been moving through the woods.

I didn’t follow them very far.

But before I turned around, I heard something deeper in the trees.

At first I thought it was an animal.

Then I heard it again.

It sounded exactly like the noise that came through the officer’s radio the moment his transmission cut out.

That same long, distorted screech.

I got back in my car and left.

I haven’t gone back since.

But sometimes, during late shifts when the phones are quiet, I still think about that caller describing the man in the torn military uniform standing by the road.

And about how he said he saw him again two miles later.

Because I checked the missing persons database afterward.

No soldiers were reported missing in that area.

No hikers.

No one.

Which means whatever that man stopped to look at on the side of the road that night…

was already out there for him.
