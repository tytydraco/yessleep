My name is Lukas Weber. I’m a physician from Freiburg, Germany. I am 32 years old.

I am trained in internal medicine, with just enough surgical experience to handle emergencies when there’s no one else around.

I moved to Brazil for a girl.

Her name is Ana Clara. We met online during the pandemic. She lived in a small, isolated riverside village called Vila Esperança do Norte, somewhere deep in the Amazon basin, accessible only by boat after hours of slow travel along a muddy river that seemed to stretch forever.

She used to send me voice messages, long ones, describing her day, the heat, the river, the way the nights sounded alive. I didn’t understand most of it at first, but I learned. Slowly. Badly.

“Você fala bonito… mas errado.”

(You speak nicely… but wrong.)

I told her I would visit one day. Eventually, I stopped saying visit and started saying move.

She stayed with me for a couple of months back in 2023.

And I did indeed moved to Brazil after 5 years of talking daily with her.

The first week felt like something out of a different life. The village was small, maybe fifty houses built on stilts, a wooden dock, a generator that coughed electricity into existence for a few hours at night. People were curious but kind, and once they learned I was a doctor, they treated me like something valuable, even if I kept insisting I wasn’t there to work.

I ended up working anyway.

It’s hard not to, when someone shows up with a child burning with fever or a man whose hand has been split open by a rusted tool. You don’t ignore that.

Ana would translate for me when I struggled.

“Ele quer saber se você sente dor aqui.”

(He wants to know if you feel pain here.)

It felt good, in a quiet way, to be useful again without the noise and pressure of hospitals.

But on the fifth day, something started to feel wrong.

A man came in with a deep wound across his thigh. He said it had happened near the forest edge. When I asked what caused it, he hesitated in a way that felt rehearsed, like he had already decided not to tell the truth.

“Foi coisa do mato.”

(It was something from the forest.)

The wound didn’t look like an animal bite. It looked like something had pressed into him with enormous force and then dragged sideways. There were no clear tooth marks, no claw patterns, just crushed tissue and tearing.

I told myself it was a jaguar, or maybe a fall against something sharp. Something explainable. Something I could categorize.

That night, I heard something in the forest.

A deep, distant cracking sound, like thick wood being forced apart, followed by a low vibration that I could feel more than hear. It went on for several seconds, then stopped abruptly.

I asked Ana about it.

She avoided my eyes.

“É longe.”

(It’s far.)

That was all she said.

Two days later, just before sunset, a woman came running out of the forest carrying a child.

She was Indigenous, from deeper in the jungle, her skin covered in dirt and streaked with sweat. She was crying in a way that didn’t sound like grief yet, more like pure panic, as if she still believed something could be done.

The child’s left arm was gone.

Not cleanly severed, not cut. The shoulder was a mess of crushed bone and torn muscle, like the limb had been twisted and pulled until it separated. I had seen traumatic amputations before, but never like that.

She kept repeating:

“Ajuda, por favor, ajuda!”

(Help, please, help!)

And then:

“Voltar! Preciso voltar!”

(Go back! We need to go back!)

I didn’t ask permission. I didn’t think about logistics. I did what I was trained to do.

I stabilized him as best as I could, improvised a tourniquet, cleaned what I could without causing more damage, and started antibiotics from the limited supply I had.

I told Ana we needed to take him to a hospital.

She translated.

The woman shook her head violently, grabbing my arm with surprising strength.

“Não hospital. Aldeia. Agora.”

(No hospital. Village. Now.)

She pointed into the forest.

Ana looked at me with something I hadn’t seen in her before.

Fear.

“She wants you to go with her,” she said in English, quietly.

I knew it was a bad idea.

But leaving that child to die because I was afraid of the forest felt worse.

So I packed a bag and followed her.

Three men from the village came with us at first, carrying machetes and speaking little. The further we went, the denser the forest became, until the light itself felt filtered and unnatural.

At one point, the same deep cracking sound echoed again, much closer this time, followed by what sounded like multiple trees collapsing in sequence. The ground trembled faintly under our feet.

The men stopped immediately.

One of them said something quickly:

“Isso não é bicho.”

(That’s not an animal.)

They refused to go further.

I looked at the woman, then back at them, and made the worst decision of my life.

I kept going.

We reached the tribe long after dark.

It was silent in a way that didn’t feel natural. No children playing, no conversations, no fires crackling. Just people watching from the edges, their faces unreadable as they let me treat the boy.

When I finished, I finally looked around properly, and what I saw made my stomach turn in a way I couldn’t control.

People with injuries that had healed wrong.

A man whose lower jaw was partially missing, replaced by scar tissue that had fused unevenly. A woman with both hands reduced to blunt, irregular stumps. A teenager with a leg that had been crushed and then healed crooked, forcing him to walk with a twisted gait.

These were not accidents.

These were repeated, deliberate acts of violence that had been survived.

I asked, slowly:

“O que aconteceu com eles?”

(What happened to them?)

The woman who brought me answered without hesitation.

“Coisa do mato.”

(Thing of the forest.)

That night, I lay awake, listening.

And I heard that sound again.

A deep repetitive rhythm, almost ritualistic, hidden beneath what I had convinced myself were natural sounds.

I couldn’t sleep much, so I wait for the sun to rise.

At first light, I packed my thing and left.

They didn’t try to stop me.

The forest felt wrong on the way back, the air smelled metallic and rusted. Paths didn’t align with my memory, and the air seemed thicker, harder to breathe, as if something was being pushed into it from far away.

Then I heard voices, in Portuguese.

I moved toward them, thinking of maybe they could give me some information on how to get back to the village. I moved slowly, carefully, until the trees opened just enough for me to see.

It wasn’t a forest anymore.

It was a clearing carved out of it, violently and recently. Mud churned into a thick slurry, machines half-submerged, generators roaring. Excavators sat like metal beasts, their arms tearing into the earth.

Looked like an illegal gold mining operation.

And then I saw the people.

Indigenous men tied to wooden poles, baking in the hot sun. Others were locked in makeshift cages built from metal scraps. A few women sat on the ground, watched by armed men, their faces empty in a way that suggested something had already been taken from them. And the worst part, the children…

One of the miners laughed as he kicked a girl who didn’t move.

I didn’t think.

I raised my phone and took pictures.

The camp. The machines. The people.

I knew it was stupid, but some part of me believed that if I could document it, if I could show someone, it would matter.

Then one of them turned.

“Quem é aquele?”

(Who is that?)

They started moving.

I ran. Branches tore at my skin, roots caught my feet, but I didn’t stop. I could hear them behind me, shouting, crashing through the undergrowth.

Then, suddenly, someone grabbed my arm.

I almost screamed, but a hand covered my mouth.

It was one of the Indigenous men from the tribe, the one with the twisted leg. I recognized his face immediately.

He gestured silently, pulling me down into a narrow depression hidden by foliage. We stayed there, pressed into the mud, while the miners passed close enough that I could smell their sweat.

After what felt like an hour, the man with the twisted leg said to me:

“Corre.”

(Run.)

And I did.

I don’t remember much of the journey back.

Only fragments. The river appearing suddenly, the village coming into view, Ana’s face when she saw me.

I showed her the photos.

Her expression changed immediately. Fear.

Within minutes, other villagers were inside the house. Someone took my phone from my hand before I could react.

“Não pode.”

(It can’t.)

I tried to protest, but they were already deleting everything.

“Se eles veem isso…”

(If they see this…)

The man didn’t finish the sentence.

It’s been three days.

No one talks about the forest now.

At night, I still hear the sounds. The cracking, the grinding, sometimes very far away screams of pain.

There is no monster out there.

Just men with machines, with weapons, with enough distance from consequences that they can do whatever they want to whoever they find.

Ana is behaving like I ruined everything. People are treating me weird, and I don’t think I can leave.

Ana says we have to stay quiet, that drawing attention will only make it worse.

But I can’t unsee what I saw.

The boy is still alive, they told us. They said he’s “melhor” (better). I don’t know what that means.

I keep thinking about the way his arm was torn. Not cut, not bitten, but pulled. Like someone testing how far a body can go before it breaks.
