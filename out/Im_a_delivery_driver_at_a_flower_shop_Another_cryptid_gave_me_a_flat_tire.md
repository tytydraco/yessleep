As a contract driver, protecting my vehicle is the most important thing I can do—no vehicle means no job. Ever since I started working for an occult flower shop, this imperative has become a thousand times more urgent. While I enjoy nearly getting tackled by seven excitable cerberus puppies as much as the next guy, it's not so cute when they scratch up the paint job on my car. The worst so far was when I had to drive through the nightmare dimension, and the manifestation of my internalized guilt (who, for reasons I have no desire to examine, bore my mother's face) left a bunch of black handprints on my windshield. Took ages to scrub that stuff off.

There are some measures I've taken on my own: hanging a bundle of sage on my rear-view mirror, hamsa hand bumper stickers, and a few Spanish silver coins I found at an antique shop. I tried hanging garlic on my car doors too, which worked on vampires, but it made my car smell like garlic so I had to take it down. One of the comments on [my last post](https://www.reddit.com/r/nosleep/comments/1r8g74e/im_a_delivery_driver_at_a_flower_shop_this_road/) suggested an iron horseshoe, but I found out the one I ordered online was actually steel and not iron. That's what I get for buying it on amazon, I guess.

As for any damage the car sustains while I'm delivering, Southwest Sunsets will comp me on certain repairs, so long as it meets several criteria. But if I don't meet those stipulations, I have to pay for it myself. This is the situation I found myself in this afternoon.

I was on the way to a wildlife rescue when it happened. It was out in the boonies, just on the edge of the designated national park area right next to our city. The delivery was just a small succulent garden for one of the wildlife rescue's staff—pretty normal delivery. That is, until my tire popped.

My car jerked to the left, and I cranked the steering wheel to right myself. The sudden motion caused my foot to slam on the accelerator, and my car revved dramatically for a second until I got it back under control. When I thought I'd recovered, the car jumped again, angling upward in a succession of lopsided thumps. Something was stuck on my wheel.

I let out a string of curses, then pulled to the side of the road. When I got out to investigate, I saw the mangled rubber of what remained of my front-left tire, tangled up in a pair of small antlers attached to the head of a dead rabbit. A wave of nausea rolled through me. I wished I had seen it before hitting it. I looked down the road to see if I could find the rest of its body, and I did, for a moment. The headless body then got up, somehow shook it off, and started hopping away, a new tiny antlered head growing out of its neck. I blinked, then looked back at my wheel, half expecting the severed head to grow a new body. It didn't.

A new problem thus presented itself: I was stuck on the edge of the desert, with a ripped up tire and no spare to speak of. I could call roadside assistance, of course, but that was a potential bill of a few hundred dollars that I didn't have to spend. Before resorting to that, I wanted to see if the shop would comp it first. I called.

No one answered, and I checked the time—4:03pm. I let out a heavy sigh; I had forgotten that the shop closed at 2pm on weekends. I guess I'd have to call roadside assistance and pray they would comp me later. I hung up, then pulled out my AAA card and dialed the number.

An hour later, the mechanic rolled up in a white van that looked like it was held together with duct tape and prayer, at least 30 years old and somehow still functioning; a testament to the man's skill as a mechanic, I suppose. It had a sun-tarnished roof rack and a faded logo painted on the side: Ilya's Very Good Car Repair. It depicted a man wearing overalls and an Ushanka, not-so-subtly carrying a hammer and sickle. The slogan beneath was written in Cyrillic.

The man who emerged wore quadruple denim: fringed jacket, overalls, undershirt, and newsboy cap. I half expected him to be wearing denim socks, but they were ordinary cotton, barely visible under his faded leather work boots. He had a gray handlebar mustache and goatee which, like every other inch of his body and clothes, bore stains of grease and wiper fluid. Predictably, he had a lit cigarette hanging out of his mouth, and a pack of Marlboros stuffed into his shirt pocket. The man was so tall and bulky that watching him exit his van was like watching someone get out of a comically small clown car.

"You need new tire, da?" the man said, with a thick Slavic accent and a deep voice frayed by years of cigarette use.

I gestured to the shredded tire.

He squatted down for a closer look. "Okh, blyat," he muttered under his breath. He looked back at me. "Why not have spare?"

"I used it the last time I hit a jackalope."

"I see." He ran his finger over the rim of the wheel, then, casually, as if grabbing a potato chip out of a bag, snapped one of the creature's antlers off its head, and began to eat it. It crunched, not like a potato chip, but like a bone breaking, scattering small splinters on the ground. He noticed me staring in disbelief, and held out the antler. "You want bite?"

"No thanks," I choked out. "I'm good."

He shrugged. "Your loss," he said, and took another bite. He narrowed his eyes for a moment, then ran one thick, calloused finger over the rim of the wheel, accruing another black stain on his skin. After quickly wiping it off on his overalls, he directed me to look closer. "Rim is damage," he said, between a mouthful of antler bits. "Need new one."

I looked. There was, in fact, a large dent in the rim. But I hadn't seen it before. "It looked fine hardly a few seconds ago."

"Maybe you look harder next time," he said. He let out a hearty baritone laugh. I didn't laugh with him. He stood up and patted my back, his hand the size of a bear paw and nearly knocking the wind out of me. "Come," he said. "I tow you to shop, no cost. We fix there, da?"

"Sure," I replied. "My car has to stay on until we get there, though. The flowers will cook in the sun if we don't leave the AC on."

"Ah, we put in van," he said. "Is no problem." Before returning to his vehicle, he offered me a handshake. "My name is Vasily," he said.

I glanced at the logo again, Ilya's Very Good Car Repair. "What about Ilya?"

"He is retire," Vasily replied. "I take over business for him."

I shrugged. "Fair enough."

We brought the flowers into the back of his van, which contained a tableau of organized chaos. Jumper cables, car jacks, auto repair tools I couldn't fathom the purpose of scattered haphazardly in boxes. He also had other miscellaneous items, notably a collection of hunting supplies, a few rifles piled into a milk crate, and a small commercial freezer full of various cuts of meat. Based on the size, and the cardboard box full of half-eaten antlers sitting on top of it, I assumed it was some kind of venison.

Normally, the shop stored prepared bouquets in a large walk-in refrigerator, and while I didn't like the idea of shoving them between piles of meat, a fridge was a fridge, and it would have to do.

When I opened the meat cooler, I noticed a smell: something metallic, maybe like copper, mixed with must and cobwebs. Not what meat was supposed to smell like. As I stared into the cooler, my gut tensed with anxiety. I couldn't place why, but I got the sense that something in that cooler would reach out and grab me. I took a deep breath, shook off my nerves, then reached in and started pushing a few cuts of meat aside, making room for the bouquet.

My hand brushed a set of human fingers.

I staggered back, choking down a scream. A flood of adrenaline swept through my veins, and I let go of the glass hood, watching the door slam shut and trap the thing behind the window. I wanted to run, but I couldn't. I was trapped, because my only real means of escape—my car—wouldn't drive on a busted wheel, which I now had to assume he had damaged further on purpose. I reckoned that must have been how this guy caught his victims: he'd find them stranded in the desert with a broken down car, tow them back to his shop, then kill them and cook them for dinner. And worse, the meat packages had price tags on them. He was *selling* them, too.

I fumbled to pull out my phone and started to dial 911, but I stopped. It would take at least 20 minutes for the police to make it this far out of town. That was plenty of time for Vasily to kill me, which he would do immediately if he heard me talking to the cops. I also wouldn't even know where to tell them to find me, since we were still in the middle of the desert. I would have to remain stealthy about this if I wanted to get out alive.

I took a photo of the meat cooler (useful evidence for later, I reckoned), then started to send the photo to my girlfriend, along with a message telling her to send the cops out to Ilya's Very Good Car Repair. When I was halfway through typing, I saw something out of the corner of my eye.

The hand twitched.

I almost didn't register it for a moment, and I leaned in closer, squinting.

The hand twitched again, climbing further towards the surface.

My breath hitched in my throat, choking down another yelp. The hand began moving its fingers, wriggling like the legs of an upturned cockroach, cold, green, making my guts twist with revulsion. I couldn't take my eyes off of it, for fear it might jump out and grab my face. But after half a second of horror and revulsion, I realized it was moving with purpose. It had climbed close to the glass window, and it was writing something in the fog.

SOS

"Who are you?" I whispered.

The last letters faded into the fog, and the hand began writing again.

ILYA

I've dealt with some truly insane stuff at this point. Most of it has been minor weirdness, some of it has been horrifying but manageable, and reading the delivery instructions will usually prevent any adverse effects of encountering an occult customer. But until this moment, I had never felt so threatened that I knew I couldn't handle it on my own. Here, with stone cold certainty, I was so profoundly out of my depth that I could never hope to comprehend it. It would be one thing if this guy was some sort of serial killer, but he couldn't be; Ilya was still alive.

It had to be a shapeshifter.

"No touch!"

I jumped at the sound of Vasily's—the Shapeshifter's—voice, staggering away from the cooler. "Sorry," I stammered.

It climbed into the back of the van, then took out two milk crates and affixed them to the top of the cooler with a few bungee cords. "You put in there," it said gruffly.

"Understood."

It nodded, then grabbed another antler to munch on and went out to grab a few of the other bouquets. And I realized there was no way to get out of this. My car was broken, and I just had to carry on as normal, load all the bouquets I was supposed to deliver into its van, let it drive me back to its lair and just hope it'll decide to repair my car before killing me.

When we were done, I nervously turned to face it, and asked, "Is it alright if I sit in my car on our way, instead of the back of your van?"

The Shapeshifter snorted. "Nonsense," it said, still using the same Slavic accent. "You sit in passenger seat."

I desperately did not want to have to ride shotgun with a shapeshifter. Mere inches away from whatever limbs it's going to use to cut my skin off and wear it? Hell no. "I'd like to listen to my own music while we drive back," I added, an admittedly flimsy excuse. "My phone is hooked up to my stereo."

"Nyet," the Shapeshifter replied, more sternly this time. "Customer stays in car while towing, liability to my business. Either you ride shotgun, or you walk home through miles of desert."

And I could have, if I wanted to. If I did, it might have killed me, or it might not. But if I left, Ilya was doomed as well.

I don't fully know what motivated my decision. Maybe I felt a sort of kinship with this man who'd been dragged into the same situation I was in, or maybe I just wouldn't have had anything better to do tonight. Something like the Trolley Problem, maybe? I can't put it to words. I just got the sensation that, no matter what happened to me, somehow or another, I would have ended up going with the Shapeshifter anyway. My subconscious mind answered before my conscious mind even registered the question.

So I agreed. I'm in his van. I'm sitting in the passenger's seat, mere inches away from a shapeshifter wearing the skin of a man who's twice my size. It doesn't really need to tow my car, it could kill me and eat me if it wanted to, but I suppose even a creature that steals and inhabits your skin needs rent money. Better that it at least get paid a hefty mechanic's fee before killing you, that way it can afford the electric bill it racks up from keeping freezers full of mystery meat and severed limbs. I'm letting it drive me towards those freezers. Wherever Ilya's Very Good Car Repair is, it's in a part of the desert where your bones will be stripped of flesh and bleached by the sun before anyone finds your body.

We're close, and I think I see the sign in the distance. I'm going to stop typing on my phone. I have to somehow figure my way out of this mess.
