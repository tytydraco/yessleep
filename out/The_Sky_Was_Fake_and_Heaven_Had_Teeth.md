I awoke upon a blackened shore strewn with the dead.

  
They lay half-buried in the wet sand as though the tide itself had tried to hide them and failed. Some had been there for days. Others looked ancient — dried hollow beneath a sky that did not feel real.

  
Their skin clung tightly to their bones like old parchment shriveled over broken branches. Empty eye sockets stared toward the sea, pecked clean by pale carrion things that still circled overhead in silence. In many of the corpses, the skulls had split open from crown to jaw, not cracked by violence, but hollowed with deliberate precision.

  
Something had fed on them.

  
Not flesh.

  
Not blood.

  
The brains had been drawn out through small black holes bored neatly into their heads, as if some patient creature had drunk the thoughts from them before leaving the husks behind.  
And though the beach reeked of salt and rot—there was no sign of the ocean making waves.  
I had little strength left to spare, and before me stood a lonely tombstone half-buried in the wet sand.

*HERE LIES LUDWIG NIERSENHAAL*  
*A man with no love.*  
*A man rotted.*

The name had been carved into the stone with such violence that the letters looked torn rather than engraved, and the memory I had of it was with hatred still burning in my veins.  
I knew where I was.

  
The Island of the Beginning.



The cursed shore where my father died the day he chose for us to depart for the Great Sea.  
He had set our eyes upon the Land of Eternal Plague, where the Hunt for the plagued raged without end beneath blood-colored moons. Men spoke of rotting cities swallowed by fog, of hunters carrying saws and torches through alleyways choked with corpses.

  
But they also spoke of gold.  
And gold has led more men into darkness than hunger ever could.

  
My father did not die at sea.  
I killed him the day we first set foot upon the Island of the Beginning.

  
He was a cruel man even without the plague in his veins. The sickness that consumed the world had never touched him, yet there was always something rotten living behind his eyes. He murdered my mother beneath the light of a dying lantern and wanted to drag me across the Great Sea so I could fight beside him in the Land of Eternal Plague.

  
He spoke of gold as if it were salvation.  
Of the Hunt as if it were glory.

  
But I had seen the hunger in his eyes long before I saw the plague.

  
So upon that cursed shore, with the fog crawling around our feet and the sea roaring like some sleeping god beyond the cliffs, I drove the same knife into his heart that he had buried in my mother.

  
And still—I buried him. It felt like a fitting end for a wicked man upon the cursed sands of the Island of the Beginning.

  
My mother had been a follower of the Goddess of Light. Even in a world drowning in rot and madness, she believed mercy was sacred. She used to say that the Goddess opened her arms even for the wicked, for all souls eventually crawled back toward the light no matter how stained they had become.

  
So I honored her final wish.

  
I dug his grave with trembling hands rather than leave him upon the shore to rot beside the others, to be torn apart by the pale carrion birds that circled endlessly above the island, waiting for the dead to soften beneath the rain.

  
Three days had passed since I awoke upon that cursed shore, haunted by memories I would have gladly carved from my own mind if given the chance.

  
It was there I remembered what my father used to call the pale carrion birds — what they had once been before the Fall swallowed the world whole.

  
Seagulls.  
At least, that was the old name for them.

  
I would not have known. I was born after the Fall, beneath poisoned skies and blood-stained tides. Truthfully, my father knew little more than I did. He had been born during the final years of the collapse, and most of his knowledge of the old world came from worm-eaten books and the stories his father whispered to him beside dying fires.

  
The creatures circling above me no longer resembled birds.

  
The plague had reshaped them into things the sea itself should have rejected.

  
Tumorous masses swelled from their bodies like clustered eggs ready to burst. Some had eyes hanging loose against their flesh by blackened nerves, while others had their skulls completely consumed beneath layers of pulsing growth. Their wings stretched impossibly wide — three times the size of their bodies — formed not of feathers, but of thin translucent skin veined like the insides of a living organ.

  
And scattered across those wings were dozens of thumb-sized tumors that twitched and swelled as they flew.

  
Sometimes they burst.  
When they did, warm blood rained softly from the sky.

  
After burying my father, I returned home.

  
I had no desire to cross the Great Sea or set foot in the Land of Eternal Plague. I would have rather starved in the gutters than fight the plagued beneath those skies of endless night where the moon never moved and the streets stank of blood and burnt flesh.

  
It was there I met Lenora.

  
She was the daughter of a fisherman, and I worked as a mercenary aboard her father’s ship, protecting the crew from the plagued things that circled above the waters at dusk. Sometimes they descended screaming from the clouds to tear apart the weak and drag the living into the sea.

  
Mr. Brekenhar was a generous man.  
But the world no longer spared generous men.

  
He suffered from tuberculosis — an ancient sickness from the old world. In an age where mankind already trembled beneath the shadow of the plague, any cough of blood was enough to summon fear. The priests declared his illness a sign of corruption. The townsfolk dragged him through the streets in chains before burning him alive after the trial.  
I can still remember the smell.

  
After his death, I married Lenora.  
And in time, we had a son.  
Silas.

  
For a while, I believed the sea had finally loosened its curse upon me. We lived quietly beside the harbor, beneath rotting skies and distant cathedral bells. But peace never survives long in this world.

  
I still do not know why Silas chose to sail toward the Land of Eternal Plague.

  
Lenora went after him the moment she learned he had boarded a vessel bound for the Great Sea.

  
And I followed them both.

  
I joined a crew of desperate mercenaries and damned men who boarded that black ship for gold, glory, or reasons they no longer understood.

  
We never reached our destination.  
Something ancient rose beneath the waves.

  
The sailors called it the Serpent of the Great Sea, though no serpent born of God or nature could have possessed a body so vast. I remember the ocean folding upward like a living wall. I remember screams swallowed by the fog. I remember enormous eyes opening beneath the water.

  
Then darkness.

  
Now I stood once more upon the blackened shore of the Island of the Beginning, staring across the endless sea.

  
I had to return to the Land of Eternal Plague.

  
Whether Lenora and Silas had reached those cursed shores or whether the sea had devoured them alongside the others, I did not know.

  
But they were the only souls who had ever loved me.  
And the only ones I had ever loved in return.

  
I had known only two ways to cross the Great Sea.

  
A ship or the throat tunnel that led beneath the earth and emerged upon the shores of the Land of Eternal Plague.

  
Sometimes, I find myself wishing I could have seen the moon my mother used to draw for me when I was a child. In her sketches, it looked gentle. Pale. Beautiful. A silver lantern hanging peacefully above a sleeping world.

  
But that moon no longer exists.

  
Now, whenever the clouds thin even slightly, all that stares down upon us is the Great Eye—vast and red and impossibly alive—watching the world from beyond the heavens like some patient god waiting for something beneath the earth to awaken.

  
There were no ships upon the Island of the Beginning.

  
The vessels only passed through these cursed waters three times a year, during the brief season when the beasts beneath the Great Sea entered their slumber and the waves grew calm enough for men foolish enough to sail them.

  
But we had been damned from the start.  
The Serpent had awakened before its time.

Something was stirring beneath the sea. Something vast enough to drive even the ancient horrors from their sleep and whatever force had awakened it was beyond anything I could understand.

  
That left only one path to the Land of Eternal Plague.  
The throat tunnel.

  
It lay in plain sight near the cliffs beyond the island, yet no sane man willingly entered it. The opening descended deep beneath the earth like the throat of some colossal creature buried beneath the world. Its walls were not stone, but flesh—dark, wet, and slowly breathing..  
Even standing near it filled the mind with sickness. The air smelled of salt, blood, and something ancient rotting beneath the soil of the world itself.

  
Old sailors spoke of the tunnels in hushed voices, as though they were not built paths but living organs hidden beneath the skin of the earth. They claimed the deeper passages pulsed with veins large enough to crawl through and that distant heartbeats could sometimes be heard echoing through the dark.

  
Yet the tunnels were not plagued. That was the worst part. They were something else entirely. A new species born within the bones of what crawled beneath the earth.  
I found the entrance near the cliffs where the earth split open like a wounded mouth.  
And before fear could convince me otherwise—I jumped.

  
The descent was far deeper than I had imagined.

  
I struck the bottom hard enough to drive the breath from my lungs, yet the fall did not break me. The wet flesh beneath my body yielded softly, cushioning the impact like the insides of some enormous living organ.

  
I raised my torch with trembling hands. Its light reached only a few feet before being swallowed whole by a darkness deeper than any night I had ever known. The walls pulsed slowly around me, veins twitching beneath translucent layers of skin slick with warm moisture.

  
The ground beneath my feet inhaled and exhaled.

  
No sound beyond the slow rhythm of the tunnel breathing around me like a sleeping thing pretending not to notice my presence.

  
So I walked deeper.

  
Crossing the Great Sea by ship took weeks when the waters were calm.

  
The throat tunnels reduced journeys to mere days, as though distance itself bent and shrank within their living depths.

  
No one truly understood how.

  
Only a handful of men had ever crossed them and returned sane enough to speak of what they had seen. They said that distances folded unnaturally inside the tunnels, time moved differently as though the laws of the world weakened beneath the earth. But whether those men were truly sane to begin with—no one could ever say.

  
*“Solomon…”* A voice drifted through the darkness. Faint and distant.

  
I dismissed it as another illusion born from the tunnel and continued walking.

  
Then I heard it again. It was closer this time. *“Solomon…”* The breathing walls around me seemed to pulse harder. *“You disgraceful little wretch,”* the voice whispered. *“You stabbed me in the back.”*

  
My hand immediately found the knife at my waist and tore it free.

  
*“You cannot kill the dead.”* Something breathed behind me.

  
I turned at once, raising the torch high, but there was nothing there beyond the endless flesh-dark of the tunnel. Yet I could still feel a presence standing impossibly close behind my shoulder.

  
*“The dread in your eyes…”* the voice murmured from somewhere unseen. *“And the warmth of your blood… it eases my anger.”*

  
“Who are you?” I shouted into the dark, though deep inside I already knew the answer. My father was dead. I had buried him myself upon the Island of the Beginning.

  
And still—his voice slithered through the tunnel around me.

  
“I know you’re dead.”

  
*“I am here.”*

  
The whisper came from directly ahead.

  
Far in the distance, I suddenly saw a small trembling light appear within the darkness, pale and weak like a lantern seen through deep water.

  
And then it vanished.

  
“Come out!” I shouted.

  
But my hands were trembling so badly that the torchlight shook against the breathing walls around me.

  
The faint light appeared again.

  
And this time—I saw the shape carrying it through the dark.

  
Something hung from the creature’s head like the lure of a deep-sea fish, a thin strand of flesh swaying gently above it. At the end pulsed a small bioluminescent bulb, casting sickly pale light across its body each time it moved.

  
Its face—God.Its face looked wrong.

  
The skin upon its head hung backward like a peeled mask, exposing layers of wet flesh beneath. And stitched crudely over that ruined face was another one entirely.

  
My father’s face. Or something that had tried to imitate it.

  
The lips twitched unnaturally against the thread holding them in place while the creature spoke through a second mouth buried deeper inside its skull — a smaller, almost human mouth that opened vertically within the larger one.

  
And through that hidden mouth came my father’s voice.

  
I staggered back instinctively.  
The thing took a step forward. Then another.

  
It crawled upon all four limbs with a slow, unnatural movement, its swollen belly dragging heavily against the living floor of the tunnel like a water-filled sack. Each movement left streaks of dark slime behind it.

  
Its skin was green-grey and rotting, hanging loose in places where the flesh had softened into decay. The smell that came from it filled the tunnel instantly — wet rot, seawater, and the sweetness of something long dead.

  
Its head resembled that of a bear left too long beneath the tide, broad and malformed, with small wet eyes positioned along the sides of its skull instead of the front.  
And its teeth—its teeth looked stolen.

  
Animal fangs fused beside human molars, all crammed together inside that inward-curving mouth as though different creatures had been forced together into one body by something that did not understand life well enough to imitate it correctly.

  
I stood there trembling as the thought of killing my father crawled back into my mind once more.

  
No matter how far I ran from those memories, they always found me in the dark.  
I could still hear my mother screaming from behind the bedroom door every night he returned home. The walls were thin in that old house. Thin enough for a child to learn what helplessness sounded like.

  
Hatred plagued me long before the world itself was consumed by plague.  
It lived inside me like a second heartbeat.

  
I remember the final night more clearly than I remember my own face. The door creaked open, and she was lying there upon the floor beneath the lantern light, a knife buried deep in her chest. Tears still clung to her eyes as though she had died begging for mercy.  
But it was the fear on her face that never left me.

  
Even in death, it remained.

  
A terror carved so deeply into her that not even the grave could bury it.  
And standing there in that moment, staring at what remained of my mother, I made my choice.

  
I decided that same fear would one day live in my father’s eyes.

  
Because if there is any true duty left for men in this rotting world, it is this: to protect the helpless from the monsters wearing human skin.

  
When the darkness finally lifted from my eyes, the creature before me had been reduced to shreds of flesh scattered across the breathing floor of the tunnel.

  
I could not remember what had happened.  
Only the hatred.

  
I remembered losing myself the moment that thing called me son.

  
There are wounds the plague cannot create and death cannot heal. Hatred is one of them. It waits patiently inside the human heart, feeding quietly for years until something finally gives it a face to destroy.

  
Whatever that creature had been—it was dead now.

  
The throat tunnel shuddered around me as though reacting to the slaughter. The walls contracted slowly, wet flesh folding inward, and the remains of the thing were pulled into the living floor piece by piece. Bones vanished first. Then flesh. Then blood.  
As though the tunnel itself had begun feeding.

  
I stood there breathing heavily, staring at the empty ground where the creature had been only moments before.

  
Then I noticed my hand. A wound had opened across my palm. Not a cut. A puncture.  
Deep enough that something sharp had clearly pierced through the flesh—a tooth, perhaps, or some hidden blade buried within the creature’s body during the struggle.  
But the wound did not look natural. The flesh around it pulsed slowly beneath the skin. I could feel something moving inside my hand, shifting faintly with my heartbeat as though another pulse had begun beneath my own.

  
I wrapped the wound tightly with cloth at once.

  
Yet even through the fabric I could still feel it breathing.

  
I walked deeper into the tunnel, surrounded by a silence so complete it began to press against my thoughts.

  
No one truly knows how the plague began. One night, the sky opened. And the Great Eye looked down upon mankind. A single enormous eye staring from beyond the clouds, vast enough to eclipse the heavens themselves. The old world ended the moment humanity realized the thing in the sky was looking back at them.

  
The earth changed soon after.

  
Throat tunnels appeared across the world like fresh wounds splitting open beneath cities and oceans alike. Then came the green veins pulsing beneath the soil — what mercenaries called the *Godroot* and the alchemists named the *Emerald Lattice*.

  
They spread through the earth like living arteries.

  
Men who dug too deep later discovered enormous lung-like tissues buried beneath the stone, breathing slowly in the dark as though something colossal slept beneath the planet itself.

  
The alchemists believed these discoveries held answers. They spoke endlessly of patterns, mutations, and the possibility of uncovering truths hidden beneath the world.

  
But they never told us everything they found. Perhaps they discovered something too terrible to name. Or perhaps the knowledge itself destroyed them.

  
Most believed the emergence of the Godroot and the tunnels was what caused the plague, though no man had ever truly proven it.

  
But after seeing that thing inside the throat tunnel—after hearing my dead father speak through its mouth—I no longer believe the plague was caused by these things emerging beneath the earth.

  
I think that the plague might be caused by something else entirely.

  
I encountered nothing else within the tunnel.

  
Perhaps I was fortunate. Or perhaps the tunnel had already tested me and found something inside me worth letting pass. Whatever the reason, the end eventually came.

  
The walls around me began to tighten without warning. The living flesh compressed violently, forcing the air from my lungs as the entire throat tunnel convulsed around me like a great beast choking on swallowed prey.

  
Then it expelled me. I was vomited from the earth onto the shores of the Land of Eternal Plague.

  
I collapsed onto black stone, coughing bile and blood while cold rain fell from the endless night above. After a moment, I forced myself upright and looked upon the city stretched across the distant coastline.

  
And despite everything—it was beautiful.

  
Towering cathedrals rose beneath the crimson gaze of the Great Eye, their spires vanishing into fog and smoke. Lanterns flickered faintly through narrow streets while the sea crashed endlessly against ancient walls darkened by age and plague.

  
Yet far beyond the city, spreading across entire districts, I saw the tumors.

  
Enormous masses of swollen flesh engulfing buildings and streets alike, pulsing slowly beneath the rain as though part of some colossal organism growing over the city piece by piece.

  
My hand suddenly burned. I looked down at the cloth wrapped around my palm. Beneath it, the wound itched and pulsed with its own heartbeat.

  
There were hunters, mercenaries, alchemists, mages, priests — every kind of soul gathered in the Land of Eternal Plague to kill the plagued and earn something in return. But most came for gold. Gold has always been mankind’s richest dream and its oldest nightmare.  
Yet the people I saw no longer looked human in spirit. They moved like exhausted ghosts beneath the endless night, wrapped in bloodstained coats and damp bandages. Many carried wounds that no medicine could truly heal. Along the shore, funeral pyres burned day and night as bodies recovered from the city were fed to the flames before the plague could claim them again.

  
And still—no one left.

  
In the center of the safe zone stood a massive stone monument darkened by rain and ash. Thousands of names had been etched into its surface — the fallen, the missing, the forgotten.

  
I searched desperately for Lenora and Silas. Their names were not there. Relief came first. Then dread. Because in a place like this, the absence of a name did not always mean the absence of death. Sometimes bodies were never found. Or maybe the guild simply stopped caring enough to carve new names into stone.

  
I knew my body was in no condition to continue.

  
At the Island of the Beginning, I had carved what little flesh remained from the fallen and carried it with me through the tunnel. Most of the corpses had already been dried to the bone by salt, wind, and time, yet I forced myself to eat what I could.

  
Not because I was hungry. Only because I feared what would happen if I stopped moving.  
But exhaustion had settled deep inside me. Not merely in my flesh, but somewhere deeper—as though the journey itself had aged my soul. It felt as if I had wandered for years beneath the earth, even though I knew only days had passed.

  
I eventually sat beside a stone wall near the market district where hunters repaired their weapons and merchants traded supplies scavenged from the plagued zones. Smoke rose endlessly from the smithies, darkening the air while iron screamed beneath hammer blows.  
And strangely, the sight felt unreal to me.

  
All my life I had heard the Land of Eternal Plague described as a kingdom of butchers drowning in blood and madness. Yet here, beneath the crimson gaze of the Great Eye, humanity had still managed to build something resembling life.

  
My father had been wrong. There were still humans here.

  
For a moment, I found myself wondering what had happened to this place—what kind of suffering could twist men into monsters while forcing others to become stronger simply to remain human.

  
But exhaustion eventually dragged me into sleep before the thought could settle.

  
When I awoke, hours had passed. Yet the fatigue remained inside my bones like sickness. Still, I had to find Lenora and Silas.

  
In a world where even the sky had turned against mankind, they were the only two souls whose existence still mattered to me.

  
I walked beneath the endless night and caught my reflection in a puddle darkened by ash and rain.

  
For a moment, I barely recognized myself. *How had I become so thin?*

  
My cheeks had hollowed against my skull, and my eyes looked sunken, like those of a man already halfway buried. I brushed trembling fingers against my face and realized I no longer knew how much time had truly passed since I truly saw myself.

  
I wore armor discarded outside the smithies — dented iron still stained with someone else’s blood — and carried a sword bought with a single gold coin I had taken from a corpse along the shore.

  
And despite everything—I had missed this feeling. The weight of steel at my side. The hunt.  
Being a mercenary had once been the closest thing to purpose I had ever known. Somewhere deep within me still lived that same hunger carried by all warriors who chase gold and glory through ruined worlds.

  
After all—I was still human.

  
The plagued that wandered the outer streets fell beneath my blade one after another, and eventually I carved a path into the city of Vaelthune.

  
Its twin cathedrals towered over the city like dying giants, their ancient stone walls consumed by enormous tumor-like growths that pulsed slowly beneath the rain. Rot spread across the towers in thick veins of flesh, as though the city itself had begun mutating into something alive.

  
The old scriptures claimed Vaelthune was built upon the very ground where the twin gods, Vaelor and Thuneth, first descended during the Dark Epoch.

  
It was said they protected mankind when the gates of hell opened and the Outer Gods declared war upon creation itself, choosing Earth as their battlefield.

  
Looking upon the ruined city now, I could not tell whether the gods had abandoned humanity long ago… or whether they had died here with the rest of us.

  
Between the twin cathedrals lay a massive shattered stone monument. The stone looked identical to the black material monument in the city. A name was etched at the bottom of the monument.

  
*Lucien Morvath.* I knew the name of the scholar about whom I had read and heard countless times when I was a child. He was the man who first discovered the veins beneath the earth.  
There was something else carved into the monument.

  
Though much of it had been broken away, one section still stood upright beneath the rain, the words etched deep into the black stone:

*Fear not the poison that darkens the blood,*  
*but the light that teaches men where to drink.*  
*For desperate dreams rot the soul first,*  
*and from that rot the plague is born.*  
*Yet those whose dreams remain uncorrupted by greed or fear*  
*shall rise again beyond death’s reach.*  
*The dead who still dream purely shall inherit the night,*  
*and deliver judgment upon the false gods wearing the masks of saints.*  
*All gods wear mercy upon their faces.*  
*Only a few dare speak their true names.*

I moved my fingers across the carvings. The moment my skin touched the stone, a terrible familiarity crawled through me. It felt as though I had heard those exact words before—not with my ears, but somewhere deep within my mind, buried beneath memories I could no longer reach.

  
I tried to think deeper.

  
Pain answered immediately.

  
Something surged through my skull like a blade driven behind my eyes, and the wound on my hand began to pulse violently beneath the cloth wrapped around it. The sensation spread through my arm in waves, each pulse carrying with it a low humming sound that did not belong to this world.

  
It was almost like singing.  
A song without words.

  
I collapsed onto my back, writhing against the cold stone as agony tore through my body. The pain was so unbearable that, for a moment, I wanted nothing more than death itself. Even the thought of Lenora and Silas vanished beneath it, drowned beneath the instinctive need for the suffering to end.

  
In that moment, I truly believed the plague had finally entered my body.  
That it had reached something deep inside me.

  
But just as suddenly as it began—the pain receded.  
Leaving only silence.  
And fear.

  
Days passed afterward in a blur of blood and exhaustion. I fought through the plagued streets, searched the cathedrals, questioned mercenaries and hunters, yet all my efforts led nowhere.

  
I found neither Lenora nor Silas.

  
I had dreamt for so long of embracing the warmth of my beloved once more, yet that dream now felt impossibly distant, like a memory belonging to another man.

  
Sleep is the most innocent creature there is, and a sleepless man the most guilty.

  
I did not know why those words clung so tightly to my thoughts. Sleep no longer came peacefully to me. Every time my eyes closed, it felt as though death itself stood patiently beside me, waiting for the world to sink a little deeper into darkness before finally claiming what remained of my soul. Sometimes I wondered if death would truly bring peace, or if this cursed world would simply reshape me into another cruel thing wandering beneath the endless night.

  
“Goddess of Light,” I whispered, closing my eyes beneath the cold rain, “please grant me the strength to find them.” My voice trembled.

  
“Mother… if only I had possessed that strength before. Perhaps I could have saved you too.”  
And somehow—my prayers did not go unanswered.

  
I found them. I found Lenora and Silas.

*For desperate dreams rot the soul first,*  
*and from that rot the plague is born.*

The words echoed deep inside my heart the moment I saw them.

  
I fell to my knees before their remains.

  
There was almost nothing left. Only bones wrapped in rotting cloth, collapsed together against the stone like abandoned relics. Yet I knew immediately who they were. The rings upon their fingers remained untouched — simple iron bands worn smooth by time. No thief had bothered taking them.

  
I wanted to cry. But the sky wept in my place. It had been weeping for mankind ever since the endless night began. What I could not understand was this—their bodies looked ancient. Not recently dead.

  
The bones were brittle and crumbling, as though they had been lying there for years beneath the rain and darkness. Even the fabric clinging to them looked impossibly aged.  
My wound suddenly throbbed again. This time the pain spread through my entire body, crawling through my veins like fire beneath the skin.

  
“O Goddess of Light…” I whispered through clenched teeth. “What is this nightmare? Is there truly no mercy left in this world? Do our words mean nothing to you? Are our prayers hollow?”

  
But no answer came.

  
Only the distant cries of the plagued wandering through the streets beyond the cathedral district.

  
Some of them approached me slowly through the fog — twisted human shapes breathing through broken lungs and rotting mouths.

  
Yet they did not attack. They only stared at me. And in their eyes, I saw something far more terrible than hunger.

  
Pain.

  
Pain and rage so deep that even the plague itself had failed to erase it before they finally turned away and disappeared back into the endless night.

*All gods wear mercy upon their faces.*  
*Only a few dare speak their true names.*

I cried beneath the endless night, calling upon the Goddess for mercy. But mercy was never granted.

  
The ground suddenly split open beneath me with a wet, shuddering sound. Another throat tunnel emerged from the earth like a living wound tearing itself apart beneath the cathedral ruins. I stumbled back trembling, my body burning with pain as the tunnel breathed beneath me.

  
I did not know whether the world intended to devour me or judge me.

  
But in that moment—I gave up.

  
I gave up on myself, on the dream that had carried me across the Great Sea, through blood, rot, and endless darkness. Lenora and Silas were gone, and with them died the last purpose my soul still clung to. My desperation ended beside their bones, and for the first time since this nightmare began, I accepted the emptiness inside me.

  
That was when the voice came from the throat tunnel.  
“You are not the one.”

  
The words echoed not through the air, but through my bones.

  
“The one who forsakes his dream in the depths of despair cannot bring light into this world. You were meant to be, yet you are not. The world requires dreamers. Within the dream the world now dreams, you are no savior. The Eye sees all, and the gods await the coming dark.”  
Then the throat tunnel closed and silence followed.

  
My wound began throbbing violently beneath the cloth around my hand. I watched in horror as clusters of tumors spread rapidly across my arm, pulsing beneath the skin like living parasites. Flesh twisted. Veins blackened. Bones cracked softly beneath the growth.

  
And in that moment, my fate was sealed.

  
I became the very thing I had spent my life despising.

  
What filled me then was not fear but jealousy.

  
Lenora and Silas had died human.

  
But I remained behind in this cruel world, reshaped into something cursed and wandering beneath a sky that no longer belonged to mankind. No light waited for me. No mercy. Only the endless night and the distant gaze of the Great Eye watching from above.

  
As I wandered through the ruins of Vaelthune, through streets once filled with prayers, dreams, and human voices, I found myself wondering about sleep.

  
What did dreams truly feel like?

  
Were they peaceful?

  
Would I ever sleep again?

  
And in the end, I think I finally understood.

  
I had never dreamt of glory, gold, or salvation.

  
I had only dreamt of a quiet life.

  
A life where Lenora still smiled beside me, Silas still breathed beneath the same roof, and where sleep came gently without fear waiting behind my eyes.
