The first time I heard it, I was sitting on the edge of my bathtub with the light off.

It was late enough that the apartment had settled into quiet. I wasn't doing anything unusual. I was just sitting there. Waiting for the tight feeling in my chest to finish burning itself out. When it finally eased, a voice come from behind me.

"Thank you".

I didn't jump. The voice didn't startle me. It fit the moment too perfectly, like it had been waiting for the same thing I was. I stayed still and just listened. Nothing else followed. No breath. No footsteps. Just the words. Thank you.

I told myself it came from another apartment. Thin walls. Open vents. Maybe a neighbor on a phone call. It was easy to believe.

It happened again three nights later.

This time I was in my bedroom, sitting on the floor with my back against the bed. My phone screen had gone dark in my hand, and I hadn't noticed. When the heaviness finally drained out of my arms, when the quiet numbness slid in and made everything feel manageable again, the voice spoke from the corner near my closet.

"Thank you".

Same tone. Not cheerful, but gentle. Like a favor had just been completed.

I stood up too fast and made myself dizzy. The corner was empty. The closet door was shut. No sound came from the hallway.

I started keeping track without meaning to. Just in my head. It never interrupted anything. It never spoke while I was spiraling. It never spoke while I was crying. It always waited until the moment passed. Until the air in my body felt different. Then it would thank me.

The voice never said my name. It never said anything else.

After a while, I realized something else. It was always close. If I was in the bathroom, it came from the doorway. If I was in the kitchen, it came from behind the refrigerator. If I was on the couch, it came from the short stretch of the hallway just out of sight. It followed distance, not location, like it didn't belong to any room, only to the space near me.

I tested it by accident.

One afternoon, instead of sitting on the floor like I usually did, I called my sister. We talked about nothing important. A show she was watching. A job application I hadn't heard back about yet. The knot in my chest loosened slowly instead of snapping apart. It felt different, quieter. When the call ended, I waited. Nothing happened. No voice.

That night, I sat in the dark for a long time. The voice didn't come. I felt stupid for being disappointed.

The next day, I went outside. It was cold and bright. Uncomfortable in the way that reminds you that your body is still very involved in your life. I walked until my legs started to burn a little. When i came back and shut the door behind me, my apartment felt wrong, just emptied. Like a sound had stopped before I arrived and hadn't started again.

That evening passed without the voice.

So did the next.

On the third night, I woke up because I could hear breathing. Slow, even, and patient. It wasn't coming from my chest, but the space beside my bed. I held my breath to be sure. The breathing continued. When I finally shifted under the blanket, the breathing stopped. A long moment passed. Then, very quietly,

"Thank you".

The words sounded thinner than before, like it had taken effort to speak.

I didn't sleep after that. I tried to go back to the way things had bed. I rearranged my evenings. I filled my time with noise. Music, videos, other people's voices. I made plans and forced myself to keep them. The voice faded, but not completely. Rather, it moved farther way. When It did come, it felt like it had traveled to reach me. From the end of the hallway. From the far side of the kitchen. From somewhere behind a wall. Still close, just not close enough. I thought that would be enough. I was wrong.

The next time I heard it clearly, it was right behind my shoulder. I hadn't realized how much I missed the quiet until it returned. The familiar stillness. The familiar release. The voice came immediately. Warm again, full again. So close I could feel the shape of it against my ear.

"Thank you".

I flinched. Not because I was scared, but because the relief had already started to fade.

After that night , the timing changed. The voice began to arrive sooner. Not by much. Seconds at first. The minutes. As if it was learning, or adjusting. Sometimes it spoke when I still felt hollow instead of calm. The words would hang in the air between us.

Once, very late, very quietly, it said something different. Not the word I was used to. Not even something clearly formed. Just a small sound. A breath pushed through a narrow space. A strained, unfinished attempt at a word. I sat on the edge of my bed and waited for the rest of it. Nothing came.

Now, when the apartment goes still, I can feel it somewhere. Just waiting, the same way I used to. The voice still thanks me, but sometimes, when the moment doesn't last as long as it used to, I hear something underneath the word. Not anger, something closer to fear, and I can't tell if it's mine
