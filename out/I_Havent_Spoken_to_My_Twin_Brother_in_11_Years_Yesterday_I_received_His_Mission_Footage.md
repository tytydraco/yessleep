The last time we spoke was our mother’s funeral. We weren’t even in the same room. “I’m sorry, Tim,” he said, the line breaking up. “I can’t make it. Tell Mom I love her.” 

“Tell her yourself.”

That was the last thing I ever said to my twin brother.

Since then, I’ve moved three times. Out of our childhood home in Jersey. Into Manhattan. Then Brooklyn with a girlfriend that didn’t last. Now I’m in Dallas, practicing civil rights litigation and pretending distance counts as closure.

We haven’t spoken in eleven years.

I don’t know who left the envelope. No postage. No return address. Just a plain manila envelope. When I asked my assistant who signed for it, she shrugged.

I dumped the contents onto my desk. Inside was a USB drive wrapped in a strip of scotch tape. Written across it in black marker:

CHARLIE\_CAM.

I didn’t know what my brother had done after he left the military. I assumed he’d follow in our old man’s footsteps. Jarhead turned cop. After watching some of the footage, I guess I have my answer now.  

I know the name of the company that hired them. It’s mentioned once during the pre-entry briefing. Aperture Seven Group. 

I’ve searched every business registry I have access to. State filings. Federal contractor databases. Corporate records. Civil court records. Nothing. No LLC registration. No procurement history. No dissolved entity. No shell subsidiaries. If it exists, I haven’t been able to locate any record of it. 

That’s why I’m posting this. 

I have names. I don’t know whether they’re real or aliases. If any of them sound familiar, message me. I’ve transcribed it as best I can. 

Perks of being a civil rights attorney, I guess. Years of depositions and body cam review.
