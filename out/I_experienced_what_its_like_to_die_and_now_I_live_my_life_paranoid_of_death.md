verything was so sudden, like the flash of a moment stretched on for miles, and yet instant. I never had a chance to react as the car came careening down the highway on the wrong side. I didn’t have a chance to truly process as my body was flown through the air. I could not have stopped myself as my body lay broken on the asphalt. But I felt it, all of it, every moment. I felt it as the impact smashed my body. I felt it as my bones shattered and splintered into my muscles. I felt it as the force tore my seatbelt away and sent me through the glass. I felt it as my body collide with the ground, pulverizing my already mangled frame. I felt it, as I lay there breathing my very last breath before the ambulance could arrive.

Cold. The best way I could describe the feeling that followed, is cold. The pain never left me, but the warmth did, trying to put my thoughts in order to make sense of everything that, in the span of mere moments, had just occurred. When the paramedics came to my body, I tried to talk, to move, to do anything really. Nothing. Not even a sound. Only pain, and cold. It’s a horrible feeling when you’re pronounced dead and still watching, feeling, sensing everything unfurling. The best thing I can liken it to, is sleep paralysis. You’re there, at least, you think you are, you feel everything, see everything, smell and taste everything. You are a prisoner inside your own body, but that at least comes to an end at some point.  
  
Everything after that is almost a blur in my mind. Eventually the pain subsided at least some, the taste of blood went away, but oh how I wish that was where the horror ended. I kept expecting to fly away, to move on to the afterlife I was promised, heaven or hell. Instead, I was trapped in the remains of a corpse, forced to watch my own funeral, closed casket, meaning I could only hear as my wife, mother, kids, all wept for me.  
  
The true horror however, is what comes after that. Being claustrophobic, the concept of being buried alive has always been one that strikes the deepest of fears in my heart. As my body was lowered into the ground, I wanted to scream, to cry, to kick, to do anything for them to see I was still alive, but nothing. I heard every shovel of dirt as it fell upon my tomb. I smelled the wetness of the soil. I tasted the dirt in the air.

Every second felt like an eternity, or was it an eternity passing in seconds? I could feel as the wood of the coffin began to rot and decay, and soon, my body followed. It started with my eyes, drying and withering until nothing remained, a pain like none you could imagine. Then, my skin began to decay, the moisture leaving me. The smell was horrid, the taste was worse, and the pain was beyond any I had ever felt. I felt every moment, as the bugs chewed and burrowed into my withering flesh, as they devoured me, making homes in my body. I felt as they lay their eggs which hatched and writhed their way free, only to start the cycle over and over.  
  
To exist in that state for years, decades, centuries and so on, to be in an eternal state of horror, agony, and isolation. I was raised catholic, lived my life with as much virtue as I could. I tried to always do the best I could for the ones I loved, to give support. I had my vices, but nothing damning. So why? Why was it that after years of living a life of selflessness was I left to this hell. Hell. No, an eternity of fire and brimstone would be preferable over this. This was beyond hell, beyond anything I had been promised, beyond anything I could imagine. I wanted to puke, but without a stomach it was impossible. I wanted to cry, but with what eyes. I wanted to laugh and scream, but with what mouth.  
  
I felt every nano-instant of every microsecond of every moment, from the top of my head to the tip of my toes, I felt everything. I know what it was real, at least in some sense, even as I woke up a week after the accident in the hospital. As my consciousness returned, a wave of smoldering bile rose from within me, followed by sobbing. I refused to talk for days after that, to eat, to do anything. Perhaps it’s ironic that I became skin and bones, the hospital having to keep me on an IV, even after I relented and tried to eat only to puke it up. Psychological trauma, something that took me a long time to overcome.

But my life never went back to normal after that. I lost my job, my wife left me, took the kids with her. I became paranoid, too scared to leave the house, too scared to do much of anything. I won't eat anything solid for fear of choking, I put cushions on all the walls and floors for fear of tripping and hitting my head, I even wear a mask for fear of getting sick. Every day I live my life a paranoid wreck, and every night I relive my experience over and over again. I’ve reached out to specialists a few times now, but what if they're actually psychotic killers?  
  
Today I got a letter from the bank that my house is being foreclosed on, so soon I’ll lose my last Bastian of safety. I want this nightmare to end, but what scares me most, what I fear more than anything now, is death.
