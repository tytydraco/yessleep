My job involves moderating emergency calls. Not dispatch – but moderation.

People happen to dial 911 all the time and then, they either stay silent, scream nonsense or resort to playing audio files instead of speaking to support personnel. My job was to decide whether it was a prank, a glitch, or something that needed escalation.

So basically my team members and I would sit down and listen to 100’s of calls no one else wanted. The job was simple enough.

However, there was only one rule drilled into us, which we had to follow ever since our training days.

**Never listen to the same call more than once.**

When we asked why, we got no answer— only told to follow the rule without question.

So, on this quiet Tuesday around 3:00 AM, a call came in and beeped on my screen. It shouldn’t have been there—the system flags silent calls automatically—but this one had slipped through manually, marked **“Audio Attachment – Priority Red.”**

There was no transcript. No metadata. Just a file name:

**LISTEN\_ONLY\_ONCE.wav**

I should have sent it straight to escalation. Instead, I plugged in my headset and clicked play.

It opened with barely-there noise—the hush you only catch when you're listening too intently. Then footsteps: someone barefoot, hurriedly dragging across a squeaking floor.

A man suddenly spoke in a hushed voice into the microphone.

“I know you can hear this,” he said. “Please don’t skip ahead.”

I froze.

Calls weren’t supposed to acknowledge us. Nobody outside the company knew about our role as moderators.

“I don’t know how long they’ll let this stay in the system,” the man continued. “If you’re listening, that means I failed. Or I still have only moments left.”

 He took a moment, drawing in a slow, measured breath, as if steadying himself before continuing.

 “They said if I described them, you wouldn’t believe me,” he whispered. “So I won’t.”

A wet sound followed—like fingers dragging across plastic.

“I just need you to understand the timing,” he said quietly. “When the screaming starts, don’t take off your headset. That’s how they get curious.”

Then the audio went dead.

My heart was already racing in my chest, but standard protocol demanded I finish listening.

That was when the screaming started.

It didn’t erupt all at once—it swelled, like something distant being dragged closer. Multiple voices layered together, stretching, distorting, overlapping until I couldn’t tell how many mouths were screaming.

I tore the headset off, but the screaming didn’t stop.

It was now coming from my computer speakers and only fell silent a full minute later. I sat rooted at my station, shaking as I stared at the waveform on the screen.

Then my system logged an alert.

**File re-opened.**

I couldn’t believe my own eyes. I hadn’t touched anything. All I could think of at that moment was that I should’ve reported it.

So in all that panic, I tried to close the file.

The player froze—then rewound on its own.

And the same person began speaking again—but his voice sounded… strained now.

“You weren’t supposed to hear it twice,” he said. “Oh God! You listened twice!”

I smashed the power button. Monitor blacked out. But the audio droned on.

 “They’re going to think you’re involved now,” he said. “That you’re trying to understand them.”

Then the audio glitched again, skipping before resuming on its own. Only this time, I could hear my own breathing embedded in the recording.

And then suddenly it stopped followed by complete silence, with the exception of my heart hammering in my chest.

I didn’t sleep that night.

 The next day, my supervisor stopped me in the hallway.

“You accessed a restricted file,” she said, her voice level and cold.

 “It played itself,” I protested. “It—reopened.”

 She stared at me in silence. 

“How much did you hear?” she asked finally.

 “The whole thing,” I replied, as the color drained from her face.

“You weren’t supposed to finish,” she said.

 Before I could speak, she cut in. “Go home. Lock your doors. Ignore calls from unknown numbers.”

 “Why?” I asked. She hesitated.

 “Because once you finish listening,” she said, “they start listening back.”

The first call came within minutes of me reaching home. I hadn’t even fully locked the house down, when my phone lit up with an unknown number. I let it ring, unanswered.

Then the landline rang. Before I had time to react, it rolled straight to voicemail—and began playing.

My heart seized as a familiar voice filled the speakers. Mine.

 “You shouldn’t have stopped it. Now they don’t know where to put you.” my voice croaked.

A low rumble swelled beneath the words—like something massive breathing nearby, listening in on the line.

 “They keep asking where the rest of the call went!!”

The voicemail ended.

This time my cellphone rang again and I couldn’t help but pick it.

“They’re outside now!!” my voice shrieked as the call went dead.

A moment later I heard a loud knock – not on my door, but the wall adjacent to me. Like as if something was trying to come out from the inside. Soon the knocks became more frantic and began spreading to the other walls as well.

I backed into the center of my apartment, heart racing, every sense screaming danger.

My laptop turned on by itself. No startup chime or delay, just the emergency system loading and notifying me of a waiting call.

**CALLER STATUS: LIVE**

The waveform moved in sharp, erratic spikes. No audio followed.  
Instead, words began typing themselves across the screen.

And I saw the words “**WE FOUND THE REST OF IT**” automatically type themselves on the screen.

The speakers suddenly roared to life. The screaming returned—closer than ever—but what truly made my skin crawl was recognizing my own voice buried among them.

When my phone rang again, it was my supervisor calling, and I picked up instantly.

“You have to let it play to the end,” she said, her voice tight. “You have to hear everything. That’s how it resets.”

 “What are they?” I yelled. She inhaled sharply.

“They’re what you get when the call doesn’t end,” she said. “When fear isn’t resolved. It just stays unfinished.

A deafening bang shook my apartment. The walls bowed inward—just enough to notice.

"They want witnesses," *she* pressed on. "Someone to carry the sound forward."

Another bang followed, this one harder and the plaster began to crack across the walls.

"Play the file!” my supervisor yelled. "All of it. Don't stop this time."

The laptop screen flickered.

**PLAYBACK RESUMED**

The man’s voice burst back, frantic and breaking.

“You didn’t listen,” he sobbed. “You stopped—and now they’re coming for you too.”

"They peel the sound off you first," he wailed. "Your breathing. Your voice. Your *name*. Everything."

A long, thin shadow slid under my bedroom door, stretching and listening as the audio suddenly sped up.

“You have to pass it on,” the man screamed. “That’s the rule! Someone has to hear it all!”

   
The walls next bowed and split, not shattering, but peeling back like living skin.

Figures slithered forward, joints twisting in ways that shouldn’t be, faces splitting into countless mouths.

All of them listening. All of them screaming. All of them coming for me.

I screamed too, every part of me surrendering to pure terror.

But I couldn’t hear my own voice, while the audio reached its final seconds.

The man whispered, “Thank you for finishing it.”

Then the file ended.

Everything went still, and I found myself at my desk again, headset on, ready for the morning shift.

As I looked at the monitor, A new file waited in the queue:

 **LISTEN\_ONLY\_ONCE.wav**

 My name glared back under Assigned Moderator.

I don’t remember volunteering. I don’t remember going home.

All I know is this. If a call ever warns you not to finish listening—

**STOP IMMEDIATELY**
