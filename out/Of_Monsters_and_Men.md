“Lennie, get a move on, the townsfolk are on our trail.”

The men had often been forced to flee towns in the dead of night, but this instance was a bit more… chaotic. Lennie had revealed his and George's dream to a bar patron, and, like most members of a sane society, she had recoiled, and called forth the other patrons of the town. Turns out most settlements don't want two cultists living in their midst. But, to each their own.

“I'm sorry, George,” Lennie said, “I was really excited about tonight.”

“I know buddy, I was too, but gotta keep our mouths shut. Okay?”

“Yes, George….”

George breathed in deeply. His legs ached from climbing through brush and branch, but both men could see the encroaching light of torches, and could hear the explosions of gunshots.

After an hour, no firelight was visible, and the moon brightly lit up their path. It was a soft crimson; the color George believed was needed to perform their cruel ritual. He wanted to make the men rich; to never have to work again, only to retire to a small piece of land in the middle of nowhere. But things were not so easy. Now, they needed to refind safety and cover. Which, illuminated by the pink moon, was on the next hill on their winding path.

The men arrived at the ranch in the dead of night, and slept in an unoccupied cabin on the far end of the property. After waking and having words with the owner, the two found food and protection for their grand plans. They just needed to wait a month, and to do good work.

A week had passed. George wiped the sweat away from his brow, his basket of tomatoes halfway full. Lennie had already filled his basket, and went back to get another. When noon came, the two men sat down and began their lunch.

“George, can you tell me the story again?” Lennie said.

“Of course, buddy.” George leaned back and smiled. “Around a hundred years ago, there was a wealthy man, so wealthy, he bought cities, counties, even states. He had mansions and villas in every corner of these United States. But before that, he was nothing. Not a penny to his name. Until,” George leaned in, “he met a beautiful woman. She had striking blonde hair, a supple, thin figure, and always wore a simple, elegant, red dress.

“People wondered how he and this woman even married; why would such a fantastical woman marry such a bum? But what they didn't know was that she was not of this world, and the only thing keeping her was the offerings of blood the man provided her. Until, one week, he forgot, and the offering of blood was made, forcefully, by him.

“And this was a story that happened throughout all of history; desperate men called upon the woman in the red dress, and to those who could please her, and fulfill her ambitions, were rewarded with riches more than any man could dream. But… those who did not follow her instructions damned their souls for all of eternity.”

Lennie smiled. “She's gonna make us rich, right, George?”

“Yup. She's gonna let you have your rabbit farm.”

Lennie giggled. He was a simple man. Although George's ambition was infinite wealth, all Lennie wanted was to stay by George's side, and raise rabbits alongside him. 

After another week, George and Lennie had received their first paycheck, and started to put action toward their grand plan. They purchased six white rabbits, which Lennie was to care for in the interim between then and the blood moon. He named them, and he loved them. George looked on with a deep sadness as Lennie played with his new friends.

“George,” Lennie asked, laying down with the largest rabbit in his large hands, “do we have to use these rabbits?”

George sighed. “Yes, Lenn; they gotta be white, and pure, and good. She won't take ‘em if they ain't.”

“Yeah, but like,” Lennie looked to George with a quiet desperation, “what about chickens? They're white, and they don't do nothing wrong, so why can't…”

“Lennie.” George bent down to the man's level. “We gotta do this. It'll be for us. You know that, right?”

Lennie looked away. “Yeah, George, I get it.”

After two more weeks, it was time for the blood moon. George took the rabbits away from Lennie as Lennie stood motionless in the corner of his bedroom. Each rabbit was asleep; unmoving and calm from the love of Lennie. 

George grabbed chalk, and drew his sigil on the ground; a large pentagram, adorned with symbols from another time, in another language. He didn't know exactly what it all meant, but Father Slim insisted on their meaning, and their importance. At each point of the pentagram, and the largest one in the center, George placed a sleeping rabbit. He took the knife out of its rune covered sheath, and prepared the ritual.

“For the grand goddess, calling upon her name…” George took the knife, and stabbed the first rabbit; it squealed as blood ripped forth from its white fur. Lennie recoiled.

“...we look upon her, and give her our blood…” he circled to the next rabbit, plunging the knife deeply. Lennie started to cry silent tears. 

“...for her crimson majesty must be satiated…” he rounded to the next rabbit, killing it with brutal efficiency. When he did, the room trembled ever so slightly. 

“...and with the cries, comes a rebirth…” piercing the fourth rabbit, its cries echoing in the room. Lennie had snot leaking out of his nose, his hands distressedly pulling at his hair. Once again, the room vibrated with more power.

“...so, with these gifts, we call unto you…” the knife sunk into the fifth rabbit. George entered the center of the pentagram as the room shook for the second to last time. Lennie was pacing back and forth out of the corner, sobbing. 

“...in her supreme brilliance, our Goddess, Cho'za-”

“NO!” Lennie cried, running into the pentagram, knocking past George and grabbing the large rabbit. “Please, George, please, no, you can't do it…”

“Lennie! Get the fuck out of the circle! Are you mad?!” George tried to push Lennie out of the way, but he sat unrelenting. 

“No! You can't hurt him! You can't hurt him!” he sobbed. Lennie started to aggressively pet the rabbit, which was now very awake and very panicked. It tried to squirm out of Lennie's hands and run away to safety, but the large man held it forcefully and tenderly.

“Lennie, please, you're going to mess everything up, this isn't safe, please, Lenn…”

“No! No! No!” Lennie screamed, his hands moving faster and rougher against the frightened rabbit. Until, a faint snap was heard, and a weakened gasp came from the rabbit. It lay limp in Lennie's arms as both men went silent. Blood started to cover the white fur around the rabbit's neck. George fell to his knees. 

“I'm… I'm sorry, George. I… I didn't…” Lennie silently sobbed, the tears coating the corpse's fur.

“It's okay, buddy,” George sighed and stood up. “There's always next time, right?” He smiled weakly at his friend.

Lennie wiped the snot away from his nose. “Y-yeah.” Both men got up, cleaned up the site of the ritual, and got into their beds. It took both men far longer than most nights, George with his head full of racing thoughts, Lennie still clutching the corpse of the dead rabbit. 

When George awoke, Lennie was still sleeping. He got up, dressed himself, and began to make breakfast for the two men. When Lennie finally wandered out of the bedroom, the eggs were just about ready.

“Hey… George…” Lennie said quietly.

“Hey buddy, listen, Lennie, don't sweat it.” George patted his back. “We all make mistakes, we just gotta learn from ‘em. Like Father Slim used to say, ‘Man…’” 

“I saw her, George.”

George went silent. “Saw… who, Lennie?”

“The woman in the red dress.”

George felt the room spin. He weakly grabbed the chair next to him, and sat down. “Lennie…” he looked at his friend with desperate eyes. “What did you see?”

“I saw the lady… she was far away, but she was really pretty. Her hair was just like you said…” Lennie looked softly into space for a bit. “and she said she wanted me. And it felt warm.”

“What did you say?” George yelled. He got up and grabbed the man's large shoulders. “The fuck did you say?!”

“I… I didn't say nothing. She was scary.” Lennie's eyes started to make tears. “You ain't… you ain't mad at me, right George?”

“No, no…” George released him, and went to the exit. “Just… join me when you're done eating.”

The two men tilled the land in silence. Normally George would talk to some of the other farmhands, or Lennie would ramble about some part of his rabbit farm, but both men couldn't think of anything to say to each other. At the end of the day, they both curled back into bed. 

When George awoke, he saw Lennie sitting on the edge of his bed, staring at his knees.

“What's going on, buddy?” George asked. 

“She… she got closer.” Lennie blankly stated. “We were in the hills by the farm, and she was closer to me. She still was pretty, but…” Lennie's eyes closed. “There was something wrong with her.”

“Like… like what, Lennie?”

“Her dress… I couldn't see her legs. But it was… squirming. Like when you lift up a rock and there's a pile of worms. I didn't want her dress to lift up, George.”

George held the man tenderly. Both men got up, ate, and worked. It was hard work, harder with what was happening, but it was a welcome distraction to both men. Lennie wholeheartedly focused on his tasks; George kept looking desperately at Lennie.

When they were done at work, George got a bottle of beer, and drank its contents while Lennie helped himself to both men's meals. George was finding it harder to eat. When they finally got in bed, George stayed up longer than Lennie; he was finding it difficult to sleep, his bloodshot eyes staring at his friend for hours. Rest found George unexpectedly, until it was broken by Lennie shaking him awake.

“George! George! George! She got closer!” Lennie cried into George's bedsheets. “She's closer to me! I can see her now!”

“What… what… calm down, Lenn, what's going on?”

“We were on the hill! She was so close! She ain't pretty!” Lennie started to hyperventilate. “She… she wants me to go there, but I don't wanna! I don't wanna, George! Please don't make me! Please!”

George sighed understandingly. “Well, buddy,” he chuckled. “The only way we can fix this is to go to the hill. You know that, right?”

Lennie stopped sobbing. “And… and that'll stop it? Right?”

George clasped him. “Yup. We gotta go, now.” Both men got into their work clothes. Lennie grabbed the dead rabbit. George grabbed his gun.

The two men walked past the field, walked past the owner drinking his morning coffee, and into the hills off the side of the property. George followed Lennie, past wild flowers and thorny plants, until they got to a clearing.

“Here… here I was.” Lennie said quietly. 

“And… you're sure you saw her here, right?”

“Yes, George.” Tears formed in Lennie's eyes. “What do I gotta do?” 

“Just gotta give her the rabbit,” George said, taking out his ritual knife and cutting a large gash into his own hand and squeezing it, letting the blood fall to the ground.

A radiance glowed from beyond the trees. It got slowly brighter and brighter as it approached the men. George couldn't see it, but Lennie could. 

“George, I see her…” Lennie cried. “She's so angry. Her hair is touching everything. And her hands are burning the trees.” 

George took the revolver out of his waistband. 

“George, please, I don't like this… I can smell her… George, please, she's gonna take me, she's gonna take me George, please, please…” He turned around to see the revolver in George's hand, and smiled desperately.

“This can't help you.” George said weakly. Lennie's face fell.

“Turn around.” George said. “Imagine our rabbit farm. Imagine all those rambunctious lil creatures. And they all belong to you. You wanna go to that rabbit farm, right?” 

Lennie nodded, his eyes fully consumed by the radiance before him.

“Grab her hand, and she'll take you there.”

“Promise?”

George smiled weakly. “I promise.” 

 Lennie, unflinching from the light, and grabbed at… something. He walked into the woods, the light disappearing with him. When there was no more light, George fell to his knees, put the revolver in his mouth, and pulled the trigger.
