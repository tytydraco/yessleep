My mother kept every birthday card I ever gave her.

I didn't know that until after she died.

The hospital sent her belongings home in a cardboard box. Most of it sat unopened for months before I finally started sorting through it. Near the bottom was a bundle of birthday cards tied together with a faded ribbon. Every card I'd ever given her was there. Construction-paper cards from elementary school. Store-bought cards from my teenage years. Even the terrible handmade one I'd thrown together the night before her birthday when I realized I'd forgotten to buy a gift.

At first I was just reading through them for the memories. Then I noticed something strange. Inside every card, my mother had written the date she received it. Beneath the date was a number. The first card said 14. The next said 15. Then 16. The numbers kept increasing every year. What bothered me was that they were always one year ahead of my actual age. When I reached my eighteenth birthday card, the number written inside wasn't 18. It was 19. A few cards also contained short notes. "Still the same." "Voice unchanged." "Remembers the dog." They weren't messages to me. They looked like reminders she'd written for herself.

I called my aunt that night. The moment I mentioned the notes, she stopped talking. After a long silence, she asked whether I remembered getting lost during a family camping trip when I was six. I remembered the story because I'd heard it my entire life. I wandered into the woods. The adults panicked. Search teams were organized. A police officer showed up. A few hours later I walked back out. Everyone always treated it like a funny family story. My aunt didn't. She told me my mother never laughed about it. Then she said something that made me drive to her house the next morning. According to my aunt, my mother believed something followed me out of those woods.

She wouldn't explain further. I spent the next few weeks digging through old photographs and notebooks. That's when things started getting uncomfortable. My mother's notes weren't about me acting strangely. They were about things she claimed happened around me. One entry described hearing footsteps outside my bedroom window every night for a month. Another described finding muddy footprints on the ceiling of our back porch after a thunderstorm. The most disturbing entries all mentioned the same thing. A tall figure standing just beyond the tree line whenever the family went outdoors. Nobody else ever reported seeing it. My mother wrote about it for years.

I eventually found the notebook she'd been using during the months after the camping trip. Most of the pages were ordinary. Grocery lists. Phone numbers. Appointment reminders. Then the writing changed. She described seeing someone standing between the trees while search teams looked for me. At first she thought it was another volunteer. Then she realized it never moved. According to her, it remained in exactly the same spot for hours. The final entry about the woods was written the day after I came home. She claimed she saw the same figure standing behind our house that night. She wrote that it stayed there until sunrise.

The last birthday card in the box was dated six months before she died. The number written beneath the date was 32 even though I was only 31. Underneath it she'd written a final note. "Still here." I spent a long time trying to figure out what she meant. Last week I found a photograph taken during that camping trip. I'd never seen it before. The picture shows a group of volunteers searching the woods. My mother had circled something near the edge of the frame. At first I thought it was a tree. Then I zoomed in. Someone was standing between the trunks, watching the search. The timestamp on the photo was two hours before I was found. What bothers me isn't that the figure is there. What bothers me is that it's wearing the same jacket I was wearing when I walked out of the woods.
