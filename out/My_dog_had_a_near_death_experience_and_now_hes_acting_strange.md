Stubbs wasn’t always Stubbs. Originally, his legal name was Crisp P. Tater, but then one fateful winter morning, ole Tater got a little too riled up by the mail truck going down the street and decided it was time for a tussle. Saw that thing coming and jerked the leash right out of my hand. He was usually  such a docile fella it caught me completely off guard. I tried to chase him down, but I couldn’t stop him in time.

Now I probably don’t need to tell you this, but an eighty pound golden retriever and a one ton Grumman are two very different weight classes. Tater lost that fight. The big idiot ran smack in front of that truck and got chucked right off the hood like a sack of, well, taters. Tore his back legs up real good.  I may not have been able to stop my buddy from getting hit, but I for damn sure wasn’t going to let him die in the road. I scooped him up and hauled his fat ass to the emergency vet as quickly as I could. It was touch and go for a bit, the hit had sent the poor fella into shock, but the doctors got him the oxygen he needed and he pulled through.

Unfortunately, Tater’s hind legs were just too mangled to be saved, and he had to have a partial amputation on each one. Animal prosthetics have come a long way though and the clinic set him up with some nice new hardware that would have him trotting along almost as good as new. It was a rough ordeal, but  when all was said and done, Tater got off pretty easy. Before long he was back home with a healthy respect for traffic and a new nickname: Stubbs.

Stubbs got back to his happy go lucky self fairly quickly all things considered. Sure, it took us a couple weeks of practice to get him adjust to the prosthetics. Ole boy was pissed as hell that he couldn’t pile on the sofa as easy as he used to, but I worked with him daily and, little by little, things got easier. It wasn’t too long before he had mastered them well enough for us to go on about our business as usual.

Here’s where things get a bit strange, so just bear with me, okay? Yall have probably picked up  on it from my writing, but if not,  I’m what most people would call a good ole boy. Born and raised by a couple of poor farmers out in the sticks where the summer’s too hot and the winter’s too cold. My parents were practical people. There were too many real  problems in life for there to ever be talk of ghosts or goblins in their household. So the idea that an animal might have a soul was not something I had given much weight to. Well Stubbs changed that.  My best buddy had himself a bonafide near death experience in that operating room, and I think when those doctors pulled him off death's door, he brought something back with him.

It was just little things at first. Stubbs has never had very much of an attention span, but a few weeks after the accident I started to notice him…noticing things. Sometimes he would be dozing next to me on the couch and he would just perk up out of nowhere, fully alert. He would sit there scanning the room for who knows what then go back to sleep. One day I was watching TV while Stubbs happily slung around one of his chew toys. He was throwing it in the air and catching it, not a care in the world, then all of a sudden he just froze  up. The toy hit the ground and rolled away, now the furthest thing from his mind. I called his name trying to get his attention, but he  just stood there for several minutes, locked in place staring off into the other room.

These episodes sporadically became a part of our everyday life. When I would take him for walks in the park, there were times that I would almost trip over him because he up and stopped in the middle of the path like he hit a brick wall. Again just staring off into some nearby woods. All the coaxing and calling in the world  wouldn’t get him moving again until he was done.

Then, things started to escalate.

The first major incident was at breakfast. I had taken Stubbs with me for a walk downtown and it was such a nice day that I decided we would have ourselves a little meal on the patio of the local diner. I had gotten myself a nice plate of eggs and hash, with an extra order of bacon on the side for Stubbs. He was enjoying a piece when the waitress walked out to refill my coffee. That piece of bacon fell right out of his mouth as she approached. His eyes locked right  onto that waitress, boring holes into her. Stubbs started to let out a low growl, something I had never heard before. I had to assure the young lady he wouldn’t bite, and to his credit, Stubbs didn’t make me a liar,  but it might have been better for her if he had. When she started to walk away, that coffee pot in her hand started to quiver, the liquid inside bubbled and hissed like it had been put back on a burner. Then the whole damn pot exploded in her hand. Poor girl got a face full of scalding coffee and glass. I called an ambulance and watched over her best I could until the paramedics arrived.

The whole thing spooked me pretty bad, but I turned a blind eye to it. I convinced myself that the situation was just an unfortunate mishap that had nothing to do with my buddy Stubbs. Ignorance is bliss, they say. Well, the next time it happened, things turned out a lot worse.

I was taking Stubbs on a walk through the neighborhood when I saw  Mr. Jones across the street. The old man was out mowing his lawn and was making a cut along the front when Stubbs locked up and started howling his head off. This time I noticed something different.  Stubbs was trying his hardest to crane his neck and look away from the man, but he couldn’t manage. It was like something had him in a chokehold and was making him watch.  Stubbs kept staring and I looked over to see Mr. Jones clutch at  his chest and fall right off his riding mower. Now your typical lawnmower has several safety features built in to prevent fatal mishaps. That thing should have shut off the instant his ass left the seat, but he hit the ground and that mower swapped to reverse. It backed right over Mr. Jones as he lay helpless and chewed the old man to a pulp.  I still can’t get the image out of my head.

That brings us to our current predicament. A couple nights ago I woke up to Stubbs frozen on the bed standing over me,  staring me dead in the eyes, that  low growl emitting from his chest. I swear I could see the sadness behind those pupils as he struggled and twitched. Eventually he managed break away his gaze and he bolted out of the room.  Since that night he’s been avoiding me. He won’t come when I call and runs away if I try to go near him, but when we do pass by one another I can see those muscles in his neck tense as he does his damnedest to avoid eye contact. It warms my heart to see my best buddy fighting to keep me safe, but I don’t know how long we can keep this up. I obviously don’t want to die, but I also don’t want him to have to go on living like this. Can anyone out there help us?
