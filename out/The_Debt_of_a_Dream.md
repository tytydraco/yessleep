I remember thinking over it again and again for months. This small little child, my son, first born of my wife and myself, barely awake, not yet aware. I remember wondering if it could even recognize itself, feel its own presence, feel its own body. But I am getting ahead of myself, I think it would be better to start sooner, before my dream came true.

Ever since I first discovered the game of chess I had been completely obsessed with it. I payed it, studied it, even saw it in the world around me. Not just the boards or the pieces, but even in conversations, how people talked to one another, how things and events happened. It was as much of a part of my as my own two hands and to go without it would be insufferable.

  
But I never got to be where I wanted to be. I had to finish high school, then college, and after that to find work. I played, yes, here and there. Local tournaments, club games, online events, but I would never get anywhere. People were better than me, much better than me in some cases. I would throw away games, making moves that felt right without thinking them through, and, seconds after making my move, hitting the clock, would I see my mistake. I was an idiot, and, I suppose I am suffering for it now. 

  
Each night, first alone, and then next to my wife, I would dream of a world where I would not only be a titled player, a Grand Master, but one of the best, and eventually, the best. The World Chess Champion, undisputed! But I knew this would never come. Work, relationships, stress, no time, no money, no energy, all these things, even life itself got in the way of my dream. I would lay in bed and just think, if only I had an ability, a special eye, that would show me the correct move. Not just the best move that computers can come up with now, no, a truly correct move, from some divine or spiritual source from the beyond that would show me, without a doubt, how to crush my opponents, both human and machine. 

I could see myself sitting at those wooden tables, those premium boards and pieces shining in the camera lights. My moves being praised and broadcasted to the world, year after year each challenger being crushed by my systematic takeover of the board, both as white and black, in ways that would put Botvinnik's strategy and Tal's flare to shame. I wanted to be the best, I would do anything to be the best. 

Time passed, years, and of course my dream could not come true. I would find myself after work wondering the library and community centers of my town, where the local chess club was, seeing framed portraits of the best. I would walk through the rows of familiar chess books, puzzles, strategy, openings, even the mental state a player should strive for during a game. I had read them all. Yes I improved, my game got better, but I knew it was too late for me. The next generation of Grand Masters were already becoming better than me at the ages of 11 or 12. 

  
Moving past the games section, through philosophy, and atlases of geography, I came to the history section, far at the back of the library. I had never been to this section before, and it seemed to run farther back into the depths of the library than I would have thought. Mindlessly I worked my way down, looking and skimming uninterested through books and series. The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire... Metalworking in Ancient China... Early Civilizations of Mesopotamia... and on and on it went. I was about to turn back when a purple book, older but somehow more vibrant than the rest, caught my eye on the bottom of the last bookshelf. Pulling it out the book read in shining gold gilded letters: "Complete Works of the Zetra Empire and it People." 

  
Flipping the front cover open the book contained a small excerpt, at least I think it is an excerpt, in a language I was not familiar with. It read:

  
“Urbales dran kelsor, virnir daldul krenebimus. 

Satro Renozavis vud Satro Piki! 

Deboud velk nevenjamus noulamus.

Fen Fen Fen, Duf Iuper Hur Mesrin!”

\- Likner Zerzeronum Zetrarum

  
To this day I still do not know what it means, but I was relieved to turn the pages and find, in English, a table of contents. Skimming though it it appears to give a full account of the Zetra people, in the far off continent of Fevron, fleeing a great war and starting their own nation. Chapters later it discusses the creation of spacial type of gas called Heliotrace, 32x lighter than air and non-flammable, though extremely radio-active, and its use in fueling the great zeppelin "Zaar- Andalus" before the "Cracking of Mount Corlock." 

Crouching there I skimmed more through the expansive table of conetnts until I cam under a section headed: "The Mysticisms of the Belzine Order and the power of Bezron." It included a section discussing "The Transmutation of Life into Dream." And from there my life would never be the same.

I brought the book home, after checking it out from the librarian who claims to never have seen the book before, and while my family slept, I read. The passages showed images, sigils and diagrams, ways of making the "Dream Tablet" and how to perform the rites and rituals. The ritual I wanted, the one I needed to finally have my dream made real existed! It was in this book! But, as anyone would, I had my doubts. Nowhere online or in any databse could this "Zetra Empire" be found, nor anything it spoke of in the book. As a child I group up Catholic, and I had seen the great wonders of the church through a child's eyes looking up into the heights of a a cathedral. But as I grew, so too did my skepticism. Prayer had never worked for me. This book at least, though unproven, gave a small glimmer of hope to the dream that had been with my for over twenty years of my life, and so I read on. 

  
Night after night I read the book, the same chapters over and over again. I could see the ritual in my mind, the words to be chanted. And as I grew more and more familiar, my visions of the fame and the glory, the mastery over the board grew more and more vivid. Each night while I would lie in bed with my wife next to me and our new-born in the crib just feet away, my consciousness would be there, over the board, I could feel the pieces, here the crowd roar win after win. The book knew my dream and it could give it to me, I knew its price, and could give it to it. 

The ritual required a sacrifice, human, and close to you, all I had to do was turn my head and open my eyes to see what was close to me.

Would my son even know that he had been alive, would he even care? What cost would it to him to be living for just months and then to not be there ever again? Was it fair that I had the chance to live my dream that I had been suffering without fulfilling for all my life? I knew it was wrong deep down, but I kept rationalizing it to myself.  
  
He wouldn't even know he had lived.  
  
I am saving him from a life of hardships, dreams not fulfilled.  
  
New born children die all the time, it would not be too hard to cover up.  
  
We could always have another.  
  
I tossed and turned for a month thinking over what I had to do, what I needed to do. I started to prepare for the ritual, gathering the plants necessary, and I kept telling myself, I would just have it ready if I ever wanted to go through with it, just to be ready! I had order a willow tree's bark and, in secret, used my own blood to paint in those blocky, clunky letters that the book's instructions had told me, ordered me, to write. And all the while, while my gaze grew colder every time I looked at my son, I told myself that I just wanted to be ready. 

And then I was. Everything was prepared, and I had stopped seeing my son as the goal of my life, as my wife constantly insisted, I saw him for what he was. A pale, snotty, loud, obnoxious maggot that did not even know what life was. 

  
My wife was out for the day, and I was left at home with it. Children choked all the time. The little idiots put things and toys into their mouths and turned blue, there was nothing to it. It had been playing with its blocks and marbles when I took it and brought it to the garage. I placed it on the tablet, and in my eyes the letters began to glow. I picked up one of those ugly, heavy glass marbles that her mother had brought for it when it was born. I put it into the thing's mouth. I pushed, it choked, and I chanted again and again:

"Zetra Lindelem Telarans Velmaram Hovet" "Zetra Lindelem Telarans Velmaram Hovet" "Zetra Lindelem Telarans Velmaram Hovet" over and over again. And then it was over. He was lying there, arms prostrate, blue and limp. I had to move fast, grabbing him and bringing him back to the rug where he was playing. I burned the tablet in the yard and through the ashed into the collected foliage bags. I called 911, I waited.

I waited through the rushing, the screaming, the tears. Through arguing, and fighting, and questioning. Through an investigation I came out clean, less so through the divorce. I waited and waited. And now the time had come.

I had not tested out my dream, though it had been in my mind for months through after the night my son choked to death at play. But, after my wife took the house (and sold it), and I moved away to live in some cheap apartment with a room-mate, I was ready to play chess. I know it sounds insane, and it is, but what was all this for if not to fulfill my dream?

And so I did.

I began at a local tournament, and I remember that game perfectly. I had walked into the tournament room, a little late, and found my table. My opponent, white, had been waiting for me. And there I saw him as well. My son, on the table, with the chess board. I knew it could not be real, but I saw the board with its pieces, and my son, on the table, both at the same time. I stood their for a moment, shocked, but in a daze I sat down and started his clock. He reached out over my son's body, grabbed his King's pawn and moved it forward two places to leave it on the e4 square, on my son's chest. Immediately I saw a red line, cutting like a knife and leaving blood dripping behind appear over my Queen's pawn and pushing it forwards to d5, over my son's stomach. It was the correct move, but the blood started to spill out over his body and over the board. He started to shriek and cry, twisting in pain that could not know its source. His eyes opened and I stared into my son's eyes as they screamed at the person who brought him to life and now to this.  

I played the move and the red line faded though the cut remained. A small arrow like line, bleeding from my son. My opponent, unaware of the agony sat and thought and played his knight out. A new line, a new cut, appeared on my boy, a move, the correct move, and the screaming began as new blood spilled from his body. And so we played. 

I do not know how many games, how many months it took, but my name got out there. A rising star in chess in his 30's, unbelievable! Unheard of! But it happened, and game after game the tortured cries of my son filled my ears, and my ears alone, over the board. Each game with new scars on his little body, growing grayer by each play, with little lines and arrows in straight and L shaped lines covering his whole body. His cries grew older and decayed, gurgling over the lungs that had never truly taken in the air of life. His eyes, those eyes that used to watch me at home, turned pale like mirrors full of the moon and faded into blindness. But I rose and rose.

I was the best, I could not be beaten. I disproved every attempt to show I was cheating, nothing could be detected on my body before, during, or after any game. I could not be stopped. I remember my championship game, no not the moves, the incisions over the boy's chest and arms, but how quiet it was in the room. Or how quiet it was supposed to be. I was sitting on a stage over a table and board, looking into my son's useless un-dead eyes while my opponent thought over his move. I looked to my left and saw a whole stadium full of spectators, watching my moves, my amazing moves, in silence. Their praise would erupt only when my work was done. It should have been silent in there, but all I heard was the bubbles of blood through his wailing. 

I do not know when it became impossible to see or do, hear or think of anything else. Even not when playing I see him, and my guilt, the price debt that I can never repay to whatever gods ruled the Zetra Empire, became all I knew, all I saw, all I was. 

I remember thinking of Van Gogh when I decided to cut my own ears out. Had he also followed a ritual and just could not take the price for his mastery over the art of panting? But I had cut both of them out and still I heard him, saw him, my baby boy. I stabbed into my ear canals and was inches from cutting my eyes out when I broke down and collapsed to the floor. Was this worth it? Was it? Were my thoughts while I passed out, blood spraying and dripping from my head onto the bathroom floor. As my room-mate, rushed into the bathroom to find me, all I saw was a pool of my own blood, and brilliant tactical combinations playing out over the growing pool, a pawn sacrifice so a King could move forwards. 

Now I am sitting here, in the hospital, hearing that familiar cry from my son, just feet way. The news had a field day, "Chess Champion Gone Mad," or something along those lines. I hear nothing else, I see nothing else, I feel nothing. I fear I will never escape my dream, its price, something that I never should have been able to, been willing to, pay for. Even in death, where some say a better place is, I doubt I will be free.
