This comes as a warning for anyone living in or moving to Clarksville, Tennessee. Let me tell you how I learned why it’s never a good idea to explore the historic district at night.

A few years ago, when I lived in Clarksville—a loud military town and one of the oldest cities in Tennessee—I decided to visit my sister’s apartment complex on the edge of the historic district. She always cooked for me, always stocked soda and snacks, always made me feel welcome. She and I were best friends, and she knew my mother and I never really got along.

That night wasn’t supposed to be anything special. The walk from the bus depot wasn’t far. I took the last bus because my social anxiety was awful back then. All I could do was sit quietly, headphones in, waiting for the depot to appear. Thinking about my sister’s homemade nachos helped me relax. Eventually, I drifted off.

When I woke up, panic hit me like a punch. It was far darker than it should’ve been—summer evenings don’t fall that fast. My phone said 5:30 p.m., but when I blinked, the numbers shifted. 2200. Military time. Impossible. My head felt foggy, thick, like I’d been drugged. Before I could make sense of it, the bus driver’s voice boomed through the empty bus, telling me it was the last stop.

That snapped me out of it. I looked outside again. The light was normal.

“Must’ve been a dream,” I muttered, rubbing my eyes.

I hopped off the bus and headed toward the dollar store down the road, listening to the newest Red Hot Chili Peppers album. To this day, I can’t listen to Stadium Arcadium without feeling that same creeping dread.

I was about to cross the street when the church bells rang—six slow, heavy chimes. As the final bell echoed, my music distorted violently, shrieking in my ears. I ripped the earbuds out and shut my phone off.

That’s when I smelled it.

The foulest scent I’ve ever encountered—rust, stagnant water, and a sharp ammonia bite that made my eyes water. My stomach lurched. I swallowed hard, forcing the rising vomit back down.

When I looked up, the world had changed.

The same darkness from my dream had spilled into reality. Not night—absence. No stars. No streetlights. No cars. No people. Just a void swallowing the horizon.

Then I heard it: distant music, echoing like a party buried deep underground. The rhythm pulsed through the empty air, hypnotic and wrong. Before I realized it, my feet were moving toward the sound. I wasn’t choosing to walk. I was being pulled.

The closer I got, the more intoxicating the rhythm became. My thoughts blurred. My body felt like it wasn’t mine. I stumbled into a narrow back alley that hadn’t been there before, leading to what looked like a small nightclub glowing faintly in the dark.

The smell hit me again—strong enough to break the trance.

And that’s when I saw them.

Tall, pale creatures with no eyes. Just smooth skin where their faces should’ve been, except for thin lips stretched into wide, unnatural smiles. They stared at me—not into me, but at me, like I was something rare and fascinating.

The stench vanished instantly. One of them gestured for me to enter.

I didn’t want to. Every instinct screamed to run. But the dread of refusing was worse.

Inside, the club was impossibly large—bigger than any building could be. No walls. No ceiling. Just swirling lights twisting through drifting smoke. The air smelled like my sister’s street tacos.

How did this place know that?

Was this tailored for me? Did everyone smell something different?

I felt trapped. Like the choice to leave had been taken from me.

Grotesque, twisted creatures filled the endless space—each more horrifying than the last. Yet none of them paid attention to me. I kept my head down, moving quietly until I found an empty booth. I pulled my hoodie up, shoved my earbuds in, and tried to drown out the music.

But I could feel them watching. Waiting.

I don’t know how long I sat there—minutes, hours, days. Time didn’t exist in that place.

Then something tapped my shoulder.

I froze.

It slid into the booth across from me. I kept my eyes down, but its hand shot forward, grabbing mine. Razor‑sharp claws sank slowly into my skin. I winced, watching bloodless wounds open across my hand. When I finally looked up, it released me. The wounds sealed instantly, leaving no pain.

The creature laughed softly and told me to look around.

Hundreds of blood‑red eyes stared back at me from the darkness.

“W‑why am I here? What do you want?” I whispered.

The crowd erupted in laughter—except the thing in front of me. Now that I could see it clearly, it resembled the doorman: huge mouth, rows of sharp teeth, no eyes, no nose. Its skin was corpse‑pale and cold. Its breath reeked of ammonia and rotting flesh. It wore a bowler hat and a suit with a grey tie.

It suddenly roared, silencing the entire club. The sound made my skull vibrate. I clutched my ears, certain my head would burst.

When I looked back at it, its mouth stretched into a grin. Its voice echoed inside my mind.

“Don’t be frightened, child. You are a guest in my domain. I welcome you.”

A wave of calm washed over me—unwanted, unnatural. Familiar somehow.

I opened my mouth to speak, but it cut me off.

“No.”

“No what?” I asked.

Another toothy smile. “I know what you were going to ask. I can see your soul. Different, but readable. You were going to ask if I intend to harm you. Hence my answer.”

“So you can read minds?” I thought.

It chuckled. “No. I see many futures based on the souls in my domain. I see, hear, and know all that happens here. All pasts, presents, and futures. Infinite timelines twisting together. Stories I can read and change. But yours…” It pointed a long finger at me. “Yours is different. I cannot edit your story. I cannot see more than a few seconds ahead.”

“Who are you?” I asked.

“I am the owner and creator of this place. I am the Void. This is God’s blind spot. Sin, primal urges, and dark fantasies live here. Some call me Him. Others call me Sin. My name is He Who Brought the Dark. You, Tim, may call me Void.”

He paused, then tilted his head upward.

“You see, reader… yes, you. I know you’re watching this story unfold. Does it entertain you? Does it frighten you?”

I blinked in confusion. “That’s not my name. Who are you talking to?”

Void snapped his head back toward me. “Ah. I mix up my layers of fiction sometimes. My apologies. Now then… Tim, you’re in God’s blind spot. No one can harm you here but me—if I wished.”

“I need to leave,” I whispered. “My sister’s waiting.”

Void let out a booming laugh. “This place exists outside the concept of time and space. No time has passed. You may leave. I’ve gotten what I wanted from you. Warn people of this area. Their stories will come to me. I’m starving.”

He looked up into the endless dark. “Soon I will wreak havoc on the world above. Pray that I succeed. For I have seen the throne of God… and it was empty.”

Everything went black. His words echoed endlessly as I felt myself falling—down, down, into nothing.

Then my sister’s voice cut through the void, screaming my name.

“Thank God you’re awake! I’ve been worried sick! I was coming to meet you at the bus stop. You looked like you woke up, but you fell right back asleep when I called your name.”

I don’t remember much after that. My mind refuses to recall the rest of that weekend. It’s like something is blocking it.

I got home fine… but the thing I spoke to is still in my head. Watching. Waiting.

And I see you reading this.

Are you enjoying yourself?

Tim brought you to me. Your story will be delicious. I can taste your fear. I can smell your breath. I know you can see this.

Come closer. Look into the void.

But remember—when you stare into the void, the void stares back.

I am everywhere, in every axis of imagination. God has left you, child. And I am always watching, waiting for the next time you sleep.

Wait… what was I saying?

Oh, right.
