Yes, it sounds strange, very strange even, but I will try to explain everything from the very beginning.

I was born a strange child. Not “special,” not “gifted,” not the kind they later talk about at school meetings, just strange. I understood that when adults lowered their voices while speaking about me, and by the way other children quickly learned to avoid me.

It was hard for me to understand when to laugh, when to stay silent, when to make eye contact. Every conversation felt like an exam, but I never had any cheat sheets. Instead of making friends like everyone else in class, I would sit in my room for hours watching dust spin in a beam of sunlight, imagining they were tiny planets and I was a god who could destroy them just by clapping my hands.

In my head I created entire scenarios where I was a general leading an army, or a criminal running from justice. I lived the lives of my fictional characters for hours or even days.

My imagination worked too well. In reality, everything was much harder.

My father noticed it before anyone else. He was one of those men who rarely speak, but if they do, they say things that stick in your head for years. One evening, when I once again refused to go to someone’s birthday party, he sat down next to me on the bed and stayed silent for a long time.

— Listen, Mike… If it doesn’t work out in the real world, — he said carefully, as if he was afraid of something, — it doesn’t mean you have to spend all your time in it…

I sat quietly, feeling awkward, as my father paused again before continuing:

— Some people build houses. Some build cars. And some build worlds. That’s not bad, son. The main thing is that you feel comfortable.

I tried my hand at creativity, trying to put the thoughts and stories I invented in my head onto paper. I drew with pencils that almost instantly wore down to short stubs, the people came out slightly crooked, with somewhat incorrect proportions, as if they were old memories of people rather than people themselves. My short stories were too dark for children, but too naïve for adults. Teachers would say, “Interesting, but rough.”

My life was gray, not in a tragic sense, but in the most disappointing one. No dramas, no victories. Days folded into weeks, weeks into years, and it all looked like one long Tuesday. I worked, ate, slept, worked again. Sometimes I talked to people, feeling how words got stuck halfway out. My father died when I was 25 from brain cancer, two years later my mother died too, and that’s how I was left completely alone in the real world.

But as soon as I closed my eyes, everything changed.

In my imagination I had a different life. There, people understood me without many words. There, I knew what to say. There, people looked at me as if I truly existed. I could imagine having a home, warmth, late-night kitchen conversations with parents I had missed so much in childhood.

And every time I returned to reality, I felt relief strange, almost guilty like I was secretly taking painkillers no one knew about. In a way I was ashamed that I escaped into my fictional worlds from a reality where I had nothing but work and a place to live, but… I liked it so much.

By the time I turned thirty, I was working remotely. A content manager, an editor, just a person who rewrites other people’s texts, making them more human. All my work fit into a laptop and a few calls a week, and I liked living in that bubble, loneliness became not an event, but a background.

That’s when I decided I needed someone.

I rarely think about them on purpose, not because they’re important, but because the memories make me uncomfortable, almost physically. They had no faces, no names, no bodies, more like functions filling the noise in the silence.

Sometimes it was a group sitting at the table with me. I didn’t see them, but I knew they were there, I spoke into the emptiness and the words didn’t disappear, instead, they returned with the feeling that I wasn’t talking to myself. At first I spoke to them in a whisper out of embarrassment, but I didn’t even notice how I started chatting freely with my new imaginary friends.

Sometimes it was one person. A companion without age, without a voice. I imagined that he nodded, that he understood, that he didn’t look at me with that polite, strained sympathy so easy to read on real faces.

I didn’t need to know what they thought. I needed to know that they existed.

It scared me less than it should have. Probably because I controlled the distance. They didn’t interfere, argue, demand attention, and if I suddenly felt ashamed or disgusted with myself, I simply stopped thinking about them and they disappeared forever without resentment.

I knew something was wrong with me.

I didn’t call it an illness out loud. The word was too heavy, with too many consequences. Pills, doctors, condemning looks that eat you from the inside, diagnoses that would stand next to your name like a warning.

I understood that a psychiatrist was the right step. And I knew I wouldn’t go. Not out of fear. Out of shame.

How do you explain that it’s easier for you to invent people than to endure real ones? That you don’t hear voices, don’t see shadows, don’t believe in conspiracies or the paranormal, you just… fill the silence? That you understand how abnormal it is, and still continue?

I was ashamed of it the way normal people are ashamed of secret habits. Not sharp shame, but terrible and viscous. Shame was background noise, like a refrigerator humming at night, it never disappeared, it just became part of the space.

I convinced myself I controlled it. That it was temporary. That it was just a way to get through a period. After all, I wasn’t hurting anyone, I wasn’t losing my job, I wasn’t confusing fiction with reality.

I told myself, as long as it helps, it’s allowed. I remembered my father’s words and felt comfortable living like that. Until I decided to invent myself a wife I would never find in real life.

I named her Ellie.

At first it was just a drawing. It wasn’t like with the friends, I decided to take this very seriously. A face not too beautiful, but not repulsive, I deliberately made her that way so I wouldn’t be afraid. Soft features, attentive eyes. I redrew her again and again until she began to look… right. As if she had always looked that way.

Then I started writing her character.

Ellie knew how to listen, didn’t interrupt, noticed small things, how I frowned or how I started speaking faster when I was nervous. Most importantly, she didn’t require me to be interesting. It was enough for her that I existed, just like for me.

I added traits carefully, as if afraid of overdoing it. A bit of irony. A bit of stubbornness. The ability to get angry but not shout. She wasn’t perfect, I deliberately gave her flaws so she wouldn’t seem fake. I thought I’d play around for a month or two and then just forget her after feeling what it’s like to be in a relationship.

That’s when the horror I can’t explain began.

I didn’t notice when Ellie became… too alive, so much so that she started developing habits I hadn’t invented. She liked leaving a coffee cup in the sink but never forgot to turn off the bathroom light, she was very annoyed by the sound of a TV running when no one was watching it. I convinced myself it was safe, because it was all in my head.

At first I just spoke to her mentally. Then out loud. Later I began setting a second plate at the table, not because I believed she would materialize, but because it felt more correct, as if I were rehearsing a normal life.

Doctors would say I had schizophrenia. I told myself it was just a story, that I was a writer. Or at least wanted to be.

I didn’t notice when I started writing down her lines in a notebook. Not as dialogues, but as facts.

Ellie doesn’t like rain.

Ellie is afraid that one day I’ll wake up and forget her face.

Ellie always knows when I’m lying.

The last one I wrote as a joke.

A month passed, and I caught myself being afraid to disappoint her.

The first strange thing happened on a Thursday. I remember the day exactly, because on Thursdays they collect the trash early in the morning, and the crash of containers woke me up. I went to the kitchen without turning on the light, poured myself some water and saw a sticky note on the fridge. Yellow. My handwriting.

— Buy milk. And don’t forget, lactose-free.

I stood there for two minutes, convincing myself I wrote it. That I just didn’t remember. Tired, overworked. It happens.

The problem was that I hate sticky notes. And I never buy lactose-free milk. That was one of her little things.

I took the note down, crumpled it, threw it away and laughed. Even said out loud:

— Very funny, Ellie.

Only later did I realize I said it as if she could hear me, as if she could be standing in the hallway and would answer me with a real, living voice.

From that moment, little things began to accumulate.

A foreign hair in the shower drain. Longer than mine, a cup standing not where I left it. The smell of shampoo I had never used something floral, too alive.

I checked the locks, checked the windows, even set up a camera in the living room, but there was nothing. I thought my illness was progressing, but the scariest thing wasn’t that, Ellie began to argue with me.

I invented a scene, a dialogue between husband and wife. I knew what she was supposed to say, prepared her lines, and then I froze in fear.

— I would never say that. You’re not listening to me, Mike.

It was a distinct voice, spoken in a different tone, my heart pounded in my throat. I stood in deathly silence, not knowing what would happen next.

— I'm just tired, — I said into the emptiness, — Sorry.

The word “sorry” slipped out on its own.

I stopped inventing friends. Not because they bothered me, just that next to them Ellie was silent, and I felt her anger somewhere in my chest; my head immediately started to ache. I quickly realized she didn’t want me to be with anyone but her.

She appeared in the reflection for the first time two weeks later. Not fully, not as a person. Just the feeling that someone in the bathroom mirror was standing a little closer than they should. A warm spot behind my shoulder, a silhouette that vanished if I turned my head. I began to feel her touch cold, chilling to the bone every time I brushed my teeth in the morning and at night.

Later it started happening when I was falling asleep something cold touched my leg, and I knew it was Ellie. It was almost funny to realize I was losing my mind more each day, that I had begun to feel the touch of my imaginary wife. And yet one evening, while brushing my teeth, I said:

— Can you stop doing that? Please.

The mirror was empty, but the bathroom light turned off by itself.

Ellie started getting angry. Quietly angry.

I noticed it in small things. In the way the cabinet door slammed. In my laptop being closed even though I was sure I left it open. In files disappearing.

One day I found a text I hadn’t written. The file was saved under my birth date.

— You promised I would be real.

— You promised you would treat me with respect.

— I’m learning. I just need a little more time.

I sat in the dark, the screen lighting my face, replaying every moment I had felt watched in the apartment. At first, when I didn’t think about her, she couldn’t speak, it was as if she didn’t exist. But now… she knew everything. She controlled me. Before, she always responded, even if only mentally. Now it was as if she decided whether it was worth it, if earlier I moved mugs myself, imagining she did it, now they moved on their own, tabs opened by themselves, damn it, I swear I didn’t do that, I don’t remember doing that. Then I wondered… What if I’m not completely sick? What if Ellie somehow… materializes. The thought made me want to laugh and vomit at the same time.

I stopped thinking about her on purpose. Stopped calling her by name. If she existed, she was somewhere to the side now, like the shadow of an object you don’t see. I started living as if she didn’t exist, and that made her furious.

Waking up one morning, I felt a wild headache, as if someone had clamped my head in a vise and kept squeezing. Ellie was screaming:

— YOU LOVE ME!!! DON’T YOU DARE DO THIS TO ME!!!

— Sorry, sorry, darling, God, I’m sorry…

All I could do was whimper and beg for mercy. And indeed, the pain passed. I was thrown into a cold sweat, lying there trying not to think about anything, because she knows, she knows everything I think about, what I want to do. Every day she became clearer. Not physically, but logically. She remembered what I forgot, reminded me of bills, knew when I lied to myself. Sometimes she said things I had never thought or written.

I don’t know how long this went on. Ellie talked about a wedding, about wanting to gain a human body so we could hug and sleep together like everyone else. I agreed with her what else could I do? I caught myself consulting her before making decisions, waiting for her reaction and fearing her silence more than her voice.

She forbade me to think about anything but her. I was no longer allowed to create worlds and escape into them, because without me she felt bad. She kept repeating that we were made for each other, that she wanted to live her whole life with me, while I suffered every day from the four gray walls of the house. It felt like the room was shrinking every second. I didn’t want to be in that horrible gray reality. I had to sit with Ellie and agree with her about everything.

Over time I couldn’t remember how I had lived without her. Eventually I couldn’t take it anymore this couldn’t go on. Ellie kept touching me, I felt her gaze drilling into me from the inside, making my organs shrink. One day I slammed my fist on the table, and her plate fell to the floor and shattered into pieces.

— GET OUT OF MY HEAD! YOU’RE NOT REAL, I MADE YOU UP! GET OUT OF MY HEAD! I’LL GO TO A PSYCHIATRIST, THEY’LL GIVE ME PILLS, YOU DAMN MONSTER! YOU’LL DISAPPEAR FOREVER!

I screamed into the emptiness, spitting, turning red, until I stopped as abruptly as I had begun. Ellie was silent. Then I heard a whisper that froze my soul and covered my back in goosebumps.

— Darling… I just want to be with you… Find me a human body, sweetheart. I love you… I’m sorry for behaving like that.

My gaze fell on one of the plate shards, then, against my will, shifted to the kitchen knife.

— Sweetheart, we’ll be happy, I promise… I’m angry that I can’t be with you in real life… You’ll start working again, I’ll cook for you and we’ll live like in a fairy tale, with me and your friends, where you’ll be needed by everyone. Just like you always dreamed, darling.

Her voice was soothing, gentle, like my mother’s voice from childhood when she read me a bedtime story. I wanted to fall into Ellie’s arms and never be separated from her. With trembling hands I took the kitchen knife, the steel gleaming in the light. Swallowing hard, I wanted nothing more than for Ellie to finally leave my head, I immediately thought about what body would suit her. Maybe the neighbor from across the street?

I’m ashamed to write this. It’s horrible. But I was ready to do what Ellie asked, and I would have, until I remembered my father’s words. I knew I wouldn’t be comfortable living with Ellie, I couldn’t let her enter our world. She knew what I was thinking.

— You betrayed me, Mike, — her voice sounded quiet and strict, like a judge delivering a sentence, — You betrayed me!

I don’t understand what happened. It was as if I lost consciousness, and I woke up already in a ward. In the hospital they stitched me up quickly and without unnecessary words. The pain was real, earthly, and there was something almost comforting about that. They gave me a sedative, and the world became softer around the edges, as if someone had reduced the sharpness.

They told me I had stabbed myself and called an ambulance. I don’t remember it, but I know Ellie made me do it. She needed my body. And then there was a conversation.

The psychiatrist didn’t come immediately. A young man, neutral voice, neutral face. He sat so as not to loom over me and said:

— Mike, I want you to tell everything from the very beginning. Even if it sounds stupid or shameful.

And I told him.

About childhood, about imaginary worlds, about functional friends, and of course everything about Ellie, how I understood she wasn’t real and how that made it worse.

I expected horror in his eyes, or pity. Or that special look people give broken things.

There was nothing like that. He nodded, sometimes asked questions. Sometimes clarified:

— Was the voice inside your head or outside?

— Could you resist it?

— Were there moments when you doubted the reality of what was happening?

I answered honestly:

— I doubted constantly.

— That’s important.

After that conversation they didn’t let me go home.

At first I didn’t understand. I lay in a ward with a high ceiling and a narrow window near the top of the wall. The light came in strangely, not like in normal rooms, but like in the stairwells of old buildings where it’s always a little dim, even during the day, making it extremely uncomfortable. I thought it was just night and that in the morning they would let me go, say something like, “Rest, take your pills, everything will be fine".

But in the morning a woman in uniform came in, smiled softly and said:

— Mike, you’ll have to stay with us a little longer.

“A little longer” turned out to be a word without an expiration date.

They explained it was for my own safety. That after self-harm and the episode with voices they had no right to just let me go. They said it was standard procedure, that it would take seventy-two hours of observation, possibly more.

— It’s not a punishment, — the doctor said, as if guessing my thoughts, — It’s a pause.

A pause. A funny word, if you’ve spent the last while living in your head where there are no pauses.

They took my belt, shoelaces, phone, everything sharp, at first they didn’t even give me a pen, only a pencil with a dull lead. I sat on the bed and felt dismantled, as if they had removed everything unnecessary from me… and accidentally taken something important too.

The first days were the hardest. Ellie was there.

Not as a voice, as pressure. I felt her as clearly as you feel a storm approaching in the air. She was angry, offended, abandoned. I knew what she would say, that I had betrayed her and that all our vows of eternal love meant nothing to me.

I sat staring at the wall and for the first time in a long while didn’t answer. Not because I was strong, because there were cameras, nurses, people, the silence here was foreign, and Ellie got lost in it.

Every day they talked to me. They asked the same things, but in different words. I answered honestly, sometimes too honestly. They wrote things down, nodded, and most importantly, they weren’t afraid.

They gave me pills. At first they made me sleepy, my thoughts became viscous, as if someone had poured syrup over them. It annoyed me terribly. I was angry at the doctors, at myself, at Ellie, at everyone. But after a few days I noticed something strange.

She became quieter. Not gone. Not dead. Just stepped one pace back.

Sometimes, waking up at night, I caught myself forgetting to check whether she was there. It’s funny that even after everything that happened, I was very afraid of losing her. Because if Ellie disappears… who will stay with me?

One day the psychiatrist said:

— You are not dangerous, Mike. You are exhausted.

It was the most accurate definition I had ever heard about myself.

They explained that I would stay there longer. Not because I was “crazy,” but because my brain had worked too long without rest, without people, without reality. That I needed an anchor. A routine. Other voices, real ones.

In the ward opposite someone quietly cried at night. In the corridor one man asked every day what year it was. There was a lot of pain here, but it was real, and for some reason that made it easier.

I even found friends here. Real ones. Some were not entirely crazy, and you could talk to them, we played chess, talked about what we would do after getting out of here. And you know… the time I spent there was the best in my life. The psychiatrist and nurses talked to me just like my imaginary friends did, only this was real.

Sometimes, in rare moments of silence, I still felt Ellie. Somewhere very far away. Like an echo in an empty room that people have already left.

I was sad to leave the psychiatric hospital, but I left with the thought that I would never run from reality again. And that would have been a happy ending. They prescribed me pills, I could start a new life, but…

When I got home, I went into the bathroom and the towel was damp.

Not wet, not soaked. But warm. As if it had just been used.

I stared at it for a long time, and in my head one version after another collapsed.

— Ellie, — I said quietly, almost angrily, — Enough.

No response.

And then I saw something I couldn’t have invented. On the glass, fogged from a recent hot shower, there was a trace of a finger. Not a word, just a short line, as if someone had drawn it absentmindedly, without thinking.

The mark slowly disappeared. I’m still sitting in the bathroom, writing this in silence, and as soon as I finish this text I will move, hoping everything will be fine. And most importantly…

I didn’t know what was scarier, that she was real, or that some part of me had always known it and just hoped it was wrong.
