My mom and I recently moved into a new house. Nothing fancy—just an old, slightly rundown place but it was ours. At first, it was fine. Quiet. Settling in.

Then I noticed the basement.

It should have been normal: concrete walls, low ceiling, faint mildew smell. But one evening, while looking for the old toolbox, I saw it, a wall that *wasn’t supposed to be there.*

It looked wrong. Uneven edges, concrete that seemed newer than the rest, paint too fresh. When I touched it, my fingers stuck slightly, as if the wall itself was damp… and breathing.

I joked to my mom, “Hey, did we get a secret room?”

She laughed, dismissive. “Nothing’s there. Stop scaring yourself.”

But that night… I heard it.

Scratching. Slow, deliberate. Nails dragging across wood or maybe fingernails. Wet nails.

I went downstairs. The sound stopped the second I hit the basement floor. But the wall… it had a handle now. Rusted, slick with something that smelled like wet soil mixed with rot. I pressed my hand against it. A shiver ran through me, it felt… alive.

I pulled.

The wall swung open. Darkness gaped inside like a mouth. I shined my flashlight, and what I saw made me want to puke and scream at the same time.

The room was filled with… *things.*

Bodies pressed flat against the walls. Not just human, but *wrong*. Skin stretched, wet and shiny, like it had been varnished with sweat and mud. Eyes wide, mouths frozen open in silent screams, tongues pressed through teeth. Some moved, slowly, limply-like jelly. Limbs pressed at impossible angles. Others just *watched*, blank and glistening.

The air was thick, hot, and wet. Breathing made it worse—like inhaling mud and rot, like inhaling the smell of something alive decomposing right in front of you.

I slammed the door. Ran upstairs. My mom was in the kitchen, calm as ever, making tea. “Anthony, you okay?”

I shook my head. How could I explain that my house was trying to swallow me? That the basement had a *living room of horrors?*

That night, the dreams came. Endless tunnels lined with faces, some *breathing*, some *licking the walls*, some reaching for me with slick, deformed hands. Whispers: *join us… stay… don’t leave…*

The next day, I tried to convince myself I imagined it. But small things were happening:

* Footsteps in the hall when no one was home.
* Doors that led to walls.
* Windows that opened to more darkness.

And always, the smell. Thick, wet, metallic, and *wrong.*

Then the wall came back. Pulsing like it had a heartbeat. I opened it.

Inside… it had changed. The floor was slick, wet with something that felt like a mix of mud and coagulated blood. The walls were lined with bodies, not just pressed flat—*stuffed into the concrete like wet clay*, limbs twisted and skin glistening like oil. Some were still alive, gasping, eyes rolling, drooling black saliva. The smell made my stomach churn, made bile rise into my throat.

A body moved toward me, limp, yet deliberate. Its hand pressed through the wall like it was soft flesh, fingertips sticky and warm. The eyes… empty but aware. It whispered, a wet gurgle: *“Join… stay…”*

I vomited on the floor. The bodies didn’t stop. They leaned, pressed, slid across the floor toward me. Some whispered, others moaned, others just stared. I realized with horror. The room… it *ate*. It consumed. It was feeding.

I ran. Somehow got out. My mom didn’t see it. She just smiled, worried. “Anthony… what’s wrong?”

I can’t tell her. I can’t tell anyone.

Because last night, the walls of my room… they were wet. Sticky. Pressed inward. Breathing. Scratching. Whispering.

And then it started spreading.

The house is changing. The walls pulse. Floors warp, soft and slick like flesh underfoot. The air is thick with the smell of rot, of wet soil, of things that shouldn’t live. My mom… I don’t know if she’s herself anymore. I swear I saw her reflection in the window once—skin pale, slick, stretching unnaturally when she moved, her mouth opening too wide to scream… but no sound came.

I’ve tried to leave. But the doors lead nowhere. The street outside bends back on itself. Trees outside lean in, blackened, twisting, dripping wet into the yard. Windows show the basement, even when I’m upstairs. I hear wet scratching *everywhere.* The walls whisper my name.

Last night, the house moved. I woke on the ceiling of my bedroom, the floor below slick with mud-colored blood, hands pressing through the walls, fingertips wriggling like worms. The whispers were loud, pleading, hungry: *“Come back… join… feed…”*

I think the house wants me. Wants to make me part of it. To press me into the walls, into the floors, into the wet, alive structure that breathes, eats, and waits.

I can hear it right now. Behind the plaster. Behind the drywall. Slithering. Moist. Patient.
