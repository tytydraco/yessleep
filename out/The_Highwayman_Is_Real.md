She’s gone and they think I killed her. I would too if I were them. 

I didn’t think it could be real. No reasonable person would.  

Val was never a reasonable person. She loved me after all.

We met three years ago at a bar called the Bricks. It’s a dive where she worked the night shift. I was the guy the owner of the place, Donny, hired to unfuck the books. I’d sit in the back booth with my laptop and Val would bring me black coffee and ask me way too many questions about myself.

I could tell early on she was flirting with me, but she was a bartender, so sweet talking for tips wasn’t out of the realm of possibilities and I doubted I was her type at all.

Val was sexy and alternative, while I was plain and simple. I think that was why she ended up liking me though. She saw a guy who wore the same suit every night, drank only black coffee, and did math for a living. She saw someone boring and stable and that was something she never had in her life before. 

One slow night, she sat down at a booth and asked me if I wanted to have coffee with her sometime. I of course said yes and she then immediately set down an extra mug of coffee on the table for her. We had our first date that night and had been going strong for three of the best years of my life. 

Then things changed… It wasn't a gradual change either. Everything was perfect one night and then the next her night terrors started and everything went to shit.

She’d scream at the top of her lungs and thrash around like she was drowning in her own sweat. When I’d shake her awake, she’d be manic, her eyes darting to the corners of the room looking for something or someone who wasn’t there. She could never remember the dream in its entirety, but said it always ended with her in a car crash. 

Val is terrified of driving and doesn't have a license because of it. She said I was the first person she’s ever felt safe being in a car with. I do drive extra safe and for good reason. When I was four, my parents died in a car crash on their way back from a date night. The roads were slick and my dad was driving too fast. It was a freak accident, my aunt and uncle always told me. But I think it was just a reckless one that could’ve been avoided if my dad just went the damn speed limit. 

Val’s complex about driving is a lot simpler and far less tragic than my own, but far more potent. She’s terrified of getting in a car crash. She’s neurotic in a lot of ways like that, but I’ll give her this, at least with this one she’s reasonable for it. 

I try to accommodate her anxiety spikes as best I can, but the night terrors were the first time I couldn’t do anything to make things better. I thought it was all linked to her anxiety about driving, but from the beginning she thought the night terrors were about something else. What though, she could never say. 

It did however become the inspiration for an idea of hers and what would be the first major bump on the road in our relationship. 

Val wanted to start a podcast. 

She wanted to call it, “Night Terrors” and it would be about the supernatural, which she began to believe was the root cause of her nightmares. I don’t believe in any of that, so I was more than a little concerned when she started talking about sleep paralysis demons like they were real entities, but Val wanting to make a podcast about them seemed harmless enough at first. 

Then she spent her entire savings on high end equipment and books for research on dreams and the supernatural. She said it would all be tax write off, but I told her that’s only if the podcast turned a profit. I don’t think she cared. She just wanted to have something reasonable sounding to say to me to justify her frivolous purchases.  

It was the first time I realized how badly she wanted this to be her way out. I knew Val didn’t like being a bartender, but I didn’t know she hated it till she finally told me one night after another night terror.

“It’s the only job I’m qualified to do and I hate it.” 

“Then let’s get you a new one!” I meant like a real job, but she took it another way.

“I’ll quit tomorrow and start doing the podcast full time….” 

I was gonna tell her I didn’t mean it that way, but when Val looked at me with tears in her eyes, I realized I would have done anything for her.

So I supported her without question. I built her a soundproof booth in the spare closet, I helped her edit scripts, reach out to sponsors, and I listened to every episode of her podcast, even though they were… rough. 

She had a tendency to go off script and ramble about things that seemed to have nothing to do with the supernatural. To me, it’s cute so I could listen to her talk about how many blue cars she saw in her dreams for hours, but people on Spotify weren’t even giving her two minutes. 

I’ll admit Val was always a bit quixotic. She was chasing a following that was never going to come her way. She didn't know what she was doing, but hated being told that. So I watched her every night record for six hours straight, edit until her eyes were bloodshot, and post new episodes every week and still the podcast’s numbers would be stuck in the single digits.

It’s hard to watch someone you love fail. It’s even harder when you knew they were going to in the first place. I knew the podcast was a longshot, but it made her happy and that’s all that really mattered to me. 

Then she learned about the Highwayman. 

It was 3:00 AM on a Tuesday. I woke up to the silence, which was a first for me at that hour. Usually, Val would wake up gasping, but this time she was just sitting upright, the blue light of her laptop casting a ghostly glow on her face.

“I’m sorry if I woke you.” She said calmly. 

“Did you have another nightmare?” The outline of sweat beneath her gave me my answer, but I felt the need to ask due to how calm she was acting.

“I found him Cal.” She said with a smile slowly peeling up her face.

“What? Val, what are you talking about?”

“The entity in my dreams. I finally found him.” 

“Oh no, not this again.” I tried to be open-minded about the supernatural bullshit she’d go on about, but when she started saying that she thought a demonic entity was following her since childhood and haunting her nightmares, I snapped. I wasn’t gonna let her write off her serious mental health issues as a spooky dream demon.  

“Baby, I love you but these nightmares aren’t caused by a demon.”

"I never said it was a demon, Cal," she whispered, “It’s an entity and it’s called the Highwayman.” 

That name being said aloud was like a spark that set off a powder keg.

“The Highwayman? Val, please tell me you’re joking with me right now.”

“I know I told you my dad walked out on me and my mom, but it was more like he drove off…” 

“Wait what?” Val only mentioned her dad on our second date and then never again. I never asked about him and she never asked about my dead parents. It was our silent agreement. “I don’t understand…” I paused, not knowing what else to say.

“When I was eight, my dad got a big promotion.” Val broke the silence, “We had to move out of state. He was thrilled and thought my mom would be too since she kept pushing him to ask for a pay raise, but she was not. She hated the idea of moving across the country and away from all our friends and family, so they fought the whole car ride.” 

She stopped typing and finally looked me in the eye.

“I had a game I’d play to drown out the screaming. I’d look out the window and imagine a version of me running alongside the car. I could leap over the pines, sprint across lakes, and run up mountains effortlessly. It was actually a lot of  fun. Then I saw him… The Highwayman.”

I wanted to nip this Highwayman nonsense in the bud, but I also knew this was the only way I was going to learn about what happened to Val’s father. 

“He wasn't running after the car, no, he was just... moving. The landscape, it passed around him like how light bends around a black hole. And those eyes… They were bright as high beams. I thought my parents would notice him as he drifted closer and closer, but they were far too busy tearing each other apart to notice. He got right up to the window and I screamed until my lungs burned. My dad swerved, nearly rolled us, and that was the end. He called us ungrateful and said he should leave us on the side of the road. Mom called his bluff, but he wasn’t bluffing. He left us on the shoulder and drove off into the dark. We never saw my dad again after that. No one did."

“Val, I’m so sorry-”

“The Highwayman got him.” 

“No, Val, your father abandoned you and your mother on the side of the road. A demon didn’t kill him.” 

“I told you, he’s an entity, not a demon and my father was never seen again, so explain that Cal? Hmm, explain that?” 

I wanted to tell her what I really thought happened to her dad, but I knew better than to say something like that to her while she was this manic. Her dad probably killed himself after doing something so pathetic or started a new life without them. Either way, she was better off. I mean what kind of father would leave their child on the side of the road like that! If I was her, I’d want to tell myself it was the Highwayman that made my dad vanish too.  

She would spend the next four hours in a fever, connecting dots I couldn't even see and mapping out the lineage of a haunting that she believed evolved into the Highwayman. 

First, he was called the Running Man.

It was a root-doctor’s tale from the Mississippi Delta, told by porters on the Illinois Central during the turn of the century. Val said The Running Man was like a shadow that chased you if you were going too fast, so legend had it that the Running Man would pace the steam engines as they went through swamps, forests, and even mountains. The kids in the back cars would see him out the window and describe him as a black silhouette keeping perfect time with a train doing fifty miles per hour. They’d say It didn't seem like he wasn't trying to catch the train… just waiting for it to stop.

She found the oldest mention of the Running Man in a collection of oral histories from the First Great Migration. The name “Highwayman” wouldn’t come about till the 1950s around the time of the Second Great Migration, which saw more African-Americans moving via a car or bus than in the First. 

Val found a police report from 1958 that the Georgia State Patrol had buried in the 'unsolved' bin.

A convoy of three cars was driving along from Macon to Detroit and they had piled up on a brand new stretch of what would become I-16.

The sole survivor of the crash claimed they were doing sixty miles per hour when a pair of high beams appeared in front of him. He thought it was a truck on the wrong side of the road, so he honked at it to no avail. He signaled to the others in his convoy and swerved to the side of the road to let the truck pass. The cars behind him though didn’t swerve out of the way. They sped up at what they saw and when they collided with the light, everything went white for the survivor. He doesn’t remember how, but when he awoke, his car was turned over. There were no broken windows or visible damage on the outside of the car whatsoever despite it being flipped over. 

The sole survivor swore that as he crawled out from his vehicle, he saw a man standing in the tree line. He said the man didn't have a face, just two glowing apertures where eyes should be. This was the first ‘true’ sighting of the Highwayman or at least the version that Val became obsessed with. 

She had a map of all the freeways crisscrossing the U.S. with every sighting and crash potentially related to the Highwayman marked in blood red ink.

I should have told Val she was losing her mind and called a doctor. Instead, I tried to be a good boyfriend and give her as sound advice as one could give in such a situation, "If you want people to believe this, Val. You need more than old news clippings. You need a living eye witness."

I wish I had told her anything else. Because of what I said, she went looking for eye witnesses on the internet and she eventually found one who was willing to talk about their experience on the podcast. 

His name was Will Flash and Val thought he was the key to everything.

She’d found him through a back-channel forum for long-haul truckers. He claimed to have dashcam footage from 2018 in high-definition of a "High-Beam Man" keeping pace with his eighteen wheeler for forty miles. He’d been coy on the phone, telling Val he only shared his story with "true believers" because the mainstream media would just call him a crank and he might lose his job.

To Val, this was the "Night Terror" season finale. This was the break that would turn her listeners from a handful of pity-subs into a real audience. She spent three days setting up for the remote interview. She cleaned the spare room, positioned her mic just right, and even bought a ring light so she’d look professional on the video feed.

I watched her from the doorway. She was vibrating. "Cal, if this is real," she said as she checked her levels for the tenth time, "I’m never going back to the Bricks."

"You and me both, Val," I gave her a smile and a thumbs up before retreating from view. 

The interview was scheduled for 9:00 PM, so I sat in the living room with a glass of scotch, listening through our thin apartment walls.

At first, it sounded great. I heard Val in her podcaster voice welcome Will to the show. I heard him start to describe the stretch of I-16 near Dublin. He talked about the "High Beams" appearing in his side mirror. He talked about how his radio started playing static that sounded like a 1920s jazz band. 

Val was loving it. I could hear the excitement in her breaths between his sentences.

"And then," Will said, his voice dropping to a gravelly whisper, "I looked over at my passenger seat. And I realized the light wasn't coming from outside the truck anymore."

"What did you see, Will?" Val asked, her voice trembling with the perfect mix of dread and curiosity. "Tell the listeners. What was in the cab with you?"

There was a long silence. I leaned forward in my chair.

"I saw this," Will said.

Suddenly, I heard Val gasp. It was not a gasp of horror. It was a gasp of pure and utter disgust followed by the wet, mocking laughter of a man who knew he’d just won a very cruel game.

Will Flash did exactly as his name warned. He exposed himself through his webcam to Val. 

"You like this night terror I got for ya right here?" he sneered through the speakers. 

Val didn't scream. She didn't even yell at him. She just reached out and slammed the laptop shut.

I ran into the room and found her sitting in the dark, the ring light still reflecting in her eyes like two tiny, cold suns. She looked smaller than I’d ever seen her.

"Val..."

"He’s not real, Cal," she said, her voice flat. 

“I heard. He was a sick fuck. Maybe we can find out who he is and-” 

“No, not him…the Highwayman. None of it is real.” 

“Val, I wish it were. Then we could blame the Highwayman for your nightmares, for your dad, for that fucking sicko right now!” She flinched and I realized I needed to pull back my anger. 

“For my parents dying…” I said somberly, “I wish I could blame anyone other than my own father for leaving me alone in this world and I know you do too.” I took her hand, “But we’re not alone anymore Val.” I gripped her hand tight while she barely held on to mine. “I love you.” 

“I love you too.” I didn’t believe Val when she said it back, but then again I never did. Insecurity would never let me. 

She limply pulled her hand away and went to bed early that night. 

The next morning, she didn't record. Instead, she spent the afternoon listing her microphones and her mixer on eBay. I thought it was over. The podcast had failed and it was time to recoup the losses. I hate to admit I was relieved.

But a week later, I came home to find a massive, white van parked in our driveway. It had a strange, articulated arm mounted to the roof with a spherical camera pod at the end.

Val was standing next to it, holding a bill of sale.

"I sold the gear," she said, not looking at me. "And I got this."

She patted the side of the van. The Google logo had been partially scraped off the door, leaving a ghostly outline. 

It was a decommissioned 2014 sedan that Google had stripped of its proprietary software but left the hardware intact. That camera turret on top looked like a giant, unblinking eyeball, perched on a metal stalk six feet above the roof.

"I’m going to map the routes the Highwayman has been frequently spotted on.”

“You can’t drive.” 

 I know, you’re going to."

"What? Val, that’s insane. I can’t drive you across the country." 

“You won’t be. We’re only driving along the I-16 since that’s where the most Highwayman sightings have been reported.”

"Even if it’s only one stretch of road, we still need to get there. That’ll take at least an entire week. You really expect me to take that much time off work for this?"

She turned to me and gave her long lost puppy dog look and before I knew it, I was packing my bags and hers too. 

We planned to set out the next day and not return for at least a week. I had never been in a car with Val for longer than an hour before, so this was going to be a true test of her anxiety. Ironically, it was me who ended up struggling the most once we started. 

The first day was the worst. The van handled like a boat, and the wind whistled through the camera mount in a high-pitched, mourning wail. Val was silent the whole time, her eyes glued to the Eye-Capture monitor she’d rigged to the dashboard. It showed a 360 degree, fisheye view of the road, stitched together in real-time.

It also had some sort of auto blurring effect for the faces of people that we passed. My guess is it was some sort of privacy feature Google uses in their cars, but Val wanted to believe it was the Highwayman’s doing. 

Things didn’t start actually getting strange until we hit the I-16.  

"Look," she whispered not even four miles in.

She pointed to the monitor. On the screen, the software was doing exactly what it was designed to do when we passed a dense thicket of pines.  A small, pixelated circle appeared over a patch of darkness in the woods. It was a face blur, but there was no face to blur.

"It's just a glitch, Val," I said, keeping my hands steady at ten and two. "The algorithm is seeing a pattern in the branches and thinks it's a face. That’s all it is."

"Three more just appeared," she countered.

She was right. On the monitor, three distinct blurs were pacing us from the tree line. I looked out the window, squinting into the gloom. 

Nothing. 

Just trees and the white strobe of the mile markers. But on the screen, those pixelated circles danced along the edge of the asphalt.

"Speed up," she commanded. Those were two words I never thought I’d hear leave Val’s lips, especially in a car.

I pushed the van to eighty. The blurs vanished.

"See?" I said, a wave of relief washing over me. "It was just a lighting glitch at that specific speed."

"No," Val muttered, her fingers rapidly typing on the keyboard. "He didn't leave. He just moved out of the camera's refresh rate. We’re not fast enough yet." She gave me a pointed look. 

“I can’t! I’m already going eighty!” I couldn’t believe it. Val would lose her shit if I went a hair over the speed limit and now she’s asking me to push 100!

“Eighty ain’t enough. We need go-”

Blue lights hit our mirrors before she could even finish her sentence. 

I pulled over onto the soft shoulder as a Georgia Sheriff’s cruiser sat behind us, its strobes painting the interior of the van in rhythmic pulses of red and blue.

The Sheriff who walked up to the window looked... old. Not just elderly, but ancient. The cracked, pot-holed ridden highway looked in better shape than his skin.  He leaned down, his eyes scanning the interior of the van, lingering on the monitors.

"Evening," he said, his voice like velcro. "You folks are moving pretty quick for a piece of equipment like this. This the new Google rig?"

"We’re contractors, sir.” Val said as she poked her head over to speak to the sheriff, “We’re just mapping the roads for the new update." She said confidently. 

Weirdly enough, he didn't ask for my license. He just leaned in a little further, a strange, half-smile touching his lips.

"Well, get to it then," he said. "Lord knows we need you. Without you folks, I’d be lost out here. All of us would be." He gestured around like there were people with him and then patted the door of the van a bit hard before walking back to his car.

"Did you see his badge?" Val asked as he pulled away.

"No," I said, my heart hammering against my ribs. “Holy shit though, we just lied to a cop!” 

"He wasn’t a real cop. He didn't have a badge. And Cal... look at the monitor."

I looked. The 360 degree camera had been recording the whole time. On the screen, I saw the van, and I saw the empty shoulder behind us.

The Sheriff’s cruiser wasn't on the monitor. And neither was the Sheriff.

According to the "mechanical eye," I had spent the last five minutes talking to a patch of empty air.

I didn’t think I could grip the wheel any tighter. My brain was trying to find an explanation. Maybe it was a glitch in the camera’s rendering? a delay in the feed? Anything but the Highwayman.

"The camera didn't catch him because the frame rate dropped," I lied. I had no fucking idea, "It’s a hardware issue, Val. The Sheriff was real. He touched the car. I-I felt the vibration."

"He didn't have a badge, Cal. And the cruiser didn't have a plate," Val said, her voice dropping into a whisper. "He wasn't a cop. He could have been…"

“No Val, he was not the Highwayman.” 

“I wasn’t gonna say that!” She protested, “He could be a proxy of his though.”

“Oh my god Val. Do you hear yourself?” I rolled my eyes and got back onto the road.  

Val turned in her seat, "Hmm, you’re right Cal. We do need more friction."

"What? What the fuck are you talking about!" 

"Every story I found, it wasn't just the speed they were going. It was what they were doing in the car too…"

“Val, you're making no sense."

“Cal, I’ve been having doubts about us.” 

“What?” I was stunned. Her tone flipped like a coin. 

“You can’t understand me the way I need you to. You’re fucking robot who processes numbers better than words. You’ll never get me.” 

I almost wanted to laugh, “Val I know this isn’t actually you talking?” 

“Is it? You know, you’re right Cal. Maybe the Highwayman is making me second guess our entire relationship. Maybe these nightmares I’m having are just caused by sleeping next to you. Do you ever wonder if I just started dating you for stability? Because I wonder about that too."

“Val please.” She knew exactly where my skin was thinnest.  

"I know you want me to fail again so I’ll go back to pouring drafts for old perverts and looking to you for the rent every month. You like me broken, Cal. It’s what keeps me with you."

"That’s not fair, Val. You don’t get to tell me how I feel about you. You have no idea how much I love you." I snapped. 

"I found the box, Cal…” It wasn’t her words that turned my blood cold, it was her tone, “The ring you chose… it made me want to gag."

I slammed on the brakes and looked her in the eyes. Mine were full of tears and hers were dry. It was the answer I needed. 

I swung the van into a violent, illegal U-turn across the grassy median. The turret on top groaned as the metal screeched in harmony with the van’s shotty suspension. 

"We’re going home, Val!"

"Ok Cal, look-" 

“No, I don’t want to hear another word.” I was fuming and the only outlet I had was the accelerator. 

“No Cal, you don’t understand. I was just trying to create friction to draw out the Highwayman. I didn’t actually mean any of that!”

“What about the ring then?” 

“ I…I mean yeah I found it a month ago.” 

“Well?”  

“Well what?” 

“Would you marry me?” I took my eyes off the road to meet hers. 

She answered me with a shriek, pointing at the windshield.

A figure was standing in the center of the lane.

He was a tall man with his back to us. I didn't even think to brake. At that moment, I swear I wanted to hit him. I wanted to hit something as hard as I could.

We swerved at the last second thanks to Val grabbing the wheel. The van's tires screamed as the world tilted around us. I pulled the van straight and looked in the rearview mirror… 

The road was empty.

Val wasn't looking at the road. She was staring at her computer. "I got him," she whispered, her voice a mix of terror and ecstasy. "Cal, look at the screen."

She turned the laptop toward me. In the grainy, low-light video, you could see the figure I’d nearly hit. But as the van swerved past him, the figure turned.

He didn't have a face. In the center of a featureless shadow, two white apertures ignited like high beams. And as the van roared past, the video showed him taking a single step out of the way. 

"He's not behind us anymore," Val whispered, her eyes fixed on the "Face Blur" monitor where a dozen pixelated circles were now swarming around the rendering of our own van. "He’s pacing us. The friction worked, Cal. He’s locked onto us now."

The rage that had fueled me curdled in an instant. I kept the needle buried at ninety, my hands cramping around the wheel. I wanted to see a sign for somewhere. I wanted to see a gas station, a mile marker, a green reflection, I wanted anything that suggested we were back on the grid.

But the road had changed.

The asphalt looked darker, smoother, like black glass. The loblolly pines on either side of the highway had become a solid, impenetrable wall. No matter how fast I drove, the scenery didn't move. It was like driving on a treadmill.

"We should have hit a town by now," I muttered. My eyes were beginning to play tricks on me. The white lines on the road started to look like teeth. "Val, check the GPS. Why aren't we seeing any exits?"

Val didn't look up from her monitor. She was mesmerized. "The GPS is spinning, Cal. We aren't on the map anymore."

Highway hypnosis is a documented phenomenon. The brain, starved for new stimuli, begins to hallucinate to fill the void. I told myself that’s what was happening. I was tired. I was emotionally drained. When I saw the semi-truck blink into existence in the lane next to us, I assumed it was just my weary mind manufacturing a shadow.

The truck was an old Peterbilt, rusted and eyeless, with no trailer. It moved with a silent, heavy grace, pacing the van at ninety miles per hour. There was no roar of an engine, no hiss of air brakes. It was just there, a wall of iron inches from my door.

"Cal, don't let it touch us," Val whispered.

I swerved away, but the truck mirrored me. As I slowed down, it slowed down. I felt a coldness radiating from the passenger side, a literal drop in temperature that made my breath bloom in the air.

Then, familiar lights hit the mirrors again. Red and blue.

“Oh thank God!” I pulled over, desperate for the rational authority of the law to shatter this nightmare. 

“I wouldn’t be so relieved.” Val said as she peered over. The truck speeding next to us was gone now, seemingly vanishing without a trace.  

My heart hammered against my ribs as the Sheriff walked up to the window. It was the same old man from before. 

"Evening," he said matter of factly. "Speeding a bit, aren't we? This the new Google rig?"

"You already pulled us over," I said, frantically "Twenty miles back I think. You gave us a warning."

The Sheriff leaned into the window. He didn't look confused. He looked like a teacher dealing with a slow student. "Well, get to it then. Lord knows we need you. Without you guys, I’d be lost out here. All of us would."

"What, no!" I yelled. "Sir, where are we? Please, we need to get home."

He ignored me, his gaze shifting to Val. "Don't look into his eyes," he whispered, leaning so close I could smell the scent of old ozone on his breath. He then tapped the door like before and vanished into the darkness. His cop car vanished right alongside him as its red and blue light flickered dead. 

A new light would take its place as the truck had returned, barreling down our lane from behind us. 

“How did he get behind us?” 

“It doesn’t fucking matter, just go!” Val snapped me out of my trance and I got the van back on the road going eighty. 

It wasn’t enough. The truck was hot on our heels. 

“Cal, don’t let it get any closer!” 

“I’m going as fast as I can.”

Val grabbed the wheel and pulled it toward her. The van swerved and barely dodged the truck as it sped on by and we nearly spun right off the road. 

“Val, what the fuck was that!” I screamed at her after slamming the brakes. 

"Cal, look at the gas," Val said, her finger pointing at the dash.

I looked and saw the needle was hovering just above empty. We had been driving for hours, yet the odometer hadn't moved a single mile. We were burning fuel, but we weren't going anywhere.

“How the fuck is that possible!”

“The Highwayman.”

“Val, don’t…just please don’t with that bullshit right now.” I yelled at her and she went quiet, jerking her gaze away from mine and towards the window. 

I was still mad at her, but I needed to calm down or else I’d just make things worse. I got the car moving again and just hoped we hit a gas station before too late, but of course none ever came. We were on a boundless road with no signs or turns in sight.   

"We have to stop," I said, my voice breaking as my mind did. "I can't... I can't keep my eyes open."

I pulled the van onto a flat patch of grass near a cluster of dead trees. I was too exhausted to fight anymore. I just wanted to close my eyes. I climbed into the back with Val, where we huddled together under a thin moving blanket.

I pulled it over our shoulders, drawing Val against my chest. She was shivering, but as I wrapped my arms around her, she seemed to relax. 

"Calvin," she whispered, breaking the eerie silence. "What I said out there... about the ring. About us. I…. I didn’t mean it like that… it’s just…  the way it came out all wrong, it was all just to attract the Highwayman."

I stared at the carpeted ceiling of the van indifferently, trying to lower the boil beginning in my chest. "You don't want a future with me. It came out pretty clear, Val." I hissed.

"No," she said, turning her head to look at me. In the dim light, her eyes were wide and honest. "I love you. I do. But I'm just... I'm not ready for forever. It really fucking terrifies me that I might end up in a marriage like my parents had."

I let out a long, slow breath as the thought of my own calmed me. "To be honest, I'm not ready either." I admitted. 

"Then why the ring?" Val asked.  

"It was my mom’s," I answered quietly. "The one my dad gave to her when he proposed. Even before I met you, I always had this specific, stupid dream of proposing with it. I guess... I guess I just wanted to feel like I was honoring their legacy, because I’m all that’s really left of it."

Val’s hand found mine under the blanket, her fingers weaving through mine. "I didn't know it was hers. I'm sorry for using it against you the way I did. That was… that was really fucked up thing for me to do." She was silent for a moment, listening to the wind whistle through the camera turret on the roof. "Do you ever think about them? Your mom and dad? How would your life have turned out if they had lived?"

"I'll never know," I said, shifting slightly against the hard floorboards. "And honestly? I don't want to. I got lucky. My uncle and aunt took me in and raised me, and that was... an adjustment for them."

Val managed a weak smile. "An adjustment? They’re like super parents."

"Now they are, but before they were a total party couple and they never wanted kids. They lived in this mid-century modern house with a wet bar in the living room and hosted these wild cocktail nights that I would always sneak out during. When I moved in, they had no idea what to do with a kid. I remember being like seven and exploring their master bedroom while my aunt was running errands and my uncle was outside doing yard work. I found a box in their nightstand drawer. They were these bright, neon-colored packages that smelled like strawberries and mint. I was convinced I'd found their secret bubblegum stash that they were hiding from me."

Val chuckled, her head resting on my shoulder. "Oh no."

"Oh yeah. I took a wrapper, bit right into it, and spent the next twenty minutes trying to scrub the taste of latex and artificial strawberry out of my mouth. When my uncle found me crying over the sink, he had to explain what a flavored condom was. He looked more traumatized about it than I was."

Val burst out laughing, a genuine, loud laugh that shattered the oppressive quiet of the highway. It was the most beautiful sound I’d heard in my entire life.

I smiled, squeezing her hand. "They stepped up. Even though it was clear neither of them ever wanted the responsibility, they gave up their twenties to make sure I had a roof over my head. But... I don't know. Growing up like that, seeing what they sacrificed, it always made me feel like I was a burden you know. Like I owe them everything. I’ve spent my whole life trying to give back to them, trying to be perfect, so they wouldn't regret taking me in."

Val’s laughter faded, replaced by a soft, knowing look. "I know, Cal."

"How do you know?"

"Because you do the same thing for me," she whispered, her thumb tracing the back of my knuckles. "Your uncle and aunt are lucky to have you. And god knows I am. But Cal, let’s be real here... I'm the one who's the burden. Look at what I dragged you into. Look at this van. Look at our lives!"

"Hey," I said, turning her chin so she had to look me in the eyes. "Stop that. Don't talk about yourself like that. I don't care what we go through. I don't care about the podcast, or the night terrors, or the crazy ghost stories. I'm here. Whatever you put me through, I am going to be right here, because I love you Val."

Val’s eyes shimmered in the cold dark, "I love you too," she said before she kissed me.

The air in the van quickly grew thick in our passion and the windows fogged over, dimming the small bit of moonlight that illuminated us. It would be our last intimate moment together. 

I woke up to Val’s ice cold hand over my mouth.

"He's here," she whispered.

The van was vibrating. A brilliant, white light was pouring through the frost on the rear windows, turning the condensation into a blinding, silver screen. It was like a spotlight had been turned on us from inches away.

The temperature in the van was dropping so fast the plastic on the dashboard was beginning to crack.

"He's flushing us out," Val whispered. "He wants us to move."

"We can't," I said, my teeth chattering. "The gas is gone."

"Then we run," she said, her eyes fixed on the door. "If we stay here, we freeze. If we run, we have a chance to get away."

I looked at the windows. The ice was thickening, forming jagged patterns that looked like long, spindly fingers reaching for us. I grabbed Val’s hand, my heart an uneven drum in my chest.

"On three," I said.

I pushed the heavy sliding door open.

We didn't step out into the humid Georgia night. We stepped into a void of howling white. It was a blizzard that felt like needles against my skin. The temperature had to be well below zero. In the span of a single breath, the sweat on my neck turned to ice.

"Val, stay close!" I yelled, but the wind snatched my voice away from her.

The light behind the van was still there, but it was growing. The silhouette of the Highwayman emerged and his gaze fell right on us. He was the shape of man made out of darkness, seven feet tall, and drifting through the snow with a terrifyingly smooth gait.

We ran. What else could we have done?

The asphalt was slick with black ice. Every few yards, a sound like a thunderclap would roll through the whiteout. It was the roar of a semi-truck passing us. We couldn't see them, only the blur of rusted iron and the spray of slush that nearly knocked us off our feet.

"Cal!" Val’s voice was barely breaking through the gale.

I reached for her, but my foot slipped. I went down hard on the ice, the breath leaving my lungs in a frozen cloud. When I looked up, the distance between us had already grown. The blizzard was a living thing, a wall of white static trying to pull us apart.

"Val, don't move! I'm coming!"

I scrambled up, my frozen fingers clawing at the road. But then I saw him. The Highwayman was no longer behind the van. He was between us.

He was pacing her. He moved like a car at a crawl, his high beam eyes fixed on her back. The light was so intense it cast Val’s shadow long and jagged across the snow toward me. She was walking toward the edge of the road, her head tilted, looking up at the sky as if she had finally found what she’d been searching for her whole life.

"Val! Look at me!" I screamed.

She stopped. She turned her head, and for a second, the wind died down. I saw her face. She wasn't terrified. She looked... relieved. 

"He’s not a monster, Cal," she shouted, her voice suddenly clear, ringing out over the storm. "He just wants us to see the truth. I love you Calvin.”

"No! Val, come back!"

The Highwayman took a step. The light flared, blinding me, turning the world into a searing, white sheet. I heard the roar of the Peterbilt again and much closer this time, the metallic scream of air brakes struggling against the ice.

Val didn't run away. She walked into the light. She walked directly into the path of the white flares.

I charged forward, screaming her name, but I was moving through molasses. The light hit its peak intensity, a flash so bright it felt like it burned the back of my retinas. Then… nothing. 

The wind vanished. The snow vanished. I was on the side of a road in the middle of nowhere.

I fell to my knees on the now dry asphalt. The air was warm and smelled of pine and diesel. The sun was just beginning to bleed over the horizon, painting the sky in shades of pink and orange.

It was all back to normal… but now I was alone.

"Val?" I whispered to the still air and awaited an answer that would never come. 

A cruiser pulled up a few minutes later. A different sheriff this time and this one had a badge. "Sir? You okay? Where's your vehicle?"

I couldn't answer him. I couldn't tell him about the loop, or the frozen Google car, or the man with high beams for eyes. I just pointed at the empty road.

They searched for three weeks. They found the Google van a few miles down the road, abandoned and out of gas and they found Val’s phone and laptop inside with nothing on it. It was like they were wiped clean.

They never found a body or any signs of fowl play.

The police still think I snapped and killed her, but they had no proof so they never pressed charges. It didn’t stop my aunt and uncle from believing I had done something wrong to Val.

I won’t lie, them turning their backs on me hurt, but I can’t pretend I didn’t understand why.    

I wouldn’t worry about me though. I know the truth. 

The Highwayman is real and I know just where to find him.
