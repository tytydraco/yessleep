My mother handed me the list when I turned twelve. All mothers did that in our community. For us it was more of a survival briefing, not a regular mother-daughter talk on puberty. The rules were what kept us safe.

In our neighborhood on the outskirts of Delhi, we - the girls grew up under a shadow. It was needed as everyone knew it was the only way to be spared by The Entity. It didn't have a name, but it had a presence - a suffocating, malevolent force that snatched girls from alleys, pulled them out of the dark, and left their broken bodies in ditches. The neighborhood elders spoke of it in hushed, terrified tones. Nobody had seen it, the ones who did, ended up in the aforementioned ditches. 

Initially we always wondered why it did not come for the men.

"It feeds on vulnerability," my mother told me, her hands gripping my shoulders so hard it almost left bruises. "If you follow the rules perfectly, you create a ward. Good, obedient girls are spiritually shielded. The ones it takes... they forgot a rule. They broke the ward." Her eyes were wide with fear. 

I believed her. I had to. It made sense at that time and it would keep me safe.

These were our rules to keep the Entity away. I lived by these rules:

**Rule 1: The Ward of Silence.** Never look straight ahead on the street. Keep your eyes glued to your feet. If you hear whispers or calls in the dark, you become a shadow. You do not flinch, you do not speed up, and you never, ever reply. No reaction means safety.

**Rule 2: The Cloak.** Your outfit must be three sizes too big, drowning your shape, no loud colors that attract attention. Your scarf must be pinned securely across both shoulders, double-layered, hiding your collarbones. No visible arms or legs. The Entity is attracted to exposed skin.

Rule 3: The Curfew. Be indoors before the streetlights flicker on. If you are late, the dark belongs to it, and you are in its domain.

**Rule 4: The Domestic Shield.** You are not entirely safe indoors. When male guests or your father’s friends visit, practice invisibility. Serve the chai perfectly, keep your head down, smile politely if spoken to, and vanish into the kitchen. You never know who the entity has latched onto. 

Rule 5: The Locked Door. Never be alone in a room with a man, even if he shares your blood. The Entity can possess the weak-willed to get to you.

**Rule 6: The Ultimate Compliance.** If the force ever corners you, do not scream or fight. High frequencies and aggression feed its malice. Be docile. Beg softly. Appeal to its cosmic order. Call them Bhaiya (brother) or Uncle. Remind them of God. Remind them you are a daughter of the soil.

When the girl three houses down from us disappeared on her way back from college, the aunties whispered that she wore western clothes and listened to loud music. She broke Rule 2, my brain rationalized, cold and clinical. She broke the ward and let the evil in... *she deserved it.* 

I felt a sickening sense of safety. I wore suffocating layers of fabric in forty-degree heat. I kept my eyes glued to the asphalt. I made the perfect chai. I treated everyone with profound, trembling respect. I was doing everything right. I was invisible thus I was safe from the monster.

Then came the monsoon of my final year of college.

The rain was a deafening sheet of gray. The streets flooded within minutes, stranding the buses. I panicked. It was 5:30 PM. The sky was bruising into a dark, violent purple, the streetlights flickering weakly against the storm. 

**Rule 3. The Entity’s hour was coming.**

I waded through knee-deep water, my heavy, soaked shirt clinging to my skin. I felt naked, exposed, breaking Rule 2 by no fault of my own. I felt an overwhelming, heavy dread, I was being watched. I could feel it, tracking my desperate movements through the downpour.

Just then a white SUV pulled up beside me. The window rolled down.

It was Sharma Uncle… thank God! He lived two doors down from us. He was a genuinely kind man - a retired government officer who spent his pension funding a local library for girls, a man who treated me like his own daughter, and a man my father deeply respected.

"Beta," he called out, his voice warm, dripping with fatherly concern. "The roads are entirely blocked. Come inside, sit in the back. I’ll drop you home. It’s not safe for a girl out here. The dark is setting in."

I relaxed. The car was a safe haven. I was indoors… I think. I calculated the rules in my head. 

**Rule 3: Get inside. Rule 4: Respect your elders.** 

He was a safe man. A good man. I thanked the Gods for sending him to me.

I got into the back seat. "Thank you, Uncle," I whispered, keeping my eyes on my lap. 

**Rule 1. Rule 6.**

He smiled in the rearview mirror. "Good girl."

The drive was silent, save for the thrumming of the rain. I kept my eyes down throughout the journey. I looked out of the windows a bit too late. We turned into the basement parking lot of an under-construction commercial building he owned. The automated garage door rolled down behind us, sealing out the sound of the storm. Sealing us into a pitch-black, concrete tomb.

The panic hit me like a physical blow. I was having a full-blown anxiety attack; sweat dripped down my forehead. My stomach rumbled in hunger and panic.

"Uncle? Where are we?" my voice trembled. "My mother is waiting."

He didn't answer. He cut the engine. The dashboard lights died, plunging us into near-total darkness. I heard the click of his seatbelt. I heard him move.

**Rule 5: The Locked Door.** Never be alone in a room with a man...

*Shit! Did the car count as one?*

He opened the back door and climbed in next to me. The smell of cheap cologne, wet leather, and stale tobacco filled the cramped space. But as he drew closer, I noticed something that turned my blood to ice.

His hand grazed my thigh. He grabbed my wrist with a terrifying, brutal strength.

"U-uncle..." I whimpered, shrinking against the door, clutching my wet scarf tightly over my chest. Look down. Don't look the demon in the eye. Rule 1.

He pinned me to the wet leather seat, his hands tearing at the heavy fabric I had relied on for protection. I felt the first tear of my kurti. I felt his hot, heavy breath on my neck.

And for a split second, I froze, waiting for the sky to fall. Waiting for the universe to strike him down for breaking its balance. But the darkness remained indifferent. The silence remained absolute.

A profound, white-hot fury sparked to life beneath my ribs. It wasn’t fear. It was a violent, liberating rage. There was no entity. It was easier to believe in the existence of an entity than the evil that hides in such men.

The rules were a lie. And if the rules were a lie, then compliance was suicide.

Rule 1: Never look them in the eye.

I snapped my head up. I drove my gaze straight into his. I didn't look down. I forced him to see the absolute, unfiltered hatred burning in my eyes. He flinched, shocked by the sudden defiance of his "good girl."

Rule 6: Do not scream. Do not fight. It angers them.

I roared. A raw, primal sound that tore from the depths of my throat, shattering the silence of the concrete basement. I didn't beg. I didn't call him Uncle. I fought back. I dug my fingernails deep into his face, ripping skin, gouging toward his eyes.

He shrieked, backing away, clutching his bleeding cheek in disbelief. "You bitch…"

But as my blood boiled with defiance, something else answered the call.

The air in the car began to vibrate, and the temperature dropped visibly. Our breath bloomed into white plumes. The rain outside seemed to halt, suspended in mid-air. From the shadows of the backseat floorboard, the darkness began to thicken, rising like black smoke.

I felt… not scared. No longer bound by rules, I felt a terrifying density filling my chest. 

Unlike what we had been taught, there was never any phantom demon coming to claim us. The real Entity wasn't born of the dark; it was born of us. It was the suppressed, choked-back rage of the many girls who had been violated and died in rooms just like this, waiting for a savior that never came. It had been fed by centuries of our silent compliance, and now, it was awakened by my rebellion.

The formless shadow drifted behind Sharma Uncle, its cold tendrils wrapping around his arms, pinning him in place. He thrashed, his eyes widening in actual, supernatural terror as the darkness locked his limbs.

A chorus of whispers echoed through the car—not a mournful lament, but a rising, vengeful chant.

“We played by the rules,” the voices hissed, pressing against the glass. “It was never a level playing field.”

The Entity held him tight. It looked at me—or rather, the space where its eyes should be aligned with mine. It was offering a pact. I somehow knew I was no longer the prey.

*Give us a voice,* the cold whispered in my head. *And we will give you the teeth.*

I understood. I accepted. I opened my mouth and drew the freezing black smoke deep into my lungs. I absorbed it.

The transition was agonizing and beautiful. I felt the final terror of every missing girl slide into my veins—the girl from three houses down, the girl from the park, the ones whose names were never spoken. I took all of their accumulated fear, condensed it into a single, freezing point of absolute malice, and forced it down Sharma Uncle’s throat.

The shadow in the backseat died down, because it was inside me now.

I looked at Sharma Uncle, whimpering, pinned against the door by the sheer gravity of my presence. The blood from the scratches on his face was turning to black ice. This was the man who organized the neighborhood festivals. The man everyone respected. A pathetic, cowardly monster when stripped of his societal armor.

"Please," he whimpered, his voice cracking, his hands trembling as he tried to pull away from a girl half his size.

 "I'm your Uncle..."

"You are nothing," I spat on his face. My voice didn't sound like just mine anymore. It was layered, heavy, and resonant.

I didn't follow Rule 6. I didn't go quietly.

No, I did not kill him. Death would be too quick, an easy escape from the world we had to build. Instead, I reached into his mind with the cold tendrils of my new skin and tore out his free will. I hollowed him out until he was nothing but a compliant husk of a human.

The rules were his to follow now.

I climbed into the front passenger seat, my wet, torn clothes completely forgotten. The freezing air subsided, and the heavy thrum of the monsoon rain slammed back onto the roof of the SUV.

“Drive,” I instructed.

His movements were stiff, mechanical, completely devoid of the grandfatherly warmth he had faked for decades. I laughed as his trembling hands shifted the car into gear, his eyes wide and vacant, completely powerless under my gaze.

I have a lot of work ahead of me now.

Let me know if you know of any Sharma uncles. It’s time to teach them some new rules.
