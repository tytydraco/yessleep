The first one was just sitting on the kitchen counter, tucked under the edge of the coffee maker like I’d left myself a reminder.

It was my handwriting. Written in my pen—the blue Pilot G2 I always keep next to the fridge. It just said: *Don't go in the basement.*

I live alone. I’ve never had memory issues, and I definitely don't sleepwalk. I do have a basement, but it’s just an unfinished concrete pit under the apartment that I use for storage. I’ve been down there maybe three times since I signed the lease. There’s literally nothing in it except some boxes I never bothered to unpack and a water heater that clicks when the temperature drops.

I threw the note in the trash. I figured I must have gotten up half-asleep and scribbled it down, then totally forgot. People do weird things when they're exhausted. I’ve left my keys in the freezer before. Brains glitch.

But the second note showed up two days later.

It was taped to the bathroom mirror, right at eye level. Impossible to miss. Same handwriting, same blue ink. It said: *You already checked. Stop checking.*

I hadn't checked anything. I didn't even know what it was talking about. But the phrasing really messed with me. It wasn’t a warning to avoid something; it was telling me to stop. Like whatever I was doing in the middle of the night was some kind of loop, and part of my brain was trying to break me out of it.

I started paying paranoid attention to the little things. Was the pen sitting at the exact same angle I left it? Were there pages missing from the fridge notepad? I literally counted them. Forty-three sheets on Monday night. Forty-one on Tuesday morning. I’d used two pages in my sleep, and I couldn't find them anywhere.

Honestly, that scared me more than the notes themselves. If I was missing two pages, that meant there were notes I’d written and hidden, or destroyed. I could be having entire, page-long conversations with myself in the dark, and I was only seeing the scraps I forgot to clean up.

On Thursday, things escalated. I found three at once.

One was sitting on my nightstand: *It's not in the boxes.* One was shoved deep inside my shoe by the front door: *You can hear it if you're quiet.* The third one was stuck to the inside of the basement door. And it was put up with a piece of heavy-duty packing tape that I don't even own. That one said: *It knows you're reading these.*

My hands actually started shaking when I peeled that last one off the wood. Not because of the words. But because the tape was warm. Like I’d just pressed it flat with my thumb minutes before my alarm went off.

I bought a camera that afternoon. Just a cheap little wireless pet monitor with night vision. I set it up on the hallway bookshelf, angled so it had a clear view of the kitchen, the basement door, and the edge of the living room. I put a piece of electrical tape over the red recording light so it wouldn't distract me, and went to sleep.

Well, I tried to sleep. I kept pulling up the live feed on my phone, staring at the green-tinted night vision of my own empty hallway. I finally passed out around 3 AM.

I checked the footage the second I woke up.

At exactly 3:47 AM, I walked out of my bedroom. But I wasn't shuffling around like a zombie. My eyes were wide open, my posture was totally straight. I moved with absolute purpose, like I was on the clock. I walked straight to the kitchen counter, picked up the pen and pad, pulled up a stool, and sat down.

Then I started writing.

I watched myself write for eleven straight minutes. Steady, even strokes. No pausing to think, no crossing words out. I’d fill a page, tear it off, and start the next one. My face was completely deadpan, unblinking. I looked like I was copying something down.

Then I noticed my mouth.

I wasn't talking to myself. My lips were moving, forming distinct, deliberate words, but I wasn't making any sound. I was responding. My head was tilted just a fraction of an inch to the left, like I was listening to someone, and then I’d give this tiny, almost imperceptible nod and go back to writing. I was taking dictation.

The camera’s audio mic is garbage, mostly just picking up the hum of the fridge and the scratch of the pen on paper. But there was something else. A faint, low-frequency sound. At first I thought it was just the water heater. I cranked the volume on my phone all the way up and pressed the speaker hard against my ear.

It was a voice.

Low, patient, steady. Speaking in full sentences, pausing just long enough for me to write them down. The cadence was incredibly calm, like a teacher reading a passage from a book.

It was coming from beneath the floorboards. From the basement.

And it was my voice.

Not something that sounded *like* me. It was my exact voice. The exact same pitch, the same rhythm, the exact same way I clip the ends of my consonants. I was sitting at the kitchen counter writing, and I was also down in the dark concrete under the apartment, speaking the words. At the exact same time.

I played it back four times. The routine was identical. The voice under the floor would speak. The waking me would mouth the words back silently, nod, and write. When a page was done, the voice would pause, wait for the sound of paper tearing, and then keep going.

None of those notes were on the counter when I woke up. I tore the apartment apart looking for them. Nothing. The sleepwalking version of me is hiding them so well that the waking me can't find them. I’m only allowed to see the ones he deliberately leaves out.

I stopped going anywhere near the basement door after that. But it didn't matter. Whatever is going on doesn't need me to go down there willingly.

The next recording was worse. Same exact time—3:47 AM. But partway through the writing session, I stood up from the stool, walked over to the basement door, opened it, and walked down the stairs into the pitch black. I didn't reach for the light switch.

I was down there for six minutes. When I came back up into the camera's view, the knees of my sweatpants were gray with concrete dust, and there was a dark, dirty smudge right in the center of my forehead. Like I’d pressed my face flat against the floor. I sat back down and kept writing.

I practically sprinted to the bathroom to check the mirror. The dust was real. It was ground into the skin of my knees. My forehead had a faint, reddish pressure mark. I had been kneeling in the dark for six minutes with my head against the foundation.

I moved the camera. I put it right at the top of the basement stairs, pointing down.

That night, I sat on the edge of my bed for twenty minutes just holding my phone, trying to convince myself to delete the app. Trying to convince myself that not knowing was safer. But the part of me that goes down there every night already knows. Waking-me is the only one in the dark.

I watched the footage. 3:47 AM. I opened the door and walked down. The camera caught the stairs and a patch of the floor. I walked to the center of the room, dropped to my knees, and pressed my forehead to the cold concrete.

My lips were moving. I was whispering directly into the floor. A steady, uninterrupted stream of words pouring into the foundation.

Then I stopped. I lifted my head up, staying on my knees, completely still. Waiting.

Something answered.

I couldn't hear words. The camera audio just caught this horrific, subsonic vibration that made my phone speaker violently crackle and pop. It lasted for maybe fifteen seconds. Then I gave that same obedient little nod, stood up, and went back upstairs.

The next morning, I found a note I hadn't seen before. It was folded up inside the pocket of the jeans I’d worn the day before. The handwriting was mine, but it was smeared. And it wasn't blue ink. It was rusty brown.

I frantically checked my hands, my arms, my face. No cuts. Then I ran my tongue along the roof of my mouth and felt it. A sharp, incredibly clean slice right on the underside of my tongue.

I’d written the note with blood from my own mouth. I unfolded it.

*Stop reading the notes.*

I don't know what is in my basement. And I don't mean that casually. I mean I have stood at the top of those stairs in broad daylight, with every single light in the apartment flipped on and my phone recording, and I physically cannot make my body go down there. My legs lock up. My chest gets tight. My brain refuses to let me descend, like my nervous system already knows what's down there and is trying to protect me.

But the guy who wakes up at 3:47 AM? He goes down willingly. Eagerly. He kneels. He listens. He writes in his own blood when the ink isn't enough to make a point.

I don't know what he's waiting for. The notes I’ve been finding were warnings at first, but lately, they’re changing. They're getting softer. Almost reassuring. Yesterday, there was a tiny scrap of paper on my pillow that just said: *It's almost finished.*

Last night, I didn't set the camera up. I told myself it was out of battery, but that’s a lie. I just don't want to see the footage anymore. Because the last time I watched myself kneeling on that concrete floor, talking to whatever is under my building... I noticed something I can't get out of my head.

I was smiling.

It wasn't a dead sleepwalker expression. It was a huge, genuine smile. Like someone who finally understands a secret they’ve been trying to figure out their entire life.

The notes have stopped entirely now. I haven't found a new one in three days. But I don't think that means it's over. I think it means I don't need the warnings anymore.

Tonight, I’m leaving the camera off. I’m going to go to sleep early. And if I wake up with dust on my skin and the taste of copper in my mouth, speaking words I don't understand into a floor that answers back... I don't think I’m going to fight it.

I think the part of me going down there knows something I don't. And those notes were never really telling me to stay away. They were just buying time. Stalling me. Keeping me distracted and terrified so I wouldn't go down there before it was ready for me.

It's ready now.
