I have loved the sea since I was 5. It always enamored me, the pristine beauty of the ocean, the many unique and interesting beings in it, and the unknown species hidden deep below. After I graduated from college 20 years ago, I joined a marine exploration party to not only investigate the ocean, but to interact with it.

I've gone just about everywhere, to the amazing beaches of the Bahamas to the extreme cold of the ironically named Greenland, but the main place we work at is around a large reef off the shores of South America.

Many of the sea life there have grown used to me and the crew, and I've actually managed to befriend a few of them. Well, as close as you can get to befriending wild creatures like dolphins or sharks. I'd seen a large variety of life, but nothing could've prepared me for what happened today.

While I was doing normal survey work underwater, I felt a bump on the back. It was a lemon shark I had helped out and befriended, who I had named Lemon (creative, I know). He was acting oddly tense, which had never happened before. Lemon seemed to gesture to me to follow him, so I decided to see what the deal was.

As I swam with Lemon, I felt that something was off. We were going to deeper waters, and I couldn't shake the uncanny feeling of being watched from below. After about 20 minutes or so, with quite a few breaks for air, I reached what he was pointing me to, and it wasn't pretty.

It was a juvenile whale, about 20 or so feet long, with some of the worst injuries I have ever seen. It had massive chunks of flesh gone, and one of its fins ripped right off. Most strangely of all, there was signs of severe frostbite stretching across the whale's head, down to the dorsal fin. I was appalled at what I saw, but also surprised. Why had Lemon of all creatures led me to this? Where was this whale's parent? And what on earth had attacked it?

I was thoroughly intrigued and terrified, and decided to get back on boat and told the rest of my crew what I had seen. I relayed all the info, but the two things that got them most intrigued were the frostbite wounds and the lack of a parent. Whales don't usually leave young behind. So what happened there?

I had to investigate. Had to find out. It could be a breakthrough discovery. More importantly, it might satisfy my curiosity. So, I asked if we could do a ROV check in the area around the juvenile whale's death, which got approved for tomorrow.

Once that day came, we set up and went down. It was usual sights from the sub, the occasional random sea creature that I'd seen a hundred times, until we found something. A full grown sperm whale, clearly battling something. When we tried to look closer at what was fighting the whale, we couldn't see it. Not because of a lack of clarity, but because its body was nigh impossible to see due to either camouflage or the color of its body. The only thing we could see was light blue crystal lining its body, and it had a serpentine-esque form.

As the two did battle, it became clear that the mysterious attacker was going to win. Before the whale died, though, we noticed a sudden extreme spike in temperature in the waters, as ice appeared, rapidly branching at the sperm whale like a vicious fire, finishing it off and freezing a large part of its body. The last we saw of it was its gleaming gold eyes, staring right at the camera.

"Well, I think we found the culprit." One of our crew members said, as we pulled the ROV up. "What do you think we should name the beast?"

Many names were thrown around, before settling on a temporary name, Jörmungandr's Shadow. It felt fitting enough, and would make do until further research and determining its shape, size, and full capabilities.
