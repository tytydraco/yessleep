Almost twenty years ago, I saw something I haven’t talked to anyone about until now. I was in high school, on summer holiday. I live in a village, and usually I’d visit my grandma, who lived in a neighboring village about 5–6 km away. Once a week, I’d take my bike, put some music on, and ride there to see if she needed any help.

The road is nice—poplar trees on each side offer shade. Once I exit my village, the road sinuously takes me through fields and some old forest. At some point, on the right side, there’s a large river no more than ten meters from the road. There’s an abrupt embankment, full of overgrown shrubbery, but sometimes you could see the river. I was always scared when looking at it—it felt… misleading, somehow. It was deep, and the water flowed silently, with a velvety consistency, breaking into hundreds of small vortexes.

Opposite the river, hidden inside the forest, there’s a military base. I was always uneasy having to go alone through this portion of the road. There was also a solitary cross at some point. I once asked my mom about it, and she told me it marked the place where a girl she knew had been killed by her jealous husband with an axe. Of course, the body wasn’t buried there, on the side of the road, but her parents wanted to put something where she died.

One day, twenty years ago, I went as usual to my grandma’s place. I stayed with her the whole day—she had some things I could help with—so I passed my usual time of leaving. It was already dusk when I headed back home. I had a pretty heavy bag on each handlebar and was riding slower than usual. My grandma has a big house; she has lots of animals, plants, all kinds of stuff, and she gave me a lot when I left. I also had to wait for her to milk the cow so she could send my mom some milk.

Anyway, about one kilometer after exiting my grandma’s village, the road makes a full 90-degree turn and then goes straight for about 700 meters. Of course, it’s the part of the road where I always felt uneasy—the river now on the left side, the forest on the right. And that’s when I saw it.

From the riverbank’s shrubbery, about 100 meters ahead of me, a thing came out. At first, I thought it was a dog. I stopped my bike and froze. The road is pretty abandoned, with low traffic, and I was afraid that if it was a dog, it might attack. But the more I stared, the more I realized that thing was not a dog.

It looked abnormal. It was extremely thin, hairless, and had a phosphorescent white color—like some cave creature that had never seen the light of day. It almost seemed to emit light in the fading evening. It left the riverbank, sniffing around, and stopped in the middle of the road. I don’t think it had noticed me. Yet.

It walked strangely, and after looking more closely, I understood why—its hind legs were longer than its front legs, giving it a syncopated, awkward gait.

It stood there in the middle of the road, still sniffing around. I was glued to the spot, scared shitless, wondering what to do. I was afraid that if I moved, it would definitely hear me. Although I’m not extremely religious, I prayed to God for a car to pass by. But no car came that night. It was just me and the creature on the road.

While I was having something close to an existential crisis, trying to find God again, the creature suddenly stood up on its hind legs. My heart sank. I could see much better how thin it was. And how tall—I think it was almost reaching two meters as it stood there. It was sniffing the air, and then it looked at me.

The thing is, I didn’t notice any distinctive features on its face—just a white shape, with no eyes or mouth. I don’t even think it had a nose to sniff with.

And then it ran into the forest with extreme speed. It ran only on its hind legs. The last I saw of it, it was still upright, heading toward the military base. I turned around and rode back to my grandma’s place like I was being chased by the hounds of Hell.

Now I had the forest on my left side, and I was terrified to even look there. I imagined the creature running parallel to me, and at some point I’d see a white flash jumping from the trees, just before I died.

I reached my grandma’s house safely. I don’t know why, but I didn’t tell her anything—I just invented some excuse to stay the night. I always roll my eyes at horror movies where people don’t say anything after seeing something strange. But I was a teenager then, and I felt it wasn’t manly to admit I was scared.

My parents came and took me home the following morning. I never told them anything either. I stopped going to my grandma’s place, and years later, whenever I pass that road by car, I still look around to see if I might catch a glimpse of the creature.

My grandma still lives there. She’s 95 years old now.
