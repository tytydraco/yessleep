Mungo Locke and I were friends. Sort of. 

We were close in the way that children usually are, dumped in a room together and forced to interact while the parents are busy drinking elsewhere. 

Twenty-some years later, I sat three rows back in the church where my parents got married, watching the minister droning on about the tragic loss of youth.

Mungo had died 6 days earlier. 

I was the first to know.

Every culture has a wailing woman. Without telling you which one I’m thinking of, I know at least one popped into your head. Whether it was Mexico’s La Llorona or Ireland’s howling banshee, you know the usual tale. Apparently there’s nothing more universally terrifying than the deathly prophetic cries of a woman.

Scotland’s wailing woman is the bean nighe (pronounced *ban nee-yuh*.) That’s in Scots Gaelic, and you’d be hard pressed to find anyone who actually speaks it. No one knows our native tongue anymore, it’s a dying language seen only on train station signs and overpriced tea towels for tourists.

Much like the banshee of Irish folklore, the bean nighe is a spirit that heralds death. Whether it’s the death of those who hear her, or their loved ones, or someone unrelated. It changes depending on who tells it, but the result is the same. Tale as old as time, really. 

It used to be that her presence foretold the death of one of the members of the more powerful clans. That was back when we had a clan system, at least 300 years ago. Not many people know that it’s not still around today, and certainly not the American tourists. If they did, we wouldn’t have any money. Or an excuse to sell them the tea towels. 

What links them all is that she’s a washer-woman, seen huddled on her knees at the banks of a body of water, furiously scrubbing  blood from the shroud of the person next to die. While it differs from region to region and from person to person, there is one thing in common with each tale. Her cries mean certain death. 

That’s why, when I woke at just before 3am in sticky sweat-licked bedsheets, artificial orange streetlight oozing through the slats in my shutters, I understood.

I’d never been a believer. Scotland’s history and folklore is bloody and dark and ancient. There’s a certain unspoken undercurrent that runs below every conversation about our country. Something that none of us speak about, not because we are afraid of it, but because we are so adamant of its existence that speaking its name is moot. A wasted breath. Because everyone knows, you don’t need to tell them.

Did you know that the Scottish Highlands and the Appalachian mountains are the same mountain range? Way back before Pangea split and our American cousins drifted away across the ocean, they were connected. When I read the stories on this forum about the Appalachian mountains I feel a sense of hometown pride. *“That’s us too”*.

I would say I’m a skeptic, if I had to. Although skeptic makes it sound like I’ve given the existence of these death-heralding spirits some thought. It’s not crossed my mind before, I’ve not given it the energy. 

It doesn’t need it. 

You know how in dreams, your perspective changes? First person, then third person, then an aerial view and back again? A smattering of conflicting camera angles, so jarring it would give any viewer vertigo. As cinematography goes, it wouldn’t win any awards.

I saw the waves thrash below me from my eyes first, then above and over me. A bird’s eye view of the crown of my skull. 

I was laid on my stomach in the dew-soaked grass. A mouse-fat garden snake on its belly, splayed out and clad in my usual bedtime uniform - a ridiculous oversized shirt and a pair of boxer shorts. I should clarify I’m a woman, but if I didn’t know the sight of my own body (as I am all too familiar with every dimple and pudge and mole) that I’d think I was looking at a particularly unfit teenage boy. My head hung over the cliff’s edge as I peered down at the inky black water, some 50 feet down.

Below me, at the bottom of the cliff, sat on her knees between two gargantuan shards of rock, was a woman.  
Her hands moved furiously on the surface of the rocks, scrubbing blood away with a clump of striped fabric. 

Whatever she was rubbing against the rock I somehow knew was maroon and white to begin with, though with each drag across the surface it became closer to being one uniform shade. Tacky, syrupy red filled the grooves of the barnacles and seeped down into the rockpools between each shard. The waves licked at the rocks just enough for a taste but not enough to fully drink it away.

Between her blood stained fingers, the gold glint of thread could be seen, catching whatever light it could. 

The woman’s speed and vigor was persistent. Her nails clawed at the rock, often missing the cloth completely and dragging her bloody fingers across the rough surface instead. From my vantage point up on the cliff I could see the slight shift in her fingernails as her fervent movements peeled them from their nail beds, exposing the gummy pink flesh beneath. Her pace never faltered.

I didn’t make a sound, bar my shallow breaths, further compressed by my position on the ground. Despite my purposeful breathing, it seemed she sensed I was there. 

As I gently inhaled, breath catching on every ridge in my throat, her head shot up. 

The waves stilled, slowed their thrashing on the rocks, gently rocking back and forth before they settled completely.

There was no breeze, the wind paused, and bar my own shaky breaths it dawned on me how silent the scene had become.

Without guidance or pointing, her eyes immediately found mine.

I hadn’t seen Catelyn Locke for years, but my mind must have filled in the gaps of the 10-some years of absence. Her hair was greyer than I remember, the creases around her eyes deeper, a growing yellow hue around the whites of her eyes that goes hand in hand with a lifetime of comfort (too rich to work) and leisure (Sauvignon Blanc).

She and I looked at one another for a moment. My breath caught in my throat once more, a chicken bone lodged sideways. There was something about her that unsettled me then, Catelyn had always been gentle to us as kids but there was something about her furious eyes in this moment. I had never seen them blink.

I went to speak, mustering up whatever courage I had to call to her but no noise escaped me.

As I did, her mouth split open into a violent red gaping maw. 

Without a visible intake of breath she shrieked, long and sharp and rattling. Screaming with several voices, layered, and long, and ancient. A distorted choir of hundreds of years of agony.

It rang in my ears long after I woke up.

\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~

I learned of Mungo Locke’s death the way that I assume most people learn of the death of a family friend, through my mum on FaceTime while she nonchalantly peeled potatoes.

“Oh Morven, did you hear who’s died?”

Died, not *deid*. Mum had always told me to ‘speak proper’, wanting me to grow up with the ivory tower perspective she never had. She was taught all the airs and graces of a high-society Scottish woman, all from the comfort of a 2-bedroom council flat in Maryhill; and she expected me to carry on the legacy she’d forged from the more roomy middle-class house we now lived in. 

We weren’t wealthy by any stretch of the imagination. Not like the Lockes or the Cockburns, or the Findlays, or the MacPherson-Lahars, but we were comfortable.

Mungo and I were friends. Like I said. 

You know how you call your parent’s friends your ‘aunt’ and ‘uncle’ when you’re young? before you can quite grasp the truth that you’re not all related. Mungo was Auntie Catelyn’s son. Her only son, her only child in fact. Much like how I was my own mother’s only child.

“What?”

“Pardon,” my mother corrected without looking up from the potato in her hand, whose eyes she was skillfully gauging out with the tip of a vegetable knife. 

With one swift flick of her wrist she had chopped the potato in half through its midsection, and thrown both halves into the pot. She flattened her already thin lips into an even finer line when the briny salt water splashed her cheek.

“I didnae - I *didn’t* know.”

She paused, noting the way my middle-class mouth stretched unnaturally over the Scots words.

I’m a halfway decent liar. I’ve had a lot of practice over the years.

I can’t lie to my mother.

“Do you remember Mungo?”

Of course I did. We were friends. Seeing each other exclusively at birthdays and garden parties as children, and more so as teenagers, even though it had been several years since I last saw him. Last I checked, he had dropped out of university and was working as a line cook somewhere in Glasgow.

“Catelyn had told me about his little problem. He’d been to The Priory you see… She thought that was all behind him.”

My stomach twisted, sharp cold stabs of fear at what I knew was coming. Mungo went to rehab, he’d had a cocaine problem since we were teenagers. It only became a ‘problem’ when he started feeling the price of it, and given his overall net-worth that was already quite far in.

When you’re mind-bendingly wealthy, it’s always the good stuff, isn’t it? Catelyn Locke wouldn’t let her precious boy snort the street-level shit up his little button nose.

It started as something he would do on special occasions, then every so often on a night out, then on a Tuesday at home in front of the Coronation Street Omnibus. 

He’d been to rehab several times over the years. He’d been to The Priory three times. 

“She thinks he was high, our Catelyn.”

The Lockes lived in a grand sandstone mansion overlooking the ocean. On the edge of a cliff.

“She thinks he was high, when he -“

The waves thrashing upon the rocks.

“He *must* have been high when he- ”

The deliberate drag of bloodied fingers, wrapped in fabric.

“When he fell.”

I finished. My mother stared at me, vegetable knife hanging limp in her grasp.

“How did you -“

“Ottilie called me.”

Lies.

“Ottilie called me this morning and told me.”

I had blocked Ottilie’s number since she suggested we ‘*talk about what happened*’ five years back. She had the sort of naive relentless positivity that churned my stomach. 

I couldn’t tell my mother what I had really seen. She wouldn’t believe me. A lot of mothers fight for their children tooth and nail, mine did not. Every bad grade was a “*try harder*” every argument with friends a “*you were in the wrong*.” As track records go, I was certain she wouldn’t believe me.

Or maybe she would. I’m not sure which option was worse.

After all, I’m not sure I believed it myself. Despite the deep feeling of certainty I had when I awoke from that dream, sitting in my stomach like a full meal. 

My forehead broke out in a slick sheen of cold sweat.

“Catelyn says the funeral is next Thursday. Do you think you’ll come up for it? It would be nice if you were there.”

My heart hammered in my chest. My mind fixated on Catelyn’s scarlet gaping mouth. Her deafening wail. My ears began to ring, several screaming voices rattling my bones.

The sudden, inescapable feeling that I knew exactly what she was saying, or rather screaming. Wordless communication, received with open ears.

I paused. Glanced away from my phone screen as if any digital eye contact with my mother would blow my cover. My eyes darted through the gaps in my shutters at the busy streets outside. Rain pummeled the pavement as floods of commuters, advancing under an interconnected shield of umbrellas began pouring down the stairs of Bethnal Green station.

“I’ll be there.”

\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~

The journey from Euston station up to Glasgow was a sombre affair. Death aside.  

My train was delayed, as trains often are. Someone fell between the train and the platform, and had to be pulled out of the tight gap.

I won’t lie, I shuddered at the tannoy’s mention of a ‘*fall*.’ 

I later learned that the lady who fell was retrieved unscathed, which was a relief. Maybe she’d listen when they say to mind the gap.

The rickety train was overcrowded, overheated, and stank like a mixture of stale piss and stale beer. Which surprisingly, smell much the same.

I’d had just enough time to dump my bags in my childhood bedroom, give an uncomfortable hug to my bony mother, and smooth out the creases on my too-small Funeral Dress. I had outgrown it since its last outing.

Not enough time to wash the day from my body, but enough time to constrict myself into clothes small enough to to ease my mother’s nerves. It was a funeral, so she wouldn't be crass enough to make comments about my body. Not on such a sad occasion. 

But she would shoot me looks, glower at the way the fabric clung to my stomach, the way the sleeves cut into my upper arms, the way that I was seemingly unconcerned with how the way I looked affected her.  
I knew she would do it, maybe whisper about it with the ladies over too-large glasses of Sauvignon Blanc with shards of ice as sharp as their tongues. Insulting me without saying anything insulting. Something funeral-appropriate.

 *“Oh Morven looks well doesn’t she?”*

The heavy fullness of what I saw rested in my stomach, in place of the morning’s breakfast.

The church was a five-minute walk from my parents’ house. I teetered along in court heels my feet weren’t made for, yearning for the comfort of my worn black boots sat by the front door.

The church was adorned in troughs of flowers, rich maroons and stark whites, the colour of Mungo’s rugby shirt. If anyone walked past, they would assume there was a wedding.

I scoffed. Mungo would have hated all the pomp and circumstance of the whole affair. He’d grin and roll his eyes and insist that his wake should have been in the sticky-carpeted back room of a pub instead, his coffin a cardboard box and buried on the cliff’s edge of his home, next to the bench he would smoke a late-night cigarette on. A lumpy, rolled cigarette. I used to roll them for him, but I’d moved Down South five years prior. A burial on the edge of the cliffs, at the boundary of his parents’ estate. Ironically enough.

I shuffled into a pew beside my parents, a respectful third row view, not too far forward that it encroached on familial space, but enough to be seen as being present. It was all about optics.

Catelyn was still greeting mourners at the door, a picture of grace in a tailored black skirt suit - did she have this custom made? A bit gauche if you ask me.

As she had yet to filter into the front row, it allowed me a straight view. The casket wasn’t ornate. As Catelyn would even know Mungo would be rolling in his (currently empty) grave if he was stuck in a filigree box for eternity.

Grief is a nasty thing, it doesn’t creep up on you, it just waits for you to notice it. A cold twisting knife you willingly walk into yourself. Through my sleepless nights of anticipation, I had been fixated solely on being *right*, that I had predicted this, that I was forewarned by her. I hadn’t taken a moment to consider what I had been warned about.

I missed my friend. It didn’t matter that we hadn’t spoken in years. That the last time we saw one another we vowed never to see each other again. Did he know I wasn’t being serious? I knew we couldn’t fight forever, we never had. We would forgive each other, we always did, it was only a matter of time.

But there’s a first time for everything.

\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~\~

The wake (we call it tea) was held at the adjacent hotel, in one of the event rooms with suitably stereotypical tartan carpets. 

My small plate was sparse, lest I encourage the mothers’ gossip further. There were always vol-au-vents at those events (my funeral tea poison of choice) and I was staring longingly at one of the roaming waiters holding a platter of them that I didn’t notice the delicate click clacking of expensive heels.

“Morven?”

Even her voice, a gentle lilt somewhere between church bells and birdsong had the same effect on me as a sharpened pencil to the ear canal. 

Ottilie Cockburn (pronounced Coh-burn) was still as frustratingly beautiful as I remembered. Long strawberry-blonde hair gently curled at the ends by device or by Mother Nature herself. Wide blue eyes slightly too far apart and a modest smattering of freckles across the bridge of her perfect nose. Straight white teeth (“*she didn’t even need braces*!” her mother Elspeth would brag) and a smile that put you at ease no matter the sombreness of the occasion. *Bitch*. 

Worst of all, the gnawing rage that chewed on my stomach, was that she was *kind*. It always makes it worse when people are beautiful and kind. If she was nasty I could forgive the resentment I felt for her, justify that somehow she deserved it, but no dice.

Where Ottilie fell short was her intelligence. She was never the brightest in our little makeshift friendship group (regretfully that was more my area of expertise) but she was kind, and sweet, and selfless. Every mother’s ‘favourite’, and only a little because the mothers (mine specifically) lived vicariously through her. Unblemished, polished, young. They probably thought she was a bitch too. 

Her kindness was what set her apart though. When people are beautiful they get away with being mean. Ottilie never had to be nice, she just did it for the love of the game. Probably why she was the first of the group to speak to me at Mungo’s funeral.

The others, from our little teenage gang of years gone by, Henry Findlay and Shreya MacPherson-Lahar stood behind her, resting their plates on top of a tall table. 

Shreya looked away towards the window an stifled a yawn with the back of her hand, Henry stared at me, tired eyes hollow.

My chest tightened further. I didn’t know Shreya would be there, although why shouldn’t she be? If I had known she would have been there, I would have bothered to brush my hair. I don’t think it would make a difference.

“Ottilie, hi.”

My eyes wandered up from my plate to Ottilie’s face. 

Ottilie Cockburn was always perfect, but that day she looked less so. I could see the smear marks in her foundation, the wobbly jagged strokes of her eyeliner, the thin and forked red lines in the whites of her eyes.

A flash of memory. Catelyn Locke’s yellowed, unblinking eyes.

The multi-voiced ringing in my ears rumbled back to life.

Ottilie Cockburn lacked polish for the first time. She looked like she hadn’t slept in days.

“Morven, we need to talk.”

She looked like she hadn’t slept.

The ringing grew louder, voices clambering over one another, a cacophony of ringing screams.

“Morven, I know you saw her too.”
