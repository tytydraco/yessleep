It's been a week or so since I posted about the burglary at my grandparents' house.

When I started posting here, I thought this would be somewhere to collect my thoughts. Somewhere I could put them down in front of strangers who didn’t know me, who couldn’t look at me with concern or pity or that careful expression people use when they think you might be losing your grip. I thought I might get some outside perspective.

A few questions to ask myself. A way of making the whole thing feel less like it was happening entirely inside my own head.

I wasn't planning on writing again this quickly. Part of me still feels stupid writing this out at all.

Two posts ago, I was talking about an [old neighbour I barely remembered](https://www.reddit.com/r/nosleep/comments/1u4sr9n/i_went_back_to_the_bungalow_now_i_wish_i_hadnt/). Last time, I wrote about an [event](https://www.reddit.com/r/nosleep/comments/1u5y5iu/i_always_thought_it_was_just_a_burglary_part_2/) that my family had spent years turning into an anecdote. I keep telling myself I'm connecting things because I want there to be a pattern. That if you stare at any part of your childhood for long enough, you can probably make it look sinister.

I've been trying very hard not to do that. To step back. Turns out that's harder than it sounds. I haven't been able to get it out of my head and the thought that I'm missing pieces to the puzzle persists. I need to know.

I'm also very conscious of how I'm coming across to Mum. She is the most understanding woman I've ever known, but she has always worried about me. Even now, with me approaching thirty, there is still a part of her that hears a tremor in my voice and turns me back into the little boy who couldn't sleep unless the hallway light was on.

I don't want to keep asking her about my childhood. Not like this. Not with the same probing questions. After my last post I decided I wouldn't bring it up to her for at least a few weeks. That way, it would seem like a new-found curiosity regarding my younger years, not the obsession it has become in my mind. That way, maybe she wouldn't worry.

But I needed more gaps filling in, so this time, I decided I would try talking to my Dad.

I mentioned him in my previous posts, but I need to explain something before I go any further. When my Dad stayed in London, he stayed in London. 

That sounds obvious, but it wasn't to me as a child. Back then, I thought things would go back to normal eventually. That some time would pass and he would come back to me. That we would soon be kicking a ball around in the local park, just as we used to.

I thought he was away for work, then away because grown-ups were sorting out things I was too little to understand, then away because that was just what Dad did now. By the time I understood that he wasn't coming back, it had already become normal. If I'm being honest, it made me feel like I wasn't good enough for him.

Like I'd done something wrong. The lies and manipulations later on didn't help. But that's another story.

Suffice to say that we aren't close. We haven't seen each other in years.

We speak every few weeks, maybe. The conversations are never unpleasant, exactly. Just thin. I tell him what's been happening with work and family life, and he spends half an hour telling me about some DIY he's done or someone at work who has annoyed him, usually in a situation where, from the outside, he sounds like the problem.

That's our pattern. It feels comfortable, in our own way. Safe because it doesn't go anywhere. I broke that pattern when I asked him about the bungalow.

There was a pause on the other end of the line when I said it. Not long, but long enough.

"The bungalow?" he said.

"Yeah," I said, "The one next to Rex."

"Christ," he said. "That place."

I told him I'd been thinking about it. That I'd spoken to Mum. That I was trying to piece together a few things from when I was little. I didn't tell him everything. I didn't tell him about the hand on the fence, or the eyes at the bottom of my grandparents' stairs. I definitely didn't tell him that I was starting to worry some of the things I'd called nightmares might not have been nightmares at all.

Instead, I asked him about the tyres.

That probably seems like a strange thing to ask about, but let me explain. In my family it's another one of those stories. Not funny like the Spider-Man lunchbox. Not exactly. But familiar. The sort of story my Mum's side speaks about on the rare occasion my Dad comes up in conversation.

Dad was unfaithful when I was younger.

I don't know the details, but I know it was multiple women and it happened over a long period of time. My Dad would never admit it, and always blamed the issues at home on something else. But there were signs. Signs that in my teenage years, I remembered, and connected the dots. Now I'm not so sure I ended up with the right picture.

Someone put nails under Dad's tyres.

That was how I always remembered it. Not that he drove over one by accident. Not that he picked one up from a road or a building site. Someone put them there.

Deliberately. Several times, in fact. Mum never knew who did it, but reasoned with me that it was likely the husband of one of the women my father had been seeing when I was little.

I must have been five or six. The same age I was when we still lived beside Rex. The same age I was when I was apparently telling my parents someone was watching him.

I don't remember the first time it happened, not clearly at least. I only have pieces. Dad crouched beside the car on the driveway. Mum in the doorway, wearing her red dressing gown.

Rex out the front, messing with something behind the hedge, watching without watching. And me, small enough that the top of the car still seemed high, trying to understand why a few little pieces of metal on the ground had made the adults go quiet.

The bungalow was all on one level. I know I've said that before, but I don't think I appreciated how important it was until recently. There was no upstairs. No landing. No sense that the bedrooms were tucked away somewhere separate from the rest of the house.

Everything was close.

My room and the living room at the front. Mum and Dad's room and the kitchen at the back. My sister's room somewhere in the middle with the bathroom. The hallway that connected these places seeming longer in my memory than it ever could have been in real life.

At night, every sound felt like it happened beside you.

The driveway ran out in front of the garage, with a narrow path between the garage and the house. That path led past the front door, which was on the side of the bungalow just behind my bedroom, and carried on towards the back gate. Dad's car was usually parked on the driveway. From my bedroom window you could easily see it. From the living room you could see it if you stood at the far end with the curtains open. The bonnet. The windscreen. Sometimes the side mirror catching a bit of streetlight in the darkness.

One of the reasons I mentioned the tyres to my Dad, is because I can barely remember the sight of the car from inside the house during daylight.

But I have a vivid memory of it sat there, on the driveway, slightly illuminated by the lamppost on the path at the end of the small cul-de-sac we lived in.

At night.

That was the part I couldn't get away from.

When I think about Dad's car during the day, the memory is vague. It could be any car. Any driveway. Any morning from that part of my childhood. Dad crouched down, Mum in the doorway, Rex behind the hedge. All of it has that washed-out quality old memories get when you've handled them too many times.

But the car at night is different. I remember the shape of it.

The way the windscreen reflected the lamppost at the end of the cul-de-sac. The way the bonnet looked pale in the dark, almost blue. The way the side mirror caught a thin line of orange light whenever I moved my head.

I don't know why I remember that so clearly.

Or I didn't.

When I asked Dad about the tyres, I expected him to be dismissive. That would have been easier. He could have told me Mum had exaggerated it, or that I was mixing things up, or that it had happened once and everyone had turned it into something bigger over time. That would have been very on-brand for Dad.

But he didn't. He went quiet. That bothered me more than I expected.

Dad isn't good with silence. He fills it. Usually with jokes that aren't funny, or complaints about work, or long explanations of things nobody asked him to explain. But when I mentioned the tyres, the line went still.

"Who told you about that?" he asked.

"Mum did," I said. "Years ago."

"She still talks about that?"

"Not really, it just came up."

He made a sound under his breath.

"What?" I asked.

"Nothing."

"Dad."

"It was a long time ago."

"I know."

"You were tiny."

"I know that too."

Another pause. Then he said:

"They weren't just nails in the tyres."

I sat up a little straighter.

"What do you mean?" I asked.

"I mean they weren't in the tyres when I found them."

For a second I didn't understand.

"They were under them," he explained.

I thought about that. Under them. Not damage discovered later. Not a slow puncture noticed after a trip to the shops. Not a nail picked up by accident on some road or building site.

Under them. Waiting.

"Like someone had placed them there?" I asked.

"Like someone had placed them there," he said.

His voice sounded different then. Flatter.

"The first time, I thought maybe I'd driven over something and only noticed when the tyre went down. Second time, I checked properly."

"And?"

"They were lined up."

I didn't say anything.

"Under the back tyres," he said. "Tucked in close. Like someone wanted them to go in when I reversed."

I looked across my own living room as he said it, miles away in another part of the country where I sit and write this now. But I wasn't really seeing it. I was seeing the bungalow. The driveway. The car under that orange light.

"How many times did it happen?"

"Twice for definite"

"For definite?"

"Maybe three. Maybe more. Depends what you count."

I hated that answer.

"What does that mean?"

"It means there were other things".

The room around me seemed to narrow. I asked him what other things. Scratches down the side once. Wing mirror pushed in. Nothing major, he said.

But despite his words, his voice still seemed different. Small, and reserved.

"That sounds like kids," I said.

"That's what I told myself."

"But you didn't think it was kids?"

"No," he said. Too quickly. I waited, but he didn't say anything else. So I asked the question I had been circling since I called him. Something even now I hate mentioning to him, scared he will get defensive and angry again.

"Was it because of the affair?"

It felt strange saying that word to him. Affair. Like I'd taken something ugly and adult and placed it directly between us. Dad didn't answer right away. When he did, he sounded tired.

"That's what your Mum thinks?"

"She said it was probably someone connected to one of the women you were seeing."

He let out a breath through his nose.

"I'm sure she did."

"Was she wrong?"

Another silence. Then quietly, he said, "Not about me."

I didn't know what to do with that. He carried on before I had to.

"But about the tyres? I don't know. Maybe. I wondered the same thing later, when everything came out, but it didn't make sense."

"Why not?"

There was another pause. Then he finally responded.

"The timing was off,"

I remember pressing the phone harder against my ear.

"What do you mean, off?"

"I mean the first time it happened, nobody would have had a reason to do that."

"But you were already seeing someone?"

He didn't answer directly.

"That wasn't known."

"Maybe someone found out?"

"Maybe," he said. But he didn't sound convinced.

I thought of Mum in her red dressing gown. Rex behind the hedge. Me in the front garden, staring at the bits of metal on the ground.

"Did you report it?"

"No."

"Why not?"

"Because I was an idiot."

It was such a simple answer that I nearly laughed.

Then he added, "And because your Mum was already frightened enough."

The laugh went away.

"What does that mean?"

He sighed, and for the first time in the conversation I had the sense that he regretted answering the phone.

"She didn't like that house."

"Because of Rex?"

"I don't know."

"You don't know?"

"I don't think it was just Rex."

That sentence has been sitting in my head ever since. I don't think it was just Rex.

I asked him what he meant, and he did what Dad always does when a conversation gets too close to something real. He stepped sideways.

"She used to say she felt watched," he said. "That's all."

A feeling.

I thought about Mum laughing softly when I asked about Rex. No more strange than any other old man. I thought about her telling me I used to say someone was watching him. I thought about the hand on the fence.

"What did Rex say about the tyres?" I asked.

Dad gave a short laugh.

"Rex always had something to say."

"What did he say?"

Dad was quiet for a second.

"He said he heard something that night, that it sounded like someone shuffling down the side of the house, and that I should stop leaving the spare key under the mat."

I didn't understand why that made my stomach turn. Not immediately.

It was too ordinary. Too specific. The sort of thing neighbours say to each other when they want to sound helpful but also want you to know they've noticed more than they should.

"You had a spare key under the mat?" I asked.

"Everyone did back then."

That wasn't true, probably. It was just one of those things adults say when they want the past to excuse them.

"Where?"

"By the front door."

The front door was on the side of the bungalow. Not on the front like most houses. You had to walk down the narrow path between the garage and the wall, past my bedroom window, to reach it.

The mat sat there, apparently. Right beside the door. Right beside the wall of my room.

I tried to picture it. The path in darkness. The garage on one side, the bungalow on the other. The mat by the door. Someone bending down. Someone lifting it.

"Why did Rex say that?" I asked.

"Because I was locked out. I mentioned it to him."

"What?"

Dad sighed. "That morning, after the tyres, your Mum was inside with you and your sister. I'd gone out to the car, found the nails, made a fuss, all of that. At some point the door must have clicked shut behind me."

"So you went for the spare."

"Yeah."

"And it was gone?"

Another pause.

"Yes."

I felt the same coldness again. Not sudden this time. Slow. Every time I'd had one of these conversations, I would hope that some more information would leave me feeling relieved. But every conversation. Every thread pulled. Tightened the knot in my stomach.

"What do you mean gone?"

"I mean it wasn't there."

“Had Mum moved it?”

“That’s what I thought.”

“Did you ask her?”

Dad gave a short laugh, but there was no humour in it.

“Not properly.”

“Why not?”

“Because things weren’t exactly great between us.”

I waited. He didn’t continue.

“Dad.”

“What?”

“Why would you think Mum had moved it?”

This silence was different. Longer. More embarrassed.

Then he said, “Because she’d threatened to before.”

“Why?”

“Because I kept forgetting my keys.”

I didn’t say anything. He knew I didn’t believe that. Or maybe he knew I believed exactly enough of it.

“And because sometimes,” he said, more quietly, “I came home when I shouldn’t have been coming home.”

There it was again. That ugly adult thing laid across a childhood memory like a sheet over furniture. I thought about Mum and how horrible that time must have been for her.

“So you thought Mum took the spare key to lock you out?”

“Maybe.”

“Maybe?”

“I don’t know what I thought. I thought a lot of things back then.”

“But you didn’t change the locks?”

“No.”

“Why not?”

“Because I didn’t think someone had stolen it.”

“But it was missing.”

“I thought your Mum had it.”

“Did she?”

He didn’t answer quickly enough.

“Dad.”

“No,” he said.

The word was almost too quiet to hear.

“No?”

“She said she didn’t.”

I sat back.

“She said she didn’t have it, and you still didn’t change the locks?”

“You’re looking at it like it happened now,” he said, and for the first time he sounded irritated. Defensive. More like himself. “At the time, it was one more stupid argument in a house full of stupid arguments. Your Mum thought I was lying. I thought she was lying. Neither of us trusted the other one enough to be worried about a sodding key.”

That shut me up.

Because I could see it. Not forgive it, exactly. But understand why in that situation something like a key might seem like a small thing he didn't want to bring to the surface.

Two adults in a dying marriage, both convinced the other one was playing some petty game. Mum thinking Dad had taken the key so he could come and go without her knowing. Dad thinking Mum had taken it so she could catch him out or lock him out or punish him.

And somewhere in the middle of that, a key was missing from under the mat beside my bedroom wall.

“What did Rex say?” I asked.

“I told you.”

“No. After. When the key wasn’t there.”

Dad breathed out slowly.

“He said kids were always messing about down that path.”

Kids. There it was again. Kids put nails under tyres. Kids scratched cars. Kids stole keys from under mats. Kids made noises outside houses at night. Kids became the explanation.

“Was Callum there?” I asked, before I really knew why.

“What?”

“Callum. My friend. Did he ever play down that path?”

“I don’t remember.”

I did.

Not clearly. Not as one whole memory. But I remembered Callum crouched by the garage once, poking a stick into the gravel along the edge of the path. I remembered him telling me he’d found something secret. I remembered not caring because Callum was always finding something secret. I almost asked Dad if the key ever turned up.

Then I realised I didn’t want to know.

I asked anyway.

“Did you ever find it?”

“No.”

The answer came quickly this time.

Too quickly, maybe.

“So there was a key to the bungalow missing,” I said.

“For a while.”

“What does that mean?”

“It means we stopped using that one. We were moving to London a few months later anyway.”

“You stopped using the key?”

“We stopped leaving one under the mat.”

“But the missing one still opened the door.”

He didn’t answer.

“Dad.”

“Yes,” he said eventually. “It still would have opened the door.”

I looked around my living room again, but once more I wasn’t really seeing it. I was seeing that narrow path. The mat. The wall of my bedroom. The car on the driveway under the lamppost.

“And you never changed the locks?”

“No.”

“Why?”

This time, when he answered, his voice sounded smaller than I have ever heard it.

“Because I didn’t want another argument.”

I don’t know what I expected him to say. That wasn’t it.

I wanted to be angry with him. Part of me was. Part of me still is. But another part of me understood something I wish I didn’t. Fear is easy to miss when it arrives dressed as something familiar.

In our house, it dressed as marriage trouble. It dressed as Dad being selfish and Mum being suspicious. It dressed as Rex being nosy. It dressed as kids messing about down the side of the bungalow.

It dressed as every explanation that was already waiting to be used. I couldn't stop thinking about that missing key. I thought about the bungalow being on one level. About the front door on the side of the house. About my bedroom just beyond it. About the path outside my wall. And I thought about the memory I have always called a nightmare.

I nearly wrote it here.

I’ve tried to, more than once, but every time I start, it comes out wrong. Too neat. Too much like a story. Too much like I’m arranging things to frighten you. That isn’t what I want to do.

The truth is, I don’t know where the memory starts.

I know there was a flash. I know there was the living room. I know I woke up somewhere I shouldn’t have been.

But there are pieces around it that don’t fit anymore. Pieces I used to leave out because dreams don’t need to make sense. Real things do.

Before I ended the call with Dad, I asked him one more question.

“Did I ever sleepwalk?”

He was quiet for a moment. Then he said, “Your Mum said you did once.”

“Once?”

“That I remember.”

“At the bungalow?”

“Yes.”

I closed my eyes.

“What happened?”

“I don’t know,” he said. “I wasn’t there when she found you.”

“Found me where?”

Another pause.

“The living room.”

I didn’t say anything. Dad cleared his throat.

“You were little. Five, maybe six. You’d probably had a nightmare.”

That was the explanation. There it was again, waiting exactly where everyone had left it. A nightmare. Sleepwalking.

A strange child doing strange child things in a house where everyone was already too tired and too unhappy to ask the next question. But now there was a key missing from under the mat.

Now there had been nails under Dad’s tyres. Now Rex had heard something on the path outside my bedroom and told everyone it was probably kids.

And now I know I didn’t invent waking up on the living room floor.

That part was real.

I’m going to speak to Mum before I write about the rest of it.

Not because I want to drag this out.

Because I need to know whether she remembers the curtains being open.
