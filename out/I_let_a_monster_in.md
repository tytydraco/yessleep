At some point in my life, I let a monster in. I don’t know how, or why. But it came, I allowed it, and so it stayed.

The earliest memory I have of it was a summer, when I was maybe 9 or 10 years old. Every summer, my Granddad would come up from where I was born in Texas, to where I now lived with my parents in New Jersey. He was my favorite person in the world, and when my step-dad made us move, I was heartbroken. So, in the summers afterward, he’d drive across the country, pick me up, and we’d have a road trip back home. Then I got to spend the summers in Texas with him.

I loved the long drive. I loved the musty motel rooms. I loved the quiet, early mornings out in the sticks. I loved the mediocre meals at the rest stop Cracker Barrels.

But one summer, when I was trying to fall asleep in the guest bedroom of my Granddad’s house, I was gripped by something. I was always an anxious kid, but that night was different. The house was too quiet. Every creak sent chills through my body. Something was different about the shadows in the corners of the room. It felt like they were moving. It felt like something was watching. It felt like something was waiting.

A tightness creeped into my chest. I felt like I couldn’t breathe. Now in my 30’s, I know that what was happening was a panic attack. But this was the first one that I ever had, and I thought I was dying. I screamed for help. I gripped my chest. My Granddad came stumbling sleepily into the room, asking what was going on. I told him, and he sat by my bedside, comforting me. But his words fell on deaf ears. For the first time in my life, I was very aware of my mortality, and the fear of death was now something at the forefront of my consciousness. As my Granddad spoke softly to me, my gaze fell upon the corner of the room, where I swear the shadows had been dancing around just moments ago.

I saw eyes. Or, at least the whites of a pair of eyes. They were low to the ground, like whatever I was seeing was my height, or crouching. They just watched me, unblinking. They never moved. Eventually, I was so exhausted that I fell asleep as my Granddad stroked my head.

When I woke the next morning, I convinced myself that it was just my imagination. That somewhere in the panic, my mind had played tricks on me. But for the rest of that summer, it felt like something was always standing over my shoulder, still waiting for…something.

When my Granddad had to bring me back home at the end of August, I saw on the retaining wall outside my parents home, and watched him drive away. I cried and cried. I hated having to come back home. My mother dealt with horrible depression that often kept her bedridden. My step-dad worked long hours, and took any overtime that came his way. He did it to provide for us, but I suspect also because it was easier to be at work than come home and face a struggling wife and 3 young children.

As I sat on that wall weeping, I started to hear a noise coming from a nearby storm drain. I looked at it, and my blood ran cold. There were those eyes again. Even in the broad daylight, in the shadow of the drain, there was something unsettling about how white they were. I listened, trying to figure out what the noise was, while not letting the eyes out of my line of sight.

It was mocking me. It was imitating my cries and sobs. It sounded just like me.

“Stop!” I screamed, and I stood and ran inside.

Many years later, my Granddad died. It was sudden. One day he was fine. The next, he was feeling a little off, and went to the hospital just as a precaution. Before I knew it, he was in a medically induced coma. He was being kept alive just so my mother and I could fly from New Jersey to say goodbye.

When we got there, he wasn’t my Granddad anymore. He was a husk. His body lay lifeless, and a tube ran down his throat pumping air into his lungs. He didn’t speak. He didn’t open his eyes. He had been gone for a while. It gutted me that I would never hear his voice again. I could hardly bear the thought. My family still gave me the chance to be alone in the room with him to say goodbye, so I did, because it felt like the thing to do. I stood there in that empty room, with the buzz of the numerous machines blaring in my ears. I wanted to hug him, but I couldn’t bring myself to touch his skin. I just stood close, and wracked my brain for the thing to say.

“I love you. I’m sorry we didn’t get here sooner.” I whispered. The tears started to fall as I fell to my knees beside the hospital bed.

Suddenly, I felt a hand on my shoulder. I wanted to believe that it was my mom, my Grandma, or an aunt or uncle offering reassurance. But something felt wrong. The hand felt cold. Something in my body begged me not to turn around. So I didn’t. I didn’t move a muscle. The hand remained. It stayed that way for probably just a few moments, but it felt like an eternity. Eventually, the silence was broken.

“I can take it from here if you want.” it said. It still, after all these years, sounded like it was mocking me. No, that’s not what it was. It was…my voice. It wasn’t quite right. There was a deep, sinister undertone to it. But it was my voice. As I reached this realization, a cold sweat broke out over my entire body. All I could manage to do was shake my head slightly. When I did, the hand left my shoulder, and it finally felt safe to turn around.

Later that night, they turned off the life support, and my Granddad died.

When the family got home, the mood was understandably low. Someone made the decision to crack open an old bottle of whiskey that my Granddad always kept in the cabinet, though I had never actually seen him drink it. They started pouring little glasses for everyone, including me. I was 18 at this point, but had never taken a drink. I was more of a solitary, indoor kid. Not much for parties, and barely had any friends to invite me to parties anyway. But I was desperate for relief from the night’s events, and so I drank it. It tasted horrible, but as the burning in my throat settled, and the euphoria set in, I was fully in love. I snuck several more glasses that night.

I slept on an air mattress alone in the living room of my Grandma’s home that night. I laid there, in a complete stupor, staring up at the ceiling. I felt a tickle in my ear that made me giggle a little bit. It kept happening, and eventually I realized what it was, and I froze. It was breathing. Something was breathing shallow little breaths in my ear. It was so close. But again, I couldn’t bring myself to roll over and find the source.

“My turn.” it whispered. And this time the voice sounded just right. Just like me.

Something shifted in that moment. I was still seeing through my eyes. I was still there. But I was in the backseat now. Someone else was driving. I looked at my hands and they felt so very far away. I drew breath, but it didn’t register the same way inside my body. I was horrified. I tried to scream, and nothing came out. My hand rose, and it shook a finger at me, as if to say “Nuh-uh-uh.”

The next few years I watched in horror as my life went on without me. Drinking. Drugging. Sex. Lies. Stealing. I watched it hurt people; people I loved. Ruin friendships.

Eventually the consequences caught up. It was either jail or rehab. It chose the latter, obviously.

The first morning in rehab, *I* woke up. Not it. Me. Back in the driver’s seat. I was in agony, shaking from the years of abuse and the withdrawal. I felt like I had to learn to walk again. Of course it had left *now.* It wasn’t fun anymore. We were trapped, in pain, and run ragged. It moved on, I guess.

Rehab was an odd experience. I shared about “my” experiences; I could recall them because I was always watching. People could relate. It didn’t feel like I was being completely honest, but trying to explain what had happened would land me a trip to the psych ward.

When I got out, I got involved in the local sober community. Went to meetings. It had alienated everyone I knew, so I welcomed the chance to build new relationships.

It’s been 2 years now. I have a job I love. I met a girl in AA that I fell completely in love with. I no longer live in fear of that thing coming back. Things are good.

But as I’m writing this, I’m sitting at my desk in the dark. It’s late. I refuse to look away from the screen. I refuse to look at the corner. Because as I sit here typing, I am fearful of losing all that I have built in these last 2 years. I fear being taken hostage in my own body again. I fear all of this because suddenly I feel a hand on my shoulder, and I am home alone.
