It used to seem exciting. Intimate. It was our small involuntary ritual: just before we kissed with that clumsy urgency of people who still desire each other after years, her pupils would dilate, her eyes would open as if she had just seen something impossible, and the skin on her arms would prickle with a visible shiver. I used to joke that she looked possessed. She would laugh and tell me not to say things like that.

A week ago, it stopped happening.

And I wish that were the only thing that’s changed.

I’m writing this here because I need to put in order what has happened. Because if something happens to me, I want there to be a version of events that doesn’t depend only on what she says.

It all started about three months ago. The first time I noticed that expression was on an ordinary night. We were on the couch, the house silent, and when I leaned in to kiss her neck I felt her tense. I thought it was desire. She lifted her gaze over my shoulder, toward the dark hallway, and opened her eyes in an exaggerated way. The skin on her neck prickled under my fingers.

“What’s wrong?” I asked, laughing.

“Nothing,” she replied, but it took her a while to look at me again.

After that, everything continued as usual. No oddities. No shadows moving.

But the second time it happened the same way. And the third. Always at that exact instant, as if something were being activated.

I began checking the hallway before turning off the lights. I secured the windows, checked the deadbolt. I even started thinking someone might be spying on us from the backyard, so I installed a cheap camera connected to my phone.

It recorded nothing.

One night I decided to watch her closely. I pretended to be distracted, looking at my phone, and suddenly hugged her from behind. I felt the immediate change in her body. Her breathing became shallow and her eyes, reflected in the turned-off television in front of us, opened with an intensity that wasn’t normal. They weren’t looking at the hallway that time. They were looking behind me.

I felt an irrational urge not to turn around.

“Do you see it?” she whispered.

“See what?”

She didn’t answer. Her pupils seemed to follow something moving slowly.

I turned around.

There was nothing there.

She laughed again, but it wasn’t natural anymore. It was a laugh trying to cover something else.

The following weeks were a succession of small details I preferred to ignore. Doors I found half-open when I was sure I had closed them. The kitchen faucet dripping after she swore she had tightened it properly. The backyard camera capturing brief interference at the same hour, almost always when we were in the bedroom.

She started sleeping poorly. She said she dreamed of someone standing beside the bed. She never saw a face, only the feeling that it was watching her with absolute, almost scientific attention.

“But I don’t feel like it wants to hurt me,” she told me one morning over breakfast. “It’s like it’s waiting.”

“Waiting for what?”

She shrugged.

What was unsettling was that she didn’t seem scared. Nervous, yes. But also… expectant.

The last time her eyes did that thing was eight days ago. I remember because that night the backyard camera stopped working entirely. I tried restarting it several times without success.

In the bedroom, while I was kissing her, I felt the same shiver on her skin. Her eyes opened, but this time they weren’t looking behind me or toward the door. They were looking directly at my face. Or rather, at something overlapping my face.

Her expression changed abruptly. Desire turned into disappointment.

“It’s not you,” she murmured.

I pulled away.

“What does that mean?”

She blinked several times, as if waking up.

“Nothing. Sorry. I don’t know why I said that.”

We didn’t try again that night. Nor the next. Nor any after that.

Since then, her eyes have remained normal. Her skin doesn’t prickle. There’s no electric tension. It’s as if something has stopped coming to our private appointment.

And that’s when the worst part began.

She became distant. Not cold, but absent. She spent long stretches sitting on the bed staring at the wall in front of us. At first I thought she was depressed. I tried to talk to her, suggested therapy. She said it wasn’t necessary.

“It’s almost ready,” she would repeat sometimes.

“What is almost ready?”

“Nothing.”

Four days ago, I woke in the middle of the night with the sensation that someone was in the room. It wasn’t a sound that alerted me, but a pressure on my chest, as if the air were heavier.

I opened my eyes.

She was sitting beside me, completely upright, staring toward the door. The dim streetlight outlined her rigid silhouette.

“What are you doing?” I whispered.

“Shh.”

Her voice didn’t sound like hers.

I followed her gaze. The door was closed. Nothing moved.

“It’s learning,” she said.

I sat up, no trace of sleep left.

“Who?”

She slowly turned her head toward me. Her eyes were open, very open, but they no longer had that nervous spark from before. Now they were empty, as if looking through me.

“You.”

A chill ran down my spine.

“What about me?”

She didn’t answer. She lay down again and, within seconds, began breathing with the deep rhythm of someone asleep.

I didn’t sleep again.

The next morning I found the backyard camera on the ground, broken. There were no signs of forced entry on the window or back door. She claimed she hadn’t touched it.

“Maybe it was the wind,” she said, though there had been no storm.

That same day, while I was working in the study, I heard her voice in the hallway.

“Almost,” she whispered.

I stepped out of the room. She was alone, standing in front of the mirror in the entryway.

“Who are you talking to?”

“No one.”

Her reflection took a fraction of a second to imitate her smile.

Maybe that last part is my imagination. I replay it over and over and I’m not sure. But at the time it seemed obvious.

I’ve started noticing things in myself too. Sometimes I catch myself staring at the hallway without knowing how long I’ve been doing it. Or I find myself holding my breath when I approach her from behind, as if expecting her eyes to widen again and confirm that there’s still something else in the room.

Last night I decided to provoke it.

I approached carefully, hugged her, and whispered in her ear. Nothing explicit, just words that used to ignite her. She responded with an automatic smile. Her hands traced my back with familiar ease.

But her eyes didn’t change.

I watched her closely, searching for that dilation, that almost animal gesture. It didn’t appear.

And then I realized I missed it.

That feeling that there was a third invisible witness, watching us from the threshold. That extra tension. That shiver that didn’t come from desire alone.

I pulled back slightly.

“Do you see it now?” I asked without thinking.

She frowned.

“See what?”

Her confusion was genuine.

For a moment I felt panic, not because there might be something in the house, but because of the certainty that maybe I had imagined everything. That her expression, her words, the interference, the reflection in the mirror… were loose pieces I had assembled to give shape to a fear.

But that same night something happened that doesn’t fit simple paranoia.

I woke again with that pressure on my chest. This time she wasn’t sitting up. We were both lying on our backs, staring at the ceiling.

“Don’t move,” she said softly.

“Why?”

“It’s right above.”

I didn’t look. I didn’t want to confirm anything. But I felt the irrational urge to open my eyes wider, like she used to.

“It’s not interested in you anymore,” she continued. “Now it’s looking at me.”

I felt an absurd stab of jealousy.

“What does it want?”

“For us to learn.”

Her hand found mine under the sheets. It was icy.

“Learn what?”

“To look.”

There was a silence so dense it felt material.

I don’t know how long we stayed like that. Eventually sleep overcame me, or maybe I lost consciousness. When I woke, dawn light was coming through the window and she was sleeping deeply beside me.

Today, as I write this, she’s in the kitchen. I hear the steady sound of the knife against the cutting board. A calm rhythm.

I’ve been reviewing old recordings from the backyard camera. In one of them, just before the interference began, something strange appears. Not a clear figure. More like a vertical distortion, like a shadow cast by no object.

The unsettling thing is that the distortion moves when we move inside the house. As if it’s reacting.

I’ve thought about leaving for a few days. Going to my brother’s house, clearing my head. But something holds me back. A morbid curiosity, perhaps. Or the need to check whether her eyes will widen again.

Because last night, for a second, I thought I saw it.

Not him, or it, or whatever it is. I saw the beginning of the change in her gaze. A slight dilation. An expectant shine. And then nothing.

As if something were about to happen and stopped.

If in a few days everything goes back to normal, maybe I’ll laugh about this. Maybe I’ll read it and think I exaggerated small gestures into a threat.

But if it doesn’t, if her eyes open that impossible way again and this time I’m not the one at the center of her gaze, I want it on record that I noticed from the beginning. That there were signs.

I don’t know if I’ll publish this or if it will remain saved in the draft of some forgotten forum. I don’t know if anyone who reads it will recognize something similar in their own house, in the way their partner looks at an empty corner before kissing them.

All I know is that tonight I’m going to try once more to approach her. I’m going to carefully observe that instant before, that suspended second when everything can be revealed.

And if her eyes open in that impossible way again, I promise I won’t look away this time.

I want to learn to look too.

If tomorrow I have something more to tell, I’ll add it here.
