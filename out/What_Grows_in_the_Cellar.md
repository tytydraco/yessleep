Michael Reilly had always told himself that moving to the country would fix things. Not heal them—nothing healed the hole where Karen used to be—but patch them over, the way you tape plastic over a broken window until spring. The farmhouse outside Derry was cheap because nobody else wanted it. The real-estate lady had smiled too wide when she handed him the keys. “The last folks just… left,” she’d said, like that explained the price. Michael didn’t ask questions. He had a seven-year-old daughter who still woke up screaming for a mother who wasn’t coming back, and a bank account that looked like it had been chewed on by dogs. Fresh start. That was the phrase he kept repeating, like a prayer.

Sadie was small for her age, all elbows and tangled brown hair, the kind of kid who talked to herself in complete sentences. On the first night they carried their boxes inside, she marched straight to the root cellar door at the back of the kitchen, the one that sagged on its hinges like a tired old man. Michael had planned to nail it shut—cellars were for spiders and broken dreams—but Sadie was already turning the knob.

“Daddy,” she said, voice bright as new pennies, “there’s somebody down here who wants to meet me.”

He laughed, the way fathers do when they’re pretending not to be afraid of the dark. “Just a mouse, kiddo. Or maybe a cricket choir.”

But Sadie shook her head, solemn. “His name is Mr. Root. He says the last little girl didn’t listen right. But I will.”

Michael told himself it was normal. Imaginary friends were what kids did when the world went sideways. He let her play down there while he unpacked. The cellar smelled of damp earth and something sweeter, like overripe fruit left too long in the sun. He figured it was just the old potatoes the previous owners had forgotten.

That first week, the house felt almost kind. Mornings were coffee and cartoons. Evenings were stories about Karen—how she used to dance in the kitchen while the radio played. Sadie would listen, nodding, then slip away to the cellar steps and whisper. Michael caught fragments sometimes. *Show me how to make the bad dreams stay away. Show me what happens when the skin forgets it’s skin.*

He should have paid closer attention.

The vines came on the ninth night.

He woke to a soft, wet sound, like someone sucking on a cut finger. In the hallway outside Sadie’s room, a thin green tendril had pushed up between two floorboards. It was thicker than a pencil, pale as a grub, and when Michael touched it, it flexed under his finger like a living muscle. He grabbed the kitchen shears and snipped it clean. The vine didn’t bleed sap. It bled red—warm, human red—spattering across the linoleum like a nosebleed. The smell was iron and something else, something that made his stomach remember every hospital waiting room he’d ever sat in.

Sadie stood in the doorway in her My Little Pony nightgown, eyes shining. “Mr. Root says you’re not supposed to cut his fingers, Daddy. He says it makes him cross.”

Michael cleaned it up, told himself it was some kind of freak root system from the old well out back. But the next morning the fridge was full of things that hadn’t been there when he went to bed. Carrots, thick and orange, lying in the crisper like severed thumbs. When he picked one up, it twitched. Tiny white teeth—perfect, child-sized—pushed through the skin where the green top should have been. They clicked together once, tasting the air, then went still. He threw the whole drawer into the trash, gagging.

Sadie ate cereal without complaint. She hummed a tune he didn’t recognize, something low and wet, like water moving under stones.

The photographs changed on the third week.

They were the cheap kind—school pictures and Polaroids thumbtacked to the kitchen wall because Michael hadn’t gotten around to frames. First it was just the eyes. In the picture of him and Karen on their wedding day, Karen’s smile had gone stiff, her mouth open in a silent scream. By afternoon her whole face was twisted, cheeks stretched like taffy, eyes bulging the way they had in the ICU when the machines finally quit. Michael took the photo down, hands shaking, and flipped it over. On the back, in pencil he didn’t recognize, someone had written: *She saw what grows.*

Sadie was drawing at the table. She held up a crayon picture: stick figures of a man and a little girl, and between them a black shape with too many roots for arms. “Mr. Root says the house is remembering,” she told him. “He says it’s almost finished waking up. The last family left too soon. They only got the daddy. Not the little girl.”

Michael called the real-estate lady that night. She didn’t answer. He left three voicemails, voice cracking like a teenager’s. When he finally reached her the next morning, she sounded like she was speaking from the bottom of a well. “The old man who lived there before—he kept saying the cellar wanted a child. Said it was older than Derry itself. Older than the Indians, even. Something that grew down there when the glaciers left. It feeds slow. It waits for the right family. Then it… finishes the garden.” She laughed, a brittle sound. “I thought he was crazy. People say all kinds of things about that house. You’re not thinking of leaving, are you? The price was so good.”

He hung up.

That afternoon he went down the cellar steps with a flashlight and a claw hammer. The air was thick, humid, like breath against the back of his neck. The dirt floor was soft under his boots, spongy, the way a belly gets soft after too many years of bad living. In the far corner, where the foundation stones met the earth, something moved.

It wasn’t a man. It wasn’t quite anything. A knot of roots and pale flesh braided together, tall as Michael but stooped, like it had been waiting in the same hunched position for decades. Two wet holes where eyes should be. A mouth that was just a split in the wood, lined with the same tiny teeth he’d seen on the carrots. It smelled like every grave that had ever been dug too shallow.

“Hello, Michael,” it said. Its voice was Sadie’s voice, but slowed down, dragged through mud. “Your girl listens so nice. She’s learning how to help me grow. How to make the whole town into my garden. One house at a time.”

Michael swung the hammer. It sank into the thing’s side with a sound like a boot stepping into rotten fruit. Sap—blood—gushed over his wrist, hot and sticky. The thing laughed, low and patient, the way old things laugh when they know time is on their side.

Upstairs, Sadie started screaming.

He found her in the kitchen, standing on a chair, reaching for the ceiling. Vines had punched through the plaster like green fingers. One of them had wrapped around her wrist, gentle as a bracelet. Where it touched skin, tiny white rootlets were already sliding in, delicate as eyelashes. Her eyes were wide, shining with something that wasn’t fear anymore.

“Daddy,” she whispered, “Mr. Root says I can stay forever. He says the town needs new seeds. He says you can be the fertilizer.”

Michael grabbed her, yanked her free. The vine tore away with a wet pop and a spray of red. Sadie’s arm was already changing—pale green lines racing under the skin like veins full of chlorophyll. He carried her out the back door, past the garden where the tomatoes had split open overnight and were showing rows of baby teeth inside their red mouths.

They made it as far as the truck before the house began to breathe.

Windows fogged from the inside. Floorboards groaned upward as roots thick as firehoses pushed through. The front porch sagged like a jaw coming unhinged. Michael fumbled the keys, got the engine started, and backed down the long dirt drive while Sadie thrashed in the passenger seat, whispering things no child should know—how the old ones had planted themselves here before the first white man ever saw the Penobscot, how they’d waited through wars and fires and suburban sprawl, patient as stones, until a lonely father brought them fresh soil in the shape of a little girl.

In the rearview mirror he saw the farmhouse settle back on its foundations, almost disappointed. The vines sank out of sight. The lights in the windows flickered once, then glowed a soft, friendly yellow, the way houses do when they’re waiting for the next family.

They drove until the sun came up, until Derry was just a smear of streetlights in the mirror. Sadie’s arm had gone back to pink and ordinary, the green lines fading like a bad dream. She slept against the window, thumb in her mouth the way she hadn’t done since she was three.

Michael kept driving. He didn’t know where they were going. He only knew that behind them, something ancient and hungry had opened its eyes all the way for the first time in a hundred years. It had tasted a child’s trust and a father’s terror, and it had liked the flavor.

Somewhere back in the dark, the cellar door stood open.

And Mr. Root—patient, endless Mr. Root—was already whispering to the next pair of headlights that would turn down that long dirt road, looking for a cheap old farmhouse and a fresh start.

He had all the time in the world.

The garden was only just beginning.
