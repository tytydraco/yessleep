It began on a Monday morning when I stepped outside the house and felt something soft crinkle beneath my shoes, and knew it wasn’t the doormat I was treading on. The sky was still winter-dark and the porch light had long ago stopped working, but I gathered it was paper of some kind, probably a letter or a leaflet. Which struck me as unusual because my mail was always deposited in the mailbox by the road, never directly to my house: a pesty and arduous ten-minute walk up a windy lane on a narrow hill.

When I gauged it under the light of my car interior, I saw that I was holding an envelope. It was brown and bare and had been sealed shut with duct tape. My initial reaction was the same as I suspect yours is right now: *Who seals an envelope with duct tape?* I considered throwing it away right then and there, figuring it was the work of bored teenagers playing a prank on the lonesome woman up in the quiet house on the hill. *This will definitely freak her out* I could picture them saying, laughing away as they did the deed. Alas, curiosity got the better of me and I opened it.

At first I didn’t think there was anything inside. No sign of a letter, nothing. But when I prized it opened further, I saw something in the bottom corner, small and stuck to the paper.

A poker card. The ten of clubs.

I turned it over in my fingers. I half expected a note of some kind in marker, but there was nothing on the front or back. Just a plain, old ten of clubs.

I laughed. A jolt of nervous energy shot through my body and into my stomach. It wasn’t really the duct tape or the card that freaked me out, but the fact that someone had carried this out with me as their target. And they had walked up to my front door to do it.

Something else played on my mind. I didn’t really believe it was young pranksters. The place I lived in was less a neighbourhood for young families than a scattered collection of homes inhabited by folk old enough to call me young . . . and I’m 43. I couldn’t even remember the last time I saw a teenager in the area.

Anyway, I put the playing card back in the envelope and left it on the back seat of my car, and didn’t think about it again until the following Monday morning – when I left the house for work and stepped on something that crinkled beneath my shoes. I felt my heart expand like a balloon in my chest then; I thought it might shoot straight up my throat and out of my mouth. *Woman drops dead from harmless prank* the headlines would read, front page of the local paper. But I withheld a gasp and composed myself, picked the thing up off the doormat and headed to my car.

Another envelope. Sealed with duct tape. I went to open it immediately, but sensed something that hadn’t occurred to me the previous Monday: that I was being watched. My eyes shot to the trees opposite my house – the most obvious place to observe me from with some degree of secrecy – but they were met by my own startled reflection in the car window. I shut off the interior light. Everything was dark and I felt sick and I didn’t want to look out, because the thought of actually seeing someone *really* would’ve made me drop dead, but I pinched myself into action. Thankfully there was nobody there: only the trees and the sway of branches and a flurry of leaves in the wind.

And eyes.

Were they eyes?

Little white dots on the far side of the wood, blinking.

*Crow’s eyes*. *Perched on a tree branch. Looking directly at me.*

I revved the car engine to be sure, and seconds later watched a bird fly off the branch and into the dark sky.

I felt silly. How could an envelope – or two – cause such anxiety? A stupid prank? I looked at the envelope in my hand and laughed, tore away at the duct tape, a new sense of bravery overriding the fear in my stomach. *It’s just a stupid prank, laugh it off and be done with it.* As I turned the envelope upside down, I started giggling to myself with a kind of crazed enthusiasm. *What will it be this time? A tarot card? A Top Trump?* But when the nine of clubs fell onto my lap that brief stint of enthusiasm very quickly morphed into something else entirely, something sinister, a choking feeling in the pit of my chest.

The nine of clubs? What did that mean? I tore the card in half and sped to work, displacing that toxic concoction of fear and anxiety with the adrenaline produced by driving 65 mph down a narrow, windy lane. I barely slowed down the whole way there.

The next day I told myself that if there was another envelope sitting on my doormat the following Monday, I would take action. And like clockwork, there was. Can you guess which card? That’s right . . . the eight of clubs. So I ordered a home security camera with night vision, took the day off work and waited up with a friend all Sunday night ready to grab a look at that son of a bitch. But nobody showed. They had placed the envelope under the windshield wiper of my car, which was out of view of the camera. Which means they were watching me. You can already guess which card was inside.

I contacted the police and they weren't exactly helpful. Such behaviour came under the definition of harassment, and could be prosecuted under the law. The only issue, of course, was the identity of the perpetrator. *Do you know of anyone who wishes to cause you harm or distress?* they had asked. And I couldn’t answer. My life was dull, uneventful, boring. No crazy exes, no nutty family members. Just me and this place: the house, the trees, the birds.

I received my first envelope almost ten weeks ago. Each week they showed up somewhere new: my backdoor, my mailbox, one was even pinned to a tree. It's Sunday evening right now, and I’m sitting by the door with a baseball bat. What would you do?
