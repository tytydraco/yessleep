When I was little, I had a brother named Alaric, Alaric Jones. He was a young man, a young man like any other, rebellious, impulsive and aggressive. At least, that was how he was. I loved him, despite everything.

This behavior of his led to threats from my parents who always said, "If you don't behave, we will send you to your grandmother's house. If you don't get good grades, we will send you to your grandmother's house."

Back then, I did not know who this grandmother they kept talking about was.

We barely left the house.

The only places I knew in my routine were school, church and my home.

We could not go anywhere else besides these.

No sleeping over at friends' houses, no staying up late on the phone.

For me at eight years old or younger, I really do not remember how old I was, maybe it is the age I am now, which is already affecting my mind, but for me as a child, that was not a bother. I played, I played board games, I spent time inside my own head, but my brother felt that lack of freedom.

He would run away every night to the local parties, drink, smoke, spend long days away.

That pushed my parents to their limit.

They packed our bags, put us in a car and took us to a deserted place, where I went through the worst horror of my life.

When I arrived, I immediately hated that place.

How can anyone live in a place so hot, so sad, so deserted?

I met my maternal grandmother, a woman with slightly sunburned skin, wrinkled eyes, sagging, her scalp exposed by the few white strands on her head.

With a gentle smile that had few teeth, she raised one hand, guiding us through the house.

The days that followed in that house were relatively calm. I would spend hours playing with my little rag doll in the living room, which had almost nothing worth reporting here.

It was on one of those afternoons that I saw something that took me years to get out of my head.

I was playing with my little doll on the floor.

I lifted my head when sounds, like whispers, low, mixed, almost inaudible, began.

My brother was not there, he had probably run off somewhere.

He hated that place, always grumbling that he would rather die than live trapped.

When I got closer to the source of the sound, I found my grandmother's bedroom door slightly open.

I peeked inside.

Terrible idea.

She was on her knees, naked, bowing to something I cannot describe, but that after my research, I found out to be a pagan god.

That was the closest I found to describe what it resembled.

When I saw that she was about to turn her head, I left, not running, walking on tiptoe, holding my breath.

After that day, I did not look at that old woman with kind eyes.

The atmosphere was always tense when we were gathered, whether to eat or to do chores outside, which mostly involved putting out food for the few animals.

My brother spent each day in that place as if he were in a prison.

He did not try to communicate with my grandmother, much less did he like the place.

He grumbled, rolled his eyes, yawned, tried to call my parents.

One night, he even lost his mind in the small room where he slept, which had once been one of her children's rooms, something curious, because my mother never spoke about having a brother.

My brother's anger reached its limit. One night, he lost his mind over something simple, when my grandmother accidentally broke something of his. He pushed her, and she fell to the floor.

It was in that moment that I saw something in that woman's eyes change.

That same night, I woke up to something. The memories are fuzzy, but I still remember that I got up, finding her on her knees in the kitchen, cleaning something off the floor, the bucket with something brown mixed with water.

I ran away to my room before she turned her face. I have always been good at hiding.

The next day, I was feeding the pigs when I was called. She had made a cake, a cake whose flavor I still remember and I keep what I found in that batter.

After that, I never saw my brother again.

He disappeared.

With him went a piece of me.

My grandmother called my parents and said that my brother had run away.

The police arrived, searched, took notes, but did not solve anything.

They ruled it as if he had simply run away, due to his history, a story that my parents accepted.

But I did not.

Years later, I still hold in my memory the simple memory of him. Even though our bond was not strong back then, I still keep everything, written down.
