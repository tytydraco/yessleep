Before I got into doll maintenance, I was a medical student. It was hard work, sure, lots of sleepless nights and the kind of headaches that come from cramming way too much into the brain in way too short a time. 

But the reason I dropped out was that I couldn’t stand the stress of being responsible for someone else’s life and death. Everything else was tough, but nothing I couldn’t stick to and grind out. The closer I got to residency, the greater the fear got. Missing key details, making fatal errors, these became less an abstract worry, and more an actual probability with each day that drew me closer to actual patients. 

I woke up one night in a cold sweat. In a nightmare, I had caused the death of a child messing up in surgery. I was about to tell the parents that the light of their lives was dead. Because of me. 

I left medical school that very day. 

It took me a while to figure out what to do with my life. I remember the first time I told my parents I wanted to be a doctor. I was probably 6, or 7. I had said it on a whim. But I’ll always remember the way my parents’ eyes lit up. The pride that flashed upon their faces. The way they praised my ambition and told me it was a noble, wonderful goal. I hadn’t wanted anything else since then. 

My parents’ pride alone fuelled me all the way through to medical school. I studied like a demon possessed. I needed to keep that approval from my parents. I craved it from others too, eventually.

Imagine my parents’ reaction when they found out I had quit med school. The pride and joy of their lives, now a failure. 

Still, I comforted myself that at least, that disappointment would be nothing compared to if I had killed someone at work from malpractice. 

Back to the doll thing. I knew I wanted to do something to do with repairing and fixing stuff. Under that heavy cloud of dread, the daily fear of messing up, I had enjoyed what I learnt in med school. I knew human anatomy well. I was fascinated by the functions and quirks of every organ, every part. I loved looking at the details given to me, the symptoms, their locations, contexts, timings etc., and putting it all together to identify a cause. To know what went wrong, why, and then be able to know just the thing to do to fix it. 

To pay the rent, I found an odd job working for an antique store right by my apartment. I couldn’t afford to miss rent and be kicked out. I couldn’t go home, couldn’t face my parents’ terrible mix of disappointment, anger, disdain and worry. 

The boss was nice enough. He taught me how to fix up and prettify the antiques that arrived at his store. Broken pocket watches, intricate old chests, ancient carved chairs, I would take whatever it was, look at the parts, identify what was off, and figure out how to fix it, then make it look good again. 

I worked on my first doll about two months into the job. The boss had told me that dolls were special. That customers viewed them differently. A scratch on an old watch could be overlooked, or seen as a symbol of its age and story.  A scratch on a doll, however, would make many customers uncomfortable. Upset. A spinning toy that didn’t balance quite well was acceptable, maybe even interesting because of it. But a doll that blinked each eye at different speeds, that would be unnerving to many. 

It was because they were made to resemble humans, was what he said. When people see flaws in dolls, they automatically associate it with flaws in humans. Which makes them think of disfigurement, pain, suffering, illnesses and death. And most customers didn’t like that. 

The boss trusted me by then. He said I had a steady hand, a way of dealing with delicate parts with gentle precision. So he taught me how to fix the doll. How to resew the clothes, refit the shoes, trim the hair and fix the clockwork. 

I fell in love with the work right from that first doll, with her beautiful ebony eyes, and the lifelike curve of a smile.

It was like I was a doctor again. Or a med student, at least. I could look at anatomy, figure out the issues, and find ways to solve them. And all without the risk of killing anyone. I could work the magic I loved, without the weight of life and death pressing down on me.  Dolls didn’t scream, bleed, or cry out. Dolls didn’t have heartbroken caretakers threatening to sue you.

It took me another year and a half of working with the antiques store owner before I developed enough skill and reputation to start my own business. My old boss was generous about it. He didn’t mind. I was specialising in doll maintenance, after all. Very niche, and not much of a competitor. He even sent me business now and again, when he had too much to fix, and dolls that he found too time consuming and complicated to deal with. 

My business did okay. You would be surprised how many people are into dolls. About a year in, I even had enough funds to hire an assistant. Let’s call her Millie. Millie was young, eager, and honestly, pretty talented in the craft. She took over much of the simpler but tedious work, including customer service and logistical stuff at the store, so I didn’t even have to be at the store on some days.

One of my best customers was a middle-aged gentleman. Let’s call him Hugh. Hugh came to my store once, took a tour of my handiwork, and requested that I pay him house calls. He had a collection of dolls he needed maintained, and he was loath to move them for fear of causing damage. He was willing to pay generously to make up for the trip down and back. 

I agreed, naturally. 

My first visit to Hugh’s home was full of surprises. Given his generous remuneration, I had half expected to turn up at a large, fancy mansion. Instead, I found myself right at the edge of what many would’ve called the dodgy part of town.

He stayed in a compact, rather shabby place. I mean it was still a sight better looking than the tiny apartment I still rented. At least he had a yard, overgrown though it might be.

I had expected the dolls to be in display cases or in their own room, but they were everywhere. From the moment I entered the house, I came face to face with one on the shoe shelf. I turned, and there were five seated around a round wooden table, in their own chairs. I looked up, and there were seven on a shelf, learning haphazardly against one another. 

They were in every part of the house. Three in his bed, eight on the bedside ledge. A few in the toilet. Several on the couch. I didn’t know how Hugh could possibly have space for himself in that house. 

Still, that wasn’t my problem. It wasn’t my place to judge. 

I got started with the first three dolls that day, taking six hours to fix them all up and make them look pretty again. I had to cut their hair, which, being made of yarn, had grown longer with the moisture. I also had to patch up their dresses, steam them, rewear them. I readjusted some awkwardly turned out limbs, and changed out the mirrors in two of the dolls’ eyes. Did you know that mirrors are often used in dolls’ eyes to provide that sparkle of life? Certain configurations can make them look truly ethereal. 

I went back to his place seven more times in the first two weeks, to fix up every doll there. Afterwards, I visited once a month for general maintenance of his dolls. 

Hugh was happy with my work, and often tipped me handsomely. He came into the store only occasionally, mostly to  buy doll outfits and accessories, something we had expanded to include in the store. He took to Millie quickly. He smiled brighter when she spoke to him. He trusted Millie’s taste in these things, and often left with more than what he had intended to buy when it was Millie who showed him the new products. 

Hugh truly did have the most fascinating dolls. He didn’t just have the pretty vintage porcelain ones, though those formed the majority of his collection. He had a dozen modern, limited edition high fashion dolls. He also had several strangely beautiful dolls that weren’t fully humanoid. One of them, for example, looked like a twisted stick, but with a face carved on where the stick naturally flattened into an oval shape. The limbs were oddly jointed, little sticks with odd angles.

It was all tied together with rough twine. 

Another was a clay sculpture that looked like a tiny person got caught in a rock face and was trying to push their way out of it. The eyes, nose and mouth were more suggestions than precise. 

My favourites were still the porcelain dolls, of course. They were created with so much love and care. You could tell the character of each one from what they wore, how they smiled, the way they batted their eyes. Even their hair-dos and accessories told a story. And you may laugh, but I truly felt like each of them had their own soul. They seemed almost pleading whenever I saw them. And when I fixed them up, I liked to think that they were grateful. 

It was around half a year into the housecalls when I really messed up. 

I had to help Millie at the shop with a doll she couldn’t fix, so I had arrived at Hugh’s only well past eight pm. 

By ten pm, I had only fixed up two dolls. I moved on to working on the third doll, Jolene. Jolene’s this beautiful raven-haired doll with silver mirrored eyes. When I was done maintaining her, I opened her box of apparels. She had different outfits, and I wanted to help her put on one of her pale blue dresses. I had to undo some straps to get the red and white checkered jumper she was wearing off her. A strap got caught in her hand. I tugged at it, but it didn’t come loose. I took a second look. Her fingers appeared to be grasping the strap. I thought, I was sure, that it was a trick of the light. Or that perhaps her hand had always been in a clenched position, I just never noticed. So I kept pulling, then began prying at her fingers.

A shrill, piercing scream erupted from Jolene. The kind of scream that rattled your insides, made you clutch at your ears. 

Shocked, I let go of her. Jolene fell. I stared at her falling, feeling time slow down. I reached out to grab her. I swear, her eyes turned to look straight at me. With anger. With hatred. With fear. 

I stared at that face, still beautiful even when contorted with that expression of hate, as my hand flew towards her. Then that face shattered, coming apart into pieces, each eye staring at me from a different piece, and her mouth torn in half. 

Then the pieces lay flat on the ground. The screaming stopped.

I froze, unable to believe what had just happened. You might not believe it, but I was truly more horrified by the grave mistake I had committed, the breaking of a doll, than by what I thought I had seen the doll do. 

In the silence, I heard the soft clicks. The whispers of small, delicate movements. I looked around me, and saw what was causing them. 

They were blinking. All the dolls were blinking. Repeatedly.

Click. Click. Click. 

A searing cold drove through my heart, and my legs turned to jelly. I would have screamed, but my throat was dry, full of cotton. 

Even the dolls that didn’t have the mechanisms to blink, were blinking. 

I sank to the floor, felled by dread and a certainty that I was close to death. 

Unthinkingly, seated there on the floor, I began to pick up the pieces of Jolene. 

A sharp piece sliced my finger. I gasped, and automatically pressed down on the cut. It was deep. Blood oozed, then began to flow in earnest. 

Swearing, I looked around for tissues, serviettes, something to staunch the blood flow. 

Then the clicking sounds of the blinking eyes were joined by something. It sounded like brown noise to me, at first, then I realised what it was. It was breathing. Rasping, grating, breathy noises. Coming from every one of the dolls.

“Mummy, kiss it better!” A sweet, child-like voice rang out behind me. I jumped, flooded with sudden adrenaline.

“Baby needs a hug!” Another voice, raspier, but still unmistakably child-like. 

“Cobbler, cobbler, mend my shoe,” Another doll sounded. 

It sank in. They wanted me to fix her. To mend Jolene.

I found something to bandage my finger, every cell in my body wanting to flee, but knowing I could not. 

I had made the mistake. I had to fix my mistake. I had to put Jolene back together again. It was a relentless conviction. I felt powerless against it. I had to do it. There was no choice there.

It took me more than an hour to glue the parts together. All the while, the place was silent, except for the sound of my ragged breathing and the blinking of the dolls. Once in a while, one of them would “speak”. Always reminding me - I had to fix Jolene. 

In my haze, I didn’t even notice the small bag that lay among the sawdust that had filled Jolene’s inside, until I was nearly done gluing Jolene together. It was a small satin bag. 

I opened it, and saw a lock of hair. Raven, like Jolene’s. 

Had it been inside the doll?

I hesitated, then placed it back within, together with the saw dust I scooped up, and glued up the last few pieces. 

I checked out my handiwork. Jolene was pretty well put back together, but there was no hiding the cracks.

Her hair was a mess. I combed it with the miniature comb I had with me, and braided it neatly. 

There was a red spot between the cracks. It was my blood, I realised. It had seeped through the bandages onto the pieces. 

I thought about removing the piece to clean it. 

Then I heard it. Silence. 

The blinking had stopped. The quiet was beautiful.

“I fixed you,” I whispered to Jolene, relieved. 

Hot tears brimmed in my eyes. I did it. I fixed it. My mistake. 

Sure, the cracks were there, but I had put her back together. I settled her on the shelf, hoping that that less obvious position would not draw Hugh’s attention so easily. 

I stared at Jolene for a while. The lock of hair in her, it unsettled me. I reached out to another doll, Anna, with a trembling hand. She didn’t scream, didn’t blink.

I removed her wig to access the inside of her head. I looked in, something I had done many times before. As usual, I saw nothing. But this time, I pulled out the cotton bits shoved into her torso and limbs. The bits and pieces were yellowed and musty. I thought I should probably change them out.

I removed another piece of cotton. There was a hint of royal blue in the doll’s torso.  I used my tweezer to pull it out. 

Another tiny satin bag. 

I opened it, suddenly filled with dread. 

Red hair. A lock of red hair. Like Anna’s. I stroked it. It felt softer than Anna’s hair. More subtle in its shine.

A key in the lock. 

Stricken with panic, I stuffed the bag and cotton back into Anna, replaced the wig. 

Hugh struggled with the keys a little, which gave me the precious extra seconds I needed. 

By the time he came into the living room, Anna was seated back on the shelf, hiding Jolene’s face behind her. 

Hugh thanked me for my hard work. He seemed surprised I was still there. 

I left as quickly as I could without being too obviously abrupt. 

All that night and the next day, I waited for Hugh to call. For him to scream, to yell about Jolene’s broken pieces. But he didn’t reach out. Not the next day, nor the next week.

I kept thinking about the locks of hair in those satin bags. They were real human hair. I was sure of it. Nothing like the synthetic plastic or yarn hair of the dolls. 

Was Hugh a pervert? Or a serial killer? Did I have to report him? 

But what proof did I have? A few locks of hair?

I ended up doing nothing. 

A few weeks later, I went back to maintain the dolls. 

Jolene was whole again. Not the glued together way I had left her. She was whole. Perfect. Like she had never been broken. 

I reached in, dug through the saw dust. The lock of hair was still there. 

I put her back down. She blinked. 

I stepped back, knee buckling. How could she be whole again? Had Hugh found out, bought a replica? Was he waiting for me to confess?

I sat down, feeling sick. 

Then I saw her. 

A new doll. The first new one in 7 months. 

There was a note next to her. It was how Hugh had labelled the names of the other dolls for me, on the first visit. 

I picked up the note.
