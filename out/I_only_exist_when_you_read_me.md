

The phone started ringing at 2:17 in the morning.  
It was a clean, metallic sound. Sharp. Close.  
A landline.  
That was strange, because I did not own a landline.  
For a moment I stayed in bed, staring into the dark ceiling while the storm pressed against the house. Rain struck the windows in hard bursts, and thunder rolled slowly through the sky like distant furniture being dragged across the floor of the world.  
Then the ringing came again.  
Clear.  
Patient.  
Unmistakably real.  
I sat up.  
The room was cold. Lightning flashed briefly through the curtains and painted the walls white before the darkness settled again. The house felt unusually quiet between thunderclaps, the kind of quiet that makes you aware of every movement you make.  
The phone rang a third time.  
I swung my feet onto the floor and stood.  
The hallway outside my bedroom stretched dimly ahead, lit only by a weak lamp at the far end. The house smelled faintly of rain and old paper, the kind of smell that lingers when storms last longer than they should.  
The ringing stopped.  
Silence returned so abruptly that it felt deliberate.  
I stepped into the hallway.  
“Hello?”  
My voice sounded smaller than I expected.  
The storm answered instead. Wind pressed against the walls. The staircase creaked somewhere below, though no one had stepped on it.  
Halfway down the hallway I noticed something that had not been there before.  
A sheet of paper lay flat on the wooden floor.  
It rested perfectly still, untouched by the draft moving through the house.  
I crouched and picked it up.  
The ink looked fresh. Still glossy. As if someone had written the words moments earlier and left them there for me to find.  
Three lines had been written in the center of the page.  
He hesitates before opening the door.  
He already knows who it will be.  
Himself.  
The storm cracked across the sky.  
Thunder rattled the windows.  
For a moment I simply stared at the words, trying to decide whether the handwriting looked familiar or if my mind was only trying to convince me it did.  
Then someone knocked on the front door.  
Three firm strikes.  
The sound echoed through the house with uncomfortable clarity.  
I straightened slowly.  
The page remained in my hand.  
Another knock followed, harder this time, the wood of the door shuddering slightly under the force.  
I walked toward the staircase.  
The house creaked beneath my weight as I descended, every step announcing itself louder than usual. The hallway below seemed narrower than I remembered, the shadows leaning closer toward the walls.  
The front door waited at the end.  
The knocking stopped.  
But I knew someone was standing there.  
My hand rested on the doorknob.  
Cold metal pressed against my palm.  
The sentence from the paper returned quietly to my mind.  
He hesitates before opening the door.  
For a moment I considered walking away.  
Locking the door.  
Pretending none of this had happened.  
Lightning split the sky again.  
The hallway filled with white light.  
And before hesitation could become fear—  
I opened the door.  
Rain rushed in with the wind.  
Cold droplets struck my face.  
A man stood on the porch.  
His coat hung heavy with water, dark with rain. His hair clung to his forehead, and his shoulders sagged slightly beneath the weight of the storm.  
Slowly he raised his head.  
The moment stretched.  
Because the man standing in the rain looked exactly like me.  
Not similar.  
Not close.  
Mine.  
But something about him was wrong.  
He was older, though not in the simple way time ages a person. His face carried the kind of wear that comes from years spent carrying something heavier than memory. His eyes looked tired, as if sleep had abandoned him a long time ago.  
Neither of us spoke for several seconds.  
Rain fell steadily between us.  
Finally he stepped forward and held out a worn leather bag.  
“You’ll need this,” he said.  
His voice sounded like mine.  
Only rougher.  
Thinner.  
Like it had been worn down by years of use.  
“What is it?” I asked.  
He didn’t answer.  
Instead he placed the bag carefully on the wooden porch between us.  
Our hands almost touched when he stepped back.  
For a moment he studied my face the way someone studies an old photograph they have not seen in years.  
Not with curiosity.  
With recognition.  
Lightning flashed again.  
The porch filled with white light.  
I blinked.  
And when my vision cleared—  
he was gone.  
Not walking away.  
Not fading into the rain.  
Simply gone.  
The street beyond the porch lay empty beneath the storm.  
No footsteps.  
No movement.  
Only rain striking the pavement.  
I stood there for several seconds, rain soaking into my clothes.  
Then I looked down.  
The leather bag remained exactly where he had placed it.  
It felt cold when I picked it up.  
Heavy.  
As if it had been sitting outside long before the man arrived.  
I stepped back into the house and closed the door behind me.  
The hallway returned to silence.  
Only the storm remained.  
I placed the bag on the table beneath the staircase and opened it.  
Inside was a manuscript.  
The pages were bound together with simple thread.  
The paper looked freshly written.  
The ink still dark.  
Still wet.  
I opened the first page.  
And felt my stomach drop.  
Because written neatly at the top—  
in the same small, deliberate handwriting from the paper upstairs—  
was my name



For a long moment I did not turn the page.  
The manuscript rested open on the table beneath the staircase, my name written carefully at the top of the first sheet. The letters were shaped with deliberate precision, the way someone writes when they want every line to look permanent.  
Thunder rolled across the sky again.  
The house creaked softly in response.  
I touched the paper.  
It felt warm.  
Not the warmth of sunlight or a room heater, but the faint warmth something carries when it has been handled recently. The ink had not fully dried yet. A small smear in the corner suggested someone’s finger had brushed the page not long ago.  
Slowly, I turned the sheet.  
The handwriting continued.  
Small. Controlled. Patient.

*The phone started ringing at 2:17 in the morning.*  
A slow pressure tightened in my chest.  
I looked toward the staircase.  
Toward the hallway where I had first heard the ringing.

*But he hears one anyway.*  
*The storm presses against the house like a warning he cannot understand yet.*

*He finds the page on the floor.*  
*Three lines written in the center.*  
*He hesitates before opening the door.*  
*He already knows who it will be.*  
*Himself.*

It was describing something that had already happened.  
Not vaguely.  
Not metaphorically.  
Exactly.

Lightning flashed through the hallway windows behind me.

*The knocking begins.*  
*Three strikes against the door.*  
*Hard enough to make the house listen.*

*He opens the door.*

The sentence there looked unfinished.  
The ink still wet.  
*He opens the manuscript.*

The line remained incomplete.

The storm pressed against the house.  
The staircase creaked softly above me.

A new sentence appeared.  
*The house is changing.*

The staircase groaned.  
And somewhere upstairs—

Like the shape of the house had been adjusted slightly.

The manuscript remained open on the table.  
The newest line still glistening.  
*The house is changing.*

Soft.  
Careful.  
The sound of someone writing.

The shadows leaning closer toward one another.

Another line forming on the page.  
*He notices the door.*  
My throat tightened.

The door remained still.

The wood darker.  
The handle slightly tarnished.  
I stepped toward the staircase.  
The house shifted faintly again.  
The scratching from the manuscript continued.  
Another sentence forming.  
*He walks toward the door.*  
My feet stopped halfway up the stairs.  
The manuscript was not simply describing the moment anymore.  
It was guiding it.  
I stood there listening to the storm.  
Trying to decide whether the door was real or simply another sentence waiting to be written.  
Behind me the pen scratched again.  
The next line appeared.  
*He opens the door.*  
The hallway felt suddenly very quiet.  
The door waited at the end of it.  
And somewhere behind me—  
the manuscript continued writing the next part of the story.

I walked toward the door.  
The hallway seemed longer with every step.  
The frames remained empty.  
The walls unfinished.  
As if someone had drawn the shape of a life but forgotten to fill in the details.  
The grey light grew brighter as I reached the door.  
My hand pushed it open.  
Beyond the doorway—  
there was nothing.  
Not darkness.  
Not shadow.  
Absence.  
The space looked like fog that had forgotten how to be air.  
A pale endless grey that stretched in every direction.  
I stepped closer.  
The air changed immediately.  
Sound disappeared.  
Not softened.  
Removed.  
When I inhaled, my breath made no noise.  
The girl stopped at the doorway.  
Her voice sounded strangely small now.  
“I can’t go there.”  
“Why not?”  
Her fingers touched the doorframe.  
“This part wasn’t written for me.”  
I stepped closer to the grey.  
The floor ended a few inches beyond the doorway.  
Beyond that point—  
there was no ground.  
No walls.  
Just that vast unfinished space.  
The absence pulled gently at the edges of the room behind me, like gravity that had forgotten which direction it belonged to.  
“What is this?” I asked.  
The girl looked down into the fog.  
“The places she didn’t finish.”  
The sentence echoed faintly in my chest.  
I leaned slightly forward.  
Something shifted inside the grey.  
Lines appeared.  
Thin black strokes forming slowly in the empty space.  
Words.  
They assembled themselves one letter at a time.  
Not on paper.  
In the air.  
*He steps closer.*  
The letters trembled faintly.  
The ink still fresh.  
“She’s writing again,” the girl whispered.  
My chest tightened.  
“From where?”  
She looked upward again.  
Not toward the ceiling.  
Toward something above the page.  
I stepped back slightly.  
The grey swallowed the words.  
The hallway returned.  
The frames remained empty.  
The door stood open behind us.  
The pen scratched again somewhere far away.  
Another sentence forming.  
The girl looked toward the hallway.  
“She’s trying to move you back into the story.”  
“Why?”  
Her voice lowered.  
“Because you’re standing somewhere she didn’t plan.”  
The notebook scratched again.  
The walls shifted.  
The hallway stretched.  
And somewhere far beyond the story—  
I felt something else.  
A faint movement.  
Like a page turning.
