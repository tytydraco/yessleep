That was the first thing Jeremy ever said to me.

I had been babysitting the boys for three days while their parents were away, and until that moment, he had not spoken a single word. Not to me, not to his brothers, not even to answer simple questions. The other two told me he was just shy. They said he had always been like that. Sweet, polite, just quiet around new people.

Still, three full days of silence felt like something more than shyness.

There were three of them, each about three years apart. Josh was the oldest, then Jonathan, and Jeremy was the youngest. He was eight, almost nine.

The first couple of days were easy. Almost too easy. There were no arguments, no complaints, no messes I could not handle. They played together, did their homework without being told twice, and ate everything I put in front of them. At night, they went to bed without a fight.

It felt wrong, in a way I could not explain. Babysitting in the past was never that smooth.

This evening, I was in the kitchen cleaning up after dinner when I realized Jeremy was standing in the doorway.

I had not heard him walk in. He was just there, watching me.

When I turned toward him, he did not look away. He did not blink. His hands hung at his sides, and for a moment I had the strange feeling that he had been standing there for a while, waiting for me to notice him.

I started to tell him something, but he spoke before I could.

“Have you ever played Three Leaves, Two Branches, One Beak?”

His voice was quiet and careful, like he was choosing each word before letting it out.

I let out a small, nervous laugh. I had not expected his first words to be something like that.

“No,” I said. “I have never heard of it. Is it fun?”

His face changed instantly. He smiled, wide and sudden, like he had been waiting for me to say exactly that.

“Yes,” he said. “I love this game.”

He looked too happy for me not to ask.

“How do you play?” I asked.

“We have to go outside first,” he said. “Then I will tell you the rules.”

I glanced at the clock. It was already late, and his brothers were upstairs getting ready for bed.

“It is too late to go outside,” I told him. “You need to go to bed.”

For a moment, he did not react. He just stood there, staring at me with that same small smile.

“Please.”

He did not raise his voice. He did not whine or complain. He just repeated it, softer each time.

“Please. Please. Please.”

Each word sounded thinner than the last, like it was being pulled out of him.

I hesitated.

I should have said no. But something about him standing there like that, after days of silence, made it hard to refuse.

“Is it quick?” I asked.

“Yes,” he said immediately.

I let out a breath. “Fine. But after that, you go straight to bed.”

He nodded once, sharp and certain, then turned and ran.

I followed him down the hall, calling after him to slow down, to at least put on shoes, but he did not listen. By the time I reached the back door, he was already outside.

Barefoot, standing in the middle of the yard, not moving.

The porch light flickered as I stepped out after him, and a strange stillness settled over everything. There was no wind, no insects, nothing but the sound of my own breathing.

For a few seconds, neither of us moved.

He did not seem impatient. He did not repeat himself. He just waited, as if the game had already started and I was the one slowing it down.

“So,” I said, trying to keep things light, “you said you would explain the rules.”

He nodded once.

“You have to find three leaves,” he said. “They have to be different.”

I glanced down at the ground. Leaves were everywhere. That part seemed easy enough.

“And two branches,” he continued. “But you cannot break them. They have to already be on the ground.”

“That makes sense,” I said. “Anything else?”

He paused, then added, a little brighter now, “Every time you pick something up, you have to say what you have.”

“Out loud?”

“Yes.”

I waited for him to explain why, but he did not. He just stood there watching me, as if that was all there was to it.

“And the beak?” I asked.

“You get that last,” he said. “At the edge.”

He pointed toward the back of the yard, where the grass faded into dirt and low brush before giving way to trees.

“There is really going to be a beak out there?” I asked.

“Yes.”

“From what?”

“Hunters leave them.”

The answer came too quickly, like something memorized. I almost questioned it, but something about the way he said it made it feel pointless.

“Alright,” I said. “I will go first.”

He nodded.

I stepped away from him and looked down at the ground until I found a leaf that looked different enough from the others. I bent down, picked it up, and straightened again, turning it slightly in my fingers.

“One leaf,” I said.

Jeremy nodded, watching me closely, his eyes fixed on my hands.

I moved a little farther and found another, wider and darker, its edges curled inward. I picked it up and added it to the first.

“Two leaves.”

He nodded again, just as quietly.

By the time I reached the third, I was already aware of how strange it felt. Standing in the yard, talking out loud like that, repeating words that should not matter. But I could not stop. It felt important to him.

“Three leaves.”

Jeremy gave the same small nod. His gaze did not shift. He kept watching my hands, as if he was both listening for something precise and making sure I was doing it right.

“Now branches,” he said.

I turned toward one of the trees and spotted a thin, dry branch lying near the base. It looked like it had been there for a while. I picked it up without thinking.

“One branch,” I said.

Jeremy tilted his head slightly.

“All of it,” he said.

I frowned, adjusting my grip. “All of it?”

He nodded.

I let out a breath and corrected myself, forcing a small smile. “Three leaves, one branch.”

That seemed to satisfy him.

I walked a few steps farther and found another branch, smaller than the first and half hidden in the grass. When I picked it up, I spoke again, a little quicker this time.

“Three leaves, two branches.”

Jeremy nodded once more, slower now, as if confirming something to himself rather than to me.

For a moment, neither of us said anything. The quiet of the yard pressed in around us, heavier than before.

Then he looked toward the woods.

“Now the beak,” he said.

I followed his gaze. The edge of the yard did not feel like a boundary anymore, just a place where things became less clear.

“You are really telling me there is going to be one just lying there?” I asked.

“Yes.”

I hesitated, then glanced back at him.

“Come with me.”

He shook his head.

“You have to get it.”

“What, can we not go together?”

“I am watching.”

That did not feel like an answer, but he did not offer anything else.

I shifted the leaves and branches in my hands and started toward the edge of the yard. The ground changed as I walked, the grass thinning into patches of dirt and uneven roots. The air felt heavier there, like the warmth from the house stopped just short of reaching it.

It was quiet. Not completely silent, but quiet enough that I noticed what was missing. No insects, no movement in the trees, just that same stillness pressing in around me.

I slowed down as I reached the first line of trees.

Then I saw it.

It was lying in the dirt a few feet ahead of me. Small. Pale. A beak.

I stopped and stared at it, trying to make sense of what I was seeing. There were no feathers around it, no blood, nothing to suggest anything had happened there. It looked clean, almost too clean.

I glanced around, expecting to find something else. A body, bones, anything.

There was nothing.

Just the beak.

“Jeremy,” I called, not taking my eyes off it, “there actually is one here.”

“I told you,” he said.

His voice was closer than I expected.

I turned slightly and saw him standing a few steps behind me. I had not heard him walk over.

I looked back down at the beak and crouched, reaching out to pick it up. It was lighter than I expected, smooth and intact, not broken or torn, just separate.

For a moment, I just held it.

Then I remembered.

“Three leaves, two branches, one beak,” I said.

My voice sounded louder than it should have.

I started to straighten, turning slightly back toward the yard.

“Okay, that is everyth”

“Say it again.”

I froze.

Jeremy’s voice was right behind me now.

I turned. He was closer than before, standing still, looking not at me but at what I was holding.

“All of it,” he said.

I swallowed.

“Three leaves,” I said. “Two branches. One beak.”

He nodded slightly, but something in his expression did not change.

“Again.”

I stared at him. “Why?”

“You have to say it three times.”

His voice was flat now, the excitement from before completely gone.

I looked down at the beak in my hand, then back at him.

“Three leaves, two branches, one beak,” I said quietly.

He smiled again, sudden and bright, like before.

“Perfect. Now one last thing.”

He ran back toward the middle of the yard, stopping at the spot where he had been standing earlier.

“Place them here,” he said, almost bouncing in place.

I walked back and knelt, setting everything down where he pointed. He immediately crouched beside them, moving quickly and carefully, arranging the pieces with surprising precision. He placed the two branches first, one flat and the other angled against it. Then he set the beak at one end and laid the leaves along the branches, spacing them evenly as if following a pattern he already knew.

I watched him for a second, trying to make sense of it.

“What is it?” I asked.

He did not look up.

“A raven,” he said.

I frowned. “How do you know what a raven is? There are not any around here. The cats scared them off years ago.”

He adjusted one of the leaves slightly, then finally looked up at me.

“That is just a story,” he said. For a moment, he smiled again, but it did not reach his eyes.

“They are still here.” He glanced past me, toward the dark edge of the woods.

“Watching.”

At the time, I remember thinking it was just something he said to scare me. The kind of thing kids repeat when they want to sound like they know more than they actually do.

I almost told him to stop.

But before I could say anything, something moved.

It was small enough that I might have missed it if I had not been looking directly at it. One of the leaves shifted. Not the way something falls or slides, but something slower, more deliberate. It lifted slightly, just enough to break the shape, then settled back into place.

I froze.

There was no wind. I could feel that clearly. The air was completely still, heavy in that way it gets at night when nothing is moving, when even the trees seem to be holding their breath.

I kept staring at it, waiting to see if it would happen again.

It didn’t.

For a few seconds, everything stayed exactly the same. The leaves, the branches, the beak. Just a pile of things arranged on the ground.

Then I looked at Jeremy.

He wasn’t looking at me. He wasn’t even looking at the ground in front of him. His eyes were fixed just past it, on the shape itself, like he was waiting for something to happen.

“Did you see that?” I asked.

He didn’t answer.

He just smiled.

Not the same way as before. Not excited, not impatient. Quieter. Like this part of it mattered more than anything we had done before.

I looked back down.

For a moment, nothing changed.

Then one of the branches shifted.

It was only a slight movement, just enough to change the angle, but it was enough. The shape no longer looked like something we had arranged. It looked like something that was holding itself together.

That was when I stepped back.

I told him it was enough, that the game was over, that it was too late and we were going inside.

This time, he didn’t argue. He just nodded, like he already knew that was how it would end.

I did not look back at it again.

I closed the door behind us, locked it, and made sure he went upstairs.

I am writing this now, maybe an hour later, and I keep trying to tell myself it was nothing. That I imagined it. That there has to be some simple explanation for what I saw.

But I have not gone back outside to check.

And I don’t think I will.

Something is making sounds outside.

A slow, deliberate pecking.

Not on the door.

Not on the walls.
