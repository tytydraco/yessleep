Lately, I haven’t been getting many calls.

As a voice actor, maybe I was never the best in the business, but I never thought I was this bad. I used to land solid roles: animated shows, commercials, small but respectable gigs. Things that made you feel like you were still moving forward.

But over the past few months, it felt like bad luck had decided to take up permanent residence right next to me.

My agent, Richard, kept trying to find work for me, with mixed results. Nothing terrible, nothing great either. Definitely nothing that made me feel like I was back in the saddle again.

Then, on one gray Wednesday morning, he called me sounding unusually cheerful. Almost bursting with excitement, really. He said he’d landed me a job.

We were going to dub a foreign film into English. He rambled on about it, maybe Spanish, maybe Italian, he wasn’t entirely sure. According to him, it was going to be some kind of “European Indiana Jones.” A guaranteed hit. And apparently, my voice would be perfect for it.

So he sold me to them immediately.

Richard had a talent for talking his way into people’s stomachs, not just their heads. And sometimes I was genuinely grateful that I wasn’t the one he needed to convince.

Long story short: the following week, they were already expecting me.

No audition. No sample lines.

Richard had taken care of everything.

And the pay, well. The pay definitely wasn’t bad.

I was there Monday morning, right at the address Richard had given me.

And almost immediately, I started having serious doubts about whether he’d actually found me a real job.

The so-called studio was in a residential apartment building. A plain, ordinary place, almost identical to the one where I rented my own apartment.

“Jesus Christ, Richard, what kind of bullshit did you sign me up for?” I asked into the phone, flicking my cigarette away in irritation.

“Relax, relax, Vincent,” Richard babbled on the other end. “Don’t get all worked up, I handled everything. Didn’t I tell you? They need English dubbing for an adventure film. Low budget, sure, but you already knew that, I explained everything. No need to stress. It’s solid. There’ll be a contract and everything…”

“Uh-huh,” I said suspiciously. “Like that dental floss commercial, right? That worked out great for me, didn’t it, RICHARD.”

“Nooo,” he backpedaled quickly. “This is different. Totally safe. They assured me everything’s chill, I’m telling you. And get this, we already got half the payment upfront!”

“Well, that’s fucking fantastic,” I muttered angrily as I stepped out of the elevator. “That’s always such a great sign, getting paid in advance.”

“Come on, Vinc,” Richard tried again. “Just do it like you always do. You’re a pro. It’ll take no time at all, and we’ll make some good money.”

“Yeah…” I grumbled. “I’ll call you later, Rich. Bye.”

I was angry and excited at the same time.

This place, this job, it all felt a little off. Not the kind of off where you immediately think, there’s an axe murderer inside, run for your life. Just unsettling enough to make you feel like someone was trying to screw you over.

When I reached the right door, I pressed the buzzer.

It opened almost instantly, as if someone had been standing there the whole time, waiting for me to finally arrive.

“Ah, welcome, my dear artist!” said the man who opened the door. He had black hair, what little of it remained, balding badly, short, with a thin mustache.

“Hello,” I replied, shaking his hand stiffly. “But I’m not an artist… I’m just a voice actor.”

“Oh, don’t be modest,” the strange man waved it off with a broad, overly friendly grin. “Everyone is a bit of an artist, in their own way.”

“Yeah… sure,” I said, stepping inside, already feeling uncomfortable.

The balding, middle-aged man quickly wiped the sweat from his forehead with a cloth handkerchief. Then, as soon as I moved farther into the living room, he reached back and closed the door behind us.

“If you don’t mind,” he said, stepping behind me and grabbing my coat by the shoulder.

“No, that won’t be necessary,” I pulled away from his touch. “I prefer to keep my things with me.”

“I see, I see,” he said, withdrawing his small hands. “In that case, allow me to show you the way. This way, please.”

The strange man led me through the living room, then into a room that felt more like a hallway or a passage than an actual space. From there, we passed through yet another room.

From the outside, the apartment hadn’t looked this big at all. Inside, it felt as if two or three separate units on the same floor had been merged into one massive space. What made the whole thing even stranger was that almost every room was empty, or filled with furniture wrapped in plastic. It was obvious no one actually lived here. The place was rented for something else. A studio, maybe. Or something I didn’t want to guess.

We practically rushed through the apartment. The balding man moved fast, his short legs pumping, and I had trouble keeping up.

Eventually, we stepped into a room that brought a small wave of relief. For the first time since arriving, I stopped worrying, at least a little, that my organs were about to be harvested and sold off to the mob.

Because this room was undeniably a recording studio.

Mixing consoles and equipment lined one side, all housed in a small, isolated control room. Behind a glass wall was the recording booth itself, something I immediately recognized from other studios I’d worked in before.

“This way, please,” the mustached man said politely, gesturing me inside. “There’s a chair over there where you can put your coat, sir. The script will be on the stand.”

“Uh… are we starting right away?” I asked, looking at him, confused. “They mentioned a contract to me… How is this supposed to work?”

“Hm…” The man looked genuinely puzzled. “We sent all the information to your agent. He should already have the contract.”

“Uh-huh… I see,” I said, unconvinced.

“Right this way, then,” the balding man continued, bowing slightly as he waved me forward.

He practically ushered me into the booth. Given how much fuss he’d made, I half-expected something unusual, but it was just a standard recording setup. Headphones, a very expensive-looking microphone, and soundproofing foam covering the walls.

“Just do everything the way you usually do, sir,” the bald man said with an ingratiating smile.

“Alright,” I said, standing in the middle of the room like a nervous kid on his first day of school.

“I’ll lock the door behind you, and once we give the signal, we can begin,” he said, and without waiting for a response, he closed the door.

I heard the lock click.

“Excuse me, but why did you lock it?” I asked immediately, my voice tightening.

“This is only for your benefit, and the work,” a pleasant female voice answered through the wall-mounted speaker. “No need to worry. We just take our work very seriously. We don’t want anyone interrupting the recording.”

“Is that really necessary?” I asked, glancing uneasily at the mirrored glass. I could only make out a few dark silhouettes beyond it, moving quietly in the dim light around the mixing console.

“It’s alright, Vincent,” the woman said gently. “Don’t be nervous. This is just a job. If there’s any discomfort, I promise I’ll personally take care of it. If there’s any issue at all, come to me. My name is Kate. I’m the manager in charge of the audio.”

“Alright, Kate…” I said, forcing calm into my voice. “I’ll collect myself, and we can start.”

I took off my coat and placed it on the nearby chair. I straightened my shirt and ran a hand through my hair. Maybe it was my way of trying to ease the tension, the kind that comes from being locked inside a room with strangers watching you through glass.

I needed every bit of composure I had to keep the people behind the glass from seeing just how fucking uncomfortable I felt. Like some kind of exhibit at a zoo. And to make it worse, more and more indistinct figures seemed to gather on the other side of the window.

“Vincent,” Kate’s voice came again through the speaker. “When you’re ready, just open the folder on the stand, and we’ll begin.”

I nodded toward the glass, even though I wasn’t sure who, if anyone, was actually watching. I had no idea which of those shapes was Kate, but I was certain she was among them.

I stepped up to the small metal stand where a thin folder rested. I exhaled slowly, preparing myself for the text, slipping into work mode.

And then I opened the folder.

What I saw made my stomach drop.

And not in a good way.

The text inside was written in a language I had never seen before, one I didn’t recognize at all.

I glanced back and forth nervously between the strange text and the figures standing behind the glass.

“Uh, Kate…” I turned toward the window. “I don’t really know how to say this, but I think something’s wrong. I mean… I don’t understand this text at all. I thought I was supposed to dub this in English.”

“There’s no problem, Vincent,” Kate replied immediately. “Just read the text. We’ll help you with everything. This is how the story begins. Shall we start?”

I just stared, blinking in disbelief. How was I supposed to deliver a good, professional dub if I didn’t even understand what I was reading? And how were they supposed to record the right tone if all I could do was stumble through nonsense?

But fine. If this is what Kate and the crew wanted, so be it. I just wanted to get this over with as soon as possible so I could tear Richard a new one for dragging me into this again.

I focused on the folder and the strange text inside. I lifted the headphones and placed them over my ears, then exhaled deeply, loudly.

“Everything’s fine, Vincent,” Kate’s voice came through the headset. “Just relax. Read it however feels natural to you. Say it the way you would pronounce it.”

Then I heard the soft beep.

We were recording.

“Et ka-rum bel-tu-ná, reksz kvi ul-tra li-men sze-desz, au-di vo-cem nosz-tram?” I tried to read the meaningless text aloud.

“Almost right, Vincent,” Kate said instantly. “But let’s try it again. Please don’t make it sound like a question. I know it’s difficult since you don’t understand it, but try reading it more like a friendly conversation.”

“I understand… I think,” I replied.

“Oh, and one more thing, Vincent,” Kate added quickly. “The karum should be deeper. Actually, the whole line could be a bit rougher, lower, more gravelly. Can you do that?”

“Yes,” I said quickly. “Can I start?”

Kate didn’t answer. The machine beeped again.

Recording started.

“Eht kha-rum bel-tu-nah, rehks khui úl-thra li-men sze-dess, au-di vo-khem nossz-tram,” I forced the words out, my voice already straining, trying to keep the friendly tone Kate had asked for.

“Vincent,” Kate said immediately when I finished. “That was excellent. Almost beautiful. Everyone’s very pleased. Shall we continue?”

“Yes,” I croaked. “Of course.”

Kate didn’t reply. I only heard the soft beep again as the next recording began.

“Non per im-pe-ri-um, szed per pak-tum te vo-ka-musz, do-mi-ne an-te-lu-cisz!” I read, sounding confident based on the rhythm of the words alone.

“No,no,” Kate interrupted almost instantly. “Please don’t do it like that, Vincent. This isn’t a commanding sentence. Try it more like you’re inviting your parents over for dinner, but keep the same deep voice. Can you manage that?”

“Sure…” I muttered, uneasy.

The machine beeped again.

“Nohn pehr im-pe-ri-umm, szehd pehr pakh-tum the vo-kha-muss, do-mi-neh an-the-lu-khiss,” I rasped, deepening my voice, pushing it from my throat.

The moment I finished the line, it felt like an icy wind swept down my spine. The kind you feel when cold air conditioning hits you in the middle of a summer heatwave.

I turned around instinctively, checking to see if something had kicked on behind me.

But there were no vents in the room. No air conditioning at all.

“That was excellent too,” Kate said. “But is something wrong, Vincent?”

“Yes…” I replied, still glancing over my shoulder. “It just felt like… never mind. Let’s keep going.”

“Alright, Vincent,” Kate said, almost excited. “Please read the third sentence as well. And remember, very slowly this time. Deep, from the throat. Like a teacher explaining something to a student.”

“Yeah, I can do that,” I said, cracking my neck like I was warming up. “Go ahead, Kate. Start it when you’re ready.”

The machine beeped again.

Recording started.

“U-rukh-tAl mekh-ru-esh, porr-tha a-pe-ri-thur szuubh no-mi-neh thu-o,” I said, so slowly that every syllable almost hurt to pronounce.

“Beautiful!” Kate cheered. “Absolutely devilishly good, Vincent! You really are a professional.”

But my relief didn’t last.

It felt like the floor beneath my feet was moving. Like the beginning of an earthquake. Subtle, deep, rolling, like the distant vibration of an approaching freight train.

“Kate?” I asked, panic creeping into my voice. “Is everything alright out there? Is there an earthquake or something?”

“No, Vincent. We don’t feel anything here,” Kate replied gently. “Is it possible you’re just a little tired? Speaking like that isn’t easy.”

I glanced back toward the dark glass wall again. The figures standing outside hadn’t moved at all. They were just staring inward. I couldn’t see their faces, couldn’t read their expressions, but it felt like they were waiting.

Hungrily. Motionless.

“If you’re okay, Vincent, can we continue?” Kate prompted.

“Sure… yeah,” I said, looking back and forth between the floor, which still felt like it was trembling, and the figures beyond the glass. “Go ahead, Kate.”

The familiar beep sounded, and I began.

“Szan-gra ka-lum, szan-gra ka-lum, i-ter tu-um jam me-mor eszt,” I tried to say it deeply, rhythmically.

“Wait, Vincent,” Kate interrupted again. “This one needs a different approach. Try saying it faster. And please, repeat it several times in a row. Like you’re singing it.”

I nodded and did exactly as she asked.

Up until then, I hadn’t really thought about the text itself, or what we were even recording. But now it felt like I wasn’t reading lines anymore.

It felt like chanting.

And the unpleasant sensation at the base of my neck kept growing stronger, telling me I was getting myself into something very, very wrong.

As I repeated the words faster and faster, I noticed something strange. In my headphones, where I was supposed to be hearing only my own voice softly, someone else was speaking too.

No.

Not someone. Many voices.

All at once.

It sounded like a crowd of distorted voices chanting the same sentence along with me.

My own voice began to fade. Not just in the headphones, but out loud. My mouth wasn’t moving anymore.

I just stood there, frozen, listening to the booming chorus in stunned terror.

“Vincent?” I heard Kate’s voice from somewhere far away. “Vincent, are you still there? Vincent, we need to finish. Please respond.”

“What? Yeah…I’m here…” I said dully. “What? Who? What happened?”

“Huh,” Kate exhaled. “You spaced out pretty badly, Vincent. I thought something was wrong. You must just be tired. Can we continue? The commercial is almost finished.”

“Commercial?” I muttered. “So this is a commercial? I thought it was some kind of European film. I…Kate, can we take a short break? I need to smoke.”

“Of course, Vincent,” Kate replied—but her voice changed instantly. Cold. Rigid. Almost unfriendly. “Gabriel will open the door and show you where you can smoke.”

I pulled the headphones off my head, overwhelmed with relief.

At that exact moment, the door behind me opened.

The strange balding man, who I now realized must be named Gabriel, stood there, looking irritated and impatient. There was no trace left of the friendly behavior he’d shown earlier.

“Come on,” Gabriel said flatly. “We’re going out to the terrace. You can smoke there.”

He headed off in the opposite direction from where we’d come.

Before following him, I took one quick glance into the room on the other side, the one facing the glass wall.

There were many people inside. All dressed in black. All bald. They looked almost identical, like copies of the same person.

And in the center of the room, a man sat slumped in a rickety armchair. His body was twisted, rigid, like he was sick, or barely holding himself together. His hands gripped the armrests so tightly that the veins stood out along his bony fingers.

“Hurry up!” Gabriel snapped. “We don’t have all day.”

I followed him.

Behind us, someone closed the door to the control room.

Gabriel rushed through the empty rooms just as quickly as he had before. We hurried all the way to what looked like a kitchen, or something pretending to be one. There was no fridge, no oven, just a few white countertops. From there, a large terrace opened up.

“There you go,” Gabriel said as he opened the terrace door, looking me up and down with open contempt.

“Thanks,” I replied with matching attitude as I squeezed myself through the doorway, which he barely bothered to open wide enough.

Gabriel stayed inside the kitchen. He pretended to be busy with something, but when I glanced back at him, I could tell he was watching me. I played along, acting like I was searching for my cigarettes.

The problem was, my lighter was actually still inside the studio, in the coat I’d thrown over a chair.

“Hey, Gabriel, you wouldn’t happen to have a light, would you?” I called into the kitchen.

“No,” he answered immediately.

“Then would you be so kind as to bring me my coat?” I asked, forcing a polite tone.

Gabriel didn’t move for a while. I could practically see the calculations running through his head, imagining what I might do if I were left unsupervised.

Then he stepped forward, shut the terrace door, and locked it.

He locked me outside.

“What the fuck?!” I shouted, completely justified. “Gabriel, what the hell are you doing?! You can’t just lock me out here!”

“It’s for security reasons,” Gabriel said bitterly. “There are a lot of valuable things in this apartment… It wouldn’t be good if something grew legs and walked off.”

“What the fuck…” I snapped, helplessly.

Gabriel turned around and walked back the way we’d come.

Out on the terrace, the cold autumn wind howled around me. And no, I won’t pretend the wind or the chill was my biggest problem.

It was the situation itself. The longer it went on, the more disturbing it became.

“Where is my damn phone…” I muttered, patting my pockets.

I finally found it in my back pocket, but there was no signal. None at all. In the middle of the city. On the terrace of a modern apartment building. How was that even possible? What was this, some kind of conspiracy?

I was on the verge of panic when the terrace door suddenly opened.

“I’m sorry, Vincent,” said a well-dressed, middle-aged woman with black hair as she stepped outside.

She wore high heels, a brown blazer, and a black coat.

“Gabriel takes his job a bit too seriously sometimes,” she said, handing me my lighter.

“Thanks…” I took it quickly and lit the cigarette dangling from my mouth, my hands shaking slightly. “This whole thing is starting to feel like too much.”

“I know, Vincent,” the woman said with a warm smile. “But I told you, if there’s any problem at all, just come to me.”

That’s when it hit me.

I recognized her voice. It was Kate.

The same voice I’d heard on the other side of my headphones.

“Come back inside, Vincent,” Kate said gently, resting a hand on my shoulder. “Let’s head back. We can talk a little on the way.”

I took a long drag from my cigarette, and followed her.

“I think I understand…” I said, forcing myself to sound calm after Kate’s explanation.

“Vincent. Gabriel is the head of security,” Kate repeated. “The project we’re dubbing is a European horror–adventure hybrid. As for the producer… well, he’s a rather unusual man. But I’ll introduce you, so everything’s clear.”

I nodded in agreement.

As suspicious as everything felt, the way I’d been speaking the text, and the things I’d experienced, maybe it really was just exhaustion. The unfamiliar, ominous atmosphere getting into my head.

At least, that’s what I told myself.

When we returned to the studio, the door to the control room was closed, but the door to my recording booth stood wide open, just as I’d left it.

Only now, there were people inside.

“Vincent. This is Mr. Gregorov, the producer of the film,” Kate said as she stepped in ahead of me, politely gesturing toward a figure sitting rigidly in a chair.

It was the same man I’d seen earlier through the glass.

Skin and bones.

He wasn’t old, but his body showed clear signs of being eaten away by illness. Every muscle was locked in painful tension, as if an invisible rope had been pulled so tight around him that he could no longer move. His hair clung to his scalp in sad, uneven patches. A frozen grimace of pain was etched across his face.

But the worst part was that instead of clothing, a blanket had simply been draped over him. His bare arms gripped the armrests with the stiffness of carved marble, like a statue locked in place. His legs were twisted together awkwardly, tangled and limp.

It wasn’t a pleasant sight.

Beside him stood a bald man wearing sunglasses, gripping the back of the chair and staring down at the floor.

“Mr. Gregorov is seriously ill,” Kate said when she noticed my discomfort. “He suffers from a rare condition known as Medusa syndrome. His body became statue-like years ago. Still, he’s a powerful and wealthy businessman.”

I nodded toward the unfortunate man.

He didn’t move an inch.

The bald man beside him didn’t react either. He didn’t even look at me, just kept staring at the floor.

“Vincent,” Kate continued, “Mr. Gregorov cares deeply about this film. He came up with the concept himself, back when he was still healthy. Because of that, he prefers to supervise every moment personally.”

“I see,” I said, glancing between Kate and Mr. Gregorov.

“So, if you’re able to continue,” Kate stepped closer, lowering her voice, “I’d like to ask, on Mr. Gregorov’s behalf, that he remain in the room with you during the recording. As you can see, he won’t interfere with your work.”

I looked at the man trapped in the chair. His body refused to move, but his eyes darted slowly from side to side.

Somewhere deep down, I felt a pang of pity for him.

“Can you continue like this, Vincent?” Kate asked, gently touching my arm.

“Yes… I can,” I nodded. “It’ll be fine.”

“Wonderful,” Kate said brightly. “Then let’s begin. Prepare yourself, there really isn’t much text left now.”

The bald man slipped out of the room without a word. Kate followed him, but paused at the doorway and turned back.

“Oh…and Vincent,” she said with a friendly smile, “please let me apologize once again for Gabriel’s behavior. He can be an idiot sometimes. Not really suited for an assistant.”

She closed the door.

Locked it again.

I stood there in the middle of the studio, watching the unfortunate, frozen man as his eyes wandered aimlessly. My thoughts had already been overloaded with information, but now that the room was empty, everything felt suspicious all over again.

Mr. Gregorov. The producer? Medusa syndrome?

Give me a break. That man looked more like a prisoner than a producer.

Sometimes they called this a film. Sometimes a commercial.

Gabriel was head of security, then suddenly just an assistant?

I had the sinking feeling that I was in very, very serious trouble. I just didn’t know what kind yet.

“Vincent,” Kate’s voice came through the microphone, “if you feel ready, we can begin.”

“Yeah, yeah,” I stalled, “just let me put my stuff away first.”

I held up my cigarette pack and lighter toward the dark mirrored glass.

The figures outside stood exactly as before. None of them moved. None of them nodded or signaled anything. I shoved the items into the pockets of the coat hanging on the chair.

While I did, I kept watching Mr. Gregorov, sitting in the chair they’d brought in. His eyes looked up at me, pleading, almost innocent.

“Are you really Mr. Gregorov?” I whispered, so quietly I could barely hear myself.

Gregorov moved only his eyes, slowly left and right.

That might have been a no.

I didn’t know what to do. I stalled for time, fussing with my coat, turning my back to the glass as if I were searching for something important. Then I pulled out my phone.

Still no service.

Something was interfering with the signal. Who the hell were these people, really?

“Vincent?” Kate’s voice hissed through the speaker. “Are you there? Can we finally continue?”

“Yes, of course,” I replied immediately. “I’m coming.”

I turned back to my coat and zipped the pockets shut.

“I’ll figure something out,” I whispered to the man.

I walked back to the microphone. I raised my thumb toward the crowd behind the glass, signaling that everything was fine. Then I put the headphones back on.

The folder lay exactly where I’d left it. The strange text still inside.

“Vincent,” Kate said in my headphones, “we should listen back to what you’ve recorded so far. So you can hear it too, make sure everything sounds right.”

“Uh…” I hesitated. “Isn’t that your job? To check the sound?”

“Yes, of course,” Kate replied. “But if you listen as well, you might notice something worth adjusting.”

Without waiting for an answer, she played the recording.

“Et karum beltu-na, rex qui ultra limen sedes, audi vocem nostram. Non per imperium, sed per pactum te vocamus, domine antelucis. Uruk-tal mekhru-esh, porta aperitur sub nomine tuo. Sangra kalûm, sangra kalûm, iter tuum iam memor est.”

The voice I heard was not mine.

The foreign words were clear. Confident. Perfectly articulated. There was no trace of the confusion I’d felt while reading them aloud. I remembered recording the lines without understanding a single word, yet here they were, flawless.

Then everything began to shake.

With the headphones still on, I looked around, the walls, the floor, the equipment, everything rattled as if a powerful earthquake had struck.

“Kate?” I asked again, terrified. “Is everything alright out there?”

“Out here?” Kate replied calmly. “Everything is perfectly fine. Now, when you’re ready, please read the next line.”

“Yeah… sure,” I said with a hollow smile.

I flipped the page in the folder. The next line continued on the other side.

“In-greh-deh-reh, rehks szi-neh for-mah, eht no-mehn thu-um hikh ehs-tho,” I said, calling into the microphone.

The voice was not mine.

I heard myself through the headphones, and yet it didn’t feel like me. Familiar, but wrong. As if something else were helping shape the words, guiding the pronunciation.

My knees trembled. Fear flooded me instantly.

The room changed. The air grew heavy. The lights began to flicker.

I tore the headphones off and stared in horror at the figures behind the glass.

“Vincent, please stay where you are,” Kate said soothingly through the speaker. “Everything is fine. Just read one more sentence.”

“Kate? What’s happening?” I asked, barely holding myself together.

“Just read one more,” she insisted.

I shook my head.

There was something in the room with me.

I couldn’t see it, but I could feel it. Something monstrous. Something the human mind was never meant to comprehend.

“I promise you, Vincent,” Kate said gently, “everything will be fine. Just read the next one.”

“Veh-ni nuhnk e rehg-na doh-nehk vokh nosz-trah e-lang-gvehsz-kit,” I blurted out.

“No, Vincent!” Kate snapped angrily. “Don’t rush it. It needs to be much slower!”

Something slammed against the glass.

I spun around, knocking over the microphone and the script stand.

Mr. Gregorov stood at the window, one thin hand pressed against the glass.

His body was twisting in amorphous spirals. Massive bulges swelled beneath his skin, then sank back in. It was disgusting, like living human dough being kneaded from the inside. Something writhed beneath his flesh, trying to claw its way out.

“What the fuck is that?!” I shouted, backing away.

“Vincent!” Kate screamed through the speaker. “Read another line!”

“Shut the fuck up!” I screamed back at the glass. “What is that thing?!”

“Tempus nostrum brevis est, sed locus paratus est!” Kate shouted from the speakers.

Mr. Gregorov clutched his head. His grotesque, writhing skin swelled even further, sagging unnaturally. He staggered toward me.

When he looked up, his eyes rolled wildly, then blood began to stream down his face like tears.

“Anzu ul gal, anzu ul kur. Esmes namluga-gu, kienna muungar,” he rasped in a warped, inhuman cadence. “Lumes kalamma gugu muunzu!”

“IT’S HERE!” Kate screamed with ecstatic joy. “It worked! It finally worked!”

“Jesus Christ…” my voice shook as I pressed myself against the wall, desperate to get away from it.

“An galam, sa kima same u erseti ul isbat. Li awilu ina paniia ul izzaz,” the thing snarled in rage. “Namlugalgu galam, ul sanu. Sa sumiia ibbu, anaia ibbu.”

“Yes!” Kate said reverently. “Yes, my king! It will be done!”

I stood there in shock, trying to hold onto something, anything, in this impossible situation. I looked from the horror before me to the glass wall beyond it.

Outside, in the dim light, I could only see the tops of heads.

It looked like everyone was kneeling.

“Babu ul gamir!” the creature suddenly screamed in agony. “Seru ša kasu anaia isbat. Ul dannu!”

What remained of the man called Mr. Gregorov convulsed violently. His shapeless left arm clawed at the ceiling as his body stretched and shrank in turns, tumor-like bulges appearing and vanishing across his flesh.

“No!” Kate screamed. “Go! Get him! He has to be saved!”

A horror stood before me.

An amorphous, naked human body, writhing with stiffened limbs. Its abdomen swelled grotesquely, stretching upward toward the ceiling like a bloated, obscene balloon.

The studio lights began to flicker violently, some bursting apart with a crackle. From the speakers came an increasingly loud, maddening static.

What remained of Mr. Gregorov let out a final scream. It was the sound of a man dying in unbearable agony.

But behind it, something else screamed too.

Not in pain.

In anger. In frustration.

The studio door flew open.

For a split second, I saw the bald, black-clad figures standing there like identical twins, all staring into the room.

Then, with a thunderous explosion, Mr. Gregorov’s body burst apart.

Organs flew everywhere. A chunk of intestine slapped against my face, splattering me with foul, fecal-stinking blood. The walls were instantly painted red as the shockwave and flying remains slammed me into the wall.

I wiped blood from my eyes and spat gelatinous clumps from my mouth.

The black-clad figures rushed into the room. One of them ran straight toward me. I reached out, begging for help.

He responded by pulling a black hood over my head.

The last thing I felt was a hard blow. Then everything went silent.

I woke up as something slammed me hard onto the ground.

I coughed, trying to rub my aching head, but my hands were bound. I could feel the thin edge of the zip tie biting into my wrists.

It was dark inside the hood, but I could feel the weak autumn sun warming my chest, while a cool, salty wind brushed against my bare arms.

“Vincent,” Kate said calmly. “I’m sorry it had to end this way. You could have been part of something extraordinary. But…” Her voice faltered. “But this… wasn’t it.”

“Fuck you, Kate,” I hissed, turning my head toward her voice. “You’re fucking insane. I swear I’ll sue you for this!”

“Of course you will, Vincent,” Kate replied sadly. “Go ahead. Go to the police.”

Someone grabbed my bound arms and yanked me to my feet.

The pale sunlight filtered through the hood, burning against my face. I could hear the waves of the sea crashing into one another.

“Rot in hell,” I muttered. “Fucking lunatics.”

“It doesn’t matter, Vincent,” Kate murmured. “I really thought a voice like yours would finally be enough. But let’s forget this. The money we paid your agent should be enough for both of you.”

“Fuck you, Kate!” I shouted. “You kidnap me like some goddamn mobsters! Are you out of your minds?! I hope this whole thing blows up in your…”

Something smashed into my stomach.

I folded instantly and collapsed to the ground, gasping like a fish pulled from the water, my lungs refusing to work. Moments later, someone grabbed me from behind and hauled me up again, this time only to my knees. I felt my legs sink into the sand.

“Vincent, we’re leaving now,” Kate said as she stepped beside me and placed a hand on my shoulder. “And you’re not going to tell anyone about this. Who would believe you?”

I said nothing. I had nothing left to say, and I was barely breathing again.

“Let him go,” Kate said.

I heard her walk away.

A moment later, someone cut the zip ties on my wrists. I heard footsteps retreating across the sand.

I didn’t move.

No matter how loud I’d been before, Kate’s certainty had sunk deep into me.

After a few moments, I rubbed my aching wrists and pulled the hood off my head.

The sun was shining softly in the cool autumn air. We were somewhere along a shoreline. Empty. Desolate.

Kate and her people were gone.

And I had lived through something I still don’t know how to name.
