I stood at the edge of my little brother’s bed and watched him sleep.

His chest had a steady rise and fall and—*God*—that pissed me off. 

*He* pissed me off. 

I slid my hand under his pillow and rubbed until I felt a little bump. *Jackpot.* His first baby tooth. 

I snuck out of his room and into the bathroom and dangled the tooth over the toilet. When I pictured how his face would look, how he’d lift up his pillow tomorrow morning and find no money, my heart jumped. 

I dropped the tooth. 

It sank through the air and hit the water with a soft *plink.* Then I flushed. I returned to my own bedroom and crawled back into bed. Mission accomplished.

\*\*\*

*Creak......*

I woke up. 

In my brother’s room, beside mine, floorboards shifted. Someone moved to the back of the room toward his bed. Then they paused.

I bit back laughter. It must’ve been the tooth fairy. Searching. *Good luck, buddy.* 

A few seconds passed before it paced back across the room. The door clicked open…then shut. *Huh.* Didn’t think fairies used doors. 

Its footsteps crept over to my door and stalled. 

Then the knob turned. 

My door squealed open, and the air chilled. I didn’t understand. Why had it come to my room? I had no more baby teeth. 

It wandered up to my bed and stood still. I pretended to sleep. 

Something shuffled underneath my pillow. *A hand.* It took a few quick swipes, then slid out.

An angry voice muttered something in complete gibberish. Then I heard buzzing all around me, and both my arms were pinned down. I tore open my eyes, staring into darkness, trying to sit up, pull my arms free, but its grip was too tight. 

Then—a light clicked on. 

*Two* fairies, the size of pit bulls, clutched my arms. Their own arms bulged with solid muscle. Their bodies were shaped like wasps, but their heads were human. They stared up at me with blank eyes. Blinking.

Then something shifted at the foot of my bed. I glanced down. 

A fairy, the size of a child, crawled onto my bed and perched between my feet. It slipped a leather bag off its shoulder, set it down, and rifled through it.

It produced a syringe. 

“Wait,” I said. “No—”

The fairy pressed the plunger. A spurt of liquid shot out. It flicked the side of the barrel, tapping out air bubbles. Then the fairy crawled forward and straddled me. It suspended the tip of the needle over the bend of my elbow and *jabbed.*     

I felt a bright *sting.* As the plunger depressed, a warm burn spread out. Then the fairy yanked out the needle, and the other fairies released me and flew into the air. 

I tried sitting. I made it several inches…then fell back. I tried again and sat up even less. The third time—I couldn’t move at all. I was stuck. Paralyzed. 

My chest tightened. I tried screaming for my Dad. But no sound came out. The fairy turned, reached in the bag, and slid out a pair of needle-nose pliers. 

Then I realized what was happening. I tried shaking my head. *Pleading* with my eyes. 

It gave no acknowledgment. 

Instead, it used a few fingers to peel back my lips. A cool rush of air hit my gums. 

The fairy peered inside my mouth like a dentist, and then it stuffed the pliers into my mouth. They bit down on a back tooth. I cringed at the scrape of metal on bone.  

Then it squeezed.

A painful pressure started building, and I forced out a small gurgle. The fairy ignored it. The pliers wiggled. Pain radiated through my gums, and with each torque, a sickening crunch squished back and forth, back and forth. 

Every muscle in my body clenched. I cried as the fairy’s face narrowed in concentration. Then…a smooching sound sucked in my head. 

An explosion of pain erupted in my head. Black dots swarmed my vision. A coppery taste coated my tongue. Liquid drained down my throat. 

Then the fairy’s jaw clenched. It took a sharp breath, clenched its muscles, and—

*Click.*

Pain drilled through the roof of my mouth. My stomach twisted. Thoughts blurred. Then—

Darkness.

\*\*\*

I tore open my eyelids. Blinked.

Sunlight poured through the window and stung my eyes, and a pain sparked in my head that I never knew existed. With each thud of my heart, I felt an icepick hammering into my skull.

I picked up my head. Glanced down. 

The bedsheets were soaked in blood. And that’s when I screamed. My Dad threw the door open, then immediately carried me to the car and raced me to the E.R. 

The doctors were puzzled. 

They’d never seen anything like it. When I explained what happened, they didn’t believe a word I’d said.

No one did. 

For the next eight years, the tooth fairy returned at random, until my twenty-first birthday, when the final tooth was pulled. Now I wear dentures. 

For a while, I was thrilled to lose that last tooth. *Delighted.* Because then there’d be none left to take. No more late-night visits. No more needles. No more pliers. Right?
