I stopped looking into the forest after my third week of living in the countryside. I learned to keep my eyes on the water right before its shore, trained away from the treeline like I'd train them from a corpse. That's how everyone else coped too. The feeling found us anyway. Nobody talked about it, almost like they were afraid that speaking about it would make it more real. I tried my best to deny it, but I felt it when I looked past the water into the forest beyond. At night, it seemed like an audible hum in the distance. 

My wife left a few weeks ago. She took the boat by our house and was never seen again. I couldn't convince myself that she was one of the ones who just *leave*; it's common for those who live far away. That kind of solitary life isn't for everybody. Some people move to towns or cities nearby, others leave to isolate themselves even more and go deeper into the wilderness. We don't usually ask questions when people move out, but I simply couldn't accept that she was one of them—that she would leave without saying a single word.

I was still processing the disappearance when, a couple of days ago, nearby travelers came to our small town. I knew that those heading to the city would need supplies for their journey. I left my home in spite of my grief and took the supplies I was willing to trade with. After a few unsuccessful attempts to barter with the visitors, I sat on a stump near a campfire. One of the men was in front of me. He was an older man with a beard and a face full of wrinkles. He was measuring black powder into small bags.
 
“What is it?” I said. “If it's gunpowder, I've got shot and caps.”

The man didn't take his eyes off the work.

“I'm not here to trade,” he said.

“I thought that's what you folk came here for,” I said.

“I ain't with the other folk,” he said, “and the less time I spend here, the better.”

I already knew where this was going.

The man was a hermit. I could tell by the way he stared at me dead in the eye, oblivious to any social rules. Hermits were a paranoid sort—observant in the wrong way. I knew he was talking about the forest, but I had always been one of the few people to deny the anomaly, which outsiders mentioned so often. 

“You don't hear the humming?” he said, ignoring my question. “Or have you just made your peace with it?

I prepared to say *no, not really*. I wanted to tell him that he was crazy, or that he had been alone for too long, but I was too tired. 

“Well… I ain't sure, really,” I said softly. It was the first time I had been honest about it.

“Every time I pass through here, I hear it,” he said. “Your people feel the need to enter the forest just the same, and yet you refuse to move away.”

His words reminded me of something.

“If someone were to go to those woods, what would happen to them?”

“Are you thinking of going?” he said.

“No, but someone I know is gone. I'm not saying they went to the woods, but if they did, what would happen?”

“How can I say it…” He paused. “Some things notice when you're looking. Better to stop asking questions.”

I stared at him, the deep lines of his old, tired face growing more pronounced.

“My advice?” he said before I could speak. “Leave this place, or at the very least, don't go toward the forest.”

With that, he tied off one of the bags and tucked it in a large satchel. He stood up and walked away. 

I must have sat on that stump for an hour, thinking over his words. Occasionally I would turn around and look across the river. The trees beyond seemed to absorb the light from the sun, and when I watched the forest, I could almost feel my vision warping and swirling together until the darkness grew across my eyes. After that, all I could hear was a muted droning noise, like an insect humming in the heat far away. But each time, I would snap out of the trance, unsure of how long I had spent watching the woods. The forest had a way of eating time when you gave it your attention.

My feelings faded slightly when I looked away, and they were replaced by something else: an intense anger directed toward the forest across the river. It had influenced me; I was sure of it. It had been in my brain. If it could do it to me, it could have done it to her—it could be the cause of my wife's disappearance.

I entered my house and exited moments later with a hunting rifle strapped across my back and a knife on my belt. I walked toward one of the boats on the shore and pushed it out into the water, jumping on it and starting the engine. I hesitated for a moment upon processing my actions, but I didn't change my mind. I had to know the truth about the forest. As the boat started across the river, I looked behind me. The hermit was watching me from afar, he shook his head slowly, but I pretended not to see him. The weight of the forest was undeniable by now. I had passed the area before on multiple occasions, but I had never felt its presence like this. The forest was deep and draped in moss so that I couldn't see what lay beyond it. When the boat drew closer, I heard a barely audible humming noise, and my vision warped again. 

I jumped slightly when the boat’s side brushed against the root of a cypress tree, waking me up from my trance. I turned off the engine just as the boat approached the land. It slowed with a gentle thud and slid onto the muddy shore. I had reached the forest sooner than I had expected, almost as if I had been asleep during the short journey. The only sound was that of the tiny waves splashing against the metal side. I grabbed a rope and jumped off, tying the boat to a tree.

The shore was covered in trees and steep banks that made it impossible to reach the forest. I walked along the shore, stepping on roots and rocks, and waiting for a place where the steep banks gave way slightly. As I walked, something caught my eye. It was shrouded in hanging moss below a huge cypress tree. I peered through the curtain of moss and confirmed my suspicion. It was a boat. The same boat that had been next to my house. The one my wife had disappeared with. There was no doubt in my mind that I was in the right place. My determination to discover what had happened to her grew, and so did my grip on the strap of the hunting rifle. I didn't know why I had taken the gun with me, but something told me it was important.
 
The steep banks finally lowered, and I climbed onto the higher, dry forest floor. It wasn't nearly as dense as it had looked from the other side of the river, but the vines and moss hanging from the maple trees made it difficult to see deep within the woods. I didn't know where I was going or what I was looking for, but I pressed onward anyway.

After walking forward for a while, I heard a loud rustle from the trees above me. 

I realized at that moment how tense my nerves had been. The faint sound had been enough to make me jump, and I forced myself to steady my breathing. Why was I so nervous?

I heard the sound again, and when I looked up, the leaves and branches obscured my sight. 

“It's just a bird,” I whispered to myself, “steady yourself.”

The second the words exited my mouth, I saw something in the underbrush. It looked like an old, decaying piece of leather. I walked over and picked it up, studying it closely. It was a boot. I put it down upon noting that it was too large to be my wife's. And besides, old shoes aren't that uncommon to find in places like that. But when I looked forward again, I saw another shoe. I scanned the ground to find another, and then more. The grass was dotted with shoes of all shapes and sizes. 

*How odd.*

I heard the same rustling sound again, this time directly above me, but no matter how much I squinted against the light, I couldn't make out what was above the dense branches. I knew it was a bird, or a squirrel, or something of the sort, but my muscles still tensed, and I felt the urge to hold my gun in my hands. I shook the feeling and kept walking forward.

The light of day was quickly dimming, and I made a mental note to leave soon after sunset. As much as I wanted answers, my growing unease was too much for me to handle. 

I stepped over more shoes on the ground. Some large and some small. Some new and some falling apart. As I entered an area where the trees spread out slightly, I stopped abruptly as my foot stepped on something strange.

I thought it was a branch at first, but it was the sound that had made me stop.

It was a dull, hollow noise. 

I lifted my leg, and my breath caught in my throat.

A bone, exiting from inside one of the shoes. 

A gentle breeze blew through the trees. It carried with it the very stench of death. I couldn't believe I hadn't smelled it earlier, but now that it was in my nostrils, I couldn't be free of it. I turned around and vomited on the grass. 

When I stood up again, I felt a gaze piercing my back from above.

I could see what was above me, now that the lack of vegetation had made the treetops visible. I already knew what I would see, but I looked up anyway. 

Every muscle in my body turned to stone. My breath slowed and exited my mouth with a long gasp. 

I saw legs dangling gently in the breeze, and attached to them the bodies of all manner of people. People were hanging from ropes high up in the trees.

There must have been dozens of them, and I could see the silhouettes of many more further away. 

Something else was strange about the sight: they were too high up. I had never heard of people hanging themselves so high up in treetops.

I suddenly heard the low hum, now even louder than before, and the pull—the influence I felt from the forest—was stronger too. It screamed at me to continue into the forest, to go deeper until I reached the heart. I tried to fight it, but my vision swirled and faded in. I was powerless to stop it; the humming sound pierced my ears and made it difficult to focus. 

When I snapped back into consciousness, I was slumped against a tree in a completely different part of the forest. I shot to my feet and looked around frantically. It was nighttime. I couldn't see the bodies anymore in the dark, but the smell was still present.
 
There was a sound coming from above me. 

It sounded like a rapid scurrying, followed by the rustling of the trees. 

My hands reached for the gun on my back.

In the shadows, I thought I saw a shape, but the darkness deceived me.

A large, black shadow, visible through the branches high above. It looked like an enormous insect, but twisted and malformed. It dangled down from the treetops.

No—that was impossible—I had to be seeing things. Maybe it was a curtain of moss, or, at the very least, one of the bodies hanging in the trees.
 
But then, it moved.

I saw a thin string appearing from its abdomen, and it lowered itself slowly toward the ground. It spun the string like silk and dropped to the floor. When the string stopped swaying, I saw its shape against the moonlight: a noose. 

The anomaly of the forest—the bodies—the web of death above me—this was the creature responsible; there was no doubt in my mind. 

I slumped against the tree, trying in vain to control my breathing. It was all one horrible nightmare. I wished I had heeded the words of the hermit—I wished I had never decided to venture into the cursed woods. 

I heard the droning sound again; it was extremely loud and clearly audible, and the shadows in the forest started to blend together. I didn't know what would happen if I fell under another trance, but I couldn't allow that to happen. I strained with all my might. It felt like I was trying to force myself to wake up from a dream. 

“No!” I heard myself scream, and the trance was interrupted. 

My voice reverberated through the forest and disappeared into the distance. I hadn't meant to be so loud, but at least I was still conscious. 

The humming stopped.

Even the frogs stopped croaking, and the crickets stopped chirping.

It was dead quiet.

I heard something scrape against the tree trunk above me. 

I spun around and pointed the weapon up, but the sight was enough to make my trigger finger freeze and, for that matter, my entire body. 

The creature that dangled from the treetop was unlike anything I had ever seen. It had the body of a spider, with a bloated abdomen and multiple thin, twisted limbs that seemed far too long for its body. I saw the silhouette of what looked like an emaciated human torso and head, connected to the top of the spider's body. Its eyes were dotted throughout the chest and abdomen of the human shape. Some of the eyes were big, others small, but all of them seemed to glow in the shadows. 

My eyes went wide, and I seized with terror. The creature opened its mouth, which was located at the base of the human form, and out came that same, slow droning I had heard so many times before. It was the sound I had heard in my home at night—the sound I heard next to the campfire—and the sound that drew me toward the forest. I knew what followed, but another trance would surely be the death of me. I refused to be another body in its web hanging from the trees.

As the noise grew louder, the sight of the creature blurred, and for a moment I almost forgot about the gun in my arms.

The gunshot shook the very trees, and the humming stopped at once. The creature hung in the air for a moment, then dropped.

I tried to lunge out of the way, but its massive body fell directly on top of me.

As soon as it hit the ground, it began twitching and convulsing violently, its malformed appendages spasming in the air. 

I clawed at the ground and escaped from beneath it, rising rapidly to my feet and running away. After I had separated myself sufficiently, I turned around to see it again. It was no longer twitching on the ground but standing perfectly still where it had fallen.

Its glowing eyes pierced mine.

Before I could even react, it scurried up the trunk with unnatural speed and disappeared into the treetops. 

My heart felt like it was going to burst from my chest, but I couldn't afford to process the situation; I had to get out of the forest. I didn't know where my boat was, but I knew that the river was south of the forest, so I decided to start there. I ran as quickly as I could, and I was sure that I could hear the creature in the trees above me.

I was determined to get there as soon as possible, but in my hurry I had forgotten to watch the darkness beneath me. I stepped on one of the shoes that littered the ground. My ankle twisted, and I fell to the floor. The rifle fell from my grasp and slid forward.

I cried out in pain, grasping my ankle. 

I reached forward and searched the floor for my weapon, but the piles of leaves made it impossible to find.

Something heavy fell to the ground behind me.

I didn't dare look back; I could already hear its many limbs against the ground. 

Finally, I felt the heavy wooden stock of the hunting rifle and pulled it toward me, spinning around and preparing to fire. The sound told me exactly where the creature had landed, and I tensed my finger around the trigger.

A sharp pain pierced my thigh before I could steady the gun, and it fired harmlessly into the distance. I looked down to see one of its legs piercing the back of my thigh, pinning me in an awkward position. I screamed.

Forcing myself through the pain, I reached for the knife at my belt.

I screamed again as a second appendage pierced my shoulder, causing me to drop both the rifle and the knife. I looked up at its malformed body.

The hundreds of glowing eyes looked down on me. It shot a rope from its abdomen and lifted itself slowly, dragging me along with it. I clawed at the dirt in vain until my body was lifted from the earth. I watched the weapons shrink further and further away as I approached the treetops. The creature curled around me and began turning me in a circle, kicking me with its legs and spinning a single rope around my chest. When it was done, it let me fall.

I managed to place my arm through the loop before it closed around my throat. But the rope squeezed my shoulder against my neck. The creature hung on to a rope nearby, watching me like a statue. It began emitting the droning noise.

Frustration grew within me, and my fear gave way to anger. I clawed at the rope with my free hand, but it wasn't just any ordinary rope; it was viscous and slippery, and the swaying of my body—and its awkward position—made it difficult to get ahold of it. Even worse, my vision was rapidly fading, and my thoughts were muddling. Only the pain was keeping me from falling into the trance. 

I looked at the area around me. The bodies of the people swayed gently, stuck in the same kind of rope as I was. I saw that many of the branches weren't branches at all but interconnected ropes stretching over the trees. It was all one elaborate web.

And I was a part of it—I would be soon.

Just as my vision faded, I heard a sound. It was muffled, as if it had sounded underwater, but I recognized it instantly:

A gunshot.

The ground approached me in an instant, and my injured leg buckled under the impact. The rope fell around me. I screamed, but the creature was on top of me before I could process anything. 

Another gunshot

The creature recoiled, giving me enough time to put some distance between us as I gasped for breath. I crawled until my hand felt the knife on the ground nearby. 

The creature lunged at me as soon as my fingers wrapped around the handle. It held me and shot another web, trying to pull me up again. Before it could lift me off the ground, I slashed at an appendage, blood spurting from the wound. Then, I brought the knife below its torso and lifted it with all my strength. When the knife connected with its body, it emitted a loud shriek that made my ears ring. Ignoring the pain in my ears, I tightened my grip on the handle and pulled it toward me, slashing its torso open and spilling its blood onto the grass. It cried out one last time and began convulsing. I crawled out from under and started in the opposite direction. 

The forest seemed to have taken an unnatural red hue. 

When I looked up, I saw a large fire burning the underbrush nearby. A man stood in front of it. He was holding a rifle. 

I recognized him immediately: the hermit from the village.

I watched him lower the gun and take a bag from his satchel—the same bags he had been preparing near the campfire. He emptied its contents into the cracks of a dry stump. He lit a match, and a huge fire erupted. 

“I… I don't understand,” I said.

The man looked up from the burning wood.

I stared at him, speechless.

“Leave this place before the fire gets too high,” he said. 

The man walked away, preparing another bag of gunpowder. 

I crawled southward for what felt like hours, looking behind me frequently, and holding the knife in my fist for the duration of the journey. When at last I reached an area of the forest I recognized, I approached the hills that lined the shore and threw myself over them, crawling to the place where my boat was. I cut the rope and started the engine.

The small boat started down the river, but I kept my eyes on the treeline the entire time.

I squinted my eyes, wondering for a moment if what I was seeing was real.

The forest was ablaze. The old hermit was standing near the shoreline, looking out into the trees.

But my eyes focused elsewhere.

High on the treetops, behind the moss and vines that grew around it, was a collection of shadows. I struggled to see them at first, but when at last I processed the image before me, I felt my skin crawl.

There were dozens—no, hundreds—of spider-like beings huddled together in clusters on the trees, the same ones as the creature I had faced earlier. They seemed to be asleep, or hibernating, and their swollen bodies dotted nearly every tree in the forest. It was only because of the fire—of the illumination from below—that I was able to see them. I forced myself to avert my gaze, but even the brief sight of the creatures massed together in clusters was enough to burn an image into my memory. 

After that, I travelled down the river and never looked back. 

---

I will never return to living in the countryside, not after what I've witnessed. My only possessions are the knife I carried and the clothes on my body.

I lay on a hospital bed in a nearby city. My wounds are deep, but at least those will heal. The same cannot be said for the memory I have of that place—of the scattered shoes and swaying bodies. I'm glad the forest is burned, and the creatures along with it.

Maybe it was luck that the old man happened to find me on his journey to burn the forest, or maybe he chose to follow me from the start. Whatever the reason, he saved my life, and more importantly, he burned that awful place to the ground. 

Even so, sometimes when I close my eyes, I hear it:

The distant droning of an insect.
 
I still feel the urge, too.

If at any point you feel the urge to enter a forest near your home, move away and never come back.
