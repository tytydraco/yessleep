The Butterfly People were our saviours.

A wicked storm tore through Harrison when I was young. It took nearly everything from us. Bungalows splintered and cars were swept off the street, along with pets and trees.
But we were safe.

The Butterfly People were our saviours.

Next came violent floods; water that would have swallowed even the tallest of men moving fast enough to tear through concrete. Not much remained of the commercial district in Harrison by the time it had passed. Any vehicles not thrown away by gale-force winds were cleaved by the harsh waters.
But still, we were safe.

The Butterfly People were our saviours.

Then there was thunder. The taller buildings, those which hadn't been toppled by the floods, became direct lines of communication between the heavens and the earth as bolts of light threatened to strike anything and anyone which dared to seek refuge on high.
There were few places for us to hide from the storm.
But we were safe.

The Butterfly People were our saviours. 

Soon came the eye of the storm. A great hurricane which stretched for miles in every direction tore through Harrison with prejudice and no remorse. Those who remained on the flooded streets were snatched into the unforgiving cyclone quickly. Rubble from fallen buildings and scraps from cars shot high into the air, the dark cloud which carried the beast swallowing them with ease.
But still, we were safe.

The Butterfly People were our saviours. 

The eye passed over the shelter under which we hid. I had never seen anything like it. Thousands of pounds of dust and debris hurtled through the air around us at immense speeds, fast and harsh enough to tear concrete walls to shreds. Some of us were picked up by the storm, some of us held on to support beams and some on to each other. 

The Butterfly People were our saviours.

When all seemed lost and the colossal storm reached our shelter, very few of us held out hope for survival. The older kids held the younger ones, shielding them from flying debris with nothing but their bodies and the clothes on their backs.
A few of us looked up.
Above us, flying around the storm's wicked mouth, was a sight like no other.
Countless angels, back-lit by a faint crack of sun peeking through the clouds, hovered with grace and benevolence. 
We felt safe.

The Butterfly People were our saviours. 

And then the angels descended, covering our bodies with their own, their wings wrapping around each and every child they chose to protect.
It was warm beneath the embrace of the angels. Their wings were wide and wet from the rain, but they did not let go. 
Not one angel said a word, nor gave a comforting nod to a scared child, but all of them were steadfast in their goal.
No child under the protection of the angels was harmed by the flying debris.
We were safe.

The Butterfly People were our saviours. 

When they left, they shed the lining of their wings. A red blanket fell softly over each of us.
It was thick and wet, and it stuck to us stubbornly. It kept us warm.
The Butterfly People were reclaimed by the storm as it moved away from us. It was a miracle we had survived. Were it not for the Butterfly People, I'm not sure we would have.
But the hurricane moved on, and we were safe.

The Butterfly People were our saviours.
