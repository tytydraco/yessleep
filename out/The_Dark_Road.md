Daniel Harper hated the night shift.

Every night at 2:00 a.m., he made the same lonely drive home from the warehouse. The highway out of town cut straight through miles of forest, and at that hour it felt like the world had forgotten the road even existed.

No streetlights.  
No houses.  
Just trees.

His headlights carved narrow tunnels through the fog as he drove.

The radio crackled with static.

Daniel sighed and tapped the dashboard.

“Come on…”

The static cleared for a moment, letting a faint country song slip through before dissolving back into white noise.

He rubbed his tired eyes and leaned forward over the wheel.

Just a few more miles.

The road curved sharply through a stretch of particularly dense forest. The trees here grew so close together that their branches seemed to form a ceiling above the asphalt.

That’s when he saw something standing in the middle of the road.

Daniel slammed on the brakes.

The car skidded and stopped just a few feet away.

A child stood in his headlights.

Barefoot.

Thin.

Wearing what looked like an oversized shirt that hung almost to its knees.

“What the hell…?” Daniel muttered.

He shifted the car into park and stepped outside.

The cold air hit him immediately.

The forest was completely silent.

Not even insects.

“Hey!” he called gently.

The child didn’t move.

“Are you okay?” Daniel asked.

Still nothing.

The child’s head was tilted downward, its face hidden by messy dark hair.

Daniel slowly walked closer.

“Did you get lost?” he asked. “Where are your parents?”

The child finally spoke.

Its voice was quiet.

Flat.

“They’re waiting.”

Daniel frowned.

“Waiting for who?”

The child slowly raised its head.

It smiled.

Not a normal smile.

Too wide.

Too still.

“For you.”

Suddenly the forest erupted with movement.

Figures rushed out from the trees.

Daniel barely had time to react before hands grabbed his arms and forced him to the ground.

“What the hell—?!”

Someone shoved cloth into his mouth.

Another person tied his wrists behind his back.

Daniel thrashed wildly, but there were too many of them.

Six.

Maybe eight.

All wearing dark robes and crude wooden masks.

The child calmly stepped out of the headlights and joined them.

Daniel’s stomach twisted with dread.

They dragged him off the road and into the forest.

Branches snapped beneath their feet as they hauled him deeper into the darkness.

He tried to scream through the gag.

No one answered.

After several minutes they entered a clearing.

Torches burned in a wide circle.

At the center stood a massive wooden structure.

It looked like a cross at first glance.

But it wasn’t.

The shape was wrong.

Older somehow.

Like something copied from a much older design.

The cultists tied Daniel to the structure.

His arms were stretched painfully above his head.

The ropes cut into his wrists.

He shook his head frantically, trying to beg through the gag.

One of the masked figures stepped forward holding a long hunting knife.

Another lit more torches around the clearing.

The flames flickered wildly in the wind.

Then the chanting began.

Low.

Rhythmic.

The sound vibrated through the ground like a distant drum.

Daniel’s heart pounded so hard he thought it might burst.

The forest answered the chanting.

Branches cracked somewhere beyond the clearing.

Heavy footsteps moved slowly between the trees.

Something big.

Very big.

The cultists dropped to their knees.

Their chanting grew louder.

More frantic.

Daniel tried to scream.

The trees shifted.

A shape emerged from the darkness.

At first he thought it was a man.

Then it stepped into the firelight.

And he realized it was far too tall.

Its limbs were long and thin, like twisted branches.

Its ribs pushed tightly against pale skin.

But the worst part was its head.

A massive deer skull.

Antlers stretched high above it like a crown of bone.

The creature inhaled slowly through the hollow nasal cavity of the skull.

As if smelling him.

Daniel’s vision blurred with panic.

The thing stepped closer.

Closer.

Its long fingers twitched as if deciding where to begin.

One of the cultists spoke loudly.

“The forest hungers.”

Another answered.

“And we feed it.”

The creature reached out.

Daniel screamed into the gag.

But just before the creature touched him—

It stopped.

The Wendigo slowly turned its skull upward.

Something in the forest had caught its attention.

High among the trees at the edge of the clearing…

a tall figure stood watching.

Daniel couldn’t see its face.

Only its outline.

Still.

Silent.

Observing.

The creature stared at the figure for several long seconds.

Then it turned back to Daniel.

The moment passed.

The antlered head lowered again.

And the screaming began.

The cultists did not stop chanting until the forest went silent once more.

When it was over, the creature disappeared into the trees as quietly as it had arrived.

The cultists untied what remained of Daniel Harper and carried it away into the darkness.

Soon the clearing was empty again.

Only the torches burned.

And the tall figure still stood in the shadows beyond the trees.

Watching.

Waiting.

As if observing something far more important than a single man’s death.

After a moment, it turned and walked deeper into the forest.

And the dark road became silent once more.
