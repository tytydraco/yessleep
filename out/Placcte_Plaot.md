The building rose from the Nebraska sands like a parasite in a dying man’s body. Surrounding this greyish hellscape were dots of light, scattered across the plains like fallen stars. What is now a silent, sterile facility was once vibrant—a place filled with the laughter of children, all silenced by the arrival of the settlers. The Institute sits atop a massacre.

In the dreary morning hours of 1656, a madman named Parker E. Borne landed on the plains of the Great Tribes. He was an exile, sent by a King who had been promised that if Borne were killed, the Crown would inherit the land for free.

But Parker E. Borne never died. The King got his land through blood and gunpowder, but the man meant to be a martyr became something else entirely. Legend says a doctor was born from the carnage. In 1960, following the Treaty of Barren Plains, a man walked into a hidden Native camp. By then, the world believed the Tribes were confined to reservations, but some remained in the shadows, guarding the old rules.

The "civilized" world laughed at their beliefs. Those who spoke of the spiraling abyss that twice unfolded to cover the skies were deemed fools or witches. But 1960 was a year of terror for the Tribes, for the God—**\[195u195u1-59u 9315\]**—was finally being sealed.

The man who walked into that camp was a believer. His father had once bellowed a warning: *“Son, the ways of the plains are more dangerous than the forest.”* The old man never realized the horrible truth: his son was no longer human. He was an ancient deity the Tribes called **Pla’ccte Plaot’**.

The treading steps of the young man—the thing that was once Parker E. Borne—re-stepped into the site of the original massacre. He was a mouse, a filthy rat plaguing the earth, building an Institute from a corrupted guild. He was never a doctor, yet he claimed the title. He built his power on the threat of resummoning the God he claimed to have trapped.

Transcripts indicate the Ritual required "worthy" intellects. Borne walked into a young, fledgling Harvard—not the prestigious icon it is today, but a corrupt den led by a president who believed himself a god. Borne, the rat, gave that president his wish: if he was a god, he would have to die like one.

By the time the **False Hope Experiment** rose, the police were too corrupt to notice the disappearances. Borne wanted to free the unknown God to "see the truth," but after **Incident 9011**, the narrative shifted.

Parker Borne had a vision that day. It didn’t come in sleep; Borne had long ago traded rest for the eternal, vibrating hum of the plains. It was a rupture in reality. Sector 12-B was destroyed and rebuilt by 1977, and a man named S’Crate was sent into the ward as a "consultant."

S’Crate discovered the Ritual. He discovered that while it "sealed" the God, it had actually birthed a replacement. S’Crate spent his remaining days screaming into the padded walls of the Institute:

*“He’s not human! He’s a placebo for something else! I don’t know… the chains, the rooster, the walls… the Placebo!”*

And so, the staff began to call him **Dr. Plac.E.Bo.**

Borne hated the name, but names have a way of sticking—especially when they carry power. The Tribes knew this. They knew that to know a deity's true name is to control it. They called him **Pla’ccte Plaot’**: *The White Walker, The Figure of Hatred.* The Institute has a true name, too. It was used to seal the God of the Great Plains—a being neither good nor evil, but simply *bored* by the constraints of time.

The Institute as such had a true name and its true name helped seal the god of the Great Plains. The god was not malevolent but also not benevolent. It simply existed beyond the plains of time and did what it wanted. To a god, whatever it did was nothing short of boring.  Names held a purpose and they would never be ones that a fool would break. The truth of the world lied in the names of the god of the Great Plains for there was never one. 

Never one for the sun and moon were never twice. Twice unfolded under the starless sky, there lay a great expanse. A man looked above and heard a rooster dying. A man whose chains had killed him a long time ago, the names of the past killed and killed.
