The day I decided to visit the wishing well was the exact day Sol finally found the courage to laugh in my face about my desperation.

​"Look, I love you, but you just don't learn," she said, pausing because her own words took on a heavier tone than she intended. But then she looked down at the face of Alexander Hamilton on the ten-dollar bill I was holding over the water, and she kept laughing. "Becky, with that ten bucks, you could buy yourself two decent burgers. You’re too much. Seriously, you need to learn how to be single." Sol adjusted her vintage blazer; from an archival brand that had ceased to exist years ago. The cemetery sun was making me sweat.

​The wishing well was in the cemetery, of course.

​I care about Sol deeply. She is the only person who truly understands me in this world, so I did my best to take her commentary as the joke it was meant to be and not take offense. I gathered my courage despite the creeping dread in my chest; I’ve seen far too many movies where this kind of thing goes horribly wrong. It’s bound to be malicious. To seal the deal, I’ll probably have to pay with half my soul. Or maybe it will grant my wish now only to claim my firstborn child later as a retroactive payment. So many terrifying possibilities, and yet, I still decided to go through with it.

​I just couldn't take it anymore. The truth was, I wasn't going to survive another first date. I couldn't bear listening to the same three boring talking points over and over again. For some bizarre reason, they all played instruments. I had dated two pianists, a bassist, a ukulelist, and two other guitarists. None of them ever knew where their lives were going. Just like me, they were all failed artists, trapped in the delusion that they were destined for greatness. And for as long as the initial spell of the honeymoon phase lasted, I stupidly believed it for them. But the most basic, fundamental flaw? None of them had any appreciation for who I actually was. It was always an act. A disguise.

​The problem was that the longer I stayed with someone, the more my disguise began to fuse to my skin. It irritated me. It maddened me that every compliment was directed at the facade, while none were meant for the real me. It always ended the exact same way: as pieces of my dress and makeup began to peel away, it became painfully obvious how much they detested the person underneath. By the time my tears completely melted the mask away, it was already over.

​So, when Sol mentioned hearing about a magical well while visiting a deceased aunt at the cemetery, it felt like the perfect opportunity to end the misery soaking through my life.

​"Sol, what did you say I had to tell the well?" I asked, my seriousness sharply contrasting with the expression of the person I trusted most, who was currently doing everything in her power to stop laughing at me.

​"Nothing, Becky. You just have to think really hard about what you want, and the well will make it happen in a way that feels completely natural," she explained, sighing as she watched me squeeze my eyes shut and drop the bill into the water.

​Except it didn't fall into the well. It caught the wind and fluttered to the ground.

​I picked it up and checked to see if it had torn. It was in perfect condition, except for one thing: a crude, hand-drawn happy face was now superimposed over the serious expression of the president on the bill, winking at me. In a deeply anticlimactic move, I had to manually plunge my hand into the water to force it to the bottom, soaking my sleeve just to ensure it wouldn't escape again.

​Nothing happened. In the movies, the world stops, or a guy who looks exactly like your type steps out from behind the bushes, or a pegasus deploys its wings, ridden by a magical space knight. There was none of that here.

​"Well, look on the bright side. You can always fish your bill back out and at least buy a soda..." Sol began.

​"Don't mess around. If I take the bill back, the spell will break," I snapped, my tone of disappointment betraying the confidence of my words. "He’ll show up later. I’m going to meet him, you’ll see."

​"I cannot believe that given the chance to ask for anything in the world, you chose to wish for a boyfriend. Becky, we seriously need to restructure your priorities," Sol said, walking with me toward the exit.

​Sol didn't bring it up again, probably because she forgot about it the second it stopped serving as my "weird stunt of the week." I stopped mentioning it too, swallowed by the shame of her parting words. Is that how people saw me? Did I really seem that desperate?

​Eventually, I managed to bury the embarrassing memory. Weeks passed, and just when I was truly on the verge of forgetting it entirely, the campus kiosk where I wanted to buy a drink ran out of change for my twenty-dollar bill. To complete the purchase, the cashier needed exactly ten dollars in small change. I immediately regretted wasting my smaller bill weeks ago on a patently false hope.

​Suddenly, the guy standing behind me in line interrupted the cashier. He said he was there to buy a large double-scoop Italian ice, which cost exactly ten dollars. I tried not to stare, but I couldn't help it; he was incredibly attractive. He smiled at me. I smiled back. Then, as he reached into his wallet to pay, he handed over a ten-dollar bill.

​There was a hand-drawn, winking smiley face superimposed over the face of Alexander Hamilton.

​"Where did you get that bill?!" I asked, completely bewildered. Was the magic real? Did the well actually have powers? Was it my own cynicism that had blinded me to the magic of the world?

​"It’s a funny story, actually," he replied naturally, taking his Italian ice and smiling. "I work part-time at a cemetery, and people are always leaving money in this one fountain because they claim it’s magical."

​"Oh... right. I see," I stammered, flushed with embarrassment, stepping aside so he could leave. He walked away, but not very far; he sat down at a nearby campus table.

​Defying my own instincts, I walked over and sat at the same table. He slipped one of his earbuds out and offered a sly, mocking smile.

​"You’re the one who put that bill in the fountain, aren't you?" he asked playfully.

​"I mean, yes, but not like, seriously. It was practically a game because I wanted to prove someone wrong," I replied, swallowing hard, trying to act like this was a minor coincidence and that I wasn't losing my mind.

​His facial expressions suggested he was sarcastically humoring me.

​"Alright, fair enough. What were you wishing for?"

​"Nothing interesting. Money. Lots of money."

​"Everyone always asks for that," he said casually, scraping the frozen edges of the ice to redistribute the artificial blue raspberry flavor. "I don't know why, you seemed more interesting than that. I thought you would’ve wished for something unique."

​"Well, it's what makes the most sense, isn't it? It would solve most people's problems," I defended myself, even though it was a lie. I had wished for something stupid.

​"Not everything can be bought with money." His dark eyes locked onto mine. "I'm Chase. It's a pleasure to meet you... What's your name? You haven't told me." He shifted the conversation with an effortless expertise. I knew exactly what this was. He was hitting on me.

​"Becky," I answered. I tore a piece of paper from my notebook and scribbled my phone number on it.

​"Great, Becky. I’ll text you. Keep an eye out. Take care," he said, turning to walk toward the engineering building. Since I major in Sociology, our paths would have never crossed if it weren't for today.

​Was Chase what I had been begging the universe for all this time? Had my wish actually come true? Had I finally done it... Had I met the love of my life? It was too soon to tell, but it was just early enough for the feeling to start taking root.

​Still, I called Sol to tell her.

​"So, what I'm gathering is that you fell in love with the guy who cleans the cemetery decorations," Sol’s voice crackled through the receiver.

​"You and I both know it’s not like that, Sol. There is something about this guy that I can't quite wrap my head around..." I began, trying to justify it. The Uber driver taking me home wasn't paying attention, but I still averted my eyes, staring out at the traffic.

​"A job, a lucrative career path, charm, and ten bucks. That’s all it is," Sol said, sighing with a chuckle. She was entirely too familiar with my sudden bursts of romantic fatalism. "Do you know what instrument he plays yet?" she mocked.

​Right on cue, a text from an unknown number lit up my screen.

​*Becky, I know we just met and this might be weird, but do you want to see how my violin cover of Come on Eileen turned out?*

​I blinked hard, trying to shake myself awake from what felt like a surreal dream.

​"According to the text I just got... the violin," I said into the phone, stunned.

​Sol laughed, wished me luck, and hung up to go eat lunch.

​Chase had sent the video. After listening to the first thirty seconds of such a cliché, overplayed song, I paused it. I noted the time and decided I would wait exactly four and a half minutes before replying, just to make it seem like I had watched the whole thing and was thoroughly impressed.

​When the time passed, I texted him back saying it was amazing, asking where he had learned to play, and telling him it was a great song choice. The fresh makeup of my new disguise felt natural, cool against my skin, untouched by sweat thanks to the car’s blasting air conditioning.

​As the weeks rolled by, my conversations with Chase deepened. I genuinely thought he was one of the most fascinating people I had ever met. He had an endless supply of stories, a distinct mole on his nose, slightly crooked teeth, and a knack for whispering the funniest jokes right when the movie theater fell dead silent.

​It was a strange thing to admit, but I loved his teeth. Not just because they were uneven, but because they were noticeably, unusually sharp. I didn't think I had ever seen anything like them. Sometimes, when he was deeply concentrated on his violin, he would bite his lip, and I could almost feel that sharp edge against my own skin.

​I could no longer tell if my mask was melting or if it had become my actual face. I didn't have to pretend to be impressed by him; beyond his talent, Chase had a remarkably clear vision of his future. It’s rare for a twenty-four-year-old to have his life so meticulously planned out without family money, but he mapped out his days in minute detail. Sometimes, he would pull out a planner and jot things down. He had a massive collection of fine pens and the most impeccably manicured nails I had ever seen on a man; though they were a bit longer than usual.

​By the fifth week, I realized the acting was gone. I was showing him my real face, and he treated my vulnerability with the same tenderness that other guys only gave to my facades. But around that time, a creeping irritation began to set in. I couldn't understand why the warmth in his smile when he was with me completely vanished whenever he interacted with anyone else. His expressions around others felt hollow.

​"You know you don't have to fake laughing at my jokes. I'm not that funny. You can just say it," I told him one afternoon, sounding sharper than I intended.

​He fell silent, looking thoughtful. I started to sweat. Panic flinched in my chest.

​"I’m not faking anything. I genuinely find you funny," he finally said, his voice entirely smooth. But there was something else underneath. I knew it.

​I began to suspect that he was wearing a disguise of his own. I decided I needed to figure out a plan to strip it away and see who he really was. When I confided in Sol, she told me I was overthinking it. I knew I wasn't.

​The opportunity finally presented itself while we were rewatching an old episode of House in his bedroom. I needed to catch him entirely off guard.

​I reached up to caress his face, and he turned his gaze to mine. His mouth and cheeks curved into a warm, affectionate smile—but it didn't reach his eyes. His eyes were entirely empty. Suddenly, the skin beneath my fingers felt sickeningly wrong. Artificial. Indescribably non-human.

​Before I could stop myself, I dug my fingernails deep into the flesh of his cheeks and pulled with everything I had.

​The skin gave way, peeling back under my grip, and I finally forced him to show his true face. It wasn't a man. It was a monster with rows of razor-sharp fangs, massive claws, and God only knows what it plans to do to me.
