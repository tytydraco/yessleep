Humanity's single greatest fortune lies in the fact that you can drown in a puddle. How sweet and personal, face down in mud you could easily drink up and yet choose to fill your lungs to the brim with.

My diary, were I to write one, would undoubtedly be not only of medical, or novelty, but also of literary value. But the writings of every day, hour, second of my life would fill thousands of tomes, more than any interested reader or literary scientist might take upon themselves throughout their entire lifetime. The critical reception of my work, thus, would be more of a competition who can consume (and receive as well as consider) the most of my steady droppings, who can sketch a map through a geological life, which blessed few gain the intuition to assume, out of nowhere, passages from the two-hundredth-and-seventh book that go on to define lives, the rare moment when something stirred in me that might still touch human flesh. You see, my flesh isn’t such and hasn’t been for a while.

There isn’t a single chance of remission. Pain might only let go of my limbs and wrinkled skin patches once the connection is suddenly cut (the tiniest moment seeming to dissolve into before and after, too meagre to comprehend as it occurs and defines me), one or another finger finally floating off into harsher waters, to dissolve ever faster and finally turn to sand squishing between the toes of a seven-year-old vacationing on a cheap beach. The nerve cells should, from what I’ve had the pleasure of noting, remain mostly indestructible, so who knows what pain these grains may never articulate – maybe they share with each other, a million tiny stings unable to connect or feel, so many different articulations of leaving! Anecdotes I would like to fill this diary with. But I cannot, as I remain human, and the human is whole.

Imagine salt. Layering, pattering the soft parts of you. Grains of acid stacking, crusting on your bare, frantic nerves, a pain that begins a low acidic buzzing and is guaranteed never to lessen, only ever to settle, to layer and plate, forming walls of burn coating your limbs and eyes.

Dependency is a natural state for any human being. To wrestle eternity is not impossible, or nowhere near as impossible as actual independence might be. For my part, I depend on a rock (more of a boulder, really), a mineral that my body sticks out of. I function as a kind of parasitic lifeform feeding off the natural chemical movements inside the dead stone, my tracts and streams hooked up by robust, if the slightest bit insensitive devices to a soft inner soul. In a thousand years, I could have never refused the offer to become this stunning outer layer stretching the boundaries of the geological and medical sciences, in the service of the sciences, the arts, ultimately the species (as seen from birds-eye view, working down in the valley eons ago). In a million years, I might have found it in me to refuse. The life of the species appears somewhat foreign now. Instead of afraid, I felt relieved when the waves began to lick at my waist, rubbing me as gently as a scientist might, exploring a future together. I’d waited a while to finally touch the one and only thing I had been seeing for most of my life.

Is it eternal, or at least very long life that makes any blessing a curse eventually? Would some reoccurring value of my writings turn any curse into an everlasting blessing? Into seeds of sand my words ought to go, sending out this reversal of all values to those who might still hold onto finite concepts.

Ol’ Poseidon pours his freshest right into my wound. Nobody can hear it crack and sizzle, hitting raw nerve that never stops tingling. Pressure from salt accumulate shoves levels, fractures and shapes formed from the residue ever deeper into the holes of my nerves, filling the spaces between, tingling with the flame and energy that all suffering registers to the unfeeling material world as. It’s a joy, really, and I could write a thousand messages in bottles just eulogizing the infinite feelings I won’t have anymore once I crack.

It’s been a while since I’ve seen anyone traveling the seas.

No one ever came to check on me, asking me to fill a questionnaire on my satisfaction with some medical institute’s services, no follow-up studies. No stone-faced science team careful not to step on any residue of mine nearby that the wind might’ve carried over the cliffs. I am the sole producer of sand in the area, a desert that might never run out, regenerated by living engine. Ending when stone ends. The longer a life, the less likely eternity appears to the intuition, and I stand firm in my belief that the end of stone, whether it takes the planet with it or leaves a blue orb to swimmingly rot, will be the last ever thing to reflect in the rough gemstones called my eyeballs, beneath all the scum. What an apocalyptic faith to hold, huh?

Or something will give, the mechanism in the stone will change, the science inevitably fail, and I might fail before my host does. Focusing all my romantic imagination, I might dream of travelling, being dragged through the oceans by any means necessary for a brief period before my vitals blink out and my brain decides to carve out that final dopamine shot. Of course, as all dying or dead ones, I will defecate, tiny pebbles rising to the surface next to my blissfully degenerating skull. Ought to ask whoever finds me, in my testament, the one thousandth of my book series, to place them gently in my eye sockets, if they find them.

Beneath the salt.

That’s where I look, where I dig for facts, love, anger, humanity, and find much of it. My feelings remain stable. My sanity has remained here, and in it, I might find the only place which has turned to stone along with my bodily hull. How am I thinking, who am I speaking to, what has kept me from going too mad even to babble for who knows how many days?

My legacy won’t be mine. It couldn’t be. Snapshots of a monument cannot capture the movement of life, the bustling of what is under the skin, the pressing and waving of a sea thousand times condemned, cursed, fucked. Within that flow is where I live, and not a moment can remain as I do. My work will be a statue of different kind entirely, perhaps written on a soggy paper note. Some classics in the libraries must be older than me. Unimaginable how they must ache. I can barely imagine how I may feel tomorrow. The next layer is never to be expected. Or pain would lose its’ purpose for flesh.

It’s tomorrow. Salt migraines.

I might share my consciousness with the boulder and never notice as the boulder has no mind.

I wish to greet a fish. I wish to scratch myself.

I long for my fingers to fall off, one by one, floating along downward, travellers detached from any chance at repair. Nobody may ever put me back together. If anyone did, they might pretend me conscious. As the newest, greatest achievement between geology and medicine, the two greatest empirical sciences who couldn’t measure across geological timespans, and thus had to fail, never knowing the rise. I knew when something lapped at my feet, licking me raw. One attunes to the slowest of changes, seeing them change all the same. A most patient observer and archivist of layers. Longing for toes, knees, cock and balls, hefty pieces of stomach to break off, letting in the greedy salt pillars that lay in wait, finally off to oblivion for a brief nerve ending holiday. I can’t begin to know where the current finally leads, but I know so well where my knowledge of it ends. Currents are strong at their ends, drifting into the horizon. What they take along on their stoic path, more cheerful than most, has no more conscious. It drowned.

Breathing in a final gasp of water, this should all turn out to be a faraway, blurry dream. Drowning child, trapped underwater, everything feels hostile and painful, agentic and malicious. Time to be either the child or the dream. Time doesn’t pass in dreams. No time has passed at all. Not a moment has gone by since my surgery, since the water couldn’t kill me, not a drop has been spilled, and I can suck my throat full of my dream, salt finally so deep in it leaves its’ brethren behind, working its’ way through, and look, I rise to geologists’ or literates heavenly next lives. When I pass away, I’d like to decline my journey. Currents run throughout the world, but you know what’d be nice and nostalgic, to help me write relatable voices? Drowning slowly, while fighting to reach up to the surface. Which I should have dared in waking life. The experiment failed, they would’ve found my corpse at sea. Blue, bloated, dissolved, friendly to the salt. Only a dead man can follow the current. It’s the opposite of sinking.
