There were six of us on that trip, and every night before we zipped the tents shut, we counted six.

That’s why it took us too long to understand the thing moving around outside wasn’t trying to get in.

It was making sure we kept counting wrong.

I know how that sounds.

If I had read that sentence online a year ago, I would have rolled my eyes and kept scrolling. I’m not the kind of person who buys into campfire bullshit, or skinwalker stories, or those Reddit threads where some guy hears one weird noise in a field and suddenly it’s an ancient Appalachian curse. I work in logistics. I spend most of my life fixing delays, counting inventory, and making sure things that are supposed to be in one place do not mysteriously end up somewhere else.

That trip should have been simple.

Three days. Two nights. One marked trail in, one marked trail out. A backcountry site on state land about ninety minutes from the city, enough distance to feel like we’d earned our beer but not enough to qualify as a survival story.

It was me, Noah, Mara, Theo, Jules, and Becca.

If you want to understand why what happened got so bad so fast, you need to understand the group, because the fear didn’t just come from whatever was out there. It came from the way it got between us.

Noah was my oldest friend. Easy laugh, always filthy, the kind of guy who somehow made every trip feel like a worse idea and a better time at the exact same time. He was the one who suggested the campsite. He said he’d found it on some hiking forum and swore it was quiet, scenic, and “just enough off-grid to make Theo complain.” That was enough to get us all to say yes.

Theo was Mara’s boyfriend. Smart, competent, and the kind of man who treated fear like a personal insult. If something scared him, his first instinct was to make it smaller by sounding smarter than it.

Mara was the opposite. She didn’t spook easily, but when something felt wrong to her, she didn’t need to dress it up in logic to justify paying attention. That ended up mattering.

Jules was one of those people who noticed everything and then apologized for noticing it. She could tell when a waiter had a migraine or when someone in a group was pretending to be less drunk than they were. She was the first one to hear the voice, and if we’d listened to her properly, maybe things would have gone differently.

Becca was the peacemaker. Warm, funny, one of those people who physically moved toward tension in order to smooth it over. She shared a tent with Jules and spent the first day trying to keep everyone fed and less annoying than they naturally were.

And then there was me, Eli, apparently the kind of asshole who will tell you the shape of a thing long before he can bear to tell you its face.

The hike in was normal enough that it hurts to think about now.

It had rained the day before, so the trail was soft and dark and gave under our boots in that satisfying way wet forest dirt does. Pine needles stuck to our socks when we stopped to drink water. Theo complained about the humidity every twenty minutes like no one had warned him forests were damp. Noah kept doing a fake nature-documentary voice whenever anyone said anything stupid.

“Observe,” he said at one point, stepping aside with both trekking poles held like antlers. “The urban male attempts to identify bird song by guessing crow every time.”

“Fuck off,” Theo said, without heat.

Mara laughed. Jules said the woods smelled good. Becca said the woods smelled like fungus and wet dog. I remember Noah turning around and walking backward for a few steps, grinning at all of us, and saying, “This is exactly why people die out here. Too much charisma in one group.”

I remember all of that because it matters that we were still ourselves then. The horror got all over what happened later, but that first afternoon was stupid and bright and ordinary in the way adult friendships feel best — half nostalgia, half irritation, all of it held together with snacks and profanity.

The campsite itself sat in a shallow clearing about a hundred yards off the trail, tucked into trees thick enough that by sunset it already felt later than it was. There was a stone fire ring, a rough bench made from a split log, and a creek somewhere downhill to the east. You couldn’t see it from camp, but you could hear it if everyone shut up at the same time.

We pitched the tents in a loose triangle around the dead fire pit.

Me and Noah in one.

Mara and Theo in another.

Jules and Becca in the third.

The ground was softer than it looked. The air had that wet, metallic smell you get when the sun goes down after rain and all the leaves start giving the heat back. By the time we had the fire going, we were all a little sweaty, a little dirty, and in the sort of mood where a cheap beer tastes expensive because you had to carry it uphill.

Nothing supernatural happened that first evening.

What happened was better, because it meant all the fear later had somewhere to rot from.

Theo burned one side of the sausages and insisted they were “charred, not burnt.” Noah dropped his in the dirt and ate it anyway. Becca found out the hard way that the cooler leaked when she picked it up by one handle and got creek-cold beer water down her leg. Jules went quiet once or twice, staring toward the trees like she was listening, but when I asked what was up she just shrugged and said, “Nothing. It’s just one of those places where you can feel how dark it’ll be before it’s actually dark.”

Noah heard that and said, in the most theatrical voice possible, “Yeah, no, don’t say shit like that when we’re about to sleep in nylon coffins.”

“Relax,” Theo said. “If a serial killer comes through here, he’s taking Noah first.”

“Because I’m the hottest, or because I’d scream best?”

“Both,” Mara said.

We laughed. The fire cracked. Somewhere down the hill a branch snapped, and six adults all looked at the trees at once and then pretended they hadn’t.

That was probably the first moment the woods got its hand in us.

Not because the branch meant anything.

Because all six of us reacted.

Because some part of the body knows when it has crossed the line into a place where hearing is more important than being heard.

By ten-thirty the fire had burned low and the cold had settled in properly. It wasn’t winter cold. Just that damp, needling kind of spring darkness that gets into your sleeves and around your neck and makes every zipper sound too loud.

We did a last pass through camp.

Hung the food bag.

Killed the fire.

Pissed at the edge of the tree line in pairs because Becca didn’t want anyone wandering off alone and Noah thought that was hilarious until he admitted he didn’t really feel like walking into the dark by himself either.

Then we zipped up.

That first headcount was Noah’s idea.

Not because he was scared. Because he was still joking.

“All right,” he said from outside our tent, his voice muffled through nylon. “Roll call for the murder forest. Theo?”

“Here.”

“Mara?”

“Unfortunately.”

“Jules?”

“Yeah.”

“Becca?”

“Yep.”

“Eli?”

“Here.”

“Noah?”

I slapped the side of the tent and said, “Still an asshole.”

Six voices.

Six people.

The noise of the creek below us.

The soft complaint of tree branches leaning against one another in the dark.

Noah zipped the last of the vestibule shut and muttered, “There. Counted. Happy now?”

We weren’t happy.

But we weren’t afraid yet either.

Inside the tent it smelled like wet nylon, dirt, and Noah’s socks. He stuffed one jacket into a corner as a pillow and immediately started talking about how Theo definitely lied about breaking in his boots before the hike because no one limps that hard after six miles unless they’re dramatic or stupid.

I remember answering him. I don’t remember what I said. The conversation blurred into that half-sleepy bullshit people talk in sleeping bags when they’re cold and tired and already drifting. The creek became background. The tent walls ticked quietly as droplets from the branches overhead hit the fabric.

I was almost asleep when Jules screamed.

Not a yelp.

A real scream. Sharp and panicked enough that my body jerked before my mind caught up.

Noah was up on one elbow beside me instantly. From the next tent over, Becca said, “Jules? Jules?” in a voice that was already too high.

Theo shouted, “What happened?”

Then Jules said, “Somebody’s outside.”

Every sound in the campsite changed at once.

Or maybe we did.

The creek sounded farther away. The tent suddenly felt too thin. My own breath sounded loud and wrong in my ears.

“What?” Noah snapped.

“Somebody’s outside the tent,” Jules said again, and now she was crying. “Someone was right outside.”

Mara unzipped her tent halfway and said, “Don’t open that.”

“I’m not opening it,” Theo said, though it sounded like he had been about to.

Noah reached for the zipper by our tent door.

I grabbed his wrist.

“Don’t.”

We stayed there, all six of us in our separate little fabric boxes, talking into the dark like idiots because nobody wanted to be the first one to admit how fucking scared they were.

“What did you hear?” Mara asked.

“My name,” Jules said. “It said my name.”

Theo let out a breath through his nose, that particular annoyed sound he made when his rationality was trying to get control back.

“You were asleep.”

“I was not asleep.”

“Jules,” Becca said softly, “what exactly did you hear?”

There was a pause.

Then Jules said, quieter now, “Noah.”

Noah looked at me.

“What?”

“It sounded like Noah,” Jules said. “It was right outside and it sounded like Noah.”

A beat passed.

Then from somewhere between the tents, in Noah’s exact voice, something said:

“Jules?”

Every muscle in my body locked.

Noah was next to me.

I could feel him there.

Warm shoulder, sleeping bag rustle, his breath catching hard in the dark.

Nobody spoke.

The voice outside didn’t say anything else right away. It moved.

That was worse.

You know how people sound moving around a campsite at night — zippers, boots, branches, swearing, the little practical noises of a body doing normal things in a cramped place. This wasn’t that. This was just footsteps. Soft, slow, measured enough that they didn’t sound careless.

They moved from Jules and Becca’s tent toward ours.

Noah whispered, “What the fuck.”

Then the footsteps stopped just outside our tent.

I could hear breathing.

Not mine.

Not Noah’s.

Outside.

Right where my head was.

If you’ve never had that particular kind of fear, it’s hard to explain how fast the body tries to separate from itself. My hands had gone numb. My jaw hurt from how hard I was clenching it. The tent wall was maybe a foot from my face, and on the other side of it was something breathing through a mouth close enough that I could hear wetness in it.

Then, very softly, right at my ear level through the fabric, Noah’s voice said:

“Eli.”

Noah made a noise beside me. Not even a word. Just a broken little inhale.

And then Theo, from the next tent, shouted, “Everybody answer right now.”

That did something useful to us.

Fear likes silence because silence makes room for imagination. Theo’s voice was the first hard thing in the dark that still sounded human.

“Mara.”

“I’m here.”

“Becca.”

“Here.”

“Jules.”

A shaking breath. “Here.”

“Noah.”

“Here.”

“Eli.”

“Here.”

“Theo.”

“Yeah.”

Six.

All six.

The breathing outside stopped.

For one insane second that almost felt like relief.

Then something walked slowly between all three tents.

Not running.

Not circling like an animal.

Just walking.

One set of footsteps in wet dirt. A careful pace. Slow enough that we all listened to it move from one side of camp to the other.

At one point it passed so close to Mara and Theo’s tent that Mara actually said, “Don’t fucking touch the wall,” into the dark, and I still don’t know whether she was talking to Theo or to whatever was outside.

It did not touch the walls.

It just kept walking.

Eventually the sound drifted toward the trees and was swallowed by the creek.

No one slept after that.

At dawn, the fear looked stupid.

That’s another thing fear does — it turns embarrassing in daylight before it turns permanent.

We all came out of the tents looking like shit. Gray-faced, damp-haired, creased from bad sleep we hadn’t really gotten. The clearing looked smaller in morning light. Less like a place and more like a patch of dirt with our junk in it.

Noah was the first one to try normal.

“Well,” he said, voice rough, “if that was one of you, genuinely go fuck yourself.”

Nobody laughed.

Jules had a death grip on her coffee mug and looked like she might throw it if anyone told her she’d imagined the voice. Mara looked tired and sharp. Theo looked pissed off in the way men look when the world has failed to obey reason and they take it as disrespect.

“I wasn’t asleep,” Jules said before anyone could say it. “I know what I heard.”

Mara nodded immediately. “I heard it too.”

Theo rubbed both hands over his face. “I heard a voice. I’m not arguing that. I’m saying sounds carry weird out here.”

“Sure,” Noah said, too fast. “Because the woods naturally reproduce my exact voice and then stand outside tents like a fucking pervert.”

Becca, who had hardly spoken at all, said quietly, “It laughed first.”

We all looked at her.

“What?” I said.

She stared into her coffee. “Before it said Jules’s name. I heard a laugh. I thought it was Noah moving around outside. That’s why I didn’t say anything at first.”

Noah’s expression changed.

“What kind of laugh?”

Becca swallowed. “Yours. But…” She looked up at him and immediately away again. “Wrong.”

Noah opened his mouth, then closed it.

That was the first moment I saw fear on him without humor covering it.

Mara said, “Okay. Rules.”

Theo exhaled like he hated that she was probably right.

“No one leaves their tent alone tonight,” she said. “If you have to piss, wake someone. No answering any voices outside after dark. No opening tent flaps no matter what you hear.”

“We should probably just fucking leave,” Jules said.

Nobody answered that immediately, because the fact was we all wanted to leave and none of us wanted to be the first person to say the trip was over because a voice in the woods had spooked us. That’s part of what shame does in a group. It makes people stay in danger longer than they should because no one wants to be the least rational person there.

Theo said, “We do one more day. If anything weird happens tonight, we pack at first light.”

“One more day for what?” Jules snapped.

“For daylight,” he said. “For not stomping down a trail in the dark because we’re freaked out and tired and hearing shit.”

“That wasn’t hearing shit,” she said.

“I know that,” he said, too quickly.

That “I know that” mattered.

Not because it was kind.

Because Theo did not say things he didn’t mean when he was scared. He only said them when saying the truth out loud felt even worse.

The day itself should have been enough to make us settle down.

Sunlight.

Trail.

Normal movement.

Water bottles and granola bars and stupid conversation.

Instead it just gave the wrongness more places to hide.

We did a short loop along the creek after breakfast. Nothing serious. Mostly because staying in the campsite felt impossible and leaving outright still felt like admitting too much. The trail ran over wet roots and through pockets of colder air that smelled like stone and creek water. Noah made a big show of taking point with a trekking pole held like a spear, which would have been funny if he hadn’t kept checking behind us every thirty seconds.

By then we had all started doing that.

Checking.

Counting.

Listening for one another without looking obvious about it.

Somewhere around noon, Theo stopped walking, frowned at the group, and counted us out loud under his breath.

“One, two, three…”

Mara heard him and said, “Seriously?”

He looked irritated at being caught. “What?”

“Don’t do that.”

“Why?”

“Because now I’m gonna do it too.”

Jules said, “I already have been.”

No one had anything to say to that.

Later, while we were eating by the creek, Becca asked Noah if he’d slept at all and Noah said, “Not really,” then, a second later, “Did I say anything weird in my sleep?”

It was the kind of question that would have been nothing in any other context.

But I remember exactly how everyone looked at him after he said it.

Not because the question was wrong.

Because it sounded like a line somebody would ask after they’d heard it used once already.

Mara asked, “Why would you ask that?”

Noah shrugged, too loose. “Because apparently my fucking voice was doing laps around camp last night.”

Theo snorted. “That’s not funny.”

“I wasn’t joking.”

And maybe he wasn’t. I still don’t know.

That afternoon the air changed. Not the weather exactly. The pressure of it. It got still in the ugly way woods do sometimes where sound seems to stop carrying right. Even the creek sounded muted, like somebody had thrown a blanket over it.

Jules noticed first.

She always noticed first.

She was standing at the edge of the clearing by the trees, staring out into nothing I could see, when I came up beside her with two beers in hand and asked if she wanted one.

She took it without looking at me.

“What?”

She hesitated, then said, “I don’t think it was trying to sound like Noah at first.”

“What do you mean?”

“That first time.” She swallowed. “I heard the laugh before it said my name. It sounded like him, but not right, like…” She made a frustrated motion with the beer can. “Like somebody trying his laugh out before they used it.”

I felt a cold line go down my spine that had nothing to do with the weather.

“That doesn’t make sense.”

“I know.”

But it did make sense later.

That was the problem.

That evening nobody wanted to admit how afraid they were, so everyone compensated badly.

Theo got more controlling.

Mara got quieter.

Jules jumped every time someone moved too fast.

Becca kept asking practical little questions — where’s the lighter, who has the bug spray, has anyone seen my other sock — in the tone people use when they’re trying to keep normal life alive by force.

Noah became aggressively funny, which was the surest sign he was rattled.

I became obsessed with details.

Who seemed tired.

Who looked away too quickly.

Who repeated themselves.

Who forgot part of a conversation.

That sounds rational when you’re in it. Later you realize it was the fear already teaching you how to mistrust.

We did the headcount again before bed.

This time nobody laughed.

“Theo.”

“Here.”

“Mara.”

“Here.”

“Jules.”

“Here.”

“Becca.”

“Yeah.”

“Noah.”

“Here.”

“Eli.”

“Here.”

Six.

The dark came down harder that second night.

Cloud cover, maybe. No moon through the branches. Inside the tent the air felt colder and wetter than it had the night before. Condensation gathered along the seams and dripped when the fabric shifted. I could smell dirt on Noah’s socks and smoke in my own hoodie and that trapped-breath smell two people make in a small enclosed space after midnight.

Neither of us talked much.

At some point I think Noah started to apologize for how weird the trip had gotten, then gave up halfway through the sentence because neither of us had the energy to pretend anymore.

I don’t know how long I was awake before the voice came back.

This time it didn’t start with a name.

It started with weight.

Something leaned, very gently, against the outside of Mara and Theo’s tent.

Not enough to bow the wall.

Just enough to make nylon stretch and whisper.

Then Mara’s voice said, from outside in the dark:

“You counted wrong.”

Every tent went silent at once.

I don’t mean no one spoke.

I mean the whole campsite seemed to stop having room for sound.

Theo was the first one to break it.

“Fuck that,” he snapped, too loud, and I heard Mara hiss his name from inside their tent.

The thing outside laughed.

Not a big horror-movie laugh. Not maniacal. Just one short breath of amusement in Mara’s exact voice.

Then it moved.

This time it moved faster. Not running, but more confidently. Between tents. Around the dead fire ring. Through the wet leaves and low brush with a gait too smooth to sound like someone stumbling around in the dark.

Noah whispered, “Don’t answer it,” even though I hadn’t moved.

Then, from right outside our tent again, his voice said:

“Eli.”

The shape of my own name in Noah’s mouth has never sounded the same since.

I could feel Noah beside me.

His shoulder pressed to the sleeping bag.

His breath.

The tiny involuntary shake in him he would have denied in daylight.

The voice outside waited.

Then, softer:

“Don’t let him fall asleep.”

That did something terrible to us because it sounded like warning, not threat.

The most effective lies are the ones that sound useful.

I turned my head toward Noah in the dark.

He turned toward me at the same time.

Neither of us said anything.

Outside, the footsteps moved off again.

Then Becca started crying.

Not loudly. The kind of crying someone does when they’re trying to breathe around it and failing.

Jules said her name over and over from inside their tent. Mara swore once, under her breath. Theo said, “Nobody opens anything,” like he was trying to nail the sentence to the air.

We stayed like that for hours.

Or what felt like hours.

At one point I must have drifted, because the next thing I remember is waking with that awful instant certainty that something had changed beside me.

No sound woke me.

No touch.

Just wrongness.

You know your friends’ sleep in ways you don’t think about until it matters. The way they breathe. Shift. Snore. Scratch. The stupid human noise of being unconscious near someone.

Noah’s breathing had always been irregular when he slept. Little pauses. Mouth open. The occasional half-snort through one nostril when he rolled onto his back.

What I heard beside me then was too regular.

Not louder.

Not stranger.

Just careful.

As if the breathing had started over and was doing a good job of itself.

My whole body locked up.

Outside, somewhere near the trees, Noah’s voice whispered my name again.

Inside the tent, beside me, the careful breathing never changed.

I did not move.

I did not answer.

I did not do a single useful thing.

After maybe ten seconds — maybe sixty, maybe five, fear ruins time — the thing beside me said, in Noah’s voice and almost not moving at all:

“Don’t answer me if it says my name.”

I have thought about that sentence every day since.

Because I still don’t know which Noah said it.

Morning did not bring relief the second time. It brought fracture.

Nobody looked rested. Nobody looked fully human, honestly. Theo’s eyes were bloodshot and too bright. Jules had that exhausted, overfocused look people get when they’ve gone beyond panic into pure vigilance. Becca wouldn’t let anyone stand behind her. Mara looked at each of us a little too long before speaking, like she was checking that the right person had come out of the right tent.

Noah came out last.

Maybe that means nothing.

Maybe it means everything.

He looked awful. Pale. Dirt on one knee from where he’d apparently slept half outside the sleeping pad. He met my eyes and I had the worst, smallest hesitation before saying, “You good?”

That hesitation was enough. He saw it.

“Jesus Christ,” he said. “You too?”

“What?”

“That look. Like you’re checking if I’m me.”

Nobody said anything.

Then Theo said, “We’re leaving.”

No argument this time.

Everyone agreed too fast, which should have felt like relief but somehow only made things worse. We started packing with that jittery, ugly energy people get after no sleep and too much adrenaline. Tents came down badly. Stakes got yanked instead of pulled. Stuff sacks ripped. Someone dropped the coffee pot and nobody cared.

Then Jules said, “Where’s Becca?”

Everything stopped.

She had been right there a minute earlier, rolling her sleeping bag. I had seen her. We all had.

Then she was just… not.

No scream.

No running footsteps.

Nothing.

For one impossible second no one moved, because every single person in that clearing had the same thought at the same time:

If we start looking, do we count again first?

I’m not proud of that.

I don’t think any of us are.

Mara was the one who finally shouted her name.

No answer.

Theo started toward the tree line and Noah grabbed his arm so hard Theo nearly swung on him.

“No one goes alone,” Noah said.

“She’s not fucking alone.”

“Neither are you.”

Jules was crying now, but silently, almost angrily, tears just running down her face while she looked into the trees like she was trying to force them to give something back.

Then, from somewhere downhill toward the creek, Becca’s voice called:

“Guys?”

Every person there froze.

The word was right.

The voice was right.

The location was wrong.

It was too far.

Too fast.

Too calm.

“Becca?” Mara shouted before anyone could stop her.

Theo rounded on her. “What the fuck did we just say?”

Another pause.

Then Becca’s voice, closer now:

“I’m over here.”

We backed toward each other without discussing it. Not huddling, exactly, but the edges of us drawing inward the way herd animals do before they admit they’re a herd.

Noah said under his breath, “Count.”

Theo looked at him like he wanted to hit him.

“Count.”

Mara did it this time.

“Theo.”

“Here.”

“Noah.”

“Here.”

“Jules.”

A shaking breath. “Here.”

“Eli.”

“Here.”

“Mara.”

“Here.”

She stopped.

Five.

That was the first real catastrophic moment.

Not hearing the voice.

Not the footsteps.

The count.

Five people in the clearing and one voice downhill in the trees asking us to come get her.

“Becca,” Jules whispered.

Then Becca’s voice said, from behind us this time and much closer:

“Why are you counting?”

I have never in my life felt fear enter a group the way it did then.

Not like panic. Panic is loud.

This was something else.

We all turned too slowly.

There was nothing there.

Just tents half collapsed, gear on the ground, the dead fire ring, and the path into trees.

But the voice had been right there. Right between us and camp.

Jules made a sound I can only describe as the sound a body makes when it wants to run in six directions at once and can’t choose.

Then Theo said the smartest and stupidest thing anyone said that whole trip.

“Back to back.”

We did it.

Five adults in wet dirt, standing in a rough circle, packs half-on, breath tearing white in the cold, each of us facing a different piece of the woods while the creek hissed below us and Becca — or something wearing Becca’s voice — moved around in the trees.

What happened next is where the pattern finally showed itself.

Because while we stood there listening outward, Jules suddenly said, “Wait.”

Nobody moved.

“Wait,” she said again, and now her voice had gone thin in a different way. “If Becca’s out there… who slept first last night?”

Silence.

Then Mara said, “What?”

Jules was breathing too fast. “Think about it. The first night — after the voice — Noah seemed weird all day.”

“Excuse me?” Noah snapped.

“And last night Becca fell asleep first,” Jules pushed on, not even hearing him. “She was out before any of us. She said she was. She said she was so tired she couldn’t keep her eyes open.”

Theo said, “Jules, not now—”

“The voice outside only starts after someone falls asleep.”

That landed.

Not on all of us equally, maybe, but on me like a steel rod through the chest.

Because the sentence was true.

I knew it the second she said it.

The outside voice always came late enough that at least one person in each tent had already drifted.

The person who seemed off in the morning was always the first one down.

The thing outside made us listen outward while something else happened where we thought we were safest.

Inside.

Beside us.

I turned my head enough to look at Noah over my shoulder.

He looked back at me.

And there it was again — that tiny disgusting pause before recognition settled.

He saw it in my face too.

“Don’t,” he said.

I don’t know if he meant don’t look at me like that or don’t finish that thought.

Maybe both.

Then the voice in the woods laughed in Becca’s voice, and we all lost whatever was left of strategy.

Theo ran first.

Not far. Just toward the trail, pack bouncing half-zipped against his back. Mara swore and went after him. Jules grabbed one of the trekking poles like it could matter. Noah shoved me hard enough that I stumbled and shouted, “Move, Eli, move.”

The woods got loud then.

Not with a creature.

With us.

Boots slamming wet ground. Branches hitting jackets. People breathing too hard. Packs thudding. Someone crying. Someone saying don’t split up over and over while everyone did exactly that.

I remember flashes.

Theo ahead of us on the trail.

Mara slipping in mud and catching herself on a tree.

Jules almost running straight into a low branch because she kept looking behind her.

Noah so close to me I could hear him cursing under his breath.

And underneath all of it, voices.

Not one anymore.

Our voices.

From between trees.

From behind trunks.

From downhill near the creek.

Close, far, then close again.

“Left.”

“Wait.”

“This way.”

“Eli.”

“Jules, stop.”

“Theo, behind you.”

“Noah?”

Not screaming.

Not theatrical.

Helpful.

That was the worst part. They sounded like people trying to keep the group together.

We only made it back to the parking lot because Mara fell.

If she hadn’t gone down hard enough to take Theo with her, I think we would have split and that would have been the end of us.

Instead, everybody piled up in one swearing, tangled crash of packs and knees and breathless panic, and for one second all five of us were physically on top of each other in the mud.

Five.

That was enough to break the spell of the voices, maybe because once bodies are actually touching, you don’t need to count in your head anymore.

We got up.

We stumbled the rest of the way down the trail together.

We found the lot.

And Becca was standing beside the cars.

Dry.

Clean enough.

Alive.

She looked at us like we were out of our minds.

“Where the fuck did you all go?” she said.

Nobody answered.

Then, because the world is obscene in exactly the ways it knows will hurt most, she looked at me and added, “Why are you looking at me like that?”

I don’t remember the drive back very clearly.

No one wanted to ride alone, so we crammed into two cars and did not take our eyes off each other at gas stations. Theo drove too fast. Jules threw up once into a fast-food bag and then apologized like she’d committed a social error. Mara kept turning around from the passenger seat to look at us in the back and every time she did I had to fight the urge to ask her to stop, because being watched had become unbearable.

At some point Noah fell asleep against the window.

Or pretended to.

I watched his reflection in the glass instead of his face.

His breathing looked normal there too.

That’s the kind of detail that ruins you after.

We did not stay a group after that trip.

You probably guessed that already.

There was one dinner about three weeks later where we all sat in a Mexican place pretending we were trying to “process” what happened and ended up talking about parking validation and work schedules instead. Nobody wanted to be the first one to use words like voice or outside or wrong. Nobody wanted to ask Becca where she had been in those few minutes before we found her by the cars, or why she remembered walking back to camp alone while the rest of us remembered hearing her in the trees.

We all had versions.

That was maybe the worst aftereffect.

Not total contradiction. That would have been easier. Our stories overlapped just enough to keep us from dismissing the whole thing, but not enough to give anyone solid ground.

Theo said the first night he never actually heard the voice outside; he only heard Mara react to it. Mara insisted Theo had whispered to her in the dark during the second night while he appeared to be asleep. Jules said Becca’s voice outside their tent had been slightly wrong from the start, like it had learned her mouth but not her fear. Becca cried in the restaurant bathroom and later told me privately that she woke up on the second morning with mud inside one sock and had been too embarrassed to say anything because she thought she might have sleepwalked.

And Noah—

Noah remembered waking up on the second night because I had grabbed his arm hard enough to leave bruises.

I remembered grabbing something.

I don’t remember if it was him before or after the breathing changed.

That dinner was the last time all six of us were in one room.

Or the last time we thought we were.

Here’s the part I haven’t told anyone else, mostly because once you say it out loud, you are more or less asking to be locked in a room with soft voices and pamphlets.

A month after the trip, I got the photos back.

Not from a disposable camera. We’re not that quaint. Noah had brought an old digital camera because he said phone cameras make everything look like a fucking property listing. He uploaded the pictures to a shared folder and then forgot about them. I forgot about them too until I got drunk enough one night to start looking through them.

Most of them were exactly what you’d expect.

Theo looking irritated at a tent pole.

Jules flipping off the camera with a beer in one hand.

Becca laughing with her hair in her mouth because of the wind.

Noah doing his fake documentary pose with the trekking poles.

Me by the fire looking tired before anything had really started.

Then I found one taken just before dark on the first evening.

I don’t know who took it.

That’s not the disturbing part.

Everyone passed that camera around.

The photo was from near the edge of camp, angled back toward the tents while we were all still unpacking. You can see all six of us in it if you take your time.

Theo by the fire ring.

Mara crouched by the cooler.

Jules bent over tightening a guyline.

Becca in the background near the trail.

Noah looking over his shoulder toward whoever took the picture.

Me carrying firewood.

Six.

And behind us, just inside the tree line where the light had started to go blue, there was a seventh figure.

You can’t see the face.

I know that’s what you expect me to say, but it’s true.

It’s just a vertical shape between two trees. Too tall to be one of us. Too narrow in the shoulders. Standing where no one had been standing when I remembered that moment. Not blurry, exactly. More like the camera had focused on everything else and accepted it by accident.

I zoomed in until the pixels broke.

The shape was still there.

I sent the photo to Noah.

He replied ten minutes later:

delete that

No punctuation.

No joke.

No “what the fuck.”

Just that.

I asked him if he saw it too.

He never answered.

Two days after that, Jules called me at 1:17 in the morning.

I remember the time because I was already awake, lying in the dark listening to my upstairs neighbor drag furniture around like a psychopath, and I stared at the screen long enough to wonder if I should let it go to voicemail.

I picked up on the fourth ring.

She didn’t say hello.

She said, “Do you remember who fell asleep first the last night?”

My mouth went dry immediately.

“What?”

“The last night,” she said again, voice thin and shaking. “Do you remember who fell asleep first?”

“No.”

That was true.

Or as true as anything else about that trip.

She started crying, but quietly, like she was trying not to wake someone in the room with her.

“Becca says it was me,” she said. “Mara says it was Noah. Theo says it was you.”

I sat up in bed.

“What do you say?”

There was a long silence.

Then Jules said, “I don’t remember going to sleep at all.”

That’s the last thing she ever said to me.

Not because she died.

Because she blocked all of us after that.

Theo and Mara broke up by the end of the year. Noah moved to Oregon for a job and stopped answering questions about the trip. Becca got engaged to someone none of us had met and mailed her save-the-date cards without a note.

Sometimes I think that’s the natural life cycle of trauma. Not dramatic endings. Just people quietly moving away from the shape that once held them.

But there are a few things I can’t move away from.

I can’t camp anymore.

I can’t sleep in the same room as another person without listening too hard for the moment their breathing changes.

And every time somebody in a group says “count heads” as a joke, some part of me still goes cold.

The thing I keep coming back to is that second night in the tent with Noah.

Outside, his voice in the dark.

Inside, the breathing beside me going still for just a second before starting over.

That’s the detail that will probably rot in me forever.

Not because it proves anything.

Because it doesn’t.

If I could prove it, maybe I could put it somewhere. Madness, haunting, sleep deprivation, mass hysteria, some local predator smart enough to mimic human voices, whatever. People survive all kinds of horror if they can name it cleanly enough.

But I can’t.

Because there is one possibility worse than all the others, and it’s the one I can’t shake.

Sometimes, usually very late, I think about the first headcount.

Noah outside the tent doing the roll call.

All of us answering.

The creek below us.

The trees already going dark.

And I think about the photo taken before the first night, with the seventh shape inside the tree line.

Then I think about Jules asking who fell asleep first.

Then I think about the fact that on the drive home, Noah said I grabbed his arm hard enough to bruise him on the second night.

I remember grabbing something.

I do not remember reaching for him.

And when I try to force the memory, the only thing that comes back clearly is a sensation I have never been able to make peace with:

my own voice, from somewhere outside the tent, calling his name softly enough that he almost answered.

So that’s the part I leave you with.

Not a creature description.

Not folklore.

Not a warning about some patch of forest you can avoid.

Just this:

There were six of us on that trip.

Every night we counted six.

And even now, after all this time, I still don’t know if that number ever belonged to the same six people twice.
