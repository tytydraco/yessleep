Growing up, my mom used to always sing this one song to me to try and get me to sleep before she left when I was six. I have faded memories of her, images of curly hair, a soft smile, and a voice that could’ve made angels weep. 

“You are my sunshine” she would sing to me until I fell asleep, humming my name at the end of the chorus. She always spoke my name like it was fragile and sacred, I haven’t heard someone use my name since she left. Everyone just calls me Rori. My mom had left me a music box that played that song and it always brought me back to those times.

I’m in college now, I still bring the box with me everywhere, even though its keys are broken. Sometimes I swear it tries to play on its own. I don’t touch it anymore. Hearing it sound so distorted feels like listening to something that remembers me.

My roommate, Maddi, thinks i should get rid of it. It freaks her out, anytime it would play weird things would happen, it felt like something was always watching. She claims there was tapping on the window too, but it was probably just a branch. We live on the third floor, there’s a large tree next to the window that we’ve used to sneak out during curfew, It still freaks her out anyways.

The way to the dorm always freaked me out, it always feels like the dimly lit hallways are changing to isolate whatever unlucky soul decided to wander here. I never go through the building alone. After class, maddi and I were going down the hallway to the stairs when I heard it.

“Iris”

It wasn’t loud, it wasn’t clear, but I know what I heard. It stopped me dead in my tracks and Maddi turned back to me and asked “what’s wrong with you?”

“Did you hear that?”

She looked at me like I was losing it 

“Hear what?”

My mouth feels dry. “Someone just said my name.” I whispered

She frowns, “Rori?”

The voice comes again, closer this time

“Iris” it hissed, its taunting me.

“No, not that one.” I say. She laughs nervously “you’re freaking me out Rori”

I hear a tap on the window, I start to turn to look but someone opened a door down the hallway and walked out, it snapped me out of whatever trance I was in.

I didn’t hear my name again until later that night.

I was sitting at my desk, highlighter uncapped, notes spread out in front of me as I tried to focus on finals. The wind outside was loud enough to be distracting. The tree’s branches scraped against the building every so often, a dull, hollow sound I barely registered anymore.

Then there was a knock on the window.

I didn’t look up. I told myself it was the tree, just like always. It had been windy all week. Still, my grip tightened around the pen in my hand.

The knock came again. Slower this time. More deliberate.

And then I heard it.

“Iris.”

The voice was quiet, almost lost beneath the sound of the wind. If I hadn’t been listening for it, I might’ve missed it entirely. My heart stuttered in my chest.

“Iris,” it said again, closer now. “Let me in.”

I didn’t move. I couldn’t. The words pressed in on me, heavy and familiar in a way that made my stomach twist.

“Iris,” the voice whispered, softer than before. “Open the window.”

It sounded like my mom.

Not like a recording. Not like memory. Like she was standing just outside, keeping her voice low so she wouldn’t wake anyone else.

I don’t remember falling asleep.

I remember the knocking stopping.

The next thing I knew, someone was pounding on the door.

I jolted upright, my heart racing, the room dark except for the glow of Maddi’s lamp. She was already out of bed, pulling on a sweatshirt as she crossed the room.

“I’ve got it,” she muttered, rubbing sleep from her eyes.

I stayed frozen on my bed, listening. The hallway outside was quiet. Too quiet.

Maddi opened the door.

I couldn’t see who was there at first, just a glimpse of curly hair, pale in the low light, and the edge of a familiar silhouette.

Then the woman stepped forward.

She had my mother’s hair. Curly, just like I remembered, though streaked with gray now. Her smile was soft and careful, the kind that used to mean everything was going to be okay.

My heart dropped into my stomach.

It had been almost fourteen years since she left.

Her eyes were duller than I remembered. Not empty, just wrong, like they didn’t quite settle on anything when she looked at me.

But she smiled wider when she saw me.

“Iris,” she said.

My first instinct was to run to her, but Maddi stepped in between us, I don’t think she even processed that she did that.

For some reason, all she would do was stare at the music box she gave me, like it was luring her away somehow.

“Do you remember it? You gave it to me.” I said as I reached for it, she nodded her head and slowly walked towards me

I go to play the broken melody, it kind of creeped me out, seeing her stare down the box like she wasn’t sure of its existence.Her smile was slightly crooked, and it was always plastered on her face. It was wrong. 

“You need to leave” Maddi said flatly.

Mom looks at her and says “Maddi.”

Maddi didn’t move.

Not even when I said her name.

Her shoulders went stiff, like her body had locked around the sound of it. The woman tilted her head, studying her the way someone might study an insect they weren’t sure how to handle.

“Maddi,” she said again, softer. Testing it.

“How do you..”Maddi started, then stopped. She swallowed hard. “You need to leave. Now.”

The woman’s smile didn’t change. It didn’t flicker or falter the way a real one would. It just stayed there, stretched too neatly across her face.

She took a step forward.

The music box began to play.

I hadn’t touched it.
