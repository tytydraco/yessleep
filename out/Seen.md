# SEEN

Nobody thinks they’re the villain in the screenshot. That’s the first rule of surviving online. You scroll. You react. You add a comment. You move on. The feed refreshes. The story expires. The moment dissolves.

It doesn’t feel permanent when your thumb is doing the work.

That’s what Isla used to think, anyway.

Now she’s nineteen, halfway through her first term at university, lying on her narrow hall room’s bed while fairy lights blink lazily above her desk. Her phone is fifteen inches from her face. It’s nearly always fifteen inches from her face.

Maya is on the floor beside her, chewing strawberry gum and staring at her own screen.

“You haven’t downloaded it?” Maya says.

“No.”

“Everyone has.”

“That’s what everyone says about everything.”

Maya rolls onto her back. “It’s not social media.”

“Everything is social media.”

“It’s anonymous.”

“Then what’s the point?”

Maya grins. “That *is* the point.”

She holds up her phone. On the screen is a black background with a single white word in the centre:

SEEN.

No logo. No bright colours. Just the word. Minimal. Serious. Important.

“So what am I supposed to do?” Isla asks.

“You confess something.”

“Ha! Who says I’ve got anything to confess?”

“You’re kidding, right?”

“Shut up Maya.”

Maya smiles. “I bet a lot of people could benefit from hearing you confess.”

“No one wants to hear me confess.”

“They do though… you just don’t know it.”

“It’s complete bullshit.”

Maya sits up and presses record.

She doesn’t hesitate.

“I sometimes pretend I don’t see messages from people because I don’t have the energy to reply,” she says into her phone. “Even when I do.”

She presses stop.

The screen pulses once.

Then it says:

Delivered.

“That’s your confession?” Isla laughs.

Maya smiles. “It’s a start!”

“No one’s gonna benefit from hearing that.”

Maya shrugs. “It’s emotional redistribution.”

“Anyway everybody ghosts.”

Maya grins wider. “Download it.”

Isla doesn’t. Not that night. A couple of days pass.

It’s the weekend, and both girls have been drinking in the union bar. Maya is asleep and the corridor outside their room is loud with Friday laughter and the bass of someone’s speaker bleeding through the walls. 

Isla finds herself in the App Store.

SEEN.

4.9 stars.

Reviews say things like:

“It matched me with exactly what I deserved.”

“I can’t explain how it knew.”

“Be careful what you upload.”

That last one makes her pause. *Be careful what you upload.*

Isla smirks, but only for a split-second.

She presses download.

The app takes a minute to download. It opens straight to a black screen.

No profile.

No username.

No sign-up.

Just a message:

*Record your confession*. It’s a courier font. It’s like the app has been coded and pushed to the app stores in a very rough state. Unfinished. A beta mode.

Isla can see her own reflection in the dark screen. She laughs under her breath.

“Fine,” she whispers.

She presses record. A thin white line starts moving vertically from the bottom left of the screen. It’s a timer. She probably has about a minute to say whatever she’s going to say.

At first, she can’t think of anything to say. Literally, her mind is blank. Empty. Her life is normal. *Nor-mal.* Maybe a little boring. She’s a pretty decent person. She isn’t cruel to anyone. She’s not dramatic. She works quite hard. She’s nice to boys. She’s doing … *okay.*

Isla is not one of those people who ruin lives.

She swallows. The line is about halfway up the screen now.

Suddenly, she says, “I think I’m kinder in my head than I am in real life.” It feels to her like the words have come out of nowhere.

Is that too vague? The words roll around in her head. She’s not even sure they make sense to her, let alone a stranger. Is she trying to sound clever? Is the tone wrong? Arrogant?

She thinks about deleting the comment. But there’s no delete button. The line has reached the top left corner.

The screen pulses.

Delivered.

A small flicker runs through her chest. Not fear. A tiny rush of adrenaline.

She locks her phone and places it face down on her bedside table.

Isla doesn’t care. This is stupid. Stupid app.

The next day the message and the app are mostly forgotten.

It’s Sunday – no lectures. Isla meets Chloe for coffee and a walk. Chloe is Chinese – an economics major. Chloe has just split up with her Chinese boyfriend. Isla had met him once and couldn’t understand what Chloe saw in him. She tries to console her friend as best she can.

After the walk Isla’s phone starts pinging. A stupid group chat argument about missing yoghurts from the halls fridge. Isla never left food in there. The boys always eat it. 

At midday there is an unannounced fire alarm. Which was funny because a lot of people were still in pyjamas. 

Isla spends a couple of hours that afternoon reading. Nothing to do with her university work. She’d started reading Carson McCuller’s The Heart is a Lonely Hunter, an enthralling book about a deaf man called John Singer who becomes a confidante to four social misfits. McCuller was twenty-three when she wrote the book, her first. It seems staggering to Isla that this could be possible. Just over half-way through and Isla is already pretty sure this is the best book she has ever read. 

By evening, Isla is walking back from the library on her own. The sky is the colour of old metal. Her headphones are in. Echo and the Bunnymen’s Seven Seas. Always such a joyous song. Isla’s thoughts are shallow.

Her phone buzzes. Just once.

She glances down.

*Unknown notification.*

SEEN

Her stomach dips slightly.

She opens it while still walking.

The screen is black again.

But now there’s a single white triangle. Not the usual play button. This one points downwards. 

*Okay*, she thinks to herself.

She presses play. Echo and the Bunnymen stops.

A girl’s voice fills her headphones. Soft. Young. Controlled.

“I kept quiet because I didn’t want to be the problem,” the voice says. “I just didn’t tell anyone.”

Isla’s steps slow.

The voice continues.



“I told myself that meant I wasn’t responsible. But they really hurt me. People I trusted.”

A flicker of something sharp moves through Isla’s ribs. A sudden warmth in her blood.

This could be anyone, she tells herself. It doesn’t sound like ai. Isla is no expert, but this sounds like a real person. It’s fuzzy. There is interference. It is clear this was deliberately recorded by this girl, not a recording of someone who didn’t know they were being recorded. 

“I laughed when they laughed,” the voice says. “Until I couldn’t laugh anymore.”

Isla stops walking completely now.

There are boys she recognises from lectures walking past her now, as she stands there awkwardly.

The voice continues.

“I still cry.”

For the first time since the message started, Isla feels uncomfortable. Like a voyeur. Or more like an eavesdropper, listening to a confession. A confession she has no part in.

“It only took one comment,” the girl says. “One person to tell another and then the whole world knows.”

The pavement tilts. Noise from the street fades under the rush of blood in Isla’s ears.

This doesn’t mean anything. Lots of people—

The voice finishes the sentence for her.

“I liked the comment that said I deserved it.”

The world narrows.

Her thumb hovers over the screen.

She can’t breathe properly.

That was—

That was specific.

*Too* specific.

She forces herself to look at the app interface.

No username.

No profile.

No date.

Just the downward-pointing white triangle.

She pulls out one headphone.

The real world crashes back in — distant music from someone’s bedroom, a skateboard rattling past.

She presses play again.

“Fuck her,” the girl declares.

The cadence is different now that she’s listening for it.

Measured. But with such controlled rage. The voice pauses. Then:

“I remember her name.”

Isla’s heart slams once, hard.

The next word crackles slightly in her headphones. Static. Then—

“Isobel.”

The audio cuts to silence. The screen goes black. And beneath it, a new line appears.

*Your confession has been successfully delivered.*

Two years ago, when Isla was seventeen and still in school, a girl in her year called Elizabeth Salmon had sent her best friend a photograph. It was a private photograph sent from one girl to another. Sent to a girl who was trusted. A girl called Isobel. It was never meant to go public. But it had. And the repercussions had come fast. 

Elizabeth was a popular girl at school. Although by no means a scholar, she was academic and good at sport, particularly hockey. She was a very pretty girl, but the boys didn’t notice. Sadly for Elizabeth, in her teens, she’d experienced terrible acne and was now dealing with the scars. No one knew why Elisabeth sent that photograph. And no one ever asked Isobel why she shared it. 

Before long, that photograph had been sent around the school. Even younger kids without phones had seen it. And kids had told their parents. Parents informed teachers. Soon it was a problem for the whole school.

It wasn’t Isla that shared the photograph. It was shared with a large WhatsApp group in our year. But Isla had been the first person to interact with it. 

Elizabeth left the school soon after. Rumours circulated that she’d had a breakdown and moved away, but those rumours were never substantiated and Elizabeth’s parents were still seen locally, although not often.

It was a big thing until it wasn’t anymore. Nobody really knew what happened, apart from maybe some of the teachers, but they never spoke of it. 

The following week after that first confession, Isla starts receiving daily confessions. Each one is more specific than the last. 

“It only took one comment.”

“Screenshots last longer than apologies.”

“People that you think are genuine are liars. The friends you have that you trust are faking.”

“When I left school, nobody asked why.”

Isla has started checking the app obsessively. On Thursday evening, Isla spends hours in front of her computer checking online forums. There is speculation that SEEN has cross-referenced school records, scraped deleted posts and maybe even reconstructed digital footprints. Where there might have been comfort in knowing that others like Isla had used and been affected by the app, she still felt alone. The guilt and shame of talking to Maya, or anyone, about this whole incident was too great. 

Isla knew that all she’d done was send one stupid emoji to a group. But she also knew that she had been the first to do so, and in doing that had maybe set a tone. Had she sent a different kind of emoji, maybe the outcome would have been different. 

Many people on Reddit were speculating that Seen didn’t just match you randomly, but pairs you with someone from the *other side* of your behaviour. Like a boy with a reputation of being mean to girlfriends, being connected with a girl who has been on the receiving end of this kind of behaviour. Confessor to consequence. 

Isla doesn’t open the app for three days. She tells herself that means she’s winning. If she doesn’t look, it can’t escalate. If she doesn’t listen, it can’t know more.

But the silence is worse.

Every notification buzz makes her chest tighten. Every time someone says her name in a lecture, she flinches before she can stop herself.

On the fourth night, she’s alone in her room. Maya’s out. The corridor is quiet. The fairy lights are off.

Her phone is on her desk. Face up. Waiting.



She opens SEEN.

No greeting. No feed. Just the black screen. Just the white words: *Record your confession.*

Her reflection floats faintly over the glass. She looks normal. Tired, maybe. A little pale. But not like a monster.

She presses play on the most recent message she hasn’t listened to. A girl’s voice. Calm. Measured.

“To begin with I saw the funny side,” the voice says. “I saw it with their eyes. In a certain light it *was* funny.”

Isla stops the audio. She doesn’t need the rest.

It *was* funny.

Those are the words that stick.

She closes her eyes. The memory doesn’t come as a full scene. It comes in flashes.

The photograph had arrived on her phone.

It *was* funny.

She remembers the feeling more than the picture.

The rush. The need to be the first to respond. Of being part of a group. To be first. *First.* An instigator. The safety of laughing with everyone else. That oh-so-brief second before she’d clicked the emoji. The stupid fucking emoji. The very brief nausea before others in the group responded.

That second is the only one that matters. Isla knew she hadn’t made a decision to send that emoji. It was done on impulse. It wasn’t creative. It wasn’t vicious. But on reflection, it *was* stupid.

She opens SEEN again.

The black screen doesn’t change.

*Record your confession.*

The vague one wasn’t enough. She can see that now.

“I think I’m kinder than I am” was camouflage. It was poetry. It was performance.

The app didn’t respond to what she said.

It responded to what she didn’t.

She sits down on the edge of her bed. Her hands are steady when she presses record.

For a moment, she can’t speak. The silence feels like it’s being measured.

“My name is Isla Grant,” she says. Her own name sounds strange out loud. She swallows. “Two years ago, when that photo was shared… I was the first to reply.”

Her throat tightens but she keeps going.

“I didn’t send it. I didn’t take it. I didn’t post it.”

She pauses.

“But I replied to the group with an emoji. A laughing emoji.”

Her chest rises, falls.

There it is. Plain. Ugly. Undramatic. The room feels smaller. The air heavier.

“I think about it sometimes,” she says, softer. “Not enough. But sometimes.”

A long pause.

Then, finally:

“And I’m really sorry I did that.”

There is still time to stop and delete the record. Still time to make it vague again. Still time to pretend she didn’t mean all of that.

But the white bar reaches the top of the screen. The app pulses once.

Delivered.

No sound. No animation. But delivered.

She waits.

Nothing happens.

She exhales slowly, almost laughing at herself.

Maybe that’s it. Maybe confession really is just that simple. You say the thing. It leaves you. You sleep.

Her phone vibrates in her hand.

A new notification.

From SEEN.

She looks down.

# Recipient is typing.

Her stomach drops.

There is no username.

No profile picture.

Just the three pulsing dots.

And they don’t stop.
