Hi, my name is Charlie and as the title says I’m a history PhD student. I received a grant to spend a month in Argentina doing archival work for a paper I’m writing on the history of Antarctic exploration. I’ve always been fascinated by the history of explorers, and researching people going to the “final frontier” of Antarctica has always fascinated me. It’s been especially exciting for me to get the opportunity to work in these archives as I get to see and use a lot of primary sources that have potentially never been thoroughly examined or used in any research context.

However, I’ve found a document recently that doesn’t make any sense. This morning was my fourth day in the archive, and after spending several hours reading old supply logbooks and shipping charts, I dropped a folder I was putting back behind the shelf. Since no one else was near me (the archives I’m working in are in a subbasement), I pulled the heavy shelf out to retrieve the folder, but found something else back there. The floor behind the shelf was entirely covered in a thick layer of dust, but as I picked up the dropped folder and its contents I saw a small, black notebook.

I work with a lot of old paper documents, but the condition of this thing was maybe the worst I’ve ever seen. The paper was weathered and yellowed, the cover torn half off. Some pages are badly ripped, and other pages seem to be missing entirely.  I spent the rest of the day reading through it and I don’t know how I should feel. Further research is obviously needed to figure out if this thing and the events discussed in it are real, but the physical state of this notebook alone indicates to me that it is likely as old as it’s dated.

I know I’ll need to turn this over to the archivists eventually, but selfishly I hid it in the same place that I found it so I could continue reading it tomorrow. If this is real, which I have a sinking feeling it somehow is, it blows the lid off of a lot of what we think we know about history, geography, and potentially biology. I wish I had any further context on this, but as somewhat of an expert in the field, all I can tell you is that this shouldn’t exist.

There is no record I know of outside of this notebook of the people or the ship discussed within it, and the contents of it seem impossible for a variety of reasons. If I turn this over now, due to the condition alone they’ll take it somewhere to restore it, and once they look inside I’m sure teams of experts will have their turns analyzing it. The way I see it, I found it, so I deserve to look through it before all the big wigs do. I got through about a quarter of the notebook today before the archive closed, but reading and transcribing the aged cursive and translating the Spanish into English is going excruciatingly slow. Anyways, I figured I’d post what I have on here because I figure it’ll be a good way to get my thoughts out and see what people think without giving away where exactly I am.

Here’s what I have so far, and let me know what you think because I really don’t know how to feel.

July 11th/1815

*"The Laplander is dead. The doctor said his lungs had filled with fluid overnight, his final moments spent drowning in his own blood. I have found myself increasingly unaffected by death. As I watched those first bodies float in the frigid waters two weeks past, I felt horror and despair I had never before known. Now, all I found myself thinking of when I heard of the death of the Laplander is the bitter irony of dying in such a fashion. The whales we kill die the same way. Drowning in blood. Regurgitating bits of tentacles and other spoils of hard won battles deep below the surface in a world man should know nothing of. I remember my grandmother's disappointment in the life I had chosen. She had told me that He did not give man gills because He did not make us to be creatures of the sea. She was wiser than I had ever given her credit for."*

July 13th/1815

*"I fear it won’t be much longer until disease and frostbite will take care of us each and every one. The Fueguens that the captain had us take some weeks ago now will likely be next. This disturbs me more than the death of the Laplander, whom I had cared for very little. To see these Fueguens, people accustomed for generations to the frost, freezing to death slowly in the hold has affected me, and I suspect most of the others, in a way we would rather not admit. I marked this day as the 13th of the month, but I am not confident that that is correct. We have sailed below the sun, where the day and night blend together into a mist of constant gray."*

*"I estimate it has been two months since the Ebony left the port of Buenos Ayres, and three weeks since we left Tierra del Fuego. This is my third whaling expedition, and while this one had felt different from the beginning it wasn’t until we had taken those Fueguens that I felt something was truly wrong about this voyage. We found the group of five Fueguen men hunting seals on the frozen beach, practically naked. They warm their bodies by coating themselves in seal fat, the smell of which was putrid to my nose. The captain ordered us to bring them on board to serve as foremast hands, as several men had backed out of the expedition shortly before leaving. How I envy them. One Fueguen had died in the struggle to bring them onboard. I envy him too. I have never known cold like this. In this godforsaken part of the world, there is no fat to coat the body."* 

*"The Fueguens are simple, peaceful people. I am only a cook, and it is not my place to cast judgement upon the captain's decision to enslave them, but I suspect the decision was not his but the Chilean’s. I only caught a glimpse of him once, as we were boarding. He was tall, his hair unkempt and whipping wildly in the wind. But I hear his voice often. My cot in the hold presses up against the captain's cabin, where the Chilean beds. He whispers things I cannot make out to the Captain, deep into the night. His voice is low, and sounds aged. It reminds of the songs sung by the whales."*

July 14th/ 1815

*"The Captain has stopped pretending like we are anything other than completely and utterly lost. It was evident to the rest of us weeks ago, after the storm. Two days after enslaving the Fueguens, one of the Basques spotted our first whale as we sailed east towards the Malvinas. I watched for the rest of the afternoon from the deck as we followed the whaleboats. Antonio, a young man who had been on my previous expedition, was the first to harpoon the beast. It put up a good fight, dragging the whaleboats behind it for hours, but eventually it died in the same manner the Laplander later would."*

*"In the excitement, we had paid little attention to the clouds, but by the time we latched the carrion to the side of the ship it was evident a storm was coming. This was the first time I had seen a dead sperm whale, and I remember being surprised at how human its dead eyes looked. I was equally surprised by the appearance of the mangled fins. I could see bones underneath, and they look not dissimilar to the hand bones of a man. We hunkered below decks, the rest of that night as the winds shrieked outside. The Basque who had spotted the whale said it sounded like the screams of men fell overboard."*

*"Shortly past midnight, the winds and rain finally subsided, and when we stepped out to assess the damage I felt as if we had entered some new world. The edges of the sky were vivid colors of green, pink and orange. One of the Russians compared it to Aurora Borealis he had seen in the Arctic seas. We had not had time to process the Spermicitti or take any meat from the carcass, and the sharks had already gotten to most of the good meat the poor creature carried."*

*"I looked down into that eye, surrounded by mangled flesh and oozing yellow liquid, then looked up into the sky, and saw what looked like that same dead eye, enlarged a thousandfold directly above us. The storm had not passed, we were only in the center of it. While a storm was not surprising, a typhoon in the South Atlantic, at this time of year, should not have been possible. Within half an hour, it was back upon us, raging for days straight as we prayed in the hold. I drifted in and out of sleep to winds that sounded like dead sailors shrieking, and through the wall the deep singsong of the Chilean. We emerged onto the deck again, two and a half weeks ago, to find the head mast destroyed, the whale carcass gone, two whaleboats absent, and several inches of snow covering the Ebony. Small chunks of ice floated by us on eerily calm waters, with no sign of birds nor any other life. The sky was completely and utterly gray, and I felt that if we were still on the earth, it was a part of the world that God was ignorant of. We were able to somewhat repair the mast, but the canvas was torn badly."*

*"We had little control over what direction we went in, but that mattered little as our current location was entirely unknown and the compass spun wildly. A Russian said he had heard of this before, and it was not uncommon if we were near some vast iron deposits, but this made little sense to me as we were not near any land and whatever ore was below us would have been leagues beneath. The Captain kept us busy for what I believe was two days and nights, before the first deaths but I am not sure as the sky maintained a steely gray appearance at all times. During this time, we did not see a single bird, seal, or any sign of life other than us whatsoever. We were already low on coal to burn as we had planned on restocking in the Malvinas around this time, and the cold was bitter, so when we first saw that distant green light no one questioned the Captain's decision to send a whaling boat to investigate it."*

*"A Basque in the Crows nest first spotted it, far in the distance. The light was cool, pulsating, a sickly looking and iridescent shade of green. We first believed it be St. Elmo’s fire, but as we got closer it appeared to be on top of an ice sheet, at least a half league starboard. It looked almost like some ghostly campfire, which was of course an impossibility in this dead and frozen ocean. Being unable to navigate the Ebony, we watched as the men, a group of Porteños like myself, rowed towards the light as we drifted aimlessly onwards. Not long after they had departed, a wind picked up for the first time since that cursed storm, and we stood helpless on the deck as a mist began to trickle in, obscuring their silhouettes as they gained on that light."*

*"Eventually, both the Ebony and the whaleboat were consumed by thick fog, snuffing out the light in the distance. Antonio and I climbed up the mast with the strongest lanterns on board the Ebony to guide the men back, but as we reached the top I heard what sounded like that wind again. This time, it was no superstition. I could hear their screams echoing across the ice, sounding like it was coming from all directions at once. The mist cleared several hours later, and to our horror we spotted the Whaleboat heading in a perfect line towards the Ebony, absent of any men. That night, or what we believed to be night, I drifted off to the Chilean’s melody only to be awoken by crying out above deck."*

*"We all rushed out to see the bodies of those four Porteños floating several feet out from the Ebony, their clothes torn and limbs bent at odd angles. The way they bobbed and turned about in the water deceived me, or perhaps I simply refused to believe my eyes, because I at first believed them to be a school of porpoises. Within minutes, those mutilated bodies sank beneath the gentle waves. We were too terrified to retrieve them, and the Captain gave no order to do so. In my state of shock, horror, and bitter cold, I found myself thinking of how comforting it would feel to return to my bed. I could sleep there forever, separate from this; below the sun, among the lapping waves, encompassed in the cooing whalesong-voice of the Chilean."*
