I was fifteen when I lost my left leg. This is my account.

It was warmer back where I had been living. Summer was kicking into full effect. I'd just finished my freshman year of high school and was looking for any way to occupy my time.

Locally, there was a trend going around. Urbexing. Exploring abandoned places. I can't remember why it was so popular that year, but I guess living near so many decrepit structures had something to do with it.

I lived in a town just south of Houston. After the recession in 08’, me and my mother found our way into the ever expanding suburban sprawl that became our home. 

Once I moved, it didn't take long to realize there was nothing to do. The town felt like a concrete coffin. Bland. Empty. Claustrophobic in the kind of way where even the wide roads and open parking lots made you feel boxed in.

That's what pushed me to explore the abandoned buildings nearby.

At first, it was just something to do. Then it became something I needed. Not just an escape, but a thrill. I felt like Indiana Jones wandering through forgotten ruins, searching for treasure no one else had touched.

And I got good at it. Over time, the local buildings started to feel familiar. I learned the routines, figured out how to slip in and out unnoticed, and knew exactly what signs to look for.

The day was June 18, 2013. A Tuesday.

I woke up to birds chirping outside my window. A distinct three-part coo from a mourning dove. Some people say the sound is nostalgic. I always found it annoying. Made it hard to sleep in.

Nothing was planned for the day. No schedule to follow, no responsibilities waiting. That's how I liked to keep my time: wasteful. To me, it meant life was okay.

I walked out of my room, down the stairs, and into the kitchen. No one else was awake yet, so I moved quietly. I wanted to savor that slice of silence for as long as God would let me.

I checked my phone. No notifications.

"Damn," I thought. "Guess I wasn't popular today."

I had to make something happen.

I got ready quickly and packed my usual everyday carry. When I didn't have a plan, I'd just ride my bike around until some random side quest presented itself. I needed to be ready for anything.

I packed a bottle of water, a flashlight, a Slim Jim, a manga I was reading, and a camera. That was enough.

Before leaving, I scribbled a note to my mom, letting her know I was out riding around town. I left it on the kitchen counter, opened the front door, and stepped outside.

That's how the day started.

By the end of it, I'd be fighting for my life.

A couple hours had passed, and I was biking along some backroads.

I was well south of the neighborhoods by then, deep in the thicker, less developed areas. The humidity was brutal. It clung to my skin and soaked through my shirt. The air felt heavy in my lungs, like it was trying to drown me from the inside out.

It didn't just feel like a jungle, it looked like one too. The brush was thick, wild, and green in a way that seemed almost unnatural. Trees pressed in from both sides, their branches stretching like arms across the narrow road. The concrete path was the only manageable trail to follow.

I was about twenty to thirty minutes from home.

Eventually, I spotted a driveway. Cracked and half-buried by roots and dirt, but still intact enough to recognize. It cut through the wall of brush like a hidden artery.

Curiosity won. I took the path.

First mistake.

The asphalt was cracked with age, weeds and grass springing up through the breaks. Waist-high grass had crept up the sides, slowly swallowing what remained of the infrastructure. On both sides, the drainage ditches brimmed with stagnant swamp water. Bugs hovered in dense clouds, and flies buzzed around my head like a halo of decay. The entire scene felt like something out of Jurassic Park. Like I'd stumbled into the lost world. A part of my mind whispered that dinosaurs were going to jump out and eat me.

Ahead, a rusted chain-link fence stretched across the path, sagging in places but still standing. A small security booth sat lopsided next to the gate, its windows fogged with mildew. The gate itself was ajar. Only slightly, but enough to make me pause. It didn't look forced open. It looked like someone had walked through and never bothered to close it behind them.

Everything else screamed abandonment. Rust clung to the metal like a second skin. Among the clutter of weather-worn warnings and faded postings, one sign stood out: large, white and green, mounted proudly on the fence. It read: *Riverside Processing Inc.*

That was the first I noticed, but not the last. As I rode closer, more signs came into view. OSHA warnings. Chemical hazard postings. Danger: Keep Out. Risk of Toxic Exposure. Each one more unsettling than the last.

I pulled up to the gate, stepped off my bike, and laid it down carefully next to the booth. The heat pressed down on me. The air was thick and still. Two things were immediately clear: this place was dangerous, potentially fatal, and this place was abandoned.

At the time, the second fact felt far more important.

I slipped through the gate, past rusted chain-link. The chances of finding something actually interesting today weren't high. The chances of stumbling on a brand new area, previously unexplored? Even lower. I was thrilled. Usually, urbexing meant biking along the train tracks until something old came into view. The only problem was that everything along the line had already been picked over. The community of bored teens with too much time and a love for abandoned buildings had definitely grown this past year. Finding a spot like this was like striking hidden treasure.

I followed the cracked entrance road until it opened into a half-overgrown clearing; Probably an old parking lot. Faded yellow lines stretched across the ground like scars, barely visible beneath sun-bleached pavement and weeds that had forced their way through the cracks. This place was way bigger than I'd expected.

Scanning the area, I spotted what had to be the main building. It stood tall, maybe two stories, with that old pebble-studded concrete straight out of the seventies. The windows were shattered, the front doors hung open like broken teeth. Spray-painted tags and crude graffiti covered parts of the outer walls. That was a good sign. If someone had tagged it, the place was truly abandoned. No one was guarding it.

Still, before going in, I needed to prepare. When you go urbexing, you're supposed to bring at least one or two other people. That way, if some homeless crackhead jumps out, you have a fighting chance. I pulled out my phone and checked for service.

Nothing. No bars. My spotty 3G barely worked in town, so out here in the dense brush it was basically useless. That should have been my cue to turn around and grab someone. You never go alone.

But alone is how I went that day.

The call of the unknown whispered sweet promises in my head. Like something outside myself had taken over, I felt pulled—no, guided—toward the building.

Two broken glass doors marked the entrance to the admin building of Riverside Processing Inc. Careful not to impale myself on the jagged shards still clinging to the frame, I ducked under the fractured remains and stepped into the reception lobby.

It smelled rotten. The air was thick with mold; A damp, sour scent that clung to everything. Green-black fuzz had spread across the walls, crept onto the desks, even crawled across the sagging paperwork. Old papers were scattered everywhere, like someone had tried to flee in a hurry but never made it out. I stood still for a moment, stunned. A place like this would've been trashed completely if more people knew about it. Despite its decay, it looked... untouched. Time had worn it down, sure, but human hands hadn't finished the job. It felt like I'd found something important.

Surprisingly, the temperature inside was manageable. The thick concrete walls must have blocked out the brutal humidity outside. I felt good. Confident. Like I was meant to be here.

I pulled out my digital camera and hit record.

"Today is June 18, 2013," I muttered into the mic, trying to sound casual. "I'm here at this old chemical factory. Check this out, bro…"

The lens panned across dusty desks, shattered monitors, and faded posters from the 90s warning about workplace safety. Everything looked frozen in time, just waiting to be found.

I strolled past the outdated computers, eyes scanning everything. Down the hall, a staircase caught my attention. It looked sturdy enough. The steps weren't caved in or warped from water damage. I gave it a once-over and decided it was safe to climb.

I continued talking to the camera as I went. "This whole place is crazy. Kind of reminds me of the abandoned mansion off—"

"Damn!"

I stopped mid-sentence. My foot hovered over a sudden break in the floor. A massive section of the second level had collapsed inward, creating a jagged pit that cut off the hallway ahead. I hadn't even noticed it. One more step and I would've dropped straight onto the wreckage below.

I said nothing, breath heavy and heart pounding.

I often found myself in dangerous areas, but catching myself one step from imminent doom? That wasn't common. I retraced my steps, walking backward down the stairs until I was back on solid ground. The near miss had rattled me more than I expected. I paused to collect myself, letting my breath steady in the stale air.

Then, just at the edge of my vision, something caught my attention: a single metal door, tucked slightly to the left. It hadn't been visible before. The deeper I went into the complex, the more the daylight faded, so I pulled out my flashlight and flicked it on.

The rusted door resisted at first, groaning under the pressure of my pushes, then finally gave way with a jarring screech after a harder shove.

I stepped through.

My hunch was right. This was the main processing warehouse.

The ceiling stretched high above me, easily tall enough to house industrial machinery. Lining the walls were rows of massive steel storage tanks, each with pipes that snaked into the walls or ceiling, all corroded with age and neglect. The floor beneath me was bare concrete, slick with dark stains from old chemical spills. My shoes made a faint sticking noise with each step forward. Those OSHA signs from the gate started flashing through my mind again, little warning flares trying to scream over the calm. But I dismissed them. I told myself it was fine. It was always fine.

High above me, rusted catwalks stretched along the upper walls like skeletal bridges, drooping under their own weight and casting long shadows across the floor.

This was perfect. A nearly untouched chemical facility.

*When I get back, I need to do some research on what this place used to be*, I thought.

I swept the flashlight across the space, tracing the forgotten machinery, the grime-glazed labels, the slow drip of moisture from somewhere unseen. It was silent. Completely silent. So silent it felt unnatural, like sound had been outlawed here. Not even the wind was present. No birds. No insects. Just stillness. A time capsule. Like I had stepped into something that wasn't meant to be found.

***Click.***

A sharp sound echoed from somewhere deep in the warehouse.

I froze.

It wasn't loud. More like a *pop* or a subtle *crack*, but distinct enough to reverberate off the metal walls.

Every part of my body tensed.

I wasn't the bravest kid, but I didn't usually scare this easily. Still, that noise… it didn't belong. The silence had comforted me, lulled me into a false sense of control. That single sound shattered it.

It didn't feel right.

No, it felt *very* wrong.

The warehouse around me suddenly felt alien. The pipes twisting along the walls, the wires snaking down from above, the looming tanks bulging from the ground. They all looked like the innards of something alive. Like I was standing inside the belly of a creature that hadn't yet realized it had been breached.

I wasn't supposed to be here.

I stood frozen for what felt like forever. Didn't even dare to breathe. My chest stayed tight, like something in the air itself was waiting to react to me. Nothing moved. Nothing called back. The warehouse held its breath with me.

Whatever confidence I'd had evaporated like water on pavement under the summer sun.

The silence returned; thick, heavy, smothering. Like a curtain had been pulled over the scene again. I raised the flashlight, slowly scanning the cavernous space.

The beam cut through the dusty air, revealing columns of light spilling in from the high ventilation slats above. Faint but sharp; thin blades of sun slicing into the gloom. The air was thick with dust that glittered as it floated, disturbed by my breath and movement. Besides those faint sunbeams, the rest of the space drowned in dimness.

It wasn't pitch black. Not yet. But shadows owned this place. The kind of shadows that felt like they watched you back.

I gripped the flashlight tighter. My fingers were slick with sweat. I started moving forward.

That was my second mistake.

Multiple hallways stretched past the warehouse, each one leading deeper into the structure. If I couldn't remember which path I took, finding my way out again would be hell. I chose to follow a red pipe protruding from a large tank. It ran along the wall beside a wide walkway, and I used it like a lifeline.

The farther I walked, the dimmer it got. The natural sunlight slowly disappeared behind me, swallowed by the concrete walls. It felt like I was sinking deeper into a sarcophagus. A dead place.

I don't remember the exact route I took, but the hallway eventually opened into a loading dock. It was massive and echoing, lined with at least a dozen garage doors. Maybe more. Thin rays of daylight slipped through cracks in the rusted metal, but they barely lit the place. I swept my flashlight across the floor, revealing dust, debris, and grime. This must've been where they processed shipments. Forklifts. Pallets. Chemicals. Whatever.

Now, though?

Now it looked like a perfect spot to skate.

That thought made me laugh. Just a breath through my nose, but it worked. I felt better. It's funny how quickly a place can shift once your nerves settle. Still, I wasn't stupid. Whatever made that sound earlier... it hadn't left my mind.

I walked for what felt like ten straight minutes. The loading bays seemed to stretch on forever. At the very end, one of the doors sat partially open. A beam of sunlight pierced through the gap, catching the dust in the air. Outside, a tree line swayed beyond a marshy ditch. The sky had turned gray. It looked like rain.

I made a mental note: Leave soon. I didn't want to bike home in a storm.

Pulling out my water bottle, I took a long, greedy swig. I'd be back. This place was too good not to share. I'd bring the guys next time, or whoever was free. It gave me hope that maybe we'd find more places like this. More forgotten spaces waiting to be discovered.

A raindrop hit my forehead. Then another.

Soon, the drizzle crept through the open door.

*Shit*, I thought. *Time to head back.*

Turning around, I noticed a maintenance tunnel I hadn't seen before. It jutted out from the far corner. Small, maybe five feet tall. Just big enough to crouch through. Something about it called to me. I wanted to check it out badly. But the rain had started, and I knew better than to push my luck.

All I could think about now was getting home... and figuring out what excuse I'd give my mom when I showed up soaked. I started back through the hallway toward the loading dock.

***Click***\*\*.\*\*

Another sound. Same as before. That same wet, organic snap. Only this time, it was close.

Right behind me.

I froze.

Slowly, I turned around, my eyes locking on the scene behind me. The open garage door, the gray sky, the crouching tunnel. Everything looked the same. Nothing seemed out of place. But something *was* different. My gaze drifted back to the tunnel. And then I saw it.

Staring right back.

I ran.

I don't even remember screaming, but I must have. It was more of a whimper—choked and sharp—as I sprinted back through the hall. My body moved without thought. Every step cracked through silence. The rain outside had grown louder... or maybe it was just my blood surging, pounding in my ears.

I had to get out.

Goosebumps tore across my skin. Every hair stood on end.

*It has no eyes.*

*It has no eyes.*

*It has no eyes.*

That was all I could think.

This wasn't fear. It was something else. Something primal. Fight or flight, but the dial was cranked only to flight.

Tears blurred my vision. I twisted back into the main warehouse at full sprint.

But I didn't see the pipe.

A rusted protrusion caught my foot mid-stride.

***Snap***\*\*.\*\*

I went airborne. My body hit the concrete with a sickening thud. Ribs scraping, elbow splitting open on impact.

The flashlight clattered ahead of me, spinning to a stop, its beam pointed up into the rafters.

That's when I saw it.

The catwalk, its twisted metal and rusted supports, shuddered directly over where I'd just sprinted. For a second, I thought it was just my vision shaking. But then it groaned.

A corroded bolt snapped loose. Then another.

The catwalk gave way.

It slammed to the ground just yards ahead with a seismic boom, sending chunks of concrete into the air like shrapnel.

Then the ceiling joined in.

A section of corrugated metal and concrete tore loose and came down hard. It was faster than I could react.

I tried to dodge, to roll away, but the floor shifted beneath my back.

A beam—thick, steel, cold—struck down across my left leg.

The impact was immediate, unforgiving. The sound was worse.

A dull, wet crunch. A sharp snap, like dry wood splitting. My vision exploded into white.

The beam pinned me just below the knee. Not clean through. Crushed. My femur escaped, but the tibia and fibula weren't so lucky. They buckled under the weight, twisted against each other until one of them punched outward beneath the skin, bulging against my jeans like a grotesque knot.

I screamed, more from shock than pain. The kind of noise your body makes before your brain catches up. My breath hitched. Every nerve below my waist was on fire.

I tried to move by Instinct. Or maybe panic. But the beam didn't budge, and neither did my leg.

The pain hit second. Like something boiling inside the muscle.

I craned my neck, forcing myself to look down.

My leg was warped. Swollen at the knee, foot splayed at the wrong angle. The skin just above my shoe had begun to split under pressure, showing slick red where the muscle was tearing. But there wasn't spurting blood. No severed artery. No gushing.

The bone was broken. Maybe shattered. But the major vessels had been spared.

My body trembled. Sweat poured down my face, mixing with dust and the rain dripping through holes in the ceiling. My leg throbbed with every heartbeat, each pulse swelling like it might burst through the skin. I could feel the beam sinking deeper, grinding tissue between metal and bone.

I wasn't going anywhere. Not crawling. Not dragging. Not even sitting up. I closed my eyes as the rest of the ceiling came down.

And blacked out.

I spent the next four days buried.

I drank what was left in the half-empty water bottle I'd brought, ate whatever food I had stashed in my bag, and lay still beneath concrete and steel. Every movement sent lightning up my spine. The pain wasn't just pain: It was a living thing, gnawing at my nerves, pulsing with every heartbeat.

By day three, I had nothing left. No water. No food. No strength.

I would've died on the fourth day—dehydrated, delirious—if I hadn't been found.

This is the version I tell.

But it's not the full story.

Because I wasn't alone down there.

My eyes opened to nothing but black. Sweat beaded on my forehead while dust coated everything else. I was alive. Whether that was good or bad, I couldn't say yet. I tried to sit up and immediately regretted it; a sharp pain stopped me cold. That's when I took inventory of the rest of my body. Burning, searing pain radiated from multiple points. Light lacerations covered my hands and forearms. Slices from broken metal and shattered concrete, shallow but stinging with every breath. I felt my torso. Deeply bruised, but nothing seemed broken. I could move, barely, and I wasn't bleeding out. That counted as a win.

I couldn't say the same about my legs.

My right leg was buried under debris, but I could still wiggle my toes. The pressure was heavy, but nothing pinned it completely. The left, though... that was different. No words could really describe its condition. It didn't even feel like a leg anymore. Just a pulsing mass of agony.

Laying there in the dark, I couldn't see it. But I could *feel* it. The way the bone ground when I shifted. The way my nerves screamed with every heartbeat. The cold burn of metal sinking into flesh.

I was trapped. Broken. Buried. But alive.

I needed to get out.

That was my first real thought. Not survival, not logic, just a desperate, primal urge to escape. But the more I shifted, the more my reality sank in. Every movement set my nerves on fire. A searing, electric pain that radiated from my leg and snaked through my spine. My head felt thick, like my thoughts had to drag themselves through molasses just to surface. Fog. Heat. Dust. Pain. It all bled together.

I wasn't buried completely... but I may as well have been.

My body was pinned flat on my back, cocooned in rubble and twisted steel. I had maybe a foot of space between my face and the slanted concrete slabs above me. Barely enough to move my arms, much less escape. The catwalk had collapsed in just the right way to save my torso from being crushed. But it also built a cage around me. Iron bars arched over my chest, walls of debris hemmed me in on either side. The beam on my leg kept me from even turning over.

I couldn't sit up. Couldn't crawl. Couldn't *breathe* without feeling the weight of the building pressing down.

About five minutes in, once I stopped gagging on the dust and got enough shallow air into my lungs, I started yelling. Words at first. The obvious ones. "Hello?! I need help! I'm stuck!" But the sound barely left my little tomb. It bounced off the concrete above me and died right there, swallowed by the rubble.

The yelling turned into begging. Then screaming. Then something beyond words. Animal sounds. Raw and cracked and humiliating. I kept going until my throat was scorched and my tongue felt like paper.

And then came the spasms.

It wasn't on purpose. It was instinct. My brain locked into panic mode and I started thrashing; Tiny, trapped convulsions as my body fought the confinement. My hands scraped at the steel bars above, fingers bloody and raw. My leg exploded with fresh pain, like someone jammed a cattle prod straight into the bone. But I couldn't stop. My limbs jerked on their own. My jaw clenched so hard I thought I'd shatter my own teeth. I was shaking, twitching, sobbing. Anything to not feel so trapped.

The dread hit hard and fast.

This was it. My grave. The wreckage didn't feel like a building anymore. It felt like it *wanted* me here. Like it was alive, and I was the infection it was trying to seal in. I was just another part of the ruins now. Another body lost to the silence.

Tears streaked down my face, catching dirt and dust and sweat. My voice gave out. I was condemned to rot here.

Time passed both too slowly and too fast.

My mind couldn't make sense of it. Skipping frames one moment, then lingering too long on the worst ones the next. I was stuck in this loop of confusion and pain, detached from the world outside yet completely buried within it. My thoughts didn't feel like mine anymore. They were scattered, overused, worn thin. But I *had* to think. I *had* to do something.

I forced myself to breathe. In. Out. Slow. Controlled. Tried to *be* in my body again. To *understand* it.

I was buried. That much was clear.

The weight above me had stopped shifting, which meant things had settled… but how much rubble was actually on me? Could I move any of it? Enough to create space, or yell louder? Even the thought of lifting something made my leg ache harder. I needed information. I needed options.

I twisted just enough to reach the strap of my backpack, which had been jammed between my back and the rubble. I slid it to my side, inch by inch, teeth gritted, until I could unzip it without moving the rest of my body.

The pain stayed lodged in my leg. Not sharp anymore, just deep. Throbbing. Like something pulsing between bone and muscle with every heartbeat. I didn't dare do anything that might restart the lightning strikes.

I popped the zipper open. Inside: my half-full water bottle, one Slim Jim, my manga, my old digital camera, and most importantly, my phone.

I pulled it out with trembling hands. The screen glowed to life. **6:17 PM**. Five hours. I'd only been here five hours. It felt like days.

No service.

My chest sank. I knew better than to count on signal, but seeing the empty bars still hit harder than it should have. No lifeline. No GPS. Just a rectangle of false hope. Still, I turned on the flashlight and swept it around.

The beam caught particles in the air; dust floating like ash in a snow globe. My little cocoon of concrete looked even more surreal under the light. The way the rubble had landed… it didn't make sense. Jagged slabs of wall and ceiling formed narrow triangles just above me. One chunk of catwalk had embedded itself into the ground beside my shoulder. Rebar jutted down like skewers, as if the whole building had tried and failed to pierce me.

The only part of me truly pinned was my leg.

I should've died. Easily. Something should've gone straight through my skull or chest. The fact I wasn't a red smear under that roof? A miracle. A freak accident that left me just barely intact.

But why *did* the ceiling fall?

I stared into the dark corners of the rubble, trying to remember. Anything to distract from the pain. Anything to keep my brain moving forward. What happened? I'd tripped, yeah. On that damn pipe. But… why did that cause the ceiling to cave?

My mind wandered deeper, crawling backward through time. Back through the fear. Through the running.

And then it hit me. I remembered why I ran.

***Click.***

"Did you know that all whistles sound the same?"

The voice came from somewhere beyond the rubble. Calm. Conversational. Like someone making small talk at a bus stop.

Silence. Then—

A whistle. Simple. Cheerful, even. The kind of tune you'd hear drifting from a kitchen window on a Sunday morning.

***Click.***

"Fascinating, isn't it?" the voice continued, still pleasant. "Pitch may vary, but the *quality*… The texture of it… it's universal. I could be a grandmother. Your grandmother, perhaps. Whistling a tune in the kitchen while she cleans."

Another pause. Longer this time.

Then the voice changed.

"Sweetheart, dinner's almost ready."

It was a woman's voice now. Warm. Maternal. So real it made my skin crawl. The kind of voice that should've been comforting. But here, in the dark, under tons of concrete, it was wrong. All wrong.

"Why don't you come wash up? You've been playing in that dirty building all day."

The flashlight trembled in my hand.

***Click.*** 

***Click.***

Then silence. Complete and suffocating.

I couldn't breathe right. Each inhale came shallow and sharp. My ribs screamed. I strained to hear footsteps, movement, *anything*. But there was only the settling creak of metal somewhere above me, the whisper of wind through broken windows far overhead.

"Please…" My voice broke. "Please, I can't… somebody help me. Please."

The words tumbled out, fractured and desperate, swallowed by the dark.

Nothing answered.

I waited. Minutes stretched into something unmeasurable. The silence pressed in from all sides, thick and suffocating. My heart hammered against my ribs. Every nerve in my body screamed at me to move, to run, to do *something*. But I couldn't. I was pinned. Trapped. Helpless.

The thing was still out there. I knew it was. I could *feel* it.

Time crawled forward. The pain in my leg became a constant companion, a baseline throb that spiked with every breath. My mouth was dry. My head pounded. The adrenaline that had kept me sharp was starting to fade, leaving behind a bone-deep exhaustion that made my thoughts sluggish and thick.

But I couldn't sleep. Not with that thing out there.

I checked my phone. **6:43 PM**. Still no service. Battery at 67%. I turned off the screen and clutched it to my chest like a talisman.

Stay awake. Just stay awake.

I tried to focus on something—anything—to keep my mind occupied. I counted my breaths. One. Two. Three. Lost count at forty-something and started over. I thought about my mom, about what she'd say when I didn't come home. Would she call the police? Would anyone think to look here?

The darkness was absolute. No light seeped through the gaps anymore. The sun had set. I was buried in a black so complete it felt solid, like I could reach out and touch it.

***Click.***

My entire body went rigid.

It was faint. Distant. But unmistakable.

***Click.***

Closer this time. Or maybe not. Sound did weird things down here, bouncing off concrete and steel, warping and echoing until I couldn't tell where it came from.

I held my breath. Listened.

Nothing.

Then—

***Click.***

Off to my right. Beyond the beam. The thing was moving. Slowly. Methodically. Each click a pulse of sound that let it see in the dark.

I fumbled for my phone, turned on the flashlight, and swept it across the rubble. The beam cut through the darkness but revealed nothing. Just concrete and steel and shadow.

***Click.***

I spun the light toward the sound. Nothing.

My hands shook. The light wavered.

"I know you're there," I whispered. My voice cracked. "I know you're—"

***Click.*** 

***Click.***

Two in quick succession. Closer. Definitely closer.

I killed the light. Maybe it could see the glow. Maybe I was giving away my position. My thoughts spiraled, irrational and panicked. It already knew where I was. It had to. But I couldn't stop myself. I turned off the phone and pressed it against my chest, curling into myself as much as the rubble would allow.

Silence.

Minutes passed. Or hours. I couldn't tell anymore.

Then I heard it.

Scraping.

Soft at first. Rhythmic. Like nails on concrete. Or claws. It was coming from somewhere near my feet; Just past the beam, in the darkness I couldn't see into.

***Scrape. Scrape. Scrape.***

Methodical. Patient. Not frantic or aggressive. Just... deliberate.

I wanted to scream. I wanted to thrash and kick and claw my way out. But I couldn't. The beam held me. The rubble held me. I was helpless.

***Click.***

The scraping stopped.

Silence again. Thick and oppressive.

I waited. My heart pounded so hard I thought it might burst. My leg throbbed in time with my pulse. Sweat dripped into my eyes.

Nothing.

The thing had stopped. Or moved away. Or was just... waiting.

I checked my phone again. **9:12 PM**. Two and a half hours since the voice. Two and a half hours of this.

I couldn't do this all night. I couldn't.

But I had to.

I forced myself to stay still. To breathe slowly. To listen.

***Click.***

Far away this time. Maybe on the other side of the rubble pile. Maybe in another part of the building entirely.

***Click.***

Fainter still.

It was moving away. Or circling. I didn't know.

The exhaustion crept back in, heavier now. My eyelids drooped. My thoughts blurred at the edges. I pinched my arm, bit my lip, anything to stay awake. But my body was betraying me. The adrenaline was gone. The pain had become background noise. All that was left was the crushing weight of exhaustion.

I checked my phone. **10:47 PM**. Battery at 61%.

I couldn't keep doing this. I couldn't keep checking the time, burning battery, staying awake. I needed to sleep. My body *needed* to sleep.

***Click.***

Distant. Barely audible.

I waited. Listened.

Nothing.

My eyes closed. I forced them open. Closed again. Forced them open.

***Click.***

So faint I almost missed it.

It continued that way until I saw the morning light shine through.

Everything after that is scattered. What I’m writing now is from memory, as best as I can piece it together. I’ll post more once I can get my thoughts straight.
