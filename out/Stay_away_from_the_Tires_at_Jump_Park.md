When I was a kid I loved going to the local park to play at the playground. I was a shy little guy all the way up until middle school, and always had trouble making regular conversation with the kids in class and forming genuine connections. But for whatever reason, going to the local park made my shyness disappear. At the park, you didn’t really have to work to make friends, you just showed up and played. Shared interests or hobbies didn’t really matter when you were playing pirates, or space adventure. Nobody has time to ask you your favorite color during an impending Martian invasion. 

The Jump Park playground was a sprawling wooden fortress of nineties ingenuity. A giant field of mulch hosted the coolest structure my 9 year old self had ever seen.  A multi level wooden castle rose from the center of the park, tall wooden steeples reaching as if to eclipse the sun. The tallest of them boasting a little ladder where kids brave enough could climb to the top and ride the giant twisting slide down to the sand pit below.  From the central fort rope bridges and jungle gyms branched out to smaller but still impressive wooden structures in all directions. Some had slides and seesaws , others had rope ladders and netting to climb. Meanwhile the ground between the structures was littered with an array of swings, giant tires and pipe tunnels. 

It was a lawsuit waiting to happen, but that was something the parents could worry about. Us kids were just there to have fun. If you didn’t get the occasional splinter or slide burn, well maybe weren’t playing hard enough. Sure, getting a surprise sting by a stray hornet trying to nest in the wood really sucked, but the experience of disappearing into your own fantasy was worth the risk.

One Saturday in the early days of summer, my parents had taken me to the park to play. The park was pretty empty for how nice of a day it was and the whole playground was filled with just a handful of kids, myself included. My buddy Chris, another regular at the park, and I were playing Temple Explorer. Chris’ parents had given him one of those cheap disposable cameras and we were going around digging for treasure and taking pictures of the “ruins” we had discovered all while doing our best Indiana Jones impressions. We were on the third level of the main fort when we heard a yell from behind us.

“Who dare’s disturb my Temple!” Another little boy I was less familiar with roared, poking his head up from the ladder leading to the second level. 

“Run!” Chris shrieked and we booked towards the ladder leading to the giant slide, the new boy hot on our heels. Our sense of urgency slightly delayed by the single person nature of the slide as we started our daring escape from the temple monster. 

I like to think that we had pretty good imaginations for elementary kids, but at the end of the day, most of our adventures were just glorified games of tag.  We would spend some time exploring or roleplaying, but whether it was pirates or aliens, there were ultimately always two factions. The runners and the chasers.

Chris hit the ground first, soon followed by myself.  
“We have to make it to the plane!” He yelled just as our pursuer was starting his descent. 

The footsteps of the temple monster rattled behind us as we made our way to the little corner see-saw that was acting as our getaway plane. You had to pick something at the edge of the park to be the safe zone otherwise it just wasn’t fun. We had gotten a pretty good headstart, but man this kid was fast.

“Let’s split up!” I yelled, turning to climb the rope ladder back onto one of the wooden forts and make my way to the see saw from there.

Chris kept beelining it on the ground but had to abruptly change paths when a second kid decided being a monster looked fun and jumped in front of him, blocking the way with a roar. I was on the bridge connecting to the structure nearest the see-saw when Chris ran under me. I paused to see him make his way to the minefield of giant tires on the ground. 

Smart guy that Chris was, a lot of the kids were wary of the tires in the summer because they had a tendency to be a nesting ground for bees and other nasty critters. The other kid decided he didn’t want to play anymore and gave up the pursuit. 

Unfortunately for me, that little pause was all the other monster needed to catch up to me. I felt hands on my shoulders followed by a gleeful

“Gotcha!” 

I wasn’t making it home to tell the tale of my adventures in the temple today. Getting caught meant one thing:I was now a monster too, a monster that had just seen where Chris ran off to hide.

Smiling, I told my new compatriot to go guard the escape route, I would flush Chris out of hiding. 

“I’m coming to get you!” I called out as I approached the tire jungle. I had to let Chris know I was after him in case he didn’t see me get caught, otherwise I was basically cheating and that was lame. I glanced around and saw his head peak around the corner of one of the upright monster tires before running further into the maze. I hurried after him and made it around the corner just in time to see him dive into one of the big tires laying on the ground.

*Too slow.* I thought to myself as I crept closer and closer, taking my sweet time looking around to savor my moment of triumph. Finally I made it to the tire that Chris had dove in and took a deep breath. 

“You’re one of us now!” I screamed, reaching in to grab the boy, but to my surprise Chris was gone.

I looked inside the tire, then looked all around again. There was no way he got away. I saw him get in the tire and hide, he had to be there. Finally, I stepped over the wide wall of rubber and into the middle of the tire myself. I wasn’t quite the scaredy cat that the other kid was, but I had to admit, I was a little wary of getting bitten by a spider. Inside the tire, I grew more confused, spinning on my hands and knees in a circle trying to find my hidden friend. I was just about to get up when the ground beneath me shifted and opened up, plummeting me into the darkness below. 

Shakily I got to my feet, my elbows and knees had gotten scraped up but overall I was no worse for wear. Light shone in from the opening above me, illuminating the small dirt tunnel I now stood in. The flimsy wooden trap door I  had fallen through swayed causing the light to shift and cast long shadows down the path. Looking down the tunnel, I could see the flickering light of a candle illuminating the end of the passage. I’m not sure if it was adrenaline from the fall, or curiosity, but for whatever reason, I found myself plunging into the darkness eager to see what awaited at the end.

Moving through the tunnel, I quickly realized I had underestimated just how big it was. As I walked towards the candlelight, other openings began to branch off the wall, each with its own tiny spec of light dotting the corridor just enough for someone to find their way. I ignored these branching paths, not feeling adventurous enough to go too deep and continued onward. Drawing nearer to the candle at the end, I started to grow more uncomfortable with the space around me. I felt like it was closing on me, like I wasn’t alone. A small wave of fear washed over me and I came to a stop. 

That’s when I heard it. Further behind me in the cave, the unmistakable plop of a single footstep on dirt. I turned behind me and saw that a black veil had fallen over the passageway. Somebody had closed the trapdoor that illuminated my entrance. 

“Chris?” I called out, a tremble in my voice. 

I was met with the rushing sound of plopping footsteps drawing closer to me from the dark. 

I turned and ran, ran as fast as my little legs could carry me. Luckily the passage didn’t end at the candle and I was able to keep going. I ran mindlessly through the tunnels turning down passage after passage. Behind me candles began flickering out as I ran, and eventually I was moving blind, running through complete darkness.  I had just turned another corner when I ran smack first into Chris. The boy screamed at the impact, the disposable camera we had been playing with flashing in his hands before clattering to the ground. The sudden burst of light sent stars and afterimages through my eyes, but I could still see the look on Chris’ face as his pupils dilated with pure terror. 

“Run!” I screamed as we turned and ran off further into the darkness, our fun game of imagination having turned sinister and too real. We ran and we ran for what felt like miles, our little hearts racing with panic and exertion. Then I saw it, Chris had just stepped past when I tugged his arm down a side passage that offered a glimmer of light. He yelped as I pulled him in and we sprinted for the tiny ray of hope shining down from the ceiling. Drawing close we found another trap door, this one pock marked with holes, allowing in our rays of salvation. Frantically we climbed the little handholds and flipped it open, flailing back into the light of day and slamming the door shut behind us. We both lay on the ground panting for a moment, before running off to our parents.

The worst part about being a kid is the lack of credibility. When Chris and I spilled our story to our parents, they laughed it off and told us how imaginative we were. Chris even got scolded for losing his camera. I begged and begged my mom to believe me, even eventually convinced her to come see the trapdoor in the tires, but when we went to investigate I couldn’t get it to open, it was like I was hitting solid ground. There was only so much I could with no proof, and eventually we went home for the evening.

I kind of retreated into myself for a few weeks after that. Mom tried to take me out to play, but I didn’t want to go to the park anymore. It got the point where she was begging me to go with her instead of the other way around. Eventually enough time went by and I relented, I had been getting lonely without seeing Chris or the other kids. 

I was wary when I stepped back into the park again, but Chris was there waiting for me. I think our parents had gotten in contact and decided we both needed some exposure therapy. With a familiar face, I quickly found myself relaxing and we  were playing just like old times. Mom had bought me a cool Tonka dump truck and we spent a good portion of the day in the sand pit, hauling and dumping loads of dirt, before eventually climbing to the top of the tower  to play on the huge slide. Chris and I ended up having a great time, and I found myself sad to leave again when it was time to go home. 

Walking back to the car, I noticed something tucked into the hinge of my truck bed. 

“Whatcha got there sweetie?” Mom asked when she noticed I had stopped walking. 

I stood still behind her, looking at the photograph that had been left for me. When my mom saw it her face went white and she snatched it away from me. 

Jump park closed the following week. After finding the photograph, Mom had immediately called the police and I spent the next day doing the best I could to recount my story about the tunnels. 

The local PD combed the park and eventually broke through the trapdoor in the tires. They found the tunnels as I had described and traced them to many more trapdoors scattered around the entirety of the park. They found antechambers within the tunnels, containing old jars of pickled food, burnt candles, and rope. They found a small room tucked away in the furthest reach of the tunnel system, fully bare save for a stained, rotting mattress. 

However, they didn’t find the gangly man that stood hunched behind me in the photo, his emaciated arm grasping at the back of my head that sat just out of reach.
