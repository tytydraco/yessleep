Much is said about appearance—that beauty is in the eye of the beholder. Honestly, that conversation is nothing more than a bunch of nonsense meant to make unattractive people feel better about themselves instead of dealing with self-pity and self-hatred.

Beauty is what brings happiness, love, and money.

Nobody likes unattractive people—people with poorly cared-for skin, full of acne, who are overweight, or who don’t at least have presentable hair.

Do you really think that many movie and music stars got where they are just because of their talent or acting skills?

People admire beauty: straight, well-groomed hair and slim bodies—not excessively thin, but rather a healthy slimness.

That’s why perfection has always been central to my existence. Everything I set out to do has to be done with excellence.

My appearance was no different. Even though I had admirable beauty since childhood—which earned me a few nice awards in children’s beauty pageants—I didn’t hesitate to get a nose job and follow strict diets with extreme commitment.

As well as running every morning in the park across from my building. Besides the gym, which is my sanctuary, and thanks to my efforts, I have an extremely beautiful, slim body with subtle curves and no stretch marks.

As another day began, I followed my usual routine. I prepared a healthy breakfast that my nutritionist included in my new diet, took a shower, changed clothes, did some stretching exercises, and left home at 6:25 a.m.

I ran into the same ugly bitches during my morning run, as if their efforts were worth anything.

With each passing day, their bodies seemed to grow fatter and more flabby.

When one of the overweight women walked past me and greeted me, I quickly turned my face away and turned up the music in my headphones.

God forbid they think I’m one of them.

I was on my third lap when I felt an extremely sharp pain in my right ankle, which made me stop immediately. When I lifted my pant leg, I found a small reddish spider covered in tiny hairs. I hit it hard with my hand and crushed it against my skin. I felt disgust and a burning sensation in the area, but I continued my run. I couldn’t let a small sting ruin my calorie burn.

The walk back home was calm. However, when I took off my sneakers, I noticed that the place where I had been bitten was red and starting to swell. I took a shower and put an ice pack on the wound before finishing getting ready for work.

I took a few pictures in the elevator mirror and thought about posting all of them, since I looked hot in every single one, but I had to make the sacrifice of choosing just one.

While I was in the taxi, I liked a post about suicide and low self-esteem from a famous psychologist. I scrolled down my feed and couldn’t help but laugh when I saw a former college friend posting a ridiculous photo at the beach. How did she have the courage to wear a bikini and post a picture with that body? Simply disgusting.

I’m a model at a conceptual agency in New York, and not to brag, but I’m the most requested one there.

I was shooting photos for the cover of Vogue when I felt sharp stings in my ankle, accompanied by a wave of intense itching. I had to go to the bathroom several times to scratch the bite.

At the gym, it was worse. I couldn’t even finish my workout circuit.

On my way home, I stopped at a pharmacy. The pharmacist looked at the bite and said it was nothing more than an allergic reaction. He prescribed antihistamines and an ointment, advising me to see a doctor if the symptoms didn’t improve.

When I got home, I took two pills and applied the ointment to the lump. From the center, a kind of clear fluid came out.

I tried to have dinner, but I felt extremely nauseous looking at the tofu in my salad.

I ended up going to bed earlier, hoping the next day would be better. However, the few hours of sleep I had were taken over by a terrible nightmare, with horrifying images of gigantic insects brutally mutilated around the rotting flesh of animals I didn’t even recognize.

Then I dreamed that I was chewing on the head of that same former friend from the beach photo earlier and savoring pieces of her brain and mutilated body.

I woke up at 3:00 a.m., extremely sweaty, and vomited everything I had eaten that day onto my bedroom floor. I spent the rest of the night cleaning my own vomit while my ankle wouldn’t stop itching.

When I finished, it was daylight again. I thought about getting ready to run, but I was extremely tired and depressed. I tried to eat a fruit salad I had made, but I felt extreme disgust and threw it away.

I got dressed and left.

I tried everything to make my day more productive and filled it with activities to try to forget the damn itching I was feeling in my ankle. Even with all my efforts, I always ended up in the bathroom scratching and scratching with my nails until blood came out of the damn lump.

At lunchtime, I ended up eating a rare steak at a restaurant I used to go to every day. The waiter looked at me with a strange and questioning expression when he placed the plate on my table.

I forgot to mention that I’m a vegetarian—for the sake of animals and blah blah blah—like many other big digital influencers around the world.

But for some reason, I couldn’t think about anything other than meat. I needed to taste the metallic and slightly sweet flavor of blood on my palate. But time got in the way, and I barely managed to satisfy my craving.

I was still hungry. And very thirsty. A terrible thirst.

When I got home, I took off and threw my shoes aside and ran to sit on my couch, placing my foot on the empty space next to me.

The lump now covered a large part of my ankle. It was extremely red and full of pus.

I almost called a doctor, but I was afraid he would prescribe medication that might somehow make me gain weight. Just thinking about gaining weight makes my stomach turn.

The pharmacist was right. This is nothing more than a small allergy. I just need to take the antihistamine and apply the ointment again.

I kept repeating that thought like a mantra to myself while trying to squeeze the thing on my ankle. I pressed the lump hard, and a thick yellow liquid with a very strong smell—almost like rotten meat—started coming out. Even so, I kept squeezing, and the liquid began to spill onto the cushion.

Even though the pain was almost unbearable, I continued squeezing until the yellow turned into dark, warm red. On impulse, I rubbed some of my blood onto my lips, and I had never tasted anything so glorious—like cherry and strawberry juice.

But as I kept rubbing the lump and licking the blood off my fingers, my palate was taken over by a sour, rusty, extremely strong taste—the kind of taste I imagine a dead animal’s carcass would have. I ended up running to the bathroom and vomiting until I passed out on the floor.

After a while, I woke up, but I couldn’t open my eyes. They were sealed shut by something sticky and cold. I panicked and tried to remove it with my hands, but all my efforts were in vain.

Whenever I tried to pull it away, it felt like more of that substance was coming out of my eyes. I stood up and reached my hands into the air until I found the sink. Carefully, so I wouldn’t fall, I stood up and turned on the faucet. I washed my face desperately for what felt like forever, splashing water and using hand soap
.
When I finally managed to see again, I fixed my gaze on the mirror and was horrified by what I saw. Small brown warts were scattered across my eyes, and a strange liquid was leaking from them.

My beautiful, striking blue eyes now had disgusting brown spots.

Frightened, I stepped back as if moving away from the mirror would change my appearance, and I ran to my bedroom, where I hid under my blankets, scared and crying like a coward.

What the hell was happening to me?

I cried and cried until I fell asleep.

I woke up in what seemed to be the afternoon. My eyes were no longer hurting, and the irritating itching in my ankle had stopped.

For a brief moment, I thought everything had just been a nightmare.

But my relief didn’t last long. When I pulled the blanket away from my body, I fell into despair.

Reddish hair—thin yet firm—was growing on my legs
.
I have always been careful about body hair because, to me, it’s a sign of neglect. Seeing all that hair spreading not only over my legs but across my entire body sent me into despair.

I grabbed my phone and called emergency services, crying uncontrollably. However, the operator didn’t believe my story and hung up on me.

Then I called my mother. I told her everything that was happening, and even though she thought it was strange, she promised she would visit me by the end of the day. She was at work and couldn’t leave.

I would have to endure until 7 p.m., maybe 8 p.m.

When Mom arrives, everything will be fine.

Everything will be fine…
