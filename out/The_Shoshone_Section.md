I’ve been sitting on this for two months.

Tom was my colleague for nineteen years. The best backcountry ranger I’ve ever worked with. He knew that district better than anyone alive, better than anyone who’s ever worked it. When he stopped checking in I knew before the search started how it was going to end.

I’m retiring at the end of this season. I can’t take this with me and I can’t throw it away and I can’t keep being the only person who’s read it.

So I’m posting it. I haven’t changed anything. The only things I’ve altered are the names and the specific location because I still work here and because it doesn’t matter where exactly it happened. It happened. That’s enough.

If you’ve ever worked backcountry search and rescue long enough you’ll recognize what he’s describing. You might not have a name for it. Neither do we. That’s sort of the point.

— D.C.

\-----

I still get the same feeling driving through the entrance after thirty one years. Something settles in my chest that doesn’t settle anywhere else. My ex-wife used to say I loved this place more than I loved her. She wasn’t entirely wrong.

New season. Bridges repaired from the spring melt, trails cleared, pit toilets dug at the backcountry sites. The usual inventory of things that broke over winter. Nothing unusual to report.

The family filed their missing persons report two days ago. Their son, eight years old, separated from them on the trail sometime in the early afternoon. They’d stopped to rest near the eastern junction. The father says he turned around and the boy was simply gone.

I know that section of trail.

I’ve known it for thirty one years.

I didn’t say anything when the briefing happened. Neither did my partner, who’s been here almost as long as I have. We just looked at each other across the room and then looked away.

SAR team deploys at first light tomorrow. Twelve personnel, two dog teams, aerial support from the next district over. Standard protocol.

I’ll run the eastern grid myself.

\-----

Full day in the field. Eastern grid from the junction south to the lake shore and back. Dog teams ran the perimeter twice. Aerial did three passes over the tree line between the trail and the water.

Nothing.

No tracks beyond the junction point. No disturbance in the undergrowth. No scent trail the dogs could follow for more than thirty yards before it simply stopped, which the handler said she’d never seen before and which she said twice, quietly, like she was trying to convince herself it was explainable.

The forest in that section is wrong in a way I’ve never been able to put into words and have never tried to put into words officially. The trees are the same trees. The trail is the same trail. But the quality of the air changes past the eastern junction — something attentive about it, something that makes the back of your neck do what the back of your neck does when you understand you’re being watched.

I’ve been past that junction hundreds of times over thirty one years.

I never linger.

My partner found me at the vehicles at end of shift. He looked at his boots for a while before he spoke.

“You know they’re not going to find him,” he said.

I didn’t answer.

“Same as the girl three years ago,” he said. “Same as the two brothers before that.”

I told him to write up his grid report and get some sleep.

He nodded and walked away and neither of us said anything else about it.

We never say anything else about it.

That’s how it works here. You notice things long enough and you stop asking what they mean and you just adjust your behavior accordingly. Every ranger who’s worked this district long enough does the same thing. Nobody issues a memo about it. Nobody sits you down and explains. You just watch how the experienced ones move around that section of trail and eventually you start moving the same way.

Nobody goes past the eastern junction alone.

Nobody lingers.

\-----

Day six of the official search. Seventy personnel total now, three helicopters, four dog teams, ground crews running grids from the junction south and east. We’ve covered terrain that should have produced something by now. A shoe. A water bottle. A thread of clothing on a branch.

The forest gives back nothing.

I’ve been running the eastern grid every day. Each time I reach the point where the scent trails died I stop and stand for a while and listen. The park has a particular silence that visitors mistake for peace. It isn’t peace. It’s attentiveness. The silence of something enormous paying close attention.

Past the eastern junction the attentiveness has a different quality. Older. More specific.

I don’t know how to write that in a field report so I don’t.

\-----

Official search scaled back today. Seventeen days, thousands of air and ground miles covered, no evidence of the boy’s whereabouts. Transitioning from rescue to recovery protocol.

I read the official statement twice. The language is careful and measured and says nothing.

He’s eight years old. He has brown hair and he was wearing a red jacket the day he disappeared.

I submitted my continued field assignment request this afternoon. Approved through end of next month, which is what I expected. After that I’ll submit again.

\-----

I asked my partner today how long he’d known about the eastern section.

He was quiet for a long time.

Then he said since his third season. Said the ranger before him never explained it directly, just steered him away from certain areas without reason given, and eventually the pattern became clear enough that explanation wasn’t necessary.

I asked if he’d ever reported it.

He looked at me the way you look at someone who has asked a question with an obvious answer.

I didn’t ask again.

We didn’t talk about what we thought was in that section. We’ve worked together for nineteen years and we have never once said out loud what we think is in that section. I don’t think we’re going to start now.

\-----

I heard something today.

Two hundred yards past the junction, in the trees north of the trail. A high sound, brief, ambiguous. Could have been a bird. Could have been the wind through a specific arrangement of branches.

I stopped and listened for twenty minutes.

It didn’t come again.

I stood at the junction for a long time on the way back.

I drove back to the station and called the boy’s parents. I didn’t tell them what I’d heard. I told them I was still looking. His mother thanked me in a voice that had been through more than a voice should have to go through.

I’m still looking.

\-----

Found something today.

A scrap of red fabric caught on a branch approximately four hundred yards past the junction, north of the trail, in a section of trees so dense the aerial searches wouldn’t have been able to see it. The size of a palm. Consistent with the jacket the boy was wearing the day he disappeared.

I photographed it and documented the coordinates and then I stood there for a very long time.

Four hundred yards past the junction is the furthest I’ve ever gone into that section alone. The trees are older here, the canopy dense enough that the light comes through in pieces. The ground is soft in a way that’s different from the rest of the park, like it has more give to it.

The silence here has a different texture. Still attentive, still old, but something else underneath it now. Something that noticed I’d found the fabric. Something that was waiting to see what I would do next.

I took out my compass.

The needle moved in a slow circle. Not spinning — just moving, deliberately, like it was being redirected.

I looked at the trail marker I’d been counting from. It was behind me where it should be.

I took a step north, further in.

The marker was still behind me.

I took another step.

The marker was still behind me.

The light between the trees had changed while I was standing still. The pieces of sky visible through the canopy were in different positions than they should have been given the time of day.

I know this place.

I know it the way I know my own hands.

I turned around and walked back to the trail marker at a controlled pace. I did not run. I counted each step. The marker came back to where it should have been after twelve steps.

I came out of the tree line at the junction.

I had been in the trees for six hours.

My watch said I had been in the trees for forty minutes.

\-----

My partner asked me this morning if I was alright.

I told him I was fine.

He looked at me for a long time and then said that whatever I found out there wasn’t worth it. That the boy was gone the same way the girl was gone and the brothers were gone and the others whose names I’d find if I looked far enough back through the incident reports. That the eastern section takes what it takes and has always taken what it takes and thirty one years of this job should have taught me that some things don’t come back.

I told him I understood.

He nodded and walked away.

I don’t think he believed me. I’m not sure I believed myself.

The family deserves to know their son isn’t coming home. I don’t know how to tell them what I know. I’m not sure I know anything that would help them.

I need to go back to the four hundred yard mark.

I need to understand what’s further in.

The fabric is still in my pack. Red. The size of a palm. A small boy was wearing a jacket made of this material the day he walked into the eastern section and the eastern section kept him.

I’m going in tomorrow morning.

I know what my partner would say about that.

I’m going in anyway.

\-----

Set out at first light. Weather clear, visibility good, full pack, satellite phone, emergency beacon.

Past the junction the air changed the way it always changes. Attentive. Old.

I reached the four hundred yard mark. The branch where the fabric had been was bare. The ground was soft. The canopy filtered the light into pieces.

I stood there for a long time.

Then I took a step north.

The compass needle moved in its slow circle.

The trail marker was behind me where it should be.

The marker was behind me.
