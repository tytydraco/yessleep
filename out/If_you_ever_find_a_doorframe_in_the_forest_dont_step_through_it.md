We were rotting on the back patio. That’s how Leo put it, and he wasn't wrong. It was one of those late-July afternoons where the humidity and heat turned the simple act of existing, even in the shade, into a demanding task. 

My friends and I were sprawled across the patio, bored out of our minds. Sarah was staring at a line of ants navigating the cracks in the wooden slats. Leo was blowing bubbles into a glass of lukewarm lemonade. I was watching a bird tirelessly tending to its nest in a nearby oak. 

This was in the years before smartphones became commonplace, so our little group usually spent the summer holidays pretending to go on grand adventures in the hills surrounding our small town. But by this point it was the end of the break, and everything felt used up. The woods were mapped, the creeks were dry, and we were bored. 

My dad came around the corner of the house, wiping his hands on a greasy rag. He’d been messing with a stubborn lawnmower engine for a few hours. He stopped, looking at the three of us slumped in the shade like sacks of potatoes. 

"You kids look like you’re waiting for the world to end," he said, leaning against the railing. 

I sighed. "There’s nothing to do..." 

He chuckled. "When I was your age, I was hardly ever bored." 

"What did you even do back then? I can’t imagine there was much to do in the dark ages, either," I asked, though it was more of a snarky statement than a question. 

"We didn't have half the distractions you do now," he replied, ignoring my tone. "Usually, we just went around causing trouble for the neighbors. Well... except for that one year. We spent that whole summer looking for a witch in the forest." 

We all sat up a little straighter. 

He continued, "my granddad told me this story when I was about your age," his eyes drifting toward the tree line. "He said she lived deep in these parts, long before this town was even founded. She practiced dark magic, causing all sorts of misfortune for the townspeople. Souring the milk, making sure the rain never fell when it was needed, causing sickness... that sort of thing. The townspeople eventually had enough. They went into those woods with torches and burned her cottage to the ground with her still inside of it." 

The boredom vanished. It was as if he’d dropped a match into a pile of dry hay. 

"Me and a couple of friends went looking for that cottage all summer, but we never found a thing," my dad said while shrugging. "It’s just a story, after all." 

Impelled by the story, we did as kids do. We spent the next half hour arming ourselves with sturdy branches that made for decent swords and practiced dueling on the lawn. Leo, being the oldest and the loudest, took charge of the expedition. He led us down the well-traversed gravel roads, swinging his stick with a whistle through the air, claiming he’d be the one to find the witch's remains. 

We had played in these woods a hundred times, but the story and our imaginations made it feel like we were exploring uncharted territory. We marched for hours, hacking through ferns and low-hanging branches, eventually deciding we had to leave the main trails if we were to find anything interesting. 

By the late afternoon, the energy was waning and Sarah was complaining about the heat and the mosquitoes. 

"Can we just go home?" Sarah asked, swatting her neck. "There’s no cottage. Your dad was just trying to get us out of his hair." 

"Just a little further," Leo insisted. He was soaked with sweat, his shirt clinging to his back, but he had that determined expression that signaled he wasn't about to give up so easily. 

We pushed through a final, dense thicket of pines, expecting more of the same. Instead, we stopped dead. 

It was a clearing nearly 100 meters across. It looked like a jagged circle carved out of the dense woods. It was filled with waist-high yellow straws of dead grass that hissed as the wind caught them. There wasn't a single tree or bush inside the clearing. Just a big, nearly empty field. 

In the very center stood an old, brown doorframe. It looked rotten, the dark wood pitted, and the frame leaning slightly to the left. It looked so fragile that a strong gust of wind should have sent it clattering to the ground. There were no walls, no foundation, no sign that a house had ever existed around it. Just that lone, skeletal wooden frame. 

"How old do you think it is?" I whispered.  

"Hundreds of years," Leo replied and started walking toward it. His bravado was returning, fueled by the shock of the find. 

"But wood doesn't stay like that," Sarah countered, her brow furrowed. "It rots. It should be dirt by now. It can't be that old." 

Leo didn't answer. He reached the frame and circled it slowly. 

"Leo, don't go so close," I called out from the treeline. "What if this really is the remains of the witch’s house?" 

He looked back at me and laughed, though it sounded forced. "What, are you scared of a story?" 

"It’s getting dark," Sarah said, her voice tight. "Let’s just go back. Please." 

Leo looked at the frame, then back at us. He hesitated for a second, a genuine pause where he could have turned around. Then, he grinned. It was that classic arrogant look that made him feel untouchable. 

"I'll see you on the other side," he joked. 

He stepped through the threshold.  

Nothing happened. No thunder, no magic. Leo stepped out the other side and turned around with his arms stretched out. "See? Still me!" 

 

I wish I could say that was the end of it, but the years that followed were a slow-motion car crash. 

Leo broke his ankle the following week, and it simply refused to heal. He had to get surgery, infection set in, and then a second break followed when he tried to walk again too early. He spent his middle school years on crutches, watching us from the sidelines. 

Misfortune became Leo’s identity. By high school, people stayed away from him. It wasn't that they were mean; they were just instinctively afraid. If you sat next to Leo, your locker would jam. If you worked on a project with him, the file would disappear. It was as if the ground beneath him had soured, and every step he took made him sink a little deeper into the muck. 

I eventually left our small town to study in the city. Our friend group drifted apart, but through my parents' friends and other secondhand sources, I kept hearing about his continued misfortune. I heard Leo had lost his first job when the company went bankrupt just weeks after his start date, and that a freak electrical fire had burned down his apartment unit.  

The dominos just kept falling until the last one fell three years ago. I found his obituary in the digital edition of our local paper. He hadn't died in a grand tragedy. He’d just given up. 

Currently, I’m visiting my parents. When I arrived, I found my dad sitting in his usual spot on the patio.  

"The Haralds were over for the holidays," he said, his gaze drifting toward the treeline. "They brought their daughter, Maya. She’s about ten now." 

"Funny thing," Dad said, a puzzled frown creasing his face. "She went playing in the woods behind our house and came back saying she’d found something in a clearing. I remember you and your friends talking about something like that when you were kids. I figured it was just your imagination back then." 

He looked at me, his eyes searching mine for a reaction. 

"Yes," I said bluntly. "Why are you bringing that up now? You didn't believe us back then." 

"Because I never told that kid the story," he said quietly. "But she came back saying she’d gone through some door she found in the forest. It just... it reminded me of what you told me all those years ago. That’s all." 

I thought of Leo and the twenty years of misery that had started with a joke. Then I thought of Maya, sitting somewhere right now, unaware that the world had just stopped looking out for her. 

I also looked toward the trees. Somewhere in there, that rotten frame is still standing. So, if you ever find a doorframe in the forest, don’t step through it.
