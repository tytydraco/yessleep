“Phew, I’ve started to believe that this place was a hoax by the locals,” Sarah said, sweat glistening on her forehead after we had walked for about an hour through the underbrush.

“Come on, it’s hard to pull off something like that. Sure, a 4.9-star rating can be bought, but all those reviews, comments, and photos? Those were from real people,” I lectured, adjusting my diving goggles on my forehead, “at least, as real as bot profiles come.”

"Yeah, yeah, I get it. This is the least known, but most famous beach in the country.”

We had stumbled upon this remote beach on our Map App, and it sure did look like the photos: Rocks and greenery flanked the bay on both sides. The ocean transitioned from turquoise to cerulean the farther it was from the shore. On the horizon, the ocean melded with the sky to become all-encompassing. A gateway to heaven.

Halfway between the beach and the horizon sat the skeleton of a wrecked ship. The rusted metal of its hull in stark contrast to the cyan of the ocean. It must have run aground on some rock.

The beach itself was in a crescent-shaped bay. Peeking out of the shade of the canopy was a dilapidated wooden hut. On its front hung a crooked sign that said “Bar”.

A soft onshore wind combed the leaves of the trees.

“I’ll check out the bar. The reviews mentioned toilets,” I offered.

“Alright, I’ll find us a nice place to stay,” Sarah said, before she trod off towards the treeline. The beach was completely empty, so she could pick wherever she wanted. I know she’s not good with limitless choices.

The so-called bar was all boarded up. It said something akin to “private - do not enter” in the local language I didn’t understand. The signs helped. Nobody would serve us booze there.

The toilets were glorified mobile toilets locked by an already busted padlock.

I took a peek inside and was surprised that they were clean. Well, not “clean” clean, but barely used. No point deduction here.

I joined up with Sarah. She was still debating where exactly to put our towels. We had the whole beach to ourselves, might as well pick the best spot, I guess.

“Well, the bar’s closed. But we still can have Sex on the Beach.”

Sarah rolled her eyes in feigned annoyance, deeming my joke unworthy of a verbal response.

“Wanna go for a swim?” I tried again.

“Finally, I thought you would never ask.” Sarah groaned. A reference to how we started dating, she liked to toss it around as an inside joke any chance she gets.

Then Sarah threw off her clothes and ran into the water. Skinny dipping is way more fun in the daylight.

After our swim, we wrapped ourselves in towels and walked along the surf.

We tried to find interesting shells, stones, or animals. There were some crustaceans, but nothing in the shells department. There were a lot of chalky, hollow stones. They seemed lighter than expected and contributed much to the color of the beach. I didn’t know why, but they gave me an odd feeling.

Sarah kept glancing toward the wreck in the bay.

“I’d love to go snorkeling there,” she said as we sat down.

“It looks a tad too far out to swim, dive, and return. You gotta have a boat for that.”

“It’s not that far.”

“I don’t want to get Tetanus from some rusty wreck. I also don’t know what kind of currents are out there.“

“You lazy bum! The least we can do is leave a review.”

Sarah pulled out her phone with a smirk. She carefully angled it down her bare legs, trying not to reveal her lack of clothing. She toyed around with the camera settings and took a photo.

“How’s that?” She showed the pic to me.

A few pearls of water were glistening on her long, tanned legs. They were pointed toward the ocean and the wreck in the background. The rusted metal of the boat was dotting the i of her brown legs. A runway to paradise.

I nodded in agreement.

"I’ll post it,” Sarah said with a wink.

“Great, now I wonder how many of those pictures were taken without clothes on.”

Sarah giggled.

A gust of onshore wind whispered through the wreck.

After that, the day flew by as most beach days do. Bursts of activity, followed by periods of rest.

We finally had that cocktail and dozed off.

\#

I was startled awake by Sarah rolling me off the towel.

“What the fuck?!”

Dusk was creeping in, and the harsh offshore wind howled through the wreck.

Sarah was frantically collecting our belongings. Shoving them in her backpack so forcefully, I was afraid she’d rip it. She seemed eager to run.

“I must go.”

“Babe, why the rush? Is your phone empty? I have a power bank. We can still enjoy the sunset.”

My speech started mimicking the rapid-fire cadence in which our stuff vanished into her backpack.

“I must go.”

She stood up and went to the shoreline.

I grabbed some random stuff and my backpack and hurried after her. On the way, I put on the diving goggles and stuffed the rest into the backpack.

I caught up with Sarah, who was swaying softly in the breeze. The wind was picking up.

I stood next to her as she looked towards the wreck.

"I must go.”

I grabbed her arms and tried to turn her towards me. Her body followed, but her head stayed trained on the ocean.

The sun was low. A fiery eye was peeking through the holes of the wreck. Its skeletal structure a black silhouette against the orange horizon and the blazing sun.

“Sarah, what’s going on?”

“Don’t you hear its call? I must go.”

She never faced me.

Then she turned to move. I tried to stop her. But she pushed me away. I stumbled back. Surprised. Sarah wasn’t strong like that.

She went into the ocean. Fully clothed, fully packed. Without hesitation.

It took a moment to get back on my feet, ditch the backpack, and run after her.

I spotted her head bobbing between the waves.

She was swimming with calm, powerful strokes. Gliding through the water like an eel.

I tried to catch up with her. I fought against the water that seemed to hold me back. I pulled down my diving goggles to shield my eyes from the splashing water. Yelling her name over and over again, my voice sounding nasally from the googles.

I almost managed to get within arm's reach of her when she dived down.

I shrieked in frustration and tasted the saltwater in my mouth.

I spat it out, inhaled sharply, and dove after her.

It was dark, but thanks to the diving goggles, I could see her. Walking calmly on the ocean, weighted down by her backpack. Air bubbles rising occasionally. Her hair alternated between forming a halo and a comet with every step.

My lungs burned. I hadn’t breathed properly, and I was exhausted by the wild chase. The surface had an unnatural orange glow to it. I saw that I was close to the wrecked ship. Its hull almost looming over me. It was way larger up close. I felt small.

I had to go up.

After I broke the surface, I breathed and breathed and breathed and made my way back underwater.

I found Sarah again and trailed overhead. She was walking in the shadow of the wreck overhead. It rested on top of a huge obsidian rock that stabbed through the floor and the water's surface. Along its base, I could make out skulls and ribcages dancing in the current.

Sarah was walking straight towards it. I saw her seize up and shake. Air bubbles ascending to the surface with an air of finality. Sarah keeling over in slow motion like astronauts on the moon. Her hair like the tail of a falling star.

I saw her lifeless body bobbing along the floor towards the pile of bones.

I dove deeper, but the pressure in my ears became unbearable. My heart was pounding relentlessly, and my muscles ached. My body demanded air. Frustration escaped my lungs as I was repelled to the surface.

I couldn’t,… I couldn’t reach Sarah.

I must have blacked out. I remember standing on the shore, the taste of saltwater in my mouth, and howling like a madman.

And a soft offshore wind caressing the skeleton of the wreck.
