I've been going back and forth about whether or not to post this update because I know how the first part read, and I know what direction people's minds went, and I'm aware that this part is going to push those people further in the same direction, and I genuinely can't do anything about that. 

So, here's what's been happening. 

The memory thing has gotten worse. Or more? I genuinely don't know which word fits anymore. It started as just sharpness, as I described before. It felt sort of like a superpower at first, you know? Like my brain had finally shifted into a higher gear and just stayed there. Now it's something else. It's started bringing in things I have no business remembering. 

Yesterday I was walking to work, and I passed the laundromat on the corner of my block, which I've walked past maybe three hundred times without a second thought, and I got hit with a memory of what that corner looked like before the laundromat was there—just a concrete lot, a chain-link fence, and a hand-painted sign that said PARKING $2. I've *never* seen that lot before. I moved here fourteen months ago, and the laundromat has been there for at least a decade, from what I was told. I googled it when I got to work, found a local neighborhood blog post from 2013 complaining about the new laundromat being built there and mourning the loss of the parking. When I went to the images, the parking lot looked exactly the way I remembered it.

I don't know what to do with that.

The worst one was the carnival.

There was a flyer stapled to a telephone pole on my walk home, around two weeks ago. It was sun-bleached and half-peeled, the kind of thing you'd walk past a hundred times without registering. I registered it, and to be honest, I don't know why. I stopped and looked at it, and it was just a standard traveling carnival advertisement, faded enough that most of the text was gone, just an illustration of a big top tent and some block lettering that said MARVEL AT THE IMPOSSIBLE and a date that had already passed by several months ago.

And then I was somewhere else.

Almost like a daydream—or, I guess, a more accurate way to describe it would be like a door opening.

I was in a crowd, pressed in close, and the air smelled like sawdust and something sweeter underneath it that I couldn't place, and everyone around me was pushing forward toward something I couldn't yet see, and there was a man's voice carrying over all of it, loud and very theatrical, saying *"ladies and gentlemen, I present to you our Savior*,*"* and the crowd surged and I surged with it because I was in it, I was part of it, I wanted to see, and then I saw.

A cage that was no bigger than four feet by four feet. There was a man inside it. I remember, in painstaking detail, just how thin he was, with a crown of thorns at his scalp, and dried blood along his jaw, his chest struggling.

He looked up at me with recognition. 

I came back to the telephone pole with my hand pressed flat against it and a woman asking me if I was okay, and I said *"yes, I think so,"* and she walked away looking unconvinced, but I can't blame her—I don't think I even convinced myself.

I stood there for a while. The flyer said the carnival had moved on. I don't know where it goes next.

Sasha noticed something was off earlier tonight and asked me about it. I tried to explain the laundromat thing, and she looked at me the way people look at someone when they're deciding whether to be concerned or dismissive, and she ultimately landed on dismissive, which was probably the right call for her own peace of mind, and so I didn't tell her about the other stuff.

About him. 

He was outside our building when I got off work. 

I came out around seven forty-five, and he was across the street, which I'm noticing is becoming a pattern—him, across the street, in the coat, hands in his pockets, watching the building. He wasn't really looking at me specifically when I came out. He was looking at the building like he was trying to memorize it. When my door opened, he turned, and we made actual eye contact for the first time.

I've been trying to figure out how to describe that moment accurately without it sounding like I'm writing a horror story, and the best I can manage is this: it didn't *feel* threatening. It felt like running into someone you knew a long time ago in a place you didn't expect to see them. The recognition felt... mutual. I could tell from across the street that he recognized me the same way I recognized him, which should have been obvious given what he looks like, but the specific quality of it was different from what I expected—he didn't look surprised. He honestly looked like he'd been waiting for me to notice.

I crossed the street. I know, I know, but I was already moving before I finished the thought, and I think part of me needed to close the distance more than I needed to be sensible about it.

He didn't run this time, thankfully. He stayed where he was and let me come to him.

Up close, the wrongness I'd noticed in the news footage was more pronounced. His face *is* my face. The jaw, the nose, the hairline, all of it matching in a way that goes past coincidence into something I can't fucking name—but his eyes were wrong, just the *quality* of them, the way they moved.

He said something before I could say anything. His voice was mine, which I somehow hadn't fully prepared for, for some reason, and he said: "Have you started remembering too?"

For a second, I thought he meant the laundromat, or the carnival, or any of the other things I’ve been trying to pretend are explainable even though I know they aren't.

I said yeah because it was true and because I couldn't think of anything else to say to my own face.

He looked at me for a second like he was trying to figure something out, or like I’d done something he wasn’t expecting.

"Okay," he said, and just that.

We stood there for what felt like a lot longer than I'm sure it was. At some point, I noticed the tree on the corner and the way I could see every single leaf on it individually, each one separate and distinct from the ones around it, I could see every vein, and every color variation, each one its own specific thing rather than just part of a tree. I don't know how long I stood there doing that. I've never been able to do that before.

"How long have you lived here?" he said, pulling me back.

"Fourteen months."

He nodded slowly, and something in his expression shifted, but I couldn't read it.

"What's your name?" I said, even though I already knew, I just needed to hear him say it anyway.

"Ren," he said.

"Yeah," I said.

He looked down at the sidewalk and then back up at me, and I could tell he was deciding something.

"The laundromat corner," he said. "You remember what was there before."

It wasn't a question.

"It was a parking lot," I said, "with a chain link fence and a hand-painted sign."

He nodded again.

"The carnival," I said.

I could see his jaw tighten just slightly. "Yeah."

"What is that?" I said. "What the fuck is that memory?"

"I don't know," he said, and the thing that got me was that it honestly sounded like he meant it, as if he'd been sitting with the same question and hadn't gotten anywhere either.

I wanted to ask him a hundred things. I had a whole list in my head on the walk across the street, but standing in front of him, in front of myself, whatever, every question felt too large to start.

"Are there more of us?" I asked because that felt like the one that mattered most.

He looked at me for a moment. Then his eyes moved past my shoulder.

I turned around.

Across the street, maybe forty yards down. A man was standing completely still under a streetlight that kept flickering on and off like it couldn't commit. In the same coat, with his hands in his pockets.

My stomach dropped.

When I turned back, the other Ren was already a few steps away, moving down the sidewalk.

"Hey," I said. "Hey, hey, wait—"

"I know," he said, without turning around. "I know, I know."

And then he was gone around the corner, and I was standing alone on the sidewalk with whatever that was across the street still watching me.

I didn't go after him. I looked back across the street.

The third one hadn't moved. He was just standing there looking at us—well, at me—with an expression I couldn't read from that distance.

A car passed through the intersection. When it cleared, he was gone.

I stood there for a while.

Then I went home and told Sasha work was fine, made dinner, and tried to let everything go flat again.

Around eleven, I went to the window for no reason I could explain. Halfway down the block, on the opposite sidewalk, someone was standing with their hands in their pockets. 

He was facing the building.
