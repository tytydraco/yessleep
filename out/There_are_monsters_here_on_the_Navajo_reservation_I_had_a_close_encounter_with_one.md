The first time I heard the story, I was a kid sitting beside the wood stove in my grandmother’s house on the Navajo Nation. Outside, the desert wind moved through the sagebrush, making a sound like something whispering just beyond the walls. My grandmother never liked telling stories after dark, but that night the power had gone out and the house felt too quiet.

She stared into the fire for a long time before speaking.

“Some things out there are not animals,” she said quietly. “And they are not people either.”

I asked her what she meant, the way kids always do when they think a story is coming.

But she shook her head.

“If you hear something calling you outside at night,” she said, her voice barely louder than the wind, “no matter what voice it uses… do not answer.”

At the time I thought she was just trying to scare me.

Now I know she was trying to warn me.

My name is Daniel Begay. I grew up **on the Navajo reservation** near the red mesas outside **Window Rock**, where the desert stretches for miles and the sky feels bigger than anywhere else in the world.

Out there, nights are different.

Cities never truly get dark. There’s always light somewhere**,** streetlights, cars, buildings glowing in the distance. But on the reservation, when the sun drops behind the mesas, the darkness becomes something else entirely.

It feels alive.

The stars burn cold and bright overhead, and every sound travels across the open land. A coyote howling three miles away sounds like it’s standing right behind you. Wind against the trailer walls can sound like footsteps.

You get used to it.

Or at least you think you do.

My uncle Raymond was the one who used to laugh about those stories.

He was a big man with a voice like gravel and a habit of working late. He watched sheep for one of the ranchers near the canyon, driving out into the desert most nights to make sure coyotes weren’t getting into the herd.

Whenever my grandmother warned him about wandering too far after dark, he’d grin and say the same thing.

“Grandma, the only monsters out here are hungry coyotes.”

Then one night he didn’t come home.

They found his truck the next morning on a dirt road about ten miles from the house. The engine was still running, the driver’s door wide open like he had stepped out in a hurry.

His flashlight was lying in the sand.

But Raymond was gone.

The police searched the desert for three days. Helicopters circled overhead, their blades thumping against the silent sky while search dogs dragged their handlers across the dry ground.

On the third afternoon they found him.

His body was half a mile from the truck.

The official report said animal attack.

But when my grandmother heard the news, she didn’t say anything for a long time.

She just sat at the kitchen table, staring at the wall.

Then she whispered something in Navajo under her breath.

I only caught two words.

**Yee Naaldlooshii.**

A week later my grandmother had to travel to visit family near **Gallup**.

She asked me to stay at her house while she was gone and feed the sheep.

I didn’t mind. I’d stayed there plenty of times before. The house sat alone about a mile off the highway, surrounded by empty desert and a rusted wire fence.

Peaceful, most of the time.

That night started normally enough.

The sun sank behind the mesas, turning the sky red and purple before the darkness swallowed everything. I finished feeding the sheep, locked the gate to their pen, and went inside.

Around midnight the power went out.

The lights flickered once and then the entire house dropped into darkness.

It wasn’t unusual. Storms sometimes knocked out the lines even if you couldn’t see the weather from the ground.

I lit one of my grandmother’s kerosene lanterns and sat in the living room scrolling through my phone until the battery finally died.

When the screen went black, the silence inside the house suddenly felt heavier.

That’s when I heard the footsteps.

They came from somewhere outside near the fence.

Crunch.

Pause.

Crunch.

At first I thought it might be a stray dog wandering near the sheep pen. Coyotes sometimes got curious and walked close to the house.

But these steps sounded wrong.

Too slow.

Too heavy.

Then I heard a voice.

“Daniel…”

My heart skipped.

The voice was familiar.

It sounded exactly like my uncle Raymond.

For a moment my brain tried to make sense of it. Maybe someone else was outside. Maybe someone from the ranch had come looking for something.

But Raymond was dead.

I had been at his funeral three days earlier.

“Daniel,” the voice called again, closer this time.

“Come outside.”

My stomach twisted into a tight knot.

I stood up slowly and walked toward the living room window. The lantern shook slightly in my hand as I pushed the curtain aside and looked out into the darkness.

At first I didn’t see anything.

Then something moved near the fence.

The moon slipped from behind a cloud, and the pale light spilled across the yard.

That’s when I saw it.

It was standing on two legs.

But the shape of its body was wrong.

Its arms hung down far too long, almost touching the ground like they didn’t belong to the rest of it. Its back was bent at an unnatural angle, making it look both tall and crouched at the same time.

The moonlight touched its face.

Or what used to be a face.

It looked like a coyote skull stretched tight with thin gray skin. The jaw hung slightly open, revealing teeth that were far too human.

And its eyes,

They reflected the moonlight like an animal’s, glowing pale and cold in the dark.

But there was something intelligent behind them.

Something watching me.

The thing tilted its head slowly.

Then it smiled.

“Daniel,” it said again.

The voice was still my uncle’s.

But the mouth didn’t move right.

The sound came out warped, like someone imitating human speech without understanding how it worked.

“Come outside.”

I stepped back from the window so fast the lantern nearly slipped from my hand.

Outside, the creature began walking toward the house.

Crunch.

Crunch.

Crunch.

Its footsteps circled slowly around the walls.

Then came the knocking.

Three heavy thuds against the front door.

THUD.

THUD.

THUD.

“Daniel,” the voice said softly.

“I know you’re inside.”

And suddenly it wasn’t my uncle’s voice anymore.

Now it sounded like mine.

Exactly like my own voice speaking from the other side of the door.

A cold wave of fear washed through my chest.

Then the thing laughed.

At first it sounded human.

But halfway through the laugh it twisted into something animalistic, a high, broken coyote scream that made every hair on my arms stand up.

The footsteps moved away from the door.

A moment later I heard claws scraping against the metal siding of the house.

Then something climbed onto the roof.

Slow.

Deliberate.

Each step above my head made the ceiling creak.

I stood frozen in the hallway, gripping the lantern while listening to the scratching sounds move across the roof.

The creature stopped directly above my bedroom.

Then it began dragging its claws across the metal.

Not random scratching.

Slow, careful lines.

SCRAPE.

SCRAPE.

SCRAPE.

The sound continued for nearly a minute.

When it finally stopped, I noticed something on the bedroom window.

Written in the dust on the outside of the glass were two words.

COME OUT.

My breath caught in my throat.

Above me, the roof groaned as the creature shifted its weight.

Then it spoke again.

But this time the voice had changed.

Now it sounded like my grandmother.

Soft.

Gentle.

The exact tone she used when I was little.

“Daniel… shíyázhí… it’s okay.”

My little one.

Hearing those words nearly broke something inside me.

For a split second my body actually started moving toward the door.

Then I remembered what she told me a few weeks ago.

*“If you hear something calling you outside…”*

*“Do not answer.”*

A thick drop of liquid suddenly hit the floor beside my foot.

I looked down.

Dark red blood dripped slowly from the ceiling.

Drip.

Another drop fell.

Then another.

Something shifted heavily on the roof above me.

The voice came one final time.

Now it sounded like every voice I had ever heard layered together.

My uncle.

My grandmother.

My own voice.

“Daniel…”

A long pause followed.

Then the whisper came through the ceiling.

“We can see you.”

I didn’t move.

I didn’t breathe.

I stood there in the hallway until the lantern flame burned low and the sky outside slowly began to turn gray.

Sometime before sunrise the footsteps moved away from the roof.

Then they disappeared into the desert.

When the sun finally rose, I forced myself to open the front door.

Tracks covered the dirt around the house.

Human footprints.

Animal prints.

And something else.

Long dragging marks like something with arms too long had crawled across the ground.

But the worst thing was the roof.

Carved deep into the metal above my bedroom were words scratched with something sharp.

The same words that had been on the window.

Except now they were different.

Now they read:

YOUR NOT SAFE

I moved away from that house a week later.

From that moment on, I never dared to utter the word because in our culture, saying it can attract them.
