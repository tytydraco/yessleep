My parents worked in this small expatriate compound in Saudi Arabia, a little no-name place that’s not even on the maps anymore. Trust me, I looked, but all that’s left are the outlines of foundations where our houses once stood. It’s all gone now, bulldozed, left to the desert and buried like some unmarked grave.

About fifteen families had lived there and fifty or so bachelors working as engineers, doctors, and nurses. I knew everyone and everyone knew me. It was small as small towns got, a handful of square miles of desert with a station to process and pump crude oil from the Eastern Province Oil Fields up into Lebanon, and rows of manicured lawns around company homes.

I was about ten years old, I think, when I woke up one morning and found my mom and dad standing by the glass door to the yard. I remember my dad holding my mom close with one arm. It was dark outside. Not like night, but shrouded like when a sandstorm hits and the haze gets into the houses.

Grasshoppers were crawling all over the glass … locusts like the biblical kind. We’d gotten them before, pushed by storms in Africa across the Red Sea and through gaps in the western cliffs of the Arabian Peninsula. I’d seen them twice before, and I hated them. Imagine your every step crunching them under your shoes and sending dozens flying all around you. I used to run home when they came. It felt like torture, walking slowly.

Thing is, these weren’t the locusts I remembered. The color was off, ash and soot like they’d just spun off some campfire, and their rasping tickling the insides of my ears. They crawled on the sliding glass door by the dozens, and in the yard by the tens of thousands and beyond that, there were millions of them buzzing around. They crawled over our manicured lawn and sat on tree branches, eating anything they could find. Thinking about them still makes my skin crawl even now.

When my mom finally saw me, she rushed over and told me, “It’s okay, it’s just locusts.” But every time I tried looking past her, she’d pull my face back and say, “They’ll be gone soon, okay?” I kept staring at the bugs, though—hard not to—and I kept wondering…

…why’d they look like they were disintegrating?

I spent the next couple of days just pestering my parents with those kinds of questions.

“When are they leaving?”

“When they’re full,” my mom said. She tried to be brave for me, but I could read her like a book I’d read a thousand times. She was scared, and so that meant I was scared.

“Where’d they come from?”

“Sudan or Egypt,” my dad said. I don’t know if it bothered him much, and he just sat on the couch, reading one of his dog-eared Louis L’Amour Westerns. If my dad was a book, I didn’t know what language he was written in. He didn’t hate me or anything, but he didn’t relate to me either.

The television wasn’t working either … something about locusts interfering with the short-wave antenna from our little transmitting station. This was before home computers and streaming were a thing. So, with nothing to do but read, fear gave way to boredom and I wandered back to my room to find some comic or toy I wasn’t bored of yet.

But I couldn’t help myself either, even though my mom had told me to stay away from the windows. I left the lights off like a spy and pulled the curtains open a bit, just so I could study  those freaky locusts leaving trails through the film of ash covering the glass. The strangest part was that their wing membranes had been eaten down to the cobweb scaffolding or whatever it’s called. How did they even fly? And why did they make sounds that made my inner ear itch? It while watching them that I saw something moving in my backyard. I just about jumped out of my own skin.

I couldn’t make the figure out but it looked like one of the old bedu who worked in camp maintenance. We called him ‘Old Hajj,’ but I couldn’t really tell with his shemagh wrapped tight under his chin.

He was walking around in the storm of locusts, just casual like this was an everyday thing, scooping up handfuls of them and shoving them in his burlap sack. The bedu ate grasshoppers, it was like a delicacy, but nah… that was all kinds of ‘nope’ for me. Couldn’t do it, even when I’d seen Old Hajj roasting a few grasshoppers over a small campfire once. He’d offered me one, but I shook my head, too scared to try them. I remember him laughing and then digging into a grasshopper with his teeth, eating it like how I used to eat chicken wings (I stopped eating them once I made that connection).

Anyway, I’m rambling. So, I see who I think is Old Hajj in my backyard and I start pulling on the curtains when suddenly, the locusts just burst into flight. I back away and watch him walk up to the window, all this ash and dust blowing all around him. Now he’s just standing outside, looking down at me. Then—get this—he runs his hand along the window sill, scoops up a handful of locusts…

…and jams a fistful right into his mouth.

I screamed, I mean Jesus, who wouldn’t, and I run into my parents as I’m trying to run out the door. I know I wasn’t making any sense to them, but by the time I got them to look out the window, the old bedu was gone.

After that, I slept on the couch. Forget my own bedroom with that window near my bed, I was done. Anyhow, another day of this storm and my dad gets a call from work. All hands on deck—they needed all the doctors and nurses to come in to treat people. Nobody told me what for, but my mom and me watched him get bundled up and run for our Buick that was parked on the street. We could barely see him, his windows were covered in bugs, and when he turned on the windshield wipers, they just crumbled and smeared the glass. My heart sank as I watched him drive away. We weren’t close, but that didn’t mean I didn’t need him.

Not long after that, the telephone rings again, startling the crap out of us—we were on edge. My mother started talking to someone in Arabic, and I could hear the worry creep into her voice. I was afraid it was about dad, and by the time I got up to see what was happening, she was already putting on her coat and covering her head in a scarf.

“Mom? Is dad--?”

“Your father’s fine.” When she took me by the shoulders, I knew that meant she *really* wanted me to listen. She said Myriam, one of the mothers, was hurt and that she needed to go help her.

“What happened to her.”

“She hurt her ears, I think.” But even my mom didn’t sound certain about that.

“How?”

“Honey-honey, I don’t know, okay? I have to go help her and her children.” I could hear the edge in my mom’s voice. Everyone in the camp trusted her and all the kids loved her. She was their teacher and mine since kindergarten. I guess that meant I had to share her as mom.

“But I want to go with you,” I said, probably whining knowing me, but she was firm in a way that told me not to argue. Then just like that, she left me alone in the house, in my little bubble, surrounded by that steady whirring that scratched deep inside my ears. It was driving me crazy.

Anyway, I was alone for a long while. I can’t say how long, but I kept hearing things. I’d be flipping through Tintin or Asterix & Obelix, and then I’d hear something like a cry or a shout. I’d stop reading … I’d just listen … but I’d hear nothing else. Did someone get scared? Were my friends running around outside? That was crazy, so I stayed put.

Finally, I got up to go pee. I’d kept ignoring the pressure on my bladder for too long, but when I opened the bathroom door, the droning just spiked in pitch and I swear I could suddenly feel it vibrating against my brain. I jammed my fingers into my ears and hummed, but nothing worked. I almost ran from the bathroom, but I was about to piss myself. When I lifted the toilet seat, though, I--I remember screaming. Yeah, just screaming until my throat seized up and I tasted copper.

There’s no pretty way to say it—the toilet was filled with locusts. I don’t mean they were crawling around inside the bowl, I mean they filled it … like a plug. I’ll never forget that, the way they just surged up and overflowed the rim. It was like they were waiting for me to find them, and then \*boom\* they were suddenly flying out from the bathtub and the sink drain too.

I bolted, slamming the door shut behind me but they were coming up the kitchen sink and more were squeezing underneath the door. Oh, and here’s the best part about all this. I was barefoot. I should have worn slippers; scorpions were always problem where I lived and the red and yellow ones could kill me, but I thought I was safe in my own home.

I swatted and batted and stomped on the ones that jumped me, and I felt them crumble. They filled the air with swirls of dust and ash. I was still screaming too, my voice pretty raw, but I couldn’t stop myself. I never felt terror like that before, but that was before I heard glass shatter. I froze. I was terrified. It sounded like it came from my parent’s bedroom, which was down at the end of the hallway. I stared at their dark room, and when I heard that rasping rise in pitch, I knew the bugs had gotten in somehow. I knew it the second before they came bursting through that open door, hundreds of them just… vomiting out into the corridor.

*How did they get in?* That’s what you’re wondering right? I thought the same thing, but then I heard more glass breaking, this time in the kitchen, and I saw a rock go skidding across our tile floor along with broken shards of glass.

Someone had thrown the rock. Someone was letting them in.

Locusts were flying past the curtains and filling the inside of my house with that terrible grating noise. Covering my ears didn’t do a damn thing, and I couldn’t think, I couldn’t see; I’m pretty sure I was hysterical because the windows just seemed to suck in more bugs and ash. I wanted to run into the closet, but then I heard the cracks of footsteps on broken glass coming from my parent’s room. Someone was inside. Someone was standing in the doorway to their room, staring down at me.

So … I ran out of the house … in my pajamas …  barefoot.

I wish I could tell you I had some kind of plan, but honestly, I really only came to my senses when I dropped to my knees in the sand, exhausted some time later. My sides ached, my legs were numb. I’d been running for a while through the storm and my muscles had turned to jelly.

I kept my eyes shut and my mouth cradled inside my elbow to breathe. The locusts kept flying into me and disintegrating, turning the air all soupy and gritty. They were crawling all over me too, trying to get inside my ears, hanging off my eye lashes. I shook my head hard, trying to get them off me. I screamed and batted them, even slapped myself good a couple of times, but they just wouldn’t stop.

All I could taste was that ash and I kept trying to spit out the bitterness. I could still feel their tiny little legs in my mouth too, and to make things worse, I was lost. The camp was clusters of homes and community buildings, but there was still the Arabian desert between everything. I probably wouldn’t end up outside the compound, we were surrounded by a chain link fence, but I was still somewhere in a long stretch of hardened desert.

Out here were horned vipers and yellow scorpions and those hairy camel spiders that creeped me out. They could all kill me with a bite and here I was, barefoot. I felt utterly crushed and I’m not ashamed to tell you that I started crying. But I started walking again, head down, hoping I’d find the big hill where my house sat.

It wasn’t easy. Every inch, the grasshoppers flew into me or hit each other and just \*poof\* into powder. I kept coughing ‘cause of all that ash in the air. It reminded me of Pompeii. My parents had taken me there one year and I just staring at those bodies covered in volcanic ash, frozen. Was that going to happen to me? Would they find me cocooned and all fetal?

My feet also hurt. There were small pebbles and stripped scrub, and every step I took crunched the grasshoppers under me. I could feel them crumble, but sometimes, I stepped on a sharp or spiney edge of them.

I kept crying out for my mom, but I had to clamp my lips shut when bugs landed on them. I … bit into a couple of them by accident and they collapsed in my mouth. It tasted so gross and nasty that I spit them out and eventually, I just started running again. I didn’t care in which direction; I just didn’t want to be out here anymore.

As I wandered around, I kept hearing things in the distance. Voices, screaming, whispers. It was hard to tell with all the hissing around me. My therapist later said that it was my brain trying to find order in the chaos. I don’t know about that. I know when something is whispering to me. I know when something’s got a voice.

I cried out for my parents again, not thinking that maybe I didn’t want to be heard, but I was so scared. That’s when I spotted someone in the ash and dust, lurching my way. I should have kept my mouth shut.

It was Old Hajj, I’m sure of it, but he was covered in a skin of locusts over his robes.

“A’kul,” he said and I swear it sounded layered like the grasshoppers were all talking to me. He’s reaching for me too, his fingers covered in soot and crawling with locusts, his mouth all filled with ash and insect legs that were still moving.

“A’kul. A’kul. A’kul,” over and over again. I wasn’t good with languages like mom but I knew enough Arabic to know that meant: “Eat, eat.”

Then he grabbed my wrist and I started screaming and pulling at his arm, trying to get him off me. That’s when my fingers kinda collapsed into his skin and his hand and forearm just crumbled. He started turning into ash between my fingers. So I ended up on my ass again, and he keeps moaning ‘A’kul,’ like he doesn’t even see that his left arm is just gone.

His foot goes next, and he falls to one knee and then the other. His robes are also turning to dust and I see the cracks in his charcoal-dry skin, like all the water got sucked out of him. Then he breaks apart … right infront of me, his legs shattering, then the rest of him breaking.

I’m sitting there, staring at this broken jigsaw puzzle, a piece of his face with just one eye staring at me and still blinking. The corner of his mouth was moving, trying to say something, and his fingertips were sticking out of a pile of dust and jerking a bit.

I don’t remember when I got up and started walking again, just this sense that I no longer cared that I was stepping on bugs, no longer feeling the stones and insect legs under my bare feet. I was disassociating, I know the word for it now, and I didn’t even realize I’d stepped on hard ground until a couple of feet in. I looked down and slowly realized that beneath the ash and bodies of locusts was asphalt … a paved road! I didn’t believe it. Maybe it would take me to the small hospital where my dad worked; or maybe the community center with its pool tables and mess hall. Or maybe past the houses with my friends. Somewhere, anywhere, was better.

I started walking on the side of the road when a cannon shot shattered the monotony of the droning. The boom dropped off suddenly, but I recognized it. It was the cannon that they normally fired during Ramadan when the day of fasting was done and the night of feasting started for the Muslim families. They usually fired that at the main gate to the compound and that’s where the hospital was. That’s where my dad was!

I started crying—well, bawling—but then heard the locust hiss rising sharply all around me. They got real loud, and the locusts ahead of me suddenly took flight in a wave, like a moving wall, racing toward me from the direction of the cannon blast down the road. It was like watching a ripple racing out, the bugs disturbed in a massive chain reaction that sent them careening into one another and bursting.

I dove to the street and tore up my knees and elbows on the asphalt. I barely managed to cradle my head just before the wall of insects and ash hit me. They battered into me, disintegrating, and I got enveloped by them. As I lay there, like those bodies I saw in Pompeii, the cannon kept firing again, and again, and again.

They eventually found me by the side of the main road, half buried under drifts of ash, almost catatonic. I don’t remember any of that. I don’t remember the days after that when I wasn’t eating or saying anything. I do remember some of my hospital stay, like when my friends visited me, but it took months before I stopped coughing, stopped feeling them on my skin all the time. My parents even cut my nails short because I was scratching myself bloody.

I found out that mom and dad had raced back home to try to find me when they realized that some people had gone crazy and were breaking windows to let the locusts in. Other folks couldn’t take the droning—'the whispering' they called it—and stabbed their own eardrums … like Myriam. My parents told me that several Bedouin including Old Haff had also gone missing, but I knew where he was. He was in the storm and in the sand. And he was in my lungs.

The next time the locusts came, they were bright and green … alive. But the camp still fired the Ramadan cannon a few times, just to make sure. We didn’t really talk about it, except once, in a kinda roundabout way when my father told me that Saudi Arabia had been a shallow sea once and that the oil had come from dead marine life. The desert always buried its secrets, he said, and once in a while, “it surrendered them too.” I guess the locusts were one secret that almost buried me, and it wouldn’t be the last one I’d experience.
