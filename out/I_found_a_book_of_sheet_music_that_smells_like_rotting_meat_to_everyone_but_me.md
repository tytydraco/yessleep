First off, I have a nerdy confession: I like to sing acapella music. Well, that’s not exactly the whole truth, because sadly, I have an even nerdier confession: I like to sing *barbershop quartet* acapella music.

Last Friday, something caught my eye at my local Goodwill: It was a tall, flat book. The title read, *12 CHARMING SONGS for MEN* in a stock font from the fifties—like the label printed on the brown packaging of an M.R.E. in the Korean War. Listed on the back cover were the 12 charming songs, many of which were standard tunes that I've been singing for the past ten years throughout the hobby. I flipped through the weathered pages and as I got to the last song, *You’re the Flower of My Heart, Sweet Adeline,* I was surprised to find that it wasn’t the standard arrangement that barbershoppers have sung for over eighty years.

I looked at the top right corner of the page where the arranger of the music is typically listed, and the name was *A. Lucente*. I had never heard of this person before, and I have a pretty good mental list of all the big-name, classic arrangers. I looked at the back cover again and realized that this song wasn’t even listed.

When I took the songbook to the front register, the lip-pierced cashier grimaced.

“That book reeks,” she said. “I almost threw it away this morning.”

I picked up the songbook, and took a big whiff. It vaguely smelled of old lady perfume—rosy and powdery, but nothing out of the ordinary and certainly not bad enough to throw in the trash.

“Weird… smells fine to me,” I said.

“You can’t smell that?” she asked. “It smells like pure death. Like literal rotting meat, dude. Are you *sure* you want that? I could throw it away right now. We get leftover stuff like that from estate sales every other day.” She lifted a small black trashcan from behind the counter.

“Nah, this is a pretty cool piece of history, actually,” I said.

She shrugged and sat the trashcan back down, then scanned the book.

“See, this is from the Barbershop Quartet Society,” I continued. “It was printed back in the fifties, and this arrangement at the back isn’t—”

“That’ll be twenty-five cents,” she interrupted.

I paid my quarter, and as she reached to the side of the cash register to grab the receipt, I sneaked a glance down at her stained, blue polo and could see the outline of her bra through her shirt. I felt a chill, and realized what I was doing, darting my eyes to the fluorescent lights above us. I hadn’t caught myself staring like that since I was a teenager. My fiancée, Jamie, would’ve smacked me in the back of the head if she saw me doing that—and I would’ve deserved it.

The second I took the receipt, I stepped outside. The cool, autumn breeze cut through my navy, oxford shirt and I zipped up my cardigan in response. When I got to my car, I pulled the songbook out of the bag and looked at it again. It was so neat to have a piece of history in my hands.

I couldn’t wait to record myself singing this arrangement; to hear all four parts together. The entire drive home it was all I could think about. I walked through the door of my apartment, threw off my cardigan, kicked off my shoes, and immediately went to my computer to start setting up my mic. I *had* to hear how this baby sang. I was so excited about this song that didn’t even notice that Jamie was in my apartment. She was curled up on the leather couch on her phone, the lit, living room fireplace crackling in front of her. I paused, noticed the fireplace in my hindsight, stopped what I was doing, and backtracked into the living room.  
“…Hi?” Jamie said, with a *nice to finally be noticed* air to her voice. She wore a fuzzy sweater, and her caramel hair was up in a messy bun. Her round glasses reflected the blue light from her phone and the flickering orange glow of the fireplace.

“Oh, hey. I didn’t know you were coming over,” I said, sitting down next to her with the songbook in my lap. I leaned in to give her a kiss, but she pushed me away right as our lips had just touched.

“Oh my god! What is that smell?” she asked.

“What smell?” I held my hand up to my face to check my breath.

“Jesus Christ, it’s absolutely *foul,”* Jamie said, jumping up from the couch to the blue rug in the center of the living room. She held her hands up in front of her and turned her head to one side, as if this smell had personally accosted her.

“I don’t smell anything. It can’t be that bad if I can’t even smell it,” I said. “Look, I got this from the thrift shop.”

“Ben, I swear that book smells like death,” she insisted as she leaned backwards, trying not to breathe. “Do you remember when I told you about that time when I was a kid? When a rat got stuck in our school’s HVAC and burned alive? It smells exactly like that. Like, a decomposing animal and burning hair.”

“I don’t smell it! Come here and sit down. I want to show you this.” I patted the seat to my left on the couch.

“Okay,” she said, as she moved to the couch. “You seem really excited about this.” Her glasses rose up on face as her nose scrunched and her eyebrows furled against the smell. I quickly filled her in on my thrift store escapades.

“Anyways, what I wanted to show you was this really neat arrangement of Sweet Adeline,” I continued. “If you look at the back, it’s not listed, but if you flip to the back of the book—” I rolled the pages under my thumb, their age had left them soft and pliable. As I did this, the—to me—nonexistent smell wafted over to her, and she groaned.

“I can’t, dude,” she said. She stood up, walked to the front door, and slipped her wool-socked feet into my black Nike slides.

“What? Where are you going?” I asked from across the room.

“I gotta step outside and get some fresh air,” she said. “That is awful. Just… awful.” She unlocked the deadbolt to the door and stepped out onto the front stoop.

I got up from the couch and followed her, the seventy-year-old yellowed pages of *12 CHARMING SONGS for MEN,* still in my right hand. When we got outside, the air felt even colder than earlier at the store. My thin, dress socks left my feet unarmed against the cold from the concrete of my front stoop.

“It doesn’t smell.” I fanned the book at her.

“You—*hurghh*—you *have to* get rid of that… oh my god—*hurrr…*” she said between gags.

  
“Why are you acting like this?” I asked. I knew she didn’t really care about this aspect of my life, this hobby, but it felt like she was just trying to get away from me. She could at least amuse my rambling for five minutes.

*“What?”* she said with a pained look across her face.

“Just say you don’t care about my hobbies. Do you want me to just not have any hobbies?”

“Ben, what the hell are you talking about?” she asked, straightening back up while still fighting the unapparent smell of the book. “Where is this coming from? I never said you couldn’t have any hobbies.”

She was right. This did kind of come out of nowhere, but I couldn’t seem to back down now. I had never fought like this before—in such an ugly way.

“All you ever want is control,” I said. “You want control over what I say, over what I do, over my finances.”

“Ben, stop it!” she yelled. “You and I both know that you’re bad with money. You said it yourself. We have a wedding and honeymoon to save for. And I have never tried to control you! I’ve had *conversations* with you about things that would be nice if you changed, but I never *made* you do anything against your will, because that’s what a healthy relationship *is!* I never, *never* said you couldn’t have any hobbies…and of all the hobbies…*barbershop?* Why would I keep you from something so unimportant?”

“‘*Unimportant!’”* I yelled. “You can’t even appreciate the *little* things I’m into! Jamie, you’re gagging at something that doesn’t even smell. You’re faking it! Why would you go through all the trouble? Why don’t you just leave if you hate my interests so much! Maybe if I—” I realized my voice was echoing off the red bricks of my apartment building. I wanted to stop, but something told me to keep pushing. I couldn’t give up now.  
She interrupted, “I don’t hate all your interests!”

I looked over to the neighboring apartment window. A single slat of the blinds dropped as soon as I glanced. I held my hand to the side of my face to block my lips from the window and whispered, *“great. Now Amber’s watching.”*

“Oh… Amber,” she said.

*“Yes, Amber,”* I continued to whisper. *“Are we really going to bring this up again?”*

“Maybe we should,” she said. “Since we seem to be rehashing all our previous fights!”

“She paid me a compliment. That’s it.”

“It was not a compliment, it was flirting!”

“It was not!”

“There’s a difference between, ‘I like your shirt,’ and ‘You look hot in that shirt,’ Ben, and you *know* that.”

“That’s just the way she talks!” *Why am I even arguing about this?* I thought. *I feel like I’m just being a contrarian—no…no, she just has me on my back foot. I can still win this.* “You know, maybe if you called *me* ‘hot’ now and then, I wouldn’t feel like I needed to get the validation from someone else!”

“What the hell is wrong with you?” Her eyes narrowed and her voice was like microwaved food—cold outside, hot inside. One more bite and I could end up burnt. “Maybe I do need to leave for a while,” she continued as she grabbed the doorknob to my apartment and stepped inside.

“Maybe you do,” I said behind her.

She grabbed her coat and purse from inside the door and huffed off to her car. As she stomped away, my slides that she still wore made the signature *flip-flop-flip-flop* that echoed off the half-empty apartment complex parking lot.

“You can call me when you figure out how you fucked up, buddy!” she yelled to me as she reached her car before slamming the door and driving away.

*Fine,* I thought. *I didn’t want her here anyway. Came over without even texting me. Now I have more time to sing through this arrangement.* I leafed to the back of the book and the mysterious arrangement that filled my thoughts. I took a deep breath of the frigid autumn wind, steadied my emotions, and went inside.

After I got my microphone set up and started a beer, I was ready to sing. I pressed the red record button on my audio software, blew a B-flat on my pitch-pipe, and began to sing the melody:

*Sweet Adeline, my Adeline,*  
*At night, dear heart, for you I pine.*  
*In all my dreams your fair face beams;*  
*You’re the flower of my heart, Sweet Adeline.*

Before I had the chance to listen back or add the three other parts, there was a knock at the door. I went to the spyhole and saw Amber. She wore a violet, cropped puffer jacket with a tight tank-top underneath, and high-waisted blue jeans. Her wavy, black hair went down to her shoulders. She squinted when I flipped on the porch light, and the cold, blue ambient light of dusk that engulfed her became warm from the glow of the bulb. It somehow made her even more beautiful than I had ever seen her before. I turned the deadbolt and opened the door. Another chilled gust of autumn wind rushed into the apartment, and I could smell her perfume: chocolate and rum.

“Hi, Amber! To what do I owe the pleasure?”

“Hey, Ben,” she said. Her concerned voice was low and sultry, and her red lips parted, revealing a perfect, white smile. “I heard some yelling outside earlier. Is everything okay?”

“Oh, yeah, probably,” I said. I had almost forgotten the argument already. I was so focused on the music. “It was just a little argument. Happens sometimes, you know? I think we’ll be fine.”

“Okay,” she said, “I was just making sure. My ex and I used to fight like that a lot. We actually just broke up last week.”

*Hot and single,* I thought. *Lucky.*

“I’m sorry to hear that,” I said. I noticed her eyes roll back into her head for just a split second, and her chest heaved as she took a labored breath. She then pulled her hands out of her pockets and held them in front of her body.

“I will be just fine, Benjamin,” she said. “Say, sweetheart, I know we use the same laundromat.” I noticed her voice had changed. It was pitched a little higher now. Each word floated in the air like a feather and landed sweetly on my ears, yet the language was stilted; it wasn’t her usual prose.

“Yeah?” I replied.

“Well, it might be unladylike to ask this, but—” She hesitated. “I’ve been missing a pair of panties since last week, see, and I wondered if perhaps they got mixed with your wash. Think I could come inside and look around for them, baby doll?”

*Is she flirting with me?* I thought. *She’s totally flirting with me. Jamie was right. Dammit.*

“They have red and black lace,” she continued. “They’re quite small. Might be hard to find, but it would surely be swell if we could find them, Benjamin.”

I blushed. *Benjamin.* No one ever calls me *Benjamin*. Not even my folks. Always *Ben.* I don’t even really like when people call me Benjamin, but I’ll let it slide for Amber. I took a step closer to her, onto the freezing concrete.

“Oh? Ha-ha, um...yeah, maybe…” I said, falling over my words. No woman had ever been so forward with me like this. *Wait…shit. Jamie. What am I doing? Am I really just going to mess around with another woman as soon as Jamie isn’t around? After one little fight?*

“I’m…” I paused. *Man, it’s really hard to say no to her. Her face, her body, it’s…she’s glowing.* She filled my senses. Her perfume was—actually… her perfume was not sexy at all anymore. Smelled like roses and baby powder. I could’ve sworn it smelled like the best cocktail I could ever drink just a minute ago. Now it smelled like a nursing home. “I’m sorry, Amber. I can’t. I love Jamie, and I can’t hurt what I have with her. I-I’m actually just about to call her to apologize.”

She leaned in and put one hand on the nape of my neck and the other on the small of my back, then pulled the side of my head to her face. I was so shocked that I didn’t know how to act. Her perfume was nearly unbearable now.

“Take me, sweetheart,” she whispered in my ear. “Take me now, dearie. Before you miss your chance. Before the wedding starts.” I grabbed her shoulders and pushed her away.

“Amber!” I yelled. “What the hell is wrong with you?” I jumped back into the doorway and grabbed the handle. I just stood there, dumbfounded by her brazenness.

“Your loss, shit-for-brains,” the crass words fell out of her like gravel. It didn’t sound like her voice at all. What was once sultry, then stilted, but light as a feather, was now full of dust, reedy and hollow. She walked back to her apartment, but before she slipped behind the door, she flipped me off. The door slammed and the resulting shockwave knocked down a picture frame on the mantle in my living room. I closed my door and walked up to the fireplace where the frame dropped and noticed the fire was beginning to die. I went to set the *12 CHARMING SONGS for MEN* on the coffee table so I could pick up the frame and—*was I holding the book the whole time?* I flipped to the last song again. Sweet Adeline, my Adeline. Honestly, such a beautiful arrangement. I walked back to the office to finish the recording. I’d take care of the picture frame later.

When I sat down at my desk, I remembered I had finished a take of the melody on the audio software. I raised the volume on my speakers, then pressed the space bar to play it. I could hear the slight crackle of the fireplace in the background, the B-flat from the pitch-pipe, my anticipatory breath, and—

*Screaming.* Bloodcurdling and real. You know, one is never quite ready to hear the sound of a woman screaming in fear. It’s louder and scarier than it is on a movie or TV show. When my voice was supposed to begin on the recording, I only heard this screaming instead. Not my voice. Not *me* screaming. This was the scream of a woman. A woman in pain. You could hear her entire soul in the scream. It didn’t sound like it was coming out of my speakers, but like she was right in front of me. In shock, I pressed the space bar again to pause the recording, and the screaming ceased.

“Je-e-esus Christ!” I blurted out in shock. My body was full of adrenaline and my heart pounded. It *looked* like my singing on the screen. The hills and valleys of my voice were there visually in the waveform, because if it was this woman’s scream, there would be no ups or downs, just one solid, jagged bar across the entire audio clip.

I hesitantly started the clip over, holding my hand over the volume knob on my speakers, just in case. Again, I heard the crackling fireplace, the B-flat on the pitch pipe, the breath, then the intense scream. I went to save the file, but before I clicked save, I felt compelled to try recording it one more time. I deleted the first take and pressed record for take two.

*Sweet Adeline, my Adeline,*  
*At night, dear heart, for you I pine.*  
*In all my dreams your fair face beams;*  
*You’re the flower of my heart, Sweet Adeline.*

Again, the guttural scream. Ear-piercing scream. I wondered, was this the scream of a woman scorned? Brutally hurt or even murdered? I wish I knew. Every time it played, it hurt my heart. I hoped she was okay.

I googled the problem to no help. Best I got was maybe this was radio interference being picked up over my microphone, but there were no radio towers nearby. And who would broadcast a woman screaming for several minutes straight? I troubleshooted the problem until midnight, then decided to go to bed. Whatever this screaming was, I’d figure out how to get rid of it tomorrow. Saturday morning. That was the day I got this song sung. The barbershop masses—the twelve of them that would even care about this—deserved to hear it.

I was so tired that I barely had the energy to take off my clothes. I collapsed into bed still in my slacks and oxford shirt. I had downed a couple more beers over the night while troubleshooting, and they were doing a good job. I instantly fell asleep and began to dream.

I was on the upper deck of a riverboat, leaning against the rail. The steam from the smokestack left a long trail behind us. I wore an orange, corduroy suit with a yellow carnation in my lapel, and a mint julep was in my hand. When I looked across the deck, I saw Jamie. She looked different. She wore a black cocktail dress, the skirt billowed with tulle fabric. On her arms were long, red silk gloves. She held a small bouquet of deep burgundy dahlias, and her hair was short and platinum blonde. She was the spitting image of Grace Kelly.

“Hello, sweetheart,” she said as she approached me.

I tried to speak but I was at a loss for words. My heart pounding. This felt…wrong—well, wrong *and* right. I couldn’t tell why just yet. I downed a swig of my cocktail and the cold ice hit my teeth. I tried talking again, but her beauty overwhelmed me.

“Aw, cat got your tongue?” she said. “How ‘bout we work out that tongue tie, huh?” She kissed me. Her lips were supple, yet cold. Although we were out in the open on this boat, the more I kissed, the further I wanted to go. I began to kiss down her neck, but suddenly it felt as if the riverboat was moving at mach speed. The wind rushed, and the balmy afternoon shined as bright as the noonday sun, overtaking my sight.

As the light died down, I opened my eyes and saw Amber in front of me. I was no longer kissing Jamie, but Amber. The deck had transformed into a choir room of a church. Colorful light from a stained-glass window filled the room and danced over the ivory keys of an upright piano. Piles of sheet music covered the top of it. An organ played a hymn softly in the background of the sanctuary of the church.

My corduroy blazer was now a black tuxedo jacket. The yellow carnation still affixed to the lapel, but now I wore a baby blue cummerbund and bow tie. Amber was in a simple, baby blue dress with white lace accents—a bridesmaid dress. The baby blue matched my bow tie and cummerbund. She set her small bouquet—now white roses as opposed to the dahlias—down on one of the choir pews as we left each other’s embrace.

I tried to ask what was going on, but before I had the chance, she grabbed my face and kissed me again. Then I heard a scream. *The* scream from the recording. I looked over Amber’s shoulder, and across the pews was Jamie under an arched doorway. She was wearing a cream-colored wedding dress with long sleeves of lace flowers, and a veil flowed down the hallway behind her. She was beautiful and terrifying, and her scream tore into my eardrums.

“Oh, shut up, Adeline!” Amber yelled. She placed a long kitchen knife in my hand. “Do it! Do it now,” she commanded, pointing me at Jamie. “Just like we talked about, Johnny.”

*Who’s Johnny?* I thought. I tried to ask, but the light of the room became a blinding white, and as it lowered back, I was there right in front of Jamie, with the knife in my hand and in her chest. Blood seeped from the blade of the knife onto her dress.

*Oh my God! Jamie, no!* is what I wanted to say, but couldn’t. I couldn’t speak. I opened my mouth and nothing came out. She just stared at *my* chest, screaming, and not a thing could break her concentration. Her eyes were fixed. I wanted to apologize to Jamie, to tell her I was going to help her—to save her, but even if I could, it would’ve been like bargaining with a lifeless mannequin that *never stopped screaming.* 

Her arm rose towards me. She had been so still that it startled me when she finally moved. She reached towards the yellow carnation on my lapel. Her hand grasped at the flower, then…past it, phasing through my lapel. Through my white dress shirt, and through the undershirt beneath that. Her grasping hand pierced the skin of my chest, and I silently cried out in pain. Her face began to radiate light, and I could no longer move. It felt as if my feet were glued to the floor. Her face glowed like a streetlamp on a dark, winter night. Every synapse yelled at my legs to move, yet they refused to listen. She continued to scream, now shining as bright as the sun, breaking into my ribs and grabbing at my beating heart. I hissed in pain and my eyes squinted against the brightness of her face, the knife still sticking out of her bloody chest.

Her skin began to pale and wrinkle, dehydrating and shrink wrapping to her skull. Her screaming jaw now barely hanging on by a thread of flesh. Suddenly, she disappeared, and in front of me was the sheet music-covered piano. On top of one of the stacks was the *12 CHARMING SONGS for MEN.* I could still hear her screaming, but it was muffled. I opened the book to Sweet Adeline, and it was suddenly just as loud and as bright as when she stood in front of me. My heart, still beating fast, began to rip out of my chest on its own and float in front of me. The yellow carnation, now caked in blood, was stuck to it. Bits of bone, blood, and sinew poured out of the hole in my chest. As it floated towards the open book, I winced in pain. The light of the book was blinding. It was so bright that I could see it through my clenched eyelids. I tried again to shout for the only person I have. The only person I love.

“JAMIE!” The yell echoed off the walls of my bedroom. Her scream had stopped. It was quiet. Softly, I could hear my ceiling fan whir. I unclenched my eyes. I was awake, sitting up on my elbows and covered in sweat, chest heaving, heart beating uncontrollably. It beat so fast that it hurt. I let my body drop back into the bed.

“Jamie…” I said again, under what little breath I still had. My eyes welled with tears, but after my breathing slowed and the pain in my chest had subdued, I sat up and went into the living room. I wouldn’t be able to fall back to sleep if I tried. I looked at the big clock hanging in the living room: it was three in the morning.

It was bitter cold in my apartment, so I decided to relight the fire that Jamie built earlier. As I walked to the red-bricked mantle, I saw the picture frame on the floor. I picked it up. It was a picture of me and Jamie. It was a selfie from our third date—when we decided to make it Facebook official. I silently chuckled at how serious that felt at the time. Our hands were arranged in a heart; my hand on the right, hers on the left. Our eyes agreed with our hands; filled with love…love and hope. The frame had cracked at one corner, and the whole thing was an inch away from falling apart in my hands. I gently sat the picture frame on the couch for safe keeping and began to rebuild the fire. After a bit of coaxing, I got it roaring again.

I grabbed a folded throw blanket off the back of the couch and laid it over my lap as I sat down. I picked up the frame and laid it on top of the blanket, the damage to the frame was irreparable. I began the process of pulling the small, black metal tabs at the back to get to the picture inside. Suddenly, I heard something to my right. On the seat of the couch, was the *12 CHARMING SONGS for MEN.*

“What the…?” I murmured as I placed the half-broken picture frame back down on my lap and picked up the book. Automatically, like a gust of wind had commanded it, the pages flipped open to Sweet Adeline and I began to hum the tune. I couldn’t stop myself from singing. I was not in control.

*Mmmm-mmm, my mmm,*  
*At night, dear heart, mmm-mm.*

I sang it over and over. Each repetition louder than the last. I sang that cheery song so many times that I lost count, but I know that the sun began to rise, and I could not look away from the sheet music. My eyes, my voice, my body were totally out of my control.

*Sweet Adeline, my Adeline,*  
*At night, dear heart, for you I pine.*  
*In all my dreams your fair face beams;*  
*You’re the flower of my heart, Sweet Adeline.*

The room began to unnaturally brighten, not by the sun nor from the fire. That’s when I heard it again: the scream. In my periphery, I could see a figure. Although I couldn’t see for sure, I knew it was the bride. *Adeline.* The incorporeal wisps of her dress and veil floated in the air, and what I could see of her skin was white, blue, and gray.

I still sang. I sang louder than ever now, practically chanting, yelling, rather than singing. She floated towards me with an outstretched hand while I sat frozen on the couch. She reached at my heart and it began to race. I shivered. She was so cold. Her icy form drowned out the heat of the fire. Her hand passed in front of the music, but I did not stop singing. I *couldn’t* stop singing. Her hand went through my oxford shirt and into my chest, just like my nightmare. It hurt before, but it hurt for real now. She did not tear the skin; she phased through it, leaving no marks to my chest. She raised her hand and pulled me up by my collar bone and the surrounding muscles. I still sang,

*SWEET ADELINE! MY ADELINE!*  
*AT NIGHT, DEAR HEART! FOR YOU I PINE!*  
*IN ALL MY DREAMS! YOUR FAIR FACE BEAMS!*  
*YOU’RE THE FLOWER OF MY HEART, SWEET ADELINE!*

She continued to lift me up from the couch, and the items from my lap hit the floor. First, the broken frame and picture, then the blanket. When the frame hit the rug, I heard the glass shatter not just onto the floor, but into my mind as well. Through the pain, past the singing, past the screaming ghostly bride. I heard the glass fracture, and then saw the face of my beautiful fiancée, Jamie, in my mind.

I remembered Jamie as I first saw her. We were in a bar, and the red and green neon lights illuminated her hair like a halo. She was laughing with a mutual friend, and her slightly crooked smile gave way to dimples in her cheeks. 

I could sense that the screaming Adeline did not care for my nostalgia. Her hand had moved violently back to my heart and she began to pull. I started to sweat profusely. The pain was more than I had ever felt, and I could feel my life being siphoned away. Every nerve and blood vessel in my body warned me that I was closer to death by the second, but with the last bit of strength I could muster, I lifted the rosy-perfumed pages and chucked them towards the fireplace. 

The book flew to the fire like a bird migrating south to a warmer climate, straight through the glowing spirit of the bride. When the book hit the flames and caught alight, the bride stopped screaming. As I finished the final line of the song, she cried out to her murderous fiancé, seventy years in the past. 

*“JOHNNY, HOW COULD YOU?”* Her reedy decomposed voice sobbed in betrayal.  
“I’m sorry they did this to you, Adeline,” I groaned. Her hand squeezed my heart and I screamed, “You didn’t deserve it!” My voice was hoarse, made thin from her song. One last time she pulled at my heart. I felt as if I was a single thread from death, but like a vacuum, the ashes of the book shot up through the chimney and out into the early morning fog. 

Adeline Lucente, the double-crossed murdered bride, exploded like a grenade blast of blue-green light. Gone. Hopefully at peace. Hopefully forever. My ears rang from the explosion, and though my vocal cords were absolutely run ragged from the singing, I still yelled out in terror. I dropped to the ground onto the blanket and the broken frame. The shattered glass pierced my left knee, yet I felt nothing. The pain from my heart, the adrenaline, had made me numb. I pulled out the shards after I sat back down on the couch. I felt the blood run down my leg and seep through my slacks. 

I picked up the picture of me and Jamie from the floor, and being careful not to smudge it with blood, stared at it, and began to cry. The bride, in her rage, had taken all of my autonomy. She pulled out the worst of me in revenge for her betrayal, but Jamie, *my bride,* broke through the phantom's incantations. If I had listened to her, I wouldn't be sobbing over a stupid old photo. When I was inches from death, Jamie’s name was on my lips. When Adeline threw temptation in front of me, Jamie is who my heart cried out for in the midst of it all, and now that I was free from Adeline’s charms, the only person I wanted to talk to was Jamie. So I walked to the bedroom, picked up my phone from the nightstand, and called her.
