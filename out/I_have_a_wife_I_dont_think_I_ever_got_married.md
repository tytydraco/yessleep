About half of people aren’t real.

They live their lives as if they are. They work at jobs. They go to school. They go through puberty, and break laws and go to prison, and they give birth, and they flirt at you from behind the cash register. But even if they act like they are people, they aren’t. They don’t even know that they aren’t, because they don’t have thoughts. They only act as if they do.

My wife is one of them. I think. I’m not sure yet.

We’ve always been different. When I look at photographs of her from high school, she’s in a cheerleader’s outfit, linked to a football player’s arm. In mine, I wear glasses, a goofy grin, and T-shirts of Ska bands. She loves parties and people. I love alone time and pets. We never should have worked, but somehow, we did. We were always different, but that was never the problem. 

The problem is that all those things about her might not be true at all. She might have never been a cheerleader or gone to high school. We might have never had a wedding, and she might have only existed as of three months ago.

That’s when they came―the fifty percent that isn't real. Three months and counting.

We all went to bed, including you. Before you went to sleep perhaps you were single. You lived in an apartment alone. You had two children.

Then we woke up. You were no longer single. You lived in an apartment with a roommate. You had three children, not two. That was the way it had always been. The world’s population doubled overnight, and nobody noticed.

 Nobody real.

My manager is one of them. Four months ago, I reported to another woman. She works in another division now. Things had to shift to make way for the newcomer.

My neighbors on the right aren’t real either. Four months ago, there was an empty lot. Now, it is occupied by a family that isn’t a family. They don’t feel. They only appear to. Their children don’t play games. They only pretend to.

Like the others, they wait. They prepare.

It is coming. Very soon their moment is coming.

Sometimes, I lay in bed, next to my wife, listening to her breath. Is she asleep? I think. Or is she only acting like she is? Are her labored breaths real or does it only seem like she is there, not existing, a thing beside me that I didn’t used to know. My heart will shrivel to a cold, hard stone. I will lay there the whole night, only pretending to sleep too.

I watch her.

As she eats dinner. As she watches TV. I study her face when she isn’t looking. The curve of her nose, every flutter of her eyes. *Something*. There must be something there, a sign or signal that will confirm my suspicion.

But of course, there isn’t. She’s too adept. All of the fake fifty percent are. Every moment, every breath they take, is controlled, measured, designed to mimic how a real person would behave. There is never a sign. There will never be one until it is too late.

And so I tell her what I know.

It is the only thing left to try.

We sit in bed, and I set down my book. I look at the thing that isn’t my wife, even though I wish it were. 

“You’re one of them,” I say. “Real people don’t remember how you all arrived. You’re one of the fifty percent.”

She smiles at me sadly. Even though it is only the semblance of emotion, a manufactured mask, a trick designed to appear like sadness, it still nearly convinces me. 

“I am,” she agrees. “You’re right. You’re right twice. Real people don’t know that we exist, not one.”

“I do.”
