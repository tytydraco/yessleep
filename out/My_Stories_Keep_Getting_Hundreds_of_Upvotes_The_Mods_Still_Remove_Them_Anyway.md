I didn’t start writing horror because I thought I was talented. I started because I was tired in a way that office work creates slowly, almost invisibly. I’m in my late thirties now, which means I exist in that strange middle layer of corporate life where you are responsible for everything but rarely respected for it. Middle managers spend most of their days absorbing other people’s problems and translating them into polite updates. Your boss expects progress reports, your team expects protection, and the systems above you expect metrics that never quite match the reality of the work being done.

After a while it becomes draining in a way that isn’t dramatic enough to complain about. Nothing terrible happens. Nothing explodes. You just spend ten hours a day managing friction between people who will never remember your name when the project ends.

Writing became a small escape from that. At night, when the house was quiet and the email notifications stopped for a while, I started imagining scenarios where the frustrations of the day turned into something darker. A colleague who constantly undermined me might end up trapped in an office that literally consumed its employees. A meeting that dragged on for hours might become a room no one could leave because the agenda kept expanding.

Eventually I started posting those stories on a horror subreddit. I didn’t expect much from it. A few strangers reading something I wrote felt like enough.

The first story did better than I thought it would. A few hundred upvotes appeared overnight. People left thoughtful comments about the ending. Someone said the idea had stayed with them long after they closed the page. It felt strange and satisfying in a way I hadn’t felt about writing in years.

Then the post disappeared.

The moderator message said the story had been removed because it wasn’t a **Scary Personal Experience**. The narrator was too detached from the events. I shrugged it off and rewrote the story so the narrator was clearly experiencing everything firsthand.

The revised version gathered hundreds of upvotes again before it was removed.

This time the message said the narrator might not realistically be able to post the story after surviving the events.

I rewrote it again.

That was when I began noticing something unsettling about the process. Each time I adjusted the story to match the rule they cited, a new rule appeared. It felt less like moderation and more like the guidelines themselves were shifting slightly each time I tried to follow them.

Eventually I decided to write a story directly about myself.

I thought if the narrator was literally me, describing an experience in the first person, there would be no ambiguity left.

The first attempt was about love.

Not in the romantic sense people usually write about, but in the suffocating sense that happens when someone gives another person too much emotional control over them. In the story I imagined what it would be like if loving someone too intensely caused something physical to happen, like slowly losing pieces of your own body without noticing until it was too late. The metaphor became literal. The narrator was so consumed by love that she eventually lost her head.

It gathered hundreds of upvotes in less than an hour.

The removal message arrived shortly afterward with the same polite tone as before. The explanation said the narrator would not realistically be able to describe the experience after the events had occurred.

I rewrote it immediately, explaining that the narrator survived and was recounting the event afterward.

The edited version was ignored entirely. The moderators never reinstated the post.

So I tried a second story, still writing as myself.

This one was about time. About what happens when someone stays emotionally attached to a person who has already left them. In the story the narrator begins losing track of time completely. Hours disappear, days blur together, and eventually she can no longer tell whether she is remembering the past or living in it. The horror was subtle, the kind that builds slowly until the narrator realizes she hasn’t moved forward in her life for years.

That story did even better. It collected several hundred upvotes and a surprising number of awards from readers who said the idea felt disturbingly real.

The moderators removed it anyway.

This time the message said the story was **not frightening enough**. If the experience were genuinely terrifying, the narrator would appear more panicked.

I read that line several times because it bothered me more than the others.

It didn’t say the story lacked tension.

It said the narrator wasn’t afraid enough.

The implication seemed to be that if the experience were real, I would sound more distressed.

I decided to lean into that idea and wrote a third story about a commenter who seemed to follow my posts. The narrator began noticing the same username appearing again and again in the comment sections, asking questions that felt oddly personal, questions about where she lived and how she wrote and why certain details appeared in her stories.

As the story progressed, the narrator began suspecting that the commenter knew things about her real life.

The post reached hundreds of upvotes within the first hour.

Then it vanished.

The moderator message that arrived afterward felt different from the previous ones. The tone was still polite, but the wording had changed slightly. It said the narrator’s fear still did not appear convincing enough. If the situation were real, the narrator would be more frightened.

The message ended with a sentence that made me uncomfortable in a way I couldn’t easily explain.

It suggested that I should consider how someone would behave if they were truly afraid.

At that point I felt strange continuing to write these things, but curiosity kept pushing me forward. I wrote another story about a place I imagined called the Devil’s Diner, a restaurant where people unknowingly traded pieces of their souls for the lives they wanted. The narrator gradually realizes that she has been returning there for years, each time leaving something behind that she can’t quite remember losing.

By the end of the story she suspects her soul is already burning somewhere else, somewhere deeper than the diner itself.

The post gathered hundreds of upvotes again.

It disappeared faster than the others.

The moderator message that arrived afterward was the most unsettling one I had received so far. It didn’t reference any specific rule. It simply said that if the narrator truly believed her soul was in danger, the fear would be far more obvious in her writing. The message suggested that the narrator should sound more distressed if the situation were genuine.

I remember sitting at my desk reading that sentence over and over again because it felt less like feedback and more like a challenge.

It was as if someone on the other side of the screen was waiting for the stories to become more real.

For the past few days I haven’t been able to stop thinking about that. I keep opening the writing document and staring at it without typing anything. My mind keeps circling the same thought, which is that the moderators seem to want the narrator to become more afraid each time.

Almost as if fear itself is the requirement they’re trying to verify.

The strange part is that it’s starting to work.

I find myself thinking about those messages while I’m driving home from work or sitting in meetings where people are arguing about quarterly projections. I notice my hands shaking slightly when I open the subreddit now. I catch myself rereading the guidelines in ways that feel less like editing and more like trying to understand a set of rules I might accidentally break in real life.

And the thing that scares me the most is how familiar the feeling is.

Because if I’m honest with myself, rejection has always been the thing I fear most. Not failure in the dramatic sense, not danger or pain, but that quiet corporate kind of rejection where someone simply decides your work doesn’t matter enough to keep.

Those moderator messages feel exactly like that.

Except they don’t stop.

Tonight I received another message.

It didn’t explain a rule violation. It didn’t mention a specific guideline.

It simply said that I was getting closer.

Closer to what, it didn’t say.

I sat at my desk for a long time after reading it, feeling a kind of pressure in my chest that I couldn’t quite explain. It was the same feeling I get right before presenting something at work that I know my boss will reject, the same feeling I used to get when waiting for someone I loved to reply to a message that never came.

Eventually I realized something that made the situation feel even worse.

If the moderators want the narrator to sound genuinely frightened, then the only way to satisfy that rule is for the narrator to actually be afraid.

Which means this story might be the first one that finally meets their guidelines.

Because right now I’m sitting here unable to stop thinking about those messages, wondering whether they’re reading this while I write it and deciding whether my fear sounds convincing enough yet.

And the worst part is that if this post disappears too, I’m almost certain I’ll try again.

Not because I want to.

Because I’ll need to know if the next one is finally frightening enough.
