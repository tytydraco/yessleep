So for context, Ashley and I had been friends since middle school when my mom and I moved to a new place after the divorce. Ashley was our next door neighbor and we had a lot awkwardness before we clicked. I still remember the first time we actually managed to hold a conversation it was about some video game. I never suspected she would be into horror as much as I was since she was a pretty put together individual. Whereas I was a troubled teen who was still shoplifting to make up for the lack of a proper father figure in my life. 

Anyways, Ashley and I were very close and shared everything. She was always there for me, sometimes I'd "run away" from home after a fight with my mom and she would sneak me in through the garage to her own room until she came crying to Ashley's parents. Though, Ashley always had difficulty coming out of her shell she was never shy with me. She usually told me about her insecurities, how tired she felt all the time, how she had trouble making friends, how much her mom and dad fought, how she worried about her appearance, or how she sometimes didn't see a future for herself 

As you can tell, we were inseparable. Were. Because on my 19th birthday we had a huge fight. I'll spare the details since it was private but it wasn't pretty. The party was held at my house, a small one with just her family and mine. After the little ceremony of blowing candles and cutting the cake Ashley and I went to my brother's room. Since it was my birthday, we hoped to pester him for some alcohol that we knew he was keeping hidden in his room. 

Well, he kicked us out. So Ashley and I went back to my room to have another sleepover. Usually, we'd just turn on a horror movie and complain the gore wasn't realistic or that the writing was bad. This time she pulled me aside and started asking me about the moving boxes in my room. I told her I was going to move out for college and I had been gathering my things early. She snapped then and said I was going to abandon her. The argument began to get worse and she ended up slapping me at which point I told her to leave. 

That was the last time I saw her. Her parents called the police by the time I woke up the next day. When I found out she went missing, I freaked out. The police questioned me, I told them everything that had happened. I knew the information was needed to find her. 

They didn't find her in the first 48 hours. The guilt was eating me alive, I knew about her depression, how much she depended on me. It dawned on me how she must've felt when she realized I'd be leaving and she'd have no one. I couldn't even face her parents knowing I was the one who drove her to running away or worse. 

I didn't even look for her, I thought that, because of me, she had committed suicide. I knew it was pointless to look for her. I just gave up and fell into a deep depression myself. When the police stopped giving updates by the end of the first months it was only solidified in my brain that I had killed her. Because of my ambition. I gave up on college and became a shut-in. 

By the 90th day... I wasn't even leaving my bed unless my bladder couldn't take it anymore. When the doorbell rang, I wasn't going to get up to open it. I just couldn't bring myself to, but I was the only one home so I dragged myself out of bed. I can't tell you the relief I felt when I answered the door. Ashley, looking just like she did the night I kicked her out of my room was standing there, shyly peering through her cracked glasses at me. 

After I took her in, gave her water, asked her if she was hungry, cried into her lap like a misbehaved child, I called her parents. Of course, everything didn't go back to normal immediately. As much as her parents were shook-up, they were walking on eggshells around her to ensure she wouldn't run again. They put her in therapy, began keeping a close eye on her through me. Since they didn't really know what had occurred that night, they still trusted me. 

I was so happy, it felt like the weight I had been carrying was lifted off my shoulders. We began having sleepovers nightly, I assured her I would never leave her side, not even for college. I made sure she felt loved, cared for, secure. 

One night, she asked me to look through our photo album together. One we had created together and kept just for the two of us. There was a page, I don't remember what her teeth looked like before but, in the picture they were slightly crooked. Ashley had an open bite, it was one of her biggest insecurities. She got braces on the second year of high-school to get them fixed and had to wear retainer every night after the braces were off. When she initially returned, I didn't pay attention to her teeth. She asked me to get her a glass of water and I did, but when I returned and she smiled at me, a big teethy grin, there was a gap between the two rows of teeth and the album was tucked back into my bookshelf.
