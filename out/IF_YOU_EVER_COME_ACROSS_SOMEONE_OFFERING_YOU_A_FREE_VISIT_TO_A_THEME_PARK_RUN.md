I didn’t expect all of this to end like this, but here we go. This story happened just a few hours ago and it deeply marked me.

I am just a broke young woman who works extra shifts where I worked to pay the bills and support myself, but with my freedom I had also lost my sense. And I hadn’t realized that yet.

It was just an ordinary Thursday afternoon, I was coming back home earlier that day, around 6 p.m. I was already arriving home, finally a day without extra work or stress, but my new neighbor, Karen, was standing in front of her house.

Karen was a young woman in her 20s and successful, well I suppose she is successful, since she lives in a three-story house, it was one of the few mansions in that simple place.

— Hey, neighbor! Can we talk for a moment?

Karen approached me with a gentle smile, a pair of sunglasses covering her honey-colored eyes, and she was wearing an outfit in neutral tones. I hadn’t noticed anything unusual up until then, since she had always seemed like a very friendly person ever since she moved there, although we hadn’t known each other for very long. I usually trusted women very easily, so I simply responded with a light smile on my face, even though the exhaustion was still visible all over me.

— Hello, neighbor. Of course. What is it about?

She gave a slight smile, never showing her teeth, it was always that same soft and wide smile… Too wide.

— I bought this house with a new purpose.

Karen paused and crossed her arms, still smiling in that same way, as if she only knew how to make that expression, then she continued speaking:

— I built a theme park and needed someone to test it.

My only reaction was to give her a friendly smile, my mind already wandering through various princesses, superheroes, or famous series. I had always been fascinated by theme parks, so I decided that if she was really inviting me to visit this theme park and test it for her, I would definitely accept. It’s not every day that you receive an invitation like that, especially from someone so rich who simply buys a house to build a park inside. I hadn’t thought about the consequences—like, how could someone build a park inside a house? Was that really possible? Even if the house was big, it didn’t seem large enough to hold a theme park. But I didn’t think about that, I was so excited about the idea of visiting that place for free that I didn’t think much about the consequences or anything like that.

— That means that…

I began, but Karen interrupted me.

— You would be a great candidate to test it. You can be the first.

Now that I stop to think about it, her tone was actually strange. She didn’t ask if I wanted to or if it was my choice—she just spoke and I accepted, as if I were in some kind of trance, or maybe I was just very naive. Having grown up in the countryside, I was used to knowing most people and trusting them, just like my family did—blindly. That was definitely a flaw I needed to work on, but that woman seemed so… trustworthy. Why shouldn’t I trust her, after all? She was just my kind neighbor asking for a favor—and a very good one at that.

— I would be happy to be the first.
Great, shall we?

Again, it was a bit strange, since she didn’t ask if I was available at that moment or suggest scheduling for another day—after all, it was only Thursday—but as I said, I was excited and exhausted from several days of extra work, so I just went along with everything in a submissive way. She began to walk away deliberately, and I followed a few steps behind her, analyzing the structure of the house. Looking more closely, it didn’t seem to have only three floors, but I couldn’t prove that from the outside. There weren’t many windows, something I had never really noticed—after all, I used to pass by early in the morning when it was still dark and return very late, and since I hadn’t lived there long, it didn’t make much sense for me to worry about analyzing someone else’s house. I kept looking, trying not to imagine things that weren’t there, especially since that neighbor seemed very kind. Why should I distrust her? Back in the countryside, people were like that too, and nothing bad had ever really happened to me—I mean, nothing bad had ever happened to me.

When we finally reached the front of her house, there was a normal entrance, just an ordinary home, so I simply went in with her. She guided me to the kitchen on the first floor. I found it strange that she had mentioned a theme park, but it was supposedly located at the back of the house. There were a few other rooms before reaching the supposed theme park. Well, it was a small kitchen compared to the size of the house, but I thought it might be some kind of large space, maybe she used it to receive clients. It still hadn’t fully sunk in that the place was completely strange, and that a theme park should be built in a more open area, not inside such a closed-off mansion like that.

Well, I was in the kitchen when I turned around and realized she had locked the front door twice. The key turned, and she placed it inside a random vase near the entrance. Once inside, she seemed more relaxed, as if daylight affected her negatively. Her expression was pale—I just assumed she might have a vitamin deficiency or didn’t like the sun, just like me. The strangest part was how she hid the key so discreetly, but I only caught a glimpse of it. I thought it was just for safety—after all, big cities can be dangerous, right?

She invited me to sit at a small table in the center of the kitchen. Behind me, there was a small window with extremely resistant glass, but there wasn’t actually any way to open it or get out through it. I didn’t think much of it, and she quickly distracted me with casual conversation.

— Working a lot, dear?

She kept smiling at me, as if that alone would make me trust her more. You tend to trust people who are always smiling more than those in movies, right?

— Quite a lot.

I replied vaguely, since I didn’t want to share too many details about my job—it was enough spending so much time there, I didn’t need others knowing everything I did.

— Do you have any family in the city?
— No, no one. They all live in the countryside, far from here.
— I see. And do you talk to them often? In what way?

I tried to pause and think about whether I should answer such a personal question, but before I realized it, the words were already coming out of my mouth.

— No, just a few messages. My mother doesn’t really use her phone much, and my father even less.
— That’s a shame. And they never visit you?

At that moment, I started to feel nervous. Karen asked her questions so naturally that I couldn’t help but answer. She was standing next to an electric kettle, heating water for tea.

— I usually visit them.

I replied casually, as if it were nothing. She nodded slowly, but it was clear she was analyzing everything I said.

— Well, let’s have some tea. Then I’ll take you to see the theme park. You’re going to love it.

She turned to the sink and poured boiling water into the two cups, then added the peppermint tea bags. I didn’t notice anything unusual at first, but a few things felt off. The taste of the tea didn’t seem normal at all—I only took a small sip because it was still boiling hot. I had been drinking tea for many years, ever since I replaced coffee with it, so I knew very well what a plain peppermint tea should taste like, and that definitely wasn’t it. It was too green and tasted like cinnamon. Wait, cinnamon? I had read somewhere that a cinnamon taste could mean something bad… Like, she could have put something in my tea and then used cinnamon to mask it. Or maybe she just liked tea with cinnamon and sugar. And with a lot of peppermint in it. I started to get suspicious, so I took another sip and felt a strange premonition. Then I just pretended I was drinking it, but there was nowhere to pour it out. Karen kept staring at me, her eyes slightly wide. So I let out a small “ow” sound.

— What happened, are you feeling something?
— I have a sore throat. The tea burned a little, I’ll stop here. It was delicious, but I think I’d better not keep drinking it, my throat really hurts.

Karen narrowed her eyes slightly, as if she were evaluating whether I was telling the truth, then forced a smile and stood up.

— Shall we go see the theme park then?
— Let’s go.

I stood up and followed her to the back of the house, still with a strange feeling. The house smelled like mold and old things, different from how it looked from the outside. The walls were peeling, and everything looked older and older. Unlike the kitchen, which was painted in white and looked presentable, the back area was in a much worse condition. I decided not to pay too much attention to it—maybe she was planning to fix it, after all it was a new house and the previous owner was an elderly woman who didn’t seem to care much about those things. But then again, I didn’t really know her well, so I couldn’t say for sure.

— I’m thinking of charging R$900.00 for a visit. Rich people like buying expensive things just to show off, right?

Karen let out a dry, shrill laugh. It made every hair on my body stand on end. I started to question whether that woman was really trustworthy. She was acting more and more sinister the deeper we went into the house, and I still hadn’t seen a single window. That house really didn’t have windows—only thick glass, as if it were resistant to punches, bullets, or something like that.

When we finally arrived at the so-called park, I couldn’t believe that was really the place. First of all, there was nothing familiar there—only strange figures and images of princesses and superheroes that simply didn’t exist. I used to follow that world of cartoons, series, and movies a lot, and I had never seen anything like that before.

The princesses didn’t exactly look like princesses—their smiles didn’t seem real, and their dresses were always dirty in the images. The others, who I assumed were some kind of bizarre superheroes, were wearing strange clothes; some had long arms, others had weird shapes, and many were life-sized, although some were just stuck onto the rides like stickers she was showing me.

— This is the first attraction of the theme park. You can win anything—a princess ring, a notebook, or some random object. You can try it if you want.

She looked at me and pointed to a lever a few steps away from me, so reluctantly I approached it and pulled it. A bizarre, shrill sound—almost like a human scream—came out of the machine. It’s worth mentioning that there were about 20 machines on one side and 10 on the other, so you walked through the middle and could choose any machine to pull. I chose the third machine on the left. What I got wasn’t anything too special, I think. It was a ring with a bright red stone, the kind you see in movies with precious gems—but of course it wasn’t a real gemstone, it couldn’t be. I smiled lightly at the object I had won and looked at Karen.

— Look how beautiful this is.

Her eyes sparkled in a strange way, as if that object might bite her at any moment. She shrugged and said that if I didn’t want it, since it was too simple or childish, I could leave it there—but I decided to put it in my pocket and keep going.

After walking for a few more seconds, we reached another part of the theme park. There, you played a kind of matching game.

— Here, you just need to match the pieces. And you’ll win makeup.
— That sounds really fun.

I played the matching game, and that one seemed more normal. I just had to match some shoes and random makeup items on the buttons, and everything would be fine. After playing for a few minutes, the machine suddenly shook and spat out a lip gloss and an eyeshadow, both dark—the gloss was cherry-scented with a very light tone.

Karen didn’t say anything, she just kept that smile that seemed more and more crooked and strange. She turned around, probably expecting me to follow her, and that’s what I did. After walking again for a few more seconds, we reached the last attraction, and if the others hadn’t made me suspicious of that woman, this one definitely did.

— This one...You just press there.

She pointed with her finger, which now seemed longer than it should be. I pressed it, and a drawer opened in front of me on that machine. Inside, there was a small plush dog. I stared at it, confused. Karen kept pointing for me to press again, so I did—and suddenly the little dog turned into a bunny plush as if by magic. She smiled, satisfied, but I decided not to touch that toy.

— Great, now let’s go to the next floor.

At that point, I no longer wanted to go anywhere with that woman. She was clearly sinister, and I was scared. I thought about protesting, about running away, but the door was locked—and if she was dangerous, it probably wouldn’t help. Maybe it was all just in my head. I needed to think positively. I always tried to think that way, and that’s exactly why I kept putting myself in questionable situations.

We reached a staircase at the end of the house. It was extremely dark, and it was hard to see anything since there was no light. I was nervous, but I went up with her. She climbed too fast, and I was left behind. As I placed my foot on the last step of that small, dusty staircase that smelled strongly of mold, I stopped. I heard a noise. I saw a man on the balcony of the house—how did that upper part look so small compared to the lower one? I didn’t understand. I didn’t know, and I didn’t have time to think clearly at that moment.

She must have gone up faster because she saw that strange man there—but that man might have been my salvation. Somehow, he had something in his hand, and then I heard a loud sound. It wasn’t exactly a gunshot, but I saw something golden—like a projectile—fly quickly toward Karen. It was a small golden ball that he threw at her, and when it touched her skin, she screamed, and smoke came out of her arm where it hit.

She already looked completely different from the kind woman I had met. At that moment, she cast a bizarre look at the man, trembled slightly, and stepped back. I didn’t know if that man was normal or if he was some kind of magical creature like Karen probably was.

Immediately, I stepped back. I didn’t know what to do—I was lost and nervous. But when I reached the end of the staircase, I came face to face with Karen, who was smiling as if nothing had happened. It was humanly impossible for her to have moved that fast, right? Unless she was something truly sinister.

I needed to get out of there, so I pretended someone was calling me. I took out my phone and faked answering it. She didn’t seem to understand much about technology—or maybe she understood too much.

— Oh, really? I’m excited to see you, Mom. I’m here at my neighbor Karen’s house.

Karen, who had been approaching me, stopped abruptly when she heard my words.

— What?
— My mom is at my house—she came for a surprise visit!
— Oh, stay a little longer, let’s go to the attic and see the last things.

I immediately pulled away when she tried to grab my arm and said I was in a bit of a hurry and needed to get home quickly. Without warning, I ran—she moved like a shadow right behind me. I realized why when I caught a glimpse of her moving with the shadows. I grabbed the key from the pot, unlocked the door, and ran out of the house, feeling much lighter outside and far safer.

It was already past 7 p.m. when I finally breathed fresh air again. When I reached the gate of Karen’s house, I took one last look at her. Her eyes no longer looked human—they were red—and her smile stretched from ear to ear, but with one detail: there were fangs.

She didn’t run toward me. She just waved in a sinister way.

I ignored it and went to my house, locking myself inside, praying every prayer I knew—to every god possible—asking for protection.

I told this story briefly to my friends and my parents. They said they would come here, but they would only arrive the next day. What was Karen? Was the purpose of that park to attract lonely people? Or people who like spending money on unique things and would be easy targets? A feeling of unease took over me. I felt the ring I had taken from her house in my pocket and looked at it. It was a beautiful red stone, but I panicked as soon as I noticed a red light coming from inside it, like a chip or something. I don’t understand much about technology, much less what that creature was capable of creating. I threw it out of my living room window into her yard. I don’t know where I got the strength to do that, but the object flew there, and soon the sound of some giant creature with four or more legs echoed nearby, followed by a loud, dry laugh in the middle of the night and scratching sounds on my roof, as if it could climb up there.

I stayed on the phone with my mother the whole time, panicking at every sound. My house was tiny and had very few places to hide.

The laughter is getting louder, and the sound of that thing scratching my roof is only increasing. My parents were already on their way, the call was failing, and I decided to write this as a record. I’m sitting in the living room, watching through my tablet camera outside. Finally, the creature came down and is now in front of my door, knocking.

Knock, knock, knock.

— Neighbor, is your family there too? I don’t smell any human besides you. Sweet and old. The kind I used to love long ago.

The camera didn’t show my neighbor anymore, but a creature. It no longer looked vampiric with red eyes and fangs. Now it was completely deformed, like a giant spider imitating a human. The head was still the same—teeth and reddish eyes—but the body was that of a spider. It could break down my door at any second.

This is my final record.

My parents are coming, but the call dropped. 

And that thing is scratching my door and calling me with a hypnotic voice.

The door won’t hold.

Neither will I.
