I was in shock. I was for the whole night, the paramedics told me that it started at that moment.

When they left. 

You don’t have to experience or witness extreme violence to feel shock. The kind that sticks to your body, makes you immovable can come from anywhere as long as it’s a:

“An extreme upset to the norm.” As one of them put it. 

I say all that, but I was a witness. It just didn’t feel the same as two people you care about, who are no longer themselves, wandering into the cold night. 

I got my bearings but it was too late, they were halfway up the driveway. 

I ran out of the house. They walked as if they didn’t know each other, spaced apart in the order they left, with no effort from either to walk side by side as they so often did. 

At the end of our drive way, by the population sign which sat just on the right side of our property was a police car. 

Thank god. I ran towards it, past my grandparents with the hope they could help or at the very least call for it. 

It was across the centre of the road, skid marks lead to it’s front and back wheels. Both doors were open but there was no one inside.  

I looked down the road, towards the direction I assumed the car was heading. Further into town.

I saw two figures, one walking fairly normally the other with a dragged limp. I ran towards the pair.

“Police?! Hello! My Grandparent’s need help. Do you have a phone! Their having an episode and need medical help”

As I got closer I got a better look at the normally walking officer. His left arm was dripping blood. I slowed my approach as I noticed.

“Officers?” 

They both ignored me and kept walking, so I decided to speed it up and ran in front of them. I finally got a better look at the two.

I saw the source of the blood on the right one’s face, the first layer of skin on his left cheek had been ripped away, blood leaked from his face, dripped down his neck and soaked his left sleeve.

The other’s foot was at a ninety degree angle, twisted at the ankle, each step pressed his foot down into a further crippling injury. A portion of his ankle bone had pushed through the skin, which made like scrapping noise against the concrete. 

“Jesus christ! You need to sit down!” I demanded, walking backwards in front of them.

They both bared the same expression as my grandparents, I grabbed hold of the limping one in an attempt to stop him but with a single arm he tossed me aside. 

I lost my balance and fell to the ground.

“into nemil” he echoed out his agape mouth.

I looked beyond them, down the pathway of smeared scraped blood and little drops of dark red that led towards their car.

The car’s radio receiver was hanging by its wire twirling in the air, it was buzzing a continuous low hum.

I picked it up and fiddled with it before saying “I found this car, the officer’s That I think own it? have some kind of brain damage? And my grandparents are having some an episode…”

Despite the passage of time their shuffling had made them very little progress.

“Hello? Is anyone there?”

The buzz continued. I couldn’t tell if what I had done was the correct way to use the thing, I don’t know how to use a radio. 

I fumbled with the buttons, trying again.

“Hello? Dispatch? Hello?” 

Amongst the hum a voice barely crackled through:

“Unit four-” Crackle “Status check” Crackle “Nimel” 

“Hello? I’m not a cop, send back up, we need more police and ambulances" 

The hum weaned from the low buzz to complete silence, the lights on the radio slowly dimmed. It was out of power. 

Help wasn’t here, these cops for whatever reason, had gotten into an accident and stopped just outside our house. Leapkmg out with no regard for their own safety. And now my grandparents were under the same motivation. I needed to go into town and find anyone who could help, not just an ambulance but someone, anyone else.

I grabbed my bike and sped west into the small town centre. 

I passed fields with cows wandering in and out of open gates, single solitary homes with wide open doors.

As I continued across the land that led to our town centre. I heard it before I saw it.

Thunder,  footsteps a thousandfold in a never ending polyrhythm. 

As the furthest edge of town slowly came into view I saw them, the mass, the horde. 

Some in pajamas, some naked, half injured, most barely fitting within the confines of the street. Their bodies pressed against each other, not in an effort to act as one. But their sporadic displacement left little room on the road, as they led and curved up it. I could make out the perimeter, but not the centre. Their heads remained perfectly still but pointed up like studs on the shoulder of a jacket.

At least 100, maybe more, the crowd’s back was closest to me but it continued out of the town centre and up a steep hill.

Littered randomly across the road, oozing red that leaked into the tarmac and smeared further by footprints. Pressed by repeated unthinking footsteps, those who didn’t make it. Those who fell, tripped. Bodies trampled and splattered like roadkill. 

I cycled faster, circling the streets and scanning windows. 

“HELLO?! HELLO?! HELLO?! HELLO?! ANYONE?! ANYONE! PLEASE!” I called in a manic cry, I hoped for a miracle. To wake up, to find anyone, a camera crew telling me this was a   
horrible prank.

My ears then picked up the worst possible sound, formally hidden amongst the footfall. 

*A newborn's cry from amongst the crowd.*
