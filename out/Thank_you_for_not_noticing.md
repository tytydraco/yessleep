The only thing I was firmly certain of was my lack of belief in anything supernatural or divine. Since childhood, I had been captivated by the search for monsters, scary stories, and anomalies. I kept journals dedicated to finding them, had fun with friends trying to summon spirits, and enjoyed the fear and adrenaline it brought. But I never actually believed in any of it, even though I often wanted to surrender myself to dreams of magic and mysticism.

As I grew older, that fascination disappeared, and now the only thing I allow myself from the “mystical” theme is watching a few horror movies on weekends with my husband. But what has been happening over the past few days made me think and write it down in my notes, so I could reflect on it later, or turn it into a scary story for my friends and my husband during another “movie night.”

The strange things did not begin with moving into a new apartment or buying some new object, like in typical stories. We didn’t perform any foolish rituals, and we didn’t watch any videos that warn you not to watch them. And my story does not end with some unknown entity killing everyone, because this is real, and I am alive.

We got married and moved in together more than three years ago, bought new furniture, and didn’t acquire any suspicious items at auctions, didn’t do anything that could (if we follow the logic of mystical stories) summon something evil. We simply lived a happy, ordinary life. I didn’t notice anything strange until I quit my job, after which I decided to take a short vacation that I could afford, and began spending almost all my time at home.

I always felt comfortable at home. Usually. We have soft furniture, soft lighting, and thick curtains. But while waiting for my husband to come home from work, I began to feel uneasy when alone, which I still attribute to boredom and nervousness from the lack of my usual routine. For the past couple of months, I haven’t been able to sit still at home, constantly feeling the urge to go out for a walk, even alone, just to get out and distract myself.

I love our apartment, and I always have. I love its comfort and atmosphere, but I cannot stay in it. I don’t understand the reason, but sometimes, while sitting and watching TV, I freeze in fear, afraid to turn around. Suddenly all the scary stories from childhood come back to me — about mirrors, televisions, darkness, and monsters.

I am paralyzed by fear, and I simply cannot make myself turn around. I am too afraid. A literal, animal terror. The last time it happened, I burst into tears from irrational fear. I am still ashamed of it. The first couple of months it was tolerable and barely noticeable, but the more time I spend alone, the more I notice it. I am starting to fear that my vivid imagination is playing tricks on me just to keep me entertained.

My reflection looks worse every day. I have clearly grown paler and lost weight. I react to loud sounds with fear, constantly on edge every day. I started flinching at noises. What finally made me write all of this was the last incident.

I woke up at night for no particular reason. I wasn’t thirsty, nor did I need to go to the bathroom. My husband was lying slightly to the side, at the edge of the bed, and I could feel his warmth against my back, which was calming. But even that didn’t help.

I was paralyzed by such a strong fear that I couldn’t move. I only had the strength to shut my eyes tightly and stay still. I was sure that if I opened my eyes, something terrible would happen — a horrible face would appear in front of me, I would be stabbed, killed, or eaten. I could almost feel a gaze on me. I was certain that someone was standing behind us, looking with desire.

I tried not to flinch at every sound in the silence. I managed to force myself, what felt like a long time later, when my husband calmly shifted in his sleep. All I had to do was open my eyes to see that nothing was there, that nothing was threatening me — but I couldn’t.

I forced myself and suddenly sat up. Before I could reach the lamp, I noticed a strange movement. Even after turning on the light and seeing that it was just a reflection in the glass cabinet, I burst into tears from pure terror and relief. I woke my husband with my crying and cried the whole night, trying to convince myself that it really was just a reflection.

I still feel terrible about it, afraid of what he might think of me. I needed to gather my thoughts and combine them into one story to show him.

Today, while he was at work, I decided to rearrange the room. I couldn’t help myself — I simply couldn’t rest knowing something like that could happen again. I pushed the bed into another corner so that our reflections wouldn’t be visible.

When I rested after moving the furniture, I fell asleep on the couch, and it happened again. I woke up in complete winter darkness and silence, with my heart racing and the same fear.

A horrible feeling. The worst in recent times.

I can’t believe I was so terrified of simple darkness and ordinary quiet sounds of an apartment building. When I managed to get up, I turned on the light in the living room, closed all the doors, and turned on a video from my favorite YouTuber in the background, but I still couldn’t relax.

I didn’t want to write anything more, and I don’t know if this is related to my story, but with each day I feel worse. Just ordinary weakness and constant sleepiness, probably just a lack of vitamins in winter, but now everything connects itself to the strange events at home.

I’ve become noticeably paler, with dark circles under my eyes. I cry often. I bought vitamins, that should help.

I am afraid of reflective surfaces. I can’t believe it — it sounds so stupid. I barely notice them out of the corner of my eye, but I constantly see something wrong in them. When I pass by the bathroom, I have to come back to make sure that in the reflection it is me, only me, and that everything else was just because I passed too quickly.

I don’t want to stay home alone anymore, but I can’t go out because I feel terrible. When I am alone, it constantly feels like everything is not as it should be, and that someone else is in the house.

Recently I tried to take care of myself, put on makeup, feel beautiful and happy, and then I had a stupid idea to check an old story — I sharply waved my hand while looking directly at my reflection. It seemed to lag behind by a fraction of a second, and I repeated it again, trying to catch it off guard.

Nothing.

Today I went out for a walk and entered a church shop. There are many of them in our area. I’ve never been interested in religion, but for a small amount of money I bought several blessed crosses. Just to feel safe.

I hung one above the mirror in the hallway, and when I turned away, I almost burst into tears again, seeing something in the corner of my eye turn with a delay, smiling back at me.

My husband believed me when I told him, and agreed to remove the glass doors from the wardrobe in our bedroom. He always believed me. He even checked the mirror, just as I asked. But nothing happened, and I cried again. He suggested we visit my parents, and I agreed.

Nothing changed in another house. We all started arguing more often, and my parents begged me to see a specialist. I am afraid to look at reflections in glass, afraid to sit by the window in a car, I cry when I pass mirrors, and at night I wake up feeling a presence.

I made an appointment with a psychiatrist.

He leaves us alone more and more often. Me and my reflection. My husband hopes the sedatives I now take will help, and I hope so too. They only make me sleepy, and because of that I feel even more afraid.

Sometimes I sleep, but still feel myself in my body and clearly understand that someone is standing over me while I sleep. I try not to look at my reflection when I am alone.

But it still looks at me.

This time I couldn’t take it anymore. When it seemed to me that something behind the glass was moving, moving not the way I would, I smashed it by throwing my phone.

It became easier. Calmer. I am stronger. I can control this.

I managed to pull myself together and clean up the shards of the mirror, putting the bathroom back in order. Each shard reflected me, not keeping up with my movements, as if unable to decide which one to be in.

I didn’t touch the trash bag with the shards and ran to the bedroom to fall asleep again. When he came home, he fussed around me, putting bandages on my fingers, which turned out to be covered in small cuts, and then took me to the hospital.

The dose of pills was increased to two.

Outside it is loud and noisy, so unusual after the silence of the house that I want to run away. In a crowd, it is harder to see my reflection, and I feel calm. I breathe fresh air and feel better than I have in years.

The pills only reduce my ability to function and increase sleepiness. They do not help with stress, so I leave them in the drawer.

The mirror in the hallway trembles, as if someone is hitting it from inside, and I cannot bring myself to walk past it. I record it on video and run to the bedroom, hiding under the blanket, hoping it will help and that whatever lives on the other side will not come out.

When he comes home, I show him everything. The videos. I cry uncontrollably, begging him to get rid of the mirror and bless the apartment.

He believes me, as always, and agrees.

I fall asleep in his arms, still paralyzed with fear.

Everything returns to normal slowly but surely. No more terrifying sounds. No more horrible nights. No more weakness, no more exhaustion. The animal fear that held me fades away.

Even physically I begin to feel better. Alive. Happy. Blooming.

The holidays are coming, and our whole family will gather at our home. It almost makes me feel light, calm, alive.

Before the celebration, we decorate the house with Christmas decorations. The ornaments on the tree shine, reflecting the lights of the garlands, but they no longer scare me. They bring comfort, as they should.

For the first time in a long while, my reflection finally mirrors my movements perfectly, bringing peace.

As a tribute to the past, I hang a cross on the wall above the table.

The festive dinner turns out rich and satisfying. I put so much effort into it that it simply could not fail.

The meat is juicy and so sweet that I cannot stop eating. The lower half of my face is smeared, my stomach already full, and yet this is only the first piece.

I don’t like bones. I don’t even bother with them. There is enough food to allow myself to be wasteful.

In food, perhaps, the process of obtaining it matters more than the result.

Ah, those sweet moments of fear. Driving people into paranoia.

And those tender kisses that a decent family man gave me, happy that his beloved wife had finally recovered.

They were sweet. Just like his flesh that now stuck between my sharp teeth.

Humans, despite their intellect, remain the most desirable prey. The sweetest. The most beloved.

So confident. So blind.

No matter how much the suffering ones try to warn them, they remain deaf and blind, becoming perfect prey.

Their feelings make them even better.

Their reactions. Their cries. Their disbelief.

Their emotions.

Sweet.

Too sweet.

I cannot finish everything at once, so I leave what remains to the scavengers that inhabit the house. I am not selfish. I am willing to share.

My helpless reflection, forever trapped behind the glass, looks at me in terror as I wash my face and restore my makeup.

I blow a kiss to the woman who lost everything, thanking her for the life she gave me.

And I leave the apartment, leaving these notes behind as a gift.

For people — a chance to understand.

For myself — a small amusement.
