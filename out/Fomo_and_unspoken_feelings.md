Part 1:
It was raining. Not a heavy downpour or calamity-like rain where it feels like the world might end, but it wasn't a gentle shower either because her words were blurring when she spoke like she usually does. And so I ended up screaming at her -"WHAT?" After all, she was standing a little farther than she should for a person I adored too much. She had been mumbling her words since the beginning of the conversation. I felt like I wasn't able to find the right radio station, and the distortion of the signal kept getting louder or softer alternatively, so the frustration was looking keenly at me. Hearing me scream, she paused between her sentence, which I still hadn't heard. I felt like I was in the 11th grade again, missing out on things that I wish I had, the way others had. I felt the FOMO toward her words intensify more and more, so I did what a logical person would do: took a step forward.

I hadn't confessed my feelings for her. She valued our friendship and held it in the highest regard. I didn't have the heart to ruin it. To go ahead and lose both my love and best friend would be a bad deal. Not how a logical person should act anyway. So I covered the flame, but it kept burning inside my chest like a warm fireplace on a winter night that I'd visit one time too often, but lately it has felt like I've been sitting near the fireplace for a prolonged time. Long enough that the heat felt a little less comfortable and more burning. Maybe I was sitting too close even; the cinders popped every now and then, scaring my skin.

The moment I took that step ahead, she teleported into my arms. She came so close so fast, I could barely see her make a move. Or maybe the rain just made my bad vision the perfect scapegoat for dissolving the horizon between reality and fiction; this woman really did appear in my embrace magically. But I hope reality is not deluded. She was soaked. I felt worried about her catching a cold, so I held her at arm's length and said, "Let's get you home." Those words made her face drop. 'This stupid woman will make us both bedridden, but oh how I love her. Have I even been trying to hear her?' I thought to myself before I finally gathered my scattered attention to her words to hear her for real this time. And something like, "I love you," spilled out of her mouth. I thought the rain hitting the asphalt must've made me hear things I desired to hear. Her nose was red at the tip. Was she crying, or was she cold already? I couldn't even tell in the rain. Or maybe I could.

I then looked into her eyes and could tell right away, yes, she was crying. "Are you crying?" She hit my abdomen in response. "Are you not listening?" she spoke with much more air and volume this time. I could finally hear. "No, what did you say before this?" "I said I love you." As those words tapped the drums of my ear, it did feel like the rain was dissolving my reality into fiction. My heart disappeared, and I was no longer feeling my knees. If not for the rain, I might as well have been able to fly in the sky right now. I was more than 50% sure I was a bird now anatomically.

We were outside the subway station in an alley where she had tripped and scraped her knee, and so I had made her sit near the curb while I was in search of a pharmacy desperately for an antiseptic and a Band-Aid. I hadn't felt that panicked since my accident, while I was still conscious and my last thought was, 'I hope I die,' while my bike was angled too close to the road and before I made contact with it in the harshest way possible. That was life and death, and so was this. So I hurried, but by the time I got back, the clouds came down. That's when all this happened. Or so I thought when I was talking about it to my friend over the phone. My day one. My childhood buddy.

He lived in the next city and had moved five years ago for his job. We were in school together, and he knew all about my life and I knew all about his. We were probably better family to each other than the real deal because that day when I presented my blood to the road, some good sir did the smartest thing possible and used my phone to call my family. No one had picked up the call. I was told later. He showed up though, my friend. He drove from his city to mine in under two hours and had run two traffic lights that day. All for me. I had asked him for the tickets to keep in my wallet so that I remember to save his life, God forbid that day ever comes, but if it does, I should be there for him.

So when I told him about everything, he said something that didn't make sense to me at all. He said, "You're doing it again, pal." And I asked, "What do you mean again?" "Being delusional. She couldn't have said I love you. She doesn't have a voice; how will she talk to you?" "Since when?" I asked with utmost confusion. "Since the beginning." And then silence followed for a second or two. "But she spoke more than that. I can swear on my life, and not just the I love you," I defended myself with everything I had. "You didn't say that to her, right? That you could hear her speak or something like that... it'll hurt her coming from you." "I'll call you back. I gotta go." "Hey, you need to tak—" I hung up on him before he could even finish the sentence.

What did he mean? How can she be mute when we've had heartfelt conversations? How would I have known she saw me only as her friend had we not talked? I was confused. Too confused.

I had dropped her back at her house and then came back to mine. I was still drying myself while we were on the call. So I was wrapped in one towel while drying my hair with another, now resting on my shoulder. I grabbed it from there and hung it on the chair next to me. My bedroom wasn't too spacious. My bed was next to the window that my door opened to, my bed aligned by it, and then my closet on the left wall and my desk on the right. I was still at my parents' house. I had found a job right where I grew up in a tech company. I took it because the pay was decent and my love was still here.

So I sat down in utter confusion on the bed, but I had to know for myself. I called her. She didn't pick up the phone. She must've been asleep. But I needed answers or I wouldn't have been able to get any sleep. I get too anxious if I don't get an answer to a question right away. It's like Googling a late-night thought real quick, but for me it was like my life depended on it. One time, I had to be restrained from hurting my own mother with my plastic spork for not answering me when I was 7 years old. I could feel my urges rising. I had to do something to know, so I called her again. And this time the call was received. "Hey, um, she is asleep. She was really tired when she got back. I'm her roommate speaking. Did you need something from her?" I could just ask her, right? "Hey, I wanted to ask something to you then." "Me?" "Yeah, you." "Okay. Shoot." "Does your roommate talk?" "Oh yeah, we do talk."

I knew it. I knew I wasn't imagining things.

"But mostly through texts."

That response landed in the grey zone and unfortunately would prove neither me nor my buddy right even if I told him about it. So I tried to be specific because I needed my answers. "No, I meant to ask—beep" went the phone. And then I couldn't have gotten my answers from anywhere now. I was getting sweaty from the anxiety. I couldn't sleep that night.
