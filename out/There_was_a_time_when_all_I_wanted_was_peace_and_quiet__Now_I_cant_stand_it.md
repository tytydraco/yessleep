When I was twenty-five, I decided I’d had enough.  
I packed my things and used my inheritance money to move to a cabin in Montana.  Silence was what I was after, and I got *plenty* of it.  
For a long time, it was peaceful.  I’ve always loved to read, and I had ample time to do it there.  *Catcher in the Rye*, *1984*, *Moby Dick*; you name it, I’ve probably read it.  I cut my own wood, hunted my own game, and grew my own vegetables.  The woods were expansive and beautiful.  Sometimes it was a little more dangerous than I liked, but it was worth the tranquility.  Only a few times a month would I have to venture into civilization to buy things like ammunition, clothes, and medicine.  It was paradise.  But things changed in the fall of ‘89.  
My garden was full of fruits and vegetables; my shed was full of lumber.  I was a little thin on meat.  A couple of elk was usually enough to feed me all winter, so I set out to get one before it got too cold.  
I strapped my rifle over my shoulder and left my cabin just after dawn.  The wildlife in the area scarcely saw people, so they weren’t terribly skittish.  I hiked up to a ridge nearby to scout for any signs of elk in the valley below.  They liked to graze there, usually in small groups.  
When I reached the top, I took out my rifle and looked through the scope.  As I scanned the plains, I picked up movement on the edge of my view.  It was a bobcat feasting on the carcass of a mule deer.  Lucky bastard.  She probably scared everything else off, though.  I packed up and set out south towards a watering hole I’d seen some wildlife at in the past.  
It wasn’t easy going, but I was strong back then.  I made the journey in three or four hours and set up far enough away that I wouldn’t be noticed.  The days were getting short; I probably had only a couple of hours of daylight left.  I pulled out my copy of *Crime and Punishment* and started reading.  Less than twenty pages later, I heard a rustling in the brush.  I readied my weapon and tracked the sound until it moved into view.  
Two elk moseyed out of the greenery and over to the pond.  One was too small to eat, but the other was a good size.  I switched off my safety and sat, still as a statue, waiting for it to take a drink.  As the good one stopped and bent down for a drink, I fired a shot right through its lungs.  It took three steps, then collapsed in a heap.  The smaller one tore off into the forest.  As I approached, I realized it was even bigger than I had thought.  Not a bad day.  
I cleaned it, wrapped the meat in its hide, and slung it over my shoulder before starting the journey back.  I’d have to hurry, but I wasn’t too worried; I hadn’t seen a bear in a couple years.   
The sun went down when I was about a half hour from the cabin.  I was close enough now that my memory and the moonlight’d be plenty for me to find my way.    
I was breathing pretty heavy now.  Like I said, it was bigger than I thought.  I could see the smoke from my fireplace against the night sky.  The stars were always so beautiful, but they made me feel so small out there.  Small and alone.  
I heard a twig snap behind me.  I whipped my head around, expecting to see a bear, but it was just an elk.  They typically didn’t come so close to my cabin, but I figured that it was less aware of its surroundings in the darkness.  I watched it for a bit, wondering if it knew I was carrying one of its brothers on my back.    
Its nose twitched, like a rabbit’s.  Then it turned its head and looked right at me.  It stood up on its hind legs and walked away.  I was frozen.  In all my years out there, I never saw any animal stand up like that.  It sent chills down my spine, but I figured it was just a strange coincidence.  A terrifying one, but a coincidence either way.    
Anyway, I got back to my cabin and started packing the meat in salt.  When I was finished, I stored it in the cellar and came back up to head to bed.  I was exhausted.  I read a few more chapters of *Crime and Punishment*, blew out my lantern, and fell asleep.  
When I woke, it was still dark out.  I lit a candle and walked to the stove to cook some old grouse I’d been meaning to finish off.   It was raining, not bad, but enough that I wouldn’t be able to get back to sleep.  Thunder in the distance didn’t bode well for tomorrow.  I could get by with the meat I had, but I wanted some more in case something went wrong.  No such thing as being too careful.  Not when you’re as isolated as I was.  
The grouse was coming along nicely when I heard a tap on my window.  I figured it was just a branch, but it scared the hell out of me anyway.  I grabbed my candle and walked over to check it out.  
My suspicions were correct—a tree branch was blowing in the wind and scraping against the glass.  I breathed a sigh of relief.  I was still on edge from that elk I saw earlier.  How the fuck did it stand up like that?  I returned to my stove.  
When I was finished cooking, I sat down to eat.  I had the meat halfway to my mouth when a window shattered, sending shards of glass flying into my bedroom.  The wind whistled through the space where the window used to be.  
Then a scream—a terrible scream—filled the room.  I looked out the window to see that same elk from before standing on its hind legs outside my cabin.  It was staring right at me.  it bellowed again, loudly, terribly, and I grabbed my rifle.  My hands shook as I tried to load it.  Bullets spilled onto the floor.  Finally, I was able to chamber one.  
I didn’t bother with the scope.  I just pointed it at the elk and fired.  The bullet ripped through its shoulder and it screamed a third time, its leg (arm?) dangling at its side by a tendon.  Whatever it was only grew angrier.  It tried to jump through the window, but only succeeded in lodging itself in it, its head and neck inside of my cabin, thrashing madly, screaming.  I chambered another bullet and fired.  It was impossible to miss this time.  The bullet blew through its head, and it went limp.  Blood dripped from its nose and mouth onto my floor.  I grabbed my keys, got into my truck, and sped away, spitting mud behind me.  
I live in the city now, with a wife and plenty of kids.  And I hate silence.  I always have music playing, day or night.  In my dreams, I still see whatever that thing was, head stuck through my window, thrashing wildly from side to side.  Part of me wonders if its skeleton remains there, a monument to all things unholy.  I’m too scared to find out.
