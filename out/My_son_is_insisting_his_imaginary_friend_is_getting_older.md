I wish my days could go back to the boredom they used to be every Saturday afternoon.

The sky outside our small Massachusetts was a dense, featureless grey, pressing down on our house like a cozy blanket. I had taken the day off to repaint the hallway. The project had sat still since breakfast. My son, Oliver, was in his room, talking to no one. 

Again.

The conversation rose and fell, murmurs and snippets I couldn’t quite catch. His laughter fell between long pauses. I had grown used to it. All kids have an imaginary friend at some point. Oliver called him Ben. But there was something different about today that made the hair stand on the back of my neck.

To break the heavy silence I called him from the kitchen. “Lunch soon, Ollie!”

“Okay Mama!”

Oliver had started mentioning Ben about six months ago, right after my husband Peter and I finalized our separation.. I wasn’t naive, I knew imaginary friends bloomed out of loneliness. His “friend” seemed harmless enough. He was polite, according to Oliver, and loved to play with trains. Oliver never blamed any of his bad behavior on Ben. That, made me relieved.

“He’s tall,” He had explained one night, as I tucked him in for bed, “But not grown-up tall. He’s eleven. He says that’s almost a teenager.”

“How nice.” I said. “And what does Ben look like?”

Oliver frowned thinking, “He was see through at first, but not anymore. Now I can see his eyes and nose when we play in the dark. He has a lot of freckles.” Then a pause. “He’s getting older Mama.”

I’d laughed. “You mean you are imagining him older?”

He shook his head so fiercely his curled bounced. “No. He said so.”

I decided to let him be right, switched off the lamp and left him to fall asleep.

By early autumn, Ben had become a regular part of our everyday lives. Some mornings I’d find a second cereal bowl set out on the table. Filled but untouched. Oliver claimed Ben was “just picky.”

One afternoon, I stepped into Oliver’s room to gather laundry. His crayons were spread in a meticulous manner across the floor. Every blue crayon was snapped in half perfectly down the middle. On a piece of paper he had drawn two boys. I bent down and picked up the drawing to look at it closer.

One of the boys was smaller with curly brown hair and a crooked smile. The other was much taller, its limbs disproportionately long, with eyes made of uneven frantic circles. All around the figure he’d scrawl one word over and over and over in messy print.

Oliver. Oliver. Oliver.

When Oliver came in his room seeing me holding the drawing, he froze like he had been caught. His cheeks turned a rosy pink.

“Who is this in your drawing buddy?” I ask trying to hide the nervous tremor in my voice.

“Ben did it,” He whispered. “He said my name is his favorite word.”

Peter came by on Sundays, always bringing something sugary to sooth his guilt. This week it was creme-filled eclairs. He sat at our dining room table while Oliver played with a balloon peter brought him.

“Is his imaginary friend still around?” Peter asked playfully trying to keep the tension to a minimum.

I nodded, sipping my too hot coffee to avoid having to respond.

“Then its fine,” he said brushing a crumb from his shirt. “Better an imaginary friend than nightmares. My friend’s daughter used to talk to a ghost named Tommy. She turned out okay.”

I didn’t tell him about the drawings. Or by how sometimes at night, when I passed Oliver’s room late at night, I swore I could hear a deeper voice mixed in with his. Not a child’s, but something else. Something foreign.

It was late October when I first hear the laughter clearly—two distinct tones.

Oliver had been up past his bedtime, his door cracked open. From the hallway I heard his whispering, then that other sound: a low chuckle that came from his closet.

I froze.

“Ben said you should come in.” Oliver said suddenly. How did Oliver see me?

I decided to step into his room, my skin prickled with sweat. The room was still except for the scrape of dying leaves against the window.

“Where’s Ben?” I asked. But I didn’t want to know.

Oliver pointed toward the closet door. His little face peaking out from his covers. “Sleeping there. He’s tired because he had another birthday.”

“How old is he now?” I managed.

Oliver beamed. “Fourteen.” 

That night, I think a I had a dream about hearing knocking within the walls. It made me stir in my sleep and question whether it was a dream or not. Restless, I pulled myself out of bed to get a glass of water.

Oliver’s room is down the hall from mine and you have to pass both of ours to get to the bathroom. 

As I passed his room, I swore I heard the same knocking as earlier. It makes me stop to check on him. Oliver never has trouble sleeping. Once his head hits the pillow he’s passed out for the whole night. I never really had issues with him as an infant. Sometimes it scared me how well he slept and I would get up just to make sure he was still breathing.

Oliver keeps his door cracked at night, I pushed it open. Moonlight fell on his face. It made my chest ease slightly. I scanned his room and then his closet. But the ease in my chest quickly turned into a knot.

Something flashed from inside his closet, a quick blur of movement I barely caught.  Every instinct urges me to grab my child. But what did I even see? It could have been anything, and just a trick of the light. I didn’t want to disturb his sleep for my anxiety.

I decide to leave his room, my hand shaking on the door handle.

By morning, the incident fell to the back of my mind. One of those things you barely thing about and brush off to things that just happen in the middle of the night in that state between being awake and half asleep.

November rolled around, and I got a call from Oliver’s preschool teacher. I didn’t like how…disturbed she sounded.

“Ms. Warner,” Her voice trembled. “Has Oliver talked about a boy named Ben at all?”

I didn’t want to say yes. Maybe I could lie and act like I had no idea what she was talking about. But I was a horrible liar and if he was causing issues at school, I needed to know about it.

“He’s mentioned him. Why?”

“Well after nap time today Oliver got mad that we opened the blinds. He said Ben does not like the sunlight and that it makes his skin burn.”

I pinched the bridge of my nose. This was actually happening. 

“We’ve had alot of changes at home. His dad and I are recently separated and…”

“I understand. I just figured I’d let you know. And…um he’s been drawing eyes a lot recently? I found some drawings in his desk after school. Just pages and pages of an eye over and over.”

“That’s his fixation right now.” I tried to laugh it off. “But thank you for letting me know.” I hung up and sat in my chair. What was I going to do with this kid?

Later that evening I found some of the drawings his teacher was talking about. The same eye drawn with a blue crayon. Each with the iris shaped like a small ring. But what caught me of guard was the middle of the iris. In black was a tiny stick figure in each pupil. Something you could easily say was just the middle of the eye. You had to look close to notice it. But there it was, a small person clear as day.

It made my skin crawl.

By thanksgiving, the house had developed a smell. I didn’t notice it immediately. It wasn’t rot exactly, but something more metallic. Like something left too long in the air. I noticed it the most outside of Oliver’s room. I searched for where the smell was coming from but it never grew stronger, it just stayed pungent. But when I walked down the hall a few feet away, the smell faded.

I scrubbed and cleaned the area outside of his room but the smell continued to linger. At this point I was stumped on how to fix it. Was there a rat corpse in the wall? Did I need to call an exterminator?

The way Oliver talked about Ben also changed. I brought up the incident at school and Oliver defended Ben. 

“They don’t get it mama, Ben is real. They were hurting him.” This was the first time Oliver had called Ben “real” and not his imaginary friend.

I keeled down to make eye contact with him. “Buddy I understand you really like Ben but he is your imaginary friend. He is not real. You can play pretend with him all you want here at home. But at school, Ben stays here and waits for you.”

Oliver stomped his foot. “No! He goes to school with me every day! He goes with me everywhere!”

I decided not to escalate the conversation and just told him to not talk about Ben at school. He shrugged it off and we didn’t talk about it again.

As I tucked him in for bed that evening I asked where Ben slept.

“In you,” he said matter-of-factly. “Until he is ready.”

I had to control my movements to not pull away from him. “What do you mean……in me?”

He just smiled. “Silly mommy.” It was as if I missed the punchline to a joke only he understood.

Soon Oliver stopped playing with his trains. Instead, he sat for hours by his closet door. Once I tried to catch what he was doing. I stood outside his room where he couldn’t see me; listening.

“It’s almost time.”

I staggered back, heart rattling in my chest. But when I looked in his room, Oliver was smiling. I darted down the hallway, I didn’t want to listen any longer.

That wasn’t Oliver’s voice, it was something else entirely. It wasn’t human either.

That Sunday, Peter stopped by again. He brought Muffins and hot chocolate, and went into Oliver’s room to visit. He was in there maybe ten minutes before he came out pale and quiet.

“What is it?” I asked.

 “Ollie said someone is in the closet.”

I rolled my eyes. “That’s just Ben. I wouldn’t worry about.” I didn’t know if I was telling him or reassuring myself. Because Oliver’s behavior was only growing more erratic.

“No, Lisa.” He said. “There’s something wrong with Ollie.”

I scoffed. “Well you didn’t help by cheating on me and leaving us. He made Ben up after the divorce.”

Peter looked guilt stricken at that. He knew what he did was wrong, and every time he came over he begged for another chance. That this time would be different. But muffins and hot chocolate don’t undo sleeping with a coworker in our bed.

He left and told me he might not visit for awhile. I didn’t know if it was because of the comment I made, or what he saw in Oliver’s room or both.

But I felt like the break would be good for us both. But I didn’t sleep at all that night, and I lied to myself that it wasn’t about my ex-husband’s reaction or the thing living in Oliver’s room.

In mid-December, I made the mistake of taking a picture.

Oliver had insisted I photograph his drawing of Ben “before he changes again.” The photo came out wrong. The lines were warped and pulled, the figure in the drawing seeming to distort outward, as though trying to breach the lens itself.

But worse was what appeared beside the drawing in the photo: a faint outline of a boy’s face, older than Oliver, freckles dusted across a nose, pale eyes tilted directly at the camera.

I dropped my phone. When I picked it up again, the image was gone—corrupted, the file unreadable.

We were snowed in on Christmas Eve. Peter used the weather as an excuse to not visit. It didn’t bother me, it would be nice to have Christmas just Ollie and I. We watched Christmas movies and bakes sugar cookies. The whole time Oliver never mentioned Ben. It gave me some relief to hang out without feeling like I had to include his imaginary friend.

After putting him to bed and reminding him Santa only comes while he’s sleeping, I drag myself back to my bedroom for the evening. I typically get up early in the morning to put out his gifts. 

I didn’t remember falling asleep, but I woke to a rustling sound outside in the hallway. I checked my phone to see that it was two in the morning. 

At first I thought it was Oliver sneaking downstairs to check his stocking, we had caught him years in the past which is why we put out gifts in the morning when we knew he was still sleeping. The sound led away from his room, into the hall towards my door.

I strained to listen and caught two voices whispering outside.

“Don’t wake her,” a deeper voice. Then in Oliver’s smaller voice: “Is she almost ready?”

Footsteps creaked closer. I froze under my covers, my pulse thudded in my ears like a drum.

“Hide,” Oliver giggled softly. Then there was silence.

When I finally dared to open my eyes, someone was standing in the doorway—a figure taller than Oliver, shaped by darkness, head slightly tilted as if studying me. Its skin gleamed faintly gray in the moonlight.

“Ben?” I whispered before I could stop myself.

It cocked its head to the side and smiled. I squeezed my eyes shut. It was dark and I wasn’t seeing clearly. The brain makes up all kinds of things when you’re half asleep.

When I opened my eyes, it was gone. But a faint scent of the iron musk lingered in my room.

By new year’s day I couldn’t stay another night.

I packed out bags, loaded everything into the car and drove down to my sister’s place in Vermont. The roads weren’t safe to drive on but even if it took forever to get there I would still do it. As long as we were out of theut of the house we would be okay.

When we were half way there, Oliver spoke. “He’s mad.”

“Ben can’t come with us, I’m sorry.” I said curtly.

He turned to me, his expression oddly blank. “He doesn’t have to,” he whispered. “He said he’s already inside.”

My eyes snapped up to look a him through the rear view mirror. Something cold crawled through me. “Inside what?”

Oliver smiled. “You’ll see soon.”

“You can’t play pretend with Ben anymore. We’re going to Aunt Velma’s and you need to behave. No talking about Ben. Do you understand?”

He didn’t respond and continued to glare out the window.

“Oliver.”

“I heard you, Mommy.”

“Do you promise to behave? And not talk about Ben?”

Again I was met with silence.

“I need you to answer me.”

“He will behave.” A deep voice responded and I jolted nearly steering us off the road. There was a shadow in the rear view mirror. The same thing I saw in the hallway on Christmas Eve. I looked straight ahead at the road. We were only five minutes away. I didn’t look at Oliver again until we were parked in my sister’s driveway.

For a few days things were normal. Oliver even joined his cousins outside throwing snowballs. I started to breathe again.

We watched them play in the snow, a warm mug of coffee sat in my lap. My sister next to me.

“What’s going on that’s got you so worked up? He seems fine to me.” Velma asked. I Was very forward about my visit. Of course she didn’t mind but it was very last minute.

I shook my head. “He’s got this imaginary friend and it’s been causing behavior issues at home. The divorce isn’t helping, I know that much.”

“Has he seen a therapist? Maybe he just needs someone to talk to. I’d be happy to recommend someone.” Velma was a therapist herself and knew you couldn’t really take family as patients.

I sighed. “Maybe, I don’t know. I—” But my sentence was cut off when screaming came from the front yard. We stood up and rushed to the front door.

Velma’s son bolted through the house and hid behind Velma. “Ollie kept trying to knock me over with snowballs and said I need to let him in!”

Velma was confused at this statement. “What? What are you talking about? Are you hurt?”

Oliver stomped in moments later. “You’re not listening to me!”

“Get away! Go away! I don’t want to play anymore!”

We separated the boys and promised hot chocolate and cookies. While they calmed down sipping their drinks, I decided to approach it again.

“So what happened outside? Did you guys have fun?” I kept my tone light and casual. They weren’t in trouble. We just needed to know what happened.

The boys were silent for a few moments. Derek, Velma’s son spoke first.

“Oliver still believes in imaginary friends and kept saying we had to let Ben play with us. But I don’t believe in that stuff anymore. It’s baby play.”

Oliver shoved his chair back. “It is not! Ben is real!”

“Okay, okay. Enough.” Velma stepped in. We exchanged a look. I knew we would be leaving tomorrow morning.

“He’s right there!” Oliver pointed to the corner. “He says your turns next!”

Velma still recommended a therapist for Oliver, and told me to visit when things calmed down.

After we went home, I scheduled doctor’s appointments as well as one with the therapist. They all said the same thing: night terrors, adjustment issues. He started having fevers at night, woke up screaming about Ben being too big.

And then the nights changed—the light in my room would flicker, my breath visible even under heavy blankets. Sometimes I would wake to find Oliver standing beside my bed, staring.

“He’s inside you now, Mama,” he said one dawn, voice flat as stone. “He borrowed my skin for a while, but he likes yours better. It’s roomier.”

The next morning, I noticed it too.

My reflection in the bathroom mirror looked wrong. My pupils seemed slightly dilated even in bright light. My wrists were unusually cold, and when I pressed a hand to my chest, my heartbeat lagged. It was off-rhythm and slow.

In the hallway, I heard Oliver murmuring to someone again.

“Ben says he can see now,” he whispered. “He likes what he sees through you.”

That night I locked my door. I remember sitting in bed, the hallway light under the crack of the door flickering.

At 2:13 a.m., something pressed its palm flat against the other side of the door. The handle trembled.

I whispered my son’s name.

A voice answered, but it wasn’t his.

“He’s growing up, Lisa. You should be proud.” My skin crawled. But I remained frozen in place. I should have brought Oliver in here to sleep with me but I was too afraid of…that thing. Whatever it was.

The door cracked open, slow and deliberate.

And standing there—half in light, half in dark—was Ben, no longer a boy but something taller, older, with Oliver’s eyes. He looked at me as though deciding which part to begin with.

My sister said I called her at 5:20 a.m., though I don’t remember dialing. She said she heard whispering behind me, like two people sharing one mouth. When she arrived, the house was silent except for Oliver humming in his room.

He told her, calmly, “Mama’s finally letting Ben drive now.”

The police found me in the hallway, sitting straight-backed, eyes open but unseeing, my lips mouthing something too quiet to catch. They said I looked almost… content.

When they touched me, I turned my head and smiled. “It’s okay,” I said. “He’s old enough now.”
