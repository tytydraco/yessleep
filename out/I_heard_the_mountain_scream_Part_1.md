To whomever this story may reach, it is paramount that you don’t attempt to follow in my steps. I wouldn’t wish this fate upon anyone. I’m only writing this because people deserve to know why I have gone missing.

From a very young age, I heard the stories of Ipshire: the island that lay approximately 30 nautical miles southwest of Plymouth, shrouded in mystery with a long history of strange disappearances. An island that lies on the border of myth and truth. 

Historical records of the island go as far back as the 1300s. Inhabitants of the island would sail to the mainland with the purpose of selling their wares. Their commodities were greatly coveted, as they were of great quality and rare stock, including goats, pigs, plums, oranges, mangoes, and more. Various travelers would also visit the island, all describing it as a marvel of agriculture and a beautiful landmark beyond compare. Though the island was described as massive, the number of inhabitants was around 15 people. There was a substantial trail of sources from around the time, which collectively gave the impression that Ipshire was a normal settlement for Brits.

But by the year 1821, this trail just dropped off. From that year and onward there are no other firsthand accounts of the island or its inhabitants. The reason is that the people from Ipshire just stopped sailing to the mainland, seemingly without a reason. What should be noted, though, which has been acknowledged by historians as well as conspiracy theorists, is how this occurrence overlaps with a very peculiar cosmic phenomenon. An event as interesting and bizarre as the island in question.

That is, the time a star fell to earth.

On a summer’s night in 1821, hundreds of Brits living in Plymouth watched as a white light whizzed across the evening sky before falling into the horizon, heading southwest. Too big and close to earth to be a mere shooting star. It was only a few minutes later that a great white blast was seen coming from beyond the horizon. It was in the days following the blast that Brits noticed the absence of boats sailing in from Ipshire.

This resulted in several boats being sent out southwest of Plymouth. Some of the boats would sail to the island to gather if anything had gone awry, while others would sail in the direction where the white blast had been spotted. What all the boats had in common is that they never returned to the mainland; neither would any boat that was sent out on the same journey in the years after, regardless of the quality of the boat or the size of the crew.

After a decade or so of no communication, all the inhabitants of Ipshire were presumed dead, along with any crew who disappeared on their journey. Fearmongering about the island spread. The waters southwest of Plymouth were deemed cursed. Ships were warned against sailing in the vicinity of the island out of fear that they wouldn’t return.

Every now and then, a new group of scientists or explorers embarks on the same journey, hoping for a better outcome. Still, not one person has ever made the return. Not even any pictures or videos of the island have seen the light of day.

It is with all of this in mind that I present the story of the time I visited Ipshire myself, which may well be the only firsthand account of the island in the last two centuries.

The idea of visiting Ipshire came to mind about half a year ago, during my endless search for an interesting subject to write about. I work as a journalist, and the past couple of years haven’t been kind to me. I hadn’t written an interesting article in a long time. I always dreamt of writing zeitgeist-defining stories that would change society or open people’s eyes to the horrors of the modern world. But, to my dismay, I have always been stuck with the most mundane and sleep-inducing stories. Stories about businesses closing down, food reviews of subpar restaurants, or celebrities being in “hot water” due to past affairs. It’s been during these low points of my life that the words of my parents come to haunt me, sending ripples throughout my mind:

*You’re never gonna get anywhere with that degree!*

*Why couldn’t economics just be your thing?*

*Don’t come begging for money when it all comes crashing down on you.*

The only reason I chose to write is because I didn’t know what else to do. For a long while I began to doubt that I could do anything. I never felt fulfilled. I was just stuck in the everyday as I watched the sands of time running through my fingers.

I saw Ipshire as the chance for a breakthrough. I thought I could finally be fulfilled, that I could find my place in the world. I could put my name on the map and receive all the admiration I had lacked for so long. Of course, I would have to do it alone; if I wanted the acknowledgement, I wouldn’t find it amongst a group of people. My name would just be a footnote in the history books describing an expedition made by more accomplished people; no one would remember me. It was all or nothing. It seemed quite simple. I would go to the island, take pictures of the town’s remains, write about what I found, and then I would go home. I didn’t think the waters were actually cursed; that was obviously just sailor stories being furthered by nothing but silly superstitions.

I started off by signing my way through a mountain of papers and wafting away the worries of most of my colleagues and family members. Before I knew it, I was sitting in the cabin of a trawler going 12 knots southwest. I wasn’t worried at all; in fact, my seasickness was cause for more worries as I sat in the cabin. After a couple of hours, I was called up to the deck. The mainland had long since disappeared behind the horizon.

“I won’t sail any further.” The captain told me. “I offer you my finest motorboat to assist you on your travels.” He pointed into the waters where a small boat was swaying with the waves. 

As I stood on the deck, my eyes were drawn to the island, which still lay far off on the horizon. This is where I came face to face with my decisions for the very first time. Though it still lay 2 nautical miles away, I felt a pit of worries forming in my stomach. Maybe I had been a bit hasty, I thought to myself. I suppose part of me had imagined that there wouldn’t actually be an island. Perhaps I’d slightly hoped that the captain would just say: “we can’t find the island.”, and then the boat would turn around and take me back; but no, the island was there, in front of me, and now I had to leave the crew behind. Even at a distance of 2 nautical miles, I felt chills coursing through my body, like a peasant staring at a sleeping dragon. 

*Nothing but just silly superstitions*

“There should be enough fuel to take you back to the mainland. Just use this when the time comes and you should be fine.” He handed me a compass.

I got into the motorboat. I watched as the trawler turned around and began sailing in the opposite direction; I somewhat wished I were still on it. I wondered if I should’ve brought warmer footwear with how cold my feet were beginning to feel. But continuing to sit in the boat wouldn’t do me any good; I had to get to the island and set up camp before I got to dark. I turned the motor on and started sailing towards the island.

The first true feature I noticed was the mountain that dominated the heavens above Ipshire. It would be impossible to miss; it shot up at least half a mile into the sky. As I sailed closer, I noticed a row of objects lined up on the shore. They stood out clear against the green forest behind them. I was about 100 meters out when I realized that the objects were numerous small boats in varying stages of decay. It didn’t seem that getting to the island was the problem for travelers. It made me wonder what awaited me on the island; it did nothing to boost my confidence. When I got close enough, I jumped into the water and pulled the motorboat onto the shore. As I felt my boots sink into the mud, I was overcome with a feeling of awe. I was standing on the muddy grounds of a place that I had only heard about in stories. It wasn’t just a myth being passed around anymore; it was real. I imagined it was like how Elijah felt when he was carried off to heaven. My feelings of dread and wonder were at odds with each other, and I wondered which feeling would emerge as the victor by the end of my trip. I took a deep breath as I listened to waves crashing against the hull of the motorboat.

Soon enough, I was alerted when I heard something moving in the bushes of the forest that bordered the shore. I turned around, looking at the forest as the stems started to move. It would have to be a big animal to make such a ruckus. I got down on my knees in the boat, hiding from whatever was coming through. Then, from between the tree stems walked not a wolf, nor a lion, nor any other animal; it was a man dressed in a three-piece suit, as if he’d just come from one of Gatsby’s parties.

“Hello mainlander,” he said, looking directly at my boat as he approached. “I bid you welcome to our little corner of the world.”

I was shocked. I couldn’t believe I was looking at another person. For a moment I thought it was some sort of mirage, but it wasn’t. The man was as real as you or I. I began to think that he was a hermit or something. Then again, I had never seen a hermit dressed quite like he was. The blackness of his trousers and jacket served to emphasize the birch-white waistcoat, which could be seen between the lapels of his jacket. His face was adorned with long sideburns and short curly hair, all colored in drab orange. On his feet, he wore something that looked like classic oxfords. Altogether, it evoked a truly pompous energy. The only missing parts were a cane and a top hat. He came within an arm’s length of me and reached out a hand, to which I reached out my own. His grip was firm as he shook my hand. I looked into his eyes: blue as ice yet radiated all the same warmth and kindness of any human being.

“It is a pleasure to meet you.” The man said. “The others will be very surprised.”

“Others?” I asked. “Are there other people on the island?”

“Well, of course.” The man said, turning around and signaling for me to follow him before walking towards the forest.

I reached my hand into my bag and pulled out the flick knife, which I had brought. Though I had brought it to defend myself against animals, I supposed it would be just as effective on people if worst came to worst. I put it into my pocket and jogged up to the man, walking into the forest.

“The name is Cottonmouth.” He said as we walked. “I am a shepherd.”

“My name…”

“Your name is not important.” He sternly rebutted.

We kept quiet as we trudged through the forest. When I looked around, I didn’t see any animals; I didn’t even hear the jolly chirps of a faraway bird. In fact, the only sound that caught my ear was what sounded like a bell, chiming every so often in some distant part of the forest. 

“Is that a bell I hear?” I asked.

“Your ears must be deceiving you, mainlander.” The man said. “There is no sound in this forest.”

“Oh, perhaps I need my ears cleaned then.” 

I chuckled, but the man remained stoic, not answering with as much as a sigh.

Trying to avert the awkward interaction, my eyes drifted to the treetops. Through the gaps in the canopy, I saw the looming façade of the great mountain, still far away in the distance. Only then I noticed that its rocky surface was coated with numerous small holes. They reminded me of papules on an acne-ridden face.

“Those holes in the mountain… are they natural?” I asked.

“They were created by His Eminence.” He said. “Now, be quiet. The sacred silence of the forest is not meant to be disturbed.”

Without really answering my question, the man left me with a million more. Regardless, I kept my silence for the remainder of our walk. Before long, the forest opened up, and I found myself standing in a milewide clearing. When I had seen the man exiting from between the tree stems, I didn’t think it could get much weirder, but I had been dead wrong. White cottages were scattered across the little parcel of land. The ground was a depressing mix of mud and trampled down yellowed grass. Amongst it all, several people were walking around I dirty tattered garments.

I had officially arrived on Ipshire: an island as solitary as an oyster’s pearl but without any of the glamour.

I felt as Cottonmouth grabbed me by the wrist and started dragging me into the town, and, though I was nervous, I followed along. As we walked through the town, I would catch the occasional weird glimpse every time we passed one of the people walking around. Every time I made the slightest eye contact with any of them, I felt a horrid chill course through my body and had to avert my eyes. Their eyes were nothing like Cottonmouth’s. There was something quaint and appalling in theirs, like a malicious force peering from beneath their pupils. There wasn't a shred of human warmth to be found. They were cold and black like obsidian. Save for the eyes, none of the people was alike in terms of facial features, but they all exhibited the same gaunt frame and pale skin. I wondered if the people suffered from a condition similar to that of the Habsburg jaw, a condition where the royal family of Habsburg was blighted with a disfigured chin resulting from generations of inbreeding, but, though I would like to assume such, I cannot rightfully claim that inbreeding would even explain a quarter of the peculiarities that I witnessed during my short stay. 

Cottonmouth suddenly stopped walking. He straightened his back, opened his mouth, and loudly proclaimed:

“I bear good news!”

From every corner of the town, the inhabitants started coming towards us. They gathered in a circle, surrounding us.

“My dearest friends,” Cottonmouth started. “I am pleased to announce the coming of another visitor to our island.”

Though I avoided their gazes, I kept glancing between the people and Cottonmouth. The man just stuck out like a sore thumb, or perhaps a healthy thumb amongst sore fingers. Not only were his clothes of a finer quality, but his face bore none of the grizzly features of his fellows. I began to wonder what was so special about this shepherd.

“I have spoken to His Eminence,” Cottonmouth continued. “He tells me that we must take great care of our new visitor. Is that understood?”

The people surrounding us started nodding weakly, and, with a swift move of Cottonmouth’s hand, the circle of people disbanded. Each person walked off in a different direction, and I was once again left alone with the man.

He grabbed me by the wrist once more, dragging me away to one of the many cottages. This cottage was clearly one of the older ones. In the doorway of the cottage stood a woman, tall and slim like bamboo. She looked at me, and, in return, I avoided looking into her eyes.

“This is Five.” Cottonmouth said. “Her cottage will be your stay. You have been granted the best bedchamber in all of Ipshire.”

I crossed the doorway and found myself standing in the kitchen. I looked around, making myself aware of the surroundings. The walls were as smooth as sandpaper. What had once been a stove had become a relic overgrown with vines and moss. The smell was rancid beyond belief. It was downright depressing. The cottage might as well have stayed empty with how I looked.

“Well, I will leave you be, mainlander.” Cottonmouth said as he turned on his heels and started walking away.

Five took me to the bedchamber, and I realized the kitchen had only been the appetizer. The bedchamber was confined to four walls with no window. Greenery grew from between floorboards. The air was filled with the stench of rotten wood and mud. The only source of light was the sunrays breaching a crack in the roof. The bed seemed like nothing more than a rough piece of thin rug stretched over a slab of wood, neither blanket nor pillow anywhere to be seen.

“What a beautiful room.” I forced a smile, still avoiding her eyes.

“Flattery means nothing if your heart believes it to be untrue.” She answered with a frown before walking out of the room, closing the door behind her.

She had seen through my white lie as if it were air. For a moment I began to worry that I had made a bad impression. I definitely didn’t want to make these people mad. I was reminded of the boats that lined the shore. There had to be a reason so many boats were left there, and I couldn’t help but imagine it would have something to do with the people. So many had disappeared throughout the years. One bad move, and my own disappearance might have been inevitable.

*Why couldn’t economics just be your thing?*

I sat down on the slab of wood, massaging my temples and letting my concerns ebb away. I had come this far, and I wasn’t going to run away with my tail between my legs. I was going to make it. I was going to prove the naysayers wrong. I would prove that I knew what I was doing. The fact that people were still living on the island would only make the article more interesting.

I marched out of the cottage, flick knife and camera in my pocket, notebook and pencil in hand, ready to write the article that would change my life forever.

I scoured the town, observing the people as they went on with their lives. A lot of the people seemed to observe me as well. I can’t say it made me comfortable. Throughout the town, I couldn’t help but notice the absence of any food sources. There were no fences with livestock, nor were there any baskets of fish or any patches of crops. This struck me as quite odd. Centuries ago, the people of Ipshire were known for selling their crops and their livestock. Questions started to sprout in my head: What could have led the people away from their pastoral lifestyle? What were they eating? 

Something even stranger, though, was the number of houses. There was maybe a total of 25 people living in the town, but there were over 40 cottages scattered around the town, and the townsfolk were seemingly still building more, even chopping down the trees of the surrounding forest to expand the clearing even further. From the historical records, I knew the number of people hadn’t been bigger, even in the years before the cutoff from the mainland. In an attempt to find something more apprehensible, I headed for the outskirts of the town, towards the forest passage from where I’d previously exited.

The moment I stepped into the forest, I heard the familiar sound of a distant bell. I began my descent into the forest, following the sound, hoping it would lead me somewhere. As I trudged through the twigs and fallen leaves, I was once again overwhelmed by the persistence of silence whenever the bell wasn’t chiming. It was as if even the wind was afraid to leave an audible mark. It was uncanny, in an idyllic sort of way. Then, suddenly, I stumbled upon something noteworthy. It was a fruit tree; I could see mangoes dangling from its branches. That wasn’t the only thing that caught my attention. Beneath the tree crown lay a heap of rotten mangoes. A heap that had clearly been piling up for several decades.

I stepped up on the heap, reached up into the branches, and plucked the ripest mango I could see. I broke the fruit open only to find that its center was nothing but runny black sludge. My nostrils were instantly laden with a rotten smell, and I threw the mango away. I repeated the failure when I tried another mango and another, and then another one. They were all the same. I jumped off the heap, disappointed that I wouldn’t get my fresh mango, and continued my walk into the forest, listening for the bell with every step. 

The further I went, the more fruit trees I found, all in the same condition as the mango tree. It was clear that the townsfolk weren’t interested in picking the fruits anymore; I just wondered what had changed those fruits. What were the people eating?

I let my thoughts float to the back of my head, as I could hear that I was nearing the origin of the sounds. Then I found it. The source, hidden away amongst the tree stems. A remnant of a time long ago, forgotten in days gone by.

It was a church.

This church appeared to be in the worst condition out of any building I had seen on the island. The facade showed notable cracks, the roof had caved in a long time ago, and not a single window remained unshattered. From the very top of the belltower emanated the sound of a church bell, chiming whenever a gust of wind was strong enough to make it move.

I proceeded to the narthex of the church and entered the nave. The only thing that looked worse than the exterior of the church was the interior. The pews had been claimed by vines and overgrown with moss. The pulpit was lying in the aisle, rotting away. As I walked up to the alter, I had to step over old wooden roof beams. At the altar, what had once been a woodcarving of the messiah had been defiled by water damage, and the baptismal font was polluted with mud.

Seeing that there wasn’t much to find inside, I walked out and found the adjacent graveyard. Much like the facade of the church, all the gravestones were cracked and overgrown with plants and moss. It was then I had a theory. Using my flick knife, I began to scrape off the moss on the gravestones and noted down every year of death.

After thoroughly checking, I found that my theory had been correct. All the headstones were several decades old, the most recent having been erected in 1820; There were no gravestones erected in this graveyard after 1821. The lack of new gravestones could be a result of laziness, lack of resources, or, perhaps, there was a more fantastical explanation.

Perhaps I should’ve cut my losses and gotten off the island at this point. But I didn’t. I was already tumbling down the rabbit hole, and I was going to see how deep it truly went. I looked to the sky and saw that the sun was setting. I stumbled my way back through the forest. When I finally got back in the town, it was almost completely dark.

When I walked back into the cottage, I was hit with a powerful stench of all the rotten potatoes and spoiled raw chicken in the world. At the table was Five, aggressively chewing on something. She was dining with a look of immense pleasure, a red liquid staining her pale face. Next to her on the table was a small red thing. It appeared to be a red apple without its stem. It was the first thing I had seen any of the townsfolk eating. I reached for the apple, and just as I was about to grab it, Five stopped eating and peered up at me, eyes burning with vexation. She put down her meal, jumped out of her chair, and picked up a knife. She grabbed me by the shoulder, pushing me up against the wall with surprising strength. I felt as the cold blade were up against my throat, heart pounding in my chest.

“The mountain’s fruits are not meant for just any soul, mainlander!” She pressed the blade up against my larynx. “Is that understood?”

I stood there, completely shaken, my tongue stumbling over itself as I tried to speak. Five pulled me closer to her, only to push me up against the wall harder, the back of my head smashing against the rough surface of the wall.

“IS THAT UNDERSTOOD?” She loudly repeated.

I nodded, trying not to get cut by the knife. She moved the knife down, let go of my shoulder, and sat down by the table, continuing her dinner. I averted my eyes and walked into my bedchamber. After closing the door, I felt the top of my head. There was no blood. Then I felt the spot where she had held the knife. It was bleeding. The knife had made a small cut. Even through the closed door, I could hear the smacking lips and slobbering sounds as Five continued to devour her feast. I took out my first aid kit, grabbed an alcohol pad, and pressed it on the wound, the stinging sensation being instantaneous. I wrapped a strip of gauze lightly around my throat and lied down on the slab of wood in my sleeping bag. Eventually, once the horrible sounds from the kitchen had subsided, I managed to fall asleep. 

*I was standing in the center of the town. I looked to my right and saw my parents. They were criticizing me, as usual. I looked to my left and saw a group of my colleagues. They were looking at me, pointing fingers and laughing, recognizing me for the outcast I was. I felt the ground slowly rising and falling, like I was standing on a giant balloon slowly inflating before slowly deflating. I looked up at the mountain and witnessed a wide crevasse forming on its surface. The air was filled with sounds of mayhem as rock broke away from stone until the crevasse neared 20 meters in width. It fully opened to reveal an enormous eye hiding within the mountain. An eye looking directly at me, my parents, and my colleagues.*

*For a brief moment, I just peered into the eye as it peered into mine. Then the screaming began. Screams so bloodcurdling and horrific that they still haven’t left my mind to this day. The screams sounded like a choir of sufferers being skinned alive before being burned and dismembered. As the screams droned on, the eye just kept staring at me, indifferent to the sounds of suffering.*

I jolted awake in sweat-stained clothes. I was back in the bedchamber, but I had not escaped the screams. I could still hear them very clearly through the walls of the cottage. I pulled out the flick knife from my bag and curled up in my sleeping bag, listening for any additional sounds, waiting for something to burst into my room. Throughout the night I didn’t hear anything but the scream: no footsteps, no talking, nor anything else. The screams never traveled closer, and the door to my bedchamber remained shut. At some point fatigue overcame me, and I drifted off to slumber once again. I awoke in the morning feeling exhausted. Silence had settled, and I felt a wave of calmness wash over me. Though the screams had ceased, the stench of Five’s dinner still hung in the air, a stench so foul that I could feel it penetrating every orifice of my face, causing me nausea and dizziness.

In quick strides, trying not to breathe in, I walked out of the cottage. I began another stroll through the town, and, though it didn’t seem to be more than a couple hours since sun-up, the town was already bustling with the sounds of the townsfolk occupied with their work. I was caught up in my own thoughts about my dream and the previous day when, suddenly, I felt a hand grab my shoulder. Startled, I spun around, elbowing Cottonmouth and knocking him to the ground. 

“Oh, I’m sorry, sir. You scared me.” I said as he lay on the ground.

As I reached out my hand to help him up, I noticed that the sounds around me had stopped. I took a glance. Every single one of the townsfolk had stopped their work and was staring straight at me. Cottonmouth chuckled as he got to his feet. He dusted himself off and raised his hand into the air.

“Do not worry, it was merely an accident.” He said loudly.

The townsfolk turned back to their work, and the town was alive with sound once again. Cottonmouth looked at me, and without any further greeting, he said:

“Is it painful to carry such weight, mainlander?”

“Pardon?”

“You carry a lot of things inside that head.” The man said. “The kind of things that would drive most people mad.”

“Sir, I’m not exactly sure what you’re talking about.”

“This morning, I spoke with His Eminence.” Cottonmouth smiled. “He wishes to apologize for disturbing your sleep. He just wanted to see what you are thinking about these days.”

“Oh,” I said, not really knowing how to answer. “I slept fine. Wasn’t disturbed in the slightest.”

“Has anybody ever told you that you are a terrible liar?” Cottonmouth said with a smile that showed off his pearly whites.

“Okay.” I gave in. “I believe there might have been someone screaming outside the cottage.”

The man chuckled.

“Those were not screams.” Cottonmouth pointed to the mountain. “Every night, the west wind sweeps over the mountain and fills the holes up; when the wind is released, it makes that horrible ruckus you heard tonight.”

“Are you sure? They sounded like real screams.”

Cottonmouth put a hand on my shoulder. “You think too much. Are you aware of that?”

I got the sense that he was hiding something from me. I decided to inquire about something else.

“Sir, I’ve been thinking about something.”

“Yes?”

“You told me yesterday that you were a shepherd, but I don’t see any animals on the island.”

“I am not a shepherd in the traditional sense, mainlander.” He looked out at the people surrounding us. “This town is my herd, and, with His Eminence as my chaperone, I guide them to the endless pastures, where they can thrive and be who they are.”

“You still haven’t told me who His Eminence is.” I said. “And why did He make those holes in the mountain?”

“Your curiosity knows no bounds, does it?” Cottonmouth chuckled condescendingly. “Many years ago, His Eminence came riding in on the night sky. He came to Ipshire, weak, seeking asylum. The people of Ipshire graciously offered up their mountain, and His Eminence accepted.” Cottonmouth talked with a sense of pride. “As a reward, He reshaped his new home, allowing the people of Ipshire to delve deep into the mountain and pluck the fruits that He had so generously created himself.”

“Fruits? In a mountain?”

“Yes, mainlander. The most delectable fruits that mankind has ever known.” He said. “Now, I wish you a pleasant day. I hope that your stay is going well.”

With that, the man walked away, leaving me alone with my thoughts. I looked up at the façade of the mountain. I thought about what Cottonmouth had said about the screams as well as the fruits coming from the mountain.

If he wasn’t going to tell me, I would find it out by myself. I had found my next destination. I began walking, heading for the mountain.
