I’ve been sitting on this for three weeks because I didn’t know how to
explain it. I still don’t. But I need to write it down somewhere, and
nosleep feels like the only place where people won’t just tell me I
dreamed it.

This happened in early October, about a month after my girlfriend Megan
and I moved into our new place in Columbus, Ohio.

-–

A little background on me: I’m 6’1”, hit the gym four or five times a
week, the kind of guy who gets described as “a big dude” in casual
conversation. I grew up here in Columbus, went to Ohio State, and came
back after graduation to work in logistics management downtown. The last
few years were good to me — promotions came faster than I expected, I
saved aggressively, and when Megan and I started talking seriously about
the future, we decided to pool our money and buy something real.

Her parents and mine both chipped in, and together we were able to put a
down payment on a condo in one of those newer high-rises near the Short
North. Top floor unit, 18th floor. The building is one of those luxury
towers positioned right in the middle of a large residential complex —
massive green space, beautifully landscaped, the kind of place where
even though you’re five minutes from the heart of the city, you
genuinely cannot hear street noise. Not a sound.

We moved in mid-September. No housewarming party yet — we’d been so
slammed with the move that we hadn’t even told most of our friends the
new address. We figured we’d do a proper get-together once we had the
place fully set up.

-–

It was a Friday night. Maybe 8:30, 9 PM. Already dark outside. We’d just
finished dinner and were on the couch watching TV, that comfortable
half-awake thing you do after a long week.

Then we heard someone shouting from outside.

A man’s voice. Deep, muffled by distance, but if you concentrated you
could make it out — and what it was saying was Megan’s name.

Megan’s full name. Not a nickname. Her full name, the way someone who
actually knows you says it.

I looked at her. “Is someone calling you?”

She tilted her head, listening. Her expression shifted. “…I think so?”

She got up and jogged out to the balcony. Our unit has one of those
open-concept layouts where the living room flows directly into a wide
balcony through a sliding glass door — we usually kept it open for the
breeze.

I followed her out, curious. Eighteen floors down, the courtyard was lit
by those warm amber path lights the complex uses, and I could make out a
figure standing in the green space below. Too far down to see a face.
But I could tell it was a tall man, broad-shouldered, standing
completely still with his head tilted up toward us.

The second I registered that — the stillness, the upward stare — the
figure moved.

I don’t mean he started walking. I mean he *launched*. Like a sprinter
off the blocks. One instant he was standing still in the middle of the
courtyard, the next he was a blur crossing the distance to our
building’s entrance in what felt like three or four seconds. I heard the
lobby doors bang open from eighteen floors up.

Megan grabbed my arm. Her face had gone pale.

“Did you recognize him?” I asked.

She shook her head. Her voice was shaking. “I don’t know who that is. We
don’t know anyone in this building. We haven’t even told people we
moved.”

She was right. We’d been so caught up in everything that our close
friends still thought we were at the old apartment across town. Nobody
knew this address. Nobody should have known her name.

I told her to go to the bedroom and lock the door. She knows I can
handle myself physically — I think that’s the only reason she listened
instead of arguing. I stayed in the living room, sitting on the couch,
watching the front door.

I waited seven, eight minutes. Nothing. No knock. No sound in the
hallway.

Megan cracked the bedroom door and said, “Can you just check? Like,
listen at the door?”

I went to the front door and looked through the peephole first. The
motion-sensor lights in the hallway hadn’t triggered — it was completely
dark out there, which meant nothing had moved in the corridor. I eased
the door open an inch and listened.

Silence.

I opened it a little more and leaned out. Our unit is one of only two on
this floor, elevator and stairwell around a bend at the end of the hall.
Nothing in either direction.

I figured: I’m already out here. Might as well check the elevator, make
sure nobody’s on their way up.

I told Megan to lock the deadbolt behind me and not open it for anyone.
She started to protest but I’d already pulled the door shut. The bang of
the heavy security door echoed down the hall — triggered every motion
light on the floor at once.

And in that sudden flash of light, out of the corner of my eye, I saw
something in the emergency stairwell doorway.

A shape. There and gone in less than a second.

I crossed the hall in about four steps and yanked the stairwell door
open.

Empty. The concrete shaft went up and down in silence, not a sound, not
a footstep.

I stood there for a moment, then told myself I’d blinked at the wrong
time, that the sudden light had played tricks. I went to check the
elevator panel — the floor indicator read 8, steady. In our building,
the elevators default to the 8th floor when idle. If someone had ridden
up, it would have moved. It hadn’t.

So either he’d climbed eighteen flights of stairs — which would explain
why he hadn’t shown up yet — or something else was going on.

I decided to go down and meet him halfway.

Floor by floor, I descended the stairwell. Every landing: empty. Every
corridor I could see through the door windows: empty. I made it all the
way to the lobby.

The lobby is normally bright and busy even at that hour — our building
has a concierge desk, a little seating area, people coming and going.
But tonight every light was off. The space was completely dark. Not dim.
Dark. No concierge. Not a single person.

I pushed through the lobby doors into the courtyard.

Also empty. This is 8:30 on a Friday in a complex of several hundred
units. There are always dog-walkers, people coming back from dinner,
couples on the benches. Tonight: nobody. The amber path lights buzzed
quietly over an empty green space.

I walked to the spot where I’d seen the figure standing. Looked up at
our building. Our balcony light was on — I’d turned it on before coming
down — and I could see the glow of the living room behind it.

For some reason — I can’t fully explain this, it just felt right — I
cupped my hands and called up to Megan.

A beat of silence. Then a small silhouette appeared at the balcony
railing, leaning out. Megan. Short hair, unmistakably her.

I was about to call up again to tell her everything was fine —

And then a second shape appeared beside her.

Taller. Much taller. Leaning out over the railing next to her, looking
down at me.

I couldn’t see a face from eighteen floors below. But the height, the
shoulders, the build —

It looked exactly like me.

I stopped breathing.

*How did someone get into the apartment? Did she open the door? The
deadbolt — she was supposed to —*

I didn’t wait for answers. I ran back to the entrance, took the
stairwell, took the stairs two and three at a time, didn’t stop until I
hit our floor gasping. I jammed my key into the lock — and before I
could turn it, the door opened from inside.

Megan. Looking at me like I’d lost my mind.

I grabbed her by the wrist and pulled her into the hallway, stepped in
front of her, and looked into the apartment.

Living room light on. TV still going. No one on the couch. No one in the
kitchen. I went room by room, every closet, under the bed, the balcony.
Nothing. No one.

I came back and told her what I’d seen from the courtyard — someone
standing on our balcony right next to her.

She stared at me. “What are you talking about? I’ve been alone this
whole time. I locked the deadbolt the second you left. I didn’t open the
door for anyone. There was no one on the balcony with me — I went out
there by myself when I heard you call.”

We argued about it, gently, the way you do when you’re both scared. She
was certain. I was certain.

Then something hit me and the floor seemed to tilt under my feet.

The figure in the courtyard — tall, broad, standing very still, looking
up.

The shape I’d caught in the stairwell doorway when the lights came on.

The silhouette on the balcony next to Megan.

Every single one of them had my height. My build. My proportions.

-–

We didn’t sleep much that night. I’ve been thinking about it for three
weeks and I keep coming back to the same wall. Megan was inside the
entire time. The elevator hadn’t moved. The stairwell was empty every
time I checked. The lobby was inexplicably dark and abandoned. The
courtyard was empty.

And whatever was standing next to my girlfriend on that balcony looked
exactly like me.

I’ve read about doppelgangers. I know what folklore says about seeing
your own double. I’m a rational person — I work in supply chain, I deal
in facts and numbers — and I genuinely cannot construct a rational
explanation for this.

-–

I’m posting this tonight because something happened an hour ago that I
haven’t told Megan yet.

I was in the bathroom getting ready for bed. I leaned over the sink to
wash my face, and when I straightened up and looked in the mirror —

There was a two-second delay.

My reflection paused. Just for two seconds, maybe less. Me with my hands
at my sides, water dripping from my face. And my reflection stood there
with its hands at its sides, looking back at me, before it caught up.

I’ve been standing in the hallway outside the bathroom for the last
forty minutes.

I don’t want to look in mirrors anymore.

-–

*If anyone has experienced something like this, please comment. I am
completely serious. I don’t know what is in my building — or what is in
my apartment — or what has been wearing my face.*
