As a journalist, I’ve spent my career documenting the worst things
humans do to each other.

People kill for money. People kill for love. People kill because they’re
angry. For sex. For drugs.

But last night, somebody dumped fifteen bodies around the fountain in
Maidnach Park. What the fuck is the motive for that?

The police have no leads. The radio has been nothing but static and
blaring sirens. Which is a problem, because I’m supposed to be covering
the story.

The thing is, I wasn’t even supposed to be here.

Twenty-four hours ago, I was driving to a house I’d bought for less than
the price of a decent used car. Which should have been my first clue
that this town was a little fucked in the head.

I just moved here, trying to convince myself that moving here wasn’t the
biggest mistake of my goddamn life. 

I came to Briarwood to bury the “why” of everything.

Arizona had become a tragedy, for lack of a better word.

I figured I could hide in the hot, red dirt of Oklahoma.  

But this town is not peaceful. It feels like the heavens are just
waiting for a reason to fall to hell.

The house I was able to get wasn’t much to look at. The paint was
peeling, the floorboards creaked, and the fireplace was cracked in at
least four different places by my count. 

But… It was mine.

So I unpacked what little I’d brought with me and tried not to think too
much about the shitty details. 

Then I turned on the TV.

I didn’t think much of the segment at the time. Later, while I was
working on the story, I pulled the station’s archived broadcast and had
it transcribed for my notes.

This is what it said:

A developing story tonight out of Briarwood, where residents across town
are reporting a series of disturbing and unexplained incidents.

Over the past several weeks, multiple households have reported hearing
knocking sounds from inside their walls and beneath their floorboards,
often late at night. Authorities say they have been unable to determine
the source of the noises, and at this time, no official explanation has
been provided.

“I heard it again last night,” said one longtime Briarwood resident. “It
sounded like somebody was inside the walls trying to get out.”

While investigators have not confirmed any connection, some residents
believe the reports may be linked to the recent disappearances of two
local girls. Out of respect for the families, we are not naming the
missing juveniles at this time.

Police continue to urge residents to remain calm and report any
suspicious activity.

Anyone with information related—

I turned off the television. 

It was just way too depressing. 

The low settling of the house wasn’t much better. 

I stood there for a moment, staring out the living room window.
Briarwood stretched out beyond the glass in browns and greens.

Everything about the town just felt… exhausted.

Instead of waiting around in an empty house, I figured I should probably
get groceries, at least. 

I’d spent the last two days living off gas station food and fast-food
burgers, which wasn’t exactly sustainable. Besides, sitting alone in a
half-empty house listening to news reports about missing girls wasn’t
doing my mental health any favors.

So I grabbed my coat, locked my door, and hopped in the old Ford.

I was just about to leave for the grocery store when I noticed movement
next door. 

That caught my attention almost immediately because, as far as I knew,
nobody lived there.

The house sat on the edge of the property line, half-hidden behind a
cluster of dying oak trees. I’d spent most of this morning looking at it
through my living room window. The place looked abandoned—sagging,
rotten porch and all. 

Then the front door opened. 

A tall man stepped outside wearing a brown suit and black leather
shoes. 

I remember thinking he looked completely out of place in a town like
this.

That, and the house looked like it belonged to a drifter.

The man looked like he was on his way to argue a court case.

He was fumbling with his keys when his phone rang.

Now, I wasn’t trying to eavesdrop. At least, not initially. But the
neighborhood was quiet enough that I heard him anyway. 

“Are you serious?” he snapped. 

He started pacing a few steps across the porch. 

“No. No, listen to me. Where the fuck would I know where Sadie is?”

I froze.

The man kept talking.

“I haven’t spoken to her in weeks.”

A pause, then:

“No, I’m not doing this again.”

He ended the call and shoved the phone back in his pocket.

A minute later, he climbed into a dark Chevy Impala and peeled out of
the driveway.

I sat there for a few seconds…

…Sadie.

I knew that name, or at least remembered it from somewhere.

For a moment, I just sat there staring through the windshield while my
brain tried to catch up with the last twenty-four hours.

Then it all clicked.

The missing girls. The posters I’d seen stapled to poles and taped to
bus stops when I first drove into town—the news broadcast.

Sadie.

I reached for my phone and typed: Briarwood missing girls.

The first result loaded almost immediately. 

Missing:

Sadie, 16.

Molly, 17.

Last seen nine days ago.

I stared at the photographs. Both girls were smiling. Everybody smiles
in missing person photos. I don’t know why that always makes me feel
worse. 

I looked back toward the “abandoned-but-not” house. 

The man in the brown suit had already disappeared down the road. 

For a moment, I considered letting it all go. 

People say similar names all the time. Maybe Sadie was his daughter, or
his niece. 

Maybe I was just doing what I do best and building a conspiracy out of
coincidence.

I locked my phone and tossed it into the passenger seat.

Then I looked back at the grocery list I’d written that morning.

Milk, bread, coffee, normal people groceries. 

I started my car, backed out of the driveway, and instead of turning
toward the grocery store, I followed the taillights way out in the
distance, watching it disappear over the roads. 

Professional curiosity. 

That’s what I told myself. 

I lost him twice at traffic lights and found him again both times.

He parked in front of a law firm on the east side of town. 

Otis & Reynolds, Attorneys at Law.

I pulled into a spot across the street and killed the engine. He was
already inside before I cut the radio.

I sat there for a minute, feeling extremely stupid.

I’d followed a total stranger across town because he said a name on his
porch. 

Something that would’ve gotten me laughed out of a newsroom.

I reached for the dial to change the station when the second report
hit. 

This time, as soon as I heard “New discovery” I pulled out my voice
recorder: 

Local police were led to a remote site outside Briarwood following an
anonymous tip. 

At the location, investigators discovered a series of makeshift altars,
each adorned with personal items belonging to the victims found in
Maidnach Park. Alongside the altars were life-sized effigies, crudely
fashioned from straw and the victims’ clothing. 

While these effigies appeared to correspond to yesterday’s park victims,
investigators also found at least twenty additional effigies with no
known victims attached. 

Authorities have not yet identified the purpose of the site or any
suspects. A new body was also discovered at the location. Cause of death
has not been determined.

I turned off the radio. Twenty effigies with no known victims attached.
I wrote that down in my notebook and sat there for a moment.

Then, I saw the law firm door open.  
 
The man in the brown suit came out first. He was followed by a woman in
a dark blazer and black heels. She was talking before the door even
finished closing behind her.

I cracked my window and put my recorder up to it. 

The air was nice enough to let their voices carry at least somewhat. I
managed to pick up:

“I understand- urgency, Mr. Harkness,” she said. 

He stopped walking and turned to face her. 

“Do you? We need to- up, Ms. Reynolds. Time is not something- have an
abundance of right now. Don’t- me come back here.”

She placed a hand on his arm. “I understand. But this town is- edge. We
can’t afford- missteps.”

Their voices dropped past what the microphone could pick up after that.
I tried positioning it better and got nothing but the wind. 

He nodded once, then walked back to his car without saying anything
else.

Ms. Reynolds stood on the sidewalk watching him go. A fearful look on
her face.

Then she went back inside. 

I sat there with my notebook open, with everything I could hear them
say. 

Time is not something we have an abundance of.

I decided to drive home; it was getting late by that point, and I
figured I could just grab groceries tomorrow.

All the street lights were out when I rolled into my driveway. Which
wouldn’t have bothered me as much if it weren’t for my porch light being
off as well.

I remembered leaving it on.

I sat in the car for a second, trying to convince myself it was
nothing—I mean, bulbs blow out all the time. 

When I finally worked up the courage to leave the car, I noticed my
front door was unlocked. 

I stood outside my car just staring at the door. It was cracked. Just
slightly.

I called Emily, my sister. She was across the country, going to school;
looking back, I was never able to tell her congratulations in person.

I don’t know why I call her, really. She was my little sister, and yet,
she always made me feel safe.

It rang twice before she picked up. 

“Amelia?” 

I couldn’t speak right away. Something about her voice made my entire
body freeze in an instant.

“Amelia, why are you outside?” 

My hand tightened around the phone. My eyes went to my front window.

“Come inside,” she said. “It’s so cold out there.” 

She paused for what felt like forever. 

“I’m home, Amelia.” 

Then the call ended.
