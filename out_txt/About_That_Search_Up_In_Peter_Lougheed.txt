I was a fish cop in Kananaskis for most of my adult life.

Conservation officer, technically. People say park ranger because that’s
what TV taught them, and I never cared enough to correct it.

So, sure. Park ranger. Fish cop. Whatever helps.

I’m saying all this because I want you to understand something before I
get into the rest of it.

I knew that country.

Not in the poetic way people mean when they say that. I didn’t have some
spiritual bond with the mountains. I mean I knew which culverts washed
out every spring, which trailheads filled up by nine on a long weekend,
which families were going to try and drag a stroller up a trail that
would eat hiking boots, and which drainages turned a radio into an
expensive brick.

I knew where the elk crossed at dusk. I knew where the bears fed in late
summer. I knew which slopes held snow after everything else had melted,
and I knew how fast a blue sky over Upper Kananaskis could turn into
cold rain.

I knew what people did when they got lost.

And I knew what animals did to bodies.

What I found up past the lakes was neither.

Her name was Emily. She was fourteen.

Old enough to think she knew what she was doing, young enough that
everyone else was responsible for her anyway.

But the awful thing is, she did know what she was doing. That’s what I
keep coming back to.

She wasn’t one of those cases where a kid takes off in flip-flops with
half a bottle of pop and no jacket. She had boots. She had rain gear.
She had a whistle, a cheap compass, a little first aid kit, bear spray
she probably would have fumbled with if she’d needed it, a field guide,
two granola bars, trail mix, and one of those orange emergency blankets
that sound like a chip bag when you unfold them.

She was camping with her family up in Peter Lougheed. It was late
August. Still busy, but not stupid busy. The kind of week where the
mornings feel like summer and the evenings remind you Alberta doesn’t
owe you anything.

From what we pieced together, she left the campground after an argument
with her dad. Nothing dramatic. Teenage stuff. He wanted her to stay
close. She wanted to go take pictures. There was a bit of yelling,
probably less than everyone remembered after. Then she walked off with
her little day pack and her camera.

By supper, her mother was worried.

By dark, RCMP had the file and we were searching.

People like to imagine searches as these big, organized grids, and
sometimes they are. There are maps and radios and assignments and
everyone signs in and signs out. SAR does their thing. RCMP does theirs.
Public Safety gets involved if the country gets ugly enough. Parks staff
cover the bits they know best. Volunteers show up with good intentions
and bad footwear.

But underneath all that, a search is mostly people walking through trees
saying someone’s name into a silence that does not care.

Emily!

Emily!

Can you hear us?

The first night was cold. Not freezing, but cold enough. We checked the
obvious places first. Trails. Lakeshore. Day-use areas. Outhouses. Camp
loops.

Nothing.

By morning, the mood changed.

It always does.

The first few hours, people still talk about the missing person like
they’re going to be annoyed when found. “She’s going to be grounded
forever.” “Bet she’ll be hungry.” “She probably hunkered down
somewhere.”

Then the light changes. The radios get quieter. Parents stop asking when
and start asking where. Nobody says it, but everyone feels the same
little adjustment.

After twenty-four hours in the mountains, you are not looking for a
runaway anymore.

I was the one who found her first camp.

It was tucked under a stand of spruce above a drainage, maybe an hour
and a half from where she’d left the maintained trail. Not impossible
country, but not friendly either. Deadfall, loose rock, soft black mud
in the low spots. The kind of place where every step takes just enough
extra effort that you don’t notice yourself getting tired.

She had scraped together a little shelter between two downed trees. Bad
angle, too low, wouldn’t have shed rain well, but it was something. She
had gathered spruce boughs for insulation. Not enough, but more than
most adults think to do.

There was one granola wrapper folded flat and tucked under a stone. Her
field guide was there, too, damp along the bottom, open to a section on
edible berries. She had marked a page with a little strip torn from the
wrapper.

I remember standing there looking at that tiny strip of plastic and
feeling so angry I could barely breathe.

Not at her.

At the country. At her father. At myself. At whatever stupid chain of
little things puts a child under a tree with a field guide and half a
granola bar.

I called it in.

We found signs of her after that. A footprint in mud. A broken branch. A
bit of orange foil snagged on deadfall. One place where she had probably
sat for a while with her back against a tree. We found three pages torn
from the field guide, folded into little triangles and wedged into
branches at eye level.

The second camp was worse.

She had made it in a shallow hollow below a rocky slope. No shelter this
time, not really. Just the emergency blanket pulled over a depression
and weighted at the corners.

There are things you learn to do in my line of work. You learn to keep
your face still. You learn to talk in a steady voice even when someone
is screaming. You learn that sympathy is useful and panic is not.

That second camp told us she had been alive at least two nights. Maybe
three. There was a little pile of berries she had picked and not eaten,
which meant either she wasn’t sure or something had scared her off them.
There was a ration wrapper, licked clean. There was no water bottle,
which worried me until we found where she’d been getting water from the
drainage.

And then, sometime after that, she climbed.

That was the part none of us understood at first.

She had been moving downhill or trying to. Lost people often do.
Downhill means water. Water means people, roads, trails, something. It
isn’t always true, but it feels true when your brain is starting to
cook.

But after the second camp, her route turned up and east across broken
rock.

Maybe she thought she saw the lake. Maybe she heard us. Maybe she saw a
clearing and thought she could get a signal. Maybe she was cold and
scared and fourteen and had run out of good choices.

Then I found it.

At the time, it was just a bad place on a bad slope. A band of exposed
rock above a narrow cut where water ran in spring and after storms. By
late August it was mostly dry, just a black thread of damp stone down
the centre and a few pools full of mosquito larvae.

I was alone when I found it.

That wasn’t ideal, and I know someone will give me shit for it if this
ever gets around. But searches aren’t clean. Radios were spotty in that
drainage, we were spread thin, and I had good reason to think I was
following her route. I had bear spray on my chest, a sidearm, and a
shotgun in hand because there had been fresh bear sign lower down and I
was moving toward what might be human remains.

I found her camera first.

It was smashed, lying in the rocks below the slope. Pink case. Little
stickers on it. One of those cheap digital ones parents buy because
phones are expensive and teenagers drop things. The memory card was
cracked. Later, someone told me they might be able to recover something
from it. I never asked if they did.

A few metres past that, I found blood.

Not a lot at first. Just dark smears on stone. Then more. Then hair
caught on a sharp edge of rock.

The fall itself wasn’t hard to read after that.

She had come down from above. Slipped or stumbled or grabbed something
that didn’t hold. There was a scrape where one boot had skidded. A
broken branch lower down. Then the impact point.

I stood there for a long time.

Longer than I should have.

She had been so careful. I had retraced her steps. Her little camp, her
shelter, her field guide and little rations. She had done everything
right.

But there I was, looking at where she had fallen.

This girl was missing for days, and alive for most of them. Maybe fine
is too strong a word, but alive. Thinking. Trying. Making choices.

Then sometime in the last day or two, maybe less, she fell.

And landed there.

Dashed against those rocks.

Maybe she was tired. Maybe she slipped. However it happened, she fell
there.

But that’s not where I found her body.

It had been moved.

At first I thought animal.

Your mind goes to the useful explanation first. Bear. Cougar. Coyotes.
Something with teeth.

I have seen what scavengers do. I have seen bears cover carcasses. I
have seen coyotes spread remains in a way that looks personal if you
don’t know better.

So I stood there and tried to make the scene become animal activity.

There was blood at the impact point, but no drag trail leaving it. No
long smear. No torn clothing caught on stone. No obvious prints in the
damp mud around the cut. There were places where something should have
stepped. Something heavy enough to move a body should have left
something.

I don’t mean there were no tracks. There were mine, from coming in.
There were old deer tracks lower down. There was bear sign from maybe
that morning or the day before. There were little bird marks in the mud
like stitching.

But between the fall site and where I found her, nothing made sense.

She was thirty, maybe thirty-five metres away, tucked beneath an
overhang at the base of the slope. Not hidden exactly.

She was on her back with her arms at her sides. Her jacket had been
pulled straight. Her boots were together. Her hair had been arranged
around her head in a damp fan, full of grit and spruce needles.

I need to be careful here.

I had the shotgun up by then.

Not dramatic. I didn’t rack it like a movie. I didn’t say anything
clever. I just had it up because I was alone in a drainage with a dead
child and a scene that would not become any kind of animal sign I knew.

The woods were very quiet.

That happens sometimes. People think forests are always full of sound,
and they are, but the sound comes and goes. Wind drops. Birds stop. Your
ears start pulling at the silence, trying to make thread out of it.

I scanned the slope. The timber above. The deadfall below. The black
spaces between the spruce trunks.

Nothing.

No bear huff. No branch crack. No raven. No squirrel losing its mind at
me from a tree.

Just Emily.

I stood like that until my arms started to ache. Then I felt stupid.
More than stupid. Ashamed.

There are things you do around the dead. You preserve the scene. You
don’t touch what you don’t need to touch. You call it in. You wait for
RCMP. You don’t stand there sweeping a shotgun over the body of a
fourteen-year-old girl.

So I lowered the gun.

Not all the way.

Just enough to feel human again.

That was when Emily took a breath.

I know.

I know what bodies do. Air moves. Gases build up. Muscles settle. A dead
deer can make a noise that will ruin a city person’s week. I’ve heard
it. I’ve seen enough to know the dead are not always as still as people
want them to be.

This was not that.

Her chest came up. Heavy, hard and fast and shrinking just a fast. Like
a sigh or a sob.

God, the sound.

I had the shotgun on her before I knew I’d moved.

The bead settled on her chest. On a child.

I remember that more clearly than almost anything else.

Her chest did not move again.

I kept the gun up anyway.

Then something behind me.

Not a branch breaking. Not a footstep exactly.

I turned too fast and nearly slipped.

There was nothing behind me.

Just trees. Deadfall. The narrow cut leading down toward thicker timber.

Then a sound. Subtle but deafening in the silence down there. Something
hiding.

Farther left.

I moved the shotgun with it.

Nothing.

I backed away from her. I should not have. Every procedure says secure
the scene, preserve evidence, do not contaminate.

I backed away with the shotgun up, trying to watch everything at once.

Emily did not move. A wind came down, moving some leaves.

The woods made one more sound, low and close enough that I felt it in my
teeth.

I don’t know what it was.

That is the honest answer.

There is a version of this where I say I made a tactical retreat. That’s
not what happened. I walked backwards until my heel caught on a root.

I turned and walked.

Then I walked faster.

Then I was moving through the trees with branches slapping my jacket and
my breath too loud in my own ears.

I did not run.

I want credit for that, though I probably don’t deserve any.

A few times, I thought I heard something moving behind me. Not crashing.
Not chasing. Just following at a pace.

Once, halfway down the drainage, I heard something. Something.

Once, I heard what sounded like a person exhaling.

I kept going.

My radio hissed twice and caught half a word from somebody on the ridge,
then died again.

By the time I reached the truck, my hands were shaking so badly I missed
the lock twice.

On the third try I jammed the key in with a zipper click and popped the
door open. Climbed in. Locked it.

The truck started on the first turn.

No sputter. It started like it always did. The A/C kicked on and blew
cold air into my face. The seatbelt chime started. The radio came back
in halfway through some Calgary station. It was so quiet in the truck.
Maybe not quiet. Normal. The sounds things are supposed to make.

I picked up the radio.

And called it in.
