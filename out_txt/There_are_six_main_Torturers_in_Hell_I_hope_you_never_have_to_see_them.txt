I ran up to the stretch limousine parked on the side of the highway
outside of the Vegas nightclub I escaped from.

​

The jacket of my powder-colored suit had torn in the back, and I could
smell the sweat I had built up. None of it was from dancing.

​

The vehicle had a glossy snowy tinge. The windows had a black enough
tint to resemble squares of ink.

​

I jogged around to the front and knocked on the driver’s side window as
pedestrians walked by and gawked at me.

​

The chauffeur rolled the glass down and faced me with a look of concern.
His name was Lucas. Although we had met hours ago, I was afraid he
either would not recognize me or would be unwilling to transport me. My
group had paid a hefty sum to use his service.

​

“I need you to take me back to the Westgate Hotel,” I said.

​

He unlocked the car and I crawled into the back. The comfortable
ivory-hued leather seats were plush as I settled in. A flat-screen
showed a boxing match.

​

Lucas turned around to face me after he pressed the lock button on all
the doors.

​

“Are the cops searching for you?”

​

The question was surreal but understandable. My knuckles felt as though
they had broken, and my right hand was starting to swell. I had a small
hematoma on my forehead. He must have seen the rip on my suit jacket in
his rearview.

​

“No,” I said, though my answer was far from confident.

​

“I don’t want to get an aiding and abetting charge if you did something
you shouldn’t have. Where’s the rest of your bachelor party?”

​

I reached into my pocket and pulled out a stack of bills. I gave the
newfound would-be legal advisor a few hundred.

​

“It’s the city of sin,” I said, “and I know you’ve seen worse than me.
I’m unaware of where my boys are. Give me your phone number so if they
call me, I can contact you to go get them and bring them wherever they
want to go. In the meantime, I’d appreciate it if you didn’t treat me
like I’m a criminal.”

​

Normal, well-adjusted people did not get into a bar fight. My devout
Catholic family would not like the circumstances I had placed myself in.

​

It was not the first scuffle I had been in. Still, it had been a while
since I engaged in such a nonsensical act of violence.

​

I could remember defending myself against a few individuals during the
chaos. There was one, in particular, I had dealt with longer than the
others in the middle of the incident. I could not bring back his face. I
assumed it was the adrenaline or the lack of fluorescence within the
establishment.

​

The driver accepted my money and wrote his phone number down on a blue
napkin. It had the letters TSC. These stood for his company, The
Sweetest Champagne. Printed below the title in bold lettering were the
words ‘luxury common carrier.’

​

After he handed it to me, I patted myself down and realized I lost my
device.

​

Lucas pulled onto the main road and headed towards the hotel. I cracked
the window to let air in. A cascade of neon flashing lights passed by
outside. This glorified wayward adult playground of depravity lost its
allure to me in less than an hour.

​

“How does the other guy look?” Lucas asked with a wry smile.

​

My head hurt and all I wanted was for the evening to end. I did not know
where my group went, and that became a bigger concern than the events
which had transpired.

​

“A chair was flung in my direction by another patron at the bar who felt
as though I had flirted with his fiancé,” I said.

​

“Did you?”

​

I did not answer.

​

The scene came back to me. After I stood, shattered bottles and
upside-down tables were everywhere. One of the bartenders pulled out a
baseball bat, and before I knew it we were fighting with staff. Not only
the bouncers, either. Even the janitor, who had to be at least fifty
years old and five foot one, felt the need to join the melee.

​

I took one guy to the ground, locked my arms around his neck, and choked
him unconscious. I let him stand again and he swung, so I gave him a
left hook and dropped him. After that, an entire entourage came in
through the front door and the last thing I could recall seeing was a
knife.

​

It was then I ran.

​

I laid my head back into the seat of the limousine and fell into a
slumber. The brightness of the marquees as they faded behind us gave me
speckles of light as I drifted off.

​

When I awoke, I stared out the window, and could not believe what I saw.
Some castles lined the side of the highway. Behind them were mountains
that appeared like giant clay prominences. Twisted trees dotted their
sides. The sky had purplish incandescence.

​

I took in a lung full of air and tried to rationalize what I was seeing.
There had to be a new Scottish-themed casino I was unaware of.

​

Something bumped against my ankle and rested on my feet.

​

I looked down and saw a rattlesnake with green scales. The hissing was
louder than the radio which blared in the background. Consistent
circular patterns dotted its body. My years in the California desert
gave me an understanding of which ones were venomous. This little guy
resembled the type I had seen growing up in the Mojave. They slithered
near the late-night parties I attended on the outskirts of the Air and
Space port.

​

I jumped and yelled for help as it wrapped itself around my leg. I
searched around for whatever I could use as a weapon. All I could find
was an Armand de Brignac bottle. It was one of many champagnes lined up
in the lower compartment of the door. I was ready to smash it into the
thing.

​

“I wouldn’t do that,” Lucas said as he peered at me in the mirror. “It’s
going to bite you. You’d also be cutting your shin wide open.”

​

“Why’d you put this thing in here with me, you psycho?”

​

He cranked on the gear shift and placed the vehicle in park.

​

I stared out at acres of woodland which had nothing but black pines and
miles of soil darker than charcoal. A fox scurried by. One of its legs
was missing.

​

I looked to my right and saw a woman in a blue dress approaching the
car. She was younger than me, had dark hair, and wore a jade necklace
around her neck. She hopped in and thanked the driver. Her voice was
dulcet.

​

I noticed how the hem of her dress fluttered on its own in the interior
of the vehicle. This struck me as odd, due to how there was no current
or air conditioning.

​

She sat across from me and carried a scroll in her hands. She looked at
me with wide eyes as the reptilian squeezed tighter.

​

“Who are you?”

​

“It’s not important, but your driver is Charon. When people die, as you
did back in the bar by a knife wound, their transition to hell begins.
Lucas is a ferryman with tons of experience. Still, he’s taken on the
form of Lucas, since it was one you were familiar with.”

​

Her words did not sink in as my stomach twisted.

​

“Our hierarchy had made it so panic does not set in immediately. It
causes more of a nervous breakdown when it’s not expected. Still, as the
person who does your processing, it is not in my best interest to lie to
you. You are going to a very grisly place. I also have the best offer to
take away some of the pain.”

​

The vehicle moved again. We rolled up a steep incline along a
mountainside with various chateaus built out of volcanic rock.

​

I tried to open the door in what would have been a suicidal attempt at
getting away. If what this woman said was true, I no longer had a
biological system susceptible to agony. I figured the risk was worth it.

​

The doors were still locked.

​

“You’re trolling me here,” I said as I folded my arms. “That’s a
ridiculous lie.”

​

She reached out to me. She placed her fingertips on my side.

​

I looked down and saw a part of her hand disappear into where my wound
was - the same place a blade had made entry earlier. The entire front of
my shirt blossomed with blood.

​

My disorientation must have made me not notice it, I thought.

​

I looked out and saw a group of people in the pine barrens. They writhed
together as one massive pit, and my mind could not accept what my eyes
were witnessing. They were all covered in glacial sleet. They snapped
off each other’s frost burnt ligaments.

​

“I know your sins,” she said as she rolled her shoulders back. “From the
time you pushed a boy off of the monkey bars in third grade to
embezzling money. The insurance company trusted you to be a good
employee. I can go on, but my knowledge of your occasional moral
bankruptcy makes little to no difference. I have a proposal, one you
would be wise to accept. I would like to recruit you to be one of our
Servants.”

​

“What would I be doing?” I asked as a lump formed in the back of my
throat.

​

“The first millennia, you would torture souls. You would use a variety
of white phosphorus weaponry to torment whoever we tell you to. After
that, your contract would expire, and, depending on how well you have
done, we will move you where we need you.”

​

“No,” I said. “I can’t bring that much pain on anyone. I like to fight
every once and again to remind myself I’m…well, alive. Yet that doesn’t
mean I want to injure or maim or torment other people who haven’t done
anything to me.”

​

I choked the words out and the horrifying reality of how things had
changed overcame me. I had the sensation of watching my entire moral
constitution erupt in flames.

​

“Fine,” she said as she undid the scroll. “Then you’re going to be on
the other side of it. Here are some of the servants you’re going to
meet.”

​

She showed me various etchings of human and monster hybrids. Each
portrait had a darkish-gray sediment used to illustrate them. Each one
had its title and name floating below them in calligraphic writing. The
job descriptions they carried were the types of punishment they doled
out. I knew, based on her verbiage, that I was to be one of their
subjects.

​

The first one she showed me was the Bothriurus. He was a towering figure
with scorpion stingers in replace of hands. His forehead had a bulbous
rhinoceros horn that protruded from it. His exposed legs had sores all
over them.

​

The second was Cressida. Her hair looked like mold, and it grew from her
flesh like a scabrous and infected layer. She held a fistful of ebon
needles and had a grotesque hunch.

​

The third was the Bastet. His torso had hundreds of thousands of goat
heads. Around his neck dangled a necklace of severed limbs.

​

The fourth was The Herald. He had a blindfold on. A tar-like liquid
drenched him. An emanation of steam came off his skin.

​

The fifth was the Storm Lion. The animalistic form had half of its head
missing. Parts of his brain matter splattered along his front. His
muscles had similar striations as those of a bodybuilder. His malformed
paws had shattered bones that pierced through his skin. Yet he had
something which stemmed from his mouth and a torn-apart mane. What stuck
out between his teeth was a rake with ends that looked sharper than
razors.

​

The sixth was Algol. The thing wore a cloak. Copperheads slithered out
from its esophagus. Her very skin had bubbles and blisters which
indicated perpetual and intense heat.

​

“You can avoid spending time with these demons if you sign here,” she
said as she pointed to a line at the bottom.

​

The snake’s head angled upwards to look at me. Its tongue flicked with a
frantic repetition, a signal for how hungry it was to put its toxin in
me. While I knew it could not kill whatever form I now inhabited, I did
not yearn for the wretchedness I would feel from it.

​

“I’ll do it,” I said.

​

She smiled and handed me a pen. The writing tool had vulture feathers at
the end with a tip chiseled from a hyena claw. She held up a fuchsia jar
of solvent for me to dip the instrument into.

​

I made a motion to stab her under the jaw with it. I telegraphed the
motion. She shoved me back with more force than a semi-truck. My ribs
broke. My chest cavity had crushed. I still had a hold of the pen, and I
used it to smash the window.

​

The snake bit down. I grabbed it to place it as far away from my body as
I could muster. I slammed it against the upholstery until I turned it
into a liquidized tube.

​

She screamed and wrapped her hands around my throat. I writhed and
managed to make it to the front seat. I could not get her off of me and
felt consciousness start to lift away.

​

I cracked Lucas in the back of the head with my free hand as she
screamed at me.

​

The limousine went past a rusted metal guard rail and slipped downhill.
Craggy formations of andesite and obsidian rushed by in a blur.

​

We crashed into a boulder.

​

The airbags went off and Lucas’ head whipped against the wheel before he
went limp.

​

The woman let go of me and was flung forward through the windshield and
against the megalithic stone.

​

I crawled out of the limousine. I heard a river nearby, and a smell that
had a combination of acrid pollution, ash, and cedar embers.

​

“Sign it,” a screech bombarded where I lay and burst my eardrums. I
stared up at the darkening horizon and saw the woman in the blue dress.
She walked up to me with the scroll and kicked me in the head. I spat my
teeth out and tasted dirt mixed in with the salt from my blood.

​

“Leave me alone,” I groaned.

​

“Sign the paper, or you’ll spend eternity wishing for it all to end.”

​

I looked up and saw something blackening the sun. It was a moth with
wings of silver the size of a city block. The main bill of its form had
body parts sewn together with a needle and thread.

​

“It’s going to come closer to feast on you unless you sign it,” she
said.

​

I submitted. I gave my signature.

​

*

​

My eyes opened. All I saw was an equilateral shape of light above me.

​

I stared around a sterile white room. There was someone else in the bed
next to mine. A flimsy curtain separating us gave an unconvincing sense
of privacy.

​

I was in a medical facility.

​

IVs lined my arms. Heart monitoring pads were all along my chest. I
reached up and pressed the help button on the remote control.

​

A nurse came in.

​

The oxygen left my lungs as I laid eyes on the healthcare assistant.

​

She was identical to the woman in the blue dress I had dealt.

​

“Where am I?”

​

“You’re in Summerlin Hospital medical center.”

​

“What happened?”

​

“You were in a fight at a nightclub. Someone stabbed you. You suffered
penetrating abdominal trauma. It didn’t hurt any vital organs, although
it was deep enough to come close. You lost a lot of blood. You required
surgery.”

​

“Who brought me here?”

​

“A friendly limousine driver.”

​

“How long have I been…”

​

“You were comatose for two hours. You stepped out of our front entrance
and hit the ground. I’m unsure if it was the concussion or the
hemorrhage which caused the coma. I can notify the doctor to come and
speak with you if you want exact clarification.”

​

I declined.

​

It was five days until my mobility came back, and another week before
they finally discharged me.

​

I went back to my white-collar desk job under the guise of how nothing
changed. I pushed down the whole experience. I did not want to give
credence to the situation as being anything other than a dream. One
wrought by alcohol, adrenaline, injury, and anesthetic.

​

Years later, I went to confession. I had not been in the chair since I
was a teenager. I knew I should seek out therapy, but I reasoned this
was as good of a start as any.

​

I told the Priest everything about the limousine ride, and the events in
Vegas leading up to it.

​

“A voice beckons to me,” the man of the cloth said. “It is telling you
to pick up hunting. It wants you to practice for what awaits you soon.”
