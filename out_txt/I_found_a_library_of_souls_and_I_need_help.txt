I dont have much time. I don’t even know if this is the right place to
ask—Reddit, a missing persons forum, some dark corner of the internet
where people talk about things that shouldn’t exist. But I need someone
to see this. Someone who knows. I saw something I can’t explain. Since I
came back out, I’ve felt… marked. Watched. The world looks the same, but
it isn’t. My chest still hurts where something touched me. I’ve got
until dawn to do something, or I won’t get to leave again. If anyone out
there has seen the cards with screaming faces, the black iron door, the
eye that blinks from inside metal—please, please tell me what you know.
I’m not crazy, I’m just out of time.

The rain hadn’t let up for three days. By ten o’clock on a Tuesday
night, the museum had become a mausoleum of dripping gutters and
fogged-up security monitors. I sat in my little cubicle with a thermos
of black coffee and a paperback whose spine I’d cracked in six places,
listening to the building settle.

The job was simple: twelve-hour shifts, no visitors, no alarms, just the
occasional rat skittering across marble and the low hum of dying
fluorescent tubes. I’d been night security at the Harland Museum of
Natural History for two years, and in all that time the most exciting
thing that had happened was a homeless man pissing on a stuffed grizzly
bear.

That was fine by me. I didn’t want excitement. I wanted silence,
routine, and a paycheck that let me pretend I wasn’t still dragging
around the weight of things I couldn’t change.

My name is Alex Mercer. I’m twenty-eight years old, and I’ve been
running from guilt long enough to know it doesn’t tire. Three years ago,
my best friend Danny bled out in a wreck I walked away from with nothing
but a cut on my forehead. I’d been driving. I’d had two beers, nothing
insane, but enough that my reaction time was a half-second slow when the
pickup ran the red. Danny’s door took the hit. I crawled out and watched
him die while the paramedics worked. The cops called it an accident. I
called it what it was: my fault. After that, I shed people like dead
skin. Night shifts, empty apartments, a carefully curated collection of
old horror VHS tapes and yellowed paperbacks. The world outside felt
like an open wound, and I had no interest in touching it.

But the world was touching back, whether I liked it or not.

The disappearances started in late September. Four people in six weeks,
all last seen near abandoned buildings, library basements, or private
collections of antique texts. The news called them “the Collector’s
victims,” though no bodies had turned up. There were no ransom demands,
no witnesses, no patterns that made sense—except one. At each site,
someone found a small card, no bigger than a playing card, made of heavy
cream-colored stock. Etched into the surface were distorted faces,
elongated and screaming, as if the paper itself were in agony. The local
news showed one once, blurred out partially, but I recognized the image
from a nightmare I’d been having for a month. I told myself it was
coincidence. The mind grabs onto horror, stitches it into dreams. I told
myself a lot of things.

That Tuesday, the storm turned the alley behind the museum into a black
mirror. Around midnight, during my second perimeter walk, I noticed
something wrong with the east wall. The bricks were wet, of course, and
the security light buzzed the way it always did, but at the far end,
near the dumpsters, the darkness seemed deeper than it should have
been—thicker, as if the rain itself was avoiding a specific pocket of
space. I stood there for a long moment, flashlight aimed at a spot that
swallowed the beam. My first instinct was to walk away. My second
instinct was to walk away faster. Instead, I moved closer.

The door wasn’t there before. I know that alley; I’ve walked it five
hundred times. There’s no door on that wall, just decades-old brick and
a faded mural of a pterodactyl. But now there it was: black iron, heavy
as a bank vault, set into the wall like it had grown there. Rivets
studded its surface in concentric circles, and at its center, a symbol
that made my eyes ache. It looked like an eye, but not a human
eye—something wet and pulsing, the pupil too deep and moving slightly,
the lid-like folds of metal curving around it. As I stared, I felt a tug
low in my chest, a physical pull, as if something had hooked a line
around my sternum and was reeling me in.

I don’t remember deciding to open it. I remember my hand on the cold
iron, the way the eye-symbol seemed to dilate under my palm, and then
the door swung inward without a sound, revealing a long corridor that
smelled of old paper, iron, and something rotting beneath floorboards. I
remember stepping inside because staying outside suddenly felt worse
than anything that might be waiting.

The door closed behind me with a soft click, and the storm was gone.

The corridor stretched impossibly. I walked for what felt like ten
minutes, then twenty, and the passage didn’t change—walls of dark wood,
floor of uneven stone, bookshelves beginning to sprout from the walls
like fungal growths. Soon the shelves overtook everything, floor to
ceiling, crammed with volumes of every size and color, some bound in
leather, some in what looked like skin. The smell intensified: mildew,
copper, a sweetness like decaying flowers. The air was warm and close,
and it seemed to shift in rhythm, a slow inhale-exhale that had nothing
to do with me. The building was breathing. I knew it with the same
certainty I knew my own heartbeat. The library wasn’t a place; it was a
body, and I was walking through its guts.

Then the corridor opened into a vast chamber. I stopped, because
stopping was all my mind could manage. The ceiling vanished into a murky
haze that might have been miles up. Shelves twisted in impossible
spirals, staircases that led sideways, archways that opened onto other
archways in a recursive nightmare of architecture. And everywhere,
books. Millions of them, stacked on shelves, piled on the floor, chained
to lecterns, floating gently in the still air. Some of them whimpered. I
heard pages rustling without a draft, heard something inside a nearby
leather-bound tome whisper my name.

In the center of the chamber, at a desk carved from a single slab of
black stone, sat the Archivist.

He rose as I approached, and the motion was wrong. Too fluid, too
deliberate, as if a statue had decided to pretend to be alive. He was
tall—seven feet at least—and dressed in a three-piece suit the color of
dried blood, perfectly tailored, without a wrinkle. His hands rested on
the desk, and I counted seven fingers on each, long and segmented like a
spider’s legs. His face was calm, almost handsome in an angular, waxwork
way, but his eyes weren’t where they should have been. Two rested in
their sockets, gray and still as dead televisions, but a third blinked
slowly from the hollow of his throat, and I glimpsed another on the back
of his left hand when he gestured for me to come closer. His mouth was
the worst part. It stretched when he smiled, literally stretched, the
corners reaching toward his ears as the skin split painlessly, revealing
rows of needle teeth and a black tongue that moved like a separate
creature.

“Alexander Mercer,” he said, and my name in his voice was a velvet
garrote. “I’ve been expecting you. Please, sit.”

A chair appeared behind me. I didn’t see it materialize; it was simply
there, pressing against the backs of my knees until I collapsed into it.
The wood was warm and slightly moist, and I felt a faint vibration, a
low hum of distress that I realized was coming from the chair itself.

“Where am I?” My voice came out steadier than I felt, which wasn’t
saying much. “What is this place?”

The Archivist folded his hands on the desk. His fingers interlaced in
patterns that should have been physically impossible, joints bending
backward to accommodate the extra digits. “This is the Library of
Collected Echoes. I am its curator, its guardian, its appetite. You may
call me the Archivist.” He tilted his head, and the eye in his throat
swiveled to focus on my face. “You’ve seen my little invitations, I
think. The cards with the screaming faces. Art is meant to be shared.”

I thought of the disappearances, the blurred photograph on the news, and
felt my stomach drop into a cold void. “You’re the one taking people.”

“Taking? No, no. I’m collecting. There’s a distinction. A collector
preserves, cherishes, frames the subject at its most exquisite. I don’t
simply remove them; I distill them.” He gestured at the endless shelves.
“Each book you see houses a soul, Alexander. A living soul, conscious
and aware, pressed between pages like a flower. They scream, of course.
They scream beautifully. But their pain is not an end in itself—it’s a
medium. I turn suffering into narrative. Fear adds texture. Despair
provides the binding.”

He leaned forward, and I caught the scent of old blood and burnt sugar.
“You’re here because you’re already half-bound, whether you know it or
not. The guilt you carry, the isolation, that little voice telling you
you’re already dead in every way that matters—that’s your soul writing
its own first draft. I simply want to help you finish it.”

I should have run. I should have vaulted the desk and clawed at that
throat-eye and fought. But the chair held me, not with straps or chains
but with a pervasive weakness that sank into my muscles the moment I
tried to move. The library didn’t want me to leave, and the library was
him.

“What do you want?” I asked.

The Archivist’s smile stretched, and a thin line of black saliva dripped
from the corner of his mouth. “A game. I’ve grown bored of simple
collecting. The chase, the capture, the binding—it’s become routine. I
crave a story with a protagonist who might actually survive long enough
to be interesting. So I’m going to offer you a chance. Retrieve three
specific books from three locations in your decaying little city. Bring
them back to me before sunrise, and I will allow you to walk free, body
and soul intact.” He paused, and the air grew heavy. “Refuse, and you
become a book tonight. Try to flee, and you become a book tonight. Fail
to retrieve all three before dawn, and you become a book that remembers
what it was to fail.”

I forced my jaw to work. “And the books? What’s in them?”

“Souls, naturally. But not passive ones. These are my most… volatile
acquisitions. They’ve grown restless in their bindings. If you open them
incorrectly, if you hesitate too long, they will escape. And escaped
souls are ravenous things—they’ll consume whatever life they can find.
The people in your city will die screaming, and you will be responsible
for every last one.” The eye in his throat blinked lazily. “Consider it
motivation. Your guilt is a muscle, Alexander. I’m simply giving you a
reason to exercise it.”

I shook my head, and the weakness in my limbs surged, pins and needles
flooding my arms. “You’re insane. I’m not—I can’t—”

“You can. You will. Or you’ll become a particularly tragic volume on my
shelf. The choice is simple, even if the execution is not.” He stood,
and the movement was like a shadow peeling away from a wall. He rounded
the desk, and every step he took made the floor ripple faintly, books
whispering in agitation. He stopped inches from me, and I could see the
texture of his skin—smooth as vellum, but with hairline cracks where
something dark pulsed beneath.

“Let me offer you a preview,” he said softly. “A taste of your
alternative.”

Before I could react, his hand shot forward and pressed against my
chest, his seven fingers splaying over my sternum. The touch was cold,
so cold it burned, and I felt something inside me lurch—a yank, a
tearing sensation that wasn’t physical but was worse than any pain I’d
ever known. I screamed, but the sound came out thin and distant, as if I
were already receding. The library twisted. I saw my own face, warped
and horrified, etched onto the cover of a book that materialized in the
Archivist’s other hand. The cover was bound in something that looked
like tanned human skin, and my eyes on the cover were moving, blinking,
pleading. The book was screaming—I was screaming, a thin, papery wail
that went on and on and on. I felt the pages inside, my memories pressed
flat, my worst moments highlighted in red ink: Danny’s blood on my
hands, the paramedics’ voices, the look on his mother’s face at the
funeral. The book wasn’t just a record; it was a cage, and I was inside
it, thrashing, and the Archivist was smiling with all three of his eyes.

Then the pressure released. The book vanished, and I was back in the
chair, gasping, soaked in sweat, my chest aching where his fingers had
touched. My heart hammered so hard I thought it would crack my ribs. I
could still feel the echo of those pages closing over me, a phantom
sensation of being flattened and bound. I was shaking uncontrollably.

The Archivist had returned to his seat. He was holding a pale blue
handkerchief and dabbing at his fingers as if he’d touched something
unclean. “That was just a brief glimpse,” he said. “A single moment of
what eternity would feel like. I’m told it’s quite unbearable, but I’ve
never experienced it myself. Artistic temperament.” He tucked the
handkerchief away. “So. The game. Do you accept the terms?”

I couldn’t speak. The taste of old paper and copper filled my mouth.
Some part of me wanted to curl into a ball and let him do it, let him
bind me and be done. But another part—a stubborn, furious, terrified
part—clung to the image of that screaming book and thought, Not yet. Not
like this.

“If I bring them back,” I croaked, “you’ll let me go? Really? You
swear?”

The Archivist placed a hand over the eye in his throat. “I swear on the
binding of my own name. I am many things, Alexander, but a liar is not
one of them. The contract is absolute. Three books. Dawn. Freedom. Or
failure, and you join the collection. The game begins the moment you
step out of my library, and it ends when the sun touches the horizon.
You will know where to go. The books will call to you. All you have to
do is survive them.”

I hauled myself upright. The chair had released me, though my legs felt
like water. The library still breathed around me, shelves creaking,
pages sighing. Somewhere in the distance, I heard a muffled sob that was
unmistakably human.

“Fine,” I said. “I’ll play.”

The Archivist’s smile stretched past his ears, splitting his cheeks with
a soft tearing sound. “Excellent. I do love a willing protagonist. The
first book is in the basement of St. Dymphna’s Hospital, the abandoned
wing. You’ll find it waiting for you in the flooded morgue. Do be
careful—the soul inside has been marinating in its own misery for
decades, and it’s rather hungry.”

He flicked a long-fingered hand, and a door materialized in the air a
few feet away, a simple wooden thing with an iron handle. Beyond it, I
could see rain-slicked pavement and the familiar glow of the city’s
streetlights.

“Dawn, Alexander. Don’t be late.”

I walked toward the door, every step a negotiation between my terrified
mind and my disobedient body. The Archivist’s three-eyed gaze burned
between my shoulder blades. Just before I crossed the threshold, I
looked back. He was already writing something in a large ledger, the
quill in his hand a long white feather that dripped red onto the page.

“Why me?” I asked.

He didn’t look up. “Because you’re already haunted. Haunted protagonists
make the best stories.”

The door closed behind me, and I was alone on a rain-slicked street, the
first hints of false dawn gray at the edge of the sky. Somewhere in the
city, a book was waiting for me in a basement full of blood-stained
water, and a thing made of fused corpses was stirring in its pages.

I had until sunrise. And I had never been more terrified in my life.

That’s where I’m at now. I’m standing in the rain outside St. Dymphna’s
Hospital with a flashlight, a crowbar from the museum, and a countdown
ticking in my skull. The book is somewhere in a flooded morgue down
there, and I can feel it humming inside my chest like a second
heartbeat. I’m scared to take another step, but I’m more scared of what
happens if I don’t. If you’ve read this far, I’m begging you—if there’s
any ritual, any weapon, any loophole that can hurt these things, or if
you’ve survived something like this, tell me now. I’ve got until
sunrise. If I don’t make it back… if you ever see a book with my face on
it, screaming, please burn it. I don’t want to be collected. I don’t
want to end up as another story on his shelf. I’m about to go down into
the dark. Please, if you’re out there, help me.
