My aunt had one rule: never sleep facing the wall. I just found out why.

I’m writing this from a motel parking lot somewhere in the Rust Belt. My
hands are still shaking, and there’s this faint, scratching sound in my
ears that I can’t shake off. I need to get this out before I lose my
mind. I need to know if anyone else has seen him.

When I was a kid, I spent my summers at my Aunt Becky’s place. It was a
decaying Victorian house in a town that felt like it was holding its
breath. One night, after a dinner of her “famous” noodles—which I
realized too late had a few stray ants crawling in the bowl—everything
changed.

I was sleeping on a cot in the guest room. Aunt Becky had a strict rule:
never sleep facing the wall.

The wall facing my bed had this… thing. A black circle on the wallpaper.
It wasn’t painted; it looked like a burn mark, or maybe rot, with
jagged, feathered edges. It had been there long before she bought the
house.

That night, I woke up to a sharp, pulsing pain in my ear. I screamed.
Aunt Becky appeared in the doorway, her face pale and indifferent. She
didn’t hug me. She just said:

“Sandy, get that filthy ant off your face.”

I wiped my cheek, but the movement inside my ear intensified. I looked
into the small vanity mirror and froze. A stream of black ants was
pouring out of my ear canal. They were biting me from the inside.

“Auntie, help me!” I sobbed. “They’re coming out of my ear!”

She just stared at me with those cold, glassy eyes and whispered:

“Don’t sleep facing the wall.”

Then she threw a pitcher of ice-cold water over me and screamed it
again: “NEVER SLEEP FACING THE WALL!”

The ants vanished. I was terrified, so I obeyed. I turned my back to the
wall and eventually drifted into a feverish sleep. Then I heard it. A
voice.

“Turn around.”

I thought it was a dream. Then it came again, louder, vibrating through
my skull.

“Turn around.”

I felt my soul being sucked out of my chest. I couldn’t scream. I
couldn’t move. I gave in. I turned toward the wall.

Inside that black circle, a face appeared. It was bone-white and
non-human. Its eyes were black pits with spiraling white pupils that
pulled me in. Ants were crawling out of its smiling mouth.

I felt a sudden, sickening warmth in my chest. I started laughing—a
loud, jagged laugh that wasn’t mine. The warmth turned into a crushing,
fiery weight. My heart bucked like a trapped animal and then… it just
stopped.

I woke up in a hospital three days later. They called it a “juvenile
myocardial infarction”—a heart attack at twelve.

Ten years have passed. Aunt Becky died last week, and as her only
relative, I had to go back to that house to clear it out. I went into
the guest room. The walls had been repapered with a bright floral
pattern. I laughed at my childhood fears. I decided to stay the night to
save money on a hotel.

I woke up at 3:00 AM. My ear was itching. A sharp, familiar sting
followed.

I ran to the bathroom and looked in the mirror. A single ant, huge and
pitch-black, crawled out of my ear and onto the porcelain.

I sprinted back to the bedroom and ripped the floral wallpaper off the
wall. The circle was there. But it was bigger now. Much bigger. And in
the center, that spiral eye was watching me.

I heard the whisper right behind my neck:

“Turn around.”

I didn’t. I jumped out the window, got in my car, and I’ve been driving
for three hours. But here’s the thing… my chest is starting to feel
warm. That light, ticklish warmth I felt right before my heart stopped
the first time.

Does anyone know about the history of the “Black Circle” houses? I think
my aunt wasn’t just afraid of it. I think she was its keeper.

Please. I don’t want to turn around.
