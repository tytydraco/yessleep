I am writing this from a burner phone in a motel room that smells like
cheap bleach. My hands are shaking, but not from fear. From withdrawal.
I haven’t changed a bandage or felt the warm, wet snap of a latex glove
in seventy-two hours, and the itch under my skin is getting unbearable.

You see, for the last eight years, I was a respected surgeon at a major
city hospital. Everyone thought I was a hero. They thought the long
hours I spent alone in the recovery rooms were because I was
“dedicated.”

They had no idea.

I didn’t want to kill people. Killing is easy. It’s boring. Once they
stop breathing, the fun is over. No, I wanted to change them.

My obsession started small. During routine surgeries, while the patient
was deeply asleep under anesthesia, I would make tiny, useless incisions
where no one would look. Under the armpits. Behind the knees. Inside the
upper lip. I would sew small things inside them. A sterilized copper
coin. A sharp piece of broken glass. A single, coarse strand of
horsehair.

I loved thinking about them waking up, going home to their families, and
feeling a strange, sharp poke every time they took a step or smiled,
never understanding why.

But a year ago, I got sloppy. Or maybe I just got bored.

A young woman came in after a bad car crash. Her legs were crushed, but
she was going to live. I was the lead surgeon. I cleared the room,
telling the nurses I needed to perform a delicate, quiet nerve
reconstruction by myself.

Once the door locked, I didn’t fix her. I used my scalpel to slowly peel
the skin away from the muscle on her left thigh, like peeling a ripe
peach. Then, I took a small plastic bag of common black garden ants I
had brought from my yard. I poured them directly into the open wound,
right onto the raw red muscle.

I watched them crawl and dig into her flesh before I quickly sewed the
skin back over them. I used a tight, waterproof stitch so they couldn’t
get out.

When she woke up days later, she screamed and cried constantly. She kept
scratching at her leg until her fingernails bled, begging the nurses to
help her. She said she could feel thousands of tiny legs biting her from
the inside. I just stood at the foot of her bed, smiling warmly, holding
my clipboard. I told her family it was just “phantom nerve pain” from
the accident. I even prescribed her painkillers that I knew wouldn’t
work. Watching her suffer, knowing I was the god of her private hell,
gave me a rush better than any drug.

She committed suicide three weeks after she was discharged. I went to
her funeral. I even cried with her mother. It was beautiful.

After that, I couldn’t stop. I did it to an old man during a hip
replacement. I scraped the marrow out of his bone and replaced it with a
toxic liquid chemicals that slowly rotted him from the inside out over
six months. I did it to a young boy during an appendectomy—I won’t even
type what I did to him because even in this dark room, it makes me feel
sick to remember the sound he made when he woke up.

But two days ago, a nurse caught me. I was in the middle of a procedure
on an unconscious guy, sewing a rusted metal wire around his main artery
so that every time his heart beat, it would scrape against the metal.
The nurse walked in early. She saw the wire. She saw my face.

I panicked. I hit her with a heavy oxygen tank, packed my bag, and ran.

The hospital knows. The police know. My face is on the local news right
now. They are calling me a monster. They are looking for me everywhere.

I know I’m going to prison. I know my life is over. But I’m not writing
this to ask for forgiveness. I don’t feel bad for what I did. I’m
writing this because I want the world to know my work before they lock
me in a dark cage forever.

I want you all to feel the terror I gave to them.

And honestly, as I sit here looking out the window of this dirty motel,
watching the rain hit the glass, I’ve realized something that makes me
smile.

I looked up my old hospital records before I blocked the tracking on my
phone. In eight years, I performed over three thousand surgeries.

Three thousand people.

Most of you reading this have had a routine surgery at some point. A
wisdom tooth extraction. An appendix removal. A tonsillectomy. You trust
the doctor. You close your eyes, you breathe in the gas, and you go to
sleep. You have no idea what they do to you while you are asleep. You
just trust that when you wake up and feel a weird, dull ache deep inside
your body, it’s just normal healing.

Take a second right now. Stop reading. Feel your body.

Do you feel that tiny, deep throb behind your ribs? That weird, sudden
itch deep inside your ear that you can never quite scratch? That tight,
pulling sensation under your skin when you stretch your neck?

I traveled a lot for guest surgeries over the years. Check the username
of this Reddit account. It’s my real name.

Go ahead. Look at your medical records. Check who your surgeon was.

Because I might be trapped in this room tonight… but something I left
behind is still living inside you.
