Part 5

-—–

I left the photograph where it was. That felt important, though I
couldn’t have said why. Perhaps it was some calcified stubbornness, or
maybe the sense that touching it again would constitute a concession. I
dressed with my back to the nightstand and walked to the kitchen without
looking into the corridor or the stairs, without doing any of the things
my body wanted to do, which were mostly variations of running. Of
fleeing.

The thermometer above the sink read minus twenty-two.

I filled the kettle and set it on the burner, watching the coil go
amber. My hands had stopped shaking sometime before dawn, leaving behind
a cottony numbness I recognized from long mixing sessions — the kind of
exhaustion that sits below the surface, too deep for trembling. I’d felt
it after every late-night edit in the early days of the podcast. After
we ran Sarah’s voicemail.

I poured the coffee when it was ready and didn’t taste it.

The morning chores were mechanical and mercifully brief. The boiler
pressure was stable at 1.3. The generator shed was clear, with no drift
over the exhaust port. The wood rack had a two-day supply, solid. I
moved through each task on rails, and for forty minutes, the lodge
narrowed itself down to the workable and the immediate: valves, fuel
gauges, shoveling snow, the satisfying thunk of split pine dropping into
the rack. When I came back inside, I set the shovel against the mudroom
wall and pulled off my gloves. I stood in the kitchen, snow still
melting off my boots, and looked at the counter.

The original chore list was there, exactly where it had been for days —
edges curled, the paper gone soft from ambient moisture.

Next to it sat a new sheet.

It was not typed, but handwritten, the same as the one from day six. It
was written in the same dark ink, with the same slight rightward lean,
and the same ornate capital that looked like it had been traced twice. I
didn’t reach for it right away. I pulled a chair out from the table, sat
down, and looked at it from across the room, the way you’d look at a dog
that you weren’t sure if it was going to bite you.

Then I crossed to the counter, picked it up, and read.

Daily Maintenance — Priority Items

-   Inspect all second-floor windows for seal failure. Cold air
    intrusion may indicate structural compromise.
-   Check heating vents in east-wing hallway — confirm airflow is
    unobstructed.
-   Test emergency light fixture at the end of the east corridor, second
    floor. Replace battery if needed.
-   Record any anomalies in the logbook for owner review.

There was no signature or date. At the bottom of the page, in smaller
print, it said: The above tasks are time-sensitive. Delay may result in
pipe freeze.

I set the sheet down flat on the counter, parallel to the edge, and
stood over it with my coffee going cold between my palms. The tasks were
plausible; that was the thing. Seal failure in a second-floor window, in
minus-twenty cold, was a legitimate concern. A dead emergency light in a
remote lodge was a liability. These were real tasks. Each one could be
explained by a conscientious property owner who wanted the job done
right.

Each one also required me to go upstairs.

I turned the sheet over. It was blank. I held it to the window and
checked for a watermark. Nothing. I folded it along the crease and set
it beside the original, lining up the edges, and stood back, and thought
about the roads.

It was twenty-seven miles to the valley floor. The storm three nights
ago had added another foot on top of what was already there. I’d seen
the ridge road from the east window yesterday morning: a white,
undifferentiated swell, the ditch indistinguishable from the lane, the
whole surface wind-packed into a crust that would swallow a standard
vehicle to the axles inside a quarter mile. The Subaru’s battery was
good, but the tires were all-seasons, and the road required chains I
hadn’t brought because no one in Brooklyn in November thinks about
chains.

The emergency radio had been static since day nine.

I went to the shelf, took it down, set it on the kitchen table, and
cycled through the channels the way I had a dozen times before. One
through eight was just white noise; channel nine was the carrier tone,
clean and unbroken. It did nothing but confirm that the equipment was
still powered. There were no voices. Nothing. I held the radio for a
moment longer, pressing the transmit button and releasing it, listening
to the hollow click of dead air where someone’s voice should have been.

I set it back on the shelf.

I could try the truck. Even if the road defeated me inside the first
mile, I’d be a mile closer to help — except that in minus twenty and
worsening, a mile wasn’t closer to anything. I’d be a man sitting in a
stalled vehicle in a ditch at altitude, with no radio and a coat not
rated for extended exposure. I’d done the math on this already, two
nights ago, and the math hadn’t changed.

Which left the third option.

I didn’t let myself name it yet. I poured the cold coffee down the
drain, rinsed the mug, and placed it on the rack, handle to the right.
The kitchen held its silence. Everything was exactly where I’d put it.

Except it wasn’t, and I knew it wasn’t, and the only reason any of it
still looked normal was that whoever was here wanted it to look normal
long enough to be useful.

I sat down at the table with the logbook.

The most recent entry was mine — the jagged one from last night, the
confession that had run three pages and left my hand stained blue at the
knuckle. I turned back to it slowly. My handwriting was recognizable but
not quite itself, the letters pitched too far forward, the line spacing
erratic. It looked like the writing of a man at the end of something.

I flipped back further. Day fifteen’s entry. Day twelve. Day nine.

I almost missed it.

The margins were narrow, barely a centimeter, and the additions were
small. Not intrusive but…careful. Each note sat beside its respective
line with the patience of something that had been waiting for me to
look. I tilted the book toward the window, where the winter light was
flat and pitiless, and read.

Beside the entry from day ten, where I had written the voicemail, the
margin held two words in an even, penciled hand: Sarah’s last call.

My finger tracked down the page.

The scandal had been annotated: what you chose to do.

The incident — I’d written that one on day thirteen, proud of my own
distance — now had a bracket around it and, beside the bracket, a single
word: No.

My mistake sat beneath a notation that read, in the same small script:
Not a mistake. A decision.

I closed the book, harder than necessary. The loud thump was quickly
muffled by the surrounding silence.

The annotations weren’t threatening. That was the thing that made my
scalp go tight and electric. A threat would have been easier to hold.
These were corrections, matter-of-fact. The patient, specific
corrections of someone who had read every word I’d written in this lodge
and found it lacking in precision. In honesty. I had spent three years
developing a vocabulary for what I did that kept the weight of it just
out of arm’s reach. Voicemail. Incident. Mistake. The way things
unfolded.

Someone had gone through my logbook with a pencil and removed the
padding. Word by word.

I pressed both palms flat against the table and let the cold of the wood
climb into my hands. Outside, the wind had picked up, and the pine trees
along the ridge answered it with a low, collective groan. The sound
moved through the west wall and dissolved into the lodge’s interior the
way all sounds did here — without echo, as if swallowed.

My professional mind ran the variables. Someone with access to the
logbook. Someone who read it, understood the meaning of and behind it,
and annotated it without disturbing the binding. Someone with enough
knowledge of the timeline to know that “the voicemail” and “Sarah’s last
call” referred to the same forty-seven seconds. The set of people who
could know all of that without being told was a set of one.

Daniel Harrow.

The name landed in my chest like a stone into still water.

I’d thought of him exactly twice in three years. Once, when his first
email arrived — I need to know if you have the original file — and once
when I read a profile of him in a magazine that ran eight months after
the scandal. He’d quit his job, the piece said. He’d stopped answering
calls. The journalist described him as “a man in the process of removing
himself from the world,” and I had read that sentence and felt, briefly
and shamefully, relieved that someone else was doing the penance I
couldn’t manage.

I hadn’t thought about where he’d gone.

The chore list sat on the counter, its tasks waiting.

I opened the logbook to a fresh page.

The pen moved before I’d fully decided to let it.

I know you’re here. I know who you are.

I set the pen down beside the book, parallel, the way I always did. Then
I stood and walked to the kitchen window and looked out at the lodge’s
exterior, at the snow-capped woodshed, the porch, the skeletal birch at
the far edge of the clearing. The sky pressed low and gray, the light
the color of old bone. Nothing moved.

He’d been here two years.

Two years of learning where the floor groaned and where it didn’t. Two
years of memorizing which window latches could be worked from the
outside, how long the boiler took to cycle, which direction the
prevailing wind came from, and what sounds it masked.

He’d moved my tools by one peg; he’d slid the kitchen chair out of
place; he’d placed a photograph at the foot of the stairs; he’d
annotated my confessions in the margins like a teacher marking up a
first draft, line by line, with the patience of a man who had rehearsed
this for far longer than I’d been running from it.

I turned away from the window.

The chore list. The second-floor tasks. The east-wing hallway, the
heating vents, the dead emergency light. All of them plausible. All of
them requiring me to climb those stairs, to move through the territory
he’d held for two years, to come to him rather than wait while he
continued his slow, methodical erosion of my certainty.

He wasn’t going to come down.

I understood that without needing to be told. He’d had every opportunity
to confront me on the main floor — in the night, during the hours I was
outside, during the long stretches when I sat with my back to the
corridor writing things I believed no one would read. He hadn’t. The
footsteps above my bed had never descended. The chore lists steered me
upward, never pulled him down. He was waiting up there, in whatever room
he’d made livable, in the silence he’d spent two years curating for
exactly this purpose.

The question was whether I would make him wait any longer.

I sat back down at the table, and I sat there for a long time.

My mind — the part that had survived the last three years by reducing
every crisis to its component variables — ran the same loop it had been
running for days, looking for an exit that wasn’t there. The roads. The
radio. The truck. Each one was a closed door. The cold on the other side
of every exit was not metaphorical. It was literal and lethal and I’d
known this for weeks and had found it, shamefully, a relief.

The guilt kept insisting on something else entirely.

It was not coherent, what it said. But its shape was something like:
Whatever is up there, you built it. You drove the truck up the ridge
road and turned the brass key in the lock and the key turned smoothly
and you noticed that and set it aside, and everything since then has
been a series of moments where you could have left and didn’t. The
roads. The radio. The list of reasons not to go was long, and honest,
and none of it was the real reason.

The real reason was simpler.

I had been running from Sarah Harrow’s last forty-seven seconds for
three years, and I was tired of running. Not recovered. Not healed. Just
tired; tired in the way that makes you stop moving and sit down in
whatever patch of ground is closest, because the alternative is falling.

The brass key was in my jacket pocket. I hadn’t thought about it
specifically — it had been there since the first night, when I’d slipped
it in after locking the door behind me, a habit from years of studio
access cards and facility keys, and it had traveled in that same pocket
through every chore and every cold morning since. I closed my fingers
around it now and felt its weight. Old brass, heavy, the bow worn
smooth.

I stood.

I left the coffee on the counter. I left the chore list face-up beside
the original. I walked to the table and looked at the logbook, open to
the page where I’d written I know you’re here. I know who you are. The
ink had dried to a flat, unremarkable blue.

In the margin beside it, in pencil, small and level:

Then come upstairs.

I slammed the logbook.

The corridor was dim, the overheads throwing their usual yellow economy
of light. My boots on the stone made the sound they always made — that
granular, high-frequency crunch — and then the transition to hardwood,
where the floorboards registered each step in their own particular
register, the C-sharp near the fireplace, the sharper crack at the
kitchen threshold. I knew this floor the way I’d known every studio I’d
ever worked in. I knew exactly where to step to make the least amount of
noise.

I did not try to make less noise.

The banister at the base of the stairs was worn to that same dull gloss
I’d noted on the first night, the wood beneath the varnish still dense
and cold. I closed my right hand around it. The brass key sat in my
left.

I looked up.

The light at the top of the stairs was the color of nothing — not dark,
exactly, but a dimness that felt deliberate, as if the second floor had
been adjusted for a specific kind of visibility. I could see the edge of
the landing, the beginning of the corridor, the faint horizontal line of
a door frame somewhere to the east.

The staircase was silent. My pulse was not.

I stood at the base with one hand on the bannister and the brass key
pressing into my palm, and I looked up at the dark above, and I did not
move yet. I gave myself that. One more moment of standing on the ground
floor, in the known territory of chores and logbooks and cold coffee,
before the thing I’d been steering around for three years resolved
itself into a staircase and a man waiting at the top of it.

I put my foot on the first step.
