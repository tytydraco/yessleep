I bought the dashcam off a marketplace app for eighteen dollars. No
brand name on the box, just Chinese characters and a barcode that
wouldn’t scan at checkout, so the seller knocked off two bucks and
called it even. I worked the overnight shift at the distribution center
off Route 9, and my mother had been texting me articles about rear-end
collisions on empty highways at 2 AM, so I figured eighteen dollars was
a cheap enough price for her to stop.

It clipped onto the windshield with a suction cup that left a ring of
fog every morning. It recorded in loops, fifteen minutes at a time,
overwriting itself once the card filled up, unless I pulled the footage
off first. I never did. There was nothing to pull. Three weeks of
identical drives — same exit, same gas station glow, same stretch of
nothing between my apartment and the warehouse — and not once did
anything happen worth keeping.

Then I watched Tuesday’s footage by accident.

I’d meant to delete it before formatting the card, just a habit, a
finger swipe before bed. The video opened to mile marker 14, the
flattest, emptiest stretch of the whole drive, fields on both sides and
not even a fence to break up the dark. I remembered that exact stretch
from twelve hours earlier. I remembered it being empty. I remembered the
radio being on, some call-in show about high school football, and I
remembered checking my mirrors out of pure boredom because there was
nothing else to look at.

On the screen, at mile marker 14, a woman was standing on the shoulder.

She wasn’t walking. She wasn’t waving for help, the way you’d expect
from someone broken down at two in the morning. She stood with her arms
loose at her sides, pale in a way that had nothing to do with the
headlights, wearing something thin and pale-colored that didn’t move
even though the air at that speed should have dragged at it. And she was
facing the car. Not the road. The car. Like she’d been waiting at that
exact point on the shoulder for my headlights to find her.

I rewound it eleven times. I am not exaggerating that number. Eleven
times, freezing on the frame where she was clearest, and every time my
own memory insisted: that road was empty. You drove that road. You would
have seen her.

I told myself it was bad sensor compression. Cheap optics smearing a
guardrail post, a road sign, a trick of headlight glare into something
vaguely human-shaped. Eighteen-dollar cameras hallucinate. That’s what
eighteen dollars buys you.

I drove that same stretch the next night and watched the shoulder the
entire time, hands sweating on the wheel, going eleven under the speed
limit just to give my eyes more time. Mile marker 14 came and went.
Empty. Nothing but dead grass and a crumpled beer can reflecting my
headlights back at me.

I got home and checked the footage anyway, already laughing a little at
myself.

She was there again. Same stretch. Same stillness. Same stare, locked on
a car that — I’d just lived through this, I’d just watched the road —
never had anyone standing beside it.

But she wasn’t at mile marker 14 anymore.

She was at mile marker 11. Three miles closer to my apartment than the
night before.

I stopped sleeping properly after that. I’d come home from my shift,
format the card like always — out of habit, out of some superstition
that wiping it might wipe her too — and then I’d sit at my kitchen table
at four in the morning watching the one clip I’d saved off before
formatting, the one from mile marker 11, scrubbing back and forth across
eleven seconds of footage like there was an answer buried in the
compression artifacts if I just looked hard enough.

There wasn’t. There was just her. Closer every time.

I tried not driving that route. I rerouted through town, added twenty
minutes to my commute, told myself the extra gas money was worth not
finding out whether she’d still be there if I gave her no road to stand
on. The dashcam didn’t care. It looped anyway. Fifteen minutes of
footage from a drive through brightly lit residential streets, gas
stations, an all-night pharmacy with its sign buzzing — and somewhere in
the middle of all that ordinary light, four seconds of a different road
entirely. A dark two-lane stretch I hadn’t driven in days. Mile marker
8.

The camera wasn’t filming my car anymore. It had stopped caring what
road I was actually on.

I called the number on the dashcam’s flimsy paper manual, more out of
desperation than any real hope. It rang into a dead line, the kind of
tone that means the number was disconnected months ago, if it had ever
been connected at all. I searched the brand name stamped on the
underside of the unit — three letters, no vowels — and found nothing.
Not a product listing. Not a single review. Not even a complaint thread
from someone else who’d bought the same eighteen-dollar mistake.

It was like the camera had been manufactured for exactly one customer.

Mile marker 5 came on a Thursday.

I know because I’d started keeping a notebook by then, dating every
clip, plotting the distance like it was a countdown I could somehow
solve if I just had enough data points. Marker 14. Marker 11. Marker 8.
Marker 5. Each one a little closer, never more than three miles at a
time, like something pacing itself. Like something that understood
patience better than I understood fear.

I worked out the math on a Tuesday night, sitting in the warehouse
breakroom on an unpaid fifteen, my coworker’s radio playing something
tinny from a phone speaker three tables over. Marker 5 sat four point
three miles from my apartment.

At the rate she’d been closing the distance, she’d reach my building in
nine days.

I quit recording after that. I peeled the dashcam off the windshield in
the parking lot before my shift even started, dropped it in the
glovebox, told myself the problem was solved, the way you tell yourself
anything when you’re too scared to think about what it actually means
that pulling a camera off your windshield doesn’t unmake what it’s
already shown you.

The drive home that night, unrecorded, was the same as always. Empty
road. My own headlights. The radio playing something I didn’t listen to.

I checked the dashcam out of habit before bed — I don’t know why, some
compulsion stronger than the decision I’d made six hours earlier not to
look anymore — and found it had kept recording anyway. Glovebox-dark
footage, mostly nothing, the inside of a closed compartment lit by
nothing at all.

Except for eleven seconds, near the end, where the dark resolved into a
hallway. My hallway. The one outside my apartment door, lit by the
flickering bulb the landlord kept promising to fix.

She was standing at the far end of it. Still. Facing the door. My door.

The footage was timestamped eleven minutes in the future.

I am writing this at my kitchen table with every light in the apartment
on and the dashcam sitting in a freezer bag in the sink, because some
half-remembered piece of advice told me cold slows things down, even
though I know, somewhere under the panic, that it has never once been
about distance. It was never about the road at all. The road was just
the only place it had a reason to be photographed.

I don’t have a working theory for what it wants, or what happens once
the distance runs out entirely. I only know the eleven minutes from that
last clip are almost up, and that somewhere very close by, on the other
side of a door I am too afraid to check, something pale and patient has
finally arrived at the only place it was always headed.
