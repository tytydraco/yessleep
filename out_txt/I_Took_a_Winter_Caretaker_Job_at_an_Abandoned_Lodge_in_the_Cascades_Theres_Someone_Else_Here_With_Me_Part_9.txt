Part 8

-—–

Daniel had not moved. He still sat against the corridor wall, watching
me with an expression I couldn’t read. It was not triumph, which was
what I’d expected — heck, it was what I had been bracing for. His face
held none of that. I thought of the days when my father would be working
outside. He’d be carrying something heavy across the yard, his face
contorted with the effort, and when he put it down, visible relief would
wash over him. That’s what I saw in Daniel’s face now. He’d been
carrying this for a very long time and had finally set it down. Except
now, he seemed unsure of what to do next.

I opened my mouth and tried to speak, and found that I couldn’t. My
tongue was thick and dry, my throat closed around something that might
have been a word or might have been a sob. I tried again. Nothing. The
third time, I managed a single syllable. “I—” and then stopped, because
what followed that word was the thing I had spent three years learning
not to say. I had given interviews and written statements. I had used
phrases like “the way the situation unfolded” and “the impact on the
family” and “I regret the decision to air the voicemail.” I had never
once said the thing that was true. I had never once said it because
saying it would have meant admitting that every justification I’d built,
every rationalization, was a lie I told myself.

I tried again.

“I knew,” I said. My voice came out wrong — too high, too thin. “I knew
it was real. From the first second. Before Marcus said anything. Before
I pulled it into the editor. I knew.”

Daniel didn’t move.

“I told myself—“ I stopped, swallowed, started again. “I told myself the
content warning made it ethical. That we were raising awareness.” My
voice cracked on the word “awareness,” and I heard how absurd it all
sounded, how completely and utterly stupid. I kept going. I couldn’t
stop. The words were coming now like water through a breach in a dam. My
hands were shaking. “The episode went up that night,” I said. “It
reached number one by morning. Downloads exceeded all previous records.
We trended worldwide for three days. I was proud of it.” My mouth curled
in disgust, but I kept going. “I refreshed the dashboard and watched the
numbers climb. I told myself it was the best work I’d ever done.”

I looked up at Daniel. His face had not changed. The exhaustion in his
eyes was bottomless.

“Her name was Sarah,” I said.

And then I couldn’t say anything else for a moment, because her name was
in my mouth. I had never said it out loud before, not once in three
years, not even in the interviews where I’d talked around her death
using words like “incident“ and “mistake.”

“Her name was Sarah Anne Harrow,” I said again, and my voice broke on
the middle name, on the ordinary, human fact of it. “She was thirty-two
years old…” The details Daniel had given me were still fresh, still
sharp-edged. I felt like I had to repeat them back to him, to let him
know I’d been listening. That I cared. “She liked terrible puns. She
drank her coffee with three sugars.”

I was crying now. I didn’t know when it had started, but the warm tears
were there, tracking down my face and into the corners of my mouth. I
didn’t wipe them away; I didn’t deserve to. “She was afraid of moths,” I
said, and my voice hitched on the word, on the absurd, specific,
heartbreaking detail of it. “Not spiders. Not the dark. Moths.” I was
shaking now, not just my hands but my whole body. The tears were coming
faster, and my breath was coming in short, ragged gasps that sounded
nothing like me.

“I’m sorry,” I said. The words came out broken, barely intelligible, my
voice reduced to something raw and animal. “I’m so sorry. I’m sorry for
what I did to her. I’m sorry for what I did to you.”

The sobs were full-bodied now. I couldn’t see Daniel anymore — my eyes
were too blurred and swollen. But I could feel him there, still and
silent, watching the truth finally spoken aloud. “She would have hated
me,” I said, and the words came out in a whisper, barely audible. “She
would have hated me for what I did. She would have been right to. I
hated myself for what I did. I’ve hated myself every day. I couldn’t
admit it. I just kept running and running and running.”

I don’t know how long I sat there, weeping into my hands. It might have
been minutes. It might have been hours. The woodstove burned down to
embers, and the lamp flickered twice, and the cold crept in around the
edges of the room. Neither of us moved. He sat across from me, and he
let me cry. When I finally looked up at it through the blur of tears,
his face was not satisfied. It was exhausted, hollowed out. Old.

There was nothing else to say. The truth was out, and I could no more
take it back than I could bring Sarah Harrow back from the dead.

The silence returned, and for the first time in three years, it was a
silence I didn’t want to escape. The tears were coming slower now, a
steady trickle that traced warm lines down my cheeks and into the collar
of my shirt. My breath was ragged, uneven, like someone who had nearly
drowned. But I was breathing. I was still breathing, and that was
enough.
