I was already sick of the terminal fans screaming because of the ambient
temperature of the room my boss forced me into. For context, I was in my
mid-20s when this happened, back in early spring 2024. I was completely
broke and couldn’t afford basic necessities, so I finally swallowed my
pride and got a job at this crumbling old retail store. The place had
four separate security offices because there had been a massive wave of
break-ins the year before. The monitors in my station were barely
functional and had minor fracture damage across the screens, but I was
desperate, so I learned to work around them. Still, working the security
office was my absolute least favorite part of the night shifts. The
ancient AC units were clinging to life by a tiny thread; if they paused
for even a single second, the room instantly turned into a microwave.

I was stationed in Server Room 4 when the air suddenly turned into a
furnace. The room’s temperature spiked ten degrees in three seconds. On
monitor three, the database migration progress bar froze at 99.4%,
glitched into a jagged line of digital static, and completely vanished
behind a blinking crimson dialogue box:

FATAL ERROR: THREAD 404 UNREADABLE.

I slammed my sweaty palms onto the desk, my knuckles turning stark white
under the sudden, strobing emergency lights. Terrified of getting blamed
for a system crash, I franticly typed a forced override command, but the
keys just dead-clicked uselessly under my fingers. The mainframe wasn’t
frozen—it was locked from the outside.

Then, the command-line interface wiped itself completely clean. A
single, pixelated text window stretched across the center screen.

User_7712 is typing…

A chill broke across my neck, sharper and colder than the server room’s
fading AC. 7712 wasn’t a random username. It was my own employee ID
number.

User_7712: She is wearing a blue, employee uniform with two buttons
broken off. She is looking at monitor three. She hasn’t looked behind
her yet.

My breath hitched. My fingers instinctively flew to the torn fabric of
my collar, touching the frayed, empty threads where those two plastic
buttons used to be. The heat in Server Room 4 suddenly felt more
suffocating than it already was, but my skin felt like ice. I didn’t
turn around. If someone was in here with me, hiding in the narrow,
shadowed aisles between the towering server racks, they had to be
watching me through a hidden lens or a gap in the steel mesh.

Panicking, I lunged for the heavy, magnetic exit door. I threw my entire
body weight against the handle, but the electronic lock engaged with a
cruel, metallic clank. The security pad beside the frame was totally
dead, pulsing the exact same angry crimson as the monitors. I was
trapped.

I backpedaled away from the door, my heart hammering against my ribs
like a caged bird. I kept my eyes glued to the center screen, waiting
for the shadow in the room to jump out at me. But the text box suddenly
dissolved. In its place, a low-resolution video feed flared to life.

The camera angle was warped, pointing upward from a cramped,
trash-littered basement somewhere far away from the store. Sitting in
the glow of three massive, high-end monitors was a man. He looked
completely ordinary—mid-thirties, wearing a faded band t-shirt, casually
sipping from a plastic bottle. He wasn’t a ghost, and he wasn’t a demon.
He was just a guy. And somehow, that made it a hundred times worse.

He looked directly into his webcam, grinned, and leaned toward a desktop
microphone. A second later, his voice tore through the server room’s
overhead intercom speakers, overriding the deafening hum of the dying
equipment.

“You really shouldn’t have tried to run, Elli,” his voice echoed,
smooth, calm, and utterly devoid of empathy. “I spent six months writing
the exploit to breach this store’s ancient network. I own the servers. I
own the lights. I own the locks. Right now, I own you.”

On the desk, my cheap smartphone suddenly buzzed. The screen lit up on
its own, completely bypassing my passcode. The front-facing camera
active-light turned green, and a text message from an unknown number
popped up across the screen: Smile for the camera.

He wasn’t hiding in the dark aisles. He was miles away, watching me
through the store’s own compromised security feeds, playing with me like
a rat in a maze.

“What do you want?” I screamed at the monitor, my voice cracking in the
suffocating heat.

The man on the screen chuckled, a wet, grating sound. “I want the
archive file your system is protecting. And since the physical safety
override switch is right next to your desk, you’re going to flip it for
me. If you don’t, I’ll just leave the AC off and seal the vents. Let’s
see how long you last in a concrete box that’s hitting 120 degrees.”

The room was already dripping with my sweat, the air thinning out. But
as I looked down at the physical terminal controller near my hand, I
noticed something he couldn’t see through the blurry, damaged monitors.
The central power breaker for Server Room 4 wasn’t digital. It was an
old-school, heavy-duty plastic toggle switch sticking out of the wall.

He claimed he owned the database, the lights, and the locks. But all of
it ran on the very electricity flowing through this room. Magnetic locks
are fail-safe—if they lose total power, the electromagnets die, and the
door drops open.

“Okay,” I lied, wiping the sweat from my eyes and letting my voice shake
so he thought I was totally defeated. “I’ll do it. Just please don’t
hurt me.”

“Good,” he sneered, leaning back in his chair to watch his victory play
out on his screens.

I stepped toward the terminal, pretending to reach for the file
override. Instead, I gripped the heavy, analog plastic breaker switch. I
looked directly into my phone’s hijacked camera, gave him a cold smile,
and threw my weight downward into the lever.

SNAP.

The server room went pitch black. The screaming terminal fans instantly
died into dead silence. The crimson emergency lights vanished.

And from the exit door, a heavy, beautiful CLICK echoed through the dark
as the magnetic lock lost its power supply and released the frame.

I didn’t wait a single second. I threw the heavy metal door open,
bursting out into the cool, dark hallway of the main store. I ran
straight for the emergency exit, leaving my phone, the job, and Thread
404 behind me forever in the dark. AFTERMATH, after the event i reported
everything to my manager and the cops, they later found the man and
arrested him for Computer Fraud and abuse, Kidnapping/Unlawful
Imprisonment, Cyberstalking and Harassment, and Extortion. I still
remember that night, and sometimes i wake up from a sweat thinking im
still trapped in that hot room.
