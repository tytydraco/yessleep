The water rose to my ankles, a welcome change from the scorching
clearing we had crossed some miles back. Several of the mosquito bites
clustered around my shins dipped below the surface, offering a few
seconds of cool relief before their stinging returned at full force. I
did not look down. The liquid was the colour of weak tea and each step
found a softer, less certain floor than the last, a slow, grasping give
beneath my feet.

Our walk had lasted days. Possibly weeks. I was ready to hazard a guess
at years.

Home remained a vague mirage, glimpsed only from the highest ground and
never quite resolving into anything real. We had eaten what we had early
on, with only the uncles maintaining any last remnants of sustenance we
prepared for what was supposed to be a simple journey home. Now the
insides of my cheeks were the only thing left to chew. Raw and familiar,
not unlike the rest of me. We were all sick by now. Soaked through and
hollow, each step became evidence of our continued existence. The Lord
knows stopping briefly meant stopping forever.

Seven little boys. Two uncles.

There was no justification for our current situation. What had begun as
a joyous journey home had dissolved, mile by mile, into a wilderness of
strange sounds and vanishing paths. A pilgrimage of honour and manhood,
transpired only into a quiet, solemn death march. The way we had been
promised was gone, swallowed up, replaced by something that had no
interest in letting us through, like slimy tongues, the bush above
tangled around our frames. Uncle had told us we were soldiers of Gaia.
Future men of the village. That we must walk as our forefathers walked
into battle, combat the green and the dark, and return to Mother.

We walked.

I focused on the crickets. Scurries and shuffles filled my ears, so as
to avoid agonising screams from my brothers. Our march continued, though
I remained steadfast in my intention to return home. One more turn,
behind the tree straight ahead, not far, ever so close. Our destination
would be there. The lustrous green surrounding our home, Mother
beckoning me into her arms with pride. I was certain of it. It must be.

Toi fell out of step.

I moved ahead of him, catching only the edge of it in my periphery — his
knees folding, the slow buckle and give. A deathly silence suffocated
the air, only disrupted by the sounds of thrashing and spluttering, as
though the noise of demise were implanted directly between my eyes. In
no time his chin touched the black surface of the water. His small
figure retched violently, thrashing water in every direction, dispelling
the steadiness we had become accustomed to. His face turned downward
into the soupy ink we had been wading through for hours. Something brown
and liquid left him, spreading outward from his mouth in a slow bloom.
His face ducked beneath the swamp, further enhancing the violent gags
escaping my sweet brother.

The uncles reached him. They dragged at his limbs, called out words I
had not heard them use before, their voices breaking against the
stillness of the swamp. Toi tried to rise, his arms searching for a
surface capable of taking his hollow weight, and yet the effort had
nothing behind it. The ground beneath him buckled, determined to
retrieve my brother from our possession. As more sickness poured from
his darkened face, as the water thickened with viscous remnants, our
brothers clambered towards him with no hesitation.

Uncle lifted him, removing him from the reach of young boys dedicated to
the cause of rescue. Toi’s body produced a looming shadow upon us all,
witnessing our brother’s frame gasping, spluttering, silent, eyes
shifting to the colour of old milk. The fountain of poison slowed and
stopped. Uncle looked at his face. Looked for a long time. Our uncles
deliberated profusely, no doubt agonising over the loss of a small child
entrusted within their care. Tears dripped from the entangled figures of
our uncles and poor little Toi.

Convinced Toi had expelled the death from his body, it became ever so
surprising to witness his small frame lowered into the swamp. His bones
protruded out from translucent flesh, before becoming all-engulfed by
both the shadows of our surroundings, and the lost wanderers stood
around him in a daze.

The shadow took Toi slowly, and then all at once.

Uncle turned away and raised his hand over his own face, shielding his
eyes from nothing. His eyes failed to leave Toi, instead following him
into the depths.

“There is no boy there no longer,” a voice said. I had not known until
that moment that Uncle’s voice shook. I aimed to rescue him. “Make
haste.”

We made haste.

We did not speak of Toi.

The word for what had happened did not exist among us yet, and so it was
left unnamed, carried in the body rather than the mouth. I felt it
behind my sternum. A new and specific pressure, pushing against the
inside of me like a stone placed there by careful hands. My uncles lost
their composure, quiet bickering overpowered the sounds of critters
surrounding us, though their words became indistinguishable. A newfound
sense of urgency guided our feet through our terrain, promoting our
steps further than before.

The water had risen. I noticed this slowly, in the manner of noticing
something you do not wish to confirm, first at my ankles, then climbing
the soft of my calf, until the hem of my shirt wicked dark and clung.
The floor beneath had grown less certain still, offering nothing solid
to press against, only the long soft give of something that had not been
walked on before. The shrubbery pinching at our faces thickened
significantly, providing all the less give as we continued. My deepest
desire became a safe haven, a dry nook in which to hide my damaged
frame, in which we can lay peacefully waiting for rescue, or maybe
fossilisation. I tried to imagine home behind every distant tree, but
the shrubbery now continuously lived knocking into my face, there was
nothing to look ahead towards, only thick green, or thick black.

I cannot say when. Only that the silence arrived fully formed, as though
it had always been there beneath the sound, waiting for the sound to
leave. No shuffles. No scurries. Not the industrious hum of the swamp we
had walked through all these days but something carved out, deliberate.
A silence with a shape to it. Uncle moved to my left. Away, and then
further away, and I could not say why my eyes tracked him so carefully,
why the small widening of the distance between us registered somewhere
deep and animal in me. He did not look at me. He looked ahead, jaw set,
one hand raised slightly at his side in the manner of a man balancing
himself. Or a man steadying himself against the urge to run.

The water rose.

Around me, not around my brothers. A shift occurred deep below my feet,
seemingly ripping me into a reality in which my fellow travellers had
lost their sights on me. The dark water gave nothing away. It simply
rose. I thought of Toi’s face. The colour his eyes had become. The way
the swamp had received him, the slow and total certainty of it, as
though it had been waiting for exactly him in exactly that moment, as
though the coordinates of his collapse had been agreed upon long before
we set foot in this place. My brothers walked ahead. Their thin
shoulders moved through the murk, the wet sound of their steps swallowed
immediately by the silence that had no interest in ordinary sound. One
of the uncles had moved further still, the distance between us now wide
enough that I could see only the shape of him, the back of his raised
hand.

I do not know why. Fear, perhaps, had consumed the part of me capable of
making any audible alert, had taken it for fuel and left only the body,
standing, the body which had walked for days or weeks or years and had
not yet been given permission to stop. My brothers moved ahead. None of
them turned. A deathly tranquillity pressed against my ears like cupped
hands, intimate and enormous, and beneath the surface of the water I
felt something pass. Not touching. Only the displacement of liquid from
every direction, a slow and massive pressure change, a dark current that
moved against my legs with the confidence of something that had never
needed to hurry. It circled. This is what I understood, standing
chin-deep in darkness, my arms out at my sides and every muscle in me
pulled taut as wire. It was not passing through. It was deciding. My
feet, entirely encircled by a thicker, evil darkness, attempted to
refuse any further movement.

I thought of my mother’s hands. I thought of the incoming grasp destined
to rescue me that seemed to take ever so long to locate me. I thought of
the sound of the village at dusk, all its ordinary noise, the noise of
people who did not know what the inside of this swamp felt like, who had
never been asked to know. I reflected on my circumstances, demanding an
explanation for my journey and the evil which has imbued our bodies. I
am weak and small, and weak and small I shall die, interpreting my
fellow travellers continued excavation as a removal of dead weight. I
had remained at the back for so long, so many nights I spent counting
the heads bobbing in front of me. They did not acknowledge me. They
probably don’t know I’m here, expecting my limp body to lay many miles
back.

Somewhere beneath the ribs, beneath the hunger and the sickness and the
grief of Toi’s face in the dark water — something old and unreasonable
and entirely without dignity gathered itself up and said no with every
blind stupid ounce of its being. The water rose and they will all leave,
they will leave me. I will be alone and they will leave me. I am weak. I
am weak and small and the water rises and they will leave.

One step forward, and then another, arms pushing against the compression
of the water, legs finding whatever soft and uncertain floor was left to
find. My chin left the surface. My neck. My chest emerged slowly from
the dark, shirt clinging, water streaming from me in thin rivulets. A
guttural screech leaves my lips as I pierced through an oily substance
which occupied the surface of our watery grave. I begged for mother and
kicked, and kicked so hard and I just kicked and kicked. The current
beneath circled once more, slow and vast, and I felt the attention of it
like a pressure behind my eyes. Face poised towards the remnants of
sunshine the vast shrubbery permits us to witness. The swamp’s hunt was
unsuccessful. My body protruded from the darkness, and fought its way
through the sludge. I grasped at small hands willing to aid my fight,
ecstatic at the return to a stronger footing.

And just as it came, it went. The quietude of a dirty marsh returned,
capable of producing doubt of the earlier events to anyone with the
misfortune to have caught us just then. A beckoning roar instructed us
to keep moving, furthering the disbelief and my earlier submersion and
encounter. For something so horrendously hysteric, the weight of the
event had little time to produce any significant impact or reaction from
the group. Conversation on said events held a threatening air. How much
did they know? How much did I? Unstable footing or a rotten grasp as my
ankles. There would never be ample time for deliberations surrounding my
descent.

The swamp floor grew more solid beneath my feet. The crickets returned,
tentative at first, then in full chorus, filling the silence back in
like water finding a shape.

Uncle lowered his hand.

He did not look back at me. But his shoulders dropped, slightly, and his
pace slowed to match ours, and I understood that too.

We walked.

I was still here.

I did not yet know whether that was the mercy or the punishment.

The next one to go was Kofi.

The vanishings ceased to announce themselves, and certainly did not
gather us around them the way Toi’s dying had. They happened in the
peripheral, in the gaps between one footfall and the next, in the
moments when eyes were trained on the water ahead or the uncertain floor
below. You looked up and the number was different. That was all.

Kofi I noticed. I had been watching the back of his head, the particular
way he held it, chin slightly raised, remaining steadfast in his journey
back home. Not yet confused by the nature of the forces that swirled
beneath us. And then the back of his head was not there. The space he
had occupied continued to exist, water and silence and the sound of
other boys moving through it, and Kofi simply was not inside that space
any longer.

I stopped.

The boy behind me walked into my back, and then he stopped too.

I became eager to meet the pace around me, believing the uncles’
hesitation surrounding my rescue being a case of necessity. Our supplies
dwindled and our uncles had their own survival to consider. I wouldn’t
wish to sacrifice myself for a scared child either. We were past the
point of being looked after, we were burdens. I will not allow myself to
be a burden again.

We searched briefly. There wasn’t ever much to look for. The space
simply wasn’t there and the surroundings of the disappearance were
beyond confusing, with the darkness preventing any substantial search to
occur. The best we could do was weep for our fallen brother, though not
enough to miss the convoy walking further into the darkness. The surface
of the water gave nothing back, no ripple, no displacement, only its
flat and lightless dark stretching in every direction with perfect
indifference. We called his name. The sound of it left our mouths and
went nowhere, absorbed immediately by the silence the way the swamp
absorbed everything we gave it.

Uncle turned. He scanned the water with the expression of a man checking
the sky for rain, practical, briefly attentive, already concluding. He
called Kofi’s name once, twice, a third time with slightly less behind
it. Then he was quiet for a long moment.

“He has strayed,” Uncle said. “We walk.”

We did not walk. We stood in the water and turned in small useless
circles and called and called until our voices cracked against the
motionlessness. Sebe waded back the way we had come, hands outstretched
beneath the surface as though Kofi might be found there, lying on the
soft floor of the swamp, simply resting. The uncles let this go on for a
time I could not measure.

Then Uncle raised his hand. “We walk.”

We walked. Five little boys. Two uncles. The palimpsest of our fallen
brothers’ absence.

We had found a raised shelf of ground, barely above the waterline, a
thin strip of earth solid enough to lie on. I had not known I was asleep
until I woke. The distinction between the two had grown narrow, sleep
and walking having become versions of the same diminished state. When I
opened my eyes the grey light was the same grey light it had always been
and two boys were gone.

Emre, alongside a brother whose name never reached me.

No sound transpired. No disturbance. The ground beside me held the
shallow impression of petite bodies that were no longer there, and the
water beyond the bank was as flat and sealed as ever, and that was all
the information the swamp was prepared to offer.

The uncles were already awake. Had perhaps not slept. Uncle stood at the
edge of the raised ground looking out across the water and I watched his
face for something, grief, alarm, the particular expression of a man
confronted with demons. He raised his fingers to his scorched lips,
aiming to remove whatever skin was left circling his nails. I found no
relief in his shaking frame, there was to be no search, we were due to
learn that our arrival at our destination was no longer the most
important aspect of the journey to our uncles. What I found was a
stillness so complete and so deliberate that it was its own kind of
answer.

He turned and saw me watching him.

“Three,” he said, quietly, as though confirming something to himself
rather than addressing me. “Make haste.”

I stood. My legs did what legs do. We made haste.

Three little boys. Two uncles. The diminishing faith that our losses
might simply be in hiding.

The swamp the same in every direction, patient and enormous, giving
nothing away about what it had done with the parts of us it had already
taken.

I counted my brothers as we walked. Counted them again. Held the number
in my chest where the stone already was, pressed it there, kept it.

Three.

I did not yet know that three was generous.

The grey light had begun to change. Not brighten, the swamp did not
permit brightness, but shift, the quality of it becoming something
slightly less absolute, as though the world beyond the trees had not
entirely forgotten us. I had begun to believe, in the wordless animal
way I had come to believe most things, that the worst was behind us.

This is the thought I carry with me now. This specific foolishness.

Dami was gone.

He was beside me, directly beside me, close enough that our arms had
been brushing with each step for the better part of an hour. I had found
comfort in this without acknowledging it, the simple fact of his
presence, the sound of his breathing, evidence of a body still operating
at full capacity beside my own. We had not spoken.

The water took his ankle.

That is the only way I can describe it. Not a current, not a stumble —
the water took him, a single decisive motion from below, and Dami went
down sideways before the sound had even reached me. I had his wrist. I
do not know how, the body moves faster than the mind in these moments,
reaches before it understands why, but I had his wrist in both hands and
I pulled.

The swamp pulled back.

What resided below the surface did not thrash or hurry. It simply
applied a pressure so vast and so absolute that my pulling became a kind
of joke, a small and desperate comedy performed against something that
had no awareness of the effort. I felt it through Dami’s wrist, through
the tendons and the fine bones he possessed, the absolute and
indifferent weight of it. Small cracks could be felt within his body,
disgusting tension forming between myself and his kidnapper. His face
was turned up toward me, above the water still, mortified.

He went under between one breath and the next. The water closed over him
with barely a sound, a soft displacement, a small dark ripple that
expanded outward and then was gone. I stood with my hands still shaped
around the wrist that was no longer there, water streaming from my
palms, the space beside me now a simple fact. I screamed. The sound left
me without permission, tore out of the place behind my sternum where the
stone lived, and it was not a boy’s sound, not anything I recognised as
coming from myself. Uncle was at my side before the sound had finished,
his hands on my shoulders, firm and immediate. He did not look at the
water where Dami had been. A smile crept to his lips, neglecting to
reach his eyes.

“Let it be,” he said. His voice was very low. Intended only for me.

“He was there,” I screeched. My hands would not stop their shape. “He
was right there.”

“Let it be.” Uncle’s grip tightened once, briefly, and then released. He
stepped back. Straightened. Raised his voice to address what remained of
us. “We walk.”

We did not walk immediately. We stood in the water and breathed and Sebe
made a sound that was not crying and not screaming but somewhere between
the two, a sound I have never heard from a human being before or since.
Uncle allowed it for a short time. Then we walked.

The loss of Sebe was faster.

Sebe. My sweet companion, Sebe, who had not complained once, who had
rationed his energy with a seriousness beyond his years, who I had
privately believed would outlast all of us on the strength of his
discipline alone. He was taller, a little bit older. The water rose
around him without warning, that same deliberate rising I had felt
against my own skin, and before any of us had understood what we were
witnessing he was gone, pulled from the surface in a single fluid
motion, a seamless transaction, no part of it wasted. We were as we
were, and then we were no more.

I moved toward where he had been. Uncle caught my arm.

“No,” he said.

I looked at his face. He was looking at the water, and his expression
was that of a man watching something he had watched before, something he
had agreed to watch, and the horror of it settled into me slowly and
completely like water finding its level. “Why,” I called. I no longer
stood convinced by the efforts of our uncles, their efforts to return us
home had dissipated, instead replaced with a callous determination to
drag themselves to our village, possibly excusing our violent demise on
our childish nature. Uncle did not answer. He released my arm. He looked
ahead, to where the trees were thinning almost imperceptibly, where the
grey light was doing its slightly different thing, and he walked.

One little boy. Two uncles.

A panic rose within my frame, a deranged anger prepared to rip the
remnants of my hair out my head, scream and run frantically. Escape
immediately. This couldn’t be a possible reality, a reality in which it
is me. Me and a swamp picking us off gradually and shadows curling
beneath our feet, aiding the whole process. I shook violently,
suppressed tears, released them slowly, grabbed my chest. Wept.

I looked for my brothers, they’re no longer here, no longer alive. I
imagined their heads bobbing front of me, walking the interminable walk
towards our release from the most torturous prison. Counted them in the
spaces they were supposed to hold, all in front of me, exasperated but
alive. My hands still shaped around nothing, the water, level and
completely still in every direction.

It was seven. It was seven earlier and it should still be seven. I let
the distance between seven and one exist inside me without looking
directly at it, the difference between seven and one existing on the
periphery, for if it came into the foreground, our shadowy killers could
collect me from the bottom of the swampy darkness with ease.

Uncle came to stand before me. He looked deeply into my eyes, slowly,
with the thoroughness of a man completing an inventory. His expression
was not relief, but something more complicated and more closed than
relief, something I had no name for and have not found a name for since.
The second uncle, who had barely spoken the entire journey, turned away
from us and looked out across the swamp. His hands were at his sides.
His shoulders rose and fell once, very deliberately, and then were
still.

“You have walked well,” Uncle said. His voice had changed. The authority
had not left. “You have walked as your forefathers walked, and duly you
will return. The decadence of our home comes at a price. You know the
route now. You know what was owed. What will always be owed.”

“It is unusual,” he said, “to stand with only one, though I suppose your
brothers simply were not strong enough. Poor Toi was a sure shame, we
had an awful lot of hope in that one. Alas, we made it.”

At the time, the words and their meaning escaped me entirely, today,
before my impending journey, I feel a guttural sickness. I looked at
Uncle’s face. He was looking at me.

“One,” I said.

“Yes,” said Uncle.

“There were seven.”

“Yes.”

I had failed to appreciate the sturdy nature of the footing I now held,
until it dissipated with a shifting movement isolating itself beneath
us. Beneath the ground, beneath the solid earth I had placed such faith
in. Slow. Massive. Willing to wait, it had feasted already, I was now
just part of a show.

It had followed us out.

“Uncle,” I said. My voice was very small. “Uncle. We are out of the
swamp.”

“Yes,” said Uncle. He did not look away from me. His eyes were steady
and completely certain. “You walked well. It is,” he paused, searching
briefly for the word, “ an honour. To be last.”

From nothing I could see.

“It has been a long walk,” Uncle said, quietly, to the space where I was
standing. His voice was gentle in a way that made it worse. “You should
rest now, it’s only a couple of hours’ walk home now. You are safe.
You’ve made it!” His voice was far too jovial for the circumstances in
which we find ourselves.

I thought of my mother’s hands embracing me deeply at my success. Did
she know? Do they all know? I thought of the stone behind my sternum,
pressed there by careful hands the moment Toi went under. I thought of
Dami’s wrist in my palms. The apology in his face. The way he had looked
at me as though my trying meant something, as though it was worth having
someone witness it, even if the witnessing changed nothing.

He had done this before.

He would do it again.

I would do it again.

My uncles crossed to me. Stopped at a distance that was almost close.
Uncle looked at my hands, still shaped around nothing, still holding the
ghost of Dami’s wrist, and something moved briefly across his face and
was gone.

“Come,” he said.

Not make haste. Not the command he had given to boys throughout all
those miles of swamp and murk and diminishing numbers. Something
different. An address between equals. Between men.

I was not a man. I was a boy with hollow cheeks and mosquito bites
clustered at his shins and the names of six brothers eternally ingrained
into the front of my mind. Written in bloody scratches on my soul, my
body, my existence.

I followed him.

Uncle walked beside me and said nothing. There was nothing to say. The
swamp had said it all, boy by boy, step by step, across however many
miles of dark water and impossible patience, and what remained now was
only the long and ordinary walk toward the rest of my life.

Seven little boys.

Two uncles.

We walked.
