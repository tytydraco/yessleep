THE LAST CORRIDOR

Chapter 1: The Call

I have not slept properly in eleven years.

Not since the night my younger brother Jake walked out of our Brooklyn
apartment, seventeen years old, earbuds in, hands in his jacket pockets,
and never came back.

The police searched. The neighbors searched. I searched — obsessively,
relentlessly, in a way that cost me two relationships, one job, and most
of my twenties. I walked every street in our neighborhood a hundred
times. I contacted every shelter, every hospital, every morgue within a
fifty mile radius. I built spreadsheets, drew maps, filed reports, and
attended support groups until the grief counselor gently suggested I
might need something more intensive.

Nothing. Eleven years of nothing.

Jake had simply ceased to exist.

I am thirty four now. I work as a structural engineer for the city,
which means I spend most of my days reviewing blueprints, inspecting
underground infrastructure, and crawling through spaces most people
never knew existed beneath their feet. My colleagues think I chose the
job because I am good at it. I know the real reason. I chose it because
it gives me access to the city’s hidden places. The sealed rooms. The
forgotten corridors. The spaces where things and people disappear.

I have never stopped looking.

Chapter 2: Underground

The call came on a Tuesday morning in November.

A sixteen year old boy named Marcus Webb had gone missing somewhere
beneath the streets of lower Manhattan. He was an urban explorer. His
last post showed him descending into a maintenance shaft near the old
Cortlandt Street station. The caption read: “Going deeper than anyone
has before. Back by midnight.”

He never came back.

I signed up for the volunteer search party before I had even finished
reading the notification email. I told myself it was because I knew the
underground system better than most. I did not examine the other reason
too closely.

The search coordinator, a compact woman named Reyes, briefed us at the
entrance to the maintenance shaft at seven AM. The morning was bitter
cold. Twenty volunteers stood in a loose group, stamping their feet,
pulling on gloves.

“The lower tunnels in this section were abandoned mid construction in
1987,” Reyes explained, spreading a laminated map across the hood of her
van. “Power has been partially restored to assist the search, but large
sections remain completely dark. You will work in teams of three. You
will stay within radio contact at all times. If you find an unlit
corridor or a sealed door, you do not proceed. You call it in and you
wait.”

She looked up and made eye contact with each volunteer in turn.

“Nobody goes dark alone. Is that understood?”

Everyone nodded.

I stared at the map. The tunnels ran deeper than they should have. The
original blueprints showed four levels. The revised blueprints showed
only three. No one had ever explained where the fourth level went.

Chapter 3: The Dark

I was assigned to Team Four — myself, a retired firefighter named
George, and a young paramedic named Sofia. Our section was a maintenance
corridor beginning at a rusted iron staircase and extending
approximately four hundred feet into the earth.

The staircase groaned under our weight. The air below smelled of wet
concrete, iron, and something else — something organic and faintly sweet
that I could not identify. Emergency lights had been strung along the
ceiling at twenty foot intervals, casting a weak yellowish glow that
left long pools of shadow between each bulb.

We moved slowly, calling out Marcus’s name, checking every doorway and
alcove. Most rooms were empty — bare concrete boxes that had never been
finished. In one room, I found a hard hat hanging on a nail, a layer of
dust so thick on the brim it looked like gray velvet. Whoever had left
it there had never come back for it.

I tried not to think about that.

An hour into the search, George stopped.

“You hear that?”

We went still. Then I heard it. A sound so faint it was almost
imaginary.

Tapping. Rhythmic, deliberate tapping, coming from somewhere below us.

Sofia immediately reached for her radio. “Team Four to base, we have
possible audio contact, requesting—”

The emergency lights went out.

Every single one of them, simultaneously, as if something had exhaled
and blown them out like candles.

The darkness was total. Absolute. The kind of darkness that has weight.

Chapter 4: Lost

The lights came back after forty seconds. I counted every one of them.

But when they flickered back on, I was alone.

George and Sofia were gone. The corridor stretched empty in both
directions. There were no footprints in the thin layer of dust on the
floor except my own. I called out their names until my voice echoed back
at me, hollow and mocking.

I radioed base.

“Team Four to base. I’ve lost contact with my team. George and Sofia are
missing. I’m alone in section seven, corridor B. Requesting immediate
assistance.”

Static.

I tried again. More static — but beneath it, faintly, I thought I could
hear something. Not voices. Something else. A sound like breathing, slow
and deep, as if the tunnel itself was alive.

I made a decision. I would not panic. I would not move. I would stay
exactly where I was and wait for someone to find me.

I lasted six minutes before the tapping started again.

It was closer this time. Much closer. And it was coming from below me —
from beneath the concrete floor beneath my feet.

Chapter 5: The Voice

I followed the tapping for what felt like an hour.

I told myself I was following it because it might be Marcus. I did not
ask myself why it always stayed just out of reach, always pulling me one
corridor deeper, one level further down.

I found a staircase that was not on any map. It descended steeply, the
walls narrowing as it went, the air growing colder with each step. The
emergency lighting ended at the top of the stairs. Below was darkness so
complete it looked solid.

I turned on my flashlight and went down.

At the bottom was a corridor unlike anything above. The walls were
smooth, almost polished, as if worn by years of use. The floor was
slightly concave, like the bed of a dried river. The ceiling was so low
I had to tilt my head to avoid it.

I had gone perhaps fifty feet when my flashlight died.

I stood in perfect darkness, one hand on the smooth wall, breathing
slowly. In the silence, I became aware of how loud my own heartbeat was.
How loud my own breathing was. How the darkness pressed against me like
something physical.

Then a voice said, quietly and clearly, directly into my right ear:

“You should not have come down here.”

I spun around. My hand flew out, hitting nothing. I backed against the
wall.

“Who’s there?” My voice came out steadier than I felt. “I’m a volunteer
search and rescue worker. Identify yourself.”

Silence.

Then, from the darkness ahead of me — not behind, ahead:

“I said the wrong thing. I’m sorry. Let me try again.”

A pause.

“My name is Jake.”

Chapter 6: The Radio

I did not speak for a long time.

The darkness pressed in around me. I could hear the voice breathing —
slow, measured, patient — somewhere ahead in the black.

“That’s not possible,” I said finally. “Jake is—”

“Missing,” the voice finished. “Eleven years. I know.”

“Who are you?” My voice had dropped to almost a whisper. “What are you?”

“I told you. But you don’t have to believe me. You just have to listen.”

My radio crackled. I grabbed it. “Hello? Base, this is Daniel — I mean,
this is me — I need extraction—”

“The radio won’t work down here,” the voice said. “It hasn’t worked
since you came down the stairs. Check it.”

I clicked the receiver. Static. I flipped open the battery cover with
shaking fingers.

The batteries were there. The radio should have worked. It did not.

“Something down here absorbs signal,” the voice said. “Radio waves,
light waves. It absorbs a lot of things.”

“What does?”

“The thing at the bottom,” the voice said. “The thing that brought you
here. The thing that brought me here, eleven years ago.”

I pressed my back against the wall and slid slowly down until I was
sitting on the cold floor.

“I know what you’re thinking,” the voice said. “You’re thinking you’re
hallucinating.”

“Aren’t I?”

“Ask me something only Jake would know.”

My throat tightened.

“What was the last thing you said to me?”

The darkness was quiet for a moment. Then the voice said, softly:

“I said I’d be back before Mom got home. And that you worried too much.
And then I put my earbuds in.”

I did not say anything. I could not.

“You were right to worry,” the voice said. “I should have listened.”

Chapter 7: Wrong Turn

Jake guided me through the darkness one careful step at a time.

The corridor turned, branched, doubled back on itself in ways that made
no architectural sense. I kept one hand on the wall and counted my
steps, trying to build a mental map, but the map kept refusing to hold
its shape. A corridor I had walked in a straight line would somehow
bring me back to a room I had already left.

“Don’t try to map it,” Jake said. “It shifts. The geometry down here
doesn’t work the way you’re used to.”

“How long have you been down here?”

A pause that lasted too long.

“Time doesn’t work right either,” Jake said. “It feels like days
sometimes. Sometimes it feels like minutes. I stopped counting a long
time ago.”

I thought about eleven years. I thought about all the maps I had drawn,
all the circles I had made, all the streets I had walked. I thought
about Jake at seventeen, earbuds in, hands in his jacket pockets.

“Did it hurt?” I asked. “When you—”

“No,” Jake said quickly. “It was fast. I didn’t understand what was
happening until it was already done.”

“What is it? What brought you here?”

“Something old,” Jake said. “Something that lives in the deep places. It
doesn’t think the way we think. It doesn’t want things the way we want
things. But it hungers. And it’s patient. And it’s very, very good at
making people take the wrong turn.”

I did not answer. I was thinking about eleven years of wrong turns.

Chapter 8: The Door

We had been moving for what felt like hours when I saw the light.

Just a thin yellow line beneath a heavy door at the end of a corridor.
After so long in absolute darkness it hit my eyes like a flare. I ran
toward it before I could stop myself.

“Stop.”

I stopped, hand on the door handle.

“Don’t open that.”

“There’s light. There are people on the other side.” I pressed my ear to
the door. I could hear voices — muffled, but unmistakably human. “That’s
the search party. They’re looking for me.”

“Listen to me. Ask them to open the door from their side.”

I hesitated. Then I called out: “Hello? I’m trapped in here! Can someone
open this door?”

The voices on the other side brightened immediately. Yes! We found him!
Just hold on, we’re going to get you out!

“Ask them,” Jake said quietly. “Ask them to open it from their side.”

“The handle’s not working from in here,” I called. “Can you push it open
from your end?”

A pause. Then: We can’t. It only opens from your side. Just turn the
handle.

“Every door down here opens from both sides,” Jake said. “I checked
every single one of them.”

I stood very still.

“Open it from your side,” I said again. “Please.”

More excuses. Someone’s hands were full. The mechanism was jammed. Just
turn the handle.

I took my hand off the door. Stepped back. Then another step back.

The light beneath the door went out. The voices stopped.

I leaned against the wall and breathed.

“It gets better at it,” Jake said, “the hungrier it gets.”

Chapter 9: Jake

I sat in the dark for a long time before I spoke again.

“Why are you still here?” I finally asked. “Eleven years. Why haven’t
you—”

“I don’t know exactly,” Jake said. “I think some part of me held on.
Waiting.”

“Waiting for what?”

“For someone to find me. Even if they couldn’t bring me back.” A pause.
“I knew you wouldn’t stop looking. I knew that even down here.”

My throat was tight in a way that had nothing to do with the bad air.

“I looked everywhere,” I said. “I looked for eleven years.”

“I know.”

“I didn’t know about this place.”

“Nobody does,” Jake said. “That’s the point. It hides itself. It makes
people take wrong turns. It brings people to it, and then it keeps
them.”

“Is there a way out?” I asked. “For me?”

“Yes,” Jake said. “That’s why I’m here. That’s what I’ve been doing all
this time — learning it. Finding the way out.”

“And for you?”

The word landed like a stone dropped into still water.

“No,” he said quietly. “Not for me. I’ve been here too long. But you —
you’re new. It hasn’t had time to absorb you.”

“Jake—”

“Don’t,” Jake said. “Please don’t. Just listen to me. I’m going to get
you out of here. And then you’re going to go home. And you’re going to
stop looking. Promise me.”

“I promise,” I said at last.

“Good,” Jake said. And for just a moment, he sounded exactly like the
seventeen year old boy who had walked out of our apartment eleven years
ago. “Now get up. We need to move.”

Chapter 10: The Thing Below

We moved fast now, Jake directing me through the shifting tunnels with
precise, urgent instructions.

Left. Straight. Stop — wait — now right. Three steps back. Don’t touch
the wall here.

I followed without question. The air was growing thicker, warmer,
heavier with that sweet organic smell. It was stronger now. Much
stronger.

“Is it following us?”

“It’s always following,” Jake said. “It just redirects. Shifts the
corridors. Tries to turn you around.”

We passed through a room I could not see but could feel — vast, open,
the floor changing texture beneath my feet from concrete to something
else. Something that gave slightly under my weight, like compacted
earth. Or something worse.

“Don’t think about it. Don’t slow down.”

The floor shifted. A low vibration moved through the walls — not
mechanical. Rhythmic. Like a pulse.

“It knows you’re close to the way out. It’s going to try harder now.
Whatever you see, whatever you hear — it’s not real. Keep moving.”

The lights came on.

Brilliant white fluorescent lights flooding the corridor ahead of me.
And in that light, I saw a figure standing at the far end.

A young man, seventeen years old, earbuds in, hands in his jacket
pockets. He turned around. He smiled.

“That is not me,” Jake’s voice said, ragged now. “Do not go toward it.
Do not stop.”

I walked past the figure without looking at it directly.

I heard it make a sound behind me — not a human sound — as the lights
went out again.

Chapter 11: The Key

“There’s one more thing,” Jake said.

We were close now. I could feel it — a change in the air, a distant
vibration that felt mechanical rather than alive. The city overhead.

“When I first came down here,” Jake said, “I found something. I think it
was left by someone who had been here before me and found the way out.”

“What was it?”

“A key. Small, old. An eagle engraved on it.”

I reached into my jacket pocket. My fingers closed around something
small and metal. I had picked it up hours ago from the floor of a
corridor, barely registering it.

I ran my thumb over the surface. Wings. A bird in flight.

“I have it,” I said.

“I know,” Jake said. “I left it for you.”

“You knew I was coming?”

“I told you. I’ve been waiting.” A pause. “There’s a door ahead. About
forty feet. Heavy, chained. The key fits the lock. Once you go through,
you keep moving. You don’t come back. No matter what you hear.”

I walked forward, counting my steps. At thirty eight feet my
outstretched hand found the door. I felt along the chain until I found
the lock. I slotted the key in.

It turned smoothly, as if it had been oiled yesterday.

The chain fell away.

“Daniel.”

“Yeah?”

“Tell Mom I’m sorry I didn’t come home.”

I pressed my forehead against the cold iron door and did not say
anything for a long time.

“I will,” I said. “I promise.”

“And Daniel?”

“Yeah?”

“Stop looking. Live your life.”

Chapter 12: The Last Corridor

I pushed the door open and ran.

Behind me, something screamed — not with sound but with sensation, a
pressure wave that rolled through the tunnel like a physical blow. The
walls contracted. The floor heaved. The air turned hot and tasted of
copper and earth.

I ran.

Jake’s voice was fading now, breaking up like a signal losing its
source.

“Straight ahead… don’t stop… almost there… straight ahead…”

The corridor tilted upward. My legs burned. My lungs burned.

“Straight… keep going… straight…”

Then — light. Real light. The pale gray light of a November morning in
New York City, filtering down through a maintenance grate in the ceiling
twenty feet ahead. The sound of the city: traffic, wind, the distant
wail of a siren.

I reached the grate, slammed both palms against it, and pushed. It gave
way and I hauled myself through, tumbling out onto wet concrete in an
alley off Worth Street.

I lay on my back in the cold rain and looked up at the sky for a long
time.

Epilogue

They found me forty minutes later. I had been missing for sixty one
hours.

George and Sofia were located in section five, physically unharmed but
disoriented. Neither of them had any memory of the lights going out.

Marcus Webb was found the following morning near the search entry point.
Dehydrated and shaken but uninjured. He said he had been walking in
circles in the dark for days. He said something had kept turning him
around.

The authorities filed a report. The maintenance shaft was sealed.

I gave my statement, went home, and slept for eighteen hours.

When I woke up, I took the map off my wall. The one covered in circles
and red strings and years of desperate notation. I folded it carefully
and let it go.

I kept the key. It sits in a drawer by my bed. Some nights I take it out
and run my thumb over the raised eagle.

I do not go looking anymore.

But sometimes, on the subway, when the train dips into the deepest part
of the tunnel and the lights flicker for just a moment —

I hear it.

Very faint. Very far away.

Just once.

Always just once.

Later, gator.
