My dad runs sometimes.

Not to exercise, not because he’s late, and never because he’s scared.
He’ll just suddenly take off running for absolutely no reason. It isn’t
common enough to get used to, but it happens often enough that nobody in
my family is surprised anymore.

The first time I saw it, I was seven. Dad was washing the dishes after
dinner when he suddenly stopped, stared out the kitchen window for a few
seconds, and let out a quiet, childlike giggle. It wasn’t loud or
unsettling on its own. It sounded like he’d just remembered something
funny.

Then he sprinted out the back door.

My mom barely looked up from her book.

“He’ll be back.”

About twenty minutes later, he walked through the door, breathing a
little heavier than before, finished washing the dishes, and spent the
rest of the evening watching television. Nobody asked where he’d gone.

As I got older, I realized the giggle always came first. It didn’t
matter if we were at church, in the grocery store, or eating dinner
together. He would suddenly stop what he was doing, giggle to himself,
and run. He never smiled while he did it. That was what always bothered
me. The giggle sounded cheerful, but his expression never changed. His
face stayed completely blank, like whatever he found funny wasn’t
something the rest of us could understand.
Whenever he came back, he’d continue whatever he’d been doing as if
nothing had happened. If anyone asked him why he ran, he’d always give
the same answer.

“I don’t know.”

He never laughed it off. He never made up an excuse. He genuinely didn’t
know.

Eventually, it became one of those strange things our family stopped
talking about. Dad runs sometimes. That’s just how he is.

Then he died.

It was a heart attack. Quick. Peaceful, according to the doctors.

A few months after the funeral, I found a box of old camcorder tapes
while cleaning out the attic. I decided to digitize them before they
deteriorated. One of the recordings showed Dad repairing our mailbox
while Mom filmed from the porch. I’d seen that tape dozens of times
growing up, so I already knew what was coming. He’d giggle, sprint
across the street, and disappear from the frame.

This time I watched it frame by frame.
As Dad ran past the camera, he turned his head toward the lens.

I frowned.

I didn’t remember him doing that.

Then I advanced one more frame.

He was smiling.

Not a grin. Just the slightest smile, like he was trying to hold back
another giggle.

I sat there for a long time, trying to convince myself I’d simply never
noticed it before. But I’d watched that tape my entire life. I knew
every second of it.

He had never smiled.

I called my sister without telling her why and asked her to watch the
video.

She called back a few minutes later.

Before I could ask what she thought, she said, “Did he always smile?”

Neither of us remembered that.

I closed the laptop and sat there in silence.
A few seconds later, I heard someone sprinting down the hallway.

The footsteps came closer and closer until they stopped right outside my
bedroom door.

I waited for the doorknob to turn.

It didn’t.

Then I heard a giggle.
