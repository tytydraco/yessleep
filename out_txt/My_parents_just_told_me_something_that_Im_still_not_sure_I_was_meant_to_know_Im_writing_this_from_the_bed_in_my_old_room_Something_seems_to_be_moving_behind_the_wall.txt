I drove up to see my parents today. Nothing special, just one of those
visits you make because enough time has passed that not going starts to
feel like a statement. My mother had spent the last week redecorating my
old room into a proper guest room, which she mentioned at least four
times on the phone beforehand, each time with the particular pride of
someone who has finally reclaimed a space they’d been waiting years to
reclaim.

The room looked good. New paint, new curtains, a fancy bed replacing my
old one. She’d cleared out the last boxes of my old things and put up a
couple of framed prints and a small lamp on the nightstand. It looked
like a room in a hotel that was trying to feel cozy. I told her it was
lovely and she beamed.

It was only when she was showing me around that I noticed the wall.

Most of it was fine. Fresh white paint, clean and even. But on the wall
opposite the window, roughly circular, maybe half a meter across, the
paint had a different quality. Slightly darker. Not dramatically, not
something you’d notice in passing. But standing in the quiet room in the
afternoon light it was clearly visible. A faint shadow in the wall, like
something behind the plaster was bleeding through.

“What’s that?” I asked.

My mother looked at the spot. Something flickered across her face, like
she was deciding in real time how much she wanted to say.

“We should probably tell him,” my father said from the doorway. He’d
been leaning there quietly, and I’d half-forgotten he was there.

My mother looked at him. Then she looked at me. Then she sat down on the
edge of the new guest bed and told me the story.

-–

I was four years old.

They’d put me to bed at the usual time and gone back downstairs.
Sometime after midnight they heard a sound from my room.

My mother described it as a commotion. Furniture moving. A sound she
couldn’t place, low and rhythmic, that she said she’d never quite been
able to describe to anyone’s satisfaction since.

My father was first up the stairs. He opened my bedroom door.

He didn’t say much about what the next few minutes looked like. My
mother said even less. What I was able to piece together from what they
were willing to tell me is this: there were two figures in the room.
Standing beside my bed. I was still in it, apparently still asleep, and
the two figures were in the process of; she used the word collecting.
They were collecting me.

When my father came through the door they turned. He described them as
women, or as things that looked like women if you didn’t look carefully.
He’s not a man given to elaboration or drama. He said they looked wrong
in a way he still doesn’t have the words for. He said the room was very
cold.

What followed was apparently brief and violent. My parents are both
practical people and I think whatever instinct took over in that moment
was simply the instinct of two parents in a room where something was
threatening their child. My mother threw something; she thinks it was
the lamp from my nightstand, the one with the yellow shade that I still
vaguely remember. My father grabbed one of them, and he said it felt
like grabbing something that was partly not there, like his hands
couldn’t fully close on it.

They left through the wall.

He said it so casually, the way you’d say they left through the door.
They moved toward the wall and then they were gone, and the wall behind
them was darker than it had been, and the room smelled like soil and
something else he wouldn’t name.

I was still asleep in my bed.

The next morning my parents moved the wardrobe in front of the spot. It
stood there for the next twenty-two years, until last week when my
mother redecorated.

She’d repainted over it twice, she said. It keeps coming back.

-–

I didn’t know what to say to that. I’m not sure there’s an obvious
response when your parents calmly tell you that two entities tried to
take you in the night when you were four and that the mark they left is
still on the wall of what is now your guest room.

I asked why they’d never told me.

My father shrugged. “What would have been the point?”

My mother said: “You were fine. You slept through the whole thing.”

We had dinner after that. We talked about other things; my job, the
neighbours, some work they’re planning on the garden. My parents are
extraordinarily good at talking about other things.

I helped with the dishes and said goodnight and came upstairs.

I’ve been lying in this bed since about eleven with the lamp on , the
one my mother bought for the guest room, small and white and completely
inadequate for the way I currently feel about this room. I have not been
able to sleep.

The spot is directly across from the foot of the bed. In the lamplight
it looks faint, almost imaginary, the kind of thing you’d convince
yourself was just uneven paint in a certain light. I’ve been trying to
convince myself of that for the last three hours.

The house is quiet. My parents went to bed at their usual time with
their usual lack of apparent concern, which is either deeply reassuring
or the most unsettling thing about this whole situation, and I haven’t
decided which.

About ten minutes ago I started hearing something.

I want to be precise about this because I’m aware of how a sleepless
brain works, how it finds patterns in silence, how anxiety has a way of
becoming auditory. So I want to be precise: it is not a sound exactly.
It’s more like a pressure change at the threshold of hearing. Rhythmic.
Low.

I’ve been lying here without moving for a while now, watching the spot
on the wall.

The lamp is still on. I’m not going to turn it off.

I think and I want to stress the word think, the light in here is uneven
and I’ve been awake for a long time, but I think the spot is slightly
darker than it was when I came to bed.

I’m probably wrong about that.

I’m going to keep the lamp on and I’m going to stay very still and I’m
going to wait for morning.

I’m sure it will be fine.

I slept through the whole thing last time.
