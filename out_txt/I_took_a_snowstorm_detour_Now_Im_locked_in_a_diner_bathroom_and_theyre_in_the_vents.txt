If you ever see a detour sign on Interstate 80 near the Nevada border
with a crude, black spray-painted ‘X’ over it, turn around. Drive until
your gas tank is empty if you have to. Just don’t take the exit.

​I did. And right now, I’m locked in the bathroom of a 24-hour diner,
listening to the ceiling tiles rattle, praying to a God I haven’t spoken
to in years. I need to get this down before my battery dies. Or before
it finds a way in.

​I’m a long-haul trucker. Two nights ago, I was hauling 40,000 pounds of
auto parts through a freak blizzard. The main highway was completely
gridlocked by a jackknifed rig, and my dispatcher was screaming in my
ear about deadlines. So, when my GPS flickered and suggested an old
logging route that bypassed the wreck, I took it.

​The road was a nightmare—barely wide enough for my rig, flanked by
towering pines that swallowed the headlights. The snow was falling so
hard it felt like I was driving through a static TV screen.

​Then, the radio died. Not just static, but a sudden, dead silence that
made my ears ring.

​A second later, something slammed into my grill.

​The impact was heavy enough to rattle my teeth and jerk the steering
wheel violently out of my hands. I hit the brakes, the rig fishtailing
on the ice before shuddering to a halt. My heart hammered against my
ribs. I thought I’d hit an elk.

​I grabbed my flashlight and a heavy steel tire iron from behind the
seat, stepping out into the biting cold. The wind howled, whipping snow
into my eyes. I walked around to the front of the cab, sweeping the beam
over the blood-spattered grill and down to the snow.

​It wasn’t an elk.

​It was pale. Bone-white and completely hairless. It lay twisted in the
snow, but its proportions were all wrong. The limbs were far too long,
ending in hands with too many joints. It was wearing the shredded,
bloody remains of a heavy winter coat.

​I froze, the freezing air burning my lungs. I took a step back.

​The thing’s head snapped toward me.

​It had no eyes. Just smooth, pale skin where the sockets should be, and
a jaw that unhinged, dropping to its chest to reveal rows of
needle-thin, translucent teeth. It let out a sound I will never forget—a
high-pitched, vibrating click that sounded like a cicada magnified a
thousand times.

​And then, from the black tree line on my left, the clicking was
answered.

​Click-click-click-screeeech.

​Then from the right. Then from behind my trailer.

​Dozens of them.

​Pure, unadulterated panic hijacked my brain. I bolted for the cab. I
heard the snow crunching violently behind me, moving impossibly fast. I
threw myself into the driver’s seat, slamming the door and hitting the
lock just as a sickening THUD rattled the heavy steel.

​A hand—long, white, and tipped with thick, jagged nails—slapped against
the driver’s side window. The glass instantly spider-webbed.

​I slammed the rig into gear and floored it. The massive engine roared,
the tires screaming for traction on the ice before the truck violently
lurched forward. I didn’t care about the road anymore; I just kept the
wheel straight and the pedal down.

​I heard them landing on the roof of the cab. Heavy, rhythmic thumps.
They were crawling over the metal like giant insects. A pale, elongated
face slid upside down over my windshield, its blind face pressing
against the glass, the jaw unhinging to bite at the reinforced safety
glass.

​I hit the wipers. The heavy blades smacked the thing in the head, and it
shrieked, its claws tearing a deep groove into the hood as it was thrown
off.

​“Come on, come on!” I screamed, pushing the rig to 60, then 70 miles an
hour down a blind, winding mountain road. The trailer was violently
swaying, threatening to flip us all into the abyss, but I couldn’t stop.

​Then, the passenger side window imploded.

​Freezing wind and shattered glass blasted into the cab, followed by a
blur of pale flesh. One of them had squeezed halfway through the window.
It lunged, its freezing, unnatural hands grabbing my right shoulder. Its
nails tore through my jacket and deep into my flesh. The pain was
blinding.

​I let go of the steering wheel with my right hand, grabbed the steel
tire iron I’d left on the passenger seat, and swung blindly. I felt the
iron connect with a wet crunch. The thing shrieked, black blood spraying
across the dashboard, but its grip only tightened. Its jaw snapped
inches from my neck, the stench of rotting meat washing over me.

​I slammed the brakes.

​The sudden deceleration threw the creature forward against the
dashboard. I reversed my grip on the tire iron and drove it straight
down into the back of its pale, hairless skull. It went limp, sliding
out of the shattered window and disappearing under the roaring tires of
the trailer.

​I didn’t stop. I drove for two straight hours, my shoulder bleeding out,
the wind screaming through the broken window, fighting to keep the rig
on the road. The thumping on the roof eventually stopped, replaced by
the solitary howl of the blizzard.

​I finally broke through the tree line and merged back onto the
interstate, pulling into the first bright neon sign I saw—this diner.

​I stumbled inside, bleeding, half-frozen, and begged the waitress to
lock the doors and call the police. The cops are on their way. They
think it was a bear attack. They think I’m in shock.

​But about ten minutes ago, I asked the manager if I could check the
security cameras that face the parking lot. I needed to know I was safe.
I needed to see my rig sitting empty under the streetlights.

​We rolled the tape back. We watched my battered truck pull into the lot.

​And then we watched three long, pale, multi-jointed figures unfold
themselves from the undercarriage.

​They didn’t run into the woods. They crawled on all fours, keeping their
bellies low to the asphalt, and slipped straight under the diner’s front
porch.

​The cops say they are ten minutes away.

But the clicking sound just started in the air vents above my head
