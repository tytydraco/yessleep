​

Havenbrook Village had been buzzing with commotion lately.

The town issued an official document mandating a grave relocation
project: all scattered old burial mounds on hillsides and farmland were
to be levelled and moved into a unified public cemetery.

While this was a standard government initiative, it ran deeply against
local customs. Ancestral graves were regarded as the root of every
family, and no villager was willing to disturb them lightly. Yet higher
authorities pressed for swift progress. Completing the task would bring
political merit; dragging feet would be deemed inadequate work.

Thus, the village launched a large-scale grave-flattening campaign. In
just half a month, nearly all graves on the mountain had been
levelled—all except one, the tomb of Old Man Hale’s son.

 

Old Man Hale was over seventy. His wife passed away when he was young,
and his daughter-in-law abandoned the family when their grandson was
only two years old. Only the old man, his son, and his young grandson
depended on each other to survive.

Misfortune never spared the wretched. Two years prior, his son had been
working on a bridge construction site out of town when the structure
collapsed. Very few workers survived, and all that was sent back to
Havenbrook Village was a box of ashes.

From that day on, Old Man Hale and his grandson lived beside the grave,
which stood as the elderly man’s last remaining comfort in life. Every
villager knew this.

 

Once the grave relocation work began, township officials visited his
home repeatedly to persuade him to comply, but Old Man Hale refused
stubbornly, repeating only one line: “Let my boy rest in peace for three
full years.”

The township could not afford to wait. Deputy Township Head Cole, the
official in charge of the project, resolved to carry out forced
levelling.

Before dawn one morning, Cole led more than a dozen labourers to the
grave site.

“Start work!” he ordered.

A young labourer raised his shovel to take the first scoop of soil, when
a bloodcurdling scream erupted behind them.

Everyone spun around to see Cole sprinting madly toward the western edge
of the village, yelling nonstop: “Stay away from me! Don’t come near!”

The group chased after him and found him climbing a decades-old locust
tree at the village’s west end. He scrambled higher and higher like a
monkey.

Then came a sharp crack. The branch snapped, and Cole crashed heavily to
the ground, his leg broken instantly.

 

Word of the incident spread across nearby towns and villages within
days. Some claimed he had angered the deceased; others said Old Man Hale
had cursed him; a few insisted Cole suffered from mental instability all
along. Speculation ran rampant, yet the grave relocation project could
not halt. The township quickly appointed a new lead official: Deputy
Township Head Garrett Moore.

 

Young, impetuous and self-assured, Garrett Moore scoffed when he heard
Cole’s ordeal. “This is nothing but feudal superstition. Can one elderly
man defy official policy?”

On his first day in charge, he headed straight for Havenbrook Village
with the work team. It was midday, under blistering sunlight. From afar,
they spotted two figures kneeling before the grave—an old man and a
small child burning joss paper: Old Man Hale and his grandson.

Garrett Moore sneered. “Today I’ll see who dares stand in my way.”

He snatched a shovel from the village committee leader and strode toward
the tomb.

 

Strange things unfolded as he drew near. The scenery blurred as if
shrouded in thick fog. Garrett Moore rubbed his eyes, looked up again,
and found the ground empty—no grave, no old man, no child, only flat
farmland stretching out.

“How odd,” he muttered, pressing forward. Suddenly another burial mound
appeared in the distance, with the same old man and child kneeling
before it. Thinking he had wandered off course, he turned to walk toward
it.

The moment he took a step, his foot landed on something raised and soft.
He glanced down, and terror shot through him: he was standing squarely
on top of a grave.

Garrett Moore stumbled backward several paces. When he lifted his head,
everything returned to normal—Old Man Hale and the boy stood mere metres
away, the tomb intact where it had always been. His clumsy retreat sent
him sprawling face-first into the dirt. The staff behind him barely
stifled their laughter.

His face turned livid, yet he forced a calm tone. “This soil is packed
hard, no wonder it’s difficult to level.”

 

Old Man Hale lifted his head, his voice thin and weathered with age.
“Leader, grant me one more year. When the three years of mourning are up
next year, I will move his grave myself.”

Garrett Moore shook his head. “Policy is policy. We cannot delay the
entire village’s project for your personal sake.” He waved his arm. “Get
to work!”

The labourers began shovelling soil, but every time they scraped a
little away, Old Man Hale threw himself over the mound to shield it.
After an hour of futile struggle, only a thin layer of topsoil had been
removed. Garrett Moore had no choice but to withdraw, tossing a bitter
parting remark: “We’ll see how long you can guard this tomb.”

 

At ten o’clock that night, Garrett Moore returned to Havenbrook Village
with his crew, planning to finish the levelling overnight.

As they reached the village entrance, a middle-aged man hurried to greet
them with an ingratiating smile. “Deputy Head Moore, I’m Old Man Hale’s
nephew, Ethan. Let’s talk about the grave matter—come to my house for a
few drinks first.”

After a moment’s hesitation, Garrett Moore agreed. The group followed
the man into the village, arriving at an old courtyard house. The main
hall was spacious, furnished with a large round table laden with wine
and dishes. A shrine stood in the corner, holding a black-and-white
portrait of a man in his thirties with an honest, simple smile—Old Man
Hale’s late son.

 

After several rounds of wine, Ethan raised his cup, his eyes glistening
with tears. “My poor cousin. He lost his mother at two, then died in a
construction accident as an adult. The compensation was only twenty
thousand dollars—twenty thousand for a whole human life…”

He drained his glass in one gulp. Silence fell over the room. Garrett
Moore felt a flicker of sympathy and patted his chest to reassure him.
“Rest assured, I will report your situation to higher authorities and
push for a delay if possible.”

Ethan smiled, pulling a thick stack of cash from his coat and sliding it
across the table. Garrett Moore feigned refusal, yet his gaze lingered
fixed on the money. The drinking continued late into the night, and
every man present eventually passed out drunk.

 

Early the next morning, villagers heading to their fields stumbled upon
an eerie sight. A dozen men sat circled around Old Man Hale’s son’s
grave, heads drooping lifelessly like corpses—Garrett Moore and his
entire work team. They sat in a ring enclosing the tomb, each clutching
handfuls of mud, covered head to toe in dirt, as if they had dug at the
grave all night long. None of them could recall a single thing that had
happened.

Panicked, the villagers scanned the crowd and realised Garrett Moore was
missing. Just then, foolish giggles echoed from the west end of the
village.

“Hehehe…”

They turned to look and gasped in horror. Perched high in the old locust
tree was Garrett Moore, wandering his gaze from side to side on the
branches, searching for something—exactly the same as Cole had been.

A split second later, a crack echoed. The branch snapped, and Garrett
Moore screamed as he plummeted to the ground, his leg shattered on
impact.

 

Midday sunlight spilled gently over the farmland at the village’s west
edge. Old Man Hale knelt before the grave with his grandson, slowly
burning joss paper. The young boy suddenly looked up and asked,
“Grandpa, why did both of them break their legs?”

Old Man Hale stared silently at the tombstone for a long time. The paper
offerings in the brazier turned to ashes, swirled upward by a light wind
as if stirred by an invisible breath.

After a lengthy pause, the old man stroked his grandson’s head softly
and murmured, “When you live as a person, you must leave a path for the
living—and a home for the dead.”

A gust of wind picked up, sending clouds of ash swirling all around the
grave. For a fleeting moment, it seemed a young man stood behind the
mound, watching the grandfather and child quietly. But when they blinked
and looked again, no one was there—only the lonely earthen tomb standing
firm in the middle of the field.
