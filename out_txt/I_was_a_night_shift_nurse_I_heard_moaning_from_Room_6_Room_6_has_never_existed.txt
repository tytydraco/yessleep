I don’t know why I’m posting this.

Maybe because I haven’t slept properly in three months. Maybe because
I’ve told this story to exactly zero people in my real life and it’s
eating me from the inside. Maybe because r/nosleep is the only place on
the internet where I think someone might actually believe me.

My name is Sarah. I’m a nurse. And what I’m about to tell you happened
on my seventh day at a new job.

I need you to believe me when I say — I am not a person who believes in
this stuff. I grew up skeptical. I studied science. I have spent my
entire career in hospitals watching people look for miracles and finding
medicine instead. I do not believe in ghosts. I did not believe in
ghosts.

I want to be very clear about that before I tell you what I saw.

It was a small private hospital. Not one of those massive city hospitals
with hundreds of staff and patients everywhere. This place was quiet.
Intimate. The kind of hospital where you learn your patients’ names on
day one. I had just started — one week in, still learning which corridor
led where, still fumbling with the door codes.

That night I was on the night shift.

By midnight the ward was completely settled. Patients asleep. Doctors
done with their rounds and gone home. The overhead lights dimmed to that
low amber glow they switch to after midnight. My colleague Diana was
supposed to stay with me the whole shift but around 1AM she looked at me
and said she needed to sit down for twenty minutes.

And then she was gone.

I sat on the bench outside the ward entrance. Alone. The silence was so
complete it almost had texture. Just the occasional beep of a monitor
behind a closed door and the hum of the fluorescent lights.

I remember thinking — this is peaceful.

I should have trusted the discomfort I felt underneath that thought. The
part of me that knew the silence was wrong. Too complete. Too heavy.
Like the building was holding its breath.

I don’t know exactly how long I’d been sitting there when I heard it.

A groan.

Low. Long. The kind of sound that bypasses your brain entirely and hits
somewhere older. As a nurse you’re wired to respond to that sound. It
doesn’t matter how tired you are or how quiet the shift has been. That
sound pulls you to your feet before you’ve made a conscious decision to
stand.

I stood up.

I looked down the hallway — the long stretch where the patient room
doors lined the left wall. Five rooms. Small hospital. Rooms one through
five.

And outside what I could only see as Room 6 — at the very end of the
corridor — there was a woman sitting on a bench.

She was hunched forward. Head almost to her knees. Rocking slightly.
Moaning in a way that made my chest hurt to hear it.

A patient who got out of bed, I thought. A visitor who collapsed waiting
for news.

It didn’t matter. Someone was hurting. I was the only one there.

I stood up and started walking toward her.

I noticed it the way you notice something wrong in a dream — slowly,
then all at once.

I was walking. My legs were moving. My shoes were hitting the linoleum.
My arms were swinging at my sides.

But I wasn’t getting any closer to her.

I watched the room numbers pass on my left.

Room 1.

Room 2.

Room 3.

Room 4.

Room 5.

And then my body stopped.

No wall. No glass. No barrier I could see or touch. Just — nothing. A
complete and total refusal from my own legs to carry me one step
further. I looked down at my feet like they belonged to a stranger. I
told them to move.

They didn’t.

I tried leaning forward. Nothing. I tried stepping sideways, angling
around it, approaching from a different direction. Every single time I
hit the same invisible point in space and could not pass it.

Room 5. And no further.

That’s when the cold hit me. A wave of it from nowhere. The kind of cold
that doesn’t come from temperature. The kind that comes from somewhere
else entirely.

And then the woman started to stand up.

She rose slowly. Unevenly. Every joint bending at the wrong moment. Her
weight shifting wrong. Her silhouette against the amber light just
slightly too strange to be a person moving the way people move.

She started walking toward me.

I found my voice somehow.

“Hey — stop. Who are you? Are you okay?”

She froze.

Dead still. Like someone had cut the strings holding her up all at once.

Then — slowly — she turned around.

I have tried to find the right words for what I saw when she turned
around. I have been trying for three months. I don’t think the right
words exist in any language I speak. But I’ll try.

She was wearing a nurse’s uniform. Exactly like mine. Same pale blue.
Same fit. Same lanyard.

Her hair was wild. Matted. Hanging in dark tangled ropes over most of
her face.

Her skin was the color of old candle wax.

And her eyes —

Her eyes were black. Not dark brown. Not dilated. Not any shade of
human. Black. Completely. Like two holes cut out of her face that went
somewhere else. Somewhere I did not want to follow.

She looked at me.

And then she smiled.

I can’t describe that smile. I won’t. All I’ll say is that it was the
shape of a smile on a face that had learned what smiling looked like but
had never understood what it was for.

She started walking toward me. Faster.

I couldn’t run. Couldn’t move. Couldn’t even step backward. Just frozen,
watching her come down that hallway with that lurching wrong walk, those
black eyes never blinking, that smile never wavering —

She reached out her hand.

Her fingers came within inches of my face.

And stopped.

She hit the same invisible wall that had stopped me — but from the other
side. Her hand pressed flat against empty air. She pushed. Harder. The
smile left her face and something worse replaced it. She slammed both
palms against nothing. Over and over. Her whole body shaking.

Then she opened her mouth.

And the sound that came out —

I won’t describe it. I genuinely cannot. What I’ll say is that it didn’t
come from a throat. It came from somewhere deeper than that. Somewhere
older than language. And it hit me like something physical.

Then the lights went out.

And so did I.

I woke up in a hospital bed.

Bright overhead lights. The smell of antiseptic. Diana gripping my hand,
white as a sheet. A doctor I didn’t recognize standing over me checking
my pulse.

I told them everything. Every single detail, as fast as my shaking voice
could manage.

The older doctor listened without expression. Then he said —

“Show me where.”

I walked with him to the hallway. Diana held my arm the whole way.

I pointed to the far end of the corridor.

“There. Outside Room 6. That’s where she was sitting.”

He looked at the hallway.

Then he looked at me.

I looked at the hallway.

The corridor ended at Room 5.

After Room 5 — after that invisible line that had stopped my feet and
then stopped her hand — there was nothing. A flat, smooth, unbroken
wall. No door. No frame. No number plate. No bench.

Just plaster. Painted white. Featureless.

Like Room 6 had never existed.

Like nothing had ever been there at all.

I resigned that morning. Still in my uniform. Sat in the administrator’s
office at 6AM and handed in my notice. Nobody tried very hard to stop
me.

I have never gone back.

I think about one thing constantly. The detail I cannot logic my way out
of no matter how many times I try.

That invisible barrier didn’t just stop me from reaching her.

It stopped her from reaching me.

Whatever that wall was — it was holding something back. Maybe protecting
our world from hers. Maybe protecting her from ours. Maybe — and this is
the thought that truly won’t let me sleep — I almost crossed into
somewhere I could never have come back from.

And whatever was on the other side saw my face.

And wanted to come through.

I don’t know what she was. I don’t know what Room 6 was. I don’t know
why I could see it when apparently nobody else ever had.

But I know this.

She was wearing my uniform.

She had been there before I arrived.

And when I called out to her —

She already knew my name.
