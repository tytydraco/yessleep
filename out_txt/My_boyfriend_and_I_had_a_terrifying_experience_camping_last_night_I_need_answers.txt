I don’t know where else to post this. We got back this morning and I
haven’t been able to stop shaking. Gio hasn’t said a word since last
night. If you know anything about Crown Land northeast of Saint-Donat,
Quebec, please read this.

I have a spreadsheet for camping.

I know how that sounds. Gio never lets me forget it either. He
screenshot it once and sent it to his sister with no caption, just to
watch me suffer. But listen, when you’re four hours northeast of
Montreal on Crown Land with no cell service, no park ranger, and no one
who knows exactly where you are, you want the person who packed the
first aid kit to have also cross-referenced the weather forecast with
the elevation map.

That’s just science.

We found the spot on Overlander, user-marked, four decent reviews,
someone had even left a fire ring. GioXplorer47, which is absolutely
Gio’s kind of username, had posted it six months ago with a photo of the
exact clearing we were now standing in. Remote, quiet, no crowds. 10/10
would return. Gio had shown me this like it was a Michelin star.

The drive up was the kind of beautiful that makes you feel briefly
insane for living in a city. We lost the highway somewhere around
Saint-Donat and after that it was just logging roads, boreal forest, and
the occasional sign warning us about something in French that Gio
translated loosely as probably moose.

We pulled in around four in the afternoon, which my spreadsheet had
correctly identified as the ideal setup window.

The clearing was perfect. Fire ring intact, flat ground, a narrow creek
maybe thirty metres east that we could hear at first.

I noticed the quiet maybe twenty minutes after we arrived, while I was
staking the tent and Gio was doing his version of helping, which
involved standing near things and handing them to me when asked.

It’s so still, I said.

Gio didn’t answer right away. He just whistled, low and tuneless, the
way he does when he’s not thinking about anything at all. The sound
disappeared into the trees immediately, like the forest swallowed it.

No other campers, he said finally, like this explained it.

And I thought, yeah. Yeah, that’s it. No families with screaming kids,
no one’s bluetooth speaker bleeding bad EDM across three campsites. Just
us and the trees and the late afternoon light coming through the canopy
in long gold columns.

I took a breath and felt my shoulders drop for the first time in weeks.

I didn’t notice that I couldn’t hear the creek anymore.

Here’s the thing about national parks that nobody tells you before
you’ve done both: they’re loud.

Not bad loud, just present. There’s always another campsite within
earshot, always someone’s kid losing their mind about a bug, always the
ambient hum of people existing nearby. You stop noticing it after a
while. It becomes the background radiation of the outdoors.

This was different.

By the time we had the tent up and the fire going, the forest around us
had gone completely, perfectly, almost aggressively quiet. No birds. No
wind moving through the canopy. No rustling from whatever small things
usually rustle. Even the creek had dropped out of earshot, which I told
myself was just the fire crackling over it.

Is it always this quiet up here? I asked.

Gio looked up from the camp stove, considered it for a second the way he
considers most things, slowly and without alarm.

Probably because we’re so far in, he said. No wind buffer from the
highway. No other campers. Sound just goes.

I nodded. That made sense. Gio is good at sense.

We ate as the sun dropped and the temperature dropped with it, faster
than I expected. That’s the thing about late August this far north. The
days still feel like summer but the nights remember what they are. By
eight o’clock I was in my fleece and by eight thirty I was eyeing the
tent.

The dark came in a way I wasn’t prepared for.

I grew up in Scarborough, have lived in Montreal for eleven years. I
know city dark, which isn’t really dark at all, just a dimmer setting on
the same world. This was something else. The fire lit maybe a ten metre
radius around us and beyond that edge the forest was absolute. Not dim.
Not shadowy. Just gone. I kept looking up expecting my eyes to adjust
the way they do, kept waiting for the treeline to resolve itself out of
the black.

It didn’t.

It’s so dark, I said, and even I could hear that it came out smaller
than I meant it to.

Gio looked up from his phone, glanced around agreeably, and went back to
whatever he was watching. He’d downloaded a season of something before
we left. Classic Gio. Zero bars, middle of nowhere, perfectly content.

I pulled my chair closer to the fire and told myself to stop being
dramatic.

I heard it at 9:47pm. I know the exact time because I had just checked
my phone, doing the mental math on how many hours until sunrise the way
I do every night, camping or otherwise.

Gio was beside me, chair tilted back, earbuds in, bathed in the blue
light of his screen. I had been watching the treeline without meaning
to. Just staring into the dark past the fire’s edge the way you stare at
nothing when your brain won’t stop.

Then, from somewhere in the trees to our left, a voice.

Hello? Hey there!

Bright and casual, the way you’d greet a neighbour across a fence.
Completely, horrifyingly ordinary.

I was on my feet before I knew I was standing.

Did you hear that?

Gio pulled out one earbud. Hear what?

Someone just said hello. From over there. I pointed at the treeline,
which told us nothing, being completely invisible.

He looked in that direction with the patient, amiable expression he uses
when he thinks I’m being anxious about something real but manageable.

Probably another camper, he said. There were a few spots marked on
Overlander near this one.

It was close, Gio. It was right there.

Sound travels weird out here. Could be from way further than it seemed.

I stood at the edge of the firelight for a long moment, staring at the
place where the dark began. I wanted to call back. Hey! Hello! Who’s
there? The way a normal person would.

My mouth stayed shut.

Come inside soon, I said. It’s getting cold.

I zipped the tent behind me and lay in my sleeping bag listening to the
silence press up against the nylon like it had weight.

Gio came in twenty minutes later, already half asleep before he hit the
pillow. Out completely in under four minutes. I know because I counted.

I lay there in the dark and stared at the ceiling of the tent and waited
for morning like I always do.

I must have fallen asleep at some point, which almost never happens.

One moment I was staring at the tent ceiling cataloguing every sound
that wasn’t happening, and the next I was somewhere thick and dark and
dreamless, and then I was awake again with no memory of the transition.
Gio’s breathing beside me, slow and even. The fire outside had died
down, just embers probably, because the light through the tent walls had
gone from orange to nothing.

I checked my phone. 2:13am.

I needed to pee.

I lay there for a full five minutes negotiating with myself about this,
which is very on brand for me. It was cold. It was dark. The bathroom
was the woods. But the alternative was lying here for another four hours
and I’ve tried that before and it doesn’t work.

I unzipped the tent as slowly as I could, which still sounded in the
silence like tearing the world in half.

Outside was cold and absolute. No moon made it through the canopy. I
switched on my headlamp and the forest jumped up around me, trees
materialising out of the black in a pale narrow corridor of light that
somehow made everything beyond it darker by contrast.

I went to the edge of the clearing, the dead leaves loud under my boots,
did what I came to do, and turned back toward camp.

That’s when I saw it.

I had been asleep. It was 2am. My headlamp caught the treeline on the
far side of the clearing for just a second as I turned and there was a
shape there that was tall and still and wrong. Not a tree. Trees don’t
have that kind of vertical. Trees don’t stand like something is deciding
whether to move.

I froze.

I stood there in the cold with my headlamp pointed directly at it and I
watched it and it was just the treeline, just branches, just the way
shadows stack on each other at 2am when you’re tired and your brain is
running hot.

I walked back to the tent. Quickly. Not running. I didn’t run.

I zipped it shut behind me and lay down next to Gio, who hadn’t moved,
whose breathing hadn’t changed, who was warm and real and completely
present beside me.

I closed my eyes.

I told myself: you were half asleep. You’d been staring into that
treeline all evening. You were primed for it. There was nothing there.

And I almost believed it.

I was almost all the way back under when I heard the snap of a branch
from the far side of the clearing. Single, clean, deliberate. Like
something that had been standing very still had finally decided to move.

It happened about forty minutes after I got back in the tent.

I had almost convinced myself. The shape at the treeline was branches.
The branch snap was an animal, a fox, a raccoon, something small and
ordinary going about its small ordinary night. I was tired and primed
and my brain had done what anxious brains do, which is take raw material
and build monsters out of it.

I was almost asleep again when I heard footsteps in the leaves outside.
Slow and deliberate, circling the tent.

I stopped breathing.

And then Gio’s voice, from outside, low and careful, barely above a
whisper.

Lalitha. Don’t make any noise. Just listen to me. Whatever is in there
beside you, it isn’t me. Come outside. Don’t wake it up.

Everything in me went cold.

I turned my head slowly.

There was someone beside me. Zipped into Gio’s sleeping bag. Gio’s face,
or what I could see of it in the dark. Gio’s smell, that specific
combination of cedar deodorant and camp smoke that I have fallen asleep
next to for six years.

Eyes wide open. Watching me.

And here is the thing I cannot get out of my head, the thing that is
making my hands shake as I type this: Gio does not wake up in the night.
Ever. In six years I have never once rolled over at 2am and found him
awake. He sleeps like something that has never once in its life been
afraid of the dark.

So why were his eyes open?

How long had they been open?

I lay there and I did the only thing I could think to do which was look
at his face and try to find him in it. The little scar through his left
eyebrow from a childhood bike accident. The way his nose sits slightly
crooked from a break he never had fixed. I looked for Gio in his face
the way you look for a word you know you know, and I found all the right
pieces, and I could not tell you with any certainty whether they added
up to him.

Outside, the footsteps had stopped.

The thing beside me hadn’t moved. Hadn’t blinked. Just watched me with
Gio’s eyes in the absolute dark.

And then it reached out and found my hand.

And held it.

And we lay there in the silence until the light changed grey through the
tent walls, and neither of us moved, and neither of us spoke, and I have
not been alone since we packed up camp this morning and drove back to
the highway.

He’s driving now. One hand on the wheel. Eyes on the road.

I keep looking over at him.

I keep looking for the scar.

Am I being dramatic? What do I do if I don’t find it?
