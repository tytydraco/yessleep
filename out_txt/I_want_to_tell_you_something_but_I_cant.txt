“I want to tell you something, but I can’t.”

—

Hi, I’m writing this post to get it off my chest. Where do I begin?

Pierce County. It was a Saturday night, the sky was a mix of gray and
purple, and I walked along the grass on my path.

I heard birds singing. I wasn’t in a hurry to go home. A bowl of ramen
and some public television? Thanks a lot. I watched the birds in the
tree; I’d been there for about four minutes. I think, so I turned to
leave.

But then I heard a bird singing in reverse, and I stopped. I looked at
the tree where the birds were; they were all looking at me and singing
as usual.

Later, at home, I was eating a bowl of ramen. I remember a moment
clearly. Why did that song sound like a whistle?

—

The next day. I had returned to that tree I had looked at the night
before, of all the birds I’d seen yesterday. There were only two left,
and I heard that song again.

I turned to look, but there was nothing there. Without a doubt, it
sounded like a whistle.

But where was it coming from? There was no one nearby, and none of the
birds in the trees had sung. I put two fingers to my mouth to whistle.

I imitated a bird’s song, silence.

The birds that had been asleep looked at me, and a passerby in the
distance looked at me. I’m standing in front of a tree, on Sunday
morning.

Despite that, I heard that song again, but it was fainter.

—

I was going to keep whistling. But I stopped; people were already
starting to come out of their houses. And I’d look like a crazy person,
leaving that strange tree alone.

I was cooking at home, a cigarette in my mouth, and my Spotify was
blasting. I made it obvious with the volume at full volume. “Should I
tell the police?” I shook my head. “Hello, police. Could you come and
check on a strange noise?”

“It’s probably stupid. Maybe I recorded it and showed it to my friend.”
I decided to turn on the news. The weather reporter was saying,
“Blizzard in Pierce County. I guess I’ll look into that bird tomorrow.”

I stubbed out my cigarette in the ashtray, turned down the gas on the
stove, grabbed my lunch, and went to the living room.

—

The day after the storm, my boots were swallowed up to my ankles by the
snow. I had bundled up.

I walked until I reached the tree again. Now there were no birds, but
there were police officers. Putting up crime scene tape, I approached to
ask, “What’s going on?”

“And who are you?” the officer asked. I showed him my driver’s license,
explaining that I lived in this neighborhood.

“You see, we found a man’s body here. He died of hypothermia,” he told
me, adding more details, but obviously not showing me the body.

A homeless man, about 45 years old, beaten and with his limbs wrapped in
tape. He’d been there for three days, weakened, but…

He kept calling for help. He was whistling.
