I’ve been reading tarot professionally for years.

It started as a hobby. Then it became a side hustle. Eventually it
turned into my full-time job.

I genuinely fell in love with tarot—not because I thought it could
predict lottery numbers or tell people exactly when they’d meet their
soulmate, but because it helped people process things they couldn’t say
out loud. Sometimes the cards gave clarity. Sometimes they just gave
comfort. Either way, people kept coming back. I connected a lot with my
deck, and ended up with a bunch of them. After years of reading tarot
almost every day, it started to feel… repetitive. Don’t get me wrong, I
still loved helping people. But after your fifteenth reading about
whether someone’s ex is coming back, or your tenth “Will I get the job?”
of the week, you start feeling like customer service with prettier
cards. I wasn’t burned out exactly. I was just bored. Whenever I got
bored, I bought crystals. It became a bit of a problem.

My house looked less like a home and more like a crystal shop that had
exploded. There were giant amethyst clusters by the front door, selenite
sticks above every doorway, black tourmaline tucked into every corner,
bowls of tumbles on every table, towers lined up on shelves, carvings
everywhere. Dragons, skulls, mushrooms, moons… if someone made it out of
stone, chances were I owned one. Friends would come over and laugh,
asking if I was opening a museum. I’d laugh too. They weren’t entirely
wrong.

One night, after finishing my last tarot reading, I threw myself onto
the couch and mindlessly scrolled through my phone, wondering what I
could learn next. Tarot suddenly felt too familiar. I wanted something…
different. That’s when my phone buzzed. An unknown number. No profile
picture. No introduction. Just one message. Do you do scrying? The word
lingered on my screen longer than it should have. Familiar enough that
I’d heard it before, unfamiliar enough that I couldn’t remember the last
time I’d actually thought about it.

Scrying.

For years, I’d buried myself in tarot. Seventy-eight cards, structured
meanings, familiar rituals. I knew exactly where the Fool stood, exactly
what the Tower meant, exactly how to interpret a spread when the cards
started talking to each other. Scrying was different. There were no
cards to hide behind. Just you. And something staring back.I don’t know
why that thought excited me. Maybe because I’d grown numb. Maybe because
I wanted something new. Or maybe because, deep down, I missed being
afraid of the things I believed in. Before I could talk myself out of
it, I opened the chat with my crystal supplier.

Do you have a scrying mirror?

The room had gone strangely quiet. The soft hum of my air conditioner
suddenly seemed louder than usual. Somewhere in the hallway, one of my
hanging crystal prisms gave a tiny metallic clink, even though none of
the windows were open. I found myself listening. Waiting. My phone
buzzed.

Black obsidian mirror? Yup! We have those.

I read the message twice.

Black obsidian.

I already owned enough obsidian to build a small wall. Palm stones.
Towers. Raw chunks. Arrowheads. Even a carved raven that sat on my
bookshelf. But somehow… never a mirror. It felt like I’d been collecting
pieces of a puzzle without realizing one was missing. I should’ve
ignored that feeling. Instead, I typed back almost immediately. “Okay.
I’ll come pick it up tonight after my last session.” The message was
delivered instantly. A few seconds later, the supplier reacted with a
simple 👍. That should’ve been the end of the conversation. Instead, I
sat there, staring at my own reflection on the black screen of my phone.
For just a moment… I couldn’t shake the feeling that something had
quietly noticed I’d made up my mind.

My last reading ended around 10:30 that night. I was exhausted. It
wasn’t until I locked my tarot decks away that I realized I hadn’t eaten
since my first client that morning. Eight readings back to back. My
stomach felt hollow enough to ache, but by then I was too committed to
turn around.

I grabbed my keys and drove across town. My crystal supplier lived about
thirty minutes away in a quiet neighborhood where every house looked
almost identical except for hers.

She always had candles burning in the windows.The front porch smelled
faintly of jasmine and sandalwood, and there were plants everywhere,
climbing the walls, hanging from the ceiling, spilling across the steps.
The place never looked neglected. Just… lived in.

I’d known her for years. She was probably in her mid-fifties. One of
those women who never outright called herself a witch, but somehow knew
exactly what crystal you needed before you asked, remembered every full
moon, and always seemed unsurprised when impossible things happened. She
greeted me with the same warm smile she always wore. “I’ve got something
for you.” She disappeared into the back room and returned carrying a
cloth-wrapped bundle.

Carefully, she unfolded the fabric. The obsidian mirror rested in her
hands. It was smaller than I expected. Maybe the size of a dinner plate.
The front was polished so perfectly that it barely reflected light at
all. It was like staring into a puddle of black ink. The back was
completely different. Raw. Jagged. The obsidian hadn’t been polished,
leaving sharp volcanic ridges running across its surface. I reached out
instinctively, then hesitated. “It’s beautiful,” I said. “But… why leave
the back like this?” I turned it over carefully. “The edges are really
sharp.” She lit another candle before answering. Its flame danced across
the mirror’s face, but somehow the glass never seemed to catch the
light. “Because that’s how it wanted to stay.” I laughed. “No,
seriously.” She looked at me for a moment. “I am.” Silence settled
between us. Then her expression softened into another smile. “The
polished side is for seeing.” She gently brushed her fingers across the
rough volcanic stone on the back. “The unpolished side remembers where
it came from.”

I wasn’t entirely sure what that meant. Maybe it was just one of those
spiritual sayings she’d collected over the years. I nodded anyway. As I
lifted the mirror from her hands, something sharp caught the side of my
thumb. “Ow.” A tiny sting. I looked down. A thin line of blood welled
from the cut before slowly running across my skin. “Oh, shoot.” I
instinctively tilted the mirror away so I wouldn’t get blood on it. I
was too late. One crimson drop landed in the center of the black
surface. It spread unnaturally slowly, almost refusing to roll. The old
woman didn’t say anything. She simply watched it. For a second… I had
the strange feeling she was waiting for something. Then she quietly
handed me a cloth. “You should wipe that off before you get home.” I
smiled, thanked her, and cleaned the mirror without giving the moment
another thought. Looking back… I don’t think she was worried about
staining the obsidian.

The mirror stayed in the corner of my reading room for almost a week. I
never used it. I couldn’t explain why. Every time I reached for it,
something in my gut told me to leave it alone. So I did. Business
carried on as usual. Clients came and went. Love readings. Career
readings. Family drama. The same questions, different faces. The only
thing that had changed was the mirror. Sometimes, before I even finished
shuffling my tarot deck, I’d catch movement in the corner of my eye. A
card would already be lying face-up in its reflection. Not on my table.
Only inside the mirror. When I finally drew the first card… It was
always the same one I’d already seen. Every. Single. Time.

I stopped looking at it after a while. Pretending not to notice felt
easier than trying to explain it. Then he walked in. He couldn’t have
been older than sixteen or seventeen.

Baseball cap backward. School uniform half untucked. Three of his
friends followed behind him, laughing before they’d even sat down. One
of them was recording on his phone. “Yo,” he grinned. “This is the tarot
girl I told you about.” Another pointed at the shelves behind me. “Dude…
she actually believes this stuff.”  The room erupted into laughter. I
smiled politely. “If you’re here to waste my time, I’ll have to ask you
to leave.” “Nah.” The boy dropped into the chair across from me. “I’ll
pay.” He slapped a few crumpled bills onto the table. “I’ve got one
question.” I sighed. “Go ahead.”

He leaned in with the smug confidence only teenage boys seem capable of.
“When am I gonna die?” His friends burst out laughing. One nearly
dropped his phone. I didn’t laugh. “I don’t answer questions about
death.” “Why not?” “Because I don’t believe anyone should know.”

He rolled his eyes. “So you can’t.” “I won’t.” “Same thing.” He leaned
back in his chair. “Knew this was fake.” I reached for the bills to hand
them back. “I think we’re done here.” That’s when every candle in the
room flickered. Not one. All of them. At exactly the same time. The
laughter stopped. The room suddenly felt… smaller. Like the walls had
quietly moved closer. I looked toward the mirror. It was facing the
table. No one had touched it. Its surface had gone darker somehow,
swallowing the candlelight instead of reflecting it. Then I saw it. A
tarot card. Face-down. Resting inside the reflection. I hadn’t drawn
anything. No one had. Very slowly… The card turned itself over. The
Death card. I froze. “Okay…” one of the boys laughed nervously. “That’s
actually kinda cool.” “No.” The word left my mouth before I could think.
“We’re done.” I stood up so quickly my chair scraped across the floor.
“You all need to leave.” The boy smirked. “What?” “Scared, did your
magic actually worked?” “I said leave.” He stood too. The smile
disappeared from his face. “Don’t tell me what to do.” “I mean it.”
“What are you gonna do?” He shoved the edge of my table. The tarot deck
scattered across the room. The mirror tipped sideways.I lunged for it.
He reached for it too. His fingers brushed the polished glass. For the
briefest second… He stopped moving. His eyes fixed on his own
reflection. His expression changed. Not fear. Recognition. Like he’d
just seen someone he knew standing behind him. “…Mom?” The word barely
escaped his lips. Then he blinked. The room was normal again. He looked
embarrassed. “What the hell…” 

His friends laughed, calling him an idiot. He pulled his hand away,
muttered a string of curses at me, and stormed out. The others followed.
One of them looked back before closing the door. “Damn…” “I think you
actually freaked him out.” I locked the door behind them. My hands
wouldn’t stop shaking. When I turned back toward the mirror… It
reflected an empty room.I was standing directly in front of it. But I
wasn’t there.
