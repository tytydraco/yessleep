The clock on the wall didn’t tick; it pulsed, a rhythmic, mechanical
heartbeat that seemed to count down the seconds of my sanity. I stood
over him, my hands still trembling, stained with the iron-rich scent of
a life extinguished. He was silent now, finally silent, after years of
his shouting echoing off these peeling wallpapers.

It wasn’t supposed to happen like this. The argument had escalated, as
it always did, into a blinding, white-hot rage that I couldn’t contain.
But then, the movement in the corner of the room caught my eye—a flash
of emerald eyes reflecting the dim hallway light.

It was Barnaby. My cat.

He sat perfectly still on the velvet armchair, his tail curled neatly
around his paws, watching me with a gaze that felt far too intelligent
for a creature of fur and instinct. He hadn’t hissed; he hadn’t bolted
for the door. He was simply observing, a silent witness to the carnage I
had wrought.

“Barnaby,” I whispered, my voice cracking under the weight of the
realization.

The cat didn’t blink. He just stared, his pupils dilated into obsidian
pits that seemed to swallow the dim light of the room. In that moment, I
felt a prickle of primal terror crawl up my spine. Animals are said to
be sensitive to the unseen, to the shifting of spirits, but Barnaby was
focused entirely on me. He wasn’t looking at the body; he was looking at
my hands, then up to my face, with a predatory curiosity that chilled my
blood.

I tried to move, to go to the kitchen to clean the evidence, but my feet
felt rooted to the floorboards. Barnaby shifted, his muscles bunching as
if preparing to pounce, but he remained stationary. He let out a low,
guttural trill—a sound I had never heard him make before. It wasn’t the
sound of a pet expecting dinner; it was the sound of a judge delivering
a verdict.

He began to groom himself, his rough tongue rasping against his paw with
terrifying indifference. It was a mundane gesture, yet it screamed
volumes. He was waiting. He knew what I had done, and he knew that I
would never be able to hide the truth from those unblinking, emerald
eyes.

I realized then that the silence in the house wasn’t the silence of
peace; it was the suffocating silence of an audience. And as I stared
back at him, I realized that Barnaby wasn’t just a witness; he was a
silent accomplice to the horror. He would stay here, in this house, long
after the police came, long after the tape was pulled across the door.
He would be the only one who truly knew the shape of my soul, and he
would never, ever forget.

The cat stood up, stretched his spine, and looked toward the hallway. He
turned his head back to me, waiting. It was time to finish what I had
started, and the only audience I had wouldn’t let me leave.
