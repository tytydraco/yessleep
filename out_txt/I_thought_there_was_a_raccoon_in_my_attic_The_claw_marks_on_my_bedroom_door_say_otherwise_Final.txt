After my wife told me that there had always been something living in the
attic, I left the house.

I didn’t pack, or grab a jacket, or even put on shoes. I just walked out
the front door in my socks and kept going.

I know that sounds dramatic, but I need you to understand what it felt
like to hear Sarah say those words. “There is always something in the
attic, Michael.”

Not, I believe you. Or, I saw it too.

It was like she was telling me the dishwasher made a weird noise.

Like the thing that had chased me down our hallway, stolen my phone,
clawed at our bedroom door, and screamed in someone else’s voice was
just another part of being married to her.

I walked for almost an hour.

I was furious.

I thought about all the nights I’d spent lying awake, listening to
something move above me while she slept beside me. I thought about the
food, missing clothes. The exterminator sitting in his van, too
frightened to tell me what he’d seen. The trapper staring blankly from
his rocking chair.

And I kept coming back to one question.

How long had my wife known there was something living above our heads?

Here I was standing in the middle of a sidewalk at ten o’clock at night
in my socks, no phone, no wallet, no plan, and the only thing I knew for
sure was that there was a monster in my attic and my wife had apparently
forgotten to mention it.

Eventually my anger ran out. It didn’t go away. More like transitioned
into fear. Because once I stopped thinking about Sarah, I thought about
the thing in the attic. And once I thought about the thing, I realized
something that made me turn around. Sarah was still in the house with
it.

The thing that had made itself very clear. It didn’t like me. I didn’t
know if it liked Sarah. So I went home.

The house looked normal when I got back. Porch light on. Curtains
closed. Everything quiet. No broken windows. No black fingers under the
door or screaming from the ceiling. Just our house.

When I opened the front door, Sarah was sitting on the couch. 

She hadn’t turned on the TV. She wore the same clothes as before. She
was just sitting there with her hands clasped in her lap, staring at the
hallway.

She looked up when I came in. Her face was pale and swollen from crying.

For a second I almost apologized. That made me angry again.

I asked her how long she’d known.

She sat there. Was she calculating the years?

Eventually she told me she was eight the first time she heard it. She
thought it was rats. Her parents thought it was rats. They called
someone. He said there were no rats. That sounded familiar.

Sarah said she spent most of her childhood trying to convince herself it
was normal. Old houses made noises, and branches scraped roofs. Pipes
knocked and animals found ways into walls. That was what people told
her, so that was what she told herself.

She said she never really saw it as a child. Not completely. Only parts.

Once, when she was twelve, she woke up because her closet door was open.
She always closed it before bed. That night it was open just enough for
her to see fingers wrapped around the inside edge. Long, black, thin.

That was the first time she understood that whatever was there wasn’t
just in the attic, it could come down.

She said she had gone years without seeing it. Years where she only
heard things sometimes. Years where missing food could be blamed on
forgetfulness or stress or bad sleep. By the time we met, she had
convinced herself it was either gone or never really existed the way she
remembered.

She told me about an apartment she lived in when she was in college. On
the second floor with another apartment above her. There was no attic,
or crawl space.

She heard it anyway.

At first she thought it was the upstairs neighbors. They thought it was
her. Both apartments complained about the other for weeks. Then one
night the woman upstairs knocked on Sarah’s door at three in the
morning. She was crying. She claimed she heard a child crawling. Inside
her floor.

Sarah moved out two days later. It followed her to the next place.

I didn’t ask any questions after that.

The thing Sarah had carried into our marriage wasn’t a secret in the
normal sense. It wasn’t a hidden bank account or an old boyfriend or
something she had done years before and regretted.

It was closer to an illness she had learned to ignore.

That didn’t make it okay, just sad.

The one thing Sarah could say for certain was that it had never acted
like this before. It had never targeted someone else, or been this
aggressive. It had never made itself this obvious.

That was what scared her most. She wasn’t calm because she understood
it. She was calm because she had spent most of her life surviving by not
reacting. Now even that wasn’t working.

We slept in the living room that night.

I say slept because I don’t know what else to call it. Sarah took the
couch. I sat in the chair with every light in the house on and a kitchen
knife on the small table beside me. It was a stupid weapon. I knew that.
But having nothing in my hand felt worse.

At some point near dawn, I woke up. I didn’t realize I’d fallen asleep.

My neck hurt. My mouth was dry. The house was gray and quiet.

Sarah was still on the couch. The knife was still beside me. And my
phone was on the coffee table.

Or what was left of it anyway.

The screen had been shattered into a spiderweb of black glass. The back
was cracked open. Tiny pieces of plastic and metal had been arranged
around it in a neat circle.

Not dropped. Arranged.

Sarah woke when she heard me stand. She sat up fast. Then she saw the
phone.

For a moment neither of us moved.

Something clicked above us. Once. Softly. Not in the attic. More like
inside the living room wall.

Sarah covered her mouth.

I picked up the knife.

The clicking came again. Closer this time. A dry little sound. Then it
stopped. I don’t know how long we stood there waiting. Five minutes?
Ten?

Eventually Sarah whispered that it didn’t want me calling anyone.

I looked at the broken phone. Then at the ceiling. I don’t think that
was quite right. It wasn’t just trying to keep me from calling anyone.
It was warning me not to try getting rid of it again.

That was the first morning I understood something I wish I still didn’t
know. Whatever lived above us was listening. And it had been listening
for a very long time.

For the next few days we settled into something that almost felt normal.
Normal, if your wife has admitted there’s something living above your
ceiling.

We didn’t talk about it much. There wasn’t much left to say.

Sarah didn’t know what it was. She didn’t know why it had followed her
all these years. She didn’t know why it had suddenly become so
aggressive.

The only thing we agreed on was that neither of us would stay in the
house alone again. If she had to leave, I would leave with her. If I had
to work, she came with me. It wasn’t practical. But neither was living
with a monster.

For a couple of nights nothing happened. No scratching. No footsteps
overhead. No clicking.

The silence made me anxious. I’d gotten so used to waiting for something
that the silence felt wrong.

Then, sometime around two in the morning, I woke to the sound of the
refrigerator door opening.

At first I assumed Sarah couldn’t sleep. I stayed in bed, expecting to
hear the refrigerator close. Instead I heard the pantry. Then silence.
Something about it didn’t feel right. I got up quietly and walked toward
the kitchen. 

Sarah stood barefoot, in her pajamas, in front of the open refrigerator.
She wasn’t looking inside it, she was staring straight ahead.

I whispered her name.

She didn’t react. Instead, she reached into the refrigerator, picked up
a package of deli meat, then grabbed a box of crackers from the counter.
Without saying a word, she turned and walked toward the hallway. Toward
the attic.

I followed her. She stopped beneath the attic panel. For a moment she
simply stood there looking up. Then she placed the food carefully on the
floor and stepped back.

She waited. At first nothing happened.

Then. Click. The soft, dry sound from somewhere above the ceiling.

Then she turned around and walked right past me. Her eyes never focused.
She climbed back into bed without another sound. She was feeding the
thing. That’s where the food had gone.

The next morning, to no surprise, the food was gone.

I waited until breakfast before I told her. She stared at me like I’d
lost my mind. Then she saw the empty spot on the counter where the
crackers had been. She didn’t remember getting out of bed, or opening
the refrigerator, or standing beneath the attic. She just kept saying
that she’d always wondered why food disappeared no matter where she’d
lived.

“I guess you can’t blame the ghost anymore,” I said.

Sarah looked at me. Neither of us smiled. We’d been making that joke
since we’d started dating. It wasn’t funny anymore.

We decided to not give the thing any more food. Or, at least give it
fewer opportunities to eat.

We bought less food. Left nothing sitting out overnight.

Sarah asked me to wake her every few hours just to make sure she was
still beside me.

The first three nights passed quietly. I woke to find her exactly where
she’d fallen asleep. I smiled. It was the first time I’d smiled in over
a week.

The knocking started that night.

Three slow taps from somewhere above the bedroom. Nothing else. A minute
later, three more. They were measured, patient.

Sarah heard them too. Neither of us spoke. Eventually they stopped.

The next day I almost convinced myself it was just the house settling.

That evening we made dinner together and watched television. We
pretended we were still the same couple we’d been two weeks earlier.
Then we went to bed.

The hallway light was already off. As we walked toward the bedroom Sarah
stopped so suddenly I nearly ran into her. She wasn’t looking at me. She
was looking up. The attic panel. It was moving. Sliding. That familiar
and hated scrape of wood against wood echoed through the hallway.

The opening was only a few inches wide when I saw them. Long black
fingers wrapped around the edge of the panel. Too many joints. Too many
knuckles. They curled around the wood as though whatever owned them was
pulling itself closer to watch us.

Neither of us screamed.

Sarah grabbed my hand. We ran. I slammed the bedroom door shut behind us
and locked it. Together we shoved the dresser against it until neither
of us could move it another inch.

Then we waited.

At first there was nothing. No footsteps. No scratching. I almost
thought we’d made it in time.

Then the doorknob turned. Slowly. Just enough to make the latch click.
Checking the lock. Once. Then again.

Sarah squeezed my hand so tightly it hurt. Neither of us spoke.

Minutes passed. The silence became unbearable.

Then I heard it.

Something very slowly dragging across the outside of the bedroom door.
Not clawing or pounding. Just, drawing a single line through the paint.

It stopped.

Then came that clicking. Soft. Patient. Like it was standing just
outside the door, listening. Eventually the clicking stopped too.

Everything went quiet.

I kept waiting for another sound. Another turn of the handle. Another
scrape across the door. But nothing.

We stayed in that room until sunlight started coming through the
curtains. Neither of us wanted to be the first person to open the door.
Looking back now, I don’t think we were hiding from it. If it had wanted
to get into that bedroom, I don’t think a locked door and a dresser
would have stopped it. I think it had exactly what it wanted. We were
the ones trapped inside the bedroom. It was free to go wherever it
wanted.

We didn’t leave the bedroom until the sun came up. Even then I opened
the door like I expected something to be waiting on the other side. The
hallway was empty. The attic panel was closed. The only sign that
anything had happened was the scratch running the length of the bedroom
door.

We spent the day out of the house as much as possible. We went to work.
Ate dinner out. Walked around stores neither of us needed anything from.

Neither of us wanted to admit it, but we had started treating our own
house like somewhere we had to return to instead of somewhere we wanted
to be.

Whenever we did go home, we stayed together. Always.

It didn’t stop the noises. Every now and then we’d hear something moving
overhead. Not constantly. Just enough to remind us it was still there.

Sometimes there would be a slow scrape somewhere inside the ceiling.
Sometimes a faint click from another room. Just enough to notice.

After a while I realized something. It wasn’t trying to scare us. It was
making sure we never forgot it was there.

A few days later I had to go back to the office. It wasn’t optional.

I offered for Sarah to come with me, but she had an appointment that
afternoon she couldn’t cancel. Neither of us liked it. Neither of us had
much choice.

She promised she’d leave the house as soon as she could and meet me
downtown.

Instead, my phone rang just after lunch. I answered before the first
ring had finished.

Sarah was crying. The kind of crying where someone is trying very hard
not to.

“Please come home,” was all she said.

I don’t remember the drive. I know I ran a few red lights. When I pulled
into the driveway, Sarah was sitting on the front steps. She wasn’t
crying anymore.

I sat beside her. For a long time neither of us said anything. Finally
she told me what had happened.

She’d been making lunch when she felt it. Not heard it. Felt it. Like
someone standing directly behind you.

She knew it was somewhere in the hallway. Every instinct she had told
her not to look. She looked anyway. Nothing. The hallway was empty. But
she knew it was there. Just around the corner. Waiting.

She called my name before remembering I wasn’t home. Then she started
telling it to leave us alone.

She said the whole house suddenly felt smaller. Like the air had become
heavier.

Then she smelled smoke. 

She’d forgotten about the pan she’d left on the stove. By the time she
reached it, smoke was already filling the room.

The smoke detector went off.

The sound exploded through the house.

Sarah said something immediately slammed into the hallway wall. Hard
enough to shake the pictures. Then she heard it scream. Not the sound
I’d heard before. It was pain. Real pain.

A second later she heard something crash somewhere above the ceiling.
Then nothing. Complete silence.

She turned off the stove, opened every window in the house, walked
outside, and called me.

That evening neither of us said much.

We sat in the living room replaying what had happened over and over
again.

Eventually I asked the question we’d both thought was too obvious. “What
if the smoke detector hurt it?”

Sarah didn’t answer right away. Then she quietly said she’d never heard
it scream like that before.

The next morning we drove to the hardware store and bought the biggest
air horn they had. While we were there, we picked up a flashlight, extra
batteries, a tent, sleeping bags, and a few other camping supplies.

On the drive home the air horn sat on the seat beside me. I kept looking
at it. It was ridiculous. A ten-dollar piece of plastic. But for the
first time since this started, it felt like we had something besides
hope.

That night I set it on the coffee table before either of us sat down.
Sarah noticed. She reached over and squeezed my hand. Neither of us said
anything. For the first time in days, the house felt almost quiet.

I should have known better.

It happened the following night.

There wasn’t any warning this time. No scratching or tapping or slow
scrape of the attic panel.

Sarah and I were sitting on the couch watching television, although
neither of us was really paying attention. The air horn sat on the table
beside us.

Then Sarah grabbed my hand. Hard.

I looked over.

She wasn’t watching the television anymore. She was staring into the
hallway behind me.

I turned my head to look.

At first I didn’t see anything. Then I heard it.

Click.

Not overhead. From somewhere in the hallway.

Another click. Closer.

Something moved. Not across the hallway but through it.

I stood up, glancing around. So did Sarah. Neither of us spoke.

The clicking stopped. For one awful second everything was completely
still.

Then I felt it.

The same feeling Sarah had tried to describe. The certainty that
something was standing only a few feet away. Watching. Waiting.

I turned around.

For just a moment I saw it. Not clearly. I don’t think my mind would let
me.

Coarse black hair clung to parts of its arms and shoulders, hanging in
uneven patches like wet animal fur. Hands that nearly touched the floor.
A body that looked less like flesh than darkness folded into the shape
of something alive.

Then it moved. Not toward me. Toward Sarah.

I grabbed the air horn. The blast filled the room. The creature recoiled
instantly. Its whole body folded backward with impossible speed.

Then it screamed.

The sound shook the windows. It wasn’t angry. It was hurt.

The darkness disappeared into the hallway.

A heartbeat later something huge was racing through the attic above us.
The entire house shook. Dust drifted from the ceiling.

Another crash. Then another.

A crack appeared in the drywall above the hallway.

Sarah’s face went white. “We can’t let it get out.”

Another impact. The crack spread across the ceiling.

Without saying another word I ran into the garage. I grabbed the old
aluminum stepladder I’d used the first time I climbed into the attic.

By the time I got back, Sarah had already dragged the dining room table
beneath the attic opening.

The attic panel shuddered. Something slammed against it from above. Hard
enough to shower us with drywall dust.

I hit the air horn again before we wrestled the table into position.

I opened the stepladder just enough to wedge it tightly between the
tabletop and the ceiling, bracing it directly beneath the sliding attic
panel.

Another impact. The ladder jumped. The table groaned across the hardwood
floor.

“More,” Sarah shouted.

We shoved the couch against the table. Then chairs. Then the coffee
table. Anything heavy enough to slow it down.

Every blow from above rattled the pile. The ladder shrieked as something
enormous threw itself against the panel again.

Then the panel moved. Only an inch. Just enough for the long black to
force its way through the narrow gap. The fingers bent where fingers
shouldn’t bend. 

They reached downward, feeling through the air. Searching.

I hit the air horn again. The hand jerked back instantly. The scream
that followed was louder than before. This time it wasn’t one voice. It
was dozens.

Men. Women. Children.

Someone laughing. Someone sobbing. Someone begging for help.

The sounds overlapped until I couldn’t tell one from another. For a
second I heard my own voice. Then Sarah’s.

She stared at me. I knew she’d heard them too.

Another impact shook the house. The ceiling cracked again. The wood
around the attic opening began pulling away. The furniture lurched
several inches. It wasn’t going to hold.

Sarah looked at the ceiling. Then around the house. When she looked back
at me, I could see she’d made a decision.

“We can’t keep it here. I love this place, but it has to go.”

I knew exactly what she meant. Neither of us said the word. We didn’t
have to.

I ran to the garage and grabbed the gas can from beside the lawn mower.
Sarah started pulling blankets and cardboard boxes into the hallway. We
worked without speaking. There wasn’t time. I poured gasoline over the
pile beneath the attic opening. The smell filled the house. Above us the
pounding became frantic. The panel shifted again. The ladder bent. One
leg lifted off the table before slamming back down.

Sarah disappeared into the bedroom and came back carrying the only
things we couldn’t replace. Photo albums. Her grandmother’s necklace. A
small box of old family letters. Everything else stayed where it was.

The hand forced itself through the opening again. Farther this time. The
fingers stretched toward us. I hit the air horn one last time. The
scream that answered sounded almost human.

I struck the lighter.

For one second I couldn’t do it. I wish I could tell you I knew we were
burning a monster. The truth is, I didn’t. I knew it bled because I’d
seen its blood on the drill bit. Knowing it was a living thing made it
harder.

Plus, this was our home. Every memory we’d made together.

Sarah reached over and closed her hand around mine. “Please.”

I dropped the lighter.

The gasoline caught immediately. Flames rolled across the blankets and
cardboard with a low whoosh. Heat rushed down the hallway. The pounding
above us became violent. The whole ceiling shook.

As we ran toward the front door the screaming changed again. Not one
voice. Not dozens. It sounded like hundreds.

Crying. Laughing.

Some calling our names.

Sarah started to turn around. I pulled her outside.

By the time we reached the yard, smoke was pouring through the roof
vents. Neighbors were already coming out of their houses. Someone
shouted that they’d called 911.

The pounding continued for another minute or two. Then complete silence.

Sarah cried the whole time. Not because she thought we’d lost the house.
I don’t think that mattered anymore. It felt like she was saying goodbye
to something that had followed her for so long she couldn’t imagine life
without it. I don’t know whether that’s grief or relief. 

We watched as the fire trucks arrived. We watched as our house burned
down to nothing. They told us we would need to find a place to sleep. We
had already planned for that.

We drove until we found a campground nearly an hour away.

Neither of us wanted another house. Neither of us wanted another attic.
We wanted open sky. Nothing above us except stars.

Everything that mattered fit into the back of the car now. A few bags.
Some clothes. The photo albums. Her grandmother’s necklace. That was
about it. Funny how quickly a lifetime of possessions becomes
unimportant.

We pitched the tent in silence. For the first time in weeks there wasn’t
a sound above us. No footsteps. No scratching. No clicking. Just wind
moving through the trees.

I didn’t realize how much I’d missed ordinary silence until that night.

Sarah fell asleep before I did. I stayed awake looking through the mesh
roof of the tent.

I remember thinking that, whatever happened, at least there was nothing
over our heads anymore. I eventually drifted off.

Something woke me.

At first I thought it had been a dream. Then I heard it again.

Click.

Soft. Metallic.

Not above me. Outside. Near the car.

I held my breath.

Another click. This time it came from underneath the car.

Click.

Then silence.

I never looked. I don’t think I wanted to know what was making that
sound.

Today I got a new phone. That’s how I’m able to post this.

I wish I could tell you we finally got rid of whatever had been living
in our attic. That burning our house ended it. The truth is, I don’t
know.

All I know is that tonight we’re sleeping in the tent again.

And when the sun goes down tomorrow…I’m burning the car.
