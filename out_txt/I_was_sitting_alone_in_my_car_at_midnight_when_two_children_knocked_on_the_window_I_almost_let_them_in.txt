I don’t tell this story often.

Not because I don’t remember it. I remember every second of it. I
remember the particular orange of the gas station lights reflecting in
the wet asphalt. I remember the way the coffee cup felt warm in my hand
right before I looked up and saw them standing at the edge of the
parking lot.

I remember the cold that came before I understood why.

That’s the part nobody talks about. The cold. The way your body knows
something before your eyes have given it any reason to know. I have
since read every account I can find — and there are more than you’d
think — and every single one of them mentions it. The dread that arrives
before the cause. The animal part of you screaming to drive away before
you can even articulate what you’d be driving away from.

I didn’t drive away fast enough.

-–

It was October. Late. I’d been at my sister’s place and stayed too long,
the way you do when you don’t see each other enough. I pulled into a gas
station off the highway — I don’t remember the town, I’ve tried — filled
the tank, got coffee, got back in the car.

That’s when I noticed the two children standing at the far edge of the
lot.

A boy and a girl. Young — ten, maybe eleven. Standing completely still
in the space where the gas station light gave out and the dark began.
Their clothes were wrong for October. Light jackets. The kind you wear
in September when you’ve given up on summer but winter hasn’t arrived
yet.

I noticed them the way you notice something peripheral that your brain
flags before you know why.

Then the boy started walking toward me.

He knocked on the passenger window. Three times. Soft. Patient.

I felt the cold before I rolled down the window. I want to be clear
about that. I felt it before I had any reason to feel it. Before I could
see anything wrong. My stomach dropped the way it drops on a roller
coaster — that involuntary, physical plunge — and I had the sudden and
complete certainty that something was not right.

I did not roll down the window.

He smiled and spoke through the glass.

“We need a ride home. It’s not far. You can let us in.”

Not — could you give us a ride. Not — would you mind.

You can let us in.

I asked where their parents were.

“Our mom is home. We just need to get there. You have to let us in. We
can’t come in unless you say it’s okay.”

That last part. We can’t come in unless you say it’s okay.

I’ve thought about that line every day since.

I asked him to look at me. Or maybe he said look at me. I’ve tried to
remember which of us said it and I genuinely cannot. But I looked at
him.

His eyes were black.

Not dark brown. Not dark grey. Black. Completely and entirely black from
lid to lid — no white, no iris, no pupil, no reflection. Like two holes
in the surface of his face. Like looking at something that went back
very, very far and had nothing at the bottom.

I started the car. I don’t remember deciding to. My hand just moved.

He put both palms flat on the passenger window.

“You have to say yes. You HAVE to say yes.”

I reversed out of the space. His hands slid off the glass. No prints. No
smear. Nothing — as if they’d never touched it.

I drove. I didn’t look in the rearview for a long time. When I finally
did, the parking lot was empty. The gas station attendant was visible
inside, sitting at his counter, on his phone.

Completely alone. Undisturbed.

-–

I’ve spent a long time trying to explain what I saw that night. I’ve
found others who’ve seen the same thing — in different cities, different
years, different parking lots and porches and doorsteps. They all
describe the same eyes. The same cold before the contact. The same
instruction.

You have to let us in. You have to say yes.

One woman did say yes.

She let them into her house. She woke up fourteen hours later on her
kitchen floor with no memory of what happened between the moment they
crossed her threshold and the moment she opened her eyes on the cold
tile.

She was never able to explain the marks on the inside of her front door.

Long. Vertical. Parallel. Four lines dragged from handle height all the
way to the floor.

As if something had been clinging to it from the inside.

As if something had not wanted to leave.

-–

I’m not going to tell you what to do if you ever see them. I think you
already know.

Don’t say yes.

Don’t let them in.
