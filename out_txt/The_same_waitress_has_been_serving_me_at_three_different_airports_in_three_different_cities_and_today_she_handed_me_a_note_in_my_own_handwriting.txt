I fly a lot for work. Two or three trips a month, mostly domestic,
sometimes to Canada or Mexico. I have TSA PreCheck and I have Global
Entry and I know the layout of most major American airports the way
other people know the layout of their neighborhoods. I mention this so
you understand that the story I am about to tell you did not happen to a
person who does not know how airports work.

I am writing this from a hotel room in a city I did not plan to be in
tonight. My return flight is tomorrow. I do not know if I am going to
take it.

The first time I met her was in September at a restaurant inside an
airport I fly through often. It is one of those sit-down casual places
by Gate B24 that serves burgers and salads at airport prices. I had a
four-hour layover and I did not want to eat vending machine snacks. She
seated me. She was in her mid-fifties, dark hair pulled back, warm
smile. Her name tag said Diane. She took my order and remembered that I
asked for no onion on the burger without writing it down. I tipped her
thirty percent because good service at an airport is rare and I wanted
her to have a good day.

I flew home. I did not think about her again.

The second time was three weeks later. Different airport. Different
city. Different terminal. I was on the way to a client meeting and I had
a two-hour connection. I stopped at a sit-down restaurant that looked
exactly like the kind of place I would sit at, ordered a coffee, and
looked up to see Diane pouring water into my glass.

I want to describe her carefully. Same face. Same age. Same hairstyle.
Same warm smile. Same name tag that said Diane. Same slight rasp in her
voice that made you think she probably smoked in her twenties.
Everything I remembered from the first restaurant, in a woman standing
in front of me in a different restaurant in a different state, wearing
an apron with a different restaurant’s logo on it.

She said, “Back again already?”

She poured my water without asking. She remembered the no onion. She
asked how my client meeting was going to go. I had not told her about a
client meeting. I had barely spoken to her the first time and I had
definitely not mentioned my work.

I told myself I was misremembering the first waitress. I told myself
Diane was a common name and dark-haired women in their fifties who
worked in airport restaurants probably all started to blur together in
my memory. I ate my food. I tipped her thirty percent again because I
was rattled and I did not know what else to do with my hands. I got on
my flight. I told a coworker about it that evening and she laughed and
said it was a coincidence.

I flew again two weeks after that. Different city. Different airport.
Different terminal. I had convinced myself the previous encounter was a
mistake and I had almost stopped thinking about it. I stopped at a
bar-and-grill by Gate C15 and sat down.

She was already walking toward me before I picked up the menu.

She said, “You look tired today. Long week?”

I stared at her. She smiled the same warm smile. She was wearing a
different apron with a different restaurant’s logo on it. Her name tag
said Diane.

I asked her where she was from. She said Ohio. She asked me if I wanted
the usual, and I have never in my life had a usual at an airport
restaurant. I said yes because I was too frightened to say no. She
brought me a burger with no onion and a coffee, and she stood there for
a second with her hand on the back of my chair, and she said, “You
should really eat more when you travel. You are getting thinner.”

I paid the check with cash and I left without finishing the food.

I did not fly for six weeks. I made up reasons at work. I took video
calls. I told my manager I was recovering from a minor procedure. I
looked up Diane on every social media platform and every
airport-restaurant employee directory I could find, and I found nothing.
Nothing that matched. Not one of the restaurants she had served me at
listed a Diane in their staff, past or present. I called two of them.
Both said they had never had a Diane and they had never had a waitress
matching my description.

I ran out of excuses at work. I had to fly last Tuesday. I picked an
airport I had never flown through before. Small regional airport. Two
gates. A single restaurant.

I walked in.

She was behind the counter.

Her apron was different. Her name tag said Diane. She looked up at me
when I walked in and her smile got a little softer, a little sadder, and
she said, “I am glad you came.”

She sat me down. She did not bring me a menu. She poured me water and
coffee. She said, “I am going to bring you the burger. You need to eat.
But I want to give you something first.”

She reached into the pocket of her apron and pulled out a folded piece
of white paper. She placed it on the table in front of me. She said,
“You wrote this. I am just the one who has been carrying it. I have been
carrying it for a long time.”

She walked away.

I unfolded the paper.

The handwriting on the paper was mine. I know my own handwriting. I have
the same loops and the same slight left slant on my lowercase L’s that I
have had since I was twelve years old. The paper looked new. The ink was
black. The message was short. It said, “Do not fly on the fourteenth.
You will not survive it. I am sorry. I have been trying to reach you.”

I stared at the note for a long time.

I put the note in my pocket. I did not eat the burger. I stood up to
leave. She came back with the burger anyway and she set it down and she
looked at me with her warm sad smile and she said, “I know. I know. Just
eat a little. You have to take care of yourself. There is more of this
ahead.”

I could not speak. I walked out of the restaurant. I walked to my gate.
I got on the plane. When I landed I called my assistant and I had her
cancel every flight I have scheduled for the fourteenth of next month,
which is nine days from now. I told her I had a family emergency.

I am in a hotel room now. I have the note in front of me on the desk. I
have taken pictures of it from every angle. My handwriting has not moved
on the page. I compared it against a birthday card I wrote to my mother
three years ago and the letters are identical, down to the way I cross
my sevens.

The fourteenth is nine days away.

I have not booked a flight on the fourteenth. I have not booked a flight
anywhere near the fourteenth. But I have to fly home tomorrow, and I
have to fly again next week, and I have to fly the week after that,
because that is my job and that is my life and I cannot stop.

Every time I board a plane between now and the fourteenth, I am going to
be looking for her.

I have to ask something and I need to be honest about it because I do
not know what else to do. If any of you fly a lot, and any of you have
been served by a waitress named Diane in an airport restaurant, and she
was in her mid-fifties with dark hair pulled back and a warm sad smile,
I need you to tell me. I need to know how far back this goes.

I need to know how many of us she has been trying to reach.
