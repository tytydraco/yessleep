A few summers ago, I worked as a camp counselor. It was at a rustic
sleepaway camp tucked deep in Vermont’s Green Mountains. This camp had
no cell service, no internet, just nature and a bunch of middle
schoolers. I signed up mostly for the paycheck, to keep myself occupied
between school years, and the experience, but I ended up getting more
than I bargained for. The camp had been around since the ’60s, mostly
unchanged. The cabins were old but solid, and each one housed about a
dozen kids and two counselors. I was paired with, we’ll call him Jake, a
senior from a nearby college who’d worked the camp before and knew the
ropes. Jake was laid back but sharp, and he knew how to handle the kids
without being overbearing. We were assigned Cabin Seven, full of eleven
and twelve year old boys. Most of them were loud and competitive, but a
few were shy or homesick during the first week. Still, it was a pretty
good group overall. Our cabin was at the edge of the camp, close to the
woods and a short walk from the archery range. I liked being near the
tree line, it was peaceful, even a little eerie at night. My bunk had a
perfect view out the window to see the moonlight reflecting off the lake
in the distance; some nights I could get a glimpse of animals shadows
walking around and drinking out of the lake. Camp had its rhythms:
morning hikes, arts and crafts, canoe races, and your typical campfire
stories at night. The kids were always exhausted by lights out, which
left Jake and me a couple hours to ourselves, and time with the other
counselors sometimes. We were on rotation for nightly patrols, walking
the grounds between 10 and midnight to make sure everyone stayed in
their bunks. Nothing ever happened though. Most nights, the biggest
issue was kids telling scary stories and spooking the rest of their
cabinmates. But about two weeks into the season, I was on solo patrol
near the archery range, when I heard it, first I heard and felt the
silence in the forest. No sound of a mosquito flying, no animal
rummaging around in the distance, not even a gust of wind through the
trees. I then heard a soft whistling coming from the trees. Just four
notes. Not a tune I recognized, but slow and oddly rhythmic. It sounded
casual at first, like someone mindlessly passing time. It stopped me in
my tracks,and I waited, listening hard, but the forest was still. I
thought maybe it was Jake trying to screw with me. I even called out,
“Jake?”but got no answer. I took a few hesitant steps towards the tree
line, shining my flashlight into the woods, but saw nothing.. After a
few minutes of silence, I figured it was an animal or maybe my
imagination. It was late, I was tired, and sound travels weird in the
woods I told myself. I finished my rounds and went back to the cabin,
telling myself it was nothing.

The next night was quiet. So was the one after. But then, one day later,
I heard the whistle again, same four notes, same time of night, and from
the same general area near the archery range. This time, it repeated
twice. I stopped and waited, but it never came a third time. I felt that
weird stillness again, like the trees were watching. The silence was so
heavy and pushed on my eardrums like i was underwater too long. I walked
by to my cabin, very on edge. I again, didn’t mention it to Jake, not
yet at least, I figured he’d just laugh or say I needed more sleep. But
I couldn’t shake the sound or how the air just shifted when it would
happen. It wasn’t threatening exactly, but it stuck with me in a way I
couldn’t explain. I started keeping track of when and where I heard it,
always between 11 and midnight, always near the eastern edge of camp.
The pattern felt deliberate, designed even. After the fourth time,
that’s when I finally told Jake. He raised an eyebrow and jokingly said
“maybe a racoon learned how to whistle and is trying to get better at
it.” But he also seemed slightly on edge about it, as if hes been
through this before That week, I casually brought it up to a few of the
other counselors. Most of them hadn’t heard anything, or at least didn’t
admit to it. But one of the nature instructors, Mara, paused when I
described the whistle. She didn’t say much, just asked, “Four notes?” I
nodded. Her expression changed slightly, subtle, but enough to notice;
the type of face you make when you don’t want to believe something but
have no other choice to. She muttered something like, “Yeah, it’s
probably nothing,” and walked off. Later that night, I caught up with
Mara again by the fire pit, after most people had gone to bed. I asked
her if she’d heard the whistling before. She admitted that she had, last
summer, same tune, same spot, late at night. She said it always stopped
if she got too close or acknowledged it. “It feels old,” she said. “Not
dangerous, but like it’s always been here.” I asked if she’d ever seen
anyone, or anything. She shook her head. “That’s the weird part. It
never sounds like it’s trying to scare you. It just wants to be heard.”
That line stuck with me. The idea that something could be out there, not
angry, not trying to hurt anyone, but still present, wanting
acknowledgment. Just watching. From then on, the whistle returned about
once every three or four nights. It never got closer, never changed its
tune. Just those same four notes, floating out from the trees. Sometimes
it was so faint I had to hold my breath to catch it. Jake heard it once,
too, while we were both walking patrol. He was asking me about it, and
asked what it sounded like. As i began to hum the first note, the
whistle eerily synchronized with me. We stopped midstep and he
whispered, “That’s it, right?” I nodded. After that, we started taking
our patrols more seriously. Not in a panicked way, just more alert. We’d
scan the tree line more thoroughly, pause more often to listen. Jake
never said it, but I could tell he was spooked, even if just a little.
The forest didn’t feel dangerous, but it didn’t feel empty either. It
was like something lived there, just on the edge of our reality,
something we couldn’t understand. One of the camp kids, Will, asked me a
strange question during lunch one day. He wanted to know who was
whistling near the lake the night before. I kept my tone casual and
asked what he meant. He said he got up to use the bathroom and heard
someone whistling in the distance.. “I thought it was one of you guys,”
he said, then shrugged. “It thought it was kind of cool.” I told him it
was probably Jake or one of the older counselors on patrol, which
satisfied him. He didn’t seem scared, just curious. That was the only
time a camper mentioned it, and I made a point not to bring it up again.
None of the other kids mentioned anything about a whistle. No one ever
got mysteriously hurt, and no one saw anything strange. It was like
whatever was out there had chosen to stay just out of reach, out of
sight, and out of mind for most. The rest of the summer passed in a haze
of bug bites, campfire smoke, and ghost stories. The “Whistler”, as I
called it, stayed a background presence. A few of us counselors would
share glances when it happened, but we never talked about it in front of
the kids. Occasionally, we’d compare experiences and thoughts on it,
“Did you hear it last night?” or “It moved farther west this time.” But
no one ever saw who, or what, made the sound. Once, we tried to record
it. We left a phone in voice memo mode near the trail where we’d most
often heard it. The next morning, the recording was full of static and
wind noise, but no whistle. We tried again, but got the same result. It
was almost like it knew. We joked that maybe the thing didn’t want to be
documented. only heard by those in person. Toward the end of the season,
the whistling became less frequent, maybe only twice a week. The last
time I heard it, it was during our final week, just after a long
rainstorm. The forest was dripping wet, thick with the smell of earth
and pine. I was on my way to the firewood shed later that night, walking
alone past the archery range when the notes came again, soft and slow,
almost wistful. I didn’t stop walking this time. I just nodded slightly
in acknowledgment, just to let it know I heard it, and kept moving. I
told Jake later that night I heard it earlier, and I thought that might
be nearing the end. He didn’t ask how I knew, he just said, “Yeah, today
felt different.” We never really had a conversation about what we
thought it was. It wasn’t a ghost, not exactly. It didn’t fit into a
clean category. All we knew was that it was real, whatever it was, and
that it had let us be. After the campers went home, the staff stayed
behind for a couple of days to pack up and just relax together. The mood
was lighter. People laughed more, and the woods seemed less heavy. Maybe
it was just the relief of finishing a tough job. Or maybe it was the
thought of our whistler fading back into the trees. Either way, those
last nights were quiet. On our final night, a few of us sat around the
dying campfire, swapping stories. Someone asked about the creepiest
thing that had happened all summer. Jake and I exchanged a look but
didn’t say anything. I think everyone there thought it,but it felt wrong
to speak it aloud, to turn it into a campfire tale, we thought that
could be giving it too much attention. We just let the moment pass and
watched the flames burn low and the ashes kiss the night sky.

The next morning, I went down to the archery range one last time. I’m
not sure why, I just felt like I owed it a visit. The forest was still.
Peaceful. But as I turned to leave, I thought I heard something, just
for a moment. Four notes, faint and distant, barely a breath on the
wind. I didn’t stop walking or break pace. I didn’t call out. I just
kept going, back up the trail and toward the cabins. The sound didn’t
follow or linger in the air. And somehow, that felt like a goodbye. Like
whatever it was had said its piece. Once I was back home, I didn’t talk
about it for a while. I wasn’t sure how to explain it without sounding
like I’d lost it, or made it up. It wasn’t dramatic enough for a ghost
story. No one vanished. Nothing attacked. No one was traumatized. But it
was very real. Now and then, when I can’t sleep, I think about that
summer. About the stillness of the woods. The soft pressure of eyes from
the trees. And that haunting little whistle that always stopped the
moment you listened too hard. Some nights, I think I hear it again, just
at the edge of perception. But it never lasts, and I never whistle back.
I kept the journal I started that summer and found it recently. Just a
small notebook with scribbles about schedules, funny things the kids
said, and notes about the whistling. I never shared it with anyone,
never will, but I can’t bring myself to throw it out either. Every time
I leaf through it, I feel that old, heavy quiet settle in again, the one
that lives just beyond the tree line, beyond the shadows. There are no
answers in the notebook. Just moments. Observations. And always that
same four note description, repeated every few pages as if I was trying
to figure out a puzzle. Over time, I looked into local legends. I
figured maybe I’d find some reference to a whistling figure, an old
story passed down through the region. But I came up empty. There were
the usual ghost stories about drowned campers, angry woodsmen, and
cursed trails, but nothing that matched what I’d heard. The Whistler
wasn’t malicious, and that seemed to throw people off, almost like
people never wanted to admit they ever heard anything; and it wasn’t
enough for folklore. It was too subtle. I’ve thought a lot about why it
never crossed the tree line. Why it stayed hidden, always just out of
view. Maybe that was part of its nature. Maybe it wasn’t meant to be
seen, only sensed. Heard from a distance. A presence, not a figure. A
whisper, not a warning. I reconnected with one of the counselors
recently too, and they remembered the whistling too. He said he’d heard
it near the mess hall one night, but thought it was a senior counselor
playing a trick since he was new at the time. He even went looking one
time but found no one. We talked about it over drinks, both a little
uneasy. He later said to me, “It’s the one thing about that place that
doesn’t feel like a memory. It feels like something that has been there
long before us, is still there, and will always be there.” That comment
stuck with me. Some experiences fade with time, blur at the edges until
they don’t feel real anymore or that we cant fully grasp at.. But not
the Whistler. That sound has stayed exactly the same in my mind. Four
notes. Paused between each one, like it wanted to be sure you were
listening. I will never forget it. I’ve returned to the woods a few
times since then, though not as a camp counselor. It shut down a few
years after I left, probably for budget reasons or safety codes. I never
saw a formal announcement. It just stopped appearing on summer listings.
I guess part of me wasn’t surprised. That place always felt like it
existed slightly outside the normal world. The last time I visited the
area, I parked on a back road and hiked up near the old, now unmarked
trails. I found my way to the old camp; the cabins were boarded up, the
canoes stacked and dry rotting by the shed. The forest had crept in more
aggressively, shrubs climbing up porches, pine needles blanketing paths.
But it wasn’t sinister. Just… quiet. Heavier than I remembered, but
familiar. I didn’t stay long. I walked past Cabin Seven, brushed a hand
along the warped railing leaned against it, and paused near the fire
pit. No whistling that day. No strange feelings or lingering glances
from the trees. But the silence wasn’t empty, it was thick, like sound
had weight, and the forest was holding its breath again in anticipation
for something. Sometimes I wonder if the Whistler ever left. Or if it
just faded deeper into the woods once the people were gone. Maybe it
only shows itself when there’s enough noise to cut through. Enough life
and motion for it to press back against. Like something ancient brushing
fingertips along the edges of our world, curious but distant. There’s a
strange comfort in how consistent it was. Never aggressive. Never
chaotic. Just those notes, slow and exact. If it had tried to speak or
show itself, it would’ve become something else, something that demanded
a response. Instead, it waited. And maybe that’s why I can live with it
and accept it as almost normal. Friends sometimes ask about my camp
years. I tell the usual stories,canoe flips, prank wars, bad food. But I
never lead with the whistles. You can’t just drop something like that
into casual conversation. It sounds too unbelievable. And at the same
time, not dramatic enough to justify the way it just exists. I think
about the counselors who stayed at the camp longer than I did. Whether
they heard it, too. Whether they eventually stopped noticing. Or if they
became used to the rhythm of it, the way you might get used to a creaky
cabin door or a distant owl. Maybe it became just another sound of the
forest to them.

I’ve told the story to only a few people in full. Usually late at night,
usually after a few drinks. Not to scare them, just to explain
something. To share the feeling of being watched by something that never
quite stepped into the light. It’s never met with laughter. Just long
pauses. People always say the same thing: “That’s weird.” Then they go
quiet for a beat longer than usual. Part of me wants to go back alone
and stay a few nights. Not to prove anything,just to listen. To see if
it’s still there. But another part of me thinks I’ve already had my
turn. That whatever it was gave me what it wanted to give. And maybe I
don’t need to chase it further. Some things belong where they are and to
not be tested. There’s a line between curiosity and calling something
out, almost challenging it. I never crossed it. I never tried to find it
in the dark or push past the trees. I respected the space it kept, I
respected what it allowed me to here from the distance. Maybe that’s why
nothing bad ever happened. I never asked it to be more than it was and I
never tried to ignore it. I always gave it some nod of acknowledgment;
the kind you give your little sibling when they think they did something
cool at the playground. Sometimes I wonder what would’ve happened if I
had followed it. Just once. If I’d walked into the trees, try to follow
it like the bottom of a rainbow, and wait in the silence. Would I have
found someone? Something? Or would I have just stood there alone,
surrounded by nothing but quiet, and realized I’d misunderstood the
whole thing? The more I think about it too, the more I realize that’s
the thing, it wasn’t trying to lure anyone. It didn’t beg for attention.
It simply… existed. Played its notes when it felt like it. Then faded
again. I never felt threatened. Just unsettled. Maybe it’s some natural
phenomenon, wind through a specific group of trees, some acoustic trick.
Maybe there’s a perfectly logical explanation. But if there is, I
haven’t found it. And I haven’t tried that hard. Part of me likes not
knowing. I still go hiking a lot. Forests don’t scare me even with all
this on my mind, but I listen differently now. I pay more attention to
silences. To the moments where everything stops, not because of danger,
but because something else is listening, too. I’ve only heard the
whistle once since that summer. Not in Vermont. Somewhere else entirely.
But it was the same. I didn’t react. I didn’t freeze or call out. I just
stood there, heart pounding in disbelief, until it faded. Then I kept
walking. Not fast. Not scared. Just respectful of the familiar noise. I
don’t think it follows people. I think it shows up where it’s always
been, and if you happen to hear it, that’s on you. A coincidence, or
something stranger. Either way, it doesn’t chase. It doesn’t claim. It
lingers.I’ve come to see it less as a presence and more like a rhythm in
the land. Something buried deeper than our stories. The kind of thing
that doesn’t need belief to exist. It just waits. Whistles. Vanishes. If
you ever find yourself in a place where the crickets go quiet all at
once, and the air feels like it’s leaning in, listen closely. You might
hear it, too. Four slow notes, drifting just beyond the edge of sight.
If you do, don’t go looking. Don’t try to answer. Just listen, then move
on.Because that’s what it does. It whispers through the woods. Watches.
Reminds us we’re not alone, not really. Not in the way we like to think.
Not always. And if it ever returns, I’ll know. Not by sight. Not even by
sound. But by that feeling in my chest, the stillness. The recognition.
The hush that always comes just before the whistle.
