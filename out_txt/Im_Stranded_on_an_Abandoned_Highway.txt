My best friend abandoned me on a highway somewhere between nowhere and
noplace. We had been fighting for almost an hour by then, bringing up
shit we thought we’d long buried. He pulled over so suddenly the tires
spat gravel across the road, then just sat there gripping the steering
wheel and breathing while the turn signal clicked in the silence.
Finally he told me to get out. I honestly thought he was bluffing until
he leaned across, shoved my door open himself, threw my backpack at me,
and told me to find my own way out. Then he turned the way we came from
and drove off, leaving me stranded with no signal, no flashlight, and
kilometers upon kilometers of highway and forest.

Life has a way of isolating me at the worst possible moments.

Maybe it’s always just been me, but it sure is better assuming that the
universe holds a grudge against you, because at least then you know
there’s a reason behind the things that happen to you. When I was twelve
I got separated from my class during a school trip and spent an entire
night alone in the woods before a search team found me half asleep
beneath a fallen tree. At sixteen I’d managed to lock myself inside an
old mausoleum while screwing around in a cemetery with friends and had
to wait there until morning listening to rain hammer against the stone
roof. Elevators breaking, getting stuck in lockers, the list keeps on
going. Internally, I laugh it off everytime it’s over, because I’m like
a damn cockroach; I’ve been lucky enough to survive everything that’s
been thrown at me.

I don’t know if I’ll be able to get out of this one.

The flashlight on my phone barely helped see through the dark. Heavy
foliage blocked out the sky above, and the thick barks of the dense
forest were way too foreboding to even consider going in. I sat down,
trying to gather my thoughts as I assessed the situation.

The First Option; Keep walking the road. I had minimal supplies and a
phone with a half depleted battery. I wouldn’t last long and my body
would eventually tire out. The alternative was—

The Second Option; Sit my ass down and wait for any passing cars. Great
in theory, but the first problem is that there aren’t any passing cars.
This fuckass road is only technically a highway, because I think it’s
dead center in the middle of nowhere and I don’t know whether anyone
except me and my friend was actually coming through here. The second
problem is the woods. They made me feel exposed somehow. Every time I
turned my back to them I’d get the urge to look over my shoulder again.
It reminded me of the fear I’d felt as a kid.

The Third Option; Go into the forest. No. Just no.

The Fourth Option; Climb a tree. I used to do it all the time, and I
think I’ll still be able to. It’s always better to know more about your
surroundings, and I was hoping that I’d see a second road or the edge of
the forest, maybe making Option 3 a bit more viable.

I picked the final one. I looked for the healthiest tree I could find,
figuring it lowered my chances of falling from a dead branch. My heart
sank every time the bark peeled away under my grip, but I kept climbing,
grabbing one branch after another, putting my feet on the former and
pulling myself higher. I tried not to look down. Near the top I stayed
close to the trunk, moving carefully between the thinner branches since
those were always the weakest. I sat down in the thickest outstretch of
the trunk at the peak, balancing my weight evenly. Slowly, I pushed my
head through the leaves, making sure nothing poked me in the face.

What I saw completely eliminated the third option.

The forest stretched on for miles in every direction beneath the stars.
From up there I could barely make out the highway cutting through the
trees, winding farther and farther away until it disappeared completely
into the dark. No streetlights. No gas stations. No nothing.

I climbed down slowly, trying not to think too hard about how stranded I
actually was. Only after my feet touched the ground did it really hit
me. Exhausted, I lowered myself onto the grass beside the road. It
didn’t feel nearly as safe as the gravel, but I was too tired to give
any more fucks.

Alright. The forest was never an option, and staying on the road was
counterintuitive to my escape. I forced myself to wake up, rubbing my
eyes as I remembered I’d left my shoes at the base of the tree’s trunk.

They were gone.

Five fucking minutes I’d lain there, and the shoes were nowhere to be
seen. I looked high and low, but they were definitely gone. Tears welled
up but I pushed them back, along with the questions of how and what. I
just started jogging beside the roadway in the direction I thought my
friend had gone, ignoring the occasional stone jabbing into my ankle. I
concentrated on conserving my stamina so that I could run for longer.

Once the monotony of my sprint calmed me down, I started noticing a few
things.

There were no sounds. Woods are quieter at this time, sure, but never
dead silent. Usually there’s at least a few crickets out there. I could
hear nothing except my own footsteps. My grandfather used to work as a
ranger, and he told me all kinds of stories about the woods growing up.

But there’s one thing he said that stuck with me. “Kid, if you can’t
hear a thing in the woods, not even the birds, get the hell out. Animals
only go that quiet when they know something’s wrong.”

I think it was a few hours in that I saw the first vehicle. I ran
straight to it, not giving a fuck about my feet being scratched and torn
by the rough gravel.

You have no idea how it felt seeing that bike laying on the road, turned
on its side. If being lost in this godforsaken path felt like I was
drowning, then this was like someone pulled me out of the water for a
second to breathe.

You also have no idea of the disappointment I felt when I saw it was
wrecked. Completely unsalvageable. The thing looked like it’d been
crushed by an invisible hydraulic press. You can’t crash something like
that even if you tried. I’d noticed something was off about this place
before, sure, but I hadn’t been this weirded out even when I’d lost my
shoes to the fucking air.

Finding the crashed motorcycle also confirmed another thing. This was
not the direction my friend had driven off to, because I sure as hell
didn’t remember seeing it before. It presented me with quite the
conundrum. Turn back the way I’d come from, or keep going forward.

I chose the latter.

Now I know that you’re all probably screaming at me for doing that, but
there’s a good reason behind my decision. For context, my friend JV
(I’ll call him that for now) and I had been on this cross-country road
trip. We were stumbling our car through obscure forest roads that
narrowed down so much they felt more like a hiking path, twisting and
winding every step of the way. I wasn’t even sure I’d even be able to
find the intersection Google Maps had led us to for entering the the
highway in the first place.

I didn’t want to erase my progress either, so I cut my losses and
stepped back into the grass beside the highway. Cursing as I removed the
gravel wedged in my feet, I gradually began picking up my pace again. I
stopped paying attention after a while. My entire world narrowed down to
the stretch of road in front of me. Every now and then I’d shine my
phone, just to assure myself that I was alone. Not that the prospect of
that was much better anyways.

Then I saw the fork in the road.

I was exhausted, my stamina barely keeping up even after I slowed my jog
to a walk. I had to stop every few minutes because the simple act of
moving forward felt like it was draining the life out of me. At first I
didn’t even register what I was looking at. My brain just accepted it
automatically, just another road cutting off into the woods. It took me
a few seconds to realize that highways aren’t just supposed to randomly
split off. Not this one, atleast.

I collapsed onto the ground, panting as I gulped down water nonstop,
rationing be damned. I pulled out my phone and shone the flashlight over
the crossroads.

The first path looked exactly like the road before it. They split at a
central circle, perpendicular to each other and running off diagonally
into the woods.

The second path was fundamentally different. The trees were oak, along
with a few other species I couldn’t recognize. Hell, even I know
ecosystems don’t fucking work like that. Two different forests don’t
just grow next to each other. But that was probably the least freaky
thing I’d seen all day. Next to the entrance of the second path, I could
make out a sign. I stepped closer, inspecting it.

“APSENS — 150 KM”

I’m getting a little bit of a signal at the clearing in the crossroads.
Google won’t load, and YouTube doesn’t even try. Only Reddit barely
works, which is why I’m posting this. I don’t know a thing about this
Apsens place, but it’s the first direction that feels like it leads
anywhere. If anyone knows what this place is, or has seen something like
this, even if you have any survival tips, PLEASE tell me what I’m
supposed to do. Literally ANYTHING WILL DO.

I can hear my own heart beating, and it’s still too loud compared to
everything else. There’s no background noise to distract me from my
situation, no wind, no insects, nothing to soften the edges of it. Just
me filling up space that doesn’t seem like it was meant to have me or
anything in it. Believe me when I say the silence is deafening.

I’m trying not to think about it, but I’ll run out of supplies soon.
This highway isn’t normal, and I don’t know how much longer I can
survive here.
