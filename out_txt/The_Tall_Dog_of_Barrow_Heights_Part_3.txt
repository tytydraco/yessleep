I swallow, lifting the watch to my lips.

‘This is Inquisitor Jhune,’ I whisper. ‘Investigating Barrow Heights
Anomaly. Requesting ETA on Protocol 5. Say again, ETA on Protocol 5.’

ErR-OR

CrIt-IcAL FAiL-uRE

TeMP-oRAL INTerfEREn-cE DEtec—

It cuts off. 

Great.

So the Tall Dog’s lair is so far removed from base reality that not even
the watch can get a message through. Guess that explains why it didn’t
pick anything up when I arrived. 

I set my jaw. 

Seems the only way out is through, then. 

I press forward, cobwebs stretching across the passage like tripwires,
catching on my face, sticking to my jacket. The floor is thick with ash
that puffs up with every step. And up ahead, I find something nailed to
the brick wall. 

A drawing. 

Another one of Florence’s. I’d recognize her linework, her stick-figures
anywhere. Only this one is rendered entirely in black. The crayon
strokes are violent, tearing through the page at multiple points, the
only spot of color her pink, triangle dress.

I lean in for a closer look, my fingers brushing the page.

The world disintegrates.

Reality peels away. A two-dimensional space expands, wrapping around me,
ripping me out of the basement and into a six-year-old girl’s worst
nightmare. 

________________________________________

Barrow Heights. I’m standing outside it, a jagged mass barely
distinguishable from the black sky behind it. The windows are holes. The
front door is a mouth.

Florence’s stick figure jolts into motion all wrong. Frames are missing.
She’s in the alley, then she’s at the front door, then she’s inside, the
in-between ripped out like pages from a flip-book. Her father moves the
same way, leading the prostitute from into Barrow Heights while Florence
watches them from the shadows. 

Speech bubbles appear and dissolve. Some are empty. Some contain words
that have been scribbled over. It’s as if Florence started to write
something and then thought better of it. One bubble, floating near the
prostitute’s head, reads only:

we can go wherever you like 

I follow them down the dead-end stairwell. The animation lurches.
Mr. Hollis shoves the janitor’s locker aside. Removes bricks from the
wall. The prostitute crawls through. He follows after her, tugging the
locker back into position behind them.

Florence watches from the stairs.

I try to reach out, try to stop her from doing what I know comes next.
But I can’t. I’m just a spectator, a ghost in her memory. She tiptoes
down the steps after them, squeezes past the janitor’s locker and crawls
into the basement vent. 

And that’s when I notice the calendar on the wall with today’s date
circled in bright red:

JUNE 5th 1936

My gut twists.

This is it. The last day anyone saw Florence alive.

______________________________________________

The crayon lines hold for a moment longer, then buckle. Depth crashes
back in like a wave: the stink of mildew, the cold pressing against my
skin. I’m on my hands and knees in the corridor, breathing hard. The
drawing is crumpled beneath my fingers.

I leave the page and keep moving.

The corridor spits me out into a boiler room, ancient and rusted, pipes
snaking across the ceiling like exposed veins. At the far end, a furnace
growls behind its grating, casting everything in a red glow that makes
the shadows move whether they want to or not.

A door creaks open to my left.

I freeze.

Nothing comes out.

My gut says keep moving. Find the exit. Get topside before the Pales
arrive. But there’s a copper plaque above the door. And the name on it
belongs to the same man who started this nightmare, who damned an entire
building to a century of horror:

MR. FREDERICH HOLLIS 

I have to know. 

I push through, and the office inside is a tomb.

Cobwebs drape from the ceiling like funeral shrouds. The air smells of
rust and decay and something that’s been sitting undisturbed for
decades. And there, slumped over the desk like he fell asleep and never
woke up, is Frederich Hollis.

Or what’s left of him. 

His body has mummified, skin pulled taut, lips peeled back into a
permanent grimace. He’s still wearing his brown suit, bowler hat askew
atop his skull, hand resting atop a leatherbound ledger. 

Answers.

I want them.

I pry the book from his fingers. The binding cracks when I open it, the
spine stiff with age, and the smell that comes off the pages is sweet
and rotten, like fruit left in the sun. It’s a building log. Maintenance
records. Tenant complaints. But the further I read, the less it looks
like a log and the more it reads like a confession. 

______________________________________________

April 1933

Today was a great day!

We not only broke ground on the apartment’s foundations, but the doc
prescribed new medication for my heart. Swore it’ll prevent another
episode. Charlene, my beloved, was so relieved. The girls were too,
though Florence was still too young to understand what a “heart attack”
even meant. She just asked if it tasted like bananas. I couldn’t help
but laugh—she keeps me young. 

June 1933

A lunatic showed up at the construction site today. Claimed to be some
kind of shaman. A tribal elder. He told me this land is cursed, said his
people buried something here centuries ago. Something evil. A “dogman,”
whatever the hell that means.

I told him to get gone, but he was frantic. Kept insisting that if we
keep digging, we’ll unearth it. That there’ll be blood on my hands. I
had to call the cops. They carted him off quick enough - hopefully to an
asylum where he can get some help.

August 1933

One of the workers found something. A ceramic sphere, buried about
twenty feet down. He claimed he saw markings on it, some kind of tribal
drawing of a wolf eating a child, but the ink bled off into the dirt
before he could show anyone else. 

I told him to get back to work - and save the ghost stories for the
woods. 

December 1934

The basement is finished - ahead of schedule, no less. I gave the crew a
holiday bonus to celebrate. The week after, not a single one of those
ingrates showed up to the site. Skipped town. Abandoned their own
families, if you can believe it. I had to hire an entirely new crew, and
the delay has cost a fortune.

To top it off, my heart medication stopped working, too. Had to double
the dose just to get through the day. 

January 1935

The new crew doesn’t know about the basement. I’ve decided to keep it
that way. Truth is, I like having a space that’s just mine. Away from my
wife’s nagging. I’ve been spending more and more time down here, just me
and the furnace. 

I’ve left some bricks loose in the wall to get in and out. 

November 1935

Haven’t heard from the shaman since the cops dragged him away. Probably
in a straitjacket these days. He said this place was a burial site. A
grave for something ancient. I liked the idea so much I named the
building after it:

Barrow Heights.

Ha! Get it? 

______________________________________

I flip the page sharply. ‘You arrogant fool, Hollis.’

‘Mr. Jhune?’

I freeze.

That voice.

I know that voice.

‘Is that you, Mr. Jhune?’

I spin, flashlight sweeping the office. ‘Tyler? Where are you?’

’Up in my room.’ His voice is tinny, distant. ‘You told me to stay put.’

The pipes.

He’s speaking through the pipes.

‘Are you safe?’ I ask.

‘Yes.’

This could be the solution I’ve been searching for. Everything depends
on the Pales finding this basement, and that depends on them finding an
entrance that isn’t supposed to exist. 

Which means everything now depends on a ten-year-old boy.

‘I need you to do something for me,’ I say into the nearest pipe,
choosing my words the way I’d choose footholds on a cliff. ‘My friends
are on their way. They’ll be coming in a big, dark truck. When they get
here, I need you to give them a message for me, okay? I need you to tell
them that the basement entrance is at the bottom of the stairs.’

‘But the Landlady—’

‘We don’t need Agnes. There are loose bricks behind the janitor’s
locker. Tell my friends to pull them out to get through to the basement.
Can you remember that?’

‘Why can’t you?’ His voice splinters. ‘You’re still coming back… right?’

‘Yes—but only if you do this for me. It’s extremely important that my
friends get down here. Understand?’

Silence. 

‘Yes, sir.’

‘Good. Now get moving.’

I hear the creak of his door opening eight floors above. Then the fading
patter of his bare feet against carpet.

Good kid.

I turn back to the journal. There’s a lot of it left. Months of entries
between the building’s completion and whatever finally killed Hollis at
this desk. I skim forward, past complaints about his wife, past notes
about the whistling getting louder, past language that grows looser and
uglier with every page until my eyes catch on an entry that makes me
stop cold.

______________________________________________

July 1935

I brought the prostitute into the basement. Strangled her with the cord
from the work lamp. It took longer than I expected. She fought. I hadn’t
anticipated how much I’d enjoy the fighting.

Afterward, the reality of what I’d done landed. A dead woman. In my
building. The rot would start within days. The smell would climb the
pipes, seep through the floorboards. Someone would notice. 

But then the furnace coughed. Ash spat from behind the grating, and the
door swung open on its own, heat rolling across my face and I understood
then that I’d been chosen. That this basement was a temple, and I its
ordained keeper.

I’ve stopped taking the pills. 

A man sustained by God’s hand has no use for a pharmacist’s. 

November 1935

The pipes are whistling. It seems to come from everywhere at once… the
walls, the floor, the fillings in my teeth. I’ve torn apart half the
plumbing and found nothing. No blockage. No leaks.

And yet they sing. 

And the more I listen, the more I like the song. 

May 2nd, 1936

Florence followed me to the basement.

The damn girl saw everything. The dead whore. The bonesaw. She’ll ruin
everything if I let her go. 

May 2nd, 1936 (evening)

I’ve told Charlene that Florence and I are taking a camping trip
upstate. Charlene didn’t think it was wise for me to be in the
wilderness with a heart like mine. She’s right. 

It’s been murmuring again. Pounding.

Almost like it’s trying to outrun something I can’t see.

May 5th, 1936

Florence won’t speak to me. But she speaks to something else. 

I gave the brat crayons thinking it might calm her, might give her
something to fixate on besides the voices in her head. But every drawing
is the same: me, standing over a woman’s body. And beside us, a dog with
arms like dead branches. 

Smiling.

May 7th

I told her the truth today.

I told her that God’s patience has limits. That she could stop crying
and come home and never speak of what she’d seen, or she could rot here
forever.

She looked at me and said the dog had already decided what would happen.

That it had been deciding for a long time.

I’ll have to kill her after all. I’ll say the current took her while she
was swimming in the river. That I tried to save her but my heart

______________________________________________

The entry ends mid-sentence, the final words disintegrating as the pen
stroke drags off the edge of the page.

I look down at Frederich Hollis.

One mummified arm is slung across the desk, the fountain pen still
loosely cradled between his remaining fingers. The other hand is buried
beneath his collar, clawed against his sternum. 

His heart.

It must’ve given out. 

I run my thumb across the edge of the journal, and a fine layer of his
skin comes away like candle wax. His pen hand is missing most of its
fingers. They’ve been snapped off at the first knuckle. 

Something took them.

Something that needed new wax for its crayons.

I wipe my fingers on the lapel of his brown suit, trying not to think
about a creature hunched over a piece of paper in the dark with a
corpse-wax finger, teaching itself to draw by copying the artwork of a
six-year-old girl it let starve to death.

The whistling curls beneath the office door.

It’s for me. 

Taunting. Goading. 

I inch outside, flashlight catching the rusted husks of machines. My
hand goes to my hip on instinct, fingers finding the warped barrel, the
cracked grip of my revolver. 

Dead weight. 

The door ahead reads: STORAGE.

I push through.

The room beyond is full of wire-mesh cages from floor to ceiling. Tenant
lockers. Never used. The cages split my flashlight, each one throwing a
lattice of shadows across the next, the geometry multiplying until the
darkness ahead looks crosshatched.

I move forward. 

The path zigzags in a pattern that feels deliberate, as if funneling me
deeper into a maze. Agnes believed her sister was alive down here. Said
she’d even spoken to her. 

‘Florence?’ I hiss softly.

No answer.

I’m starting to think the storage room is a dead end when my light
catches a scrap of faded pink. Florence. Her dress. 

I break into a jog, the beam bouncing. 

Then stagger against a cage. 

There she is, sitting against the wall. 

Her pink dress has collapsed into a rumpled nest around a frame too
small to belong to anyone over seven. Her skull rests in the cradle of
her lap. It’s as though she fell asleep leaning against the bars and
gravity had done the rest. 

Whatever Agnes heard speaking to her, it wasn’t Florence. 

I crouch, hand against the cage. ‘I’m sorry, kid.’

It’s all I can offer. 

Scattered like fallen leaves around her are drawings. Dozens. The paper
has yellowed, the edges curling inward. It’s the same trembling style
from the stairwell. And in every one, looming behind a pipe or a boiler
is the same shape. 

Tall. 

Hunched.  

My stomach twists, and I wonder if I even want to see how this ends. If
I can bear it. But I still need more data on the Tall Dog, more data to
end this nightmare for once and for all. 

So I reach out. Touch a page that’s been placed apart from the others,
face-down, as though Florence herself couldn’t bear to look at it.

And the basement lurches.

The wire. The bones. All of it falls away.

And something terrifying rises in its place.
