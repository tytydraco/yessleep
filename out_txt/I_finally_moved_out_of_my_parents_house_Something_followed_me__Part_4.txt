Part 1 | Part 2 | Part 3

I don’t remember if I slept that night.

I might have dozed off at some point, waking up with my cheek against a
stack of papers and my back hurting, but that was it.

I mostly spent the time going over the reports again and again. They
seemed to know me better than I knew myself.

Every time I was close to remembering something, it slipped away
inexplicably.

It really felt like they were doing it on purpose, teasing me. They had
let me enjoy the smell, but it wasn’t time yet for the meal.

Leo and I, soaked through, standing side by side and laughing like
idiots, two mischievous children going on the adventure of a lifetime.

Willingly disrespecting our parents’ rules, because that’s what made it
exciting.

I could still feel it: the tree house. All around me.

The apartment seemed to be getting smaller and smaller the more I
imagined it, but I didn’t force it.

I let it surround me a little, gave in to it, as if it were natural.

Still entrenched in that state between sleep and wake, I could sense the
rain pouring outside and the smell of the wet wood.

With my eyes closed I could see it, not clearly, but close enough.
Closer than I’d gotten in the last sixteen years, that’s for sure.

Just the two of us, two shadows standing next to each other. The hatch
between us. The dark infinity below. Until there was only one left.

But every time I got too close, it all went away.

I had survived another night, and so had the truth.

The morning light blinded me once more. I don’t know why, but I’d
expected things to be different. I’d expected another day never to come,
expected to remain stuck in an endless night, forever tormented by my
guilt.

But that wasn’t the case.

I got up reluctantly and first went to check the lock at the door. It
had become my ritual and one of those days it would’ve given me the
answers I needed.

I walked from the door to the kitchen, to the bedroom, and back. Not out
of routine, but because at that point I had no idea what to do anymore.

Every time I walked past the door I checked the lock again and looked
through the peephole. At times when I felt braver, I would even unlock
the door and open it. Check the mat and the carpet, only to close it
again and resume my never-ending walk.

And that’s when I noticed it.

I had almost forgotten all about it, the previous night. The flashlight
I was sure I’d left in the kitchen drawer was there now.

On the kitchen counter next to the bag of dirt and bark I had collected
the night before. It felt like it was welcoming me back to a place I
hadn’t visited in a long time.

For a second I even thought that it was I who had moved it. Maybe in one
of my nocturnal walks, tired of not knowing where it was hiding.

But I was just lying to myself. By that point, the idea that the
flashlight could move on its own felt like the most reasonable
explanation.

Yet it still didn’t feel right, because as much as I tried to ignore it,
I couldn’t help but notice that, between it and the bag, there was yet
another leaf.

I couldn’t even tell if it had decided to leave the bag on its way, or
if it was another gift from my old friend.

I didn’t really care at that point.

I spent most of that day trying to act normal.

I showered and made coffee. Opened the windows for ten minutes, then
shut them because I couldn’t distinguish the smell coming from outside
and the smell from inside, and that bothered me too much.

I tried eating a half-burnt toast, but threw half of it away because it
felt like eating tree bark.

By that point every room in the apartment seemed to have shrunk in size.
Everywhere I went, it seemed like big chunks of it were missing.

I felt trapped. I had pretended not to notice until now, but everything
was bringing me back. Back to the tree house, back to that night, back
to something I had spent years not looking at directly.

The whole apartment had started to resemble it. And not only in feeling.

Every time I blinked I could see wooden planks replacing the floor,
while the walls had started growing moss and vines. The whole of it felt
smaller. Too small.

Every time I took a step, I would hear it creak like an old board.

A draft under the door would feel like night air from outside the yard.

Shadows from traffic outside would move across the wall and for a second
my brain would turn them into branches.

And once I noticed it, it wouldn’t stop.

Around noon my phone rang. It was my father this time.

Usually I would ignore a couple of their calls, but lately I had this
feeling of wanting to talk to my parents more.

As if they would just give up and just spill it.

Every answer that I was looking for.

He didn’t say hello.

“Your mother told me what you found.”

I looked at the bag of dirt and bark still sitting on the counter.

“Did she?”

“She said you found some debris in your apartment.”

Hearing it described like that made me angry.

“Is that what we’re calling it? Debris?”

He sighed. “I’m not doing this with you if you’re going to start like
that.”

“Start like what? Like I found wet bark inside my apartment after
something talked to me through the door?”

“It was not Leo’s voice.”

At that point, I didn’t know if he was in denial. If he was trying to
convince me of it or himself.

But at that point it didn’t really matter. I had hoped that the truth
would’ve eventually come out from them, but after sixteen years, I don’t
know why I still believed it.

I didn’t answer right away. I wanted those words to linger in the air
for a little bit more, not for me, but for him.

As if, once he had realized what he’d said, he would take them back or
would at least bring him back to his senses.

Because why even bother correcting me? What if it had not actually been
Leo’s voice? What would’ve changed.

There still would’ve been the weirdly specific sentence, the flashlight,
the leaves, the bark. Plenty of proof that what was happening to me
wasn’t normal. That I at least had the right to be heard and understood
and comforted.

I wanted to scream it to them. I had wanted it for a long time. What was
happening right now was just the culmination of years of being ignored
and being made to feel small.

“How can you be sure? You weren’t there.” I asked.

Nothing.

“Dad.”

That wasn’t fair of me. God only knows how much he regretted not being
there the night Leo died. And the thought of losing his other son while
being completely helpless to stop it was probably eating him from the
inside.

I might have been too harsh on him, on them. I realize it now. I wanted
to blame the lack of sleep, all the weird circumstances I’d been living,
but in the end, the truth was, that I knew that I was the one at fault,
for everything that had happened.

“You need to stop reading those papers.”

It startled me. I was so lost in thought, I had forgotten he was still
on the line.

“You do not get to tell me that now.”

“I’m serious.”

“So am I.”

I heard him breathe through the phone. And when he spoke again, he
sounded tired, in a way I hadn’t heard before.

“Some things were buried for a reason,” he said.

“Because they make me look bad?”

“No.”

“Because they make you look bad?”

“No.”

“Then why?”

“Because they are meant to stay buried.”

I didn’t know how to answer that.

“Have you slept?”

“What do you think?”

“You need to come home.”

“No.”

“It would be better if you were here.”

He was right.

It seemed like everything had started going south when I left their
house. Maybe going back there was the solution I was looking for.

But I didn’t want to let them have it their way.

When I was younger, putting me in my place had always been their answer.

It didn’t matter if I had gotten upset by something a kid did at school,
or scared by the figure in the dark, or if I was too fixated on
something.

They never tried to comfort me or anything, just repeat those rules ad
nauseam, until I gave in.

“I’m not a kid anymore,” I said.

“No,” he said. “That’s part of the problem.”

Then he hung up.

I felt defeated.

I spent the next hour walking in circles around the apartment replaying
that call in my head.

It was not Leo’s voice.

Some things were buried for a reason.

That’s part of the problem.

At some point I realized I was pacing the same loop over and over, just
as I had all morning. Kitchen. Front door. Living room. Hall. Bedroom.
Back again.

Same route. Same turns.

This time it hit me all at once what it felt like.

A route that felt way too familiar.

The apartment no longer felt like one, but more like a small raised box
with only one way out. I stopped walking after that, but it didn’t
really help, the sensation wouldn’t go away.

The whole place had started to feel wrong in a way I couldn’t stop
noticing and things only got worse later that afternoon, when somebody
knocked on the wall.

No, not on my door like a couple of nights before. But on the living
room door.

Just three soft knocks. Quiet enough to be interpreted as the auditory
hallucinations of a madman, but so loud as to freeze me in place like a
deer in headlights.

The sound came again, same cadence, same pause as to release me from my
momentary immobility, like a stupid game of green light, red light.

I moved sluggishly towards the wall and pressed my ear against it. I
must have looked so stupid in that moment.

A second later I heard Mrs. Werner’s muffled voice from next door,
talking to someone in the hall.

I breathed a sigh of relief, sitting on the sofa as I held my head in my
hands.

A weight lifted from my chest, as if all my problems had disappeared.

Something had finally started to make sense, and as tiny as a simple
knock from the hallway was, I couldn’t help but imagine that all the
other problems of the previous nights, all the fears, anxieties, and
worries could reach the same result.

I hadn’t felt relief in weeks, but that didn’t last long. After a couple
of seconds, another knocking, this time much louder, more discontinuous,
and heavy, brought me back to reality. It was different from what I was
used to, and I noticed it immediately: this knocking was human. I didn’t
even turn to the wall, I knew it wasn’t coming from there and I walked
towards the apartment door.

Mrs. Werner was standing there in her slippers, wrapped in the purple
cardigan I’d seen her wear almost every day. She gave me a grim greeting
before I could even utter a word.

“I’m sorry to bother you,” she said. “I just wanted to ask if everything
was all right.”

“Why?”

She frowned slightly as if the answer to my question was obvious, and I
was simply playing dumb.

“I heard you talking, you know.”

“When?”

“Last night. And a bit this afternoon.”

I felt a pang in my stomach. I’d spoken to my parents that morning, yes.
But the night before? And the afternoon? I didn’t remember doing so. For
a moment, I thought maybe she was mistaken; she’d simply heard me
yelling at my parents and misinterpreted what time of day it was.

Something told me I was the one who was wrong. But if that was true,
when had I been talking? And to whom? I’d walked a lot that afternoon,
pacing the apartment perhaps a hundred times, and perhaps I’d uttered a
few words aloud without realizing it during my constant brooding, but
certainly not loud enough for my neighbor to hear, nor so often or for
so long that she could have misinterpreted it due to the long
conversations she now accused me of.

“I live alone,” was all I could say.

“That’s what I thought.”

Those words made me uneasy.

“What exactly did you hear?”

She hesitated.

“At first I thought maybe you had a television on. I heard something
that sounded like a child’s voice, got curious, and looked through the
peephole in the door.”

I swallowed. My hand tightened on the door, as if it might slip away at
any moment and leave me alone to experience this moment.

“I saw you standing by the door,” she went on. “I thought you were on
the phone.”

“Was I saying anything?”

She seemed uncomfortable continuing, as if she wasn’t sure what effect
her next words would have on me, and whether they were worth saying.

After a couple of seconds, she gave up, perhaps having finally made
peace with her senses.

“You kept saying, ‘Don’t go outside.’”

Those words shook me more than I’d like to admit. Not because I didn’t
remember saying them, but because they seemed to have come straight from
my mouth, as if I were certain that the phrase was mine. As if it had
belonged to me, for over a decade now.

I don’t remember how my conversation with Mrs. Werner ended; at some
point, I’m sure I closed the door and began staggering back to my room.

My neighbor had probably protested this, a little indignant, a little
concerned about my behavior, but honestly, I didn’t care anymore.

Don’t go outside.

That sentence echoed in my head, like a mosquito buzzing on a sleepless
summer night. Could I have actually said it? And to whom?

I had to lie down. I couldn’t stand anymore.

And all the while, I continued to rack my brains and try to remember.

It didn’t come all at once, as expected. But slowly, an image began to
form in my head, no. It was already there. But what until a few seconds
before was simply a shapeless shadow lost in the depths of my
subconscious was now growing larger and encompassing everything else.

The image of a rainy summer night in an all-too-familiar setting. My own
voice, much more fragile and younger, repeated that phrase ad nauseam.

Before I knew it, I was back on my feet and crossing my apartment, with
a quick and decisive step, but with shaking knees. I was rushing toward
the front door, the old wood creaking under my every step, as if the
same boards were no longer accustomed to being walked on.

I think at a certain point I realized I was moving, but I didn’t even
try to stop, because I knew it wasn’t what he wanted, that it wasn’t
what I wanted.

On the other side of the door, I began to hear noises, faint at first,
but they grew louder with every step I took until I recognized them:
someone was knocking on my door.

I didn’t want to run anymore; this time I would face my fears head on.
Had I been wiser, things would have turned out differently. I would have
taken a different route, maybe I would have checked the peephole first
to see what was waiting for me on the other side of the door. If that
thing wanted me so badly, well, why keep it waiting any longer?

As much as I had pretended up until that point that I didn’t know what
was waiting for me, I could no longer lie to myself.

The sound of rain, the creak of boards under my feet, the smell of wet
leaves and wood, my own sobbing voice, and my brother’s faint words on
the other side. I wasn’t in my apartment anymore.

I had spent my whole life trying to forget that night, and now it was
waiting for me on the other side of the door.

The time had finally come to meet an old friend.
