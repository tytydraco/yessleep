I sat in the kitchen, paralyzed by the wet slap-thud echoing through the
plastic speaker. My daughter, Lily, was three months old, and my husband
was working the night shift at the mill. It was just us, and whatever
was currently ascending the stairs.

​I grabbed a steak knife from the counter—a pathetic, serrated thing—and
sprinted toward the nursery. I didn’t turn on the hallway lights. I
didn’t want to see what was making that sound. But as I burst through
Lily’s door, the room was silent. The crib was empty.

​The window was wide open, the sheer curtains dancing in the humid night
air like ghosts. I leaned out, screaming her name into the dark treeline
of the backyard, but only the crickets answered. Then, I heard it. A
soft, gurgling coo coming from directly beneath the floorboards.

​I ripped up the corner of the nursery rug, exposing the small crawlspace
hatch we’d never used. It was hanging open. I climbed down into the
darkness, the smell of damp earth and rot hitting me like a physical
wall. My phone’s flashlight cut through the gloom, reflecting off
something pale in the corner.

​It was Lily. She was lying on a bed of dried leaves, staring up at the
joists with wide, unfocused eyes. But she wasn’t alone. Crouched over
her was a figure that looked like a man stretched on a rack until his
skin went translucent. His limbs were too long, ending in fingers that
had too many knuckles. He wasn’t hurting her; he was feeding her.

​He held a cracked porcelain bowl filled with a grey, pulsating slurry.
As I watched, frozen, he dipped a finger into the mess and pressed it
against Lily’s lips. She swallowed it eagerly. The creature turned its
head 180 degrees to look at me, its face a featureless slab of flesh
except for a vertical slit where a mouth should be.

​“She has the taste now,” it hissed, the voice sounding like dry leaves
skittering on pavement. “The milk of the earth is sweeter than yours.”

​I lunged, swinging the knife blindly. The creature didn’t fight back; it
simply unspooled its impossible limbs and vanished into a narrow
drainage pipe in the foundation. I scooped Lily up, sobbing, and ran
back to the light of the kitchen.

​I washed her face, scrubbed the grey slime from her chin, and held her
until my husband got home. I told him everything. He called the police,
but they found nothing in the crawlspace but a pile of old animal bones
and an empty, centuries-old bowl. They told me it was postpartum
psychosis. They gave me pills and told me to sleep.

​But that was three nights ago.

​Lily hasn’t cried since. She doesn’t want my milk. She won’t touch the
formula. She just sits in her crib, staring at the floorboards, her eyes
turning a dull, cloudy grey. Last night, I woke up to find her sitting
upright—something she shouldn’t be able to do yet. She wasn’t looking at
me. She was looking at the vent in the baseboard.

​And from deep within the walls, I heard the wet slap-thud of something
heavy climbing toward us. Lily didn’t scream. She smiled, and for the
first time, I noticed her teeth were coming in. They weren’t white. They
were jagged, black shards of flint, perfectly designed for tearing
through soft, warm meat.

​I’m locking the bedroom door now, but I can hear the wood splintering.
It’s not coming from the hall. It’s coming from inside the mattress
