I’ve been sitting on this for a year. I wasn’t going to post it.

Here’s what makes it hard: I don’t fully believe it myself. I was there.
I saw what I saw — or heard most of it from inside a closet with my back
braced against the door — and I still can’t hold it in my head as
something that actually happened. My brain keeps trying to file it
somewhere else. A dream. A breakdown. Anything that fits inside the
normal shape of things.

Danny is still in my phone. Kira’s Instagram is still up — last post a
photo from the drive up, the road and the trees and the afternoon light.
Forty-six likes. Fourteen comments that stopped after the second week.
Her mom still follows me.

Five people I knew were there on Friday and gone by Saturday. I’ve told
this to the police, to a therapist, to one person I trusted who hasn’t
spoken to me since. I’m telling you now because the anniversary is in
three days and I haven’t slept more than a few hours at a stretch since
last July.

I know how this sounds. I know what you’re going to think. I’m not
asking you to believe me. I just need to get it out of my head and put
it somewhere else for a while.

I’ll start with what I’m certain of. Everything else I’ve questioned a
thousand times. The key card I haven’t.

The key card had a Meridian Property Services logo on it. I was the one
who picked it up from the lockbox on Friday afternoon. I put it in my
jacket pocket and kept it there the whole weekend.

I still have it. I don’t know why.

There were six of us. Me, Mara, Danny, Jake, Kira, and Tom. Fourth of
July weekend. We’d been planning it since March. Mara found the listing
and sent it to the group chat at eleven on a Wednesday night — just
“this one” — and we all said yes within ten minutes.

The cabin was on a private lake about three hours north. No cell service
past the last gas station, which we all thought was charming on the way
up. Kira made a joke about it — something about no service meaning no
problems. We all laughed.

That was Friday — the Fourth. That night you could see fireworks
somewhere far across the lake — distant enough that we couldn’t hear
them, just light flashing on the horizon. Kira had her camera out trying
to catch them.

Saturday was fine until it wasn’t.

I want to get something out of the way first. We weren’t stupid. We
weren’t drunk. We weren’t the kind of people who split up to investigate
a noise or wander off alone at midnight. We were six adults who followed
every reasonable instinct we had. I’m not telling you this to excuse
what happened. I’m telling you because it didn’t matter.

After dinner Saturday, Kira went back down to the dock. Said she wanted
to catch the last of the light — she was always chasing the shot. The
rest of us stayed inside. Mara and I cleaned up and the others played
cards. We didn’t notice she was gone until I went to call her in.

We called for her. We walked down to the dock. Her phone was sitting on
one of the chairs, camera still open, last photo a blurred shot of the
tree line across the water.

We searched for two hours. We told ourselves she’d gone for a walk and
gotten turned around. We convinced ourselves a lot of things.

By eleven o’clock, we stopped pretending.

Danny took his keys and went to drive to the gas station for cell
service. He said he’d call the police and be back in an hour. Tom walked
him out to the car and came back inside. We sat there not talking,
watching the clock.

Twenty minutes later, Mara thought she heard something on the porch. Tom
opened the front door.

Danny’s car was still in the driveway. Door open. Keys on the ground. No
Danny.

We didn’t go back outside after that. All four of us locked every door
and window and stayed in the main room with all the lights on. We didn’t
sleep. We talked about whether we could make it to the car together,
whether it was worth trying, whether there was something out there or
whether we were losing our minds.

Around two in the morning, we heard knocking at the back door.

Then a voice calling Mara’s name. In Kira’s voice.

Mara stood up. She was shaking. I grabbed her arm.

That’s Kira, she said.

It sounds like Kira, I told her. It’s not.

She’s hurt, I remember her saying. Listen to her.

From outside, a voice begged. Said please. Said it was hurt. Said to
just open the door.

I was listening. That was the problem. I was listening very carefully,
and something about the way the voice kept hitting the same note —
steady, patient, not quite panicked enough for someone who’d been
missing in the dark for hours — made the back of my neck go cold.

Jake had been quiet the whole time. Sitting there, listening. All of a
sudden he stood up and went to the back door. He unlocked it and pulled
it open.

I don’t know how to describe what happened next. Jake was a big guy —
six-two, played college football — and it was over in seconds. The door
swung shut on its own.

Then the knocking started again.

Tom was on his feet before the door closed. He ran to the front door and
threw it open and went out onto the porch. I heard his footsteps on the
boards.

Then something that sounded like Jake’s voice drifted in from somewhere
out in the dark. Asking for help.

I heard Tom’s footsteps move toward it. Then stop.

Then nothing.

And then there were two of us.

Mara looked at me. Completely still. The calmest I have ever seen
anyone, and I understood in that moment that it was the calm of someone
who had already decided something.

I told her we couldn’t open the door. She said she knew. I said we stay
together. She said she knew.

I moved toward the hallway. When I turned back, Mara was standing at the
window.

I shouted at her to get away from it.

She said she just wanted to see. Wanted to know if it was really her.

I don’t know what she saw. She didn’t scream. She stepped back from the
window, sat down on the floor, and looked at her hands.

She kept looking at her hands like she didn’t recognize them.

She said it in a flat voice, like the fear had already left her. That I
couldn’t help her. That it was too late.

I ran back to her.

I grabbed her arm and told her we were going. Right now. Together.

She looked up at me. Her pupils had gone huge, swallowing the color,
like something behind them had turned a light off. A thin line of blood
ran from her left nostril and she didn’t wipe it. Didn’t seem to feel
it.

Then it broke. I remember it like it was yesterday. For just a second,
her eyes focused. She was back.

Get away, she said. Go. Now.

Mara—

I should have stayed. I’ve known that every day since.

I ran. The closet at the end of the hall. I threw the door open, pulled
it shut behind me. Wedged my back against it and braced my feet against
the opposite wall and held it. Until my legs stopped working and then
held it some more.

I heard things during those hours that I won’t describe here. I won’t
describe them because I’m not sure I can, without losing it.

At some point the sounds stopped. I don’t know when. I was still holding
the door. I think I was still holding it when the light started coming
under the crack at the bottom. I waited until it was full daylight. I
waited a little longer. Then I decided that if there was something still
out there, it was going to get me anyway, and I opened the door.

I walked straight out through the front door, to Danny’s car. Keys still
on the ground. I drove to the gas station and called 911.

The police found the cabin empty. All five of them gone. No blood, no
signs of struggle. Just an empty cabin. Dishes in the drying rack. Cards
still on the table. Danny’s car in the driveway, door still open. They
didn’t find anything else.

That was a year ago this week. The case is still open. Listed as missing
persons, all five. There’s a detective assigned to the case. I sat
across from him for six hours the first day. They looked at me hard for
a while — I understood why. There was nothing to find. Eventually they
stopped calling.

I know you’re going to search for this. I would too. You won’t find
anything — not because I’m hiding it, but because five adults
disappearing from a remote lake cabin without evidence of foul play
doesn’t make the news. It makes a case number in a small county
sheriff’s department. No bodies, no crime scene, no press conference.
The detective asked me not to share identifying details publicly while
the investigation is active, and I’m not going to be the reason it gets
closed on a technicality. I’ve changed their names. I’ve left out the
location. Everything else is exactly as it happened.

I still have the Meridian Property Services key card in the drawer of my
nightstand. I called the number on it once, a few weeks after. The line
rang twelve times and then a recording picked up — not a voicemail, just
a recording, a man’s voice reading back the number I’d dialed — and then
it clicked off.

I looked them up. Clean website. Professional. Property management for
vacation rentals across the region. They manage dozens of cabins.

Last week I was searching my email for something else and found the
original rental confirmation. I’ve read it a hundred times since. I
don’t know why I opened it again.

Listed amenities: dock access, private lake, kayak rentals, fire pit,
hiking trails, quiet surroundings.

At the bottom of the listing: “Please exercise caution near the
waterfront at all times. Dock and lake access should be avoided after
sundown. Do not approach or engage with local wildlife. Property
management assumes no liability for injuries or incidents occurring
after dark.”

It was there the whole time. I just never read it.
