  actually happy with, and the one that best conveys how I remember

Up until I was about twelve or thirteen, my family and I lived in the
same apartment.

It was not really ours. My parents could not afford a house yet, so we
lived in my grandmother’s place: an old apartment on the second floor of
an old two story building, in a poor, forgotten city somewhere in South
America. There was nothing charming or picturesque about it. It was not
the kind of old building people describe as “full of character.” It was
just old.

It had been built in the early seventies. I lived there in the nineties.
As far as I know, no real renovation had ever happened between those two
points in time. The walls carried decades of hands, smoke, dust, heat,
and neglect. Some places were stained darker where people had touched
them over and over again through the years. The whole place had that
permanent grime old buildings get when they are still being used, but no
one has the money or energy to make them new again.

The entrance from the street was a narrow metal-framed door with glass
panels. It made an awful noise every time someone opened it. Past that
door was astaircase, narrow and ugly, with dirty walls, and the steps
were painted a deep crimson. There were thirty two steps. I remember
counting them one day.

At the top of the stairs, you turned left into a small hall. That hall
was the subject of my nightmares for years.

It was almost square, about two and a half meters by two and a half
meters, a little over eight feet by eight feet. If you came up the
stairs and turned left, the actual entrance to the apartment was right
in front of you: another metal framed glass door, usually left open. On
the wall to your left there was another one of those same doors. This
one was always closed.

Nobody knew where the key was. Nobody even knew if there was a key
anymore. My sisters and I had shoved chewed up bubblegum into the
keyhole long before any of this happened, so even if the key had
existed, I doubt it would have mattered.

To the left of that closed door, on the same wall, was the corridor. My
sisters and I called it the “cabaret,” long before any of us had any
idea what that word meant. It was a long, dark corridor running parallel
to the staircase, with another set of stairs at the far end leading up
to the roof. Its right side was packed with junk my hoarder grandmother
refused to let anyone touch.

During the day, when she was asleep for her afternoon nap, we would
sneak in there and dig through it.

We found all kinds of things. My uncle’s old poetry notebook. My
grandmother’s wedding dress, full of tiny holes chewed through by
silverfish. Old silver cutlery. Enamel dishes with people’s names
engraved on them. I lost count of how many times I got the belt for
rummaging through her things. That was normal back then. I do not hold
it against her. My grandmother was a good person, despite that.

At the very back of the corridor there was a small window. At night, a
little light from the streetlamp outside would come through it, just
enough to keep the darkness from being complete. Not enough to make the
place feel right.

Past the open door into the apartment, immediately to the left, was the
TV room. The closed glass door in the hall was part of that room too,
though from the inside it was blocked by an armchair that had been
pushed against it for as long as I could remember. To the right sat our
old Windows 95 computer.

This was before the internet reached where we lived, so my greatest
entertainment was creating abstract masterpieces in MS Paint and playing
3D Pinball Space Cadet like it was the height of human achievement.

That was where I spent most of my time when I was six or seven. Maybe
even before that, but my memories from earlier years are mostly gone.
Sometimes I watched TV while questionable South American shows played in
the background. Sometimes I sat at the computer, making art that I was
sure could rival Picasso.

And always, just beyond the room, there was the hall.

Because the town was small and crime was almost nonexistent, the street
door downstairs was basically never locked. The doorbell did not work. I
do not remember it ever working. If someone came to visit, they simply
opened the door, climbed the crimson stairs, and announced themselves
from the hall. Because of that closed glass door, we could always see
the silhouette before the person spoke.

A shape would appear behind the glass. Someone would call out. A
relative, a neighbor, a friend. It was normal. Mundane. Part of the
daily life of that apartment. Which is probably why, for a long time, I
did not understand that what I was seeing was not normal.

For as long as I can remember, I had the feeling that someone was there.
Not always. Not clearly. Just a suggestion of movement caught in the
corner of my eye. A figure standing where no one should be standing. A
dark shape slipping out of view. But every time I turned my head, there
was nothing.

I mentioned it once to my older sister with the casual innocence of a
child describing something funny.

“You know when you see someone there, but then you look and nobody’s
there?”

She said no.

I remember being confused by that. To me, it happened so often that I
assumed it happened to everyone. I am still grateful for the
stubbornness of my childish mind, because instead of being frightened by
her answer, I decided she was lying. She had a habit of doing that. So I
ignored it. For a while, nothing more than that happened. I never really
saw anything. Not directly. Not enough to be sure.

Then the nightmares started.

I had normal childhood nightmares too, of course. The velociraptor from
Jurassic Park ate me more times than I can count. Snakes crawled out of
the shower drain to bite me. Monsters chased me through places that only
made sense in dreams.

But there was one nightmare that came back over and over again. In it, I
was doing ordinary kid things, and my father got angry because I had
done something wrong. He was an incredible father in real life, but for
a while he and my mother believed in punishing first and explaining
later. They eventually understood their mistake, changed, and apologized
for it. But as a child, I did not understand punishment. I only
understood fear.

In the dream, the punishment was always the same. My father would tie my
wrists and ankles behind my back. Then he would carry me to the middle
of the entrance hall, the little square hall at the top of the crimson
stairs. He would turn off the lights.

Then he would clap three times.

The claps were not for me. They were to call something. Something I
never saw. Then he would leave me there. That was usually when I woke
up.

But waking up was almost worse than the dream. As soon as my eyes
opened, the sounds began. They were never loud. That may have made them
worse. Loud noises can be explained. A crash, a shout, a motorcycle
passing in the street. These were quiet enough to make me question
whether I was hearing them at all, but clear enough that I knew I was.

Footsteps.

Distant, slow footsteps coming from the cabaret.

A man’s voice, low and indistinct, saying words I could not understand.

The sound of someone moving through the clutter in the corridor,
shifting old objects aside, searching for something.

Cracks in the wood floor. Scrapes. Little impacts.

And I would lie there in bed, frozen. My younger sister and I shared a
bedroom with my grandmother. She slept two beds away from me. All I had
to do was call out to her. All I had to do was say, “Grandma.” But I
never did. Some childish part of me believed that whatever was out there
had not noticed I was awake yet. If I moved, if I spoke, if I made even
the smallest sound, it would know. So I stayed silent.

I tried talking to my mother. I did not tell my father, because he was
in the nightmare, and I felt ashamed of that in a way only a child can
feel ashamed of dreams. My mother told me it was my imagination. Or
noises from the street. Or someone passing by outside. That is what
parents say, because what else can they say?

The nightmare kept coming back. Several times a week, sometimes. Always
the same. The hall. The ropes. The lights going out. The three claps.
And then I would wake up to different noises. Sometimes footsteps.
Sometimes voices. Sometimes the sound of hands moving through my
grandmother’s forgotten things in the corridor.

It got so bad that I peed the bed because I was too scared to get up and
walk to the bathroom only a few meters away. I imagined tiptoeing to the
bedroom window and peeing out of it instead, because that seemed safer
than crossing the apartment and passing the line of sight to the hall. I
never even managed to do that.

But fear is strange. If something happens enough times and never
actually kills you, part of you begins to get used to it. Not
comfortable. Never that. But used to it. Eventually, the fear changed
into something else. Curiosity.

I started trying to see it.

When I watched TV, I would stare at the closed glass door. When I played
on the computer, I would glance at it again and again, trying to catch
the movement directly instead of from the corner of my eye. For a while,
I saw nothing.

Then one night, I did.

I do not remember the day. I do not remember what was on TV. I do not
remember what anyone else in the house was doing. But I remember the
image. I was sitting on the couch. I looked at the closed glass door.
And something was there.

Not a person. Not exactly.

Behind the glass, very clearly, there was a shadow.

It was shaped almost like a person, but not enough to be one. A dark
blur, upright and wrong, standing where no one should have been. I
looked straight at it. And it moved. It slid backward, away from the
glass, toward the corridor.

I will never forget the cold that ran down my spine. Even now, writing
this, I feel it again.

Of course, no one believed me.

I had always been the kind of child who made up stories. Bad stories. I
had once claimed for years that I had seen the chupacabra by the side of
the road. I told people I had fought a lion and won. My family assumed
this was just another one of my fictions, except I had gotten too
involved in it.

But this did not feel like a story. What made it worse was that the
thing never came inside. It stayed in the hall. In the corridor. Behind
the glass. Near the cabaret. As if it was confined there. As if it
belonged there.

After that night, things changed.

I do not know how to explain this without sounding ridiculous, but it
felt like whatever was there knew that I had seen it. The noises after
the nightmare became more frequent. The movement behind the glass became
less shy. It appeared when it wanted to. I saw the shadow many times
after that, moving back and forth in the hall or slipping toward the
corridor.

For more than a year, my family still did not quite believe me. Then one
night, my mother almost did. She had come home from her night classes.
She was a teacher, and it must have been around ten. She was helping me
with homework in the living room, right by the apartment entrance.
Because of the hour, the door to the hall was closed. I looked toward
the glass.

The shadow was there. Moving.

I pointed at it and told her. I remember my voice breaking. I remember
crying. I remember saying it was there, that it was moving, that I could
see it. My mother looked and she saw nothing. But I think that was the
moment she believed I was seeing something, even if she could not see it
herself.

Not long after, we moved. Not because of it, no. My parents didn’t
believe me enough for that, but I was thankful ayway.

My parents had been building a house, but they had to sell it to cover a
debt my uncle had with a loan shark. He was my mother’s brother and my
father’s best friend. It was a terrible situation, but my parents did
the right thing. And somehow, almost as if the universe wanted to repay
them, they found another house they could afford. It was only half a
block away from the apartment.

My mother wanted to stay close to my grandmother, who had health
problems and had already suffered a couple of ischemias. So we moved
near enough to help her, but far enough that I no longer had to sleep
under the shadow of that hall.

And when we moved, the nightmares stopped. The sounds stopped. The
sightings stopped. For the first time in years, whatever had lived in
that corridor no longer had access to me.

Years passed.

I graduated high school and went to college. About two weeks later, my
grandmother died. A few months before that, my uncle had died too. He
had been, although my mother’s other siblings might disagree, her
favorite son.

After my grandmother died, the apartment was left to my grandfather. I
have avoided mentioning him until now because he was a despicable man. A
racist, miserable piece of shit. I wanted nothing to do with him.

But my mother, living only half a block away, still had to take care of
him. She worked two jobs, so she hired caretakers using his retirement
money. After my grandmother was gone, I had no reason to go back to that
apartment. I did not want to see my grandfather, and I wanted even less
to see whatever I believed was still waiting in the corridor.

Then one weekend, I came home from college to visit my mother. It was a
lazy Sunday. I had no tests coming up. I was trying to bring my old
green Game Boy Color back to life when my mother came to me with that
look mothers use when they already know you are going to say no, and
they are prepared to make you feel terrible about it.

She said she did not want to ask me. She said she had no one else. One
of the caretakers had messed with the TV in my grandfather’s apartment,
and now it was not working. Since it was Sunday, none of the people my
mother usually called were available. My grandfather was sleeping. The
caretaker had nothing to do but sit there, and her only entertainment
was watching her Sunday shows of questionable taste.

I refused.

My mother insisted.

Then she used every card mothers keep hidden up their sleeves for
exactly these moments. So I went.

Half a block down the street. To that miserable metal framed door, with
the number 13 written by hand beside it. Up the thirty two crimson
steps. Into the hall.

Nothing was there.

The apartment felt old and dead, but empty. I moved quickly. I went
inside, found the problem, and fixed it. Somehow the caretaker had
managed to unscrew the coaxial cable from the TV. I connected it again,
told my mother I would not stay there another minute, and left.

I went down the stairs as fast as I could without running.

At the bottom, I opened the noisy glass door to the street.

Then, for some reason, I looked back.

One final glance.

At the top of the stairs, in the hall, the shadow was there. Watching
me.

I closed the door.

And I never went back.
