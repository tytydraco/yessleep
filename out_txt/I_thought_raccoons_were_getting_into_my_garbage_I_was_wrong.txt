Monday (8:52 am) 

Woke up to find the trash bag torn open again. Coffee grounds all over
the driveway, chicken bones scattered across the lawn like someone had a
picnic and didn’t bother cleaning up. Mrs. Alvarez two doors down gave
me a look on her way to work,  not the first time, either. I know what
she’s thinking. She thinks I’m the guy who never fixes his fence and now
the guy whose trash looks like a crime scene.

Raccoons, obviously. Big, ballsy ones. Guess I need a better garbage
can.

Tuesday (8:32 pm)

Spent nearly 200 dollars I didn’t need to spend on bungee cords and a
“raccoon-proof” trash can that a guy at the hardware store swore up and
down would solve the problem. Heavy-duty bags, too, the contractor kind.
I laughed about it in line and told the cashier that some fat raccoon in
this neighborhood eats better than I do. She gave a wimpy, weak smile,
more like a wince. Comedic genius strikes again. Whatever. I strapped
that lid down like I was securing a hatch on a submarine.

Wednesday (7:03 am)

It got in anyway.

That’s the part I can’t get past. I checked the straps myself before
bed. Checked them again this morning. Still hooked. Still tight. And yet
the bag was outside the can, ripped open, food gone, lid still strapped
shut as if nothing had ever happened.

I jotted a note down on my sticky pad for later: 

There has to be a hole somewhere in the can. 

There has to be. Things don’t just get into locked containers. I’ll
check every seam tomorrow.

I stood there a long time just looking at it. Confused. Like when you’re
sure you did the math right, and somehow, the answer’s still wrong.

Thursday (9:44 pm)

Decided to be scientific about it. Started keeping track of exactly what
goes in the bag each night so I can figure out what’s attracting it.
Tonight: steak scraps, some fish skin I trimmed off dinner, half a
rotisserie chicken carcass.

Woke up to a disaster. Straps snapped clean off. Bag shredded.

Its routine was changing. That bothered me more than it should have.

Friday (11:49 pm)

Left for work at 7:40am. Came home at 6:15pm. Whole street looked
normal;  nobody’s trash disturbed, no complaints waiting for me on the
porch. I actually felt a little proud walking up to the can, like I’d
finally won.

Opened the lid to check.

Only remnants of cardboard shipping boxes inside. I hadn’t put cardboard
in there. I’d put in the usual stuff you expect:  food waste, some meat
trimmings. Somebody or some thing had gone through my trash, taken
exactly what it wanted, and left the rest arranged almost neatly on top.

Shipping boxes. My shipping boxes? I hadn’t been home all day to open my
deliveries. Was this thing also stealing my packages? I checked the
mail, nothing. 

Odd. 

I didn’t think much of it at the time. I wrote it down and went to bed.

Saturday (4:13 pm)

Started testing things on purpose now. I don’t know when this turned
into an experiment instead of a nuisance, but here we are.

Night one: a bag of shredded paper and junk mail. Untouched by morning.

Night two: a raw chicken package, the plastic and styrofoam tray,
everything. Destroyed. Not just opened, but completely dismantled. The
styrofoam was in pieces I couldn’t have replicated with a hammer.

I’m intrigued now, if I’m being completely honest. There’s something
almost fun about it. Like a word problem that’s finally starting to make
sense. Almost at the answer, and I’m going to get it right this time. 

Sunday (7:56 pm)

Kept testing.

Fruit - a bag of soft, overripe bananas and an old cantaloupe. Nothing
touched.

Vegetables, the same. A whole bag of wilted lettuce sat there untouched,
going bruised and slimy in the morning sun.

Fish. Gone. Bones and all. Even the eyeballs. 

I made a chart in this notebook to keep track of important information.
I don’t know how long I planned for this experiment to run, but
expectedly long enough to keep the findings. 

  ITEM LEFT OUT      TOUCHED (Y/N)   TIME ELAPSED
  ------------------ --------------- --------------
  Paper products     N               > 24 hr
  Raw Chicken        Y               < 24 hr
  Fruits & Veggies   N               > 24 hr

Whatever this is, it doesn’t care about garbage in general. It wants
something specific. 

Meat. 

Protein. 

Animal. 

It’s not a scavenger. It’s a diner with a very narrow menu.

Monday (6:38 pm)

The smell finally got bad enough that Mr. Petrossian complained directly
to my face this time, not just to Mrs. Alvarez behind my back. Told me
to call animal control before the county does it for me. 

Fair enough.

They came out this afternoon. Walked the yard. Checked the fence line,
the gap under the shed, and the drainage ditch behind the property.

Nothing. No paw prints. No claw marks on the can or the fence. No scat
anywhere, which the guy seemed weirdly fixated on… Kept saying that
whatever’s eating that much protein should be leaving something behind.
He shrugged eventually, wrote something on a clipboard, told me to keep
the lid weighted down, and left.

I don’t know why that bothered me more than the trash did. It should
have made me feel better. Instead, the house felt too quiet after he
drove off.

Saturday (10:15 am) 

Went on a trip to my sister’s for a couple of days, first vacation I’ve
taken in ages. Left three bags of trash out, figuring the can would
either be a wreck by Sunday or full to the brim.

Came home to nothing but flattened cardboard boxes and the trash exactly
the way I’d stacked it, untouched down to the last banana peel I’d
tossed on top out of habit.

A week. Nobody home. And it left it alone.

That’s when I stopped thinking animal.

Tuesday (10:56 pm)

Ordered a security camera. Overnighted it, paid extra, which tells you
something about where my head’s at even though I was still cracking
jokes about it to myself while I mounted the bracket. 

Let’s finally meet my freeloading tenant, I said out loud to an empty
backyard, feeling pretty clever.

Confident tonight. Camera’s aimed right at the can. 

We’ll have this solved by morning.

Wednesday (7:45 am)

Pulled the footage first thing. Nothing for hours, just the can, the
fence, a moth or two drifting through the porch light.

11:58 PM.

Recording ends.

Just… ends. No error, no corrupted file warning. The app says the
battery died. I charged that thing to a hundred percent before I mounted
it. I checked. I remember checking. The supplier promised 36-hour
battery life. 

Cheap inputs, probably. 

Eh. I’ll just get a better one.

Thursday (8:36 pm)

Bought a different camera. Better brand this time, the kind with actual
reviews. Ran an extension cord out from the garage instead of trusting a
battery. No excuses this time.

Set it up right after dinner. 

Friday (6:17 am)

The camera’s gone.

Yeah. 

Found it eventually, thirty feet from where I’d mounted it, sitting in
the grass near the fence line. Not chewed. Not scratched, not gnawed at
the way an animal would test something with its mouth.

Stepped on. Crushed flat, dead center, like something had simply put its
full weight down on it in passing without breaking stride. The lens is a
spiderweb of cracks pressed into the housing.

I stopped joking about a “freeloading tenant” tonight.

Saturday (1:34 am)

Spent the evening on forums instead of sleeping properly. 

Coyotes, people said. 

Black bears wandering further into suburbs than usual, somebody
insisted, sending links. 

One guy was very sure it was a typical homeless dude simply going
through people’s trash and getting spooked by the camera light.

None of it fits. 

Coyotes don’t step on cameras and walk away.  Bears don’t ration
themselves to protein only, night after night, for weeks, without ever
once finishing the job. I don’t think a homeless man would still be
strong enough, after however many nights of trash-diving, to flatten a
device like that without so much as tipping it over first.

My entries are shorter, thoughts more hurried.  I’ve noticed that. I
don’t have much to say most nights except that I didn’t sleep well
again.

I don’t know how many days it’s been like this. I don’t remember the
last time I clocked in to work. Every day and night just blend
together. 

All I can think about is whatever it is that visits me outside my front
door every night.  

Sunday (2:33 am) 

Decided I’d stay up and watch myself tonight, no camera, no experiments.
Just me and the porch light and the street.

Wrote for a while about how quiet the neighborhood gets after eleven: no
cars, no dogs, not even wind most nights. Tonight it felt quieter than
usual. The kind of quiet that makes you notice your own breathing.

Nothing happened. I gave up around 2 and went to bed feeling a little
embarrassed about the whole thing. Maybe everyone’s right. 

Maybe I’ve been building a monster out of raccoons and bad luck.

Monday (3:24 am) 

Nothing again. Sat out there for two hours and watched absolutely
nothing happen. Felt stupid enough that I didn’t even bring the notebook
out with me the second night. 

I think I’m done watching for a while.

Tuesday (11:54 pm)

I heard it tonight.

Not digging. Not the clatter of a knocked-over can. Something slower, 
careful, almost. Like footsteps that didn’t want to be footsteps. Polite
is a strange word to use for a noise, but that’s the one that came to
me, sitting frozen on the edge of my bed at ten past midnight.

I went to the window. Looked out through the gap in the blinds.

I could see something near the can. I couldn’t tell you what, exactly.
My eyes kept trying to make it into a person, and something in me kept
refusing that answer, back and forth, like two people arguing inside my
skull. 

I didn’t move. 

I didn’t turn on a light. 

I stayed there until my legs cramped, and then I stopped writing for the
night, because I didn’t know how to finish the sentence.

Monday  (11:23 pm) 

I made myself go outside tonight. I don’t fully know why. I think some
part of me needed this to be over one way or another,  needed it to turn
out to be a person, a raccoon, a bear, anything with a name I already
knew. I was tired, past the point of exhaustion. Past the point of
taking the easy answer and being done with it. 

Flashlight in one hand, the other one shaking slightly, which
embarrassed me even alone in my own yard. Heart beating hard enough that
I could feel it pounding in my ears and down into my fingertips. 

I kept telling myself, all the way across the grass, that it was just
somebody stealing cans for the scrap money.

People do that. 

It happens all the time. 

I only needed to round the corner of the fence and see a tired man with
long matted hair and a shopping cart, and this would all collapse into
something small and explainable, a story I’d tell at work in a few weeks
and laugh about.

I rounded the corner.

Someone was crouched beside the trash.

For one full second, I felt relief so total it was almost dizzying. 

A person. Just a person.

Then the feeling turned over in my chest, slow and wrong, like a held
note going sour.

It was too narrow. 

That was the first thing. 

Whatever was crouched there was built wrong for a person, too narrow
through the shoulders and too long everywhere else, folded down small
the way you’d fold a lawn chair, not the way a person kneels.

I couldn’t understand how its back bent like that. There was a curve in
it that didn’t belong to a spine I recognized, a smooth wrong arc where
I expected a straight line.

It looked like someone wearing too many coats, bulk in places that
didn’t match up with how thin the rest of it seemed, like it was
carrying its own extra material folded up against itself.

For a second, I thought its elbow was its knee. I genuinely could not
tell which joint I was looking at.

Then it moved.

It reached into the bag, unhurried, the way you’d reach into your own
refrigerator at home.

And then another arm unfolded out from underneath the first one. Not
from the shoulder. From somewhere lower, somewhere along the ribs, like
a second limb had been folded away and tucked out of sight and was only
now being needed.

I stopped breathing. I resisted the urge to vomit. 

My mind kept rejecting it, kept throwing the image back at me and asking
me to try again, try a simpler answer. No, I remember thinking. Just
that. No.

Then the flashlight beam caught its hand as it lifted something from the
bag, and I saw the fingers. Six of them. 

It turned to look at me.

No roar. No shriek, no bolt for the fence, none of the things every
animal on every forum thread would have done. It simply turned,
unhurried, the way anyone turns when they hear a noise behind them, and
it looked at me.

It had a face.

Trying so very hard to be ordinary.

The skin hung a little too loosely across the bones beneath it, as
though it had been draped there instead of grown. The features all
existed. Eyes. Nose. Mouth. All where they belonged.

Just… not quite.

The distance between them shifted every time I looked.

Its jaw looked almost normal until it moved, and then I couldn’t
understand where the hinge actually was. My eyes kept trying to settle
on a face they recognized, and every time they thought they had,
something small would be wrong again.

The corners of its mouth pulled tightly upward into what I think was
meant to be a smile.

Not a broad grin.

The restrained, polite smile you give a stranger while waiting for an
elevator.

Only it stretched too far toward the ears without ever widening. The
muscles beneath the skin seemed uncertain of which direction they were
supposed to pull, tightening and relaxing in tiny, independent twitches
until the expression settled into something that only resembled
friendliness from a distance.

It looked practiced.

Like someone who had spent a very long time watching people smile
without ever quite learning why they did it.

I knew that face.

My brain caught on it immediately, the way your tongue finds a chipped
tooth without you meaning to. I couldn’t place it. Somewhere ordinary.
Somewhere I’d seen a thousand times. It looked at me with patient
expectation, waiting for me to catch up.

Then it tilted its head.

Not like an animal.

Like someone waiting for you to remember their name.

Then it stood.

My fingers opened before I realized I’d let go. The flashlight hit the
grass with a dull wet thud, the beam rolling sideways across the yard.

It had not been as tall as I first thought, crouched there… it had been
folded. Its proportions kept resolving, piece by piece, into something
that had been pretending to be shaped like a person, and had simply
stopped bothering to keep up the pretense now that I’d seen it.

Then, without any hurry at all, it picked up my garbage bag by the
twist-tie, the way I might pick up my own bag on my way to the curb. It
walked into the dark at the back of the yard, into the crawl space under
my house, where the basement entrance is. The flattened shipping boxes
were stacked beneath the steps. 

It was completely unbothered, almost domestic, like this was simply what
Monday nights looked like for it and had looked like for longer than I
wanted to think about. I don’t know how long I stood there after it was
gone. Long enough that my legs had gone stiff by the time I picked up
the flashlight and made it back inside.

I locked the door. Like that would do anything. This thing had already
been here… I don’t want to know how many times. 

I stood in the kitchen for a long time, staring at nothing, trying to
remember where I’d seen that expression before. Calm. Patient. Faintly
inconvenienced. Like I’d interrupted something.

I don’t know why I ever thought it was an animal. It knew exactly what I
was. It knew exactly what I had seen. Not because of the face. Because
of the angle.

I’d seen it through my bedroom blinds. Every morning I’d convince myself
I’d imagined it. Just my peripheral vision making shapes out of shadows.
It wasn’t waiting for my garbage scraps, it was waiting for me.

The trash had never been food. It had been bait.

Garbage pickup is Tuesday.

Today is Monday.

I don’t know what it comes for tomorrow.
