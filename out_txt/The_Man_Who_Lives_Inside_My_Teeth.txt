I have a man who lives inside my teeth. His name is Bartholomew, and he
pays rent in pennies and secrets.

I first found him when a molar fell out. Not from decay or age, it just…
popped. One moment I was chewing a cracker, the next I was holding a
small, blood-slicked bone. But it wasn’t hollow. Inside, where the nerve
should be, there was a tiny wooden door with a brass knob.

I poked it with a thumbtack. The door swung open, and a little man no
bigger than my fingernail, wearing a tiny tweed suit, leaned out. He
tipped a minuscule hat at me and said, “A pleasure to finally meet you,
landlord. The rent is due.”

He crawled out and walked across my palm. His shoes made a faint
clicking sound. He explained that he had been living in my jawbone for
the last seven years, subsisting on sugar and forgotten memories. He
said he was the one who made the song in my head get stuck sometimes. It
was his radio.

The rent he paid was strange. Every night, he would leave a single,
tarnished penny under my pillow. In exchange, he would tell me a secret.
Not my secrets. Other people’s. He told me my mailman was afraid of
spoons. He told me my boss cries into a pillow shaped like a dog every
night. He told me the woman at the grocery store checkout was born with
a tail that she keeps tucked into her sock.

I became obsessed. I started craving the secrets. I stopped brushing my
teeth, hoping more would fall out so more tenants could move in. I
started eating only hard candy and jawbreakers. I wanted a whole city of
them in there. A whole parliament of tiny men whispering the world’s
dirt to me.

Last Tuesday, my front left incisor went. This tenant wasn’t
Bartholomew. This one was a woman, naked and covered in what looked like
fine moss. She didn’t speak. She just pointed at my reflection in the
mirror and laughed, a sound like grinding gravel. The penny she left was
warm and pulsing.

That’s when Bartholomew got nervous. He emerged from the molar, his tiny
face pale. “She’s a Gapper,” he whispered, his voice trembling. “They
don’t pay in secrets. They pay in gaps. They take things.”

That night, I woke up to a clicking sound. The moss woman was standing
on my nightstand, pointing at my sleeping husband. She was pointing at
the space between his ring finger and his pinky. I watched in horror as
a tiny, shimmering line appeared there, a gap in reality, and then his
wedding ring simply fell through it onto the floor.

I tried to scream, but my mouth was full of them. Dozens of them were
crawling out from my gums, pouring onto my pillow like ants. They were
all different shapes and sizes. Some were made of bone, some of shadow,
one was just a writhing knot of hair with a single, unblinking eye in
the center.

They weren’t paying rent anymore. They were taking their payment.

One of them, a little man made of what looked like burnt toast, crawled
onto my face and pointed at my eye. I felt a sudden, cold emptiness. I
couldn’t see out of my left eye anymore. It wasn’t blind; it was just…
gone. A gap.

They are taking my body apart piece by piece. I can feel them in there
now, chiseling at my bones, hollowing me out to make more room.
Bartholomew is gone. I think the Gapper took him. I haven’t felt a
secret in days.

I’m typing this with one hand. The other one is gone now. I don’t know
where it went. There’s just a smooth, numb space where it used to be.

I can hear them in my teeth, the ones I have left. They’re not
whispering secrets anymore. They’re chanting. They’re celebrating.
They’re finishing the renovations.

I have to go. I think I just felt a gap open where my heart used to be
