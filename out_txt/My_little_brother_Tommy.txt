​

I need to write this down before I forget it. Before my mind changes it
into something softer. That’s what minds do, right? They protect you.
But I don’t want protection anymore. I want someone to believe me.

My name is Claire. I was thirteen. My brother, Tommy, was eight. That’s
all you need to know for now.

It started on a Tuesday. Our school was old, built sometime in the
1950s, the kind of building with too many hallways and not enough
windows. Tommy was small for his age, always getting picked on, always
losing things. That day he lost his water bottle somewhere near the old
gym, the one they didn’t use anymore because the floor was rotting.

I went with him to look for it after school. Everyone else had gone
home. The halls were empty in that way that makes your footsteps sound
too loud, like the building is listening to you walk.

We found the water bottle near a door I had never noticed before. It was
small, painted the same grey as the wall, easy to miss if you weren’t
looking right at it. There was no doorknob. Just a small hole where a
knob should be, like someone had removed it on purpose.

Tommy being Tommy, he pushed his finger into the hole before I could
stop him.

The door opened by itself.

I want to be clear here. I did not open that door. It opened on its own,
slow, like it had been waiting for a reason to.

Inside was a stairwell going down. The air coming up from it was cold,
colder than a fridge, and it smelled like wet paper and something else I
didn’t have a name for back then. I know the name now.

Tommy stepped in before I could grab him. Kids do that. They don’t think
about what’s on the other side of dark places. I followed because that’s
what older sisters do. We follow our brothers into the dark so they’re
not alone in it.

The stairs went down longer than they should have. Our school only had
two floors. We must have walked down for what felt like ten minutes,
maybe more. Tommy held my hand the whole way and his hand was shaking,
but so was mine, so I didn’t say anything about it.

At the bottom was a room shaped like our classroom. Same desks. Same
chalkboard. Same little poster of the alphabet on the wall. But
everything was covered in a thin layer of dust, like it hadn’t been
touched in decades, and the desks were arranged in a way that made my
stomach hurt to look at, though I couldn’t tell you why.

Sitting in one of the desks was a boy.

He looked like Tommy. Not similar. Exact. Same haircut, same gap in his
front teeth, same scar on his eyebrow from the bike accident two summers
ago that only our family knew about.

He looked up at us and smiled, and he said, in Tommy’s exact voice,
“You’re late again.”

I grabbed Tommy’s hand and we ran. We ran up those stairs faster than I
have ever run in my life, and the whole time I could hear that boy’s
voice behind us, not shouting, just talking, calm, like he already knew
how this was going to end.

“You’re late again. You’re late again. You’re always late.”

We got out. The door slammed shut behind us on its own. We told our
parents we were just playing near the old gym. We never mentioned the
door.

That was four months ago.

Here is the part I haven’t told anyone. Tommy has not been the same
since. He does his homework now without being asked. He stopped being
scared of the dark. He started calling me by a nickname he never used
before, one that only makes sense if you knew our grandmother, who died
before Tommy was even born.

Last week I asked him what happened in those few seconds when I was
looking away, back near that door, before we ran.

He looked at me, and for one second, one single second, his smile did
not look like his smile at all.

Then he said, “Nothing happened. We should go back sometime though. He
gets lonely down there.”

I checked the old gym yesterday.

The door is gone. Not locked. Not hidden. Just gone, like it was never
built into that wall at all, like I imagined the whole thing.

But Tommy still has dust on his shoes from that day. I checked his shoes
this morning while he was sleeping. Grey dust, the exact colour of that
classroom floor, and it has not come off no matter how many times my mom
washes them.

Tonight he asked me to tuck him in, which he never does anymore. As I
turned off his light, he whispered something into his pillow. I almost
didn’t catch it. But I did.

He said, “Don’t worry. I told him you’ll be late too.”

I don’t know what that means. I don’t want to know what that means.

I just keep thinking about how that boy at the desk said “again.” Like
this had happened before. Like it will happen again.

I haven’t slept in three nights because every time I close my eyes I
hear it. Chalk on a chalkboard, somewhere far below the house, writing
something over and over.

I finally worked up the courage to sneak into Tommy’s room an hour ago
to check what it was.

It’s my name. Written maybe two hundred times.

And under my name, in smaller writing, it just says: soon.
