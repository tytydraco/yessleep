The rain started three days after the Outbreak

At first, people thought it was some kind of virus. Videos flooded the
internet—shaking phone footage of corpses twitching on morgue tables,
old men clawing out of graves, patients biting nurses in overcrowded
hospitals. Governments denied everything until denial became impossible.

Then the power grids failed.

Then the screaming began.

Ethan hadn’t seen another living person in six days.

He sat alone in the upstairs bedroom of his childhood home, shotgun
across his lap, listening to the storm hammer the roof. Every few
minutes, thunder rolled through the empty town like distant artillery.

The house smelled of mildew, candle wax, and blood.

Not all of it was zombie blood.

He stared at the bedroom door.

Scratch.

Scratch.

Scratch.

The sound came again.

His fingers tightened around the shotgun.

“Dad?” he whispered before he could stop himself.

The scratching paused.

Then came a wet, gargling sound from the hallway.

Ethan shut his eyes.

Three nights ago, his father had come home after sunset, pale and
shivering. He’d said he’d been attacked near the gas station. Said it
was “just a bite.”

By midnight, he’d started screaming.

By one in the morning, Ethan had locked him upstairs.

By dawn, whatever was behind that door was no longer his father.

Scratch.

Scratch.

A low moan drifted through the crack beneath the door.

Ethan stood slowly and backed away.

The thing outside had learned something.

It never scratched unless it heard him move.

A sudden crash exploded downstairs.

Glass shattered.

Ethan froze.

Another crash.

Something had entered the house.

Then silence.

His pulse thundered in his ears.

The zombies outside usually wandered aimlessly. Slow. Stupid.

But sometimes…

Sometimes they remembered things.

A floorboard creaked downstairs.

Not the uneven dragging footsteps of the dead.

A careful step.

Human.

Ethan crept to the bedroom window and peeked through the curtains.

No movement outside.

The street was empty except for abandoned cars and bodies bloating in
the rainwater.

Another creak below him.

Someone was inside.

He raised the shotgun and opened the bedroom door.

The hallway was dark except for flashes of lightning through the
stairwell window.

At the end of the hall stood the locked attic door.

Something wet dripped from underneath it.

His father was awake.

A whisper floated up from downstairs.

“Hello?”

A woman’s voice.

Soft. Nervous.

“Please… I’m not infected.”

Ethan almost cried from relief.

Another survivor.

He hurried halfway down the stairs before stopping.

Something felt wrong.

The living had become more dangerous than the dead weeks ago. People
killed for batteries. For canned food. For dry socks.

“For God’s sake,” the woman called weakly. “Please help me.”

Lightning flashed through the living room window.

For an instant, Ethan saw her silhouette.

Standing perfectly still in the middle of the room.

Head tilted.

Not moving.

Not breathing.

Another flash.

She was gone.

Ethan stumbled backward.

A cold hand clamped around his ankle from beneath the stairs.

He screamed.

A corpse dragged itself from the darkness below, jaw hanging loose, eyes
milky white. Ethan fired instinctively.

BOOM.

The blast painted the wall black.

The sound echoed through the house.

Then came the pounding.

From outside.

Dozens of fists slammed against doors and windows.

Moans erupted from the street.

They heard the gunshot.

“Shit,” Ethan gasped.

Glass exploded downstairs.

Shapes began pouring into the house.

Rotting figures stumbling through broken windows, twitching unnaturally
as rainwater streamed from their gray flesh.

Ethan ran upstairs.

Behind him came the sound of snapping teeth and dragging feet.

He slammed the bedroom door shut and pushed the dresser against it just
as bodies crashed into the other side.

The wood splintered immediately.

Too many.

Way too many.

Then—

Knock.

Knock.

Ethan turned slowly toward the closet.

The knocking came from inside it.

He stared in horror.

“Who’s there?” he whispered.

A little girl’s voice answered.

“Daddy?”

His blood turned to ice.

His daughter had died in the first week.

He had buried her himself.

Knock.

Knock.

“Daddy, it’s cold.”

Tears filled Ethan’s eyes.

“No,” he whispered. “No no no…”

The zombies outside the bedroom suddenly stopped pounding.

The entire house went silent.

Even the storm seemed quieter.

Then every voice spoke at once.

From the hallway.

From downstairs.

From the closet.

From the attic.

“LET US IN.”

The bedroom door burst inward.

Ethan fired once.

Twice.

Three times.

Rotting faces surged through the opening.

Hands grabbed him.

Cold fingers tore at his skin.

And above the chaos, through the thunder and screaming, Ethan saw
something standing at the end of the hallway.

Tall.

Motionless.

Watching.

Not dead.

Not alive.

Its hollow eyes glowed pale white in the dark.

Smiling.

As if all of this—

The infection.

The hunger.

The voices—

Had only been the beginning.
