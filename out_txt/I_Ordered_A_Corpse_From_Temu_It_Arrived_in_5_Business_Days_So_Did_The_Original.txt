Before you judge me, before you call the cops on me, before you get your
proverbial pitchforks out for me, let me explain myself.

I am not a criminal, unless you consider filmmaking a crime.

Okay, I guess ‘amateur aspiring film school dropout’ will be more
appropriate, but you get the idea. Low budget, high ambition. The usual
story for someone like me, sitting in South Asia, with one camera and
too much free time.

I’ve been working on a short horror film for the past eight months.
Something about an autopsy gone wrong. Three weeks ago, a production
company out of Singapore reached out. They’d seen my previous work. They
wanted a rough cut of the new one.

Ten days, they said.

So I had ten days, a half-finished script, and one very specific
problem: I needed a prop corpse.

If you’ve never tried to source a realistic prop dead body from South
Asia, I’ll save you the effort. There are incredible listings on Amazon.
Hollywood-grade silicone bodies with actual pore texture, weighted
limbs, the works. Exactly what I needed. Then you see the international
shipping fee, which costs more than the product itself, and you close
the tab and sit quietly for a moment.

So I went to Temu.

I wasn’t expecting much obviously. I typed in “prop corpse”, mostly out
of desperation. Surprisingly, I did find a seller, with an entire store
page dedicated to nothing else. Just corpses. Rows of them. Different
builds, different ages, different ethnicities. The prices were absurd.
Twelve dollars. Fifteen. The one I ended up ordering was twenty,
shipping included.

I needed a Caucasian male for the script. My country is entirely brown
in race, so this seller was my only realistic option. I selected a
blonde male, added it to cart, and checked out before I could think too
hard about it.

The listing photos looked disturbingly lifelike. Not mannequin-lifelike.
Something harder to explain. I told myself it was just good product
photography.

It arrived in five business days.

I unboxed it in my studio that morning. It was day seven of working
straight. The deadline was in three days. I had been surviving on bad
coffee, and the specific kind of focus that only kicks in when you’re
genuinely terrified of failure.

At first glance, it was exactly what I needed.

The texture was wrong, in the right way. It did feel like skin, without
being skin. The weight was distributed the way a body actually is, heavy
in the wrong places. The face had this stillness that a mannequin never
quite manages.

I set it on the studio floor and stepped back and thought,

Okay. This works.

Then I looked closer.

The eyes were slightly asymmetrical. The teeth were the wrong number. I
counted twice. The proportions of the limbs were just slightly off. It
felt like someone had been reconstructing a body from memory, and had
second-guessed themselves on the fingers.

It felt less like a prop, and more like a copy.

A BAD copy, that is. A knockoff dead body. A bootleg of the real thing.

Classic Temu, I thought

The seller messaged me through the app that same night, asking for a
review. I typed back: “Good effort, but it looks like a bootleg, man.”

His reply came almost immediately.

“The original product was lost during delivery. Had to improvise.”

I stared at that message for a while.

I typed back asking what he meant. The message showed as delivered. No
response. I checked the store page. The listing was still up, but the
seller’s last active time stopped updating. After two days, I stopped
checking.

I should have been unnerved by that.

Honestly, I should have been a lot of things. But I was on day eight,
and running on fumes. In fact, the corpse, prop, whatever… had
accidentally changed the direction of my film. The imperfection of it
was more interesting than anything I had originally written. I scrapped
the autopsy script entirely. Started rewriting a story around the prop
itself. A horror film about a body that isn’t quite right. Something
assembled close enough to pass a first glance, but wrong in ways that
compound the longer you look.

I was writing the climax scene where the dead body comes alive. That’s
when I fell asleep.

I don’t remember it happening actually. One moment I was staring at the
prop on the table, genuinely puzzling over how I was going to stage the
practical effect of it moving. The next moment I was opening my eyes on
the studio floor at night. I had a crick in my neck, and no idea what
time it was.

Then someone rang my doorbell.

I want to be really precise about this part, because I know how it
sounds.

I opened the door.

There was a man standing outside. Caucasian. Blonde. He looked
disoriented in the way people look when they’ve woken up somewhere
unfamiliar. Not scared, just unmoored. He was dressed normally. He
wasn’t injured.

I recognized his face before I understood why I recognized it.

And then I understood.

Same bone structure. Same hairline. Same jaw. Features I have seen
before, but the correct version. Symmetrical eyes looking at me. The
right number of teeth when he spoke.  

The version of the face that the prop had been trying to be and hadn’t
quite managed.

I remember feeling a chill run down my spine.

He said he wasn’t sure how he’d gotten here. He said the last few days
were blurry. For some reason, he was drawn to this particular house- my
house. That was the exact word he used… drawn.

He didn’t say it like someone traumatized. He said it like someone
embarrassed about running late to something, almost apologetic. He asked
if he could have a glass of water.

I gave it to him. I don’t know why I didn’t say anything. I think I was
still processing.

He drank the water, thanked me, and asked for directions to the nearest
bus stop.

I told him.

He thanked me again, politely, and left.

I stood in the doorway and watched him walk down the street until he
turned a corner. Then I went back inside and sat down on the studio
floor next to the prop, and both of us just stayed there for a while.

I could write a hundred scripts, but none of them would come even close
to what I had just experienced.

I immediately checked the seller’s store page.

The corpse listing was gone. The entire collection had been replaced.
Different faces, different bodies, completely restocked, like none of
the previous inventory had ever existed. The chat log with the seller
was still there. His last message was still there.

“The original product was lost during delivery. Had to improvise”

I closed the app.

I didn’t know what to do. I didn’t know who to share this with. Who
would believe me?

That’s when my phone buzzed with a Temu notification. One of those
algorithmic ones: Based on your recent purchase, you might also like-

I opened it without thinking.

It was a listing photo. A body, positioned like a corpse, lying on a
studio floor. MY STUDIO FLOOR. I recognized the stack of equipment cases
in the background. I recognized the specific way the cables were coiled
near the wall. I recognized ink stains on the hand from hours of writing
a script manually. I recognized the sign of stress and exhaustion on the
prop corpse’s face. MY FUCKING FACE !

The listing title read:

Adult Male, Brown, 20s. Good condition. One available. Ships in 3-5
business days.

Price $18.

Free shipping included.

If anyone else has ordered from this seller, or found a store page like
this, please tell me. I don’t know what I’m looking for exactly. I just
need to know if I’m the only one.

And if you’re reading this and you recognize the studio in that listing
photo-

Don’t open the link.

As for me, I’ve been trying to finish the film.

The seller’s store is still up. The new collection has been up for a day
now.

I haven’t looked at it long enough to count the teeth.
