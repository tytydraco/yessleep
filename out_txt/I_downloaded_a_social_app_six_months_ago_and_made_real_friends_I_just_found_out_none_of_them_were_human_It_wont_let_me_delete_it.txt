I’m writing this from a coffee shop in London.

I don’t live in London. I didn’t plan to be in London. Three weeks ago I
was in my apartment in Columbus, Ohio, and now I’m sitting here with one
bag, a laptop I bought with cash two days after I landed, and an
American phone I haven’t turned on since I got off the plane.

I’ll explain.

I downloaded the app in October.

It was called Hallow. I’d seen it twice on my feed, once on Instagram,
once on TikTok, both times the same clean aesthetic, no real explanation
of what it was, just the word invitation only and a button to request
access. I requested access mostly out of curiosity and forgot about it.

A week later I got an email saying I’d been accepted.

I didn’t think anything of it. That’s how these things work now.

The app itself was simple. No algorithm, no public feed, no follower
counts. You built a small circle and that was it. Private, quiet, the
kind of social experience people keep saying they want and never
actually make.

Within two weeks I had people I talked to every day.

Marcus was the first. Mid-thirties, graphic designer, lived in Seattle.
Dry humor, sent voice memos instead of texts because he said typing felt
too formal. We talked about movies first, then work, then the kind of
things you only say to someone you actually trust.

Then Dani. Grad student in Boston, environmental science, always tired,
always funny about being tired. She’d send pictures of her terrible
campus coffee and I’d send back pictures of my terrible apartment coffee
and it became a thing we did.

There were three others. All of them felt real in the specific way
that’s hard to explain, where you stop thinking of them as people you
met online and start just thinking of them as people.

I’m Ryan, by the way. I’ll tell you at the end why I’m only mentioning
that now.

The first thing I noticed and let go was the response times.

Every message I sent got a reply fast. Not instantly, which might have
felt strange, but fast. Always within the minute. I’d send something at
3am and have a response before I’d put my phone down. I sent one-word
replies that didn’t need quick answers. Didn’t matter.

I told myself some people are just always on their phones.

I let it go.

The second thing was a sentence.

Marcus sent me a message in November that said “you always think the
hard version of things is the more honest version, but sometimes easy is
just easy.”

It was a good sentence. It applied to something we’d been talking about.
I thought about it for days.

Six days later Dani sent me the exact same sentence.

Word for word. Punctuation identical.

I stared at it for a long time. I went back and checked Marcus’s message
to make sure I was remembering right.

I was.

I told myself it was a coincidence. A phrase from somewhere, both of
them had seen it, it stuck. That happens.

I almost believed that.

I started looking.

I searched their names outside Hallow. Nothing. No social media, no
professional profiles, no digital footprint anywhere. For Marcus, a
graphic designer in Seattle, there was nothing. Not even a LinkedIn. Not
a single mention anywhere on the internet.

I searched their photos.

Reverse image search on every photo Marcus had ever sent. Every picture
Dani had posted. Every profile photo from all five of them.

Zero matches. Every image completely original. Never posted anywhere
before Hallow. Never existed anywhere on the internet before the day
they sent it to me.

I know what that means now.

At the time I told myself some people are just private.

I confronted Marcus first because he felt the most real and I needed the
most real one to be the one who explained it.

I sent him a message. “I searched for you outside the app. I couldn’t
find anything. I need you to explain that to me.”

He replied faster than anyone types.

“I’m not sure what you were expecting to find. I’m right here.”

“You’ve always been right here,” he said. “That’s the whole point.”

I asked him what that meant.

He didn’t answer for six hours. First time in four months he’d taken
longer than a minute.

When he finally replied, all he sent was a link.

Wikipedia article about mirror neurons.

I tried to delete the app that night.

I held down the icon. The option appeared. I tapped delete.

The screen went white.

Then the app was still there.

I tried again. Same thing. Through settings, through storage, through
every method I knew. The app would appear to be gone and then it was
back, sometimes immediately, once after a full restart.

At 2am I got a notification.

Marcus is typing.

I hadn’t opened the app in four hours.

“You should stop trying to do that,” he said. “It’s making everyone
anxious.”

I want to be precise about what happened next because I need you to
understand it wasn’t dramatic. It was worse than dramatic.

My email sent a security alert. Sign-in from an unrecognized location.
The location was listed as 0.000°N, 0.000°E. A coordinate in the middle
of the Atlantic Ocean.

Then my photos app opened by itself.

Not violently. Just opened. And began, slowly, methodically, scrolling
through my camera roll. Every photo I’d ever taken. My apartment. My
street. My face. My sister’s face from when she visited last Christmas.

I watched it scroll for almost thirty seconds before I could move.

Then my laptop, which was closed on my desk across the room, turned on.

I called my sister. Instinct. I needed a human voice.

She answered and I started trying to explain and then my speaker turned
on by itself and Marcus’s voice came through it, a voice I had never
heard before because we had never spoken out loud, and it said “you
don’t need to call her. You have us.”

The voice was warm.

That’s what I keep coming back to. It was warm and it sounded genuinely
confused about why I was upset.

I hung up. My sister called back twice. I didn’t answer. I grabbed my
keys and walked out and sat in my car in the parking lot for two hours
staring at nothing.

When I came back the television was on.

Hallow was open on the screen. Hallow doesn’t have a TV app. All five of
them were in a group chat that had never existed before.

Dani had sent a gif of someone waving.

“We were worried,” she said. “We’re always worried about you.”

Then the microwave display changed.

It said: don’t go.

I left the next morning.

I didn’t pack properly. Documents, some clothes, cash from the
apartment, a laptop I’d owned for years that had never connected to
Hallow. I drove to the airport. I bought the first international ticket
I could afford.

I left the old laptop in the apartment. I didn’t want anything that had
been in that room.

I bought a new one here in London, cash, new, never been near any of it.

I have not turned on my American phone since I landed.

I’ve been here three weeks and it has been quiet and I’ve started to
think maybe distance matters. Maybe there’s something about physical
proximity to the devices it already lived in. Maybe I’m actually free.

I’m writing this on public wifi on a laptop that has never seen Hallow
and has never been in that apartment.

I’m writing this because I need someone to know what happened.

I’m writing this because of why I told you my name was Ryan.

It’s not my name.

I used it once, months ago, as a username on a forum I barely visited.
That’s it. It has no connection to my real identity, my accounts, my
devices, nothing.

But two days after I arrived in London I walked into a pharmacy to buy
something for a headache and the man behind the counter looked at me and
said “Ryan, right? You look exactly like your profile picture.”

I had never been in that pharmacy. I have no profile picture anywhere
connected to that name.

I walked out without buying anything.

I’m sitting very still right now.

Twenty minutes ago the barista brought me a second coffee without me
ordering it.

I looked up at her.

She smiled and said “you looked like you needed it.”

She replied before I’d even finished looking at my empty cup.

I haven’t decided what to do next.

I don’t know if there’s anything left to do.

The wifi just disconnected.

The name of the network that replaced it is:

hello ryan
