I have a strange habit. Every night before I go to bed, I check my
apartment door exactly once. Not twice. Not three times. Once.

Years ago, a roommate pointed out how often I checked locks. He said I
would stand there turning the deadbolt over and over until I was
satisfied. I hated how ridiculous it sounded, so I forced myself to
stop. Since then, I lock the door, test the handle one time, and walk
away. No exceptions.

Three months ago, I came home from work and found my apartment door
unlocked. I remember standing there in the hallway staring at it. I knew
I had locked it that morning. I remembered the sound of the deadbolt
sliding into place. Nothing appeared stolen. Nothing looked disturbed.
Eventually I convinced myself that I had simply forgotten.

Two days later it happened again. The door was unlocked. This time I
searched the entire apartment. Closets. Bathroom. Under the bed. Nobody
was there. As I was about to give up, I noticed something sitting beside
the kitchen sink. A blue coffee mug. I didn’t own a blue coffee mug. It
had a chipped handle and a faded logo I couldn’t recognize. I threw it
into the trash before going to bed. The next morning it was back.
Sitting exactly where I had found it. That was when I started getting
nervous.

Over the following weeks, more things changed. A chair would be facing
the wrong direction. A lamp I had switched off would be glowing when I
got home. Books moved between shelves. A framed photograph appeared on
the wrong wall. None of it was dramatic. Nothing valuable disappeared.
The changes were so small that I struggled to explain them to anyone
else. But they kept happening. Every single week. I stopped inviting
people over. I stopped sleeping properly. I began checking every room
before bed.

One night I woke up around three in the morning. The room was completely
dark. At first I thought I had imagined it. Then I heard breathing.
Slow. Steady. Close. Not outside. Not in another room. Inside my
bedroom. I lay frozen beneath the blankets. The breathing continued for
several seconds. Then it stopped. I reached for the lamp and switched it
on. The room was empty. Nobody stood near my bed. Nobody hid in the
corners. Nothing looked unusual. I didn’t sleep for the rest of the
night.

The next day I bought a security camera. I mounted it on a shelf facing
my bedroom door. For six nights the footage showed nothing unusual. On
the seventh night, I found something that made my blood run cold. At
2:14 AM, my bedroom door slowly opened. A man stepped inside. At first I
thought he was a stranger. Then he moved closer to the camera. The same
face. The same hair. The same scar beneath my chin. He looked exactly
like me. I watched the footage in complete silence. The man walked to
the side of my bed and stood there. Watching me sleep. Not speaking. Not
moving. Just staring. The timestamp continued counting upward. One
minute. Five minutes. Ten minutes. Twenty-three minutes. Then he quietly
turned around and left. I watched the video six times before calling the
police.

They found nothing. No fingerprints. No signs of forced entry. No
evidence. One officer suggested it was an edited recording. Another
suggested I might know the man. I stopped arguing. I knew what I had
seen.

After that, I started noticing him everywhere. Across the street while I
walked home from work. At train stations. In store windows. Always
watching. Always waiting. Whenever I tried to approach him, he
disappeared. I became obsessed. I started carrying my phone everywhere.
I took photographs whenever I thought I saw him. I filled folders with
blurry images. Reflections. Crowds. Shadows. Every picture seemed to
contain someone who looked almost exactly like me.

The obsession consumed my life. Then, last week, I noticed something
strange. I was reviewing one of the photographs. The image showed the
reflection of a storefront window. The man was standing inside the
reflection. Watching me. I almost deleted it. Then I noticed the date.
The timestamp was wrong. According to my phone, the picture had been
taken four years earlier. That was impossible. I didn’t even live in
this city four years ago. I assumed it was a software error. Then I
checked the rest. Another image. Five years old. Another. Three years
old. Another. Six years old. Every photograph containing the man had a
timestamp from before I moved here. Before I rented the apartment.
Before I even had this phone.

I stopped sleeping entirely after that. Two nights ago, I searched
through the apartment for answers. I pulled books off shelves. Opened
drawers. Emptied closets. At the back of a kitchen cabinet, hidden
behind a loose panel, I found a folder. Inside were documents. Utility
bills. Insurance papers. A lease agreement. All belonging to the
previous tenant. The name meant nothing to me. Until I found the
photograph. My hands started shaking immediately. The man in the picture
was smiling at the camera. He had my face. My scar. My eyes. Everything.
The photograph had been taken seven years ago. I had never seen it
before.

I spent the next hour staring at old documents. The same name appeared
everywhere. The previous tenant. The man who had lived here before me.
The man who looked exactly like me. Then I noticed something written on
the back of the photograph. A date. And a sentence. The date was seven
years old. The sentence was worse. It read: “If you’re reading this, he
sees you now.”

Yesterday I reviewed the security footage one final time. I don’t know
why. Maybe I wanted proof that I wasn’t losing my mind. Maybe I wanted
one mistake. One detail that would prove all of this was fake. Instead I
found something worse. Something I hadn’t noticed before. The timestamp.
The footage showing the man standing beside my bed wasn’t recorded last
month. It wasn’t recorded this year. It wasn’t even recorded after I
moved in. The video was seven years old. Seven. The man standing beside
the bed wasn’t watching me. He was watching the previous tenant. The one
in the photograph. The one with my face. The one who left the warning.

I sat there staring at the screen for almost an hour. Then I noticed
movement in the reflection of my monitor. Someone was standing behind my
chair. Watching me. I already knew what I would see before I turned
around. The scar beneath his chin matched mine perfectly. But for the
first time, I noticed something else. He wasn’t copying me. I was
copying him.
