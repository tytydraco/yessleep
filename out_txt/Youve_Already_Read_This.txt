For the first time, the entrance hall smelled like wet paper.

Not dampness. Not mold. Wet paper — like someone had torn dozens of old
books apart and left them to rot between the floors.

I noticed it at night when I was coming home after my shift.

The elevator was out of order again. The stairwell light flickered in
short white bursts, as if someone upstairs kept switching a breaker on
and off.

On the landing between the sixth and seventh floors, there was an
envelope.

No stamp.
No address.

Only my name.

The paper felt cold.

For some reason, I immediately understood: I shouldn’t open it.

And I opened it anyway.

Inside was a thin gray card.

In block letters:

ARCHIVE No. 47
DO NOT READ THE RECORDS ALOUD

And also — a small key.

Old, rusty, like it belonged to a mailbox.

I stood on the stairs for about five minutes, listening to water flowing
somewhere above.

Even though there had been no hot water in the building for a week.

The next day I decided it was some stupid prank.

I threw the card into a kitchen drawer.
The key too.

And almost forgot about it.

Until the evening.

At half past midnight, someone started knocking on the door.

Not aggressively.

Very calmly.

Knock.

Pause.

Knock.

Pause.

Knock.

I looked through the peephole.

No one.

Just the stairwell.
A dim lamp.
And something dark at the threshold.

When the knocking repeated, I opened the door abruptly.

On the doormat lay a folder.

Damp.

As if it had just been pulled out of water.

On the spine:

“ARCHIVE No. 47. FIRST RECORD.”

I slammed the door immediately.

Then I just sat in the kitchen for about ten minutes, staring at the
folder.

The strangest thing —
it was warm.

As if someone had been holding it a second ago.

Inside was a single sheet.

Yellowed, thin.

The text was typewritten.

—

RECORD 1

RESIDENT FROM APARTMENT 82

If an extra apartment appears in your building —
do not count the floors.

Do not check.

Do not ask the neighbors.

Especially after 1 a.m.

An extra apartment exists only as long as it is being thought about.

If the door is ajar —
do not look inside.

There may be someone there who left this building many years ago.

—

At the bottom, someone had added in pen:

HE HAS ALREADY GONE UP

I read it three times.

Then I laughed.

Nervously, of course.
But still — I laughed.

Because I live on the eighth floor.

And there are exactly eighty apartments in the building.

There cannot be an apartment 82 here.

At two in the morning, I heard footsteps above.

Slow.

Dragging.

As if someone was walking barefoot on concrete.

I assumed it was a neighbor from the ninth floor.

Then I remembered:

there is no ninth floor in this building.

The footsteps stopped right outside my door.

Silence.

And then —

a very quiet voice:

“Excuse me… which floor is this?”

I froze.

Heavy breathing came from the other side.

“I’m lost again…”

The voice was elderly.
Hoarse.
And strangely wet.

As if the person was speaking with water in their mouth.

I slowly approached the peephole.

And at that moment I remembered the line:

Do not look inside.

But it was too late.

I was already looking.

A man was standing on the landing.

Tall.
Very thin.

Wearing a long black coat.

And his face…

It was moving.

Not skin.
Not muscles.

As if something alive was writhing beneath it.

Slowly.
Under the flesh.

The man lifted his head.

Even though I hadn’t made a sound.

And smiled.

That’s when I saw it.

Behind him — a door.

It hadn’t been there before.

A gray metal door with a number:

The handle slowly lowered by itself.

The door began to open.

And from the darkness inside, I heard:

the rustling of hundreds of paper pages.
