The sky bellowed, a deep, tearing sound that rippled through the ocean
itself.
I didn’t look up. I was already watching the water.
It moved wrong.
Not the waves. Not the storm.
Something underneath them.
The support vessel pitched hard to starboard, sending a toolbox skidding
across the deck. Nobody paid much attention. We’d spent the better part
of three days fighting rough seas in the middle of the Indian Ocean. By
that point, everything rattled, groaned, or slid somewhere it wasn’t
supposed to.
Still, I couldn’t take my eyes off the water.
The movement was subtle. A shift beneath the surface. Slow. Deliberate.
“You seeing this?”
I turned. Mason stood a few feet away, holding a coffee mug with both
hands.
“Seeing what?”
“The swell.”
I looked back over the railing.
The water was calm again.
“Nothing.”
Mason frowned.
“Yeah. That’s what I thought.”
At the time, neither of us said another word about it.
I wish we had.
The official story was simple. A privately funded expedition had located
a submarine nearly nine hundred meters below the surface, roughly
seventy miles southwest of the Maldives.
The wreck didn’t exist on any public records.
No military database acknowledged it.
No government claimed ownership.
It was just there.
A steel ghost sitting upright on the ocean floor.
The man paying for the expedition, Adrian, called it the discovery of a
lifetime.
The rest of us called it a paycheck.
There were seven people aboard.
Me.
Mason.
Sarah.
Eli.
Grant.
Noah.
And Adrian.
The plan was straightforward. Use a remotely operated vehicle to map the
wreck, identify safe entry points, then send divers inside.
Simple.
Except every drone we’d sent down had stopped transmitting.
Every single one.
The first was written off as equipment failure.
The second was blamed on pressure damage.
The third simply disappeared.
No signal loss.
No warning.
Just gone.
I remember standing in the operations room the night before our first
dive, watching the final seconds of footage recovered from Drone Three.
The submarine sat in complete darkness.
Its hull was covered in decades of marine growth.
Nothing moved.
Then the image distorted.
A burst of static filled the screen.
For a single frame, something pale appeared in front of the camera.
The footage ended immediately afterward.
Nobody could agree on what we’d seen.
A reflection.
Debris.
Compression artifacts.
Anything except what it looked like.
A face.
The next morning, we descended.
I’ve done deep dives before.
Nothing prepares you for that kind of darkness.
The deeper you go, the more it feels like the ocean is replacing the
world.
Light disappears.
Color disappears.
Eventually, even your sense of scale disappears.
The submarine emerged from the blackness without warning.
One second there was nothing.
The next, an enormous wall of rusted steel stood directly in front of
us.
I remember feeling relief.
It sounds ridiculous now.
But seeing the wreck felt comforting.
Solid.
Predictable.
Human.
That feeling lasted less than five minutes.
We entered through a damaged maintenance hatch near the stern.
Inside, the submarine looked frozen in time.
Loose cables hung from ceilings.
Control panels sat untouched beneath layers of corrosion.
Doors remained sealed exactly where the crew had left them.
There was no sign of bodies.
No sign of evacuation.
No sign anyone had ever tried to escape.
It was as though the crew had simply vanished.
Then Eli found the first footprint.
Not human.
Not even close.
It was pressed into a layer of sediment near the reactor compartment.
Long.
Narrow.
Three-toed.
Fresh.
I know how insane that sounds.
Nine hundred meters beneath the ocean.
Inside a sealed submarine.
Fresh footprints.
Nobody wanted to say it aloud.
We all knew the same thing.
Whatever made them was still here.
And judging by the direction they led…
It already knew we were too.
